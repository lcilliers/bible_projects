## Full Text

### T1 — Definition

A definition in the making.

T1 establishes the current best description of a characteristic before the analytical tiers begin. It has three elements: Name, Kind, and Boundary. All three are written from knowledge at hand at the point of first encounter. All three are explicitly held open — reframeable as the research that follows produces findings. T1 is revisited at the conclusion of the full analysis, and the definition is updated to reflect what has been found. The definition grows in precision and depth as the research progresses.

T1 is therefore visited twice: at the beginning to orient the enquiry, and at the end to receive and capture what the research has revealed.

**Name.** What is this characteristic called, and what does the name itself signal? This includes the primary Hebrew and Greek terms and what their root meanings reveal at the definitional level. Name is the most stable element of T1 — it does not change, though its significance may deepen as analysis proceeds. The name of a characteristic is not a label assigned by the programme; it is the word Scripture uses, and what that word means in its original language is the starting point for everything that follows.

**Kind.** What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be at the point of first encounter? Kind is not a category label applied from outside — it is a description that encapsulates the characteristic within the constitutional location it occupies, the faculties it engages, and the impact it produces in the person. It is written as a working description from current knowledge and rewritten after the analytical tiers have completed their work.

The programme's dimension vocabulary — Emotion (Positive), Emotion (Negative), Cognition, Volition, Moral Character, Relational Disposition, Vitality / Existence, Transformation, Agency / Power, Dependence / Creatureliness — provides reference categories useful for pattern recognition and inter-characteristic comparison. Kind is not reducible to a dimension assignment. It is a descriptive synthesis that the dimension vocabulary may inform but does not constrain. The dimension vocabulary itself is open: it represents the programme's current best reading of the kinds of inner-being phenomena Scripture describes, and it is subject to revision as the analytical work deepens across the full registry.

**Boundary.** What is this characteristic not? The structural opposite of the characteristic is identified and described. The boundary defines the characteristic by contrast — it sharpens the description by naming what this characteristic stands against or excludes. Like Kind, Boundary is written from current knowledge and is reframeable. What appears to be the structural opposite at first encounter may be refined or corrected as the analytical tiers produce their findings.

**Scope boundary.** T1 does not ask where the characteristic is located within the human constitution, through which faculties it operates, how it relates to others, how it develops over time, or how it connects to adjacent characteristics. Those questions belong in T2 onward. T1 asks only: what is this characteristic, as best as can currently be described?

**The relationship between T1 and the analytical tiers.** T1 is not a definition that governs the analysis — it is a description that opens it. The risk of a fixed definition at the outset is that it shapes what the analyst looks for and limits what the evidence is permitted to say. T1 avoids this by treating Name as stable, Kind as provisional, and Boundary as working. The analytical tiers then do their work without the constraint of a definition they must confirm. When they are complete, T1 is revisited — and the description is either confirmed in its initial form or substantially rewritten in light of what the evidence has shown.

This revisit is not a correction of error. It is the programme's designed mechanism for allowing the evidence to produce the description rather than the description to precede the evidence.
