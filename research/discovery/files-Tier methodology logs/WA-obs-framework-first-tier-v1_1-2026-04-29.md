## Governing Question

*What are the inner workings of the human being as described in Scripture — characterising and grouping the inner characteristics, mapping their interrelationships, describing the spirit-soul-body boundary, and documenting the relational interfaces with God, other humans, and other spiritual beings?*

---

## The Framework Structure

The observation framework defines the complete scope of analytical enquiry for every inner-being characteristic studied in the programme. It has two layers.

The **first tier** defines the major analytical domains — the areas that together constitute a complete characterisation of any inner-being characteristic. There are eight first-tier domains: T0 through T7. Not every domain will be richly populated for every characteristic — some domains will be silent for a given word. The first tier defines what could be explored; the evidence determines what is found.

The **second tier** defines the constituent components within each first-tier domain — the specific questions and elements that analytical work within that domain addresses. The second tier is under development and is documented separately.

The framework functions as a discipline of consistent and complete enquiry. Applied to every word in the programme's scope, it ensures that no area of the inner being is systematically missed, and that findings across words are produced on the same analytical footing.

---

## Epistemological Basis

The framework rests on an epistemological basis that applies uniformly across all tiers.

**Scripture is the primary and governing source.** What Scripture says about a characteristic within any tier is the finding. If Scripture is silent on an aspect of any tier, that silence is noted — it is not filled by inference or from other sources.

**Original language analysis** — Hebrew and Greek lexical and semantic study — is the primary evidential tool. It is a sub-discipline of the Scripture source, not a separate source.

**Human science frameworks** — psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, linguistics, hermeneutics — serve as interpretive lenses and light sanity checks. They illuminate and organise what Scripture describes. They help ensure that the reading of the evidence is coherent and complete. They do not add content to any tier.

The scope of human science engagement is selective and practical — not exhaustive. A full human science treatment of any single tier represents a lifetime of scholarly work for specialists. The programme's engagement is bounded: it draws on human science frameworks where they help ensure completeness and coherence; it does not attempt to replicate specialist scholarship in each discipline.

## First-Tier Domains

---

### T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

> The purposive frame of the programme. T0 holds the foundational question of why this characteristic exists and what it reveals about the human person as image-bearer of God.

T0 is the purposive envelope within which all the analytical tiers operate. It does not ask how a characteristic works, where it lives, or how it connects to other characteristics. It asks why it exists — what it reveals about the nature of God whose image the person bears, and what created purpose it serves in the human person.

The human person is made in the image of God — *imago Dei*. Every inner-being characteristic exists within that frame. The characteristics the programme studies are not merely psychological or relational phenomena; they are constitutive features of image-bearing persons. T0 examines what each characteristic reveals about the divine nature it images and the created purpose it serves.

T0 operates at two levels. At the level of the individual characteristic, it asks: what does this characteristic reveal about the nature of God? What created purpose does it serve in the human person? How does it express God's own character reflected in the image-bearer? At the level of the programme as a whole, T0 asks: what does this entire constellation of inner-being characteristics reveal about what it means to be made in the image of God — what is the human being designed to be, do, and become?

The individual-characteristic level of T0 is addressed in Session B. The programme-level question is the governing concern of Session D. T0 is present at every stage of the programme, but it comes fully into its own in Session D, where the characteristics studied across the programme are held together and the integrating question of the divine image is examined in the aggregate.

Sexuality finds its primary home in T0. The male-female construct in Scripture is not merely biological or constitutional — it is used typologically throughout Scripture as a language for expressing relational, creative, and covenantal realities that transcend the biological. God describes himself as Father; the church is the bride of Christ; the covenant between God and his people is imaged in the marriage bond. These are not incidental analogies. They are Scripture's deliberate deployment of the created reality of sexuality to point toward and participate in the structure of the divine-human relationship. T0 is where that significance is examined.

*Scope boundary: T0 does not ask where a characteristic is located, through which faculties it operates, or how it connects to other characteristics. Those questions belong in T2–T7. T0 asks why the characteristic exists and what it reveals about the divine image and created design.*

### T1 — Definition

> A definition in the making. T1 establishes the current best description of a characteristic — what it is called, what kind of inner-being phenomenon it appears to be, and what it is not.

T1 establishes the current best description of a characteristic before the analytical tiers begin. It has three elements: Name, Kind, and Boundary. All three are written from knowledge at hand at the point of first encounter. All three are explicitly held open — reframeable as the research that follows produces findings. T1 is revisited at the conclusion of the full analysis, and the description is updated to reflect what has been found. The definition grows in precision and depth as the research progresses.

T1 is therefore visited twice: at the beginning to orient the enquiry, and at the end to receive what the research has revealed.

**Name** — What is this characteristic called, and what does the name itself signal? This includes the primary Hebrew and Greek terms and what their root meanings reveal at the definitional level. Name is the most stable element of T1 — it does not change, though its significance may deepen as analysis proceeds.

**Kind** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be at the point of first encounter? Kind is not a category label applied from outside — it is a description that encapsulates the characteristic within the constitutional location it occupies, the faculties it engages, and the impact it produces in the person. It is written as a working description from current knowledge and rewritten after the analytical tiers have completed their work. The programme's dimension vocabulary provides reference categories useful for pattern recognition and comparison — but Kind is not reducible to a dimension assignment. It is a descriptive synthesis that the dimension vocabulary may inform but does not constrain. The dimension vocabulary itself is open: it represents the programme's current best reading of the kinds of inner-being phenomena Scripture describes, and it is subject to revision as the analytical work deepens.

**Boundary** — What is this characteristic not? The structural opposite of the characteristic is identified and described. The boundary defines the characteristic by contrast — it sharpens the description by naming what this characteristic stands against or excludes. Like Kind, Boundary is written from current knowledge and is reframeable as research proceeds.

*Scope boundary: T1 does not ask where the characteristic is located, through which faculties it operates, how it relates to others, or how it develops. T1 asks only: what is this characteristic, as best as can currently be described?*

### T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

> T2 maps a characteristic onto the constitutional structure of the human person — identifying where it is seated, where it is expressed, and how it moves across constitutional boundaries.

Constitutional locations are not physical organs. The body and its structures — including the brain — are the design layer through which the constitutional person interfaces with the physical world. A characteristic may be expressed through the body without being housed there. Cognition is not located in the brain — the brain is the physical connector through which cognition engages the physical world; the cognitive faculty transcends the body and persists beyond it (Scripture shows the dead knowing, remembering, and recognising — Luke 16).

The constitutional locations T2 examines are: the spirit (the deepest level of the person and the primary site of encounter with God); the soul (the seat of the faculties and their expression into the characteristics of innermost personal experience); the heart and mind as named subsets of the soul (with other soul subsets held open where the evidence surfaces them); and the body (the physical design layer, with DNA as its design print carried forward generationally, and specific body parts explicitly linked to characteristics in Scripture).

When Scripture links a characteristic to a body part, T2 examines the significance of that link — what Scripture is doing by making the connection. The link may be emphatic (giving vividness to a soul-level reality), functional (the body part genuinely participates in the characteristic), expressive (the body gives outward utterance to the inner reality), indicative (the body signals the inner state), or mediating (the body is the channel through which the characteristic enters the person from outside). T2 also examines the direction of the body-characteristic relationship — whether the soul expresses through the body, the body feeds back to the soul, or both — and whether sustained operation of the characteristic leaves a constitutional deposit in the body or its design.

*Scope boundary: T2 asks where a characteristic is constitutionally located, where it originates, and what Scripture is doing when it links the characteristic to constitutional elements and body parts. The question of where a characteristic originates — whether it is generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally — is a constitutional location question and belongs in T2. The analysis of why it originates there — which faculty is engaged in that origination, how it operates relationally in being received or given, what structural relationship it has with adjacent characteristics — is examined in T3, T4, and T6 respectively. T2 maps the constitutional what; the other tiers examine the why and how.*

### T3 — The Inner Faculties

> T3 examines a characteristic through the lens of the human person's active inner capacities — the faculties through which every characteristic is received, processed, expressed, and chosen.

The inner faculties are not locations (T2) and are not characteristics being studied — they are the constitutional equipment the person brings to every inner-being encounter. Every characteristic engages some of these faculties and not others; the pattern of engagement is part of the characteristic's identity.

The faculties T3 examines are: perception (the inner senses — Scripture evidences inner analogues to each of the five physical senses: inner hearing as spiritual receptivity — *he who has ears to hear*; inner sight as the eyes of the heart — *that the eyes of your heart may be enlightened*; inner taste as direct experiential knowledge — *taste and see that the Lord is good*; inner touch as the sense of presence and reality; inner smell as the perception of quality — *a fragrant offering*. Beyond these five, Scripture and human experience point toward a sixth faculty of perception — spiritual discernment or intuition: knowing beyond rational process, awareness that does not arrive through the five senses or through cognitive reasoning. This sixth perception is provisionally seated in the spirit level of the constitution and is held as a live question for the programme's analytical work); cognition (knowing, understanding, discerning — not housed in the brain but transcending the body); memory (the temporal depth of the inner person — holding and retrieving inner-being reality across time, distinct from cognition of the present); affect (feeling and emotional experience); creativity (the faculty of origination — encompassing imagination and the capacity to bring into being what did not exist; grounded in the image of the Creator God); volition (the God-given capacity to choose — constitutionally given, distinguishing the human person from other creatures, and operating under the constraints of DNA, formation, and circumstance); agency (the capacity to act, initiate, trigger, and make happen — distinct from volition, which precedes it); moral evaluation (the capacity to assess against a standard — right, wrong, good, true); conscience (the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — distinct from moral evaluation as the broader faculty); conscientiousness (the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action); and relational capacity (the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person).

*Scope boundary: T3 asks what inner capacities the person brings and how a characteristic engages them. It does not ask where the characteristic is constitutionally located (T2), how it operates across relational interfaces (T4), or how it develops the person over time (T5).*

---

### T4 — Relational Interfaces

> T4 examines how a characteristic operates across the three relational interfaces of the human person — with God, with other humans, and with other spiritual beings.

The governing question names relational interfaces explicitly: the human person exists in structured relationship — not as an isolated individual but as a being constitutionally oriented toward God, other humans, and the wider spiritual reality. Every inner-being characteristic has some operation across these interfaces — it is received through them, expressed through them, shaped by them, or shapes them.

**The divine interface** examines the characteristic in the God-to-human and human-to-God directions: what it reveals about God's disposition toward the human person, the basis on which God extends or withholds it, and how the person seeks, receives, and responds to it in worship, supplication, and covenant.

**The human interface** examines how the characteristic operates between persons: whether and how the person who has received it extends it to others, what relational orientations enable or block its extension, and whether it operates differently within existing relationships versus across relational boundaries.

**The spiritual beings interface** examines the characteristic's operation in relation to other spiritual beings — both angelic and adversarial. Scripture is explicit that the human person inhabits a wider spiritual environment populated by beings other than God and other humans: angels who serve and communicate, and adversarial spiritual powers who oppose, tempt, accuse, and oppress. Every inner-being characteristic exists within this wider spiritual environment. Some characteristics are explicitly shaped by encounter with spiritual beings — fear in the presence of an angel, deception by an adversarial spirit, strengthening through angelic ministry. Others are the terrain on which adversarial spiritual activity operates — the mind as the field of deceptive thought, the conscience as the site of accusation and counter-accusation. T4 asks whether this characteristic has a named relationship to the spiritual beings interface in Scripture, and what that relationship reveals. This interface is currently underrepresented in the observation question catalogue — the programme recognises this as a gap and will address it as the analytical framework develops.

*Scope boundary: T4 asks how a characteristic operates across relational interfaces. It does not ask where it is constitutionally located (T2), through which inner faculties it operates (T3), or how it develops the person over time (T5).*

---

### T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

> T5 examines how a characteristic shapes the person over time — what it does to the person across time, through what process and mechanism, in what direction, and toward what ultimate end.

T5 is distinct from T3 (which asks what inner capacities are engaged) and T4 (which asks how the characteristic operates relationally). T5 asks about the developmental arc — the person before, during, and after the characteristic has done its formative work.

T5 examines the nature of the transformation the characteristic produces — whether it changes the person's condition or the person's orientation to their condition. It examines the sequence of inner states through which the characteristic moves the person, and the mechanism of change — whether through discipline, transformation, encounter, or something else. It examines the relationship between the characteristic and suffering and affliction as formative contexts. It examines how the characteristic shapes the person in the longer arc of formation and sanctification. And it examines the eschatological trajectory — the ultimate direction the characteristic points toward, and what its present experience anticipates about a future fullness.

The constitutional deposit question from T2 feeds into T5: where T2 identifies that sustained operation of a characteristic leaves a constitutional deposit in the body or its design, T5 examines the developmental consequence of that deposit — what it produces in the person over time, and in those who come after.

*Scope boundary: T5 asks how a characteristic shapes the person over time. It does not ask where it is constitutionally located (T2), through which faculties it operates (T3), or how it connects to other characteristics (T6).*

---

### T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

> T6 maps a characteristic within the wider landscape of the inner person — identifying connections, dependencies, sequences, overlaps, and distinctions with other characteristics.

The governing question names the mapping of interrelationships as a programme purpose. No characteristic exists in isolation. Every characteristic has structural relationships — some characteristics precede others, some produce others, some exclude others, some co-occur consistently, some share vocabulary at the root level. T6 makes these relationships visible and examined.

T6 examines co-occurrence patterns (which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently in the same verses), sequential relationships (which characteristics precede, follow, or accompany this one), causal and constitutive relationships (whether this characteristic produces another or is produced by one), vocabulary and root sharing (which adjacent characteristics share terms or root architecture), distinctions (where apparent overlap between characteristics requires careful differentiation), shared verse anchors (where a verse functions as a primary anchor in more than one characteristic's study), and dimensional sharing (how many of this characteristic's confirmed dimensions are shared with another).

*Scope boundary: T6 asks how this characteristic relates structurally to other characteristics. It does not ask how it operates within the person (T2, T3) or how it develops the person over time (T5).*

---

### T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

> T7 defines where the knowledge of a characteristic comes from and how it is derived with integrity. It is the epistemological basis that underlies all other tiers.

T7 is qualitatively different from T0–T6. The other tiers define the body of understanding — the substantive areas that together constitute a complete characterisation. T7 defines the epistemological foundation — the sources and disciplines from which understanding in every other tier is derived and validated. T7 does not stand alongside the other tiers as a peer analytical area; it underlies all of them.

T7 has three components. **Lexical and semantic analysis** covers the original language vocabulary of the characteristic — Hebrew and Greek terms, root meanings, grammatical forms, semantic range, canonical development across the Testaments, LXX bridging, and NT coinage. This is the primary linguistic evidence base from which all findings across every tier are drawn. **Verse and literary interpretation** covers how the characteristic functions within its verse evidence — the word's role in the sentence, the logical structure of the argument, the literary form (narrative, psalm, prophecy, wisdom, epistle), and the contextual setting (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological). **Human science frameworks** covers the interpretive lenses drawn from psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, and related disciplines — applied as sanity checks to ensure coherence and completeness, not as co-equal sources alongside Scripture.

*Scope boundary: T7 is the evidential and methodological foundation. It underlies all other tiers. It does not itself produce substantive findings about a characteristic — it validates and grounds the findings produced by T0–T6.*
