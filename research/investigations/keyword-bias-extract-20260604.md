# Keyword bias-trap — eschatological · defilement · corrupt

> **Read-only review extract, 2026-06-04.** For each loaded interpretive keyword, every
> meaning+keyword **anchor** whose keywords contain the token, with the **actual verse**, the
> **Pass-A meaning**, and the **keywords**. Purpose: trap over-reading / imposed interpretation.
> Script: `scripts/_exploratory_keyword_bias_extract_v1_20260604.py`.
>
> **How to mark up:** flag any row where the meaning/keyword reads *more* into the verse than the
> text supports (e.g. "eschatological" on a plainly present/physical rescue; "defilement" or
> "corrupt" as a moral verdict the verse does not make). Add your note inline after the entry.

## Candidate bias signals (CC — for your adjudication, not verdicts)

**1. `eschatological` looks like a reflexive overlay, not a reading.** 16 of the 18 instances are the
*identical* phrase `salvation eschatological`, attached to almost any salvation verse regardless of
whether the text is about *future/end-state* salvation or *present/physical/temporal* deliverance. The
keyword does not discriminate. Strongest candidate over-reads:
- **Gen 49:18** "I wait for your salvation, O Lord" — Jacob's blessing; tagging it *eschatological*
  imports later (NT) eschatology onto a patriarchal expression of trust/temporal deliverance.
- **Hab 3:8** "chariot of salvation" — a theophanic/historical war image, labelled eschatological.
- **Mat 16:25** "whoever would save his life…" — meaning reads *"eschatological self-realization"*, a
  loaded modern phrase the verse does not carry.
- **2Cor 7:10** godly grief → salvation — a present-tense process of repentance, still tagged eschatological.
→ *Question for the audit:* is "eschatological" being applied **from the verse**, or as a habitual gloss
on σῴζω/"salvation"? If the latter, it is precisely the bias we want to trap.

**2. `defilement` shows two category slips.**
- **"Unclean spirits" conflated with ritual/moral defilement.** **Mar 3:11**, **Luk 6:18** — demonic
  *akathartos* ("unclean spirit") is a *different concept* from ritual/moral defilement, yet tagged
  `defilement subjugated` / `defilement removed`. Demonology folded into purity language.
- **Inner-being / "will" language on involuntary bodily-ritual states.** **Lev 15:7** (discharge contact),
  **Lev 15:19** (menstrual impurity), **Num 19:11** (corpse contact) are *involuntary, bodily* ritual
  conditions — not acts of the will or inner moral life. The meanings here are mostly restrained
  ("body-sourced", "death contact"), but the deeper question is **scope**: does ritual impurity belong in
  an *inner-being* study at all, or is its inclusion itself a category error?

**3. `corrupt` is the best-grounded** — the 32 verses genuinely concern moral corruption (Sodom, the
bribe, the perverted heart, the old self "corrupt through deceitful desires"). The one systematic note:
the **inner-faculty lens is applied very uniformly** — nearly every verse is rendered as `will oriented
evil` / `heart corrupted` / `inner corruption`. Mostly apt, but watch for slight additions, e.g.
**Exo 23:8** (a bribe corrupts *judgement/perception*) gains a `will compromised` tag the verse doesn't
quite state. Risk: a uniform lens can flatten real differences between verses.

**Counter-signal (good):** the meanings *do* sometimes self-limit — **Mat 24:40** is explicitly marked
*"not inner forgiveness … no direct inner-being forgiveness content."* So the bias is not total; it is
selective, which is exactly why a targeted audit (not a blanket distrust) is the right tool.
