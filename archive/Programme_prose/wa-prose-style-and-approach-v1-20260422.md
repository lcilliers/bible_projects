## 1. The style in one sentence

Direct, factual, to the point. No padding, no imaginative commentary, no rhetorical closings. Every sentence is either a statement of fact about the programme or carries information the reader needs.

## 2. What this style requires

### 2.1 Facts, not framing

A sentence that introduces what is about to be said, but does not itself say anything, is not a fact. Examples of what to cut:

- "The programme's method is shaped by the question it investigates..." — framing; the method itself is the fact.
- "Each stage has a different character and a different role." — filler before the stages are described.
- "The governing premise is the second-lens position." — label on what the next sentence already says.

### 2.2 No motive attribution or speculation about alternatives

The prose describes what the programme does, not what it avoids doing at the level of hypothetical alternatives. Cut:

- "A registry built hastily, or built around a researcher's interests, would produce a study whose boundaries are interests rather than evidence." — speculation.
- "A different programme, with different aims, could make a different decision. This one has made this one." — rhetorical.
- "the programme accepts the pace this imposes because the alternative is the thing the method is designed to prevent." — psychologising what the programme does.

### 2.3 No metaphors or labels layered on facts

Where a metaphor or editorial label has been added to clarify, the fact alone usually suffices. Cut:

- "fourth arc" → the researcher's term "final step".
- "first substantive act of discipline, not a preliminary housekeeping task" — label.
- "the accretion of interpretive residue" — metaphor.
- "the programme's disappointments; they are its shape" — flourish.
- "The constraints are not the programme's disappointments..." — tidy closing.

### 2.4 No tidy summations at the end of paragraphs

The paragraph has done its work. A final sentence that restates the paragraph in different words is padding. Examples cut from Area 2:

- 2.1 closing: "...is the thing the method is designed to prevent."
- 2.2: "The entries do not carry findings; they are the starting point from which findings are produced."
- 2.5: "The programme's interest in publishing is the reader's interest."
- 2.6 principle 4: "It is the reason the corpus is the corpus and not a curated selection."

### 2.5 Researcher's own vocabulary over Claude AI's paraphrase

Where the researcher has articulated a principle or concept in their own words, use that vocabulary. Examples:

- "Three critical building blocks" (researcher message 2) — kept as the structural frame for 2.1.
- "No guessing, no assumptions, no made up results" (researcher message 2) — kept verbatim as principle 5 heading in 2.6.
- "Irrespective of what contribution or meaning it may have" (researcher message 2) — kept verbatim in principle 4 in 2.6.
- "Final step" (researcher message 2) — replaced Claude AI's "fourth arc" in 2.1.
- "Interrelationships and impacts of words in the inner soul" (researcher message 2) — replaced Claude AI's speculation about Session D.

### 2.6 Internal cross-references when a concept is covered elsewhere

Where a sub-section's territory overlaps with another sub-section, the later sub-section references the earlier rather than re-describing. Example from 2.5 v2: the audience and judgement criteria are carried by `prog_purp_expected_outcome` in chapter 1; 2.5 does not re-describe them.

### 2.7 Closed-corpus rule — describing the programme from the prose alone

The prose must be self-sufficient. No "see [external document]" references. If a concept is needed for understanding, it is stated in the prose. Internal cross-references to other prose sub-sections are permitted and encouraged (per 2.6 above).

## 3. What the style does NOT require

### 3.1 Brevity as such

Facts that take a paragraph to state take a paragraph. A sub-section can be short (2.7 at 422 words) or long (2.4 at 925 words); what matters is that every sentence is doing work.

### 3.2 A single register

The programme's subject matter is serious, and the register is theologically serious. The discipline is against rhetorical flourish, not against substance. Where the fact is structural, it is stated structurally; where it is technical, it is stated technically.

### 3.3 Avoidance of metaphor at all costs

Some metaphors are accepted programme vocabulary — "anchor verse", "inflection point", "second lens". These are not imaginative additions; they are the programme's working terms. The discipline is against Claude AI's added metaphors, not against vocabulary the programme has settled on.

## 4. Working practice for Claude AI

### 4.1 Before drafting

Identify the facts the sub-section must carry. These come from:
- The researcher's articulation (messages in this session or prior sessions).
- Source documents (instructions, Science Framework, registry files).
- The database state and schema.
- Cross-references to other sub-sections where the fact belongs there.

### 4.2 During drafting

State each fact in the shortest form that carries it. Do not add framing, labels, or rhetorical connectives.

### 4.3 Before presenting

Self-audit every sentence: is this a fact about the programme, or is it Claude AI's commentary on the fact? If the latter, cut.

Specific patterns to check for:
- Opening paragraphs that introduce rather than state.
- Closing paragraphs or sentences that restate what has already been said.
- Transition phrases ("This is why...", "The discipline is...", "The constraint is what...").
- Paired oppositions ("stable in its boundary but live in its membership").
- Pattern of [fact] + [gloss on the fact].

### 4.4 In the obslog

Record specific phrases cut and why. This creates an audit trail the researcher can review. It also trains the next drafting cycle to avoid the patterns that needed cutting in this one.

## 5. Factual correction discipline

Where Claude AI has invented a fact (most often through confident speculation about programme mechanics that are not actually documented), this is a factual error, not a stylistic one. Examples from session prose v2:

- 2.3 v1 described Session D findings as including "where one word splits into two that had been wrongly conflated" — this is not a stated Session D function per the orientation document.
- 2.3 v1 conflated Session B's Readiness/Output structural split with the 10-step analytical pass — two different organising structures treated as one.
- 2.5 v1 carried the same Session D speculation as 2.3.

Factual corrections are named as such in the change note, and the source (instruction document + section) that grounds the correction is cited.

## 6. Handover content — what every session must do

Every prose-drafting session, from this point forward:

1. Loads this document at startup alongside the global rules file.
2. Applies the style checks in §4 before presenting any draft.
3. Records style-related cuts in the obslog, not just in a general audit note at the end.
4. Flags factual errors explicitly (§5) when they occur or are discovered.
5. Before closing, confirms that the sessions's drafts are consistent with the style carried in Area 1 (post-preamble supersede), the preamble (v2), and Area 2. If an inconsistency is found, either the drafts or the style document are revised — not both, not silently.
