## 2.1 Research method

The programme's research method is shaped by the question it investigates and by a considered decision about how to investigate it. The question is what Scripture reveals about the human inner being. The method is to proceed corpus-level from the biblical text, word by word, through a disciplined sequence that separates data gathering from analysis, treats the evidence before interpreting it, and returns to the full body of findings only once every word has been worked through on its own terms.

The method rests on three building blocks, in a definite order. **First, the registry** — a list of English words that name inner-being characteristics, each mapped to the Hebrew and Greek terms through which Scripture expresses it. The registry is built against the programme's working definition of the inner being and is completed before any word is studied. It is the scope, and everything downstream — which verses are read, which terms are classified, which findings the programme produces — is defined by what is in it. **Second, data collation** — for every word in the registry, the programme assembles every verse in which the word or any of its related Hebrew or Greek terms occurs, together with the lexicon data for all those terms. The data is gathered without interpretation: a verse qualifies because the original-language term is present in it, not because a particular reading of the verse has been proposed. Collation is completed for the whole registry before analysis begins. **Third, analysis** — once the data is complete, each word is examined in its full corpus. Similar verses are grouped together into characteristic-perspective groups; each group is given an anchor verse that represents it; the groups are assigned to dimensions of the inner being; and each word is then worked through a standing catalogue of questions that interrogate the verse evidence and the lexical data together. The answers are persisted in the database and become the finding for that word.

A fourth arc closes the work. Once every word has been analysed, the findings are read across the corpus — synthesis at the level of clusters of related characteristics, at the level of relationships between words, and at the level of the inner-being account as a whole. Synthesis does not begin until the per-word analysis is complete; it is a consequence of the first three blocks, not a parallel activity.

The sequencing between the three blocks is a methodological commitment, not a convenience. Building the registry before collation means the programme's scope is settled before the evidence is gathered, and the registry's boundary is a matter of definition rather than of interest. Completing collation before analysis means the analytical frame is not built from the early words and then imposed on the later ones; every word is read against the same completed corpus of data. Persisting analytical findings into the database — with prior findings discarded each time the data is re-read — means the evidence remains the primary reference, and findings cannot drift away from the data that produced them.

The method is biblical research first. Scripture is the primary lens, and the analytical work is work on the biblical text. A second lens — contemporary scientific literature on the inner life — is held alongside it, engaged at a defined point in the per-word analysis rather than running in parallel. The reasoning behind the second-lens discipline, and the way science is engaged within it, are described in the sub-section on the role of science.

The method is discovery-oriented. Categories are not imposed on the data; they emerge from it. Dimensions of the inner being are identified by reading the verses and asking what the verses themselves engage. A category that cannot be grounded in verse evidence is not a category the programme uses. A finding that cannot be traced back to a specific verse or lexical source is not a finding the programme records. The discipline is evidence-first throughout, and the programme accepts the pace this imposes because the alternative — proceeding faster by assuming what the evidence would show — is the thing the method is designed to prevent.

## 2.2 Word selection and the registry

The programme's scope is carried by a single instrument: the registry of inner-being words. Every word it contains is a word the programme will study; every word outside it is outside the programme's scope. The integrity of the registry therefore decides the integrity of everything downstream — what is analysed, what is synthesised, and what the programme's completed account describes. The registry was built with that weight in mind, through a staged process that treats the working definition of the inner being as the sole admission criterion and documents the evidential warrant for each entry.

Registry construction proceeded in four stages, each with a different character and a different role.

The first stage was **direct extraction** from prior work. The programme drew on the vocabulary of inner-being characteristics already identified in Framework B, retaining those words where a single Hebrew or Greek term carried the English sense in a lexically direct way. These entries required no inferential work: each modern English word mapped to one or more biblical terms through their standard meanings in the primary lexica, and their verse exemplars are immediate. This stage established the registry's stable core.

The second stage was **inferential extension**. A set of modern psychological and conceptual terms — emotion, personality, intuition, manipulation, ambition, among others — names territory that Scripture clearly engages but does not lexicalise under a single matching term. To include such words required a different kind of warrant: not a direct lexical mapping but an explicit, flagged inferential one. Each entry in this stage carries one of three inference flags. *Inferred* indicates that no biblical term directly translates the modern word, but the closest semantic equivalents have been identified. *Partial* indicates that a biblical term covers part of what the modern word means but not all of it. *Absent* indicates that the concept is present in Scripture narratively but has no single lexical expression. The discipline is transparency. The registry does not pretend to lexical directness where none exists, and the flags carry forward into the analytical phase so that later work treats these words on their actual evidential footing.

The third stage was **systematic gap analysis**. The first two stages had been shaped by Framework B's prior analytical focus — the distinction between soul and spirit. That focus had produced a vocabulary weighted toward terms relevant to the soul-spirit question and had under-represented adjacent domains: the cognitive and mental vocabulary, the moral and conscience vocabulary, the relational and social vocabulary. The gap analysis applied the inner-being definition freshly to the full semantic range of biblical Hebrew and Greek, working from the primary lexica — Brown-Driver-Briggs, Köhler-Baumgartner, Bauer-Danker-Arndt-Gingrich, Thayer — and from standard treatments of biblical anthropology. The stage added approximately eighty-five words to the registry, distributed across eight domains: cognitive and mental, volitional, affective and emotional, moral and conscience, relational and social, spiritual and God-ward, character and disposition, and identity and selfhood. Its corrective function was explicit: to close the gap the prior focus had left.

The fourth stage runs continuously during data collation. As the programme assembles the full verse and lexicon data for each registry word, the STEP-based retrieval sometimes surfaces related Hebrew or Greek terms that meet the inner-being definition but were not on the original list. A term that satisfies the definition by the same criterion as the existing entries — presence in the original-language text of verses that engage the inner being — is a candidate for registry inclusion. Approximately thirty words have been added through this fourth stage to date. The mechanism matters: it propagates one level up the same discipline that governs verse selection, where original-language occurrence, not interpretation, qualifies a verse for inclusion. Applied to the registry, the same discipline admits words surfaced by the evidence itself. The registry is therefore stable in its boundary criterion but live in its membership — closed to interpretive expansion, open to evidence surfacing from within the corpus.

Every registry entry carries the data the next phase needs. Hebrew terms appear with their transliteration, a concise gloss, and sample verses anchoring the meaning. Greek terms are recorded in the same shape. Each entry has a list of conceptual search terms — alternate English glosses, theological synonyms, and characteristic collocations — which support cross-checking and allow verses that use the underlying term in less common English translations to be found. Each entry also carries a STEP-Bible search suggestion, identifying the landmark verses that seed the analysis. The entries do not carry findings; they are the starting point from which findings are produced.

At present the active registry stands at approximately two hundred words — the working figure already stated in the scope sub-section. The total record count is larger, because the registry retains entries that have been excluded on considered grounds rather than deleting them, so that the reasoning behind exclusion remains visible and reviewable. The retained exclusions are not active scope; they are the registry's audit of its own boundary.

## 2.3 Programme flow

The programme moves each word through a defined sequence of phases. Each phase has an input it receives, an output it produces, and an inflection point that must be satisfied before the word advances. The sequence is not a rolling conveyor; it is a disciplined progression in which every phase's output is persisted into the database, and the database state is what entitles a word to enter the next phase. A word that has not satisfied its current phase stays where it is; no phase is skipped; no assessment made at one phase is treated as final before the next phase confirms it.

The phases are six in number, named in the order in which they run.

**Session A — Term extraction.** For each registry word, Session A assembles the complete source data: the Hebrew and Greek terms that carry the word's meaning, the full set of verses in which those terms occur, and the lexicon data for every term. The source is STEP Bible, from which the verse inventory is retrieved and prepared in a structured form. Session A runs in two phases within a single working window — a data-preparation phase that produces a source file for researcher verification, and an import phase that produces the database import record. Its output is the complete per-word dataset: every term, every verse, every lexical attribute, ready to be read. Its inflection point is import into the database and confirmation that the data is complete for that word.

**Verse Context — Verse reading and grouping.** Once a word's data is in the database, the Verse Context stage reads every verse in which the word's terms occur. Each verse is filtered for relevance — does it engage the inner being through this term, or is the term present in a sense that falls outside the programme's question? Relevant verses are grouped by contextual meaning. Groups describe what a verse is about — the inner-being characteristic it engages — rather than what the term does within the sentence. The same term can therefore serve different characteristics across its corpus, and different groups can hold different angles on the same word. Each group is given an anchor verse — the citation that represents the group most clearly and that serves as the primary input to subsequent analysis. The output is a complete map of the word's verses: which are in, how they cluster, which verses anchor each cluster. The inflection point is completion for the full registry of an OWNER term — every verse classified, every group anchored, every word re-exported in its updated form.

**Dimension Review — Dimensional placement.** The groups produced by Verse Context are then read across a cluster of related registries at once, and each group is assigned a dimension of the inner being. The dimensions are data-derived — a working vocabulary that emerged from the corpus and that is refined by continued contact with it, not a list imposed from theological tradition or psychological category. A dimension that cannot be grounded in at least one verse in the registry's corpus is not a dimension for that registry. The Dimension Review also validates that each group's description remains characteristic-centric and corrects groups that had been formed under an earlier, term-centric model. Its output is a set of confirmed group placements, ready for analytical work. Its inflection point is the cluster's completion: every active registry in the cluster with every group dimensioned and locked.

**Session B — Word analysis.** With verse context grouped and dimensioned, Session B runs the deep analytical pass on each word in turn. Session B is organised in two halves — a readiness half that establishes the state of the data and the analytical frame for the word, and an output half that produces the written work. A biblical pass is done first: semantic characterisation of the word, dimensional placement, treatment of the divine and somatic dimensions as they appear in the verses, relational and causative patterns, anchor-verse analysis. A scientific pass follows — never simultaneous — engaging the relevant contemporary research at defined points. Every finding is substantiated by data in the database, and every answer is persisted there. The analytical process is contained by this persistence: on re-reading, prior findings are set aside and the data is read freshly. Session B's output is the complete analytical record for the word. Its inflection point is a word whose analysis is closed — every standing question answered, every finding traceable to evidence, the record ready for publication.

**Session C — Publication.** Session C produces the reader-facing written study for each word. It is the primary presentation document. It reads from Session B's analytical record and presents the word in a form a reader can work with — structured, navigable, written for the audience described in the expected-outcome sub-section. Session C stands on its own: a reader encountering a single word study gains a considered account of that aspect of the inner being, grounded in the evidence and transparent about what remains uncertain. Session C is produced one word at a time, and a word's publication depends on its analysis being closed.

**Session D — Cross-registry synthesis.** Once every word in a cluster has moved through Session C, Session D reads the results across the cluster as a whole. It produces the second-order findings that are not visible at the single-word level: where two words turn out to be different angles on a single reality, where one word splits into two that had been wrongly conflated, where a characteristic runs across a group of words in a consistent way. Session D is the instrument of synthesis at cluster scale; beyond it lies the programme-level synthesis that becomes possible only when all clusters are complete. Session D's inflection point is the cluster-level account: a written synthesis that adds to the per-word studies what they cannot say alone.

The pipeline handles a word once at each phase and carries its state between phases through the database. When a word is at Verse Context, the database records that state; when it moves to Dimension Review, the database is updated; Session B reads the state to know which words are ready to receive analytical work. The state is also what allows the programme to pause and resume safely — a session is a span of work on words that are ready for that phase, not a fixed timetable, and work can be taken up at whatever point the database shows it left off.

## 2.4 Science in action

The relationship between Scripture and contemporary science is described conceptually in the sub-section on the two lenses. This sub-section describes the operational consequence: how the scientific lens is engaged within the programme's work, at what point, through what instrument, and with what discipline.

The governing premise is the second-lens position. Scientific literature relevant to the inner being is genuinely vast — psychology, neuroscience, physiology, behavioural science, developmental and cognitive psychology, and evolutionary biology each address territory the programme's corpus covers. Treated as a co-equal research programme, the scientific material would demand a second corpus-level effort matching the biblical one in scope and rigour. That demand is rejected. The programme does not require comprehensive literature coverage; it requires representative and reliable coverage of the landmark findings in each relevant field, held alongside the biblical analysis as the second lens through which the same territory is read. This single decision — science as second lens rather than co-equal lens — resolves the scope problem and shapes everything that follows in scientific engagement.

**When science enters the work.** Scientific engagement happens during Session B and is sequenced after the biblical pass is complete. Steps one to six of Session B's analytical protocol are the biblical pass — semantic characterisation, dimensional placement, divine and somatic dimensions, relational and causative analysis, anchor-verse work. Steps seven and eight are the scientific pass. Steps nine and ten are the integrating synthesis. The sequencing is a methodological commitment, not a matter of scheduling convenience. Running the two passes simultaneously risks the scientific frame — which is often rich, well-developed, and rhetorically compelling — distorting the biblical reading before the biblical analysis is complete. For words where the scientific literature is particularly substantial (shame, fear, grief, and comparable affective territory), the risk of contamination in the reverse direction is especially strong. The biblical-first discipline guards against it.

**The three-question structure.** The scientific pass proceeds through three questions. First, what is the primary scientific field for this word? The programme works with six fields — neuroscience, clinical and social psychology, physiology and epigenetics, behavioural science, developmental and cognitive psychology, and genetics and evolutionary biology. Most words have a clear primary field; some span two. Field assignment is a short task that shapes the rest of the pass. Second, what are the landmark findings in that field for this characteristic? Not a literature review — the settled, well-replicated observations that any serious researcher in the field would cite. Third, where does the scientific finding engage the biblical analysis? This is the analytical heart of the pass. It is the only step that cannot be substituted by a reading list or a database lookup, because it requires the judgement of reading two accounts against one another. One substantive paragraph per word, identifying the specific point of convergence or divergence.

**The reference shelf.** The instrument that makes the scientific pass tractable is a curated reference shelf — a set of foundational texts and landmark papers organised by field, chosen for breadth of coverage against the corpus. The shelf is not a reading list to complete before analysis begins; it is a standing reference consulted during the scientific pass of each word. Where the shelf carries a text of high relevance to the word being analysed, that text is the primary scientific reference for the session. Where a word falls outside the shelf's coverage, a targeted search fills the gap, and a recurring gap across multiple words signals that the shelf should be extended. The shelf is therefore a living instrument, extended by the work rather than finalised before it.

**The no-database decision.** The programme does not build a structured scientific database to match its biblical database. The decision was explicit and is worth naming. A parallel scientific database at the depth of the biblical one would constitute a second programme of comparable scale — plausibly years of work — and would defer synthesis indefinitely. The data types are not structurally equivalent: biblical data is lexical and classifiable, while scientific findings are arguments, observations, and interpretations that require judgement to apply. A structured database of the latter would produce the appearance of systematic coverage without the substance. And the programme does not need it — the second-lens role is served by reliable knowledge of landmark findings, which the reference shelf provides at a fraction of the cost.

**Convergence and divergence.** The analytical work of the scientific pass is organised by a framework that distinguishes three kinds of convergence and three kinds of divergence. Convergence can be phenomenological — the two accounts describing the same experience from different angles. It can be mechanistic — science describing the how of what Scripture describes as a reality. It can be structural — the architecture of the inner life having the same shape in both accounts. Divergence can be register divergence — the accounts addressing different levels of the same reality and only appearing to conflict. It can be reductive divergence — a scientific account that loses dimensions the biblical account treats as primary. It can be genuine divergence — claims in actual tension that cannot be harmonised by reframing. The discipline is to name the kind of convergence or divergence in play, rather than collapsing every point of contact into agreement or every point of tension into contradiction. Genuine divergence is flagged for the cross-registry synthesis phase rather than resolved prematurely at the word level.

**What the discipline produces.** The output of the scientific pass for each word is a concise annotation: the primary field, the two or three landmark findings drawn on, and the paragraph that identifies the convergence or divergence with the biblical analysis. The annotation is carried into the word's Session B record and appears in the published word study as the scientific engagement for that word. It is small by weight compared with the biblical analysis, by design. It represents the second lens doing exactly the work the second lens is for — illuminating, grounding, and occasionally challenging the biblical account without displacing it as the primary account.

## 2.5 Publishing

The programme is a research programme, but research that is not put into a readable form does not reach anyone. Publishing is the output-side of the pipeline — the disciplined production of documents that a reader can work with, built from the analytical record but distinct from it. The programme produces three orders of published output, in three registers, for a defined audience.

The first and primary order is the **per-word study**. Each word in the registry receives a written study produced at Session C. The study is the reader-facing form of the word's analytical record: what the term means across its Hebrew and Greek originals, how its occurrences distribute across Scripture, what contexts and characteristics the verses reveal, how the term engages the spirit-soul-body continuum, and how the biblical analysis stands in relation to the relevant scientific findings. The per-word study is designed to stand alone. A reader who encounters a single study — without having read any of the others, without seeing the full registry — gains a considered account of that aspect of the inner being, grounded in the evidence that produced it and transparent about what remains uncertain or provisional.

The second order is the **cross-word synthesis**. Once a cluster of related words has been studied individually, Session D reads across them and produces the second-order findings that single-word studies cannot carry. These syntheses identify the places where two words illuminate a single reality from different angles, where one concept splits into two that had been wrongly conflated, where a characteristic runs across a group of words in a consistent pattern, and where relationships between words — causative, oppositional, overlapping — become visible only at the cluster level. Syntheses are not produced ahead of their evidence; they are produced when the per-word studies for their cluster are complete.

The third order is the **programme-level account**. When clusters have been worked through and their syntheses produced, the programme's full description of the human inner being becomes assemblable — a sustained and defensible account built from the bottom up, word by word, in the order in which the evidence produced it. The present prose corpus is part of this third-order work: the self-description that makes the programme's structure and findings readable as a whole, and that allows the programme's completed output to function as a navigable account rather than as a collection of individual studies.

**The audience.** The programme's intended readers are not primarily academic, although the work should be defensible in an academic setting. The readers are people who care about the subject — teachers, practitioners, thoughtful readers who have encountered Scripture's vocabulary of the inner life and want a careful treatment of it that is accountable to the evidence. The register is theologically serious without being technical for its own sake, empirically grounded without being reductively scientific, and slow enough to treat the subject with respect. The work is written to be navigated — a reader who needs a particular word, or a particular dimension of the inner being, or a particular relationship between characteristics, should be able to find what they need without reading through to the point at which they need it.

**What publishing does not mean.** The word is used here in a specific sense: the production of readable documents that carry the programme's findings to its audience. It does not mean commercial publication, external release, or any particular distribution channel. The programme's interest in publishing is the reader's interest — that the findings are put into a form the reader can actually use. The documents produced at Session C and Session D are the programme's published output regardless of how or whether they are subsequently made available externally. Publication decisions of the latter kind are outside the programme's methodological scope and are handled as a separate matter.

**The relationship between analysis and publication.** The analytical record — the per-word findings held in the database — is the research. The published studies are the form in which the research reaches the reader. The two are distinct. The database can answer questions that the published studies do not explicitly address, because it carries the full set of answers to the standing catalogue of questions that every word is worked through. Where a reader's question is more specific than the published study's treatment, the database supports the deeper look. Publication is therefore not the end of the analytical record — it is a curated view of it, designed for the reader's typical use, with the fuller record available when a more specific question is asked.

## 2.6 Key methodological principles

The method described in the preceding sub-sections rests on a set of principles that shape how every phase of the work is done. They are stated here as principles because they govern the whole programme rather than any one phase, and because they need to be legible as commitments — the things the programme has chosen to do, and to do consistently — rather than as incidental habits. They are also the commitments under which a finding is admissible as a finding.

**Registry completeness against the inner-being definition.** The first commitment is that the scope of the study is defined by a word list built deliberately against the programme's working definition of an inner-being characteristic, and that the list is as complete as the evidence permits before any word is studied in depth. A registry that is built hastily, or built around a researcher's interests rather than the definition, will produce a study whose boundaries are interests rather than evidence. The registry is therefore the programme's first substantive act of discipline, not a preliminary housekeeping task.

**Data collation is separated from analysis — a hard sequence.** The second commitment is that the full data for every registry word is collated before analytical work begins on any of them. Early words are not analysed first and then used to shape the analytical frame for later ones. The separation is a guard against the most likely form of drift in a corpus-level study: allowing the impressions formed from the first few words to become the categories into which the remaining words are read. Every word is read against the same completed body of data, and the analytical frame is discovered from that body as a whole, not constructed from its first members.

**Words are not forced into preconceived categories or assumptions.** The third commitment is that the programme works inductively. Dimensions of the inner being are identified from the verse evidence; groups are formed from what the verses engage; findings emerge from the questions applied to the data. Where a traditional theological category or a familiar psychological classification suggests itself, it is treated as a hypothesis to test against the data rather than as a conclusion to impose on it. A category that cannot be grounded in verse evidence is not a category the programme uses.

**Verses qualify by original-language occurrence, not by interpretation.** The fourth commitment is the rule that governs what counts as evidence. A verse is included in a word's corpus because the relevant Hebrew or Greek term is present in it, irrespective of how the verse has been read in the tradition, what the verse is commonly thought to say, or what contribution it might make to a particular line of argument. This is the same criterion that admits a word to the registry in the first place — lexical presence, not interpretive fit — and it propagates up the work. It is the reason the corpus is the corpus and not a curated selection.

**Every finding is substantiated by data — no guessing, no assumptions, no made-up results.** The fifth commitment is the evidential rule that governs what counts as a finding. A finding in the programme is a statement that can be traced back to a specific verse, a specific term, or a specific pattern in the evidence. A statement that cannot be so traced is a hypothesis, to be labelled as such, or an interpretive move that the programme does not treat as a finding at all. The rule is not a preference for cautious writing; it is the criterion of admissibility.

**Analysis reads the data as a whole; it does not pick and choose angles.** The sixth commitment is that the analytical work for each word runs through a standing catalogue of questions that every word is worked through in the same way. The catalogue is applied in full; the analyst does not select which questions to address based on what seems interesting or productive for a given word. This discipline is the defence against a subtle form of bias — the tendency to interrogate the data in ways that happen to confirm the direction a word's analysis is already going. A word's answer to every question, including the questions that produce thin or unexpected answers, is part of its record.

**The database is the analytical memory and the discipline of re-analysis.** The seventh commitment concerns what happens when analysis is redone. Every answer to every question is persisted in the database, and the database is where the word's findings live. When re-analysis occurs — prompted by a dimension correction, a new verse grouping, a programme-level insight, or simply a deeper look — the prior finding is set aside and the data is read freshly, and the new finding replaces the old in the database. The discipline is what prevents the accretion of interpretive residue: every finding is the current reading of the current data, not a layer on top of previous readings.

**Biblical lens primary, scientific lens secondary.** The eighth commitment is the relationship between the two accounts of the inner being that the programme engages. Scripture is the primary lens; science is the second lens, held alongside it but not in parallel. The biblical pass is completed before the scientific pass begins, at each word, so that the biblical reading is not distorted by a richer or more rhetorically compelling scientific frame while the biblical analysis is still in progress. The decision and its operational consequences are described more fully in the sub-section on science in action; it is stated here as a principle because it governs every word's analysis and not only the scientific pass.

**Synthesis follows analysis — bottom-up, not top-down.** The ninth commitment is about the programme's larger findings. Cross-word synthesis — the claims that become visible only when groups of words are read together — is produced after the per-word analytical work for those words is complete. It is not produced ahead of its evidence. The programme-level account of the inner being is produced when the cluster-level syntheses have been completed, not before. This is the principle that stops the work from becoming an exercise in confirming a prior view of the inner being; the prior view, if any, is held loosely while the evidence is built up, and the larger claims are made when the evidence supports them.

These nine commitments are not independent. Each one makes the next one possible. Building the registry against a definition makes a complete collation meaningful. Completing collation before analysis makes inductive reading possible. Inductive reading makes the catalogue of questions applicable as a uniform instrument. Uniform application makes the database a neutral record of findings. A neutral record of findings supports re-analysis on the same footing as original analysis. And re-analysis on a stable footing makes cross-word synthesis possible without the drift the hard sequence was designed to prevent. Seen together, they describe a programme that has chosen, at each decision point, the discipline over the shortcut.

## 2.7 Key constraints

The principles described in the preceding sub-section shape how the programme works. The constraints described here shape what the programme does not attempt. A research programme is defined as much by what it excludes as by what it covers, and the programme's constraints are not inadvertent limits imposed by circumstance — they are deliberate commitments that make the rest of the method workable. Each one is accepted because accepting it is the condition under which the programme can do what it has set out to do.

**Scope is bounded by the registry.** The first constraint is that the programme studies the words in the registry and does not study other vocabulary. Biblical terms that lie outside the registry — including terms that might be interesting, theologically significant, or adjacent to inner-being concerns — are not within scope. The constraint is what makes the programme completable. A study that remained permanently open to new vocabulary would also remain permanently open in its scope; what the programme produces would never be an account of a defined subject but a running commentary on an expanding one. Registry membership is open to evidence surfacing from within the corpus, as described in the sub-section on word selection, but it is not open to interpretive expansion from outside it. The registry is the boundary; the boundary is the scope.

**The scientific lens is bounded by the second-lens decision.** The second constraint has already been described operationally in the sub-section on science in action and stated as a principle in the preceding sub-section. It appears here as a constraint because it is also a deliberate restriction on the programme's ambition. The programme could, in principle, engage the scientific literature at the depth of its biblical engagement — a parallel corpus-level treatment of what psychology, neuroscience, physiology, behavioural science, and the adjacent fields have established about the inner life. The programme does not do this. The reasons are practical (the scale of the commitment), methodological (the data types are not structurally equivalent), and proper to the programme's identity (the work is biblical research, and the second lens serves the primary one). A different programme, with different aims, could make a different decision. This one has made this one.

**No structured scientific database.** The third constraint is the operational consequence of the second. The programme does not build a structured scientific-literature database alongside its biblical database. Scientific findings are engaged through a curated reference shelf and a disciplined per-word annotation; they are not collated as structured data in the database. The database is the biblical research memory; the scientific engagement is carried in Session B's written record and in the reference shelf itself. This constraint is what keeps the scientific engagement tractable.

**Synthesis does not run ahead of its evidence.** The fourth constraint is the rule that the programme does not make claims at scales larger than the evidence that currently supports them. A per-word study is produced when its analytical work is complete; a cross-word synthesis is produced when the studies for its cluster are complete; a programme-level account is assembled when the cluster syntheses are complete. There is no place in the method at which a larger claim is produced in advance of the smaller claims on which it rests. The constraint is accepted because the alternative — producing large claims first, and then testing them against the evidence as it comes in — is the failure mode the data-first discipline was designed to prevent.

**The programme is corpus-level, not survey-level.** The fifth constraint governs depth of engagement. Each word in the registry receives a full analytical treatment: every verse read, every term classified, every standing question answered. The programme does not treat its subject at survey level — a rapid pass over a wide territory — even though survey-level treatment would produce a readable account in a shorter time. Corpus-level work is slower. It is also what the programme has decided the subject requires. The constraint is that this choice, once made, determines the programme's cadence and the reader's expectation; the completed output will have the depth that corpus-level work produces, not the breadth that survey-level work produces in the same time.

**No per-word short-circuits to publication.** The sixth constraint is that a word's published study is not produced before its analysis is closed. There is no practice of producing an interim study and updating it later as further analytical work is completed. The reasons are in the method: if prior findings are set aside on re-analysis and replaced with current readings, then published findings produced ahead of analytical closure would carry a retraction risk that undermines their reliability at the point of reading. The constraint is accepted because the alternative — a published study that the programme would then need to amend — would harm the reader more than a longer wait does.

**Registry membership is not reopened on interpretive grounds.** The seventh constraint protects the registry from a particular kind of late-stage drift. Once a word has been admitted to the registry under the inner-being definition, it is not re-examined for admission because a particular interpretive frame would exclude it, or because the analytical work on it has produced findings that sit uncomfortably with a current reading. A word's membership is a matter of the original-language evidence that admits it, not of the outcomes of the analysis that follows admission. Where analytical findings raise a definition-level question — what the inner being is, where its edges are — that question is handled at the level of the definition, not by editing the registry after the fact. The constraint keeps the registry from becoming an instrument of analytical tidying.

**What the constraints jointly produce.** The seven constraints, held together, describe a programme that has chosen a narrower ambition in order to have a completable one. It studies a defined vocabulary, at corpus depth, from the biblical text primarily, with scientific literature as the second lens only, with findings produced only where evidence supports them and at scales no larger than the evidence currently permits, published only when the analytical work for a word is closed, and within a registry whose boundary is evidential rather than interpretive. The constraints are not the programme's disappointments; they are its shape.
