## eritheia — Rom 2:8

**Reference:** Rom 2:8
**Term:** eritheia (G2052)
**Classification:** CORE
**Genre:** epistle
**Speaker or subject:** Paul, writing to the church in Rome, describing the condition of those who are self-seeking in the context of his argument about divine judgement.
**Situation:** Paul is developing an argument about God's impartial judgement of all people according to their deeds. The surrounding verses describe God rendering to each person according to their works — eternal life to those who seek glory by patient well-doing, wrath and fury to those described in this verse.
**The inner-being moment:** The verse records: "but for those who are self-seeking (eritheia) and do not obey the truth, but obey unrighteousness, there will be wrath and fury" — eritheia characterises the inner orientation of a category of person; the self-seeking is not a single act but a disposition that determines obedience patterns; the person with eritheia is described as one who obeys unrighteousness, placing eritheia as the inner allegiance that drives a disordered pattern of submission.
**What the person does with the inner state:** The person with eritheia obeys unrighteousness rather than truth; the inner disposition of self-seeking directs the will into a sustained pattern of disobedience.
**Bundling note:** Single verse.

**Term:** eritheia (G2052)
**Classification:** CORE

**Speaker or subject:** Paul, writing from imprisonment to the Philippian church, reflecting on the mixed motives of those who preach the gospel while he is in prison.
**Situation:** Paul has noted that some preach Christ from good will and others from rivalry. **What the person does with the inner state:** Those with eritheia proclaim Christ while intending to cause Paul distress; the inner disposition of self-seeking produces an act of apparent faithfulness that is simultaneously an act of hostility.

## eritheia — Jam 3:14; Jam 3:16

**Reference:** Jam 3:14; Jam 3:16
**Term:** eritheia (G2052)
**Classification:** CORE
**Genre:** epistle
**Speaker or subject:** James, writing a general epistle, in a passage addressing the nature of wisdom, the tongue, and inner motivational states.
**Situation:** James has been addressing the problem of teachers and the power of the tongue. The surrounding passage distinguishes earthly wisdom (characterised by jealousy and eritheia) from wisdom from above (characterised by purity, peacableness, and gentleness). These two verses frame the same inner state from two angles within the same passage.
**The inner-being moment:** Jam 3:14 records: "But if you have bitter jealousy and selfish ambition (eritheia) in your hearts, do not boast and be false to the truth" — eritheia is explicitly located in the heart; it is a condition of the heart that corrupts outward speech and makes boasting a falsification of truth. Jam 3:16 records: "For where jealousy and selfish ambition (eritheia) exist, there will be disorder and every vile practice" — eritheia is named as the generative condition from which disorder and every vile practice flows outward into the community.
**What the person does with the inner state:** The person with eritheia in the heart boasts and is false to the truth (v.14); the presence of eritheia in a community produces disorder and every vile practice as its predictable outcome (v.16).
**Bundling note:** These two verses are bundled because both name eritheia in the same letter, the same passage (James 3's treatment of wisdom and the tongue), by the same author, addressing the same inner state in the same argument — v.14 naming it as a heart condition and v.16 naming its community consequences, together forming a single inner-being observation across two aspects.

## Situation and Genre Summary

All 10 CORE verses in the ambition cluster are from the Pauline and General Epistles; there are no Hebrew, narrative, prophetic, wisdom, or apocalyptic occurrences in the STEP data for this entry. The genre is entirely epistolary. The voice across the corpus is divided between Pauline first-person reflection on his own inner motivation (Phili 1:17; Rom 15:20; 2Cor 5:9) and second- or third-person address to communities about their inner states (Phil 2:3; Gal 5:20; 2Cor 12:20; 1Th 4:11; James 3:14-16; Rom 2:8).

The range of situations in the eritheia verses is concentrated around community dynamics and mixed motives — the pathology of Christian communities when self-seeking drives behaviour within the body (Gal 5:20; 2Cor 12:20; Jam 3:14-16), the diagnosis of motivation behind outwardly good acts (Phili 1:17), and the theological-eschatological characterisation of the self-seeking person's orientation before God (Rom 2:8). Phili 2:3 stands apart as the verse that most directly names eritheia as an inner motivational orientation to be refused in favour of its opposite.

The range of situations in the filotimeomai verses is concentrated around apostolic self-definition: Paul naming the aspiration-drive that governs his strategic choices (Rom 15:20), his existential orientation toward God in all circumstances (2Cor 5:9), and his instruction to the Thessalonians about the redirected form of this drive in community life (1Th 4:11).

The voice distribution shows that eritheia is named both from the inside (Jam 3:14 — "if you have it in your hearts") and from the outside (Phili 1:17 — diagnosed in others). filotimeomai is named exclusively from the inside — it is the self-description of the apostolic inner life, not an observation about others.
