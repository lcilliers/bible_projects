# WA-005 — anguish — Phase 1 Analysis

**Word:** anguish  
**Registry ID:** 005  
**Source List:** Missing Inner Being Words  
**Category:** Affective/Emotional  
**Date:** 2026-03-06  
**File Version:** 2  

---

Anguish qualifies as an inner-being word with MEDIUM-HIGH confidence. The term set is the most diverse in the Phase 1 series so far — 28 distinct terms or sub-entries spanning Hebrew and Greek — but it is also the most heterogeneous, with a significant portion of the verse set covering external-circumstantial distress rather than named inner states. The qualification rests on a core of terms that carry explicit inner-being location statements: Paul's unceasing anguish in the heart (odunē, Rom 9:2), Paul's anguish of heart in pastoral tears (sunochē, 2Cor 2:4), the heart in anguish within me (chul, Psa 55:4), bitterness of soul (mar, Job 3:20; 7:11; Isa 38:15), sorrow in the heart all the day (ya.gon, Psa 13:2), and — most structurally important — anguish as shortness/crushing of the spirit (qo.tser ru.ach, Exo 6:9). The word was not in the original inner-being list; its inclusion from the Missing Inner Being Words list is confirmed by the data.

The defining feature of this entry's root vocabulary is kinetic: the chul root (to twist, writhe, dance, tremble, give birth) names anguish as involuntary bodily movement. Anguish in Hebrew is not simply a felt state but a state that moves through the body — the same root names the dance, the birth, and the terror-trembling. The reduplicated intensification chal.cha.lah (anguish as doubled writhing) and the derived noun chil (agony/writhing) show that the intensification of inner pain and its somatic expression are a single continuum in the Hebrew conceptual field.

## Term-by-Term Analysis

### odunē — anguish — G3601 — Greek

**What this term means**

odunē is a noun (2 occurrences) meaning anguish, grief, pain of body or mind. The LSJ range spans physical pain (bodily pain, the pain of wounds) and mental pain (grief, distress), with the singular and plural both attested in classical Greek. The related verb odunaō (G3600, 4 occurrences) carries the same range. The root is ancient — Homer uses it for the pain of wounds and for grief — and by NT times the mental/emotional sense has become primary in the NT usages, both of which locate the odunē explicitly in the heart or in the soul-level consequence of misdirected desire.

**Core verse observations**

Rom 9:2 is the structural inner-being statement of the entry: Paul has great sorrow and unceasing anguish in his heart. The location in the heart (kardia) is explicit. The anguish is unceasing — it is not an episodic pain-event but a persistent inner state. The cause is Paul's grief over Israel's unbelief, making this anguish a compassion-driven inner state — pain in response to another's spiritual condition. This is the same complex inner-being structure as Jesus's anger-with-grief at the hardness of heart in Mar 3:5.

1Tim 6:10 uses the plural of odunē: those who crave money have wandered from the faith and pierced themselves with many pangs. The image is internal wounding — the inner person is self-pierced by the anguish that results from misdirected desire. The verb is periepeirō (to pierce all around), suggesting the anguish encircles and penetrates from multiple directions. This is the entry's most explicit statement connecting a motivated inner orientation (love of money) to an inner-being pain consequence.

For odunaō: Luk 2:48 documents Mary's inner anguish at losing Jesus — your father and I have been searching for you in great distress. The passive participle places Mary as the one acted upon by the anguish — the inner state is not self-generated but arrives as a consequence of circumstances. Luk 16:24-25 uses odunaō in the parable of the rich man in torment — an extended register that is eschatological/parabolic rather than directly inner-being in the present-experience sense, but the use of the same word connects the present inner experience of anguish to its ultimate eschatological intensification.

### sunochē — anguish — G4928 — Greek

**What this term means**

sunochē is a noun (2 occurrences) meaning anguish, distress — primarily a being held together, compression; in NT metaphorically distress of mind, anxiety. The LSJ range is remarkable for its structural precision: holding together, the narrows or straits of a road or sea passage (where the way contracts and the walls close in), conflict in battle, continuity, combination of elements, line of union, and metaphorically distress and affliction. The root verb sunechō means to hold together, to hold fast, to oppress.

**What the spatial metaphor reveals**

The spatial-constriction sense of sunochē (the place where the road contracts to a narrow passage, where the sea squeezes through a strait) is not merely decorative. The NT metaphorical application of this compression-image to inner distress reveals the same narrow-passage conceptual structure as the Hebrew tsar/tsa.rah root (straits, distress — from tsar meaning narrow). Both Hebrew and Greek anguish vocabularies reach for the same spatial metaphor: the inner-being experience of anguish is the experience of being in a narrow place, of being compressed and held, of having no room to move. This convergence across languages is one of the structural observations of the entry.

**Core verse observations**

2Cor 2:4 is the entry's most important inner-being verse: for I wrote to you out of much affliction (thlipsis) and anguish (sunochē) of heart and with many tears, not to cause you pain but to let you know the abundant love that I have for you. Three terms appear together: thlipsis (pressure/affliction), sunochē (anguish — the compression/constraint), and dakruōn (tears — the somatic expression). The anguish of heart is the inner state; the tears are its outward somatic expression; the love is the motive that makes the anguish creative rather than destructive. This verse is the NT's fullest picture of apostolic inner anguish as a productive pastoral inner-being state.

Luk 21:25 places sunochē in a national/eschatological register: distress of nations in perplexity because of the roaring of the sea and the waves. The spatial-constriction sense of sunochē here maps the distress of nations onto the image of the sea in the straits — the nations are in the narrow place.

### agōnia — a struggle — G0074 — Greek

**What this term means**

agōnia is a noun (1 occurrence) meaning contest, violent struggle; agony, anguish, anxiety. The LSJ range begins in athletic and military contest (struggle for victory, gymnastic exercise) and moves through the agonistic event to the inner state it produces: agony, anguish — the state of the mind in extreme fear and great distress. The verb agōnizomai (to struggle, to agonise) and the compound sunagōnizomai (to struggle alongside) name the action; agōnia names the inner state produced by being in the contest.

**Core verse observation**

Luk 22:44 is the only NT occurrence: being in agony (en agōnia) he prayed more earnestly, and his sweat became like great drops of blood falling to the ground. The grammatical form is dative (in agony, within the agōnia state). Three things are notable. First, the agōnia produces prayer — the inner state drives the person toward God rather than away. Second, the agōnia produces somatic expression — the bloody sweat is the body's registration of an extreme inner state. Third, the agōnia intensifies prayer rather than disrupting it: he prayed more earnestly. This is the NT's definitive inner-being picture of anguish as the context of intensified God-ward orientation — the soul in its most extreme distress pressing hardest toward God. The Gethsemane agōnia connects to the Servant's soul-anguish in Isa 53:11 (a.mal of the soul) — the vicarious inner suffering of both the Servant and the Son is named using the anguish vocabulary of the 181.

### thlipsis — pressure — G2347 — Greek

**What this term means**

thlipsis is a noun (45 occurrences) meaning trouble, distress, oppression, tribulation — primarily physical pressure or compression; metaphorically affliction and distress of mind. The LSJ grounds the term in physical pressure: pressing, compression, of the pulse, of medical procedures. The metaphorical extension to oppression and affliction follows the same constriction logic as sunochē: inner anguish is being pressed and squeezed. The root verb thlibō (to press on) and its compounds (apothlibō, sunthlibō) show that the pressing action is the primary image.

**What the grammatical context reveals**

thlipsis has the largest occurrence count of any Greek term in the entry (45), but most occurrences are in the external tribulation/persecution register rather than the inner-being anguish register. The explicitly inner-being uses are relatively few: 2Cor 2:4 (affliction of heart), 2Cor 1:8 (utterly burdened beyond strength to the point of despairing of life), and Joh 16:21 (woman no longer remembers the anguish for the joy of a human being being born). The concentration of the explicitly inner-being thlipsis in Paul's apostolic self-description is significant: apostolic suffering as thlipsis is inner-being suffering, not merely external circumstance.

**Core verse observations**

2Cor 2:4 (see under sunochē above) pairs thlipsis and sunochē as the two dimensions of Paul's inner anguish — the pressing (thlipsis) and the constraining (sunochē) together name the full inner-being compression.

2Cor 1:8 documents extreme inner-being thlipsis: we were so utterly burdened beyond our strength that we despaired of life itself. The comparative grammatical construction (beyond our strength) indicates the pressure exceeded the inner person's load-bearing capacity — a structural failure of inner-being resilience. The word for despaired (exaporeomai) is a compound meaning to be utterly at a loss, to have no way through. thlipsis at its limit produces the inner state of aporia — total loss of way.

Joh 16:21 is theologically significant: a woman giving birth has sorrow (lupē) because her hour has come, but when she has delivered the baby she no longer remembers the anguish (thlipsis), for joy that a human being has been born. The use of thlipsis for the birth-anguish parallels the Hebrew chul vocabulary (writhing in travail) and connects the inner-being anguish entry to the transformation-to-joy theme: anguish that is productive — that gives birth to something — is superseded by the joy of what it produces. Jesus applies this image to his disciples' coming grief at his death and joy at his resurrection.

### mar — bitter — H4751 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

mar is an adjective (39 occurrences) covering bitter/bitterness from the physical-taste sense (bitter water, Marah) through to the inner-being bitterness-of-soul in lament. The idiom mar nefesh (bitter of soul) is the dominant inner-being construction — used of Hannah (1Sa 1:10), Job (3:20; 7:11; 10:1; 21:25), Hezekiah (Isa 38:15), and others in acute inner suffering. The related words reveal a rich cluster: ma.rar (to make bitter, to provoke), me.ri.rut (bitterness), mo.rah (bitterness), me.re.rah/me.ro.rah (gall, bile), mor (myrrh — bitterness as aromatic, bitterness refined into something precious), and tam.rur (bitterness, a rare intensified form).

**What the taste-to-soul connection reveals**

The movement from bitter water to bitter soul in the mar vocabulary maps directly from the physical sense organ (taste) to the inner-being. This is the Hebrew anguish vocabulary's gustatory dimension: what the body tastes as bitter, the soul can also taste as bitter. The naming of anguish in taste-terms grounds it somatically in a different organ than chul's kinetic-muscular grounding — both are somatic registrations of the same inner state approached through different body-anchors.

**Core verse observations**

Job 7:11 is the structurally most important inner-being verse: I will speak in the anguish of my spirit (be.tsar ru.chi) and will complain in the bitterness of my soul (be.mar nafshi). Both spirit (ruach) and soul (nefesh) are named as the locations of anguish and bitterness respectively. The parallel structure equates anguish-of-spirit with bitterness-of-soul — the two inner-being dimensions (spirit and soul) experiencing the same state named by two different vocabularies (anguish-of-narrows and bitterness).

Isa 38:15-17 documents Hezekiah's inner-being transformation through anguish: I walk slowly all my years because of the bitterness of my soul (v.15); behold, it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness (v.17). The retrospective interpretation of the bitterness is theological: the inner-being suffering was for my welfare. The verse names anguish as a God-deployed instrument of inner-being formation — the suffering that produced good was not accidental but purposeful from the divine side.

Eze 3:14 is the prophetic inner-being statement: the Spirit lifted me up and took me away, and I went in bitterness in the heat of my spirit (be.cha.mat ru.ach). The compound be.cha.mat ru.ach (in the heat of the spirit) places bitterness in the spirit, using the che.mah thermal vocabulary from the anger entry. The anguish and anger entries converge at this verse: Ezekiel's inner state is simultaneously the heat of anger (cha.mah) and the bitterness of anguish (mar) — a compound inner-being state in which the two affective dimensions are inseparable.

### tsa.rah / tsar (distress) — H6869B / H6862B — Hebrew

**What this term means**

tsa.rah is a feminine noun (70 occurrences) and tsar is an adjectival form (31 occurrences) from the same narrow-straits root. The meaning — straits, distress, trouble — derives from the physical experience of being in a narrow place, a constricted passage, a tight constraint. The root verb tsa.rar means to constrain, distress, vex, rival. The related noun tse.ror (bundle, pebble) shows the binding/compressing dimension of the root: anguish is what it feels like to be bound or pressed into a narrow place.

**What the spatial metaphor reveals**

The tsar/tsa.rah root cluster converges structurally with the Greek sunochē/thlipsis compression vocabulary: all name anguish as a spatial-constriction experience. The root even produces a separate noun for the human agent of constriction: tsa.rah as vexer (one who narrows another's space). This relational sense — anguish produced by a person who makes your world narrow — connects the anguish entry to the social-relational dimension of inner-being formation.

**Core verse observations**

Gen 42:21 is the entry's richest narrative inner-being verse: we are guilty concerning our brother, in that we saw the distress of his soul (be.tsar nafshow) when he begged us and we did not listen. The brothers' retrospective naming of Joseph's inner anguish is a significant inner-being diagnostic moment — they identify, years later, the inner state Joseph was in (soul-distress) and directly connect it to their moral failure to respond to it. The tsa.rah of the soul was legible in Joseph's behaviour at the time; their failure to act on that legibility is their guilt.

Jon 2:2 locates tsa.rah at the extreme: I called out to the Lord out of my distress (tsa.rah), and he answered me; out of the belly of Sheol I cried. The tsa.rah here is the inner experience of being in Sheol-conditions — surrounded, enclosed, at the limit of existence. The prayer-response to tsa.rah — calling out to God from within the narrowness — is the structural pattern across the tsa.rah verse set (Gen 35:3; Psa 18:6; 20:1; 77:2; 86:7; 120:1; Jon 2:2): tsa.rah produces the cry, God answers out of the tsa.rah.

Exo 6:9 (qo.tser ru.ach — shortness of spirit, from a different root) parallels tsa.rah structurally: Israel did not listen to Moses because of shortness of spirit (qo.tser ru.ach) and harsh slavery. qo.tser means shortness, impatience, and anguish — derived from qa.tsar (to be short, to reap). Anguish as shortness-of-spirit is the most explicit spirit-location statement in the entire anguish entry: the ruach itself is contracted, compressed, shortened by the weight of suffering. The verse identifies anguish as a spirit-level state — not merely an emotional or psychological state — that manifests as shortened, contracted inner capacity.

### chul — to twist — H2342 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

chul is a verb (56 occurrences across five STEP sub-entries: tremble, give birth, writhe in pain, dance, anticipate/wait anxiously) covering the complete range of twisting-and-writhing motion. The stem range is extraordinary: Qal (dance, twist, writhe, whirl); Polel (writhe in travail with/bear/bring forth, wait anxiously); Pulal (be made to writhe, be brought forth); Hophal (be born); Hithpolel (whirling, writhing-suffering-torture, wait longingly); Hithpalpel (be distressed). A single root covers dance and birthpang and terror-trembling and patient-waiting — all experiences of the body twisting around an intense inner state.

**What the root reveals about inner-being experience**

The chul root is the most structurally significant root in the anguish entry for any analysis of the body-soul-spirit relationship. It reveals that in Hebrew, the distinction between inner anguish and its somatic expression does not exist at the level of vocabulary: the same word names the inner state and the movement that the inner state produces. The body writhing (chul) is the anguish being expressed — it is not a sign of the anguish but the anguish in motion. The somatic expression of inner states is not secondary to them but constitutive of them — the body in anguish and the inner state of anguish are a single event described from two angles.

The most remarkable structural feature of the chul root is its Polel sense: to writhe in travail with, to bear, to bring forth. Anguish is not merely an endpoint but a generative process — something is brought forth through the writhing. The Hithpalpel (to be distressed) represents the reflexive or sustained form of the same motion: the body is distressed through its own involuntary twisting.

**Core verse observations**

Psa 55:4 is the entry's most explicit inner-being chul verse: my heart is in anguish (yachil, Qal imperfect of chul) within me. The location is the heart, and the anguish is named using the writhe/twist root. The verse continues: the terrors of death have fallen upon me — the anguish is precipitated by existential terror.

Jer 5:3 is a diagnostic verse for the transformation research: O Lord, do not your eyes look for truth? You have struck them but they felt no anguish (cha.lu, Qal of chul). The absence of anguish in response to divine discipline is itself a diagnostic indicator of inner-being hardening — a person who cannot writhe in response to discipline has a formation pathology. This parallels Heb 12's treatment of the person who does not receive the Father's discipline.

Isa 26:17-18 provides the non-productive anguish image: like a pregnant woman who writhes and cries out in her pangs when she is near giving birth, so were we before you, O Lord — we were pregnant, we writhed, but we have given birth to wind. The writhing was real, the anguish was real, but nothing was brought forth. This is the anguish entry's image of unproductive inner suffering — the motion without the birth.

### chil / chal.cha.lah — agony / anguish — H2427A / H2479 — Hebrew

chil (agony, 7 occurrences) is the derived noun from the chul writhing root. chal.cha.lah (anguish, 4 occurrences) is the reduplicated intensified form — the doubling of the root signifying an extreme or sustained writhing state. Both are predominantly in the corporate/national prophetic register (the writhing of Philistia, the anguish of Cush, the writhing of Zion) — but the root-connection to chul keeps the somatic image alive even in the national register.

### sha.vats — agony — H7661 — Hebrew

sha.vats is a hapax legomenon (1 occurrence) meaning cramp, agony, anguish — meaning uncertain. The sole verse is 2Sa 1:9: anguish has seized me and yet my life still lingers. This is the dying soldier in Saul's court begging David to kill him — anguish that prolongs dying, the inner state of the one caught between death and not-yet-death. The root connection to sha.vats (to weave) and tash.bets (checkered/filigree) suggests a cramping or tight-woven constriction — anguish as a weaving-tight of the inner person. The filigree image (mish.be.tsot) — intricate metalwork — applied to anguish gives a strikingly precise picture: the inner person caught in a tight, ornate, inescapable constriction.

### qe.pha.dah — anguish — H7089 — Hebrew

qe.pha.dah is a hapax legomenon (1 occurrence) meaning shuddering, anguish. The root qa.phad means to roll or curl; the related word qip.pod is the porcupine (the animal that curls into a defensive ball). The image is visceral: anguish as the inner person curling inward like a threatened porcupine, the shuddering-and-curling-in of the self when threatened. This is one of the most precise somatic images in the entry.

### qo.tser ru.ach — shortness of spirit — H7115 + ruach — Hebrew

qo.tser (1 occurrence) from qa.tsar (to be short, to reap) names anguish as shortness — specifically shortness of spirit (qo.tser ru.ach) in Exo 6:9. The verse is structurally critical: the Israelites under slavery could not receive Moses's message because of shortness of spirit and harsh slavery. The inner state (qo.tser ru.ach) and the external condition (harsh slavery) are named together as co-causes of their inability to receive. The verse identifies anguish as a spirit-level state — not merely an emotional or psychological state — that contracts the spirit, reducing its capacity to receive, respond, and hope. The anguish of Israel under Pharaoh was not merely psychological distress but a spirit-level contraction that impaired spiritual receptivity. The verse raises the question whether sustained suffering under oppression produces inner-being changes — a shortening of the spirit — that alter the person's capacity for transformation, and whether this spirit-level effect is distinct from the emotional and psychological dimensions of distress.

### ya.gon — sorrow — H3015 — Hebrew

ya.gon (14 occurrences) names grief, sorrow, and anguish as a sustained inner-being state. The root ya.gah (to suffer) and the related noun tu.gah (grief) ground it in the experience of ongoing inner pain. The verse cluster covers: the sorrow that threatens to follow Jacob to Sheol (Gen 42:38), the sorrow in the heart all the day in the face of the enemy's exaltation (Psa 13:2), the life spent with sorrow (Psa 31:10), the heart sick within the prophet (Jer 8:18), and Baruch's complaint that God has added sorrow to pain (Jer 45:3).

Jer 45:3 is distinctive: the Lord has added sorrow (ya.gon) to my pain (ke.ev). The compounding of two inner-being pain-states — sorrow added to pain — documents the accumulation of inner suffering as a distinct inner-being experience. ya.gon is not merely a synonym for anguish but names the sorrow-dimension specifically — the grief-quality of prolonged inner distress.

Isa 35:10 and 51:11 provide the eschatological reversal: sorrow and sighing shall flee away. The future flight of ya.gon confirms it as a present persistent inner-being resident — something that will have to flee rather than simply stop, because it has made itself at home.

### ne.ha.mah — groaning — H5100 — Hebrew

ne.ha.mah (2 occurrences) names growling/groaning — a somatic-vocal expression of inner anguish. The single inner-being verse is Psa 38:8: I am feeble and crushed; I groan because of the tumult of my heart. The groaning (ne.ha.mah) is caused by the tumult (hamyon) of the heart — the inner chaos produces the somatic vocal expression. This maps the same inner-to-somatic pathway as chul (writhing) and mar (bitterness expressed in bitter crying): the inner state is not silent but produces sound.

### tsir — pang — H6735C — Hebrew

tsir (5 occurrences) means pang, distress. Isa 21:3 is the entry's most striking tsir verse: my loins are filled with anguish; pangs have seized me like the pangs of a woman in labor; I am bowed down so that I cannot hear; I am dismayed so that I cannot see. Four somatic registrations of inner anguish are named in sequence: loins filled, pangs seizing, bowing down, and senses impaired. The anguish has disrupted the body's capacity to function — hearing and sight are lost under the weight of it. This is the fullest picture in the entry of total somatic-anguish in a prophetic inner-being context.

Dan 10:16 adds a parallel prophetic experience: by reason of the vision pains have come upon me and I retain no strength. Visionary inner-being experience produces tsir — the anguish of receiving divine vision.

### a.mal — trouble — H5999 — Hebrew

a.mal (55 occurrences) is predominantly the Ecclesiastes toil vocabulary — the labour and trouble of life under the sun. Its anguish sub-sense appears primarily in Isa 53:11: out of the anguish of his soul (me.a.mal nafshow) he shall see and be satisfied. The Servant's soul-anguish is named using a word that primarily means labour and toil — the vicarious suffering of the Servant is framed as the most intense form of the toil that all human existence involves. The cross-registry link between Isa 53:11 (a.mal of the Servant's soul) and Luk 22:44 (agōnia of Jesus in Gethsemane) names the inner-being anguish of atonement using both the Hebrew and Greek anguish vocabularies.

### basanizō — to torture: anguish — G0928H — Greek

basanizō (12 occurrences) has its primary sense in testing and torture — the basanos was the touchstone used to test gold, and the verb moved from testing to torture to applying physical pain. The NT anguish sub-sense (G0928H) is the passive: to be in pain, to be tormented. The key observation is that basanizō grounds anguish in a testing framework: anguish as the experience of being tested, examined at intensity. The birth-pang use in Rev 12:2 (crying out in birth pains and the agony of giving birth) connects to the chul/travail vocabulary. Most of the basanizō verse set is physical disease, waves beating on a boat, and eschatological torment — peripheral for inner-being analysis.

## Root Clusters Across the Term Set

The Hebrew anguish vocabulary organises around four structural images. The first is the narrow-place cluster: tsar/tsa.rah (straits/distress), me.tsar (straits), ma.tsoq/mu.tsaq (constraint/pressing), tsu.qah (anguish as pressed). All name anguish as the experience of being in a constricted space where movement is impossible. The second is the twisting-writhing cluster: chul (twist/writhe/dance/give birth/tremble), chil (agony/writhing noun), chal.cha.lah (reduplicated anguish), sha.vats (cramping). All name anguish as involuntary kinetic movement through the body. The third is the bitter-taste cluster: mar/ma.rar (bitter/make bitter), me.re.rah (gall), me.ri.rut (bitterness). The fourth is the sorrow-grief cluster: ya.gon (grief/sorrow/anguish), tu.gah (grief), ya.gah (to suffer).

The Greek anguish vocabulary organises differently: the compression cluster (sunochē, thlipsis — both naming anguish as being held and pressed) parallels the Hebrew narrow-place cluster; the contest-anguish vocabulary (agōnia, agōnizomai) names anguish as the inner state of one in mortal struggle; odunē names it as pain (somatic and psychological); and ponos names it as the suffering produced by hard labour.

## Open Questions

1. **WORD-LEVEL: qo.tser ru.ach and spirit-level anguish.** Exo 6:9 is the most direct statement in the OT that anguish can contract or shorten the spirit. Does the biblical data across the anguish term set consistently treat anguish as a spirit-level state — not merely an emotional or psychological state — that reduces the spirit's capacity for receptivity and response? Does the biblical evidence indicate whether the spirit-contracting effect of severe sustained anguish operates as a distinct mechanism from its psychological and emotional dimensions?

2. **WORD-LEVEL: the anguish-of-Gethsemane / anguish-of-the-Servant link.** Two of the most theologically central anguish verses are agōnia (Luk 22:44) and a.mal nefesh (Isa 53:11). Both name the inner-being suffering of Christ using the anguish vocabulary. Does the biblical data — in these verses and across the broader term set — address the relationship between inner-being anguish and the alignment of the will with God? The prayer not my will but yours immediately follows the agōnia in Luk 22:44; does the biblical evidence consistently connect anguish at its extreme with this kind of will-submission?

3. **WORD-LEVEL: productive vs unproductive anguish.** The chul root generates two contrasting images: the woman who writhes and gives birth to a child (productive anguish), and the woman who writhes and gives birth to wind (Isa 26:18 — non-productive anguish). Does the biblical data consistently distinguish between anguish that is formative (produces transformation or brings something forth) and anguish that is merely suffering (produces nothing)? The Hezekiah verse (Isa 38:17: for my welfare I had great bitterness) suggests the distinction is real and theologically significant — is it consistently attested elsewhere?

4. **WORD-LEVEL: anguish and spiritual receptivity.** Exo 6:9 (qo.tser ru.ach) shows anguish blocking spiritual receptivity — Israel could not receive Moses's words. Jer 5:3 (they felt no anguish in response to discipline) shows the absence of anguish as itself a spiritual pathology. Do these two data points together define a framework for the relationship between anguish and spiritual formation — too much anguish contracts the spirit and blocks reception; too little anguish (hardened unresponsiveness) also blocks formation; the right anguish (in the right measure) is a formative instrument?

5. **WORD-LEVEL: the narrow-place convergence across languages.** The Hebrew tsar/tsa.rah root (straits/narrow place), the Greek sunochē (compression/the narrows), and the Greek thlipsis (pressing/pressure) all independently reach for spatial-constriction as the primary image for anguish. Does this convergence across languages and cultures constitute evidence for a universal somatic-experiential basis for the anguish concept — that the inner experience of anguish is cross-culturally experienced as constriction, as having no room?

6. **WORD-LEVEL: the somatic range.** The anguish entry has the widest somatic register of any entry in the Phase 1 series: writhing (chul), loins filled with anguish (tsir), groaning from the tumult of the heart (ne.ha.mah), bitterness expressed in bitter crying (mar), sweat like blood (agōnia), being pierced all around (odunē in 1Tim 6:10), being pressed and crushed (thlipsis). Does the biblical data across this term set treat anguish as more deeply body-integrated than other inner-being states — or does the somatic vocabulary simply reflect the intensity of the state rather than any specific body-level location of the anguish itself?

7. **CROSS-REGISTRY: boundary with agony.** Phase 1 for agony is already complete. The overlap in terms is significant (agōnia, chil, chal.cha.lah appear in both). For Phase 2, does the biblical data support a principled boundary between agony and anguish — or do the terms in their attested uses treat these as intensity variations of the same inner-being state (anguish at lower sustained intensity, agony at higher acute intensity)?
