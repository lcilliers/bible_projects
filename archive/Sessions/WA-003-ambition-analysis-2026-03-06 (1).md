# WA-003 — ambition — Phase 1 Analysis

**Word:** ambition  
**Registry ID:** 003  
**Source List:** Low Confidence / Inferred  
**Category:** not stated  
**Date:** 2026-03-06  
**File Version:** 2 (rebuilt from corrected source)  

This word is provisionally assessed as BORDERLINE under the inner being definition, with MEDIUM confidence. The STEP data for ambition returns two Greek terms only — eritheia (G2052, 7 occurrences) and filotimeomai (G5389, 3 occurrences) — and no Hebrew terms. The source file inference note correctly states that Scripture does not use a morally neutral equivalent of the modern English word 'ambition'; it handles the concept through two opposite poles: selfish self-seeking (eritheia) and honour-aspiring earnestness (filotimeomai). Both poles name genuine inner-being orientations — eritheia as a heart-disposition driving external conduct (Jam 3:14, Phil 1:17), filotimeomai as an inner drive of honour-love directed toward a chosen goal. The qualification is BORDERLINE because neither term covers the full semantic range of the modern word 'ambition', and the absence of Hebrew data leaves the Old Testament dimension entirely uncharted from this entry. The combined list terms qana, gav, and zelos, which the analyst associated with ambition conceptually, belong to their own separate word entries and are to be assessed in Phase 2 cross-word synthesis, not here.

## Term-by-Term Analysis

### eritheia — rivalry — G2052 — Greek

**What this term means**

eritheia is a feminine noun (7 occurrences) meaning selfish ambition, faction, or strife. The meaning section distinguishes three registers: the service of a party or party spirit; feud and faction; contentious disposition and selfish ambition; and by implication untowardness and disobedience. The LSJ etymology traces the term from erithos (a hired worker or day-labourer working for wages) through the classical sense of canvassing for public office and political intriguing, arriving at the NT sense of selfish or factious ambition. The single related word is erethizō (to provoke or irritate), which shares the root and reveals that the semantic field of eritheia is close to provocation — the antagonistic stirring-up of opposition. Notably, the LSJ entry cites the NT pairing zelos kai eritheia (jealousy and selfish ambition), confirming that eritheia regularly appears alongside zelos as its companion inner-being state.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Genitive Singular Feminine. The genitive case in the anchor occurrence places eritheia as a noun of source or characterisation — acting out of eritheia, or marked by eritheia. This is consistent with its verse usage: eritheia is consistently a motive or disposition from which conduct flows, not a named state that the person passively experiences. It is a generative inner orientation.

**What the English translation conceals**

English translations of eritheia are noticeably inconsistent — 'selfish ambition', 'rivalries', 'self-seeking', 'contentious disposition', and 'party spirit' all appear across the seven verses. This scatter reveals how compressed the Greek term is: eritheia names a single inner-being disposition that simultaneously generates self-promotion, factional behaviour, hostility to others, and disobedience to truth. No single English word holds all of this. The day-labourer root (working for pay rather than principle) is entirely absent from English translations and provides a useful inner-being insight: eritheia names the orientation of one who works for personal gain rather than from conviction — a motive-structure, not merely a personality trait.

**Core verse observations**

Phil 2:3 is the structural inner-being verse for this term: do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves. eritheia is placed in direct opposition to humility, and the contrast is expressed as a choice about how the self is oriented toward others — upward (self-exalting) or downward (other-regarding). This makes eritheia a disposition that shapes the entire relational orientation of the inner person.

Phil 1:17 shows eritheia as the motive behind an external act: some proclaim Christ out of selfish ambition, not sincerely. The same outward act (gospel proclamation) can originate from eritheia or from genuine inner orientation — the term is functioning as a diagnostic of motive, not behaviour.

Jam 3:14 explicitly locates eritheia in the heart: if you have bitter jealousy and selfish ambition in your hearts. This is the clearest inner-being location statement in the corpus — eritheia as a condition of the heart, paired with bitter jealousy, that falsifies speech and contradicts truth. The verse also shows eritheia as active in the relational space: it is something you have in your heart that corrupts what comes out of you toward others.

Jam 3:16 reveals eritheia as generative: where jealousy and selfish ambition exist, there will be disorder and every vile practice. The inner disposition produces a predictable external environment — eritheia is not merely a private inner state but a productive force that reshapes the community around it.

Rom 2:8 characterises those with eritheia as people who do not obey the truth but obey unrighteousness. This frames eritheia as an inner allegiance — the self-seeking person is not merely self-interested but is in a state of active obedience to unrighteousness. eritheia here describes the orientation of the will, not just the motivation of a particular act.

Gal 5:20 lists eritheia among the works of the flesh alongside idolatry, sorcery, enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, dissensions, and divisions. Its placement in this catalogue positions eritheia as a flesh-driven inner-being orientation — consistently negative in the NT, with no positive or neutral use attested.

2Cor 12:20 embeds eritheia in a community-pathology catalogue: quarrelling, jealousy, anger, hostility, slander, gossip, conceit, disorder. Its position among inner states (anger, hostility, conceit) and their social expressions (quarrelling, slander, gossip) confirms that eritheia sits at the boundary between inner disposition and communal damage — the inner condition that generates the external disorder.

### filotimeomai — to aspire — G5389 — Greek

**What this term means**

filotimeomai is a verb (3 occurrences) meaning to have an ambition, aspire to a goal; primarily to be ambitious of honour; by extension, to exert oneself to the utmost, endeavour earnestly. The term is a compound of philo (love, disposition toward) and timē (honour, worth, value) — literally to love honour, to be driven by the aspiration toward what is valued or esteemed. The LSJ shows the classical range moving from seeking personal glory and fame, through competitive emulation of others (vying emulously, rivalling), to earnest striving for a goal. The related words cluster is the full philo- family — a large semantic family of terms naming specific motivational orientations as love-dispositions: love of money (filarguria), love of pleasure (filēdonos), love of God (filotheos), love of dispute (filoneikia), brotherly love (filadelfia), hospitality (filoxenia), friendship (filia), selfishness (filautos). This cluster reveals that filotimeomai belongs to a coherent inner-being category: the structure of desire-as-orientation, where the moral character of the drive is determined by the object it is aimed at.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Verb Present Middle or Passive Deponent Participle Accusative Singular Masculine. filotimeomai is a middle-deponent verb — it takes middle or passive form but carries active meaning. The middle voice signals that the action is one in which the subject has a personal stake; for filotimeomai this is entirely appropriate, since the aspiration named is not a neutral act but one in which the subject's own honour-orientation is the driving force. The present participle indicates an ongoing, characterising action: one who is habitually aspiring, not making a single aspiration-act.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'ambition', 'aim', and 'aspire' distribute what filotimeomai covers in a single term. More significantly, none surfaces the honour-love root. English 'ambition' is now largely value-neutral; the Greek reveals that the underlying inner-being structure is philo + timē — a love of honour as a motivating orientation. The NT occurrences do not suppress this structure but redirect it: Paul makes it his love-of-honour to preach where Christ is not yet named (Rom 15:20), to please God (2Cor 5:9), and to live quietly (1Th 4:11). The honour-drive itself is preserved but its object is transformed. English cannot show this inner continuity between the motive-structure (love of honour) and its redirected expression.

**Core verse observations**

2Cor 5:9 is the foundational inner-being verse: whether at home or away, we make it our aim to please him. The filotimeomai orientation is directed entirely toward God as its object — the drive that in classical usage sought public fame is here aimed at pleasing God, whether anyone sees it or not. The phrase 'whether at home or away' strips away all audience, leaving the aspiration as a purely inner-directional orientation with no external validation available. This is filotimeomai at its most inner-being: the entire motivational structure aimed at an invisible referent.

Rom 15:20 shows the same drive shaping apostolic strategy: I make it my ambition to preach the gospel not where Christ has already been named. The love-of-honour orientation is directed toward gospel mission in virgin territory — the competitive drive to be first is retained but redirected from personal fame to strategic obedience.

1Th 4:11 contains a striking inversion: aspire to live quietly. The honour-love drive is directed toward quietness, unpretentiousness, and self-sufficiency — the opposite of the competitive recognition-seeking the root implies. This is the NT's most pointed redirection of filotimeomai: the aspiration-energy is preserved, the direction is inverted.

## Root and Stem Patterns Across the Term Set

The two terms represent opposite poles structurally as well as morally. eritheia has a single related word (erethizō, to provoke/irritate), placing it in a small semantic field centred on friction, antagonism, and self-interested agitation. filotimeomai has a large related words cluster — the full philo- family — placing it in a broad inner-being category of love-orientations where the object determines the moral character. The two terms together expose the biblical frame for what English calls 'ambition': not a neutral drive toward goals but a motivational orientation that is always relational — directed either toward the self at the expense of others (eritheia) or toward a chosen object of honour-love (filotimeomai). The LSJ's explicit pairing of zelos kai eritheia confirms that the zeal entry in the 181 will need to be consulted alongside this entry in Phase 2 to complete the motivational picture. No Hebrew root patterns can be observed from this entry's data.

## Open Questions

2. eritheia: The LSJ root (hired day-labourer working for wages rather than principle) introduces a motive-structure distinction — self-interested work versus principled work — that the NT uses do not make explicit. Does this etymological insight appear to be supported or contradicted by any of the seven NT occurrences, or does the NT usage depart sufficiently from the etymology that the root image should not be applied?

3. eritheia: All seven NT occurrences are negative; there is no positive or neutral use of the term in the NT. Does this absolute negativity — unlike, say, zelos which can be positive or negative — mean eritheia names a structurally disordered inner-being state rather than merely a misdirected one? Phase 2 question.

4. filotimeomai: The three NT occurrences all redirect the honour-aspiration drive toward God-honouring goals. Does the NT assume the underlying philo-timē drive is morally neutral and therefore redirectable, or does it assume a transformation has already occurred that makes such redirection possible? Phase 2 question.

5. filotimeomai: The philo- family in the related words cluster names a range of love-orientations (love of money, love of pleasure, love of God, love of dispute, selfishness, friendship). Does Scripture treat these philo- orientations as a coherent category in which the moral character of any given instance is determined by the object of the love — and is this pattern consistent across the NT occurrences of philo- compound terms? Phase 2 question.

6. WORD-LEVEL: Phil 2:3 places eritheia in direct contrast to humility and other-regard. Does this contrast — selfish ambition versus humility — constitute a recognisable two-state typology for this motivational domain in the Pauline corpus, and does Paul describe a third state beyond these two poles elsewhere in his writings?

7. WORD-LEVEL: The zelos entry in the 181 will carry the motivational energy dimension that the combined list summary attributed to ambition. Once zelos is analysed, should the ambition and zeal entries be treated as a paired cluster in Phase 2 synthesis, given the LSJ's explicit pairing of zelos kai eritheia?
