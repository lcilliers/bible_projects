## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** grief | **Registry ID:** 071 | **Source list:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-08 | **Version:** v5

This assessment covers the eight terms in Part 3. The testament_coverage for this part is "both" — it contains two Hebrew/OT terms (cho.li, pu.qah) and six Greek/NT terms (o.du.ne, penthos, lu.pe, lu.pe.o, ste.na.zo, pen.the.o). Part 3 is the only part in this word set containing Greek terms.

The Greek terms qualify strongly as inner-being words. lu.pe and lu.pe.o together constitute the most developed grief vocabulary in Part 3 by occurrence count, with lu.pe explicitly naming sorrow as a state located in the heart (Joh 16:6 — "sorrow has filled your heart") and lu.pe.o covering the full action of grieving, being grieved, and causing grief in others, including the Holy Spirit (Eph 4:30). pen.the.o and penthos form a well-defined mourning family with verse records spanning Beatitude mourning, eschatological mourning, and mourning over sin. ste.na.zo is a distinctive term covering inward groaning oriented toward a not-yet-realised future — a different inner register from the other terms. o.du.ne qualifies but with only one verse record, limiting its assessment.

The Hebrew terms are more qualified. cho.li (sickness) qualifies as an inner-being word primarily through the Suffering Servant passages (Isa 53:3–4) where the noun denotes grief-as-sickness borne by the Servant on behalf of others. Its dominant use in the verse records is physical illness rather than inner grief. pu.qah is a hapax legomenon whose single occurrence (1Sa 25:31) uses it figuratively for pangs of conscience — a genuine inner-being use but insufficient in isolation to establish it as a grief term.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### CHALAH — cho.li (H2483)

*This root family also appears in Part 1 (cha.lah, H2470I). Root-level conclusions here are provisional until Session D cross-part synthesis is complete.*

cho.li is the noun of the CHALAH root, meaning sickness. With approximately 24 occurrences and 21 verse records, the sample is at approximately 88% coverage — nearly complete. The related word cluster connects cho.li directly back to cha.lah (Part 1), covering the full semantic range of the root: weakness, illness, grief, and the verb of putting someone to grief.

The verse records for cho.li in Part 3 divide sharply between two registers. The larger register — covering most of the historical narrative verses — uses cho.li for straightforward physical illness: the sickness of kings (2Ki 1:2; 2Ki 8:8–9; 2Ki 13:14; 2Ch 16:12; 2Ch 21:15–19), the sickness God removes as covenant blessing (Deu 7:15), the sicknesses threatened as covenant curse (Deu 28:59–61), and Hezekiah's recovery from sickness (Isa 38:9). In these verses, cho.li is physical — a condition of the body that can be fatal, that God appoints or removes, and that is assessed by whether the afflicted person seeks the Lord.

The theologically significant register is concentrated in Isa 53:3–4. Verse 3 describes the Servant as "a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief (cho.li)." Verse 4 states "he has borne our griefs (cho.li) and carried our sorrows." Here cho.li operates as an inner-state word — the Servant's familiarity with grief and his vicarious bearing of others' grief. The same term that names bodily illness in the narrative books names the Servant's grief-intimacy and grief-bearing in the prophetic context.

The god_as_subject flag is true: throughout the Deuteronomy and Chronicles verses, God is the agent who appoints, removes, or sends cho.li as a covenantal act. Psa 41:3 — "the Lord sustains him on his sickbed" — presents God as the one who sustains the ill person. The somatic_link flag is true: cho.li is primarily a bodily phenomenon across most of the verse set.

Isa 1:5 is a transitional verse: "the whole head is sick (cho.li implied), and the whole heart faint" — where sickness is applied to the nation corporately with a head-to-heart frame, suggesting the metaphorical extension of physical sickness language to the nation's inner condition.

**Verdict:** cho.li qualifies as an inner-being word specifically through the Isa 53 usage, where it names grief that can be borne, carried, and acquainted with — an inner-state use that is distinct from the majority physical-illness use across the verse set. The tension between these two registers within the same term is the primary analytical feature of cho.li. Root-level conclusions must await Session D synthesis with cha.lah (Part 1).

### PUQAH — pu.qah (H6330)

pu.qah is a standalone term with a single root family entry and a single occurrence in the OT. The root PUQAH covers tottering, staggering, or stumbling, and the sole verse record (1Sa 25:31) uses the term figuratively for pangs of conscience — Abigail telling David that he should not have "cause of grief or pangs of conscience for having shed blood without cause." The somatic_link flag is true: the term's core image is bodily staggering, and its grief meaning is a figurative extension of that physical instability onto the inner experience of moral unease.

The related words (puq — to reel; piq — tottering) confirm that the root image is one of physical unsteadiness, and the grief meaning in 1Sa 25:31 is the conscience version of that same loss of equilibrium. This is a distinctive inner-being image — grief-as-staggering, or the loss of inner stability caused by moral failure.

**Verdict:** pu.qah's inner-being contribution is real but minimal. Its single occurrence and entirely figurative grief meaning means it contributes a striking image to the grief word set — conscience-staggering — but cannot be assessed for distributional patterns. It has no consolidation candidate in Part 3. Phase 2 should determine whether the root's broader occurrence set (puq, piq) illuminates this figurative inner use further.

### ODUNE — o.du.ne (G3601)

The meaning block covers both physical and mental pain and includes "sorrow, grief" as a gloss, with the LSJ description affirming both body and mind registers.

### PENTHOS — penthos (G3997) and pen.the.o (G3996)

The PENTHOS family contains the Greek noun (penthos) and its corresponding verb (pen.the.o). Both terms carry the primary meaning of mourning, grief, or lamenting, with the LSJ noting that penthos is especially associated with grief for the dead and with mourning as a public practice.

**penthos (G3997)** has approximately 5 occurrences and 3 verse records — approximately 60% coverage. The SMALL_VERSE_SAMPLE flag applies. The three verse records are all from Revelation: Rev 18:7–8 depict the mourning that comes upon Babylon/the harlot city as judgment, and Rev 21:4 announces the eschatological removal of mourning — "neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore." This means the entire penthos verse sample in Part 3 is from Revelation's eschatological and judgment contexts. The missing verses (likely including Jam 4:9, which appears under pen.the.o) would bring more diversity of context.

The Rev 21:4 use is significant: penthos is listed alongside death, crying, and pain as the conditions that will be abolished in the new creation. This positions penthos as one of the defining negative conditions of present existence — a grief-mourning that belongs to this age and not to the age to come.

**pen.the.o (G3996)** is the verb, with approximately 10 occurrences and 10 verse records — complete coverage. The meaning covers mourning as an active process, with three sub-uses: lamenting over others (transitive, 2Cor 12:21), absolute mourning as a state of sadness (Matt 5:4; 9:15; Mk 16:10), and the middle voice of bewailing oneself or feeling guilt (1Cor 5:2).

The verse records cover several contexts. Matt 5:4 is the most theologically distinctive: "blessed are those who mourn (pen.the.o), for they shall be comforted." This positions mourning as a disposition that is blessed by God and that will be met with divine comfort — a positive orientation on grief-as-mourning that is not present in the OT terms in this part. The cross-registry link notes the connection to humility (poverty of spirit in Matt 5:3) as the immediate context.

Jam 4:9 pairs pen.the.o directly with wretchedness and weeping as the appropriate response to spiritual pride: "be wretched and mourn and weep; let your laughter be turned to mourning." The same mourning that is blessed in Matthew appears here as the right response to having been in a state of pride and worldliness — a mourning-over-sin use.

The Revelation uses of pen.the.o (Rev 18:11; 18:15; 18:19) cluster around the merchants mourning over Babylon's fall — mourning over the loss of economic prosperity rather than mourning in the grief-over-death sense. This is mourning as lamentation over external loss, and it stands in contrast to the inner-state mourning of Matt 5:4 and Jam 4:9.

2Cor 12:21 — "I may have to mourn over many who sinned" — shows pen.the.o used by Paul for pastoral grief over others' sin, a distinctly relational use.

The somatic_link flag is true for pen.the.o: mourning in the NT, as in the OT (a.val, Part 1), is expressed bodily — Rev 18:19 includes throwing dust on heads, and weeping consistently accompanies pen.the.o across the verse set.

**Verdict within PENTHOS:** penthos and pen.the.o function as noun and verb of a mourning family that covers a wider range in the NT than the verse set might initially suggest. penthos names the condition of mourning as one of the defining features of mortal existence, destined for eschatological removal. pen.the.o names the active process of mourning, which in the NT carries a distinctive positive dimension (Beatitude mourning, mourning-over-sin in response to pride) alongside its expected uses in grief and lamentation. The two terms are not consolidation candidates. The partial verse coverage of penthos limits the verdict on the noun.

### LUPE — lu.pe (G3077) and lu.pe.o (G3076)

The LUPE family is the most developed and most theologically rich in Part 3, containing the noun lu.pe (grief, sorrow, pain) and the verb lu.pe.o (to grieve, cause grief, be grieved). Together they have 37 verse records across a stated occurrence count of approximately 42 combined — approximately 88% coverage.

**lu.pe (G3077)** has approximately 16 occurrences and 13 verse records. The meaning block covers sorrow, grief, and pain for both body and mind, with the metonymic use (the cause of grief as grief itself) noted at 1Pet 2:19. The somatic_link flag is true.

The verse records show lu.pe operating in three distinct ways. First, as a sustained inner condition: Rom 9:2 — "I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart" — places lu.pe in the heart as a named location and characterises it as continuous. Psa 31:10 is not a lu.pe verse but the pattern is confirmed by the Johannine uses — Joh 16:6 ("sorrow has filled your heart") places lu.pe explicitly in the heart. The heart-filling image suggests lu.pe as a condition that can occupy the inner person.

Second, as a condition produced by specific circumstances: Luk 22:45 — "the disciples found sleeping for sorrow" — presents lu.pe as a physical and mental exhaustion that produces sleep; Joh 16:21 uses childbirth sorrow as the analogy for temporary grief that is replaced by joy. Phili 2:27 — "lest I should have sorrow upon sorrow" — presents lu.pe as an accumulating condition.

Third, and most distinctive in the NT: 2Cor 7:10 — "godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death." This verse explicitly distinguishes two qualities of lu.pe by their source (from God vs. worldly) and their outcome (repentance leading to salvation vs. death). This is the most theologically developed use of any grief term in Part 3 — and possibly in the entire word set — because it classifies grief itself by its orientation and effect rather than simply naming it as a negative state.

Heb 12:11 — "all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant" — uses lu.pe in the context of God's disciplinary activity, presenting grief as the immediate experience of discipline whose long-term fruit is righteousness. 1Pet 2:19 — "one endures sorrows while suffering unjustly" — places lu.pe in the context of unjust suffering endured with mindfulness of God.

**lu.pe.o (G3076)** is the verb, with approximately 26 occurrences and 21 verse records — a representative sample at approximately 81%. The meaning block covers both the active form (to cause grief) and the passive (to be grieved, sorrowful, distressed), with the LSJ adding the meaning of harassing or troubling an enemy — a metaphorical military use not present in the NT verse set. The causative_form_present flag is true.

The verse records cluster in three main areas. The Synoptic Gospels contribute eight verses showing lu.pe.o as the passive — being sorrowful, distressed, grieved: Mat 19:22 (the rich young ruler went away sorrowful), Mat 26:22 (the disciples were sorrowful at Gethsemane), Mat 26:37 (Jesus himself began to be sorrowful and troubled). These verses establish lu.pe.o as the term for the experiential moment of grief in the Gospel narratives.

The 2 Corinthians cluster (2Cor 2:2–5; 7:8–9; 7:11) is the most developed active/causative use of lu.pe.o in the NT. Paul uses the verb repeatedly to describe the deliberate causing of grief through a letter and the subsequent resolution of that grief through repentance. The sequence shows grief being deliberately administered (Paul grieved the Corinthians), the grief producing a response (repentance), and the overall intent being pastoral. 2Cor 7:9 — "you were grieved into repenting" — shows the causative of lu.pe.o producing a change in inner direction. The grief is not merely felt but is effective.

Eph 4:30 — "do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed" — is the NT counterpart to Isa 63:10 (a.tsav, Part 2). The same action — grieving the Spirit through human behaviour — is named in both Testaments, with lu.pe.o (NT) and a.tsav Hiphil (OT) as the respective verbs. The cross-registry link notes this correspondence explicitly.

1Th 4:13 — "that you may not grieve as others do who have no hope" — distinguishes Christian grief from hopeless grief, using lu.pe.o in a way that implicitly positions the grief itself as neutral but shapes how it is to be experienced by reference to the resurrection hope. 2Cor 6:10 — "as sorrowful, yet always rejoicing" — presents grief and joy as simultaneously present, a pattern also seen in Part 1's wisdom literature.

**Verdict within LUPE:** lu.pe and lu.pe.o are doing genuinely different work within the same root. lu.pe names the condition of sorrow as it is inhabited by the person — capable of being located in the heart, of being classified by its source and direction (godly vs. worldly), and of accumulating. lu.pe.o covers the action and causation of grief — being grieved, causing grief, being grieved into change. The 2Cor 7:10 distinction between godly and worldly grief (lu.pe) is the single most analytically important theological distinction in the entire Part 3 verse set, and one that appears to have no direct parallel in the OT grief terms of Parts 1 and 2. The two terms are not consolidation candidates.

### STENAZ — ste.na.zo (G4727)

ste.na.zo is a standalone verb meaning to groan, sigh, or grumble, with approximately 6 occurrences and 6 verse records — complete coverage. The related word cluster is the richest of any Greek term in Part 3: anastenazō (to sigh deeply), stenagmos (groan), sustenazō (to groan with), katastenazō (to groan), and stenō (to moan) — a family of groan-related terms suggesting ste.na.zo is the basic form within a developed vocabulary of sighing and groaning.

The somatic_link flag is true: the verb's core meaning is a physically audible or physically felt expression. Mar 7:34 — Jesus looking up to heaven and sighing before healing the deaf man — places the sigh as a discrete physical action accompanying prayer. The groan in Rom 8:23 — "we ourselves groan inwardly as we wait eagerly for adoption" — is explicitly inward and eschatologically oriented: the groaning is produced by having the firstfruits of the Spirit while still awaiting the full redemption of the body. This is a unique inner-being use in the Part 3 set: grief as groaning that arises from the gap between present experience and future hope.

2Cor 5:2 and 5:4 extend this eschatological groaning to the experience of being "in this tent" (the mortal body), longing for the heavenly dwelling. The groaning is produced by the weight of embodied mortality and the desire for what is not yet realised. Heb 13:17 uses ste.na.zo negatively — leaders should be able to serve with joy and not with groaning, because groaning in this context would be to the detriment of those served.

Jam 5:9 introduces the grumbling use: "do not grumble against one another, brothers, so that you may not be judged." This is the only instance in Part 3 where a grief-related verb is redirected toward interpersonal grumbling — a use that is at the edge of the grief register and closer to the complaint or anger register.

**Verdict:** ste.na.zo is doing distinctive inner-being work not represented by any other term in Part 3. The eschatological groaning of Rom 8:23 and 2Cor 5:2–4 names grief as the inward pressure of a person who has the Spirit and longs for what the Spirit points toward but has not yet received. This is grief-as-longing-under-constraint, and it is a register not present in the OT terms across Parts 1–3. ste.na.zo has no consolidation candidate in this part.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-Terms Do Different Work

Two root families in Part 3 contain two terms each where the verse sets reveal meaningful differences.

**PENTHOS family: penthos vs. pen.the.o.** The three penthos verse records are all from Revelation's eschatological contexts, making penthos specifically the noun of mourning as a present-age condition destined for removal. pen.the.o is the active verb covering four distinct uses: Beatitude mourning (blessed disposition), mourning over sin (Jam 4:9; 1Cor 5:2), pastoral mourning over others' sin (2Cor 12:21), and commercial lamentation over loss (Rev 18:11–19). These are genuine functional differences, not labelling distinctions: the noun penthos is eschatologically oriented while the verb pen.the.o shows mourning as a multi-directional active process. The differences are real and both terms are necessary for the word set.

**LUPE family: lu.pe vs. lu.pe.o.** lu.pe is the noun of the sorrow condition — its most distinctive NT feature being the classification of sorrow by its source and outcome (2Cor 7:10). lu.pe.o is the verb covering the action of grief — being grieved, causing grief, and being grieved into change. The 2Cor 7 cluster is significant: both lu.pe (the noun) and lu.pe.o (the verb) appear across the same passage, with lu.pe naming the quality of the grief (godly vs. worldly) and lu.pe.o naming the action (Paul grieved them; they were grieved into repenting). This is the only passage in the Part 3 set where noun and verb of the same root work together in close proximity to articulate a theological distinction. The differences between lu.pe and lu.pe.o are genuine and both are necessary.

The single-term families (CHALAH in this part, PUQAH, ODUNE, STENAZ) have no internal sub-term comparison to make. The CHALAH root has a cross-part sub-term comparison pending in Session D.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The testament_coverage field is "both." This section compares the two OT terms (cho.li, pu.qah) with the six NT Greek terms on the basis of the verse records in this part only.

**What the NT vocabulary introduces that is absent from the OT portion of this part.** The NT terms in Part 3 introduce several registers with no counterpart in cho.li or pu.qah. Most significantly: lu.pe (2Cor 7:10) introduces the distinction between godly grief and worldly grief, classified by source and outcome — a theological differentiation of grief into a type that leads to repentance and life, and a type that produces death. No OT term in any part of this word set makes this kind of explicit internal classification of grief by its productive orientation. Second, ste.na.zo (Rom 8:23; 2Cor 5:2–4) introduces eschatological groaning — grief as the inward longing of the person who has the Spirit but awaits the not-yet of resurrection and full redemption. This is an inner state caused by the gap between present experience and eschatological hope, which is a distinctly NT theological frame. Third, pen.the.o (Matt 5:4) introduces Beatitude mourning — grief positioned as a blessed disposition that will be met with divine comfort. The OT has reversal promises for grief (as seen in ya.gon's verse set in Part 2), but none in this part phrase mourning itself as a disposition that qualifies the person for blessing.

**What the OT vocabulary does that the NT portion of this part does not.** cho.li grounds the grief word set in the language of bodily sickness in a way that the Greek terms in Part 3 largely do not. The sickness-grief connection — where grief is named using the same word as physical illness — is a Hebrew distinctive. This is most significant in Isa 53:3–4, where the Servant's acquaintance with cho.li (grief/sickness) is the frame for his vicarious bearing of human grief-as-illness. No Greek term in Part 3 uses this sickness-as-grief image. pu.qah introduces the conscience-staggering image — grief as inner instability caused by the weight of moral failure — which is also absent from the NT vocabulary in this part.

**What is present in both Testaments across this part's verses.** Both the OT and NT portions of this part show God as the one who acts upon grief: in cho.li, God appoints and removes sickness/grief; in lu.pe (Heb 12:11) and lu.pe.o (1Pe 1:6), grief is produced by God's disciplinary activity and testing respectively. The pattern of God having an active role in the grief experience spans both Testaments in Part 3.

**The single most significant observed difference.** The most significant difference between the OT and NT registers within Part 3's verse set is that the NT introduces an explicit internal classification of grief by its source and outcome (2Cor 7:10 — godly vs. worldly lu.pe), while the OT terms name grief as a condition or event without classifying its quality. In the OT verses of this part, grief is described, caused, removed, or borne; it is not classified by whether it is productive or destructive. The NT distinction at 2Cor 7:10 is a categorical development that has no formal parallel in the OT terms present in this part.

All differences identified here become [OT/NT] prefixed open questions in Section 6.
