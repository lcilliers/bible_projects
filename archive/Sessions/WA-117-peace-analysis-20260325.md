## Section 2 — Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 Primary Meaning and Semantic Range

Peace is the broadest single vocabulary in the programme. The Hebrew *shalom* (H7965G, 148 verses; H7965I, 46 verses) is not a simple word for the absence of conflict but a comprehensive concept whose core meaning is **wholeness** — the state in which everything that belongs together is together, intact, and functioning. The SHALAM root (sha.lem: to be complete/whole, sha.lem: to restore/pay, she.lem: peace offering) is the structural foundation: peace is not merely a relational condition but a state of completion. Understanding this root relationship is essential to reading the entire registry.

**SHALOM — the core term family (H7965G/I/J/K/L/H, ~210 verses):**

The shalom verse set reveals five distinct but interlocking dimensions:

*Wholeness and welfare (H7965G/I):* The largest cluster. Shalom covers physical safety (Gen 26:29 — "sent you away in peace"), bodily health (Psa 38:3 — "no soundness/shalom in my flesh"), national security (1 Ki 4:24 — "peace on all sides"), personal flourishing (Jer 29:11 — "plans for welfare/shalom and not for evil"), and communal wellbeing (Jer 29:7 — "in its shalom you will find your shalom"). The question "Is it shalom?" (2 Ki 9:17-22) functions as a comprehensive welfare inquiry. Shalom encompasses the whole person in the whole community in the whole created order.

*Covenant peace (H7965G):* Num 25:12 — "my covenant of peace"; Isa 54:10 — "my covenant of peace shall not be removed"; Eze 34:25, 37:26 — "covenant of peace" as eschatological promise; Mal 2:5 — "my covenant with him was one of life and peace." Shalom is specifically covenantal — it names the condition of a relationship that is intact before God. The covenant of peace is not merely a treaty but the relational wholeness between God and his people.

*False peace — the contra-pattern:* Jer 6:14, 8:11 — "Peace, peace, when there is no peace." This is the most important contra-pattern in the registry. False shalom is the claim to wholeness where wholeness does not exist. The prophets identify it as the central deception of false prophecy: giving a surface diagnosis of health to what is internally broken. The structural significance is that shalom has an objective character — it can be genuinely present or genuinely absent regardless of what is claimed.

*Eschatological peace:* Isa 9:6-7 — Prince of Peace; the increase of his government and of shalom without end. Isa 57:19 — "Peace, peace to the far and the near." Isa 66:12 — "I will extend peace to her like a river." Shalom in its fullest form is not merely present reality but eschatological promise — the wholeness of the restored creation.

*Peace as greeting (H7965K):* 1 Sa 25:6 — "Peace be to you, and peace to your house, and peace to all that you have." The greeting form encodes the wish for comprehensive wholeness. Every shalom greeting is a miniature prayer.

**SHALAM root — completeness as the structural ground of peace (H7999A/B, H8002, H8003):**

The SHALAM root illuminates why shalom means what it means. Sha.lem (to complete, to make whole, to pay, to restore) in its 94-verse set is almost entirely about restitution and fulfillment of obligations — Exo 21-22 legal codes, vow-paying (Psa 50:14, 56:12, 116:14), repayment (Pro 6:31, 20:22), and restoration of what was taken. Peace (shalom) is what exists when all debts are paid, all wrongs are made right, and all obligations are fulfilled. Peace is completed justice. The structure is: wholeness is what you have when everything that belongs has been returned to its place.

The peace offering (H8002, she.lem, 84 verses) is the sacrificial enactment of this structure. The peace offering is the only sacrifice where the offerer eats with the priests from the same animal (Lev 7:11-18) — it is a shared meal with God, the expression of restored relationship. The offering does not primarily deal with sin (that is the sin offering's function) but enacts the wholeness of the relationship between worshipper and God. Peace is sacrificially performed.

Sha.lem as adjective (H8003, 27 verses) — "a whole heart" — is the inward completeness that corresponds to outward peace: 1 Ki 8:61 — "let your heart be wholly true to the Lord your God"; 1 Ch 28:9 — "serve him with a whole heart and a willing mind." The whole heart is the interior dimension of shalom — the person in whom inner and outer are aligned.

**REST vocabulary (H5117 nu.ach, 140 verses, and the nuach family):**

Nu.ach is the largest single term in the registry by verse count. It carries a distinctive structural role: rest as the activity God performs and gives. The verse set divides into two major patterns:

*Divine rest-giving:* Exo 33:14 — "My presence will go with you, and I will give you rest." Jos 21:44 — "The Lord gave them rest on every side." 2 Sa 7:1, 11 — "the Lord had given him rest from all his surrounding enemies." 1 Ch 22:9 — "I will give him rest from all his surrounding enemies; his name shall be Solomon (shalom), and I will give peace and quiet to Israel in his days." The connection between Solomon/shalom and nu.ach/rest is explicit and structural — the reign of the son of peace is the reign of rest.

*The Spirit resting:* Num 11:25-26 — "the Spirit rested on them." Isa 11:2 — "The Spirit of the Lord shall rest upon him." This is the peace vocabulary applied to the Spirit's inhabitation — divine rest as the presence of God settling upon a person.

*Sabbath rest:* Exo 20:11 — "the Lord rested on the seventh day." The created rhythm of shabbat is a nu.ach rhythm — the world's peace is built into the structure of time.

The rest noun forms (H4496H me.nu.chah, 21 verses; H4494 ma.no.ach, 6 verses) concentrate the resting-place dimension: Psa 23:2 — "beside still waters" (me.nu.chah); Psa 95:11 — "they shall not enter my rest"; Isa 11:10 — "his resting place shall be glorious"; Psa 116:7 — "return, O my soul, to your rest." The resting place is both spatial and relational — a location of settled security in God.

The two single-verse rest terms (H4771 mar.go.a, H4774 mar.ge.ah) are analytically significant beyond their size. Jer 6:16 — "walk in it, and find rest (mar.go.a) for your souls" — is the closest OT parallel to Matt 11:29. Isa 28:12 — "This is rest (me.nu.chah); give rest (nu.ach) to the weary; and this is repose (mar.ge.ah)" — compounds three rest terms in a single verse of divine invitation, with the people refusing to hear. Rest is something God offers and people resist.

**SILENCE/STILLNESS vocabulary (H2013, H2790A/B, H2814, H1826A/H, H1827, H1748, H1820):**

The silence vocabulary is a distinct dimension of peace — peace as the cessation of noise, speech, and agitation. Three registers emerge:

*Commanded silence before God:* Hab 2:20 — "let all the earth keep silence before him." Zep 1:7 — "Be silent before the Lord God!" Zec 2:13 — "Be silent, all flesh, before the Lord." This is the peace of creaturely quieting before divine majesty. The earth is commanded into stillness.

*God's silence as a problem:* H2790B (cha.resh: be quiet, 43 verses) applied to God — Psa 28:1 — "be not deaf to me, lest...I become like those who go down to the pit." Psa 83:1 — "do not keep silence, do not hold your peace, O God!" Isa 42:14 — "For a long time I have held my peace; I have kept still." God's silence is a problem for those who need his intervention; his speaking and acting is the peace they need. The silence vocabulary generates its own contra-pattern: divine silence is distressing to the person who waits for God.

*Still small voice (H1827 de.ma.mah):* 1 Ki 19:12 — "after the earthquake a fire, but the Lord was not in the fire. And after the fire the sound of a low whisper (de.ma.mah daqah — thin/whispered silence)." This verse is structurally decisive: the de.ma.mah is the form in which God chose to speak to Elijah after the wilderness of storm and fire. Divine peace is not the absence of divine presence but its most intimate mode of address. The whisper follows the breaking and the burning — it is peace on the other side of devastation.

*Zep 3:17 — the peace of divine quieting:* "He will quiet you by his love." This is H2790B with God as subject and humans as the object of quieting — God's love functioning as the force that settles the inner agitation of his people. The causative structure is explicit: God is the agent; the human soul is what is quieted.

**BLESSING vocabulary (H1288 ba.rakh, 249 verses; H1293 be.ra.khah, 64 verses):**

The blessing vocabulary enters the peace registry because blessing is the spoken-verbal form of shalom. The Aaronic blessing (Num 6:24-26) is the most concentrated single passage in the registry: "The Lord bless you and keep you; the Lord make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you; the Lord lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace (shalom)." The blessing culminates in shalom — it is the comprehensive gift that blessing communicates. The entire blessing vocabulary is structured toward this terminal gift.

H1288 (ba.rakh: to bless) at 249 verses is the largest blessing corpus in the programme. The verse set is dominated by divine blessing toward humans (Gen 1:22, 1:28, 2:3 — the blessing of creation; the Abrahamic blessing chain across Genesis; the Mosaic blessing tradition). The structure is consistent: divine blessing is the active conferral of wholeness, fruitfulness, and peace on those to whom God turns his face.

**SOOTHING/AROMA (H5207 ni.cho.ach, 43 verses):**

The soothing aroma (re.ach ni.cho.ach — "pleasing/soothing aroma") appears across 43 offering verses from Gen 8:21 through Ezekiel. The structural role is clear: the offering produces a soothing effect directed Godward. Gen 8:21 — "when the Lord smelled the pleasing aroma, the Lord said in his heart, 'I will never again curse the ground.'" The soothing aroma is the mechanism by which the offering creates peace between God and the worshipper — it soothes divine displeasure and establishes the conditions for covenantal wholeness. This is the olfactory dimension of peace — the peace vocabulary extends even into the sense of smell.

**QUIET/EASE vocabulary (H8252 sha.qat, 41 verses; H7962 shal.vah, 8 verses; H7600 sha.a.nan, 10v; H7961 sha.lev, 8v):**

Sha.qat (to quiet, be at rest, 41 verses) is the most important ease term in the registry. The verse set divides between the land having rest from war (the Judges formula: "the land had rest for forty years," repeated across Judg 3-8 and 2 Chr 14), and individual/national quietness. Isa 30:15 — "in returning and rest you shall be saved; in quietness (sha.qat) and trust shall be your strength." Isa 32:17-18 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace (shalom), and the result of righteousness, quietness (sha.qat) and trust forever... My people will abide in a peaceful habitation, in secure dwellings, and in quiet resting places." These verses establish the causal chain: righteousness → peace → quietness. Peace is what righteousness produces; quietness is the form peace takes when it has settled into permanence.

The ease/security cluster (shal.vah, sha.a.nan, sha.lev) covers the condition of undisturbed security that can be either genuine peace or false complacency. Eze 16:49 — the guilt of Sodom included "prosperous ease (shal.vah)" without aid to the poor. Dan 8:25 — the adversary prospers "without warning" (beshal.vah — in ease/surprise). The ease vocabulary has a double edge: genuine ease is the fruit of God's peace; ease without righteousness is complacency that precedes judgment. H7600 (sha.a.nan: complacency/ease, 10 verses) confirms this: Amo 6:1 — "Woe to those who are at ease in Zion" — ease become self-satisfaction.

### 2.2 Structural Polarity — The Two-Forms Structure

**Form 1 — True shalom:** Wholeness that is real, covenantally grounded, and sustained by right relationship with God. Characterised by: completeness (sha.lem), restored relationship (the peace offering), divine rest-giving (nu.ach), the covenant of peace (brit shalom), righteousness producing peace (Isa 32:17), the mind stayed on God (Isa 26:3 — "perfect peace" = shalom shalom, doubled).

**Form 2 — False shalom:** The claim to peace where peace does not exist. Jer 6:14/8:11 — "Peace, peace, when there is no peace." This is the central contra-pattern. False shalom is the diagnosis of wholeness applied to brokenness. The false prophet says "shalom" over a wound that requires surgery. False shalom does not negate the existence of true shalom — it confirms that shalom has an objective character that can be mimicked but not faked.

**Complacency as degraded peace:** The ease vocabulary (sha.a.nan, sha.lev, shal.vah) traces a third form — ease that was once genuine peace but has calcified into self-satisfaction. Jer 48:11 — "Moab has been at ease from his youth and has settled on his dregs; he has not been emptied from vessel to vessel." Undisturbed ease without covenant loyalty produces spiritual stagnancy.

**Eschatological contra-pattern:** Rev 6:4 — "its rider was permitted to take peace from the earth." The eschatological reversal of peace is a programme-level observation: what God installs, the adversarial forces of the end times remove. Peace is not self-sustaining in the created order; it requires divine maintenance.

**Transformation dynamic:** The movement from Form 2 (false/absent peace) to Form 1 is never self-generated in this corpus. True shalom is given: by God (Num 6:26 — "give you peace"), by the Prince of Peace (Isa 9:6), by the blood of the cross (Col 1:20 — eirēnopoieō), by Christ personally (Joh 14:27 — "My peace I give to you"). Peace is a gift structure — it cannot be self-manufactured.

The body is involved through the somatic dimension (see Section 5). The spirit dimension is engaged through the divine names and the covenant relationship. But the dominant register is the soul — the middle term that holds together the whole person in relation to God and others.

## Section 4 — Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God as Subject — the fullest divine engagement in the programme

The peace registry has more GOD_AS_SUBJECT terms than any other registry examined. Sixteen active terms carry the flag, plus the entire theological anchor cluster. The divine dimension is not peripheral to peace — it is structurally central.

**YHWH-Shalom — God named as peace (H3073, H7965H):**
Judg 6:24 — "Then Gideon built an altar there to the Lord and called it, The Lord Is Peace (YHWH-Shalom)." This is the theological anchor of the registry. God does not merely give peace; he is named Peace. The divine name is not a description of what God does but of what God is. This means that peace, like holiness and love, is a perfection of the divine nature — not an achievement but an attribute.

Judg 6:23 provides the context: "The Lord said to him, 'Peace be to you. Do not fear; you shall not die.'" Gideon's altar is named after the divine word that stilled his terror. YHWH-Shalom is the name God earns in the moment of peace-giving. The word that quells inner fear names the God who spoke it.

**God as giver of rest (H5117 nu.ach with GOD_AS_SUBJECT):**
The formula "the Lord gave them rest on every side" (Jos 21:44; 23:1; 2 Sa 7:1; 1 Ki 5:4; 1 Ch 22:18; 2 Ch 14:6-7; 14:15; 15:15; 20:30) is one of the most repeated divine action phrases in the historical books. Rest is what God gives as the fruit of covenant faithfulness. The Sabbath rest (Exo 20:11) — "the Lord rested on the seventh day" — establishes rest as a divine activity before it is a human one. Humans rest because God rested; the created rhythm of peace is an imitation of divine pattern.

**God's quieting action (H8252, H2790B with GOD_AS_SUBJECT):**
Zep 3:17 — "he will quiet you by his love." This is peace as divine action directed inward — God himself performing the stilling of the troubled inner life. The causative structure is explicit: God is the agent; human agitation is what he addresses. This is the closest OT parallel to the NT "the peace of God will guard your hearts" (Phil 4:7) — both present peace as something God does to the person rather than something the person achieves.

Isa 42:14 — "For a long time I have held my peace; I have kept still and restrained myself." Here God is the subject of silence — divine patience expressed through sustained quietness. God's peace is active restraint, not mere absence of action.

**The divine name cluster and peace:**
The theological anchor terms establish that the God of peace is defined by a specific character:
- *YHWH of Hosts* (H6635B, 156 verses) — the God whose peace is secured by omnipotent power. Hag 2:9 — "in this place I will give peace, declares the LORD of Hosts." The armies of heaven guarantee the peace of earth.
- *Righteousness* (H6664G/H, 112 verses total) — Psa 85:10 — "righteousness and peace have kissed each other." Peace is the fruit of righteousness, not its alternative. Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace." The God of peace is the God of righteousness; they are not in tension.
- *El Elyon — Most High* (H5945B, 25 verses; H5943/H5946 Aramaic, 14 verses) — the Most High who rules over the nations (Dan 4:17, 4:25, 4:32, 4:34) is also the God who gives peace. Dan 4:34 — Nebuchadnezzar's reason returning is linked to blessing and praising the Most High. Peace of mind follows the recognition of divine sovereignty.
- *Almighty/Shaddai* (H7706, 48 verses) — El Shaddai appears predominantly in Job and the patriarchal narratives. The Almighty is the God whose power establishes covenant peace even through suffering.
- *Jealous/Qanna* (H7067G/H, 6 verses combined) — the jealous God (Exo 20:5, 34:14; Deu 4:24, 5:9, 6:15) is the one for whom covenant peace matters absolutely. Divine jealousy is not incompatible with divine peace — it is its guarantee. The God who will tolerate no rival is the God who gives undivided peace to those who are entirely his.
- *Everlasting/Olam* (H5769G, 272 verses; H5769H, 1 verse) — Isa 9:7 — "of the increase of his government and of peace there will be no end." Peace in the divine economy is not temporary; it has eternal character. Gen 21:33 — El Olam, the Everlasting God.

**NT: God as peace, Christ as peace, Spirit as peace:**
G1515 (eirēnē, 84 verses) — the NT peace vocabulary is saturated with divine agency: "God of peace" (Rom 15:33, 16:20; 2 Cor 13:11; Phil 4:9; 1 Th 5:23; Heb 13:20), "peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ" (standard epistolary greeting formula), "he himself is our peace" (Eph 2:14), "peace of God which surpasses all understanding" (Phil 4:7), "peace of Christ" (Col 3:15). The NT peace vocabulary concentrates divine agency in the Trinity: the Father gives peace, the Son is peace, the Spirit produces peace (Gal 5:22).

G1517 (eirēnopoieō, 1 verse — Col 1:20) — "making peace by the blood of his cross." This is the only NT occurrence of the peace-making verb applied to the cosmic act of atonement. The cross is the structural peace-making event in the NT — the act by which the wholeness broken by sin is restored. This is shalom (completeness restored through sacrifice) translated into the sacrificial vocabulary of the NT.

**The divine silence and the divine word as peace:**
Isa 57:11 — "Have I not held my peace, even for a long time?" God's sustained silence is presented as a test of trust. Psa 85:8 — "Let me hear what God the Lord will speak, for he will speak peace to his people." The psalmist waits for the divine word — peace is what God speaks when he speaks to his people. The divine speech is peace; divine silence is what must be endured while waiting for the peace-word.

## Section 5 — Somatic Dimension

**Somatic engagement: MODERATE — present through specific terms and the body-as-peace-location theme.**

**Body as the location of peace (and its absence):**
Psa 38:3 — "there is no soundness (shalom) in my flesh because of your indignation; there is no health in my bones because of my sin." Shalom appears as a descriptor of bodily condition — flesh without shalom is flesh that is physically unwell. Peace and physical health share the same vocabulary. This is not metaphorical: the verse presents both as concrete bodily states. The person without shalom has a body that is not intact.

Job 3:26 — "I am not at ease (sha.lah), nor am I quiet (sha.qat); I have no rest (nu.ach), but trouble comes." Three peace terms cluster as descriptors of Job's somatic state — ease, quiet, rest — all absent. The absence of peace is experienced as bodily restlessness, physical agitation, inability to find rest. Peace is felt in the body as much as in the soul.

**The somatic peace of sleep:**
Psa 4:8 — "In peace I will both lie down and sleep; for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety." Sleep is the body's peace. The ability to lie down and sleep without fear is the somatic expression of shalom. This verse is the most direct body-peace statement in the corpus: divine protection enables the body to fully rest.

Ecc 5:12 — "Sweet is the sleep of a laborer, whether he eats little or much, but the full stomach of the rich will not let him sleep." The laborer's shalom is experienced through untroubled sleep; the anxiety of wealth disturbs it. Peace is the difference between sleep and restlessness.

**Offerings and the body:**
The soothing aroma (ni.cho.ach, H5207) and peace offering (she.lem, H8002) involve the body explicitly — the animal body consumed, the aroma ascending, the shared meal eaten. The peace offering is the most embodied of all sacrifices: the worshipper eats part of the offering. Peace is enacted through the body's participation in the sacrificial meal. Shalom is something that passes through the mouth.

**Muzzling and forced silence (G5392 fimoō, 8 verses):**
The NT muzzling vocabulary (Mar 1:25 — Jesus commanding the demon: "Be silent!"; Mar 4:39 — "Peace! Be still!" to the sea) reveals the somatic and environmental dimensions of peace. The silencing command produces immediate quieting — the storm stills, the demon quiets. Peace is commanded into external conditions. The verb's root sense (to muzzle an animal) shows that peace in the chaotic/demonic register requires forceful restraint, not merely inner adjustment.

**The still small voice — somatic attunement:**
1 Ki 19:12 — the de.ma.mah (whisper/stillness). Elijah hears the voice of God only after the wind, earthquake, and fire have passed. The structural implication is that the body must pass through external storm before it is attuned to the whisper. The somatic peace of the de.ma.mah is the peace of the person who has been stripped of everything loud and can now hear the quiet. This is the somatic formation arc of peace: body put under maximum pressure, then receiving the whispered presence.

## Section 6 — Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Causative Pathways

**Pathway 1: God → the person/community (direct peace-gift):**
This is the dominant pathway. Num 6:26 — "give you peace" (divine gift through priestly blessing). Jos 21:44 — "the Lord gave them rest." Zep 3:17 — "he will quiet you by his love." Isa 26:3 — "you keep him in perfect peace." The divine peace-giving is the foundation of every other pathway.

**Pathway 2: Covenant faithfulness → peace:**
Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace." Psa 119:165 — "great peace have those who love your law." Pro 3:17 — "her paths are peace" (of wisdom/Torah). Obedience to the covenant produces shalom as its fruit. This is not earned peace but consequential peace — the natural outcome of covenant alignment.

**Pathway 3: Sacrifice/offering → peace:**
The peace offering (H8002, 84 verses) is the sacrificial mechanism by which peace is enacted between worshipper and God. The soothing aroma (H5207) is the mechanism by which the offering reaches God. The blood of the cross (Col 1:20) is the NT fulfilment. Peace with God requires a peace-making act; it is not presumed.

**Pathway 4: Christ's presence → peace:**
Joh 14:27 — "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you. Not as the world gives do I give to you." The distinctiveness of Christ's peace-giving is explicitly contrasted with the world's — the world gives peace conditionally, situationally, temporarily. Christ's peace-giving is unconditional, relational, and permanent. Joh 20:19, 21, 26 — "Peace be with you" (three times, post-resurrection, to the gathered disciples). Peace is the first word of the risen Christ.

**Pathway 5: Human→human peace-making:**

## Section 7 — Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Phil 4:7 — Anchor: The peace of God as guardian
*"And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."*

**Contribution:** This is the defining NT statement of experiential peace. Several things are established: (1) Peace is attributed to God — it is not natural human equilibrium but divine property. (2) It surpasses understanding — it exceeds the capacity of cognitive processes to produce or explain. The peace exists beyond the reach of analysis. (3) It guards (phrourēsei — military garrison metaphor) hearts and minds. Peace functions as protective interior force, standing between the person and inner agitation. (4) The location is "in Christ Jesus" — this peace is not generic but specifically available within the relational sphere of union with Christ.

**What this adds beyond the term level:** The term level gives eirēnē as "peace." This verse gives the mechanism (guarding), the quality (surpassing understanding), the source (God), and the location (in Christ). None of this is visible from the term alone. The military garrison metaphor is striking: peace is not passive contentment but active defence.

### 7.2 Isa 26:3 — Anchor: Perfect peace and the stayed mind
*"You keep him in perfect peace whose mind is stayed on you, because he trusts in you."*

**Contribution:** "Perfect peace" is the doubled shalom — shalom shalom. The Hebrew repetition intensifies: complete completeness, wholeness upon wholeness. Three things are established: (1) The doubled form signals totality — this is shalom at its fullest expression. (2) The mechanism is the stayed mind (yetzer samukh) — the mind/imagination fixed on God. Yetzer is the formative impulse of the inner life — the deepest shaping tendency of thought and imagination. To stay the yetzer on God is the deepest possible cognitive posture. (3) The ground is trust — bitachon. Peace, cognitive focus, and trust form a single complex: trust produces the staying; the staying opens the person to double shalom.

**What this adds:** The stayed-mind mechanism is not visible from any term in the inventory. This verse establishes the cognitive pathway to peace that no lexical entry contains.

### 7.3 Judg 6:24 — YHWH-Shalom: God named as peace
*"Then Gideon built an altar there to the Lord and called it, The Lord Is Peace."*

**Contribution:** This is the foundational moment of the registry's divine dimension. The naming occurs immediately after Judg 6:23 — "Peace be to you. Do not fear; you shall not die." Gideon's encounter with the divine messenger had produced terror (Judg 6:22 — "alas, O Lord God! For now I have seen the angel of the Lord face to face"). The peace-word that stilled the terror becomes the name of an altar. Several things are established: (1) The divine name YHWH-Shalom is given in a specific existential context — fear of death, divine appearance, terror. Peace is the divine response to the terror of God-encounter. (2) The altar memorialises the peace-word as the defining characteristic of the encounter. The worshipper remembers God as the one who spoke peace. (3) The name is not merely descriptive but confessional — naming God as peace is a theological act.

### 7.4 Isa 32:17-18 — The righteousness-peace chain
*"And the effect of righteousness will be peace, and the result of righteousness, quietness and trust forever. My people will abide in a peaceful habitation, in secure dwellings, and in quiet resting places."*

**Contribution (multi-circle intersection):** This passage sits at the intersection of Circle One (secure habitation), Circle Two (bodily rest), and Circle Three (inner quietness). Three things are structurally established: (1) The causative chain is explicit — righteousness produces peace; peace produces quietness. This is the macro-structural law governing the relationship between these characteristics. (2) "Forever" — the ultimate form of this peace is permanent, not situational. (3) The triple description of the habitation — peaceful, secure, quiet — maps onto the three registers of shalom: relational peace (peaceful habitation), external security (secure dwellings), inner quietness (quiet resting places). Shalom is simultaneously relational, environmental, and interior.

### 7.5 Psa 85:10 — Righteousness and peace kiss
*"Steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other."*

**Contribution (unexpected register — poetic structural law):** This verse is formally a description of the eschatological renewal of the covenant. But its structural claim goes further: righteousness and peace are not merely associated but are in intimate union. The kissing metaphor is the strongest relational language available — these characteristics do not merely co-occur; they embrace. The verse establishes that peace without righteousness is not true peace (contra false peace), and righteousness without peace is incomplete. The paired structure in v.10 — love/faithfulness and righteousness/peace — shows the covenant virtues as interlocking pairs rather than separate items.

### 7.6 Joh 14:27 — Christ's personal peace-giving
*"Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you. Not as the world gives do I give to you. Let not your hearts be troubled, neither let them be afraid."*

**Contribution:** The explicit contrast with the world's peace-giving is the analytically decisive element. The world gives peace conditionally, circumstantially, and temporarily — peace as the absence of external trouble. Christ's peace is not defined by external circumstances but by his personal bestowal. "My peace" — this is the peace that belongs to Christ, given to the disciples as a possession. The peace is given ahead of the cross (Joh 14 is the farewell discourse) — it is not post-trouble peace but peace given into the trouble. The direction is counter-intuitive and programme-significant: peace precedes the tribulation that would seem to preclude it.

**CRL activation:** Connects directly to Fear (061) — "neither let them be afraid." Peace and fear occupy the same inner space; Christ's peace displaces fear rather than accompanying it.

### 7.7 Col 1:20 — Peace made by blood
*"And through him to reconcile to himself all things, whether on earth or in heaven, making peace (eirēnopoieō) by the blood of his cross."*

**Contribution (sacrificial mechanism — unexpected register):** This verse is structurally decisive for the registry's coherence. The peace-making verb (eirēnopoieō — only NT occurrence of this verb in cosmic scope) enacts what the peace offering (H8002, 84 OT verses) pointed toward: a sacrificial act that restores wholeness between the worshipper and God. The OT peace offering was the shared meal of restored relationship; the cross is the ultimate peace offering — the blood that reconciles "all things, whether on earth or in heaven." The scope is cosmic: not merely individual peace but the reconciliation of the entire created order to its maker. This is shalom as cosmic restoration, not merely personal equilibrium.

**CRL activation:** Peace-Righteousness connection confirmed (reconciliation presupposes both). Peace-Guilt connection (reconciliation addresses the breach). Connects to the SHALAM root (sha.lem: to complete, to pay/restore) — the cross is the ultimate sha.lem act.

### 7.8 1 Ki 19:12 — The still small voice
*"And after the earthquake a fire, but the Lord was not in the fire. And after the fire the sound of a low whisper (de.ma.mah daqah — thin stillness)."*

**Contribution (unexpected register — somatic and formational):** The full context is essential. Elijah has fled from Jezebel, collapsed in the wilderness, been ministered to by an angel, journeyed to Horeb, and complained of isolation. The divine response is not the resolution of his complaint but the enactment of divine presence in its most intimate form — the whisper after the breaking. Three things emerge: (1) God is not in the spectacular (wind, earthquake, fire) — not because he cannot be, but because the de.ma.mah is the chosen mode. Peace is the form of divine presence that requires the stripping away of the spectacular. (2) The still small voice is the divine peace after devastation — peace on the other side of everything being broken. (3) The formation pattern: the prophet must go through maximum external turbulence before the whispered peace becomes available. This is the inverse of what is expected — peace is not the avoidance of breaking but its aftermath.

## Section 8 — Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field
**Primary:** Positive Psychology — wellbeing, flourishing, eudaimonia.
**Secondary:** Psychophysiology — autonomic nervous system regulation, parasympathetic activation; Neuroscience — threat-detection systems, prefrontal-amygdala dynamics.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1: Eudaimonia vs. hedonia — two wellbeing models (Aristotle, Ryan & Deci)**
Psychological wellbeing research distinguishes hedonic wellbeing (pleasure, positive affect, absence of pain) from eudaimonic wellbeing (flourishing, meaning, virtue, living according to one's deepest nature). The shalom vocabulary maps most closely onto the eudaimonic model: shalom is not pleasant feelings but comprehensive flourishing — the whole person intact, in right relationship, oriented toward their highest good. The convergence with the eudaimonic model is structural and deep.

**Finding 2: Autonomic nervous system and the physiology of calm (Porges — Polyvagal Theory)**
The parasympathetic nervous system (specifically the ventral vagal complex) produces the physiological state associated with safety, social engagement, and calm. This state is characterised by lowered heart rate, relaxed muscles, accessible cognition, and social availability. Polyvagal Theory identifies this as the "safe and social" state — the biological substrate of what the biblical vocabulary calls shalom. The somatic peace vocabulary (sleep, rest, ease, quiet body) maps directly onto this biological state.

**Finding 3: Rumination, worry, and inner peace (Nolen-Hoeksema; Borkovec)**
Research on rumination and worry shows that repetitive, self-focused negative thought patterns are the primary psychologically disruptive processes — they interrupt sleep, reduce wellbeing, and predict depression and anxiety. The biblical peace vocabulary of the stayed mind (Isa 26:3), the returning soul to rest (Psa 116:7), and the guarded heart and mind (Phil 4:7) maps onto this literature. The stayed mind is the opposite of the ruminative mind. The cognitive mechanism identified in Isa 26:3 (trust-anchored cognitive fixation on God) functions as an anti-ruminative posture.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Phenomenological convergence:** The biblical shalom vocabulary and the psychological wellbeing literature share the same general territory — the comprehensive flourishing of the person. Both identify the condition as more than the absence of negative states and more than pleasant feelings. Both describe the whole person as the unit of analysis.

**Mechanistic convergence:** The physiological correlates of biblical peace (rest, sleep, ease, quietness) correspond to parasympathetic activation. The muzzling of chaos (G5392), the stilling of the sea (Mar 4:39), and the quieting by love (Zep 3:17) all describe the shift from sympathetic (threat-activated) to parasympathetic (safe-and-social) states.

**Structural convergence:** The false peace / true peace distinction maps onto the psychological distinction between defensive adaptation (suppression, dissociation, numbing) and genuine integration. "Peace, peace when there is no peace" (Jer 6:14) is the biblical equivalent of psychological pseudo-adjustment — the appearance of calm without the underlying resolution.

**Register divergence — the covenantal ground:** Biblical shalom is covenantally grounded — it is the condition of the person who is in right relationship with the covenant God. Psychological wellbeing is person-centred and relationship-neutral with respect to God. The deepest wellbeing in the biblical account requires a specific relationship; in the psychological account it does not. This is not merely a methodological difference but a substantive one: the biblical claim is that true shalom is structurally unavailable apart from the covenantal relationship.

**Genuine divergence — cosmic peace:** Col 1:20 — "making peace by the blood of his cross" — is the reconciliation of all things in heaven and earth. There is no psychological category for cosmic reconciliation. Psychology addresses the person and the social system; it does not address the reconciliation of the created order to its creator. The cosmic scope of NT eirēnē exceeds every psychological framework.

**Reductive divergence:** Psychology can describe the benefits of inner peace (lower cortisol, better sleep, stronger relationships, reduced depression). The biblical account does not primarily justify peace by its benefits but by its nature as the gift of the God who is himself Peace (YHWH-Shalom). Explaining shalom by its adaptive utility misses the theological claim that peace is appropriate because it is the condition of the creature in right relationship with the Creator. Psychological peace and biblical shalom overlap phenomenologically; they diverge at the ground.

**Genuine divergence — false peace as prophetic category:** The prophetic contra-pattern (Jer 6:14, 8:11 — "Peace, peace when there is no peace") has no psychological equivalent as a moral category. Psychology can describe denial or suppression; it does not name the diagnostic error of a false prophet as a moral failure of the same category. The biblical false peace is not merely a cognitive distortion but a lie in a covenantal context — a word spoken by one responsible for truth that misleads the community about its actual condition before God.

Characterised by: sha.lem (whole heart), covenant peace (brit shalom), rest given by God (nu.ach), righteousness producing peace (Isa 32:17), mind stayed on God (Isa 26:3), the peace of God guarding (Phil 4:7).

Transformation dynamic: True shalom is given, not manufactured. The transformation pathway is covenantal (sacrifice restoring relationship), cognitive (the stayed mind receiving peace), and relational (Christ giving his own peace). ### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Conceptual Word Register candidates:**

1. *Wholeness* — The English word most directly equivalent to shalom's core semantic content. But "wholeness" in English is primarily psychological/holistic and lacks shalom's covenantal, sacrificial, and eschatological dimensions. The biblical concept requires the full inventory — completeness (sha.lem), covenant peace (brit shalom), rest-giving (nu.ach), and divine naming (YHWH-Shalom) — to be fully rendered. "Wholeness" is a partial translation; shalom exceeds it in every direction. This is potentially a distinct entry in the Conceptual Word Register with shalom as its primary biblical instantiation.

2. *Reconciliation* — The NT peace vocabulary (eirēnopoieō, G1517; the grace-peace formula; Col 1:20) maps closely onto reconciliation — the restoration of a broken relationship to wholeness. But reconciliation is a relational term; shalom is comprehensive (relational, inner, somatic, cosmic). Reconciliation is a subset of shalom. Anchor registry: Peace (117), cross-reference to Guilt (073), Repentance (135).

3. *Flourishing* — The eudaimonic wellbeing parallel. "Flourishing" captures the positive fullness of shalom better than "peace" in English (which primarily denotes absence of conflict). But the eudaimonic concept lacks the covenantal grounding, the sacrifice mechanism, and the divine naming. Flourishing is shalom seen from the human output side; shalom names the same condition from the divine gift side. Anchor registry: Peace (117); cross-reference to Joy (097), Hope (078).

**Emerging cross-word patterns:**

1. **The righteousness-peace axis:** Isa 32:17, Psa 85:10, Jam 3:18 — "a harvest of righteousness is sown in peace by those who make peace." Righteousness and peace are the paired virtues of the covenant community. They cannot exist apart: righteousness without peace is harsh; peace without righteousness is false. Session D should examine whether this pair constitutes a structural binary in the same way the patience-anger polarity does in registry 116. Possible programme-level law: where righteousness is absent, true peace is impossible (Isa 48:22; 57:21 — "no peace for the wicked").

2. **The peace-fear-trust complex:** Judg 6:23 (peace displacing fear), Isa 26:3 (trust anchoring peace), Joh 14:27 (peace given into fear), Phil 4:7 (peace guarding against anxiety) — these verses establish a three-part complex: trust → peace → absence of fear. Peace is the middle term that trust produces and that fear displaces. This links peace directly to Fear (061) and — through the trust mechanism — to Hope (078) and Faith (anticipated registry). Session D should examine whether trust/faith is the foundational disposition that generates both hope and peace.

3. **The false-peace pattern as a prophetic category:** Jer 6:14; 8:11; Eze 13:10, 16; Mic 3:5 — "Peace when there is no peace" is a repeated prophetic indictment specifically against prophets and priests who diagnose health where there is disease. This is not merely a cognitive error but a moral failure in a covenantal context. The false-peace pattern appears to be unique to the peace vocabulary — no other word in the programme has an equivalent false-word contra-pattern with this specific structural form. Session D should examine whether the false-[virtue] pattern is a broader structural feature (false peace, false hope, false comfort) or whether it is specific to peace.

**Programme-level structural hypotheses:**

1. **Peace as the integrative virtue:** The peace vocabulary is the broadest in the programme, spanning wholeness (sha.lem), rest (nu.ach), silence (da.mam), blessing (ba.rakh), offering (she.lem), and divine naming (YHWH-Shalom). No other word in the programme touches as many domains. This suggests that shalom may be the integrative term for the entire soul word vocabulary — the condition that exists when all the other characteristics are properly ordered. If humility is the positional virtue (creature before God) and patience is the temporal virtue (creature through time), shalom may be the comprehensive virtue (creature in wholeness). Session D should test whether shalom functions as an integrating summary of the programme's findings.

2. **The covenant of peace as eschatological category:** Eze 34:25 and 37:26 — "covenant of peace" as eschatological promise; Isa 54:10 — "my covenant of peace shall not be removed"; Num 25:12 — "my covenant of peace" to Phinehas. The brit shalom is not merely a present covenantal condition but a future eschatological promise. The ultimate shalom is the covenant that cannot be broken — the eternal condition of wholeness in the new creation. Session D should examine whether the eschatological peace (Isa 9:7 — "of the increase of his peace there will be no end") is structurally distinct from the present peace or its ultimate fulfilment.
