## bdeluktos — Tit 1:16

**Reference:** Tit 1:16
**Term:** bdeluktos (G0947)
**Classification:** CORE
**Genre:** epistle
**Speaker or subject:** The author addressing characteristics of false teachers in Crete; the persons described are those who claim to know God.
**Situation:** The letter addresses the problem of those who claim God-knowledge but whose conduct denies it. The surrounding passage concerns appointing elders who hold firm to sound teaching and rebuke those who contradict it. The persons described are teachers whose inner profession and outward behaviour are in direct contradiction.
**The inner-being moment:** The verse records that these persons are detestable (bdeluktos), disobedient, and unfit for any good work — the inner claim of God-knowledge and the outer conduct of denial are named as producing a state of abominableness in the person.
**What the person does with the inner state:** The persons deny God by their works; no inner response to or awareness of their state is recorded in the surrounding passage.
**Bundling note:** Single verse.

## Situation and Genre Summary

The CORE verses in this word cluster are distributed primarily across wisdom literature (Proverbs, Psalms, Job, Ecclesiastes) and prophecy (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Amos, Micah, Malachi, Hosea), with epistle and narrative contributing smaller numbers. The wisdom distribution is notable: the Proverbs corpus provides the densest concentration of to.e.vah verses with explicit inner-being referents (heart, thoughts, orientation), and the Psalms provide the clearest inner-being uses of ta.av and za.am in both first-person and divine-subject registers. Prophecy provides the largest total verse count for to.e.vah, but many prophetic verses operate in extended registers (corporate, divine-relational, national) rather than individual inner-being referents.

The range of human situations in the CORE verses includes: moral self-assessment (Eze 6:9; Eze 36:31), inner contradiction between profession and conduct (Tit 1:16; Rom 2:22), extreme social isolation and rejection by companions (Job 19:19; Job 30:10), grief over corporate moral failure (Eze 9:4), suffering from the consequences of moral failure (Psa 38:5), inner transformation of a moral category through divine instruction (Act 10:28), the fool's inner orientation making moral change repugnant (Pro 13:19), the heart as a container of abominations (Pro 26:25), and the inner orientation of rulers toward justice (Mic 3:9; Amo 5:10). The recurring situation across multiple terms is the gap between inner orientation and expected or required conduct.

The voice distribution across the CORE verse set is mixed. The first-person voice (the sufferer or worshipper naming their own or God's inner state) is primary in the Psalms and Job laments (Psa 7:11; Psa 119:163; Job 9:31; Psa 38:5). The third-person observer voice naming another person's state is primary in the Proverbs corpus (Pro 15:26; Pro 16:5; Pro 26:25) and in prophetic indictment passages (Jer 6:15; Mic 3:9; Amos 5:10). Narrative description accounts for the historical and epistolary CORE verses (1Ch 21:6; Act 10:28; Rom 2:22). No single voice dominates the full CORE set; the distribution is genuinely broad across all three registers.

## Peripheral Verse Flags

The following PERIPHERAL verses warrant notation for Phase 2 attention:

Eze 4:14 (pig.gul): Ezekiel's protestation against eating tainted meat is a first-person declaration of personal food purity practice sustained from youth; Phase 2 may find the inner-being dimension of personal purity conviction worth examining here even though the term names a food category.

Eze 8:10 (she.qets): The engraved images of creeping things and idols on the temple wall, which Ezekiel is shown in vision, are classified peripheral because the verse describes cultic objects; however the visionary context (God showing Ezekiel what the elders are doing secretly) may be relevant to Phase 2 analysis of inner secret worship practices.

Lev 18:22 and related Leviticus sexual-ethics passages (to.e.vah): These verses are classified peripheral but several describe acts the law names as abomination that have inner-moral orientation (desire, choice) as their prerequisite; Phase 2 may find the relationship between prohibited act and the inner orientation that produces it relevant.

No other PERIPHERAL verses in this word cluster contain situational features that warrant Phase 2 flagging beyond what the verse classification captures.
