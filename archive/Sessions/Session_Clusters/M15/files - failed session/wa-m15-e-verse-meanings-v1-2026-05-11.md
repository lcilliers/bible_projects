## Observations — M15-E

**OBS-E-001 — Jer 29:11 is a defining verse for divine purposive planning**
"For I know the plans I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope." This verse uses cha.shav for God's deliberate inner purposive planning for his people — not reactive but formed with specific content (welfare, hope, future). This is one of the clearest statements of divine purposing in the OT. It deserves explicit attention in Session D synthesis alongside Isa 14:24-27 and the za.mam divine purposing verses.

**OBS-E-002 — za.mam (847-002): divine purposing that does not relent**
Three verses (Jer 4:28, Jer 51:12, Lam 2:17) use za.mam for God's resolute purpose that is formed and executed without relenting. In contrast to cha.shav (which in several verses is held open to conditional relenting), za.mam names the irreversible form of divine purposing. The distinction between conditional and unconditional divine purposing is a significant finding — two different inner-being dynamics in the divine will.

**OBS-E-003 — Gen 50:20 is the definitive parallel-purposing verse**
"You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good." Both the brothers' evil purposing and God's redemptive purposing use the same word (cha.shav). This verse makes explicit what is implicit in many other texts: divine and human deliberate purposing can act through the same events toward opposite ends. It is the programme's clearest statement of parallel inner purposing.

**OBS-E-004 — Isa 9:6 (Wonderful Counselor) is the messianic summit of the ya.ats group**
The name Wonderful Counselor (ya.ats) names the Messiah as the supreme expression of divine counsel — inner purposive wisdom directed perfectly toward those governed. This is the telos of the ya.ats trajectory from human counsel through divine counsel to messianic counsel.

**OBS-E-005 — Luk 22:42 (boulomai): the tension between divine and human willing in Gethsemane**
"If you are willing, remove this cup... not my will, but yours, be done." Boulomai names both Christ's human purposive desire (remove the cup) and the Father's sovereign will. This is the programme's most significant NT statement of willing in tension — the inner purposing of the incarnate Son submitted to the Father. The verse is assigned to 509-002 (God's/Christ's inner purposive will), which fits the reference to the Father's will, but the human willing of Christ is equally present.

**OBS-E-006 — 2Pe 3:9 (boulomai): the universality of divine willing**
"Not wishing that any should perish, but that all should reach repentance." Boulomai here names the scope of divine wanting — the divine inner purpose directed toward all. One of the NT's clearest statements of the reach of divine will. Warrants attention in Session D alongside other divine-purposing material.

**OBS-E-007 — Jon 1:4 (cha.shav): purposive language applied to an inanimate storm**
"The ship threatened to break up" (literally: cha.shav — the ship thought/devised to break). This is an unusual anthropomorphising use of cha.shav — purposive deliberative language applied to the storm's violence. It is an outlier within the 3334-002 group (purposive planning) and warrants flagging as a distinctive use.

**OBS-E-008 — Psa 40:17 (cha.shav): God taking thought for the needy**
"The Lord takes thought for me." Cha.shav here names God's purposive attending to the poor and needy psalmist — divine inner purposing directed toward care. This positive use of cha.shav (3334-002) demonstrates that the same deliberate inner purposing that schemes against others (3334-001) is also used for the God who forms purposive care for the vulnerable.

**OBS-E-009 — VCG 3578-A and 3578-B (shit) are correctly split**
The two shit VCGs correctly distinguish: (a) the active deliberate attending (Psa 13:2 — taking counsel in my soul; Psa 48:13 — consider well the ramparts; Pro 24:32 — I considered it; Jer 31:21 — consider the highway), and (b) the failure or suppression of attending (Exo 7:23 — Pharaoh did not take it to heart; 1Sa 4:20 — she did not pay attention; 2Sa 13:20 — do not take this to heart). The split is appropriate and meaningful.
