## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**T5.1.1** — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change condition, orientation, or both?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. God's righteousness transforms the person who genuinely encounters it — by changing orientation first. Felix's alarm (Act 24:25, VSG M26-A1-006) shows an immediate re-orientation of inner posture (fear, recognition) without sustained transformation, because he chose not to receive the standard. The person who genuinely submits to God's righteousness — "you are righteous; we have sinned" (Neh 9:33; Dan 9:7, VSG M26-A1-006) — undergoes an orientation change that produces the condition for all other transformation: the acknowledgment that God's standard is the measure.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Both — condition and orientation. The condition changes: the person moves from non-righteous to righteous (or the reverse in Ezekiel's forfeiture model, Eze 18:24, VSG M26-A2-002). The orientation changes: the righteous person is oriented toward God (A2-014), toward others (A2-012), toward the future (A2-017). The NT transformation is the more comprehensive one: "born of him" (1Jo 2:29, VSG M26-A2-014) names a new-birth transformation — the deepest possible change of condition alongside a complete re-orientation of the person.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Primarily orientation. The change produced by uprightness is the inner directional alignment of the person toward what is straight. The path metaphor (Pro 2:13; 4:11, VSG 1211-002) is an orientation metaphor — the person is turned toward the path of uprightness. The condition that results (the person who walks in uprightness, Pro 14:2) is the sustained expression of the orientation change.

**[C — Justification]** E. Primarily condition — the person's standing before God changes from guilty to righteous. This is the most explicit condition-change in the cluster. However, it produces a secondary orientation change: the freed and justified person is oriented toward God as heir and toward continued righteous living ("go, and from now on sin no more," Joh 8:11; VSG 3194-001 context). The condition change (standing before God) precedes and enables the orientation change (heir-living).

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Primarily orientation — the transformation D produces is the re-orientation of the wronged person away from autonomous vengeance toward trust in God. "Leave it to the wrath of God" (Rom 12:19, VSG 6841-002) is an orientation change: the person's inner posture toward injustice is transformed from self-execution to entrusting. This orientation change produces the condition of non-retaliation.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Both. Freedom from condemnation produces a condition change (from under-verdict to freed) and an orientation change (from guilt-weight to forward-movement: "go, and sin no more," Joh 8:11). The self-condemnation of autokatakritos (Tit 3:11, VSG 4848-001) is itself a condition — the condition of being under the self-verdict. Transformation requires something external to break that self-reinforcing condition.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Primarily orientation — the reckoning act re-orients the person toward the declared reality. "Count yourselves dead to sin and alive to God" (Rom 6:11, VSG 3410-001) — the counting changes how the person is oriented toward their own condition (dead to sin) and toward God (alive). This is an orientation change applied to an existing condition (the justified standing of C).

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Condition change — sustained wickedness changes the person's condition: the heart hardens (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001), the person accumulates wrath, the capacity to repent diminishes. This is a condition change in the wrong direction: the person becomes more firmly established in wickedness over time.

**[CLUSTER — T5.1]** E. The cluster produces transformation in both condition and orientation — but the sequence matters. The condition change of justification (C) precedes and enables the orientation changes (A2, B, D, F). The cluster's transformative arc is: God's righteousness proclaimed → encounter produces recognition (A1) → faith-response receives justification (C) → condition changed → orientation transforms throughout the inner life (A2 direction toward God and others, B uprightness-orientation, D trust-orientation, F reckoning-orientation). The G-path runs in reverse: wickedness hardens condition and progressively distorts orientation.

---

**T5.1.2** — Is the transformation reversible or irreversible?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Mixed in the verse evidence. The Ezekiel stream (A2-002) presents the state as explicitly reversible in both directions — the righteous can turn to wickedness and forfeit, the wicked can turn to righteousness and receive life (Eze 18:21–27; 33:12–19). This is the most explicit statement of reversibility in the cluster. The stability stream (A2-013: "the root of the righteous will never be moved," Pro 12:3) presents a different picture — the righteous person is established and stable. The NT material introduces a new mode: the righteousness conferred through the new birth (1Jo 2:29) and constituted in Christ (2Cor 5:21) is presented as secure — not as perpetually renegotiable. The tension between the Ezekiel reversibility and the NT security is unresolved in the verse evidence alone.

**[C — Justification]** E. Presented as irreversible in the NT material — the justified person is an heir (Tit 3:7), is freed from condemnation through Christ's intercession (Rom 8:34, VSG 4846-002), stands in a new status before God. Gal 5:4 ("severed from Christ, you who would be justified by the law; you have fallen away from grace") implies that turning away from the basis of justification does produce a change in standing — suggesting some reversibility in the wrong volitional direction. The irreversibility is secure when the faith-basis is maintained; it is threatened by the abandonment of that basis.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The hardening of the heart (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001) implies increasing irreversibility in the wickedness-direction: the heart becomes progressively harder, suggesting that the capacity for repentance is not indefinitely available. The eschatological crystallisation in Rev 22:11 ("let the evildoer still do evil... let the righteous still do right") presents the final state as irreversible — moral identity is fixed at the eschatological endpoint.

**[CLUSTER]** The verse evidence holds a genuine tension: the OT (Ezekiel) presents moral standing as reversible in both directions; the NT presents the justified standing as secure through Christ's intercession; the eschatological endpoint presents both as irreversible. The tension is not resolved by the verse evidence — it is part of what the programme should document rather than smooth over.

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — the Ezekiel stream (A2-002) describes a before/during/after sequence with exceptional clarity. Before: the person is righteous (the righteous person, tsad.diq). During: the turning point — the person "turns away from righteousness and does injustice" (Eze 18:24) — the volitional moment of departure. After: the former righteous deeds are not remembered; the person is assessed by their current state. The reversal sequence is equally clear: before: wicked. During: turning from wickedness. After: "he shall surely live" (Eze 18:21–22). The sequence is stark and explicit.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — before: under the weight of judgment (the condition that makes justification necessary); during: the act of faith that receives justification; after: the new standing of heir-status and freedom from condemnation (Tit 3:7). The before-during-after of justification maps directly onto the cluster's wider arc.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — before: the wrong has been done, the person bears the injustice. During: the period of the cry — persistent appeal to God for justice (Luk 18:3–8, VSG 944-001). After: God gives justice speedily (Luk 18:8) — the resolution. The sequence of D is: wrong endured → cry sustained → justice given.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — before: the person is either under external verdict or under self-verdict. During: the encounter with freedom — "has no one condemned you?" (Joh 8:10, VSG 4846-002). After: "neither do I condemn you; go, and from now on sin no more" (Joh 8:11) — the freed person is sent forward. The sequence: under verdict → encounter with the one who does not condemn → freed and commissioned.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — before: the person has received the declared reality of justification (through C). During: the act of reckoning — counting themselves dead to sin, alive to God (Rom 6:11). After: the consequent action — not letting sin reign (Rom 6:12). The sequence: declared reality → deliberate counting → changed behavioral orientation.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — the hardening sequence in Rom 2:5 (VSG 3207-001): before: the opportunity to repent. During: the continued refusal — "hard and impenitent heart." After: the accumulated wrath. The sequence: refusal → hardening → accumulation of judgment.

**[CLUSTER — T5.2]** E. The cluster consistently provides before-during-after sequences across all sub-groups. The most explicit sequences are: the Ezekiel turning sequence (A2), the cry-and-response sequence (D), the encounter-and-freedom sequence (E), and the declared-reality-to-reckoned-action sequence (F). Together these sequences reveal that righteousness in M26 is not a static state — it is a dynamic reality that moves through the person in recognizable inner-state sequences.

---

**T5.2.2** — What do the sequences reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The sequences reveal a consistent pattern: righteousness is received or maintained through active volitional engagement at the turning-point of the sequence (the decision to receive justification by faith, to cry out rather than execute vengeance personally, to reckon from the declared reality). The turning point is always where the volitional faculty is most engaged. The before-and-after states are condition-changes; the during is the volitional moment that determines which direction the change runs.

---

**T5.2.3** — If silent on sequence, note explicitly.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. B shows a partial sequence — the path of uprightness can be entered (taught and led in, Pro 4:11) or forsaken (Pro 2:13). The sequence is: formation → walking in uprightness → possibility of forsaking. The before/during/after is present but less sharply articulated than in A2 or C.

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1 addresses the divine attribute and does not provide a human inner-state sequence as such. The closest is the pattern of acknowledgment (A1-006: encounter → recognition → confession) but this is contextual rather than a developmental sequence within the person.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Proclamation and encounter — God's righteousness reaches the person through the declared word (A1-005: proclaimed, declared, witnessed to) and through direct encounter with the standard (A1-006: Felix's alarm through Paul's reasoning). The mechanism is revelatory: the standard is made known and the person is confronted by it.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Multiple mechanisms: (1) new birth — "born of him" (1Jo 2:29) — sovereign transformative action from God; (2) faith-reception — receiving the credited righteousness through believing God (Gen 15:6/Rom 4:3); (3) formation through instruction — "I have taught you the way of wisdom; I have led you in the paths of uprightness" (Pro 4:11, VSG 1211-002, B-adjacent but directly relevant); (4) practice and habituation — "everyone who practices righteousness" (1Jo 2:29) — the sustained practice of righteous living deepens the condition.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Formation through instruction and the internalization of the fear of the Lord. "I have led you in the paths of uprightness" (Pro 4:11) — the mechanism is teaching and leading, resulting in the person who walks in the path. The fear of the Lord (Pro 14:2: "whoever walks in uprightness fears the Lord") suggests that the mechanism in B includes an encounter with divine holiness that re-orients the inner person toward uprightness.

**[C — Justification]** E. Declarative — the mechanism is the divine verdict pronounced over the person. God declares, the person receives by faith, the standing changes. The mechanism is not gradual formation but instantaneous declaration. The change in standing is not produced by the person's improvement but by God's declaration received through faith.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Petition and response — the mechanism of D is the cry and the divine answer. The person cries out persistently (Luk 18:3–8, VSG 944-001); God responds to the persistent cry ("he will give justice to them speedily," Luk 18:8). The mechanism is prayer-and-response — the divine response to the human petition.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Encounter and declaration — the mechanism of freedom from condemnation is the personal encounter with the one who does not condemn (Joh 8:10–11, VSG 4846-002) and the declaration of freedom. The mechanism is judicial-declarative, analogous to C: the verdict changes the standing.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Deliberate cognitive act — the mechanism of F is the commanded deliberation: "count yourselves dead to sin" (Rom 6:11). The mechanism is not passive but active — the person is instructed to perform a specific inner cognitive-volitional act that then produces a changed behavioral orientation (Rom 6:12). The mechanism is instruction → deliberate inner act → changed action.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Gradual hardening through sustained refusal — the mechanism of wickedness's development in the person is not sudden but progressive: the heart becomes hard through repeated refusal (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001). The mechanism is incremental — each act of refusal makes the next refusal more natural and the capacity for repentance more attenuated.

**[CLUSTER — T5.3]** E. The cluster deploys several distinct mechanisms: (1) sovereign new birth (A2); (2) declarative verdict received by faith (C, E); (3) revelatory proclamation and encounter (A1); (4) petition and divine response (D); (5) commanded deliberate inner act (F); (6) formation through instruction (A2, B); (7) gradual hardening (G). The diversity of mechanisms is itself a finding: righteousness enters and shapes the person through many channels, not one. The primary mechanism in the NT material is declarative-received-by-faith (C); the primary mechanism in the OT wisdom material is formative-through-instruction (B); and the developmental mechanism in the negative direction is gradual hardening (G).

---

**T5.3.2** — Does the evidence distinguish mechanisms in different contexts?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Yes — the declarative mechanism (C: justification) is specifically NT in its fullest expression; the formative mechanism (B: instruction in uprightness) is primarily OT wisdom material; the sovereign new-birth mechanism (A2: 1Jo 2:29) is NT. The contexts produce different dominant mechanisms, but the mechanisms are not mutually exclusive — the justified person is also formed through practice (A2: "everyone who practices righteousness") and through the reckoning instruction (F).

---

**T5.3.3** — If silent on mechanism, note explicitly.

All sub-groups show evidence of mechanism. No silence to record.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — suffering is a persistent context for righteousness in A2. The stability material (A2-013) is explicitly set against the backdrop of pressure and difficulty: "the righteous falls seven times and rises again" (Pro 24:16, VSG M26-A2-013); "the righteous holds to his way and grows stronger" (Job 17:9, VSG M26-A2-013). The righteous person under opposition (A2-016: the righteous targeted by the wicked) is a recurring figure across Psalms and Proverbs. Righteousness does not protect the person from suffering — it determines how they respond to and grow through it.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — D arises from suffering: the cry for justice (944-001) presupposes that suffering has occurred (the widow is being wronged, the martyrs have been killed, Rev 6:10, VSG 944-001). D is specifically the characteristic that operates when suffering is produced by injustice.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the person under the weight of condemnation (4846-001) is in a state of suffering — the judicial verdict produces an inner-being burden. The experience of being condemned (unjustly) is the suffering condition against which the freedom-from-condemnation verses (4846-002) are set.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — explicitly. "I consider that the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us" (Rom 8:18, VSG 3410-001). The reckoning faculty is directly applied to suffering — the act of weighing present suffering against future glory is the primary application of the reckoning faculty in its comparative mode. F is the characteristic most directly engaged with suffering in the cluster.

**[CLUSTER — T5.4]** E. Suffering is a persistent context across the cluster. The cluster's relationship to suffering is characterised by: (1) righteousness persisting through suffering without being eliminated by it (A2-013); (2) suffering produced by injustice generating the cry for justice (D); (3) the weight of condemnation as a form of suffering resolved by freedom from verdict (E); and (4) the reckoning faculty applied specifically to suffering as its primary object of comparative assessment (F). Righteousness does not remove suffering from the believer's experience — it provides the orientation, resilience, and reckoning capacity needed to traverse it.

---

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Tests and reveals. The stability material (A2-013) is explicitly about righteousness under testing: "God establishes the righteous through testing — you who test the minds and hearts" (Psa 7:9, VSG M26-A2-013). Suffering tests whether righteousness is a governing state or a fair-weather quality; those who hold to their righteousness under pressure are revealed as genuinely righteous. Job holds to his way and grows stronger (Job 17:9) — the suffering deepens rather than destroys.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Suffering is the raw material that the reckoning faculty is applied to — the faculty that weighs suffering against glory (Rom 8:18) is strengthened through the practice of that reckoning. The person who learns to weigh suffering correctly develops a more robust reckoning capacity.

**[CLUSTER]** E. The cluster presents suffering as the testing and revealing context for righteousness — not its producer, but its refiner. The righteous person who falls seven times and rises (A2-013) is revealed by the pattern of rising, not by the falling. Suffering does not generate righteousness ex nihilo — it reveals and deepens what is already there.

---

**T5.4.3** — If silent on suffering, note explicitly.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. The B verse evidence does not directly engage suffering or affliction. The uprightness material describes the disposition and its expression without placing them in the context of suffering. Silence noted.

**[C — Justification]** S. Justification itself is not described in relation to suffering — it changes the standing that enables the person to traverse suffering, but the suffering context belongs to other sub-groups. Silence noted.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "the fruit of righteousness comes through Jesus Christ, to the glory and praise of God" (Phili 1:11, VSG M26-A2-007). The fruit-metaphor implies a developmental process: the person grows in righteousness, producing increasing fruit. The practiced righteousness of 950-002 ("everyone who practices righteousness") names the habitual exercise of righteousness as a formative practice. "The fine linen is the righteous deeds of the saints" (Rev 19:8, VSG M26-A2-017/1099-001): the bride's garment is the accumulated righteous living of the sanctified community — righteousness is the substance of sanctification's output.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — the path of uprightness is walked over time (Pro 4:11; Pro 14:2, VSG 1211-002). Uprightness is both a direction and a practice: the person does not arrive at uprightness; they walk in it. The walk is the formation process. "Then you will understand righteousness and justice and equity, every good path" (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002) — understanding deepens with the walk: the process of formation produces increasing discernment.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — justification establishes the platform from which sanctification proceeds. "Being justified by his grace we might become heirs" (Tit 3:7) — the becoming of heir-identity is a developmental process. Justification does not complete sanctification — it establishes the conditions under which sanctification can proceed. The cluster shows C as the foundation and A2 (practiced righteousness) as the ongoing formation process built on it.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the reckoning faculty is formative when exercised consistently. "Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Rom 12:2, adjacent context to the reckoning material) — the renewal of the mind through which sanctification occurs is the same faculty that reckons. The sustained exercise of right reckoning is itself a formative practice.

**[CLUSTER — T5.5]** E. Righteousness participates in the longer arc of sanctification through: (1) practiced righteousness as habitual formation (A2); (2) the walk of uprightness as sustained directional formation (B); (3) the platform of justified standing that makes ongoing formation possible (C); and (4) the reckoning faculty as the cognitive-formative mechanism applied over time (F). The cluster presents a coherent account of sanctification: declared standing (C) → reckoned identity (F) → practiced righteousness (A2) → accumulated righteous character (Rev 19:8). This is the formation arc of the cluster.

---

**T5.5.2** — What role does this characteristic play in that arc?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness plays both foundation and content roles in sanctification. As foundation: the declared standing of C establishes the relational position from which formation proceeds. As content: practiced righteousness (A2-002 positive stream, 950-002) is what formation produces — the person who grows in sanctification grows in the practice of righteousness. Righteousness is simultaneously where formation begins (as received condition) and where it is heading (as practiced and accumulated character).

---

**T5.5.3** — If silent on formation/sanctification, note explicitly.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** S. D's verse evidence does not directly address formation and sanctification. The character change produced by entrusting vengeance to God (the non-retaliatory posture of Rom 12:19 development) is addressed in T5.3, but the explicit formation-arc connection is not present in D's verse evidence. Silence noted.

**[E — Condemnation]** S. The freedom from condemnation enables formation but is not itself described in the formation-arc terms. Silence noted.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — "to bring in everlasting righteousness" (Dan 9:24, VSG M26-A1-007); the eschatological acclamations of divine righteousness in Revelation (Rev 15:3–4; 16:5; 16:7; 19:2, VSG M26-A1-002: "just and true are your ways"; "your judgments are true and just"). God's righteousness is the standard by which the eschatological judgment is executed and the basis of the eternal order that follows.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "the crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous judge, will award to me on that day" (2Ti 4:8, VSG M26-A2-001). "The fine linen is the righteous deeds of the saints" (Rev 19:8) — the accumulated righteousness of the saints is their eschatological identity and adornment. "In righteousness you shall be established... far from oppression, for you shall not fear" (Isa 54:14, VSG M26-A2-013) — the eschatological security of the righteous is the fullness of what righteousness promises but only partly delivers in the present order.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7). The heir-status established by justification is oriented toward its eschatological fullness: inheritance, eternal life, the kingdom. Justification in the present is the first-fruits of the eschatological verdict that will be publicly declared at the final judgment.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the eschatological cry of the martyrs (Rev 6:10, VSG 944-001) and the eschatological execution of divine justice in Rev 19:2 (VSG 944-002) are the fullest expressions of D in the cluster. Avenging justice reaches its fullness at the eschatological judgment when all accumulated injustice is finally addressed.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the "punishment of eternal destruction" (2Th 1:9, VSG 949-001) and the "eternal fire" (Jude 7, VSG 949-001) locate the fullness of condemnation in the eschatological future. Condemnation in the present is a preview and warning; its fullness is at the final judgment.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — "the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed to us" (Rom 8:18, VSG 3410-001). The reckoning faculty is explicitly eschatologically oriented: the act of weighing present suffering against future glory is an act of inner eschatological orientation. The faculty is exercised in the present toward what is coming.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — Rev 22:11 (VSG M26-G): "let the evildoer still do evil, and the filthy still be filthy, and the righteous still do right, and the holy still be holy." The eschatological crystallisation of moral identity — wickedness reaches its terminus in permanent exclusion from the new order.

**[CLUSTER — T5.6]** E. Every sub-group in M26 has an eschatological trajectory. This is one of the most consistently eschatological clusters in the programme. The pattern: God's righteousness is the standard of the eschatological judgment and the eternal order (A1); human righteousness reaches its fullness in the bride's garment and the crown (A2); justification's heir-status reaches its fullness in inheritance (C); avenging justice reaches its fullness in the final divine reckoning of all injustice (D); condemnation reaches its fullness in eternal punishment (E); the reckoning faculty's comparative weighing reaches its fullness when the glory that is to be revealed becomes present reality (F); wickedness reaches its terminus in permanent exclusion (G). The cluster is oriented throughout toward a final state in which all the partial and temporary modes of righteousness in this age give way to the complete and permanent.

---

**T5.6.2** — What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The present experience of righteousness in M26 is consistently framed as anticipatory — it is the first-fruits of something larger and more complete. The justified person is an heir-in-waiting (C: Tit 3:7); the righteous person's crown is coming (A2: 2Ti 4:8); the cry for justice will be answered speedily in God's timing (D: Luk 18:8); the reckoning of present suffering against future glory makes the future present in the inner life (F: Rom 8:18). The present is the already of what is not yet. The present experience anticipates: full public vindication, complete righteous standing, the final execution of all justice, and the permanent order of righteousness in the new creation.

---

**T5.6.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

No silence on eschatological trajectory. All sub-groups: evidenced.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**T6.1.1** — Which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently alongside this one?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Faithfulness (e.mu.nah, H0530) — paired with righteousness in Deu 32:4 ("A God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he") and in the covenant material (A1-003: Zec 8:8; Psa 85:10). Steadfast love (che.sed) — consistently paired with righteousness in the covenant context (A1-003: Psa 85:10; Jer 9:24). Salvation (ye.shu.ah) — travels inseparably with righteousness in Isaiah (Isa 45:8; 46:13; 51:5, VSG M26-A1-004). Holiness (qo.desh, H6944) — paired in the doxological acclamations of Revelation (Rev 15:4, VSG M26-A1-002: "who will not fear... for you alone are holy"). ### T6.5 — Distinctions

**T6.5.1** — Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what precisely distinguishes them?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness vs. Holiness]** E. Holiness (H6944 qo.desh; G0040 hagios) is the characteristic most closely adjacent to righteousness in its governing-state mode. Both name a total inner orientation of the person — holiness toward the God who is holy, righteousness toward the God who is righteous. The distinction: holiness is primarily a separateness quality (set apart, consecrated, belonging to God's own domain); righteousness is primarily a conformity-to-standard quality (being right, conforming to what is morally right). A person can be holy in the sense of being set apart for God without specifically being righteous in the sense of moral conformity to the standard. In practice they co-occur (the holy person is also righteous; the righteous person is also holy), but they are conceptually distinct.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness vs. Faithfulness (e.mu.nah/pistis)]** E. Faithfulness is consistently paired with righteousness (Deu 32:4; Psa 85:10) — they are related but distinct. Faithfulness names reliability, steadfastness, covenant-keeping through time; righteousness names moral conformity to the right standard. Faithfulness is temporal and relational (staying true over time to a commitment); righteousness is moral and relational (being right in relation to the standard). A person can be faithful (reliable) without being righteous (morally conforming); a person can be righteous (morally right) in a specific instance without being faithful (sustained over time).

**[F — Moral Reckoning vs. Cognition generally]** E. The reckoning faculty in F is specifically moral-evaluative cognition — it differs from general cognition in that its object is always moral worth, moral standing, or moral reality. General cognition includes moral and non-moral objects; logizomai in the programme's F-context is specifically about assigning moral worth and living from that assignment. The distinction is in the object and outcome of the cognitive act, not in the cognitive process itself.

---

**T6.5.2** — Where apparent overlap exists, what is the precise boundary?

**[Righteousness vs. Justice (mish.pat)]** E. The most important boundary question for M26. Righteousness (ts.d.q/dik-) names the quality or state of conformity to right; justice (mish.pat) names the act or standard of judgment. Righteousness is primarily a condition of being; justice is primarily a practice of doing. "Execute justice and righteousness" (Jer 22:3, VSG M26-A2-012) treats them as distinct: righteousness is the inner condition; justice is its enacted expression. The boundary: righteousness is the source-state; justice is the enacted application of that state in judicial and social contexts. They are inseparable in practice but conceptually ordered: righteousness (inner state) precedes justice (enacted application).

---

**T6.5.3** — Is the distinction one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?

**[Righteousness vs. Holiness]** E. Kind — both are governing states but they name different qualities of that state (conformity to moral standard vs. separateness/consecration).

**[Righteousness vs. Faithfulness]** E. Direction and time — faithfulness is temporal-relational (sustained over time); righteousness is moral-conformative (right at the point of assessment). Both are relational but in different directional modes.

**[Righteousness vs. Justice (mish.pat)]** E. Constitutional level — righteousness is the inner governing state; justice is the enacted expression of that state in the social-judicial domain. One level inward from the other.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms and what do their root meanings reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Three root families govern M26:

**Root Family 1 — ts.d.q (צדק):** H6662 tsad.diq (righteous, adjective); H6663 tsa.deq (to be or declare righteous, verb); H6664G/H tse.deq (righteousness, standard, noun); H6666 tse.da.qah (righteousness, quality/act, noun). The root meaning: conformity to a norm or standard; being in the right in relation to the standard. The primary metaphor is relational-judicial: being right *in relation to* something external. This root family governs A1, A2, and C.

**Root Family 2 — y.sh.r (ישר):** H3476 yo.sher (uprightness, noun); H3477 ya.shar (upright, adjective); H3483 ye.sha.rah (uprightness, noun); H4339 me.shar (uprightness, equity, noun); H5229 ne.kho.chah (upright, straight, noun). Greek equivalent: G2118 euthutēs (straightness, uprightness). The root meaning: straightness, levelness, not crooked. The primary metaphor is spatial: a straight line as opposed to a crooked or devious path. This root family governs B.

**Root Family 3 — dik- (δικ-):** G1342 dikaios (just, righteous); G1343 dikaiosunē (righteousness); G1344 dikaioō (to justify, declare righteous); G1345 dikaiōma (righteous act, ordinance); G1347 dikaiōsis (justification); G1349 dikē (justice, punishment); G1341 dikaiokrisia (righteous judgment); G1556 ekdikeō (to avenge); G1557 ekdikēsis (vengeance); G1558 ekdikos (avenger); G1738 endikos (just, deserved). The root meaning: conformity to the standard of what is right; in the judicial context, the verdict in accordance with the standard. This root family governs A1 (NT material), A2 (NT material), C, D, and E.

**Additional terms:** G3049 logizomai (to count, reckon, reason — governs F in its primary mode); H6186B a.rakh (to value, compare, set in order — governs F in its Hebrew dimension); G0515 axioō (to deem worthy — governs F's valuation mode); H7561/H7563 ra.sha (to be wicked/the wicked — governs G's negative pole); H5765/H5766A a.val/a.vel (to act unjustly/injustice — governs G's injustice pole).

**T7.1.2** — What is the grammatical range of the primary term?

**[ts.d.q family]** E. The ts.d.q root covers all major grammatical categories: adjective (tsad.diq — the righteous person or God as righteous), verb (tsa.deq — to be righteous, to declare righteous, to justify), and two distinct nouns (tse.deq — righteousness as standard; tse.da.qah — righteousness as quality, saving act, or gift). The grammatical range reveals that righteousness in Hebrew operates simultaneously as state (adjective), process (verb), standard (noun 1), and gift/quality (noun 2). The range is wider than most English uses of "righteousness" would suggest — it covers the full spectrum from noun to verb, from state to act.

**[dik- family]** E. In Greek, the dik- root is even more grammatically productive: dikaios (adjective), dikaiosunē (noun — the quality), dikaioō (verb — to justify), dikaiōma (noun — the righteous act or ordinance), dikaiōsis (noun — the act of justification), dikē (noun — justice/punishment), plus the compound forms (ekdikeō, ekdikēsis, ekdikos, dikaiokrisia). The grammatical range reveals that Greek legal-moral vocabulary is highly differentiated: Greek distinguishes the act (dikaioō), the resulting status (dikaiōsis), the standard (dikaiosunē), and the specific act-of-righteousness (dikaiōma) in ways Hebrew does not.

---

**T7.1.3** — What is the semantic range?

**[ts.d.q/dik-]** E. Broad: the ts.d.q / dik- root covers conformity to standard (moral sense), judicial acquittal (legal sense), vindication of the oppressed (social justice sense), covenant faithfulness (relational sense), and the Messianic-eschatological righteousness (theological-salvation sense). The semantic range spans: (1) moral quality; (2) legal verdict; (3) social vindication; (4) covenant character; (5) eschatological gift. This width confirms the cluster's compound character — the term covers all five modes simultaneously in different verse contexts.

**[logizomai]** E. Semantic range: to count/reckon (mathematical), to reason/deliberate (cognitive), to consider/regard (evaluative), to impute/credit (judicial-theological). The range runs from concrete arithmetic to abstract theological imputation. This width is what makes the term appropriate for F's evaluative-faculty role.

**T7.1.4** — Does the vocabulary distinguish disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Yes — the vocabulary makes several significant distinctions:
- **State vs. act:** tsad.diq (the righteous person — state) vs. tsa.deq (to justify — act); dikaios (righteous — state/quality) vs. dikaioō (to justify — act). The Hebrew and Greek both distinguish the state of being righteous from the act of declaring or making righteous.
- **Condition vs. quality:** tse.da.qah (righteousness as quality, something the person possesses) vs. tse.deq (righteousness as standard, the norm against which the person is measured). Both are nouns from the same root but carry different emphases — the condition of the person vs. the external standard.
- **Disposition vs. act:** yo.sher/me.shar (uprightness as inner disposition) vs. tse.da.qah in its acted sense (righteous acts, expressed righteousness). The B-vocabulary specifically captures the dispositional dimension that the A-vocabulary covers more broadly.
- **Received vs. exercised:** dikaiōsis (the act of justification as received — the event of being declared righteous) vs. dikaiōma (the righteous acts of the saints — the expressed righteous living). The Greek distinguishes what is received (C: justification as event) from what is exercised (A2: righteous acts as evidence).

**T7.1.9** — Is there a term newly coined in the NT?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Yes — several terms in M26 are NT-specific or extremely rare before the NT:

The concept of the person who is self-condemned — whose own inner being pronounces them guilty — is the NT's unique contribution in the E-sub-group. - **dikaiōsis (G1347)** — justification as the completed act. This rare noun appears in Rom 4:25 and Rom 5:18. It names the justification-event as a completed act (dikaiōsis = the act of justifying), distinct from dikaiosunē (the quality of righteousness) and dikaioō (the verb to justify). - **dikaiokrisia (G1341)** — righteous judgment (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001). A compound of dikaios (righteous) + krisis (judgment). While judicial language is OT-rich, this specific compound naming God's judgment as characteristically righteous is NT-specific.

What the coinages reveal: the NT needed new vocabulary to name experiences and realities that the OT vocabulary could not fully capture — specifically the reflexive-judicial reality of self-condemnation (autokatakritos), the completed event of justification as an act (dikaiōsis), and the characteristically righteous nature of divine judgment (dikaiokrisia). These coinages map the theological developments that the NT introduces into the righteousness domain.

**T7.1.10** — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The full vocabulary arc of M26 spans from the oldest Hebrew ts.d.q root through the LXX dik- bridge to the NT's compound innovations, revealing a continuous and developing semantic tradition. Several structural observations:

1. The vocabulary is constitutively relational throughout its arc: ts.d.q names conformity to a standard that is external; dik- names rightness in relation to the norm. The relational structure never disappears across the arc.

2. The vocabulary becomes more differentiated in the NT: the OT uses four basic ts.d.q forms (adjective, verb, two nouns); the NT uses at least eight dik- forms (dikaios, dikaiosunē, dikaioō, dikaiōma, dikaiōsis, dikē, dikaiokrisia, plus compound forms in D). The increased differentiation reflects the NT's more developed theological account of what justification is and how it works.

3. The y.sh.r (uprightness) vocabulary remains primarily OT in distribution — the NT equivalent (euthutēs) appears only once (Heb 1:8). The dispositional-straightness vocabulary is an OT-dominant semantic field; the NT focuses more on the declarative-judicial mode (dik- vocabulary).

4. The reckoning vocabulary (logizomai) bridges OT and NT without discontinuity — the same term names both Abraham's credited righteousness (Gen 15:6 via LXX) and the NT instruction to count oneself dead to sin (Rom 6:11). The faculty is consistent; its application develops.

5. The structural-opposite vocabulary (ra.sha/a.val) is predominantly OT — the NT relies more on abstract terms (anomia: lawlessness; adikia: unrighteousness) than on the OT's vivid person-type designations (the wicked one). This shift suggests that the NT is less interested in the category-type (the ra.sha as a social-moral identity) and more interested in the universal condition (all are under unrighteousness, Rom 3:10).

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. In Deu 32:4 — "A God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he" — tsad.diq (righteous/just) functions as a predicate adjective in an appositive construction: it is one of a series of four quality-descriptors applied to God (perfect, faithful, without iniquity, just and upright). Its function is characterisational — it describes the essential quality of God, not an action or a circumstance. The placement alongside "without iniquity" (its structural opposite immediately adjacent) reveals that the verse is defining righteousness by contrast as well as by affirmation.

The BOUNDARY sub-group contains five terms that support the cluster's judicial and evaluative activity without themselves constituting inner-being characteristics. **endikos (just/deserved):** This adjective qualifies outcomes as legally deserved — "the punishment endikos" (Heb 2:2, in boundary material). It is a verdict-qualifier, not an inner-being state. These are institutional-functional terms — they name the office and its acts without naming the inner-being condition of the one who holds the office. **Summary:** The BOUNDARY terms collectively enable M26's judicial architecture by providing: the procedural frame (charge and culpability), the epistemic ground of accurate judgment (divine heart-knowledge), the qualifier of just outcomes (deserved), and the institutional category of judging. The analytical consequence for the programme: findings from the OT and NT on this question are not in tension and do not require harmonisation. They describe the same reality at different resolutions. OT close reading and NT categorical statement arrive at identical conclusions about the constitutive God-relation. The two testaments are reading at different focal lengths, not saying different things.
