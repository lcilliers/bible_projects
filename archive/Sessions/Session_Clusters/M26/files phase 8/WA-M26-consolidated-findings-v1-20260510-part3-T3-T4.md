## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness is heard: "the Spirit convicts the world concerning righteousness" (Joh 16:8, VSG M26-A1-005), and proclamation of it is made to be heard and received (Psa 35:28; 71:24; 145:7). Encountering God's righteousness produces an inner perception — Felix "heard" the reasoning about righteousness and self-control and was alarmed (Act 24:25, VSG M26-A1-006). The perception here is cognitive-auditory: the standard of divine righteousness is received through hearing and registered internally.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — hunger and thirst for righteousness (Mat 5:6, VSG M26-A2-006) names a perceptual-desire orientation: the person is constituted to perceive righteousness as desirable, to sense its absence as a lack. This is the spiritual-perceptual analog of physical hunger — an inner sense that registers the absence of what is needed. Uprightness enables recognition of right paths (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002) — a discernment function that is inherently perceptive.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — uprightness enables a specific perceptive capacity: "then you will understand righteousness and justice and equity, every good path" (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002). The upright person perceives what is right — they see what others miss. Isa 30:10 (VSG 1214-001) names the refusal of this perception: "do not see," "prophesy to us smooth things" — the demand that the prophets not perceive and report what is right. Uprightness is in part a perceptive virtue — the capacity to see truly.

**[C — Justification]** S. Justification as an act does not primarily engage the perceptive faculty. The act is declarative and received by faith, not by perception. Silence.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the cry for justice (Luk 18:3, VSG 944-001) presupposes perception of injustice: the widow perceives that she has been wronged and appeals on the basis of that perception. The capacity to perceive that wrong has occurred is the precondition for the cry for justice. D's perceptive engagement is the moral recognition of injustice.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — self-condemnation (autokatakritos, Tit 3:11) presupposes self-perception: the person perceives their own conduct and finds it condemnable. The condemning conscience operates by perceiving moral failure in the self. The Ninevites perceiving the greatness of the one before them (Mat 12:41, VSG 4846-001) — their repentance arose from spiritual perception.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the reckoning faculty engages perception in a specific mode: the faculty considers (logizomai), weighs, and deliberates — all of which require perceiving the things being compared. "Count yourselves dead to sin and alive to God" (Rom 6:11) requires perceiving one's own status accurately. "The sufferings of this present time" (Rom 8:18) must be perceived before they can be weighed. The reckoning faculty is a form of organized inner perception applied to moral reality.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — but inverted. "They do not know how to do right" (Amo 3:10, VSG 1214-001) names the failure of moral perception in the wicked. "None of the wicked shall understand" (Dan 12:10, VSG 1225-001). Wickedness impairs the perceptive faculty for righteousness — the wicked person in the land of uprightness "does not see the majesty of the Lord" (Isa 26:10, VSG 5617-001).

**[CLUSTER — T3.1]** E. The perceptive faculty is engaged across the cluster in specific modes: (1) auditory perception of the proclaimed standard (A1); (2) hunger-perception of righteousness as desirable (A2); (3) discernment-perception of right paths (B); (4) perception of injustice as the trigger for the cry for justice (D); (5) self-perception as the ground of self-condemnation (E); (6) organized deliberative perception as the mode of moral reckoning (F). Wickedness impairs all these perceptive modes (G).

---

**T3.1.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair perception?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables and deepens perception: uprightness in B produces the capacity to understand "every good path" (Pro 2:9); the righteous person in A2 is one who can discern (A2-011: the thoughts of the righteous are just, Pro 12:5); the reckoning faculty in F is sharpened through right use (sustained weighing of suffering against glory, Rom 8:18). Wickedness impairs perception: the wicked do not know how to do right (Amo 3:10); none of the wicked shall understand (Dan 12:10). The pattern is consistent: righteousness is perceptually enabling; wickedness is perceptually disabling.

---

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The consistent enabling of perception by righteousness and impairing by wickedness reveals that righteousness is not morally neutral in its cognitive effects — it is epistemologically transforming. The righteous person perceives what the wicked cannot. This is a significant finding for the programme: righteousness is not only a moral condition but a perceptual condition. The inner state of righteousness enables a kind of seeing; its absence produces a kind of blindness.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — "knowing that God's righteous judgment" (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001); the proclamation of God's righteousness produces knowledge in those who hear it. Felix's encounter (Act 24:25) was a cognitive engagement with the standard of righteousness through Paul's reasoning.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "the thoughts of the righteous are just" (Pro 12:5, VSG M26-A2-011). The cognitive content of the righteous person — their inner reasoning and deliberation — is characterised as just and right. Righteousness shapes cognition: what the righteous person thinks is shaped by righteousness.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — "then you will understand righteousness and justice and equity, every good path" (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002). Understanding is a cognitive function; uprightness produces understanding. B engages the cognitive faculty in its discernment mode.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "for we hold that one is justified by faith apart from works of the law" (Rom 3:28, VSG 3410-001, in the F-group but directly relevant to C's cognitive dimension). The cognitive act of understanding and believing the grounds of justification is integral to receiving it.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** S. The cry for justice is primarily volitional and affective rather than cognitive. Silence on primary cognitive engagement.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — "by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned" (Mat 12:37) — the cognitive content of speech becomes the grounds of verdict. Self-condemnation (autokatakritos) involves cognitive self-assessment.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — this is the sub-group most explicitly engaging cognition. Logizomai is a cognitive-deliberative act: reasoning, counting, reckoning, considering. The entire F-sub-group is a study in how the cognitive faculty is applied to moral reality.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — but impaired. "They do not know how to do right" (Amo 3:10); "none of the wicked shall understand" (Dan 12:10). Wickedness distorts and disables the cognitive faculty's application to moral truth.

**[CLUSTER — T3.2]** E. Cognition is engaged across A2 (righteous thoughts), B (understanding), C (belief in the grounds of justification), E (self-assessment), and F (the deliberative reckoning act). It is impaired in G. The reckoning faculty (F) is the most explicitly cognitive sub-group. The cluster reveals that righteousness and cognition are deeply connected: what the person thinks and how they reason is shaped by their moral state.

---

**T3.2.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness deepens and enables cognition (A2: just thoughts; B: understanding; F: accurate moral deliberation). Wickedness impairs it (G: moral blindness, inability to understand). The F-sub-group is specifically the sub-group in which cognition is most actively exercised — the reckoning faculty IS a cognitive function applied to moral reality.

---

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern reveals that righteousness is not only a moral state but a cognitive state — the righteous person thinks differently, understands more accurately, and reasons more honestly about moral reality. The impairment of cognition in wickedness is not merely an intellectual failure but a moral one: the failure to understand arises from the refusal to engage with what is true. This connection between righteousness and cognition supports the cluster's profile of the reckoning faculty (F) as the cognitive-moral organ that links the declared reality of justification to lived experience.

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — "future generations shall proclaim it" (Psa 22:31, VSG M26-A1-005); "your righteousness shall be proclaimed to a people yet to be born" (Psa 22:31). The proclamation of God's righteousness across generations requires memory — the community holds the testimony of what God has done and passes it on. The psalmist who declares God's righteousness "all day" (Psa 35:28; 71:24; 145:7) is drawing on remembered encounters with God's righteousness.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — memory is used against the righteous person and in favor of them. "None of the righteous deeds that he has done shall be remembered" (Eze 18:24, VSG M26-A2-002) — in the Ezekiel forfeiture material, righteousness is non-accumulative: past righteous deeds are not held in God's memory when the person turns to wickedness. Conversely, "none of the transgressions he has committed shall be remembered against him" (Eze 18:22, VSG M26-A2-002) when the wicked person repents. Memory — specifically God's remembering or not remembering — is the mechanism by which righteousness is assessed in the Ezekiel material.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "as David also speaks of the blessing of the one to whom God counts righteousness apart from works" (Rom 4:6, VSG 3410-001). The appeal to David and Abraham (Rom 4:3–9) is an appeal to remembered precedent: the historical instances of credited righteousness are held in memory and applied as typological patterns. Justification by faith is not a novelty — Paul argues from remembered Scripture.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** S. Memory is not a primary engagement in D's verse evidence. Silence.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the Ninevites' repentance is held in memory and applied as a standard of condemnation for the present generation (Mat 12:41, VSG 4846-001). The memory of past righteous responses condemns present unrighteous non-response.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** S. The reckoning faculty is a present-deliberative act — it operates in the present moment of counting, not through memory retrieval. Silence.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. Memory is not a primary engagement in G's verse evidence. Silence.

**[CLUSTER — T3.3]** E. Memory engages the cluster specifically in: (1) the communal transmission of testimony to God's righteousness (A1); (2) the assessment of past righteous deeds in the Ezekiel forfeiture mechanism (A2); (3) the typological appeal to Abraham and David as remembered precedents for faith-righteousness (C); and (4) the use of past righteous responses as standards of condemnation (E). The cluster reveals that righteousness is not ahistorical — it accumulates testimony and creates a remembered pattern that the community carries forward.

---

**T3.3.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The Ezekiel material reveals a specific mode: righteousness is forfeitable in God's memory — past righteous deeds are not counted when the person turns to wickedness (Eze 18:24). This is not memory impairment in the person but a statement about how God's judicial memory functions. God's judicial memory does not accumulate righteousness as credit; it assesses the current state.

**[CLUSTER]** E. The cluster engages memory primarily in the communal-testimonial and judicial-historical modes. Righteousness enables testimonial memory (the psalmist's sustained declaration); the forfeiture mechanism (A2) reveals how God's judicial memory functions differently from human accumulative memory.

---

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern reveals two distinct memory-functions in the cluster: (1) the testimonial memory of the covenant community that preserves and transmits the record of God's righteousness across generations; and (2) the judicial memory of God that assesses the current state rather than accumulating past deeds. These two functions operate differently — community testimony is cumulative and transmitted; divine judicial memory is present-state-focused. The non-accumulation principle in Ezekiel (righteousness is not banked) is one of the OT's most searching treatments of how moral standing works.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — alarm (Act 24:25, VSG M26-A1-006: Felix was alarmed when righteousness was discussed), guilt and shame (the repeated pattern of "you are righteous; we are in guilt," Neh 9:33; Ezr 9:15; Dan 9:7), and the awe of the heavens declaring God's righteousness (Psa 50:6; 97:6). Encountering God's righteousness produces affective states in those who hear it.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — and richly. VSG 3246-003 (the immediate response group for A2) is explicitly affective: gladness, exultation, and praise are the inner affective responses to righteousness. Hunger and thirst (Mat 5:6, VSG M26-A2-006) are affective-desire states. The palm tree image (Psa 92:12, VSG M26-A2-001) and flourishing imagery convey a positive affective state associated with righteous living. Bold as lions (Pro 28:1, VSG M26-A2-013) names the emotional affect of security and confidence in the righteous person.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — "my inmost being will exult when your lips speak what is right" (Pro 23:16, VSG 1212-002). The word me.shar (uprightness in speech) produces an exultation in the speaker's inmost being. Uprightness generates an affective response in the person who hears or expresses it.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — hope (Tit 3:7: "heirs according to the hope of eternal life"). Hope is an affective-anticipatory state. The justified person's characteristic affect is hope — the forward-looking orientation of the one who knows their standing before God.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — 2Cor 7:11 (VSG 945-001) explicitly names the affective states produced by the righteous grief that issues in the desire for justice: "earnestness, eagerness to clear yourselves, indignation, fear, longing, zeal, punishment." The D-group surfaces a rich affective vocabulary. The cry for justice (Luk 18:3, VSG 944-001) is itself an affectively-charged act — the persistence of the widow is driven by affective engagement (longing, urgency, determination).

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the weight of condemnation is an affective state (the burden of being under verdict). The release of freedom from condemnation (Joh 8:10–11, VSG 4846-002) is an affective relief. The Ninevites who repented at the preaching of Jonah (Mat 12:41) responded with an affective-repentance (metanoeo: a change of mind and heart with emotional dimension).

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — indirectly. The reckoning act of weighing suffering against future glory (Rom 8:18, VSG 3410-001) is not emotionally neutral: it is an act of reorienting the affective response to suffering by placing it in a larger framework. The counting produces a changed relationship to the affective experience of suffering — not denial of the affect but its recontextualization through deliberate reckoning.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — the hardened and impenitent heart (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001) names an affective state: the absence of fear, the absence of compunction. The wicked person "stores up wrath" (Rom 2:5) — an accumulative judicial state that will produce affective experience at the day of judgment. Wickedness is associated with an affective deficit: the inability to feel the weight of what righteousness requires.

**[CLUSTER — T3.4]** E. The affective faculty is richly engaged across the cluster. The signature affects of righteousness in M26 are: gladness and exultation (A2), hope (C), earnestness and zeal (D), relief from condemnation's weight (E), and the recontextualization of suffering (F). These are not incidental emotional responses — they are constitutive of what it means to live righteously. Righteousness in M26 is affectively textured throughout.

---

**T3.4.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables specific positive affects (gladness, hope, zeal, relief) and re-orients potentially destructive affects (the recontextualization of suffering through reckoning in F; the entrusting of vengeance rather than the destructive weight of self-executed resentment in D). Wickedness impairs the healthy affects (the hardened heart cannot feel appropriate fear or compunction, G). The cluster reveals that righteousness is an affective organiser — it enables right affects and orients potentially destructive ones toward productive outcomes.

---

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern reveals that righteousness in M26 is not a cold judicial concept — it is affectively alive throughout. The gladness of the righteous (A2), the zeal of the one who cries for justice (D), the hope of the justified (C), the exultation at upright speech (B) — these are not secondary accompaniments to righteousness but intrinsic to it. The affective richness of the cluster confirms that righteousness is a whole-person characteristic, not merely a legal or cognitive state.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — the human person can choose to submit to God's righteousness or resist it. "Seeking to establish their own, they did not submit to God's righteousness" (Rom 10:3, VSG M26-A1-006). The volitional choice of submission or resistance to the divine standard is the central volitional engagement of A1 with the human person.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — hunger and thirst (Mat 5:6, VSG M26-A2-006) names an active volitional orientation: the person chooses to pursue righteousness, to hunger for it, not to settle for its absence. The Ezekiel material (A2-002) reveals that the forfeiture of righteousness involves a volitional turning: "when a righteous person turns away from his righteousness" (Eze 18:24) — the turning is a volitional act. Righteousness in A2 is sustained by volitional commitment; it can be forfeited by volitional abandonment.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — walking in uprightness is a volitional choice: "who forsake the paths of uprightness to walk in the ways of darkness" (Pro 2:13, VSG 1211-002). The person chooses to walk in or to forsake the path of uprightness. The disposition is inner, but its maintenance involves ongoing volitional commitment.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — receiving justification by faith is a volitional act: "justified by faith" presupposes the volitional act of believing and trusting. The alternative — "severed from Christ, you who would be justified by the law" (Gal 5:4, VSG 3194-001) — names the volitional choice that short-circuits justification. Reception requires the will's cooperation in faith.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the relinquishment of personal vengeance (Rom 12:19, VSG 6841-002) is an explicitly volitional act: "never avenge yourselves." The person must choose not to execute vengeance personally. The persistent widow's appeal (Luk 18:3, VSG 944-001) is volitional persistence — she will not give up.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — "judge not, and you will not be judged; condemn not, and you will not be condemned" (Luk 6:37, VSG 3201-001). The act of condemning is volitional; the choice not to condemn is equally volitional. The hypocritical judge (Rom 2:1) chose to pass judgment — the choice is the problem.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — "count yourselves dead to sin" (Rom 6:11). The counting is a volitional act: the person is commanded to reckon, implying that they have the capacity to choose to do so or not. The consequent "therefore do not let sin reign" (Rom 6:12) is also volitional. The reckoning faculty requires the will to engage it.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — "when a righteous person turns away from his righteousness and does injustice" (Eze 18:24, VSG M26-A2-002, G-adjacent). Wickedness involves volitional turning: the hard and impenitent heart (Rom 2:5) is hard because the person has not chosen to repent. Wickedness is not merely weakness — it is volitional resistance.

**[CLUSTER — T3.6]** E. Volition is engaged throughout the entire cluster — every sub-group involves volitional choices. The volitional dimensions span: choosing to submit to or resist God's righteousness (A1), choosing to pursue or forfeit righteousness (A2), choosing to walk in uprightness or devious paths (B), choosing to receive justification by faith or resist it (C), choosing to entrust vengeance to God or execute it personally (D), choosing to condemn or not to condemn (E), choosing to reckon (F), and choosing to repent or harden (G). Volition is one of the most pervasively engaged faculties in this cluster.

---

**T3.6.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables and empowers volition by clarifying what the right choice is and providing the inner resources (faith, hope, love of righteousness) to make it. Justification (C) creates the freedom in which volition can be exercised toward God rather than away from him. Wickedness impairs volition progressively: the hardened heart is less and less capable of repentance (Rom 2:5) — the volitional capacity atrophies under sustained wickedness.

---

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pervasive engagement of volition across the cluster reveals that righteousness is not a passive condition but one that requires sustained volitional engagement. The person does not fall into righteousness; they receive it by faith (a volitional act), pursue it (a volitional orientation), walk in it (a volitional commitment), and reckon from it (a volitional exercise). The cluster's portrait of righteousness is deeply volitional throughout.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness is expressed through his acts: saving, delivering, vindicating, judging (A1-004; A1-002). The divine agency is expressed through righteousness — what God does expresses who he is. This establishes the model: righteous character expresses itself in righteous action.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "execute justice and righteousness" (Eze 45:9, VSG 911-001); "do justice and righteousness and deliver from the hand of the oppressor" (Jer 22:3, VSG M26-A2-012). Agency is required: righteousness that does not express itself in just action is the concern of A2-003 (distortion and counterfeit). The person who is righteous acts righteously — the inner condition generates agency.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — "he who walks righteously and speaks uprightly, who despises the gain of oppressions, who shakes his hands, lest they hold a bribe" (Isa 33:15, VSG 1212-002). Walking, speaking, shaking hands — all are acts of agency expressing the inner disposition of uprightness. Uprightness generates specific agentive expressions.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "go, and from now on sin no more" (Joh 8:11, adjacent to C through E-connection). Justification (C) and the freedom from condemnation (E) that it produces result in a commission to agency: the freed and justified person is sent to act differently. The justified person is described as becoming an "heir" (Tit 3:7) — heirs act, they are not merely passive recipients.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the cry for justice (944-001) is an agentive act: the person takes the situation to God rather than passively accepting it. The commissioned avenger (Rom 13:4: "an avenger who carries out God's wrath") is an agent of divine retributive justice. Agency is central to D.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — "condemn not, and you will not be condemned" (Luk 6:37, VSG 3201-001). The act of condemning is an agentive act. Self-condemnation (autokatakritos) is the conscience acting against the self — an inner agency directed inward.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the reckoning act produces agency: "count yourselves dead to sin... therefore do not let sin reign" (Rom 6:11–12). The reckoning (cognitive-volitional act) issues directly in commanded action. The reckoning faculty is the inner preparation for agency.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — "they band together against the life of the righteous and condemn the innocent to death" (Psa 94:21, VSG 1225-002). Wickedness expresses itself in agentive harm — it actively oppresses and destroys. Wickedness is not merely passive absence of righteousness; it generates its own destructive agency.

**[CLUSTER — T3.7]** E. Agency is engaged throughout the cluster. The consistent pattern is: inner condition (righteousness/wickedness) → agentive expression (righteous acts/harmful acts). Righteousness in M26 is not an interior state that remains interior — it issues in action. The cluster portrait is one of morally active persons: executing justice, speaking truth, delivering from oppression, entrusting vengeance, crying for justice, refraining from condemning. Righteousness is agentive.

---

**T3.7.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables and directs agency toward its proper objects (justice, deliverance, truth-speaking). Justification (C) liberates agency from the paralysis of unresolved guilt. Wickedness distorts agency, directing it toward harmful ends (oppression, false condemnation, systemic corruption). The commissioned agent (D: Rom 13:4) shows agency bounded and directed by divine commission — the proper form of human retributive agency.

---

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern reveals that righteousness is constitutively directed toward action: it is not a private spiritual state but a condition with public, agentive consequences. The righteous person does justice, speaks truth, delivers the oppressed, and cries for justice when none is given. The cluster confirms that righteousness is not passive — it is one of the most agentively active characteristics in the programme.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness is the standard of moral evaluation. "He does no injustice" (Zep 3:5); "he does wickedly, and the Almighty will not pervert justice" (Job 34:12, VSG 1225-001). God's righteousness is the measure by which all other moral claims are evaluated. The standard itself is the moral evaluation faculty at its source.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "the thoughts of the righteous are just" (Pro 12:5, VSG M26-A2-011). The righteous person's moral evaluation is accurate — they think justly. The testing of the righteous (Psa 7:9, VSG M26-A2-013: "O God who tests the minds and hearts") engages moral evaluation as the mode of divine assessment of the person.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — uprightness enables moral evaluation of right paths (Pro 2:9). The person with uprightness can evaluate what is straight and what is crooked. Isa 30:10 (VSG 1214-001) shows the rejection of moral evaluation: "do not prophesy to us what is right; speak smooth things" — the demand for smooth things is a refusal of honest moral evaluation.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "will you even put me in the wrong? Will you condemn me that you may be in the right?" (Job 40:8, VSG 1225-002). The moral evaluation of who is right — who can stand before God's standard — is the question that justification answers. Justification is God's moral evaluation of the person through Christ.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — "God's righteous judgment" (Rom 2:5, VSG 3207-001) engages moral evaluation: the identification of wrongdoing and the determination of appropriate retribution. D presupposes accurate moral evaluation as its precondition: the avenger must correctly identify wrong before acting.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — condemnation is the negative verdict of moral evaluation. "He who justifies the wicked and he who condemns the righteous are both alike an abomination to the Lord" (Pro 17:15, VSG 1225-002). The accuracy of moral evaluation in E is critical — corrupt evaluation (condemning the righteous, justifying the wicked) is itself condemned.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — and centrally. The reckoning faculty in F is the moral evaluation faculty applied to specific questions: what is this worth? How does this compare? What is the standing of this person? Moral reckoning (F) is moral evaluation in its most explicitly cognitive and deliberative mode.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — but distorted. "They do not know how to do right" (Amo 3:10); "he who justifies the wicked and condemns the righteous" (Pro 17:15) — the sub-group names both the failure of moral evaluation (inability to do right) and its inversion (justifying the wicked, condemning the righteous). Wickedness distorts moral evaluation in both directions.

**[CLUSTER — T3.8]** E. Moral evaluation is engaged throughout the entire cluster and is arguably its central faculty engagement. M26 is fundamentally a cluster about moral evaluation: what is right and wrong, who stands and who falls, what deserves vindication and what deserves condemnation. The reckoning faculty (F) is the most explicitly named instance of moral evaluation; the judicial vocabulary (C, D, E) frames moral evaluation in its formal-judicial mode; the righteous thoughts of A2-011 are moral evaluation in its everyday-cognitive mode.

**T3.8.2-3** — Enable/deepen/bypass/impair; pattern revelation.

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables and sharpens moral evaluation; wickedness distorts it (condemning the righteous, justifying the wicked). The cluster reveals that accurate moral evaluation is both an expression of righteousness and one of its primary functions. The person who is righteous evaluates rightly; the person who is wicked evaluates corruptly. Moral evaluation is the faculty most fundamentally at stake in the M26 cluster.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — "when Paul reasoned about righteousness and self-control and the coming judgment, Felix was alarmed" (Act 24:25, VSG M26-A1-006). The encounter with the standard of divine righteousness activated Felix's conscience — alarm is the conscience's response to the recognition that one falls short of the standard. God's righteousness invades the conscience of those who hear it.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "For our boast is this, the testimony of our conscience, that we behaved in the world with simplicity and godly sincerity, not by earthly wisdom but by the grace of God, and supremely so toward you" (implied in A2 through the conscience's testimony to righteous behavior). More directly: the righteous person's conscience provides testimony to their righteousness — and the absence of guilt in the conscience is itself a marker of righteous standing (1Cor 4:4, in the unrouted group: "I am not aware of anything against myself, but I am not thereby acquitted" — the conscience's clearance is not the final word, but it is a real signal).

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — the freedom from condemnation (4846-002) is a conscience-state as well as a judicial state. "Heb 9:14" (adjacent to the 1099-001 group in C): "how much more will the blood of Christ... purify our conscience from dead works to serve the living God." The justified conscience is a purified conscience — justification reaches the conscience directly.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — and centrally. Autokatakritos (self-condemned, Tit 3:11, VSG 4848-001) is the conscience turned against the self: the person whose own inner witness pronounces them guilty. 1Cor 4:4 (unrouted group): "I am not aware of anything against myself" — the conscience as the instrument of self-assessment. The E-sub-group is where conscience operates most explicitly in its judicial mode.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the instruction "count yourselves dead to sin" (Rom 6:11) is addressed to the conscience-moral complex: the person who has received justification is told to reckon their conscience in accordance with the declared reality. The reckoning faculty directly engages and reorients the conscience.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — the hard and impenitent heart (Rom 2:5) names a conscience that has been suppressed or silenced. The wicked person whose conscience has ceased to function is described by Paul as one who "stores up wrath" — the suppression of conscience does not eliminate the judicial reality; it only delays its encounter.

**[CLUSTER — T3.9]** E. Conscience is a primary faculty engaged throughout M26. The key conscience-engagements: (1) alarm at the divine standard (A1: Felix); (2) testimony of righteous behavior to the self (A2); (3) purification through justification (C); (4) self-condemnation as conscience turning against the self (E: autokatakritos); (5) reorientation of conscience through reckoning (F); (6) suppression and hardening of conscience in wickedness (G). The session log finding (confirmed here) that conscience is the primary faculty for the M26-E sub-group is fully supported.

---

**T3.9.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness deepens and enables conscience — the righteous person has a clear conscience (A2), an honest self-testimony (A2), and a conscience that accurately signals moral failure (E: autokatakritos is the conscience working properly in a negative case). Justification purifies the conscience (C). The reckoning faculty (F) reorients the conscience in accordance with declared reality. Wickedness impairs conscience through progressive hardening (G: the hard and impenitent heart). The conscience is a central locus of M26's operation.

---

**T3.9.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern reveals that conscience is not peripheral to righteousness in M26 — it is the primary inner-being faculty through which the judicial dimension of righteousness is experienced. The conscience is the organ that registers the divine standard (A1: alarm), testifies to righteous or unrighteous conduct (A2, E), and is either purified by justification (C) or distorted by wickedness (G). The cluster confirms the session log's finding that conscience is a central faculty for this cluster — specifically identified in the session log as "the primary faculty for moral-judicial dimension."

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The comprehensive integration of inner orientation with behavioral consistency is conscientiousness in its fullest expression.

**[C — Justification]** E. Indirectly — "the righteous deeds of the saints" (Rev 19:8, VSG 1099-001) as the bride's garment implies the accumulated conscientious righteous living that produces the identity of the justified community.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. **[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. **[CLUSTER — T3.10]** E. Conscientiousness is engaged primarily in A2 (steadfast righteousness over time), B (comprehensive integration of uprightness into all behavior), and D (persistent cry for justice). The cluster's portrait of the righteous person is one of conscientious consistency — not episodic righteousness but sustained, integrated, whole-life moral living.

**T3.10.2-3** — Enable/deepen/bypass/impair; pattern revelation.

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables and deepens conscientiousness — the righteous person sustains their inner orientation into behavioral consistency. Wickedness impairs conscientiousness: the wicked person's behavior is inconsistent with any righteous inner standard because the inner standard has been suppressed. The cluster portrait of the righteous person is deeply conscientious.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness is the basis of covenant relationship: "I will be their God, in faithfulness and in righteousness" (Zec 8:8, VSG M26-A1-003). Righteousness is constitutive of the divine-human covenant — it defines how God relates. The appeal to God's righteousness (A1-004: "in your righteousness deliver me") is a relational act — it engages God through his character as the basis of appeal.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — M26-A2-012 (righteousness extending outward to others) is entirely relational: guiding the neighbor (Pro 12:26), caring for the animals (Pro 12:10), doing justice and delivering from the oppressor (Jer 22:3), treating workers fairly (Mat 20:4), flowing as an ever-flowing stream (Amo 5:24). Righteousness in A2 is constitutively other-directed — it reaches out in relationship.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — uprightness is expressed in truthful speech toward others and in the refusal to participate in systems of oppression (Isa 33:15). Uprightness is a relational quality — it determines how the person relates to others through honesty, truth-telling, and the refusal of corrupt relational practices (bribery, deceit).

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — justification is fundamentally a relational act: it changes the person's standing before God. "Being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7) — the relational category of heir-status follows justification. The justified person stands in a new relational position before God.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — D is relational throughout: the wrong done is relational (one person wronging another), the cry for justice is relational (addressed to God), the commission is relational (the avenger serves the wronged on behalf of God). D engages relational capacity in all three of its relational dimensions.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — condemnation is a relational verdict: the judge and the condemned are in a specific relational position. The freedom from condemnation (Joh 8:10–11) is relational healing: Jesus' declaration ("neither do I condemn you") restores the woman to a relationship no longer defined by guilt-verdict.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the reckoning faculty engages relational capacity indirectly: the person who reckons correctly ("count yourselves dead to sin, alive to God") reorients their relational standing — they now relate to God as one who is alive to him, not dead in sin. The reckoning act reframes the relational position.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — wickedness distorts relational capacity: it oppresses (Jer 22:3), exploits (Jer 22:13), persecutes (M26-A2-016: plotting against the righteous), and falsifies judicial relationships (Pro 17:15: justifying the wicked and condemning the righteous). Wickedness is relationally destructive.

**[CLUSTER — T3.11]** E. Relational capacity is engaged throughout the entire cluster. The cluster reveals that righteousness is inherently relational — it determines how persons stand before God (C, D, E), how they relate to other persons (A2-012, B, D), and how the community is constituted (A2-015, A2-012). Wickedness is relationally destructive across all the same dimensions.

**T3.11.2-3** — Enable/deepen/bypass/impair; pattern revelation.

**[CLUSTER]** E. Righteousness enables, deepens, and extends relational capacity — it enables right relationship with God (C: justified standing), other persons (A2-012: outward-flowing righteousness), and the community (A2-015: preservative presence). Wickedness impairs and destroys relational capacity at every level. The cluster portrait is of a characteristic that is constitutively relational in all its modes.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness reaches the human person through: (1) proclamation (the declared standard that confronts the conscience, A1-005/006); (2) the Messiah (A1-007: God's righteousness incarnated and extended to humanity through Christ); (3) covenant faithfulness (A1-003: God's righteousness expressed toward his covenant people as reliability, keeping promises, betrothing in righteousness); (4) saving acts (A1-004: deliverance, vindication, restoration — all flowing from God's righteousness toward the person in need).

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — righteousness is given to the person by God: "born of him" (1Jo 2:29, VSG M26-A2-014); "the righteousness of God" in the person (2Cor 5:21, VSG M26-A2-008); credited to Abraham (Gen 15:6, VSG 3410-001). God extends righteousness to the human person as a gift received by faith — this is the primary mode of the God-to-human interface in M26.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — justification is definitionally a God-to-human act. "God who justifies" (Rom 4:5, VSG 3194-001); "justified by his grace" (Tit 3:7). Justification flows exclusively from God toward the person — the human person cannot justify themselves.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — God executes avenging justice toward wrongdoers: "he has judged the great prostitute... and has avenged on her the blood of his servants" (Rev 19:2, VSG 944-002). God also executes justice toward the wronged through response to their cry (Luk 18:7–8, VSG 945-001: "he will give justice to them speedily").

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — God condemns: "he condemned sin in the flesh" (Rom 8:3, VSG 4846-002); condemnation falls on those who disobey the gospel (2Th 1:9, VSG 949-001). God's condemnation is directed toward sin and toward those who reject righteousness.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — God reckons: "it was counted to him as righteousness" (Rom 4:3–11, VSG 3410-001). The divine reckoning of faith as righteousness is the most explicitly God-to-human operation in F.

**[CLUSTER — T4.1]** E. God's righteousness operates toward the human person in every mode: as gift (A2, C), as proclamation (A1-005), as incarnation through the Messiah (A1-007), as saving deliverance (A1-004), as reckoning of faith (F), as avenging justice (D), and as condemnation of sin and unrighteousness (E). The God-to-human interface in M26 is comprehensive and operates in multiple directional modes.

**T4.1.2** — What is the basis on which God extends this characteristic?

**[A2 — Human Righteousness / C — Justification]** E. Primarily grace received through faith. "Being justified by his grace" (Tit 3:7); "faith was counted to Abraham as righteousness" (Rom 4:9, VSG 3410-001). The basis is not human moral performance but divine grace received by faith. The OT material shows that righteousness is also extended on the basis of covenant faithfulness: God keeps his covenant with the righteous across generations (Psa 103:17, VSG M26-A1-003) — the covenantal basis.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Responsive — God's avenging justice is responsive to injustice done: it is triggered by the cry of the wronged (Luk 18:3–8) and by the accumulated injustice done to his servants (Rev 19:2). The basis is just response to wrong.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. On the basis of assessed guilt — God's condemnation is the just verdict pronounced on those whose standing before his righteousness is one of guilt. The basis is not arbitrary — it is the just response of the righteous judge to wickedness.

**[CLUSTER]** The basis varies by sub-group: grace through faith (C, A2), covenant (A1-003, A2-014), just response (D, E). The common thread is that God's righteousness operates from his character — he acts in accordance with who he is in every mode.

---

**T4.1.3** — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The comprehensive range of God's extension of righteousness toward the human person reveals a disposition that is simultaneously just and gracious: God's righteousness is not indulgent (he condemns sin and wickedness) and not merely juridical (he justifies the ungodly through Christ). The same righteousness that requires condemnation provides justification — through the cross, where "he is just and the justifier" (Rom 3:26). God's disposition toward the human person is that of a righteous judge who is also a gracious Saviour — not despite his righteousness but through it.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God?

"In your righteousness deliver me" (Psa 31:1; 71:2; 143:1; 143:11, VSG M26-A1-004). Bringing offerings "in righteousness" (Mal 3:3, VSG M26-A2-014) — worship as the righteous person's movement toward God.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — "whoever walks in uprightness fears the Lord" (Pro 14:2, VSG 1211-002). The walk in uprightness is simultaneously a walk toward the Lord — the two are inseparable. The preparation before God in prayer (a.rakh, Psa 5:3, VSG 6753-003 in the F-group): preparing oneself before God as an act of devotional orientation — the reckoning faculty in its devotional mode.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — receiving justification by faith is itself a movement toward God: the person who believes God (Rom 4:3–5) is turning toward him, accepting the basis he has provided for right standing. Justification creates the conditions for ongoing approach to God through the new standing it establishes.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the cry for justice (Luk 18:3; Rev 6:10, VSG 944-001) is the primary human-to-God movement in D. The wronged person turns to God rather than to themselves — this is an act of faith and dependence: bringing the injustice to God and trusting him to respond.

**[E — Condemnation]** S. Freedom from condemnation enables movement toward God — but E itself does not primarily describe a human-to-God movement. Silence on direct human-to-God direction in E.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — "count yourselves dead to sin and alive to God" (Rom 6:11) — the reckoning toward God is a movement of orientation: the person reckons themselves as belonging to and alive for God. The devotional preparation (a.rakh, Psa 5:3, VSG 6753-003): preparing oneself before God in prayer is the reckoning faculty directed toward God in worship.

**[CLUSTER — T4.2]** E. The human-to-God interface is present across the cluster: appeal on the basis of divine righteousness (A1), righteous living as movement toward God (A2), upright walk toward the Lord (B), receiving justification by faith (C), the cry for justice as dependent prayer (D), and the reckoning of oneself as alive to God (F). The cluster reveals that righteousness in all its modes orients the person toward God — it is constitutively God-ward.

**T4.2.3** — What does the human-to-God direction reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The human-to-God direction of righteousness reveals that relationship with God is the constitutive context of righteousness in M26. Righteousness is not primarily a social virtue or a moral performance — it is the orientation of the person toward the God who is himself righteous. Every human-to-God movement in the cluster (appeal, worship, cry for justice, faith, upright walk) is an expression of righteousness as a relational disposition toward God.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. God's righteousness is not given by one human to another — it is the divine standard. S.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — righteousness flows outward to others in multiple modes: guiding the neighbor (Pro 12:26), caring for animals (Pro 12:10), delivering from oppression (Jer 22:3), dealing fairly with workers (Mat 20:4), interceding for the community (Abraham's intercession for Sodom, Gen 18:23–28, VSG M26-A2-015). The righteous person's inner condition generates a giving movement toward others.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — upright speech is given to others: "he who walks righteously and speaks uprightly" (Isa 33:15) — the uprightness of speech is expressed toward those who hear. "Whoever speaks the truth gives honest evidence" (Pro 12:17, VSG M26-A2-011) — truth-speaking is a giving act toward those who need honest testimony.

**[C — Justification]** S. Justification as such is not given by one human to another — it is God's exclusive act. However, the human judicial act of vindicating the innocent (947-002: Deu 25:1; 2Sa 15:4) is a human-to-human expression of the justification principle. The human judge who acquits the innocent is extending a form of the justification-act toward the person before them.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the commissioned human avenger (Rom 13:4) extends justice toward wrongdoers on behalf of the wronged. This is a human-to-human giving of justice — the authority serves others by executing the retribution due to wrongdoers. Moses avenging the oppressed Israelite (Act 7:24, VSG 945-001) is a human giving of protective justice toward the vulnerable.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the human judge extends condemnation toward the guilty (Mat 12:37; Luk 6:37). This can be legitimate (passing righteous judgment) or illegitimate (condemning the righteous, Pro 17:15). The human-to-human extension of condemnation is a reality in the cluster — and it requires careful scrutiny because it is susceptible to corruption.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the human judge who "reckons" the worth of a case and passes verdict (947-002: the judges who decide between persons, Deu 25:1) extends the reckoning act toward others. Paul's instruction to "consider" others (implied in the adiakritos material, G0087) — impartial assessment of others is a giving of honest moral reckoning toward them.

**[CLUSTER — T4.3]** E. The giving interface is present in A2 (outward-flowing righteousness), B (truth-speaking to others), C-adjacent (human judicial vindication), D (commissioned justice), E (human judgment), and F (impartial assessment of others). The cluster reveals that righteousness in the human person is inherently other-directed — it reaches toward others in giving modes across multiple forms.

**T4.3.2** — What enables genuine extension toward others?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The inner conditions enabling genuine extension of righteousness toward others: (1) being genuinely righteous oneself — "the righteous guides his neighbor" (Pro 12:26) implies the guide is actually righteous; (2) receiving justice before giving it — the commissioned avenger acts under delegated authority (Rom 13:4), not from personal grievance; (3) freedom from the self-serving distortions (Pro 17:15: the judge who justifies the wicked for personal gain is the failure case). Genuine giving of righteousness requires the giver to be constituted in righteousness themselves.

**T4.3.3** — What must the person have received before they can give?

**[CLUSTER]** E. The pattern across the cluster is clear: before the person can give righteousness to others, they must have received it from God. The righteous person who guides their neighbor (A2-012) is first themselves righteous. The human judge who acquits the innocent (C-adjacent, 947-002) exercises a delegated judicial authority that comes from God. The commissioned avenger (D: Rom 13:4) acts on the basis of divine commission, not personal authority. The prerequisite is reception: the person must first stand in right relationship with God (received righteousness, C) before they can credibly extend righteous orientation toward others.

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S — as noted above.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the person receives the verdict of condemnation (external, from judge), the relief of freedom from condemnation (from God through Christ), or the self-condemnation of the conscience (from within, through autokatakritos). **[CLUSTER — T4.4]** E. Reception of righteousness from others operates through teaching/formation (A2, B: instruction in right paths), through the declared verdict of justification received by faith (C), through received justice from God or commissioned agents (D), and through received verdict of condemnation or freedom (E). ### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries
