## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. God's righteousness is not derived from anything external to himself — it is declared foundational and permanent across all his ways and all his works (Deu 32:4; Psa 145:17; Psa 111:3). The verse evidence reveals that righteousness in God is not a quality he exercises when appropriate; it is the constitutive character of everything he does. He cannot act unrighteously — not by constraint but by nature: "he does no injustice" (Zep 3:5). His righteousness is structural: it is the foundation of his throne (Psa 89:14; Psa 97:2). This reveals a God whose essential nature is moral order, truth, and right — not as principles he follows but as what he is.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The verse evidence shows human righteousness positioned consistently as derived from and answerable to God's righteousness. God's righteousness is the standard against which human standing is measured — "you are righteous; we have acted wickedly" (Neh 9:33; Ezr 9:15; Lam 1:18; Dan 9:7). In the NT, human righteousness is explicitly a participation in God's nature: the person is "born of him" who is righteous (1Jo 2:29), and the believer is constituted as "the righteousness of God" in Christ (2Cor 5:21, VSG M26-A2-008). The characteristic in the human images the character of God precisely because it is not self-generated but received.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. The uprightness sub-group (yo.sher, me.shar, ne.kho.chah) reveals that straightness and level-ness are qualities God possesses and expresses: "I the Lord speak the truth; I declare what is right" (Isa 45:19, VSG 1212-001). The Messianic scepter of uprightness (Heb 1:8, VSG 1218-001) reveals uprightness as a divine-royal quality. The path of the righteous is "level" because God makes it so (Isa 26:7). What is straight in God becomes the ground for what is straight in the person.

**[C — Justification]** E. The verse evidence reveals a God who is simultaneously just and the justifier — this pairing in Rom 3:26 (VSG 3194-001) is the theological core of justification. God's nature as righteous judge is the same nature that makes his justifying act possible. Justification is not God setting aside his righteousness — it is God expressing his righteousness through Christ. The term dikaiōsis (justification) in Rom 4:25 (VSG 3203-001) names the restoration of right standing as flowing from God's own righteous nature.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The verse evidence reveals that God's avenging justice flows from the same righteous character that grounds all his other acts. "Vengeance is mine; I will repay" (Rom 12:19; Heb 10:30, VSG 6841-002/004) names God as the exclusive legitimate possessor of retributive authority. His judgments in their most severe expressions are declared "just and true" (Rev 19:2, VSG 944-002). Divine retribution is not rage — it is the expression of a nature that cannot permit wrong to remain unaddressed.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The verse evidence reveals that God's condemnation is an expression of the same nature that makes his justification significant. Condemnation is the negative judicial verdict of a God whose righteousness is real — "he condemned sin in the flesh" (Rom 8:3, VSG 4846-002). The ministry of condemnation exists because God is just (2Cor 3:9, VSG 738-001). The self-condemned person (Tit 3:11, VSG 4848-001) reveals that the moral structure God has built into persons includes the capacity for conscience to echo divine judgment.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The verse evidence reveals a God who reckons — who counts, credits, regards, and assigns moral standing. "It was counted to him as righteousness" (Rom 4:3–11, VSG 3410-001). This divine reckoning is not arbitrary; it is the act of a God whose nature is truth and right. The term kardiognōstēs — heart-knower (Act 1:24; 15:8, in VSG M26-A1-002) reveals that divine reckoning penetrates to the actual inner state, not mere appearances. God's moral reckoning images perfect knowledge joined to perfect justice.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The structural opposites (ra.sha, a.val) reveal God's character by contrast. He does no a.val — no injustice (Deu 32:4; Zep 3:5). The very existence of the wicked category (ra.sha) assumes a God against whom wickedness is defined. The wicked are "not so" (Psa 1:4) — they are the negative image of what God is. The opposites exist in the cluster specifically because God's righteousness needs a contrast by which its absolute character becomes visible.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster as a whole reveals a God whose essential nature is righteousness — not as one attribute among others but as the architectonic quality of his being from which all the sub-groups derive their coherence. A1 names what God is; A2 shows the human person as derived image; C, D, and E are the modes in which God's righteousness acts in history; F is the faculty by which God's righteousness assesses; B qualifies the inner texture of righteous character; G defines the boundary. Every sub-group traces back to one source: the nature of God.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes, explicitly and repeatedly. "The Lord is righteous in all his ways" (Psa 145:17); "just and upright is he" (Deu 32:4); "righteous Father" (Joh 17:25). The attribution reveals that righteousness in God is prior to and constitutive of everything else — before law, before covenant, before creation. This means righteousness in the human person is not an achievement but a participation in something that already fully exists in God. The human instance is always secondary and derivative; its significance in the person is precisely that it reflects and images what is primary in God.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "born of him" who is righteous (1Jo 2:29); "the righteousness of God" in Christ (2Cor 5:21, VSG M26-A2-008). The attribution of righteousness to God reveals that when the human person possesses it, they are participating in something of transcendent origin. In the OT, this explains why the righteous person can appeal to God on the basis of their righteousness (Psa 7:8; 18:20, VSG 942-001) — the appeal works because righteousness in the person reflects and aligns with righteousness in God.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — "I the Lord speak the truth; I declare what is right" (Isa 45:19); the Messianic scepter of uprightness (Heb 1:8). That God possesses and expresses uprightness reveals that straightness/level-ness in the human person is not a merely human virtue — it is an imaging of divine character. The human who "walks in uprightness" (Pro 14:2) is walking in alignment with the directional quality of God himself.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — God is "just and the justifier" (Rom 3:26). The dual attribution is crucial: God does not set aside his justice to justify — he is the righteous one who justifies. This reveals that human justification is not God relaxing his standards but God expressing his righteousness through a new mode. The significance for the human person: justification is not a legal fiction but a genuine moral transformation grounded in the one who is himself righteous.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord" (Rom 12:19; Heb 10:30). The exclusive attribution of retributive authority to God reveals that avenging justice in the human is always derivative, commissioned, and bounded. The magistrate is "God's servant, an avenger" (Rom 13:4) — not an autonomous agent of retribution. The significance: human retributive action is legitimate only when it images and serves divine retributive justice, not when it expresses personal vengeance.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — God condemns sin (Rom 8:3); his judgments are just (Rev 15:3; 19:2). The verse evidence attributes condemnation to God as a judicial act expressing his righteousness. The significance for the human person: condemnation is a real and serious verdict because the God who passes it is genuinely righteous. The freedom from condemnation (VSG 4846-002) is therefore a profound inner-being reality — not merely an administrative acquittal but a status secured before the actually-righteous God.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — God counts faith as righteousness (Rom 4:3–11); God is heart-knower (Act 1:24). The attribution of reckoning to God reveals that the human capacity for moral reckoning images a divine faculty. The significance: the human inner act of moral reckoning (logizomai in Rom 6:11; 8:18) participates in a mode of knowing and assigning worth that God himself performs. Human reckoning is an image of divine reckoning, which is why Paul can instruct believers to "count yourselves dead to sin" as a spiritually real act.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Scripture does not attribute wickedness or injustice to God — precisely the opposite: "without iniquity" (Deu 32:4); "he does no injustice" (Zep 3:5). The significance of this non-attribution: wickedness in the human person is not a divine quality distorted — it is the absence of a divine quality. Wickedness is not an alternative to righteousness; it is the failure of righteousness. The human who is ra.sha is a person in whom the image is broken at the righteousness point.

**[CLUSTER]** Scripture's explicit attribution of righteousness to God is the single most theologically significant fact about this cluster. It means that every sub-group is not studying a human quality that God happens also to possess — it is studying a divine quality that humans participate in, image, fail to image, or need to receive from outside themselves.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. Not applicable — Scripture is not silent here. God's righteousness is one of the most extensively and explicitly attested divine attributes in the entire biblical corpus.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** S. The relevant silence is not about God lacking righteousness but about the specific mechanisms of human righteousness (hungering, forfeiting, accumulating) not being attributed to God. God does not hunger for righteousness — he is righteous. The developmental and conditional aspects of human righteousness (M26-A2-002; M26-A2-006) have no divine counterpart, which suggests these aspects are specifically creaturely — they belong to the nature of finite moral beings who must grow into what God simply is.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. The specific vocabulary of B (yo.sher, me.shar as human inner disposition) is not extensively attributed to God in its inner-disposition mode. The struggle toward uprightness — the human inner work of aligning with what is straight — is not a divine struggle. This silence suggests that uprightness in B is a specifically creaturely mode of relating to the divine quality: the finite person's inner work of conforming to what God simply is.

**[C — Justification]** S. God is not justified — he is the justifier. The complete absence of God as the recipient of justification confirms that justification is a relational act flowing from God toward the creature. Justification presupposes a gap between the standard and the one being assessed. No such gap exists in God.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** S. The specific mode of petitioning for divine vengeance (944-001: the cry of the wronged) has no divine counterpart. God does not cry out to a higher authority for justice — he is the final authority. This silence places the cry for justice firmly in the creaturely domain.

**[E — Condemnation]** S. God is not condemned — he is the one who condemns. Self-condemnation (autokatakritos, Tit 3:11) is specifically a creaturely phenomenon: the conscience turned against the self. This has no divine analogue.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** S. The inner struggle of reckoning — working out whether to count oneself dead to sin (Rom 6:11), weighing sufferings against future glory (Rom 8:18) — is specifically creaturely. God's reckoning is immediate and complete; the human reckoning is laboured and instructed. The silence on God struggling to reckon suggests that the faculty itself images a divine act but must be exercised in a creaturely mode.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. The complete silence on God possessing wickedness or injustice is definitional. God's nature is the measure against which G-terms are defined as their opposite. The silence is the most important thing about G in relation to God.

**[CLUSTER]** The pattern of silence in T0.1.3 reveals a consistent structure: the divine-to-human direction is source-to-image, standard-to-measured, giver-to-receiver. The specific creaturely modes (struggling, petitioning, forfeiting, being condemned, working to reckon) have no divine counterpart because they arise from the gap between the creature and the standard — the gap that the cluster's narrative (from A1 through C) is designed to close.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1 addresses God's righteousness, not human created purpose directly. The created-purpose question is answered primarily in A2.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The verse evidence across A2's VSGs reveals that righteousness equips the human person for relationship with God, stability in life, and protective presence for others. The righteous person is "seen, tested, known, upheld" by God (VSG 3246-002); stands before God in prayer and petition (VSG 942-001); is guarded and established (VSG M26-A2-013). Community function: the presence of righteous persons carries protective power — Abraham's intercession for Sodom (Gen 18:23–28, VSG M26-A2-015) shows the righteous as a preservative force within their communities. The person is equipped to be: in right relation with God, stable in life, generative toward others, and aligned with the grain of how reality is structured.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. The B verses show uprightness equipping the person for honest speech, discernment, and recognition of what is right. "Then you will understand righteousness and justice and equity, every good path" (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002). Uprightness enables the person to walk in ways that are not devious (Pro 14:2) — equipped for reliable, straight-dealing life.

**[C — Justification]** E. Justification equips the person for heir-status and new standing before God. "Being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7, VSG 3194-001). The purpose is not merely forensic acquittal — it establishes a new relational position enabling participation in divine life.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The D verses suggest that the capacity for avenging justice equips the person to entrust retribution to God rather than executing it personally. "Leave it to the wrath of God" (Rom 12:19, VSG 6841-002) — the inner act of relinquishing personal vengeance is itself an act requiring conviction that God will rightly repay. The purpose: the human capacity to recognise wrong and desire its correction is real and good, but it is purposed to flow toward God (petition, trust) rather than self-execution.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The E verses reveal that the capacity for condemnation serves a diagnostic and moral purpose. Self-condemnation (autokatakritos, Tit 3:11) shows that the person is built with an inner mechanism tracking moral failure. The condemning capacity — even directed outward (Mat 12:41–42, VSG 4846-001) — reveals a moral faculty oriented toward truth. The purpose: the capacity for condemnation equips the person to take moral distinctions seriously.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The F verses show the reckoning faculty equipping the person for sustained moral orientation. "Count yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus" (Rom 6:11, VSG 3410-001). The reckoning faculty enables the person to apply theological reality to inner experience — to treat as true what God has declared. The purpose: the reckoning faculty is needed to receive and live from justification. Without it, the declared status of justification would have no inner-life traction.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The G terms reveal created purpose by negation. "They do not know how to do right" (Amo 3:10, VSG 1214-001) names the failure of the capacity for moral orientation. The structural opposites reveal that the created purpose of righteousness is to enable the person to know and do what is right — a capacity that can be forfeited.

**[CLUSTER]** Across the cluster, righteousness equips the human person to: (1) stand in right relation with God (A2, C); (2) live stably and generatively in the created order (A2, B); (3) function as a moral agent who can recognise right and wrong (F, E); (4) serve as a preservative and redemptive presence for others (A2-015); (5) entrust retributive justice to God rather than executing it autonomously (D). The cluster reveals a comprehensive created purpose: the person made righteous is equipped for full participation in the moral order God has established.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1's verse evidence addresses God's eternal righteousness. The divine attribute is prior to both original design and the fall.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Both. The OT assumes some persons are righteous — Abraham, Noah, Job — without specifying a restored or new state; the wisdom material speaks of the righteous as a recognisable category within the created order. The Ezekiel cluster (M26-A2-002) presupposes that persons can be righteous and then fall — implying original capacity. However, the NT explicitly reverses the OT assumption: "none is righteous, no, not one" (Rom 3:10). In the NT the primary mode is received through faith in Christ — a response to the fallen condition. Both streams present: the created design includes the capacity for righteousness; the fallen condition makes the reception-through-faith mode necessary.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Both. The B verses speak of uprightness as a path the person walks in or turns away from (Pro 2:13; Pro 14:2). This implies an original design — the path exists and can be taken. The effort required (teaching, leading, Pro 4:11) implies uprightness is not automatic — it requires formation. Both elements present.

**[C — Justification]** E. Specifically a response to the fallen condition. The terms all presuppose a prior moral deficit — a gap between actual and required standing. Job 4:17 ("Can mortal man be in the right before God?") frames the question that justification answers. Justification is not an element of original design — it is God's response to the condition in which persons find themselves unable to stand before him.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. A response to the fallen condition — it presupposes wrong done, harm inflicted, rights violated. In a world without injustice, there is no category for ekdikēsis or ekdikeō.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. A response to the fallen condition. Condemnation presupposes guilt — a departure from right standing. Self-condemnation (autokatakritos) presupposes that the person has acted in ways their own inner being recognises as wrong.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The reckoning faculty appears to belong to the original design — the capacity to weigh, count, and assess moral worth is part of how rational moral agents function. However, the specific content of reckoning in the verse evidence (counting oneself dead to sin, reckoning faith as righteousness, bearing accountable before God) is shaped by the fallen condition. The faculty is original; the specific reckoning tasks are responses to fallenness and redemption.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Wickedness and injustice are responses to the fallen condition — they name what has gone wrong. The presence of ra.sha as a recognisable category implies a departure from an original state.

**[CLUSTER]** A clear pattern: A2 and B belong to both original design and fallen response; C, D, and E are specifically responses to the fallen condition; F's faculty is original but its specific exercise is shaped by fallenness; G names the fallen state by opposition. The created person was designed with the capacity for righteousness; the fallen person requires justification (C), faces condemnation (E), and needs avenging justice (D) because that original capacity has been compromised.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — Dan 9:24 — "to bring in everlasting righteousness" — and the eschatological acclamations in Revelation (Rev 15:3–19:2, VSG M26-A1-002) locate God's righteousness as the goal toward which history moves. God's righteousness is not only what he currently is — it is what will be fully revealed and universally acknowledged.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — "the fine linen is the righteous deeds of the saints" (Rev 19:8) — the bride's garment is the accumulated righteousness of the people, pointing to eschatological completion. "The righteousness that comes by faith" in Heb 11:7 (VSG 4846-001) frames righteousness as heir-status oriented toward a promised future.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. The B verse evidence does not contain explicit eschatological language. Uprightness is presented as a current orientation and way of walking. Silence noted.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7, VSG 3194-001). Justification establishes the person in new standing oriented toward future inheritance. The resurrection context in Rom 4:24–25 (VSG 3203-001) links justification to the risen Christ and the eschatological reality his resurrection inaugurates.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the cry of the martyrs in Rev 6:10 (VSG 944-001) is directed toward the final day of judgment. The "days of vengeance" (Luk 21:22, VSG 945-001) are eschatological. God's righteous judgment as the eschatological culmination (2Th 1:8, VSG 945-001) shows D pointing toward a future fullness in which all wrongs are finally addressed.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the freedom from condemnation in Rom 8:34 (VSG 4846-002) is grounded in the risen and interceding Christ, pointing toward the final judgment. The "punishment of eternal destruction" (2Th 1:9, VSG 949-001) locates final condemnation in the eschatological future.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — "the sufferings of this present time are not worth comparing with the glory that is to be revealed" (Rom 8:18, VSG 3410-001). The reckoning faculty is explicitly applied to the eschatological future: counting present suffering against future glory is an act of inner orientation toward what is coming.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — Rev 22:11 (VSG 1225-001): "let the evildoer still do evil... and the righteous still do right, and the holy still be holy." The eschatological crystallisation of moral identity — the future fullness is one in which the distinction between righteous and wicked is finalised and permanent.

**[CLUSTER]** Across the cluster, righteousness is oriented toward a future fullness that is both individual (the person becoming fully righteous) and cosmic (God's righteousness universally acknowledged and all wrongs addressed). B is the sole silence. This consistent orientation suggests that righteousness is not primarily about present moral performance but about a movement toward full alignment with God's own righteousness that is as yet unrealised.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1 addresses the divine character itself. The question is answered primarily in A2.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The verse evidence in A2 reveals that righteousness in the human person instantiates the moral character of God — the quality of being rightly ordered toward God, neighbour, and creation. The person who "does what is just and right" (Eze 18:21, VSG M26-A2-002), who "hungers and thirsts after righteousness" (Mat 5:6, implied in VSG M26-A2-006), who has "God's law written on their heart" (M26-A2-005), is expressing the image of a God who is himself perfectly rightly ordered.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. B instantiates the quality of truth and straightness in speech and conduct. "He who walks righteously and speaks uprightly" (Isa 33:15, VSG 1212-002) images the God who "speaks the truth" and "declares what is right" (Isa 45:19). The inner disposition of straightness in the person images the divine quality of being utterly without deviation from truth and right.

**[C — Justification]** E. Justification does not itself express the divine image in the person — it restores the conditions under which the divine image can be expressed. C creates the conditions for the image-expression that A2 and B describe. The dikaiōma of Rev 19:8 — "the righteous deeds of the saints" as the bride's garment — shows that the accumulated righteous acts of justified persons become their identity.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The commissioned human avenger (Rom 13:4) images a specific aspect of divine character: the attribute of just retribution. The magistrate as "God's servant, an avenger" is explicitly functioning as a delegated image-bearer. The delegation itself (not autonomous but commissioned) is the key: the image-bearing is real but bounded.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The human capacity for moral condemnation — recognising guilt, calling wrong "wrong" — images the divine capacity for just judgment. When the Ninevites condemn the generation that rejected greater light (Mat 12:41, VSG 4846-001), they instantiate the divine image in its judicial aspect: the recognition that moral distinctions are real and that failing to respond to moral truth carries consequences.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The reckoning faculty images the divine capacity for moral assessment. God reckons faith as righteousness (Rom 4:3–11); the human person is instructed to reckon themselves dead to sin (Rom 6:11). The human reckoning is the image of divine reckoning — the faculty by which moral reality is apprehended, assigned, and lived from.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The structural opposites reveal the image by negation: the ra.sha person is one in whom the image is distorted or denied. Wickedness is not a different kind of image-bearing — it is the failure of image-bearing. The existence of the G-category confirms that the divine image includes righteousness as a constitutive element: to be wicked is to fail at being what the image-bearer is designed to be.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster reveals that the divine image, in the domain of righteousness, has several facets: moral character (A2), truthful straightness (B), just judgment (D, E), and moral reckoning (F). These are not separate facets — they are the same image expressed through different modes of the same underlying quality.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1 is the divine instance. The question concerns the human instance's relationship to the divine.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Both genuinely shared and structurally analogical. The sharing is real: "born of him" (1Jo 2:29) implies genuine participation in the divine quality; "the righteousness of God" in 2Cor 5:21 is stated as the actual righteousness of God being constituted in the person. However, human righteousness can be forfeited (Eze 18:24, VSG M26-A2-002); God's cannot. The correct reading: the sharing is genuine but asymmetric — the human participates in a quality whose original and inexhaustible instance is in God.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. The uprightness vocabulary is used of both God and persons. The quality is shared — but directionally: God is the source and standard of what is straight; the person is the one who aligns with that standard. Analogue-with-genuine-participation is the correct framing.

**[C — Justification]** S. Justification as such is not a shared characteristic — it is a unidirectional act from God toward the creature. God does not receive justification; he performs it.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The commissioned human avenger shares the quality in a delegated sense — genuine but derivative. "Vengeance is mine" simultaneously attributes the quality exclusively to God while authorising its delegated expression. The sharing is real but bounded.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The human capacity for condemnation is a creaturely analogue with genuine participatory elements. But self-condemnation (autokatakritos) is exclusively creaturely — it has no divine analogue because God's inner being does not work against itself.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The reckoning faculty is genuinely shared in its nature but asymmetric in its exercise. God's reckoning is omniscient and penetrating; human reckoning is instructed, partial, and effortful. The genuine participation is established by Paul commanding the human reckoning (Rom 6:11) as a real inner act with real consequences.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. Wickedness and injustice are not shared between God and humans.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster evidence consistently points to a shared characteristic that is asymmetric: the human person genuinely participates in a quality whose perfect and inexhaustible instance is in God. The participation is not metaphorical — it is constitutive of what the human person is when righteous. But it is bounded, derivative, and dependent — the human instance cannot exist independently of the divine source.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** S. A1 does not address the condition of the divine image in the human person.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The verse evidence is explicit: righteousness in the person reveals an image in alignment with its source; its absence reveals an image in disorder. "The righteousness of the righteous shall be upon himself, and the wickedness of the wicked shall be upon himself" (Eze 18:20, VSG M26-A2-002) — the person's righteousness or wickedness is their own inner state. The presence of righteousness reveals a person whose inner being is rightly oriented toward God and neighbour. Its absence (M26-A2-003: distortion and counterfeit; M26-A2-004: far from righteousness) reveals an image that has been distorted, abandoned, or never formed.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. "They do not know how to do right" (Amo 3:10, VSG 1214-001) — the absence of uprightness reveals an image that has lost its orientation toward what is straight and true. The inner compass is broken. The presence of uprightness reveals a functioning moral compass, aligned with the divine quality of truth and straightness.

**[C — Justification]** E. Justification does not reveal the condition of the image in the present person — it transforms it. The person who receives justification passes from broken-image standing (guilty before God) to restored-image standing (righteous before God). Heb 11:7 — "an heir of the righteousness that comes by faith" — shows the justified person as one whose image has been rehabilitated through an act of grace.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** S. The presence or absence of the avenging faculty does not in itself reveal the condition of the divine image. The image condition is revealed by how the faculty is exercised, not merely by its presence.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The capacity for self-condemnation (autokatakritos, Tit 3:11) reveals an image that is self-aware enough to recognise its own failure — a genuine moral faculty functioning to expose departure from righteousness. The freedom from condemnation in Rom 8:34 reveals an image that has been restored — conscience no longer working against the self because the verdict of God has changed.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The reckoning faculty's presence reveals a person capable of engaging in the moral-theological work of assigning right standing and weighing moral worth. Its exercise in the right direction (counting oneself dead to sin, alive to God) reveals an image functioning in alignment with divine reckoning. Its misapplication (reckoning not from faith — Rom 14:23) reveals an image whose reckoning faculty is misdirected.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The presence of wickedness (ra.sha) and injustice (a.val) in a person reveals an image in profound disorder — a person who has turned against the moral order that the image is designed to instantiate. The ra.sha person is not merely imperfect — they are a person from whom the central quality of the divine image (righteousness) is absent or actively opposed.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster reveals that righteousness is a load-bearing element of the divine image — its presence indicates an image in alignment with its source; its absence or distortion (A2-003, G) indicates an image in disorder. The cluster also reveals gradations: from the person in whom righteousness is stable and protected (A2-013), to the person in whom it is conditional and forfeitable (A2-002), to the person in whom it is absent (A2-004), to the person who actively opposes it (G).

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — extensively. Melchizedek as "king of righteousness" (Heb 7:2, VSG M26-A1-007) is a typological figure. Wisdom who "walks in the paths of righteousness" (Pro 8:20, VSG M26-A1-007) participates typologically in Messianic-divine quality. The righteous Branch (Jer 23:6; 33:16), the righteous Servant (Isa 53:11), the Messianic king who is "righteous and having salvation" (Zec 9:9) — all are typological deployments pointing toward Christ as the incarnate expression of divine righteousness.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — Noah's righteousness (Heb 11:7, VSG 4846-001) is typological: his righteous act of building the ark in faith points toward the righteousness that comes by faith in Christ. Abraham's credited righteousness (Rom 4:3–9, VSG 3410-001) is explicitly typological in Paul's argument: Abraham's case is a type of the faith-righteousness available to all believers.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. The Messianic scepter of uprightness (Heb 1:8, VSG 1218-001) is explicitly typological — the uprightness of the Messianic king's rule is the perfected instantiation of what the uprightness vocabulary names.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — the entire OT judicial vocabulary (tsa.deq, 947-002: acquitting the innocent) is typological in relation to divine justification. The human courtroom (Deu 25:1) is a type of the divine courtroom; the acquittal of the innocent is a type of the justification of the ungodly by faith.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — Moses' act of avenging the oppressed Israelite (Act 7:24, VSG 945-001) is identified as part of the pattern of Israel's rejection of its deliverer, pointing toward Christ. The persistent widow's cry (Luk 18:3–8, VSG 944-001) is deployed by Jesus as a type of the elect's eschatological cry for divine justice.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — the Ninevites' repentance as a condemnation of unrepentant Israel (Mat 12:41–42; Luk 11:31–32, VSG 4846-001) is typological: a past generation's response to less light condemns a present generation's response to greater light.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Abraham's faith being counted as righteousness (Rom 4:3–9, VSG 3410-001) is the most extensively worked typological passage in the cluster. Paul explicitly uses Abraham's case as a type applicable to all who believe.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. The structural opposites do not appear to be deployed typologically in the verse evidence. They define the boundary of righteousness but are not used to point toward a greater reality. Silence noted.

**[CLUSTER]** Typological use of righteousness-vocabulary is extensive and structurally important across the cluster — A1, A2, B, C, D, E, and F all show typological deployment. The pattern is consistently Christological: OT instances of righteousness point toward Christ as the one who fulfils, exceeds, and incarnates the divine righteousness that was only partially visible in the OT types. G is the sole silence.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Divine-to-human: God's righteousness establishes the pattern, and the Messianic types participate in and point toward the divine instance.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Human-to-divine: Abraham's human righteousness (credited by faith) points typologically toward a larger reality — all who believe participate in the same credited righteousness (Rom 4:23–24). The suffering righteous person of the Psalms (M26-A2-016) points toward Christ.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Divine-established-pattern toward Messianic expression — the divine uprightness establishes what the Messianic king's scepter represents.

**[C — Justification]** E. Human-to-divine: the human judicial pattern (acquit the innocent, Deu 25:1) points toward the divine judicial act of justification. God uses the human courtroom as a type and then surpasses the type by justifying the ungodly by faith.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Human-to-divine: Moses' act points typologically toward Christ as the deliverer rejected by Israel.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Human historical instance points toward eschatological divine judgment.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Human-instance-revealing-divine-pattern: Abraham's case reveals the divine pattern (credited righteousness), then divine pattern applied to all subsequent believers.

**[CLUSTER]** Both directions of typology are present in M26, but the dominant direction is human-to-divine: human instances of righteousness, justice, and reckoning point toward and are fulfilled by the divine instance in Christ. The exception is A1, where the divine establishes the pattern and the creaturely instances participate in it downward. This confirms the cluster's essential structure: A1 is the source from which all other sub-groups derive their meaning.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Named by its source and possessor, not by its content. This signals that God's righteousness is definitionally prior: it is the form of righteousness from which all other sub-groups derive their meaning. The name signals that we are studying righteousness at its source, not at its expression in the human person.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Named "Human Righteousness — State of being right." The qualifier "state of being right" is analytically significant: it names righteousness as a condition, not an act or a quality among others. It signals that righteousness in the human is not primarily what one does but what one is — a governing state of the person.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Named "Moral Uprightness: Inner Disposition of Straightness." Two signals: (1) dispositional characteristic — an inner orientation, not merely a behavioural pattern; (2) the root metaphor is spatial — straight, level, not crooked. The Hebrew vocabulary (yo.sher from ya.shar — to be straight; me.shar from the same root) confirms this spatial-moral axis.

**[C — Justification]** E. Named "Justification: Declaring or Making Righteous." Signals a process — an act performed on someone. The compound descriptor ("declaring or making") holds open the classic theological debate between forensic and transformative justification — the verse evidence contains both.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Named "Avenging Justice: Retribution for Wrongdoing." Signals that this is not abstract justice but enacted, responsive justice — triggered by a specific moral failure and directed toward its correction or punishment.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Named "Condemnation: Judicial Sentence Against." Signals the judicial frame — this is a verdict. "Judicial sentence against" positions E as the negative counterpart of C.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Named "Moral Reckoning: Evaluative Faculty of Worth." Signals that F is a faculty — a capacity of the inner person — not a state or an act. This is the only sub-group in the cluster that names itself primarily as a faculty. Analytically significant.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Named "Structural Opposites: Wickedness and Injustice." Explicit: these are definitional opposites. They exist in the cluster to define righteousness by what it excludes.

**[CLUSTER]** The names together reveal the cluster's analytical structure: a source (A1), a derived state (A2), a dispositional quality (B), a process (C), two judicial acts (D, E), a faculty (F), and a defining boundary (G). This is not a list of synonyms for righteousness — it is a map of a complex moral-theological reality with different elements playing different structural roles.

---

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. The primary OT terms — tsad.diq (H6662), tse.da.qah (H6666), tse.deq (H6664G/H) — share the root ts.d.q, whose core meaning is conformity to a norm or standard. Applied to God in A1, the terms reveal that God is the one who conforms perfectly to the norm — indeed, he IS the norm. The Greek term dikaiosunē (G1343) carries the same semantic weight: rightness, conformity to the standard of what is just and true. Both root families point to a relational-judicial concept: being right in relation to the standard.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The same Hebrew root (ts.d.q) and Greek root (dik-) govern A2, applied to the human person. The human is tsad.diq — one who conforms to the norm, who is right in relation to the standard. The definitional revelation: righteousness in the human is relational, not self-referential. To be righteous is to be right in relation to God's standard.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. The B terms — yo.sher (H3476), me.shar (H4339), ne.kho.chah (H5229), ye.sha.rah (H3483) — come from a different root family (y.sh.r: to be straight, level, right). The euthutēs (G2118) is the Greek equivalent. These terms reveal a spatial-moral concept: the inner being oriented in a straight line, not deviously or crookedly. The B vocabulary is distinct from the A vocabulary — different root, different primary metaphor, overlapping semantic territory.

**[C — Justification]** E. The primary terms — tsa.deq (H6663, verb: to be or declare righteous), dikaioō (G1344, verb: to justify/declare righteous), dikaiōsis (G1347, noun: justification) — reveal a process character. These are action terms: the focus is on the act of declaring or making righteous. The Greek dikaioō in particular carries a judicial sense: to pronounce a verdict of righteousness, to acquit.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The D terms — ekdikeō (G1556), ekdikēsis (G1557), ekdikos (G1558), antapodidōmi (G0467), dikaiokrisia (G1341) — all carry the ek- prefix meaning "out from" or the anti- prefix meaning "in return." The definitional revelation: D-terms are response terms. They name actions that arise from a prior wrong — they require a prior violation as their trigger.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The E terms — katakrinō (G2632), katadikazō (G2613), katakrisis (G2633), dikē (G1349), autokatakritos (G0843) — all carry the kata- prefix meaning "down against" or "in accord with." The definitional revelation: E-terms name downward judicial acts — verdicts directed against a person on the basis of assessed guilt. The reflexive form (autokatakritos) reveals that the inner person can turn the judicial faculty against itself.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The F terms — logizomai (G3049), axioō (G0515), a.rakh (H6186B), hupodikos (G5267), adiakritos (G0087) — reveal a cognitive-judicial faculty. The logizomai root (related to logos: word, reason) reveals that moral reckoning is a rational act — it involves inner deliberation, assessment, and conclusion. The faculty is cognitive in mode but moral in content.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The G terms — ra.sha (H7561: to be wicked; H7563: the wicked one), a.val (H5765: to act unjustly; H5766A: injustice) — name states and acts that are directionally opposed to the ts.d.q root. Ra.sha is consistently paired with tsad.diq throughout the OT as its exact opposite. A.val names a deviation from the straight — a perversion of what should be upright.

**[CLUSTER]** Two distinct root families govern the cluster: ts.d.q / dik- (rightness, conformity to standard) and y.sh.r (straightness, uprightness). These carry different primary metaphors and occupy different semantic territory. F has its own distinct vocabulary (logizomai, axioō, a.rakh). G draws on a third root (ra.sha, a.val). The three root families together reveal that "righteousness and justice" is a composite cluster holding together conformity-to-standard, straightness-of-orientation, and cognitive-reckoning as related but distinct aspects of one moral reality.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Relational and constitutional both present. Relational: God's righteousness is expressed toward his people (covenant faithfulness, A1-003), toward the world (judgment, A1-002), toward the Messiah (A1-007). Constitutional: righteousness is the foundation of the throne (Psa 89:14; 97:2) — structural to God's nature, not circumstantial.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Strongly relational: righteousness in the human is always defined in relation to God's standard, to covenant, and to other persons. The directional implication runs consistently: oriented toward God (A2-014), toward others (A2-012), toward God's standard (A2-006). Constitutional also: righteousness becomes a governing state, not merely a quality applied situationally.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Constitutional and directional: the y.sh.r vocabulary names an inner orientation — a direction of the inner being toward what is straight. The person walks in uprightness (a path metaphor), and the inner being is either straight or devious (Pro 2:13–14).

**[C — Justification]** E. Directional and relational: justification is always directed from the judging party toward the judged person — a one-directional act with a specific relational structure. The relational implication: justification changes the standing of the person before God.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Directional: avenging justice flows from the wronged party (or their advocate) toward the wrongdoer. Always responsive — triggered by prior injustice. Relational: involves at minimum three parties — the wronged, the wrongdoer, and the avenger.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Directional: the kata- prefix in all E-terms names a downward direction — sentence passed down on the condemned person. The directional implication of autokatakritos (self-condemned) is reflexive: judgment turns back on the self. Relational: condemnation always involves judge and condemned.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Object-directed: one always reckons something. The directional implication: reckoning moves from the inner faculty outward toward the object being assessed, then back inward as a basis for action (Rom 6:11). Constitutional: the faculty itself is a structural feature of the inner person.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Constitutional and directional: wickedness and injustice name constitutional orientations of the inner person that set the person against the moral order. The directional implication: the ra.sha person moves away from God and toward harm of others.

**[CLUSTER]** The dominant implication across the cluster is relational: righteousness in all its sub-group forms is defined in relation to God's standard, to covenant, to other persons, and to the moral order. The secondary implication is directional: each sub-group names a specific direction. The constitutional implication appears specifically in A1, A2, and G — the governing-state character of righteousness at its fullest.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Quality — and more precisely, a constitutive attribute. God's righteousness is not something he does (act), a way he tends to behave (disposition), or a state he finds himself in (condition). It is what he is: the structural quality of his nature from which all righteous acts flow. The verse evidence establishes this through permanence (Psa 111:3; Isa 51:6), universality (Psa 145:17: "all his ways"), and structural role (foundation of the throne).

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Condition — a governing state of the person. The verse evidence in A2 consistently positions righteousness as a state the person is in (or fails to maintain), not primarily as a discrete act. The Ezekiel cluster (M26-A2-002) treats it as a standing — forfeitable, non-transferable. The stability material (M26-A2-013) describes it as a rooted and established condition. The person-type term (tsad.diq — the righteous person, VSG 3246-001) confirms that righteousness names a kind of person, not merely a kind of action.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Disposition — specifically, an inner orientation of the person toward what is straight. The path metaphor (walking in uprightness, Pro 2:13; Pro 14:2) and the directional language (speak uprightly, walk righteously, Isa 33:15) identify B as a dispositional characteristic: the habitual orientation of the inner being in a particular direction.

**[C — Justification]** E. Act — specifically, a judicial act directed at a person's standing. The verb forms dominate (dikaioō, tsa.deq in its acquitting sense): justification is something done, not a state one inhabits or a disposition one develops. The act produces a new condition (the justified state) — but the characteristic itself is the act, not the resulting condition.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Act — responsive and enacted. The D-terms are all action terms (avenge, repay, punish, give justice). The characteristic is a movement from injustice-suffered to justice-enacted.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Act — judicial pronouncement. The E-terms name the verdict (katakrisis), the act of passing it (katakrinō), and the resulting state. The reflexive form (autokatakritos) reveals a special case where the act is also the condition — the self-condemned person is one who has passed and is under the verdict simultaneously.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Faculty — the evaluative capacity of the inner person. Logizomai names an inner act (reckoning, counting) but the repeated use across diverse contexts (weighing suffering, considering oneself dead to sin, crediting righteousness) reveals that F is not a single act but a capacity exercised in multiple modes. The characteristic is the faculty itself, not any particular exercise of it.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Condition — the state of being wicked or unjust. Ra.sha and a.val name what a person is (ra.sha: the wicked one) and what they do, but the consistent use of ra.sha as a person-type identifies the primary character as conditional — a governing state of the person.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster contains all five categories of inner-being phenomenon: quality (A1), condition (A2, G), disposition (B), act (C, D, E), and faculty (F). This heterogeneity of kind-categories confirms that M26 is not a single characteristic but a cluster of related phenomena centred on one moral domain. The governing state/condition character of A2 and G frames the cluster: at its core, M26 is about what kind of person one is (condition), grounded in who God is (quality), with acts (C, D, E), a faculty (F), and a disposition (B) as sub-components.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or compound?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Compound — seven distinct modes identified in the nine VSGs of A1: permanent foundational attribute (A1-001), expressed through judgments (A1-002), inseparable from covenant (A1-003), ground of saving acts (A1-004), must be proclaimed (A1-005), standard exposing human failure (A1-006), defining quality of the Messiah (A1-007). These are not seven separate righteousnesses — they are seven modes of one quality expressing itself in different relational contexts.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Highly compound — 26 VSGs reveal extensive internal differentiation: received vs. practiced (950-001 vs. 950-002), stable vs. precarious (A2-013 vs. A2-002), inner possession vs. outward orientation (A2-005 vs. A2-012), present reality vs. far from it (A2-001 vs. A2-004).

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Relatively simple at first encounter — the y.sh.r root gives the sub-group a coherent identity. However, the evidence shows differentiation: uprightness of speech (Isa 33:15), uprightness of the inner being/heart (1211-001), uprightness of refusal (1214-001), and the Messianic uprightness (1218-001).

**[C — Justification]** E. Compound — the evidence holds two distinct modes: the OT judicial act of acquitting the innocent (947-002) and the NT divine act of declaring the ungodly righteous by faith (3194-001). The OT acquittal assumes an actually innocent person; the NT justification justifies the ungodly. The compound structure reflects the theological novelty of the NT mode.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Compound — the evidence differentiates: the cry for justice from the wronged (944-001), the divine execution of vengeance (944-002; 945-001), the commissioned human avenger (3250-001), the relinquishing of personal vengeance (6841-002), and the impossibility of placing God in debt (6841-004).

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Compound — external condemnation (4846-001), freedom from condemnation (4846-002), self-condemnation (4848-001), condemnation produced by the law (738-001), condemnation as punishment following moral failure (949-001), condemnation by one's own words (3201-001).

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Compound — the logizomai group alone shows the faculty operating in at least four distinct modes: theological reckoning (crediting faith as righteousness, Rom 4:3–11), ethical reckoning (counting oneself dead to sin, Rom 6:11), comparative reckoning (weighing suffering against glory, Rom 8:18), and deliberative reckoning (reasoning about practical decisions, Joh 11:50; Mar 11:31).

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Relatively simple — ra.sha and a.val are each coherent negative-pole terms. However, the verse evidence shows gradations: individual wickedness, corporate wickedness, wickedness in the place of justice.

**[CLUSTER]** Every sub-group in M26 is compound — none is simple. This is consistent with the cluster's character: righteousness is not a single thing that can be captured in one definition. It is a domain of related phenomena whose constituent elements are visible only through the multi-sub-group structure.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. God's righteousness is the constitutive quality of his nature by which all his ways and works conform perfectly to what is right — not as a standard external to him but as what he essentially is. It expresses itself through his judgments (A1-002), his covenant faithfulness (A1-003), his saving acts (A1-004), his proclamation (A1-005), his standard of exposure (A1-006), and supremely through the Messiah who embodies it (A1-007). Constitutional location: not applicable in a human sense — it is the architectonic quality of divine being.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Human righteousness is a governing state of the person — the condition of being rightly oriented toward God, expressing itself in measurable life consequences (A2-001), received by faith in Christ (950-001), practiced in daily living (950-002), and oriented outward toward others (A2-012) and God (A2-014). It engages the whole inner person — the heart (A2-005: law written on the heart), the will (A2-006: hungering), the speech and discernment (A2-011). Its impact is comprehensive: the righteous person is rooted (A2-013), upheld by God (3246-002), walking a recognisable path (A2-017), and serving as a preservative presence for their community. Constitutional location: the heart, the whole inner being. In the NT, constituted "in Christ" (2Cor 5:21).

**[A2 — qualification: conduct/state bidirectionality]** The relationship between the state and its conduct expression is bidirectional, not one-directional. The dominant pattern is state → conduct as evidence: the governing state of righteousness produces observable evidence in behaviour, speech, and orientation (Mat 25:37 is the clearest case — the righteous serve without calculation, the action is the evidence, the actor is not performing for an audience). But Ezekiel's framework presents a genuine reverse direction: conduct can constitute the state, not merely evidence it. Eze 18:5–9 — "if a man is righteous and does what is just and right... he is righteous" — lists specific behaviours and concludes from them that the person *is* righteous. The doing defines the being rather than merely evidencing it. Deu 6:25 reinforces this: "it will be righteousness for us, if we are careful to do all this commandment" — obedience produces righteousness as a result, the conduct comes first and the state follows. This bidirectionality is a genuine feature of the verse evidence, not a tension to resolve: the state must express itself in conduct to remain genuine, and sustained righteous conduct constitutes and deepens the state. Neither direction cancels the other.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Moral uprightness is an inner disposition of the person toward what is straight and true — an orientation of the inner being that aligns speech, conduct, and inner life with the directional quality of God's own truthfulness. It engages the inner will, speech (Isa 33:15), and the heart (1211-001). Its impact: the person with uprightness can be trusted, walks in alignment with God's ways, and is capable of recognising what is right (Pro 2:9). Constitutional location: the heart; the inner orientation of the will.

**[C — Justification]** E. Justification is a judicial act directed at a person's inner standing before God, declaring or making them righteous through the work of Christ received by faith. It engages the faith-faculty of the person (reception), the judicial assessment of God (declaration), and the inner standing that results. Its impact: the justified person passes from guilty-standing to righteous-standing before God, becoming an heir of eternal life (Tit 3:7). Constitutional location: the new status inheres in the person's standing before God — declared and received condition.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Avenging justice is a responsive moral act — the impulse and execution of retribution for wrong done, expressed either as the human cry for God to act (944-001) or as the delegated human commission to execute God's wrath (3250-001). It engages the will (commitment to leave vengeance to God, 6841-002), the moral faculty (recognising wrong as wrong), and the relationship with divine authority. Its impact: the one who entrusts vengeance to God is freed from the destructive drive of self-executed revenge.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Condemnation is a judicial sentence directed against a person on the basis of assessed guilt — producing the inner experience of being under verdict, or of being freed from it. It engages the judicial faculty, the conscience (autokatakritos), and the faith-faculty (reception of freedom from condemnation, 4846-002). Its impact: the condemned person bears the weight of divine judgment; the self-condemned person bears their own conscience turned against them; the freed person lives without the condemning voice (Joh 8:11).

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Moral reckoning is the evaluative faculty of the inner person — the capacity to weigh moral worth, credit standing, assign accountability, and live from the conclusions of inner moral deliberation. It engages the cognitive-rational dimension of the inner person (logizomai: to reason, reckon), the moral assessment capacity (axioō: to deem worthy), and the evaluative-judicial faculty (a.rakh: to compare, set in order). Its impact: the person whose reckoning faculty is properly engaged counts themselves dead to sin and alive to God (Rom 6:11), weighs present suffering against future glory (Rom 8:18). Constitutional location: the mind, the inner cognitive-moral capacity.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Wickedness and injustice are the governing conditions of a person whose inner being is oriented away from God's moral order — not merely episodic failures but constitutional states of the person who has turned from righteousness. Their impact: the wicked person lives under the judgment of God (Psa 1:5–6), is cut off from the community of the righteous, and will not stand in the final judgment.

**[CLUSTER]** The best working description of M26 as a whole: Righteousness and Justice names the moral domain centred on the quality of conformity to God's standard of right — expressed as God's constitutive attribute (A1), realised in the human person as a governing state (A2), expressed dispositionally as inner straightness (B), conferred through justification (C), enforced through avenging justice (D), and marked by its negative verdict (E). The reckoning faculty (F) is the inner-person mechanism that makes the reception and exercise of righteousness possible. The structural opposites (G) define the domain's boundary. The cluster is unified by one moral domain but internally differentiated across multiple kinds of inner-being phenomenon.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Human unrighteousness — the human failure to conform to the standard God embodies. "You are righteous; we have acted wickedly" (Neh 9:33; Ezr 9:15; Dan 9:7, VSG M26-A1-006). No divine opposite exists — only creaturely failure.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Wickedness (ra.sha/ra.sha) — the condition of the person who has turned from or never attained to righteousness. The OT pairs tsad.diq and ra.sha as consistent structural opposites throughout (Psa 1; Psa 37; Pro passim). In the NT, the structural opposite is the pre-justification condition of the ungodly (Rom 4:5).

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Crookedness or deviousness. Pro 2:13–15 names those who "forsake the paths of uprightness to walk in the ways of darkness, who rejoice in doing evil and delight in the perverseness of evil, whose paths are crooked." Ne.kho.chah (uprightness) is explicitly contrasted with wickedness and corruption in Isa 26:10 (VSG 1214-001).

**[C — Justification]** E. Condemnation (M26-E). Deu 25:1 pairs them as judicial opposites within a single sentence. The NT confirms: "it is Christ Jesus who died... who is to condemn?" (Rom 8:34, VSG 4846-002). Justification and condemnation are the two poles of the judicial verdict.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Impunity — the non-execution of retribution, the tolerance of wrong without consequence. The structural opposite is not mercy (a different relational mode) but the failure to act when action is due.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Freedom from condemnation — the state of being acquitted or declared not guilty. Rom 8:1 ("there is now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus," implied in VSG 4846-002) and Joh 8:10–11 name the opposite explicitly. The opposite is not merely the absence of the verdict but the positive presence of freedom.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Corrupt assessment — the failure to reckon honestly. More precisely, reckoning without grounding in faith (Rom 14:23: "whoever has doubts is condemned if he eats, because the eating is not from faith"). Reckoning without faith-grounding produces the wrong conclusion and the wrong action.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. G is itself the structural opposite of A1 and A2. The question does not apply recursively usefully.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster contains its own internal structural opposite: A1+A2+B form one pole; G names the other. C and E are internal structural opposites of each other. The cluster is a self-contained moral architecture with its defining boundaries built in.

---

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Partiality, injustice, and inconsistency. "He does no injustice" (Zep 3:5); "there is no a.val in him" (Deu 32:4). It resists the accommodation of the guilty without ground — which is why the cross is necessary: "that he might be just and the justifier" (Rom 3:26).

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Distortion and counterfeit (M26-A2-003), the toleration of wickedness, the absence of orientation toward God (A2-004), and the forfeiting of the state through turning to injustice (A2-002). It resists the claim that righteousness can be transferred from one person to another independent of their own inner state (Eze 18:20).

The resistance to self-generated righteousness is more than exclusion — it extends to active contamination. The verse evidence establishes that the attempt to produce righteousness without the God-relation does not merely fail to produce the real thing; it produces something that looks like righteousness but is actively defiled. Isa 64:6 — "all our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment" — names human righteous acts as contaminated in the absence of the God-relation; the garment is worn and visible as something, but what it actually is is unclean. Deu 9:4–6 — "not because of your righteousness... the Lord explicitly denies that Israel's righteousness is the operative cause" — identifies the self-attributed righteousness as real enough to be disclaimed, but as having no independent causal power before God. Luk 18:9 names the inner consequence of self-generated righteousness: the person who "trusts in themselves that they were righteous" develops contempt for others — the counterfeit state produces a specific and destructive inner orientation. Mat 23:28 closes the picture: the outward appearance of righteousness masks "hypocrisy and lawlessness" within. These five passages together reveal that self-generated righteousness is not a partial achievement but a self-deceiving distortion that produces specific negative inner consequences: contamination (Isa 64:6), ineffectiveness before God (Deu 9:4–6), contempt (Luk 18:9), and inner lawlessness (Mat 23:28). Human righteousness excludes and actively resists the self-generated form.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Crookedness, deviousness, smooth-talking falsehood (Isa 30:10: the refusal to hear what is right and the demand for smooth things, VSG 1214-001). It resists the accommodation of false appearances.

**[C — Justification]** E. Self-justification — "you who would be justified by the law; you have fallen away from grace" (Gal 5:4). It resists the claim that human moral performance can achieve the standing that only God can confer.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Personal, autonomous vengeance (Rom 12:19: "never avenge yourselves"). It resists the impulse to take retribution into one's own hands outside the limits of delegated commission.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Hypocritical judgment — "you condemn yourself, because you, the judge, practice the very same things" (Rom 2:1, VSG 4846-002). It resists the pretence of moral superiority in the one who passes verdict while sharing the same fault.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Prejudiced, partial, or faithless assessment. Adiakritos (impartial, VSG in sub-group) names the quality of assessment that is "without partiality and without hypocrisy" (Jam 3:17). The reckoning faculty resists conclusions not grounded in truth and faith.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. By definition, G is excluded from A2 and resists A1. Wickedness excludes standing before God (Psa 1:5), excludes the community of the righteous, and resists the standard of divine righteousness.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster collectively excludes: self-generated righteousness (resisted by C), autonomous retribution (resisted by D), hypocritical judgment (resisted by E), corrupt assessment (resisted by F), and the whole domain of wickedness and injustice (named by G).

---

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Not identical with mercy, though they coexist. "Steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other" (Psa 85:10, VSG M26-A1-003). The meeting implies distinction. Not identical with God's power or holiness — though paired with them. Righteousness specifically names conformity to the moral standard of right.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Not moral performance — not identical with the accumulation of righteous acts (Eze 33:12–13: "the righteous shall not be able to live by his righteousness when he sins"). Not self-righteousness (Rom 10:3). Not sinlessness — the righteous person in the OT can sin and repent. Not transferable (Eze 18:20). It ends where unbelief and deliberate turning to wickedness begins.

Most precisely, human righteousness is not a self-generated state. The verse evidence establishes a hard distinction between two versions of what presents as righteousness. Phili 3:9 is the sharpest statement: Paul explicitly contrasts "a righteousness of my own that comes from the law" — which he names and counts as rubbish (Phili 3:8) — with "that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God." These are not two points on a spectrum; they are structurally different things. The self-generated version has enough surface reality to be named and contrasted; it is not merely nothing. But it is not the same state. Paul exercised the self-generated version to apparent perfection ("blameless" under the law, Phili 3:6) and judged it as a loss compared to the genuine state. This two-righteousness distinction is not peripheral — it is where the NT's account of righteousness becomes most precise about what human righteousness actually is: received from God through faith, not generated from within through moral effort, however sustained or impressive that effort appears.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Not identical with righteousness (tse.deq) even though they consistently co-occur (Pro 2:9; Isa 33:15). Uprightness names the inner disposition of straightness; righteousness names the condition of being right. One can have the disposition without having the full condition.

**[C — Justification]** E. Not sanctification — it does not make the person morally better in the moment of declaration. It changes standing, not necessarily immediate conduct. It ends where practical moral formation begins — C establishes the ground; A2 (practiced righteousness, 950-002) is what follows.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Not personal revenge (Rom 12:19). Not identical with mercy — they serve different functions in the moral economy. Avenging justice ends where personal emotion-driven retribution begins.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Not the same as discipline (1Cor 11:32: "judged by the Lord... disciplined so that we may not be condemned with the world"). Discipline is formative; condemnation is judicial verdict. Condemnation ends where forgiveness and freedom from verdict begin.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Not the same as intuition, emotion, or impulse — it is an inner act of deliberation and conclusion. It ends where unreflective response begins. Not the same as the moral conscience (though related) — the conscience registers right and wrong; the reckoning faculty weighs and assigns standing.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Wickedness is not merely weakness or failure — it is a governing orientation, not an isolated lapse. The ra.sha is not the same as the person who sins and returns (who is covered by the A2-002 Ezekiel material). Wickedness names a settled condition of opposition to the moral order, not temporary deviation.

**[CLUSTER]** Adjacent characteristics not covered by M26: mercy and love (M05, C17), holiness (a separate cluster), faithfulness (C17). The cluster explicitly excludes these adjacent characteristics even while frequently co-occurring with them in the verses.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. God's righteousness operates in the human context through: (1) appeal — the psalmist appeals to God's righteousness as the ground for deliverance (A1-004); (2) exposure — it exposes human unrighteousness by contrast (A1-006; Act 24:25: Felix alarmed); (3) proclamation — it must be declared and testified to (A1-005); (4) covenant — as the ground of God's faithfulness toward his people (A1-003).

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Human righteousness operates through at least seven distinct inner modes: (1) governing state structuring the whole inner life; (2) orientation toward God (A2-014: walking with him, born of him); (3) orientation toward others (A2-012: flowing outward); (4) response — gladness, exultation, praise (3246-003); (5) pursuit — hungered for, actively sought (A2-006); (6) identity — worn, carried, defining characterisation (A2-009); (7) life path — recognisable trajectory (A2-017).

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Uprightness operates through: (1) direction of speech — speaking what is right and true (Isa 33:15; Isa 45:19); (2) direction of the will — choosing the straight path (Pro 2:13–14); (3) orientation of the heart — uprightness of heart as inner state (1211-001); (4) recognition of what is right — enabling discernment of right paths (Pro 2:9).

**[C — Justification]** E. Operates: (1) as divine declaration — the verdict is passed and the person's standing changes (3194-001); (2) as received condition — received by faith (Gal 3:24; Tit 3:7); (3) as historical precedent extended — Abraham's case becomes the pattern for all believers (3410-001 cross-reference); (4) as inner transformation of self-understanding — the justified person can count themselves as one who stands rightly before God.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Operates through: (1) the cry — the inner movement of the wronged person toward God in petition (944-001); (2) the commission — the inner acceptance of delegated authority to execute retribution (3250-001); (3) the relinquishment — the inner act of entrusting personal vengeance to God (6841-002); (4) the inner recognition of God's freedom from human claims (6841-004).

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Operates through: (1) the external verdict — judicial sentence directed at the person from outside (4846-001); (2) the inner weight — the person's experience of being under verdict; (3) the reflexive self-verdict — the inner being condemning itself (4848-001); (4) the release — the inner experience of freedom when the verdict is lifted (4846-002: Joh 8:10–11); (5) the moral testimony — the righteous generation condemning the unrighteous by comparison (4846-001: Ninevites).

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The reckoning faculty operates through: (1) theological accounting — crediting faith as righteousness, counting status before God (3410-001: Rom 4); (2) ethical self-application — counting oneself dead to sin, alive to God (Rom 6:11); (3) comparative weighing — present suffering against future glory (Rom 8:18); (4) practical deliberation — reasoning about specific decisions (Mar 11:31; Joh 11:50); (5) moral valuation — deeming worthy or unworthy (1185-001: axioō); (6) devotional orientation — preparing oneself before God in prayer (6753-003: a.rakh).

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Wickedness operates through: (1) systemic injustice — oppressing the righteous through corrupt systems (M26-A2-010 cross-reference); (2) active opposition — plotting against and persecuting the righteous (M26-A2-016); (3) suppression of uprightness — demanding smooth things instead of what is right (Isa 30:10, VSG 1214-001); (4) occupation of righteous space — "wickedness is in the place of justice" (Ecc 3:16).

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster reveals that righteousness operates through a remarkably diverse set of inner modes: as appeal, as proclamation, as governing state, as pursuit, as identity, as orientation, as verdict, as self-application, as valuation, as entrusting. The diversity of modes is itself an analytical finding — righteousness does not operate in one way but engages the whole inner person through multiple faculties and in multiple directional vectors.

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — toward his covenant people (A1-003: faithfulness), toward the wicked (A1-006: exposing and judging), toward the Messiah (A1-007: the defining quality that becomes incarnate), toward the world (A1-002: righteous judgment). Same quality, different modes depending on relational direction.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — the OT mode is primarily conditional and forfeitable (A2-002: Ezekiel); the NT mode introduces received-through-faith righteousness (950-001) as a new mode. The characteristic operates differently at different points in the redemptive narrative.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — uprightness operates at the level of speech (what one says), conduct (how one walks), and inner orientation (what one's heart is directed toward). At each level the same inner disposition manifests differently in practice.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — OT mode (947-002: acquitting the innocent in a human court) versus NT mode (3194-001: justifying the ungodly by faith). The OT mode presupposes actual innocence; the NT mode operates on the guilty made righteous through Christ. Same term, different constitutional logic.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — operates differently for: the private person (not to avenge oneself), the commissioned human authority (Rom 13:4: may and must execute wrath), and God himself (the exclusive and final avenger). Same impulse toward justice operates under different rules depending on the agent's position.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — condemnation as external verdict (4846-001), as inner weight (the experience of condemnation), and as reflexive self-verdict (4848-001: autokatakritos) are three different modes of operation.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the logizomai faculty operates in theological mode (crediting righteousness), ethical mode (self-application to sin/life), comparative mode (suffering vs. glory), and practical deliberative mode. The faculty is the same; the content and direction of its exercise differ significantly.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — wickedness operates at individual, communal, and systemic levels. The constitutional level at which wickedness operates determines the scope of its impact.

**[CLUSTER]** Every sub-group shows context-dependence in operation. This context-dependence is a structural feature of righteousness in M26, not a defect of description.

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — God's righteousness operates through proclamation. The psalmist's tongue declares it all day (Psa 35:28; 71:24; 145:7, VSG M26-A1-005); future generations shall proclaim it (Psa 22:31); God's righteous word goes out (Isa 45:23); revealed through the gospel (Rom 1:17). The proclamation of God's righteousness is not merely informational — it participates in the reality it declares.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — speech and discernment are named as expressions of righteousness in VSG M26-A2-011. The righteous person speaks justly. Speech is not merely expressive of righteousness — it is one of the primary modes through which righteousness in the person becomes visible.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Yes — this is the central mode of B's communicative operation. "He who walks righteously and speaks uprightly" (Isa 33:15, VSG 1212-002). The term me.shar specifically names the quality of speech (Pro 8:6; Pro 23:16: "my inmost being will exult when your lips speak what is right"). Uprightness in speech is a defining mode of the B vocabulary.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — "by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned" (Mat 12:37, VSG 3201-001, adjacent to C). Justification is declared — it is a speech act of God, a verdict pronounced. The receiving person confesses faith.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — the cry for justice is the primary communicative mode of D. "Give me justice against my adversary" (Luk 18:3, VSG 944-001) is speech directed toward God. The cry is not mere complaint — it is an inner act of faith directed toward the God who will answer.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Yes — "by your words you will be justified, and by your words you will be condemned" (Mat 12:37, VSG 3201-001). Condemnation operates through speech — both the external verdict declared by the judge and the inner speech of the condemning conscience.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Yes — the reckoning faculty is associated with deliberation and inner speech. Logizomai in Mar 11:31 names a deliberative conversation. The inner act of counting (Rom 6:11) is a form of self-addressed speech: "consider yourselves dead to sin." The reckoning faculty operates through internal deliberation that functions as a speech act directed at the self.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Yes — wickedness has a communicative mode: it speaks falsehood, demands smooth things instead of truth (Isa 30:10, VSG 1214-001). The speech of the wicked is named as a marker of their condition throughout the Psalms.

**[CLUSTER]** The speech-based mode of operation is present in every sub-group — from God's proclamation (A1) to the human cry for justice (D) to self-addressed inner counting (F) to the condemning verdict (E) to upright speech (B). The cluster consistently shows that righteousness is not merely an inner state but one that seeks expression through speech — and that speech is both an expression of and a mode of operation of righteousness.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. A double movement: humbling and acknowledgment. The pattern in A1-006 verses is the immediate acknowledgment of human guilt: "the Lord is righteous; we have acted wickedly" (Exo 9:27; Neh 9:33; Ezr 9:15; Lam 1:18; Dan 9:7). Pharaoh's acknowledgment, Israel's confession, and the prophet's lament all show the same immediate inner response. A secondary immediate response is alarm: Felix was alarmed (Act 24:25, VSG M26-A1-006) — conviction without conversion; recognition without submission.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The immediate response to being in the state of righteousness is gladness, exultation, and praise — VSG 3246-003. The righteous person who encounters their own state or God's action toward them responds with joy. The righteous also respond to opposition with trust in God rather than panic (M26-A2-016).

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. The verse evidence in B does not specifically address the immediate response to receiving or encountering uprightness. The material describes uprightness as a path and disposition rather than a stimulus producing a response. Silence noted.

**[C — Justification]** E. The immediate response to justification is trust and hope: "being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7). The justified person receives a new standing that produces hope as its immediate inner orientation.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The immediate response to the cry for divine justice being answered is relief and perseverance. The parable of the widow (Luk 18:3–8) shows persistent appeal, not despair. The answer to the cry — "he will give justice to them speedily" (Luk 18:8) — produces hope and continued faithfulness.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The immediate response to receiving the verdict of condemnation is weight. The immediate response to freedom from condemnation is release: "has no one condemned you? ... Neither do I condemn you" (Joh 8:10–11, VSG 4846-002). The release of being freed from the expected verdict.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The immediate response to the reckoning act is action: "count yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus... therefore do not let sin reign" (Rom 6:11–12). The reckoning act is immediately followed by a command to act from the conclusion.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. The verse evidence for G does not specifically address the immediate response of the person encountering wickedness or being in the wicked condition. Silence noted.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Varies systematically. The unrighteous person encountering God's righteousness responds with humbling and guilt-acknowledgment. The righteous person appealing to God's righteousness responds with confident petition (Psa 31:1; 71:2; 143:1). Felix responds with alarm (Act 24:25). The variation is systematic — the response depends on the moral standing of the one encountering the standard.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Varies between the response to being in the righteous state (gladness — 3246-003) and the response to opposition or threat (continued trust, A2-013; A2-016).

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. Silent on immediate response.

**[C — Justification]** E. Consistently oriented toward hope and relationship (Tit 3:7). The variation in the Job material (947-001) is notable: Job's sense of being in the right before God but unable to answer him (Job 9:15–20) shows the complexity — rightness before God does not necessarily produce peace when the legal-relational framework feels hostile.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Consistent: the wronged person cries out and persists; God promises to answer speedily. The consistency of the widow parable reinforced by the martyr cry (Rev 6:10).

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Varies: the woman freed from condemnation (Joh 8:11) is instructed to go and sin no more. The self-condemned person (Tit 3:11) is described as warped and sinful — no immediate repentance evident. The Ninevites who generate the condemnation of Israel repented — in response to preaching, not condemnation.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Consistent: the correct reckoning produces action (Rom 6:11–12). The incorrect reckoning (doubt-based, Rom 14:23) produces condemnation. Variation between right reckoning (freedom and right action) and wrong reckoning (condemned state).

**[G — Structural Opposites]** S. Silent on immediate response.

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S — silent on immediate response.
**[G — Structural Opposites]** S — silent on immediate response.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Increasing confidence in appeal (the psalmists return to it repeatedly — Psa 31:1; 71:2; 143:1; 143:11, VSG M26-A1-004) and deepening capacity for proclamation (declaring God's righteousness "all day" — Psa 35:28; 71:24). Sustained encounter with God's righteousness generates ongoing testimony.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. The sustained effect of righteousness in the human person is: rootedness and stability (M26-A2-013: "the roots of the righteous will never be moved"), increasing capacity for outward orientation (A2-012), and the development of a recognisable life path (M26-A2-017). The image of the palm tree (Psa 92:12, VSG M26-A2-001) implies growth over time.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Uprightness sustained produces the capacity for moral discernment — "then you will understand righteousness and justice and equity, every good path" (Pro 2:9, VSG 1212-002). The sustained orientation toward what is straight produces increasing capacity to recognise what is right. The Preacher's uprightness in writing (Ecc 12:10, VSG 1211-003) suggests that sustained uprightness produces reliable, truthful expression over time.

**[C — Justification]** E. Justification sustained as a received condition produces an heir-status that grows in the person's identity and expectation: "heirs according to the hope of eternal life" (Tit 3:7). The sustained effect is the development of hope — not as a mood but as an orientation toward promised future reality.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. The sustained entrusting of vengeance to God (6841-002) produces the inner capacity to bless rather than curse — Rom 12:14–19 shows the entrusting of vengeance as part of a sustained posture of non-retaliation and active goodwill.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. The sustained freedom from condemnation (4846-002: Rom 8:34) produces increasing security before God — the person who lives in the knowledge that Christ intercedes is established in a condition that nothing can overthrow.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. The sustained exercise of the reckoning faculty produces increasing clarity about moral reality. Rom 8:18 — the sustained reckoning of present suffering against future glory — produces the capacity to endure suffering without being crushed by it.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Sustained wickedness produces increasing hardness and insensitivity to divine righteousness. Rom 2:5 (VSG 3207-001) names the "hard and impenitent heart" that is "storing up wrath" — sustained wickedness accumulates a weight of future judgment.

---

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained possession of this characteristic establish?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Capacity for confident appeal (the psalmist pattern), the orientation of testimony and proclamation (A1-005), and recognition of human inadequacy before the divine standard (A1-006). It establishes the orientation of the Messianic community: the proclamation of God's righteousness becomes the community's identity and vocation (Psa 22:31).

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Establishes: rootedness (A2-013), outward orientation toward others (A2-012), capacity for gladness and exultation in God (3246-003), stable community identity (A2-015), and the life-path of the righteous (A2-017). The person sustained in righteousness becomes recognisably a certain kind of person — not merely performing righteous acts but being righteous.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Establishes: the capacity for accurate moral discernment (Pro 2:9), the habit of truthful speech (Isa 33:15), and the inner orientation toward what is right rather than what is smooth (Isa 30:10 — the contrast with those who demand smooth things).

**[C — Justification]** E. Establishes: heir-status (Tit 3:7), freedom from the weight of condemnation, and the capacity to count oneself rightly before God (the reckoning connection through F). Justified standing establishes the platform for all other inner-being development.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Establishes: the inner capacity for non-retaliation, the posture of prayer-petition toward God regarding injustice, and the recognition that God's authority over retribution is a relief rather than a constraint.

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Establishes: inner security before God's judgment, a conscience that no longer works against the self, and the orientation toward continued obedience that freedom from verdict makes possible ("go, and from now on sin no more," Joh 8:11).

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Establishes: increasing theological clarity (the capacity to count by faith, not by sight), moral realism about one's own condition (dead to sin, alive to God), and the orientation of endurance (weighing suffering against future glory).

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Establishes the hardened and impenitent heart (Rom 2:5) — a state that becomes increasingly resistant to righteousness. The accumulation of wrath is both a judicial reality and an inner-person developmental consequence.

---

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Yes — immediate response is guilt-acknowledgment or confident appeal; sustained effect is the development of proclamation capacity (A1-005) and the orientation of the community around testimony to divine righteousness. From immediate recognition to sustained witness.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Yes — immediate response is gladness (3246-003); sustained effect is rootedness and recognisable path (A2-013; A2-017). Immediate joy develops into structural stability.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** S. B is silent on immediate response, so the comparison cannot be made here.

**[C — Justification]** E. Yes — immediate response to justification is hope (Tit 3:7); sustained effect is the development of the heir-identity and increasing freedom from condemnation. The hope of the immediate becomes the identity of the sustained.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Yes — immediate response to God answering the cry for justice is relief and worship (Rev 19:2). Sustained effect of entrusting vengeance to God is the development of the non-retaliatory posture (Rom 12:19).

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Humility and acknowledgment — "you are righteous; we are in guilt" (Ezr 9:15; Dan 9:7, VSG M26-A1-006). The person who can receive God's righteousness as saving and sustaining first acknowledges the contrast between God's standard and their own condition. Pride and self-justification block reception (Rom 10:3: "seeking to establish their own, they did not submit to God's righteousness").

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Faith — the condition for receiving righteousness credited by God (950-001: Rom 4; Gen 15:6). Hunger — "blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness" (Mat 5:6, VSG M26-A2-006). Walking with God (A2-014) — relational orientation as the condition. In the NT, the explicit condition is faith in Christ.

**[B — Moral Uprightness]** E. Teachability — "I have taught you the way of wisdom; I have led you in the paths of uprightness" (Pro 4:11, VSG 1211-002). The fear of the Lord — "whoever walks in uprightness fears the Lord" (Pro 14:2, VSG 1211-002): the inner condition of reverence before God as the enabling condition for upright living.

**[C — Justification]** E. Faith — "justified by faith" is the consistent condition across the NT justification material (3194-001: Rom, Gal, Tit). The OT material (947-001: Job's question about standing before God) shows that the inner condition of awareness of one's own insufficiency before God is the preparatory condition.

**[D — Avenging Justice]** E. Conviction that God is righteous and will answer — "O Sovereign Lord, holy and true" (Rev 6:10). The cry presupposes faith in God's justice. For entrusting vengeance to God (6841-002): trust in the divine promise — "it is written, 'Vengeance is mine; I will repay'" (Rom 12:19).

**[E — Condemnation]** E. Faith in Christ's intercession: "Christ Jesus is the one who died... who indeed is interceding for us" (Rom 8:34, VSG 4846-002). For self-condemnation (4848-001): a functioning conscience — the person must be morally self-aware to be self-condemned.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Faith — "whatever does not proceed from faith is sin" (Rom 14:23, VSG 4846-001 adjacent). The reckoning faculty functions rightly when grounded in faith-conviction about what is true. The instruction to "count yourselves dead to sin" (Rom 6:11) presupposes the inner condition of believing the declaration to be true.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. The inner conditions enabling wickedness are the absence of the conditions listed above: pride (not humility), self-justification (not faith), hard-heartedness (Rom 2:5), and the refusal to know what is right (Amo 3:10).

**[CLUSTER]** The enabling conditions across the cluster cluster consistently around: humility, faith, hunger/desire, teachability, and fear of the Lord. These are not independent conditions for each sub-group — they are the same inner conditions enabling reception of righteousness in all its modes. Faith and humility are the master conditions from which all others flow.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[A1 — God Righteousness]** E. Pride and self-sufficiency — the attempt to establish one's own righteousness (Rom 10:3). Human anger — "the anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God" (Jam 1:20) — the inner condition of wrath distorts the capacity to receive what is right.

**[A2 — Human Righteousness]** E. Self-trust — trusting in accumulated righteousness (Eze 33:13). The distortion of M26-A2-003 (counterfeit and failure) — presenting the appearance of righteousness without the inner reality.

**[C — Justification]** E. Self-justification by law — "severed from Christ, you who would be justified by the law" (Gal 5:4). The inner condition of trust in self-generated righteousness blocks reception of justification.

**[F — Moral Reckoning]** E. Doubt — "whoever has doubts is condemned if he eats, because the eating is not from faith" (Rom 14:23). Partial or prejudiced reckoning (the opposite of adiakritos impartiality) distorts the reckoning outcome.

**[G — Structural Opposites]** E. Wickedness itself blocks all reception: the hard and impenitent heart (Rom 2:5) is the inner condition that makes reception of righteousness impossible without repentance.

**[CLUSTER]** The blocking conditions: pride, self-trust, preference for comfort over truth, self-justification, autonomous vengeance-impulse, hypocrisy, doubt. The cluster reveals that the conditions blocking righteousness are not primarily external constraints but inner orientations.
