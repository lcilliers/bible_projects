# Three questions: (a) explicit definition, (b) inferences, (c) NT/OT change

## Question A — Do the verses give a clear definition of what a righteous God means?

**Short answer: No. Not a single verse defines it.**

The verses do not say "God's righteousness is X." What they do instead is one of
the following: declare that God is righteous, describe an act of God as righteous,
contrast God's righteousness with human unrighteousness, or call on God's
righteousness as a basis for appeal.

The nearest the text comes to definition is a cluster of what righteousness is
*paired with* — which suggests the edges of the concept:

- Paired with justice: Psa 89:14 ("righteousness and justice are the foundation of your throne"),
  Psa 97:2 (same), Hos 2:19 ("betroth you...in righteousness and in justice"),
  Jer 9:24 ("I...practice steadfast love, justice, and righteousness"),
  Psa 33:5 ("He loves righteousness and justice")
  
- Paired with faithfulness/truth: Zec 8:8 ("in faithfulness and in righteousness"),
  Rev 15:3 ("Just and true are your ways"), Rev 16:7 ("true and just are your judgments"),
  Psa 119:138 ("appointed your testimonies in righteousness and in all faithfulness"),
  Isa 45:19 ("I the Lord declare what is right") paired with "not in secret, not in chaos"

- Paired with salvation: Isa 45:21 ("a righteous God and a Savior"),
  Isa 45:8 ("let...righteousness...bear fruit" alongside salvation),
  Isa 46:13 ("I bring near my righteousness...and my salvation will not delay"),
  Isa 51:6 ("my righteousness will never be dismayed" alongside "my salvation"),
  Isa 51:8 ("my righteousness will be forever, and my salvation to all generations"),
  Mal 4:2 ("sun of righteousness shall rise with healing")

- Paired with holiness: Rev 16:5 ("Just are you, O Holy One"),
  Act 3:14 ("the Holy and Righteous One"), Isa 5:16 ("the Holy God shows himself holy in righteousness")

- Paired with mercy/grace: Psa 116:5 ("Gracious is the Lord, and righteous; our God is merciful"),
  1Jo 1:9 ("he is faithful and just to forgive us")

**Observation:** The consistent pairings are not accidental. They locate righteousness 
within a cluster of divine attributes — justice, faithfulness, truth, salvation, holiness, 
mercy — but they do not isolate and define it. The verses treat these as mutually 
illuminating, not as independent definitions.

**One near-definition emerges from Ezekiel's contrast (though this is OT only):**
Zep 3:5: "The Lord within her is righteous; he does no injustice" — 
here righteousness is implicitly defined by its negative: doing no injustice.
This is the closest any verse comes to stating what righteousness *is* rather than 
what it is paired with.

## Question B — What can be inferred from the verses?

### Inferred characteristic G1 — God's righteousness is a stable, foundational attribute of his nature, not a state he enters or exits

The verses treat righteousness as a permanent, constitutive quality of God — 
not something God exercises occasionally or that varies.

- Psa 145:17: "The Lord is righteous in all his ways" — without exception, across all actions
- Dan 9:14: "the Lord our God is righteous in all the works that he has done" — all works, not some
- Psa 119:142: "Your righteousness is righteous forever" — eternal quality
- Isa 51:6: "my righteousness will never be dismayed" — cannot be shaken or removed
- Isa 51:8: "my righteousness will be forever, and my salvation to all generations" — permanent
- Job 37:23: "abundant righteousness he will not violate" — God possesses it abundantly and will not compromise it
- Psa 111:3: "his righteousness endures forever" — enduring as an attribute
- Psa 89:14 / Psa 97:2: "righteousness and justice are the foundation of his throne" — 
  righteousness is the structural basis of God's rule, not an occasional expression of it
- Dan 9:7: "To you, O Lord, belongs righteousness" — it belongs to him intrinsically

**Inference:** God's righteousness is not a response to circumstances. It is foundational —
part of the structure of who God is and how he rules. It cannot be lost, violated, 
or set aside.

---

### Inferred characteristic G2 — God's righteousness is expressed through his judgments — and those judgments are consistent with what is right

The single largest operational expression of God's righteousness in the dataset
is his role as judge. Multiple verses describe his judgments as righteous.

- Psa 7:11: "God is a righteous judge" — judicial role directly attributed
- Psa 9:4: "sat on the throne, giving righteous judgment" — judgments from his throne
- Psa 9:8: "he judges the world with righteousness" — righteousness is the instrument of judgment
- Psa 96:13 / Psa 98:9: "He will judge the world in righteousness" — universal scope
- Rev 16:5: "Just are you, O Holy One...for you brought these judgments" — 
  God's judgments declared just precisely because they correspond to what he is
- Rev 16:7: "true and just are your judgments"
- Rev 19:2: "his judgments are true and just"
- Rev 15:3: "Just and true are your ways, O King of the nations"
- 2Th 1:5: "righteous judgment of God" — judgment characterised as righteous
- 2Th 1:6: "God considers it just to repay with affliction those who afflict you" — 
  reciprocal justice as an expression of what God considers right
- 2Ti 4:8: "the Lord, the righteous judge, will award to me" — 
  righteous judge who awards correctly
- Act 17:31: "he will judge the world in righteousness by a man he has appointed"
- Joh 5:30: "my judgment is just, because I seek not my own will" — 
  justice in judgment comes from alignment with the Father's will, not self-interest

**Inference:** God's righteousness is not merely a disposition — it is expressed
through action, specifically through judgments that correspond to what is right.
The righteous judge does not judge according to preference or power but according
to what *is* right. This is the most consistently evidenced characteristic in the dataset.

---

### Inferred characteristic G3 — God's righteousness is inseparable from his covenant faithfulness toward his people

A significant sub-cluster shows God's righteousness expressed as faithfulness to 
his commitments — keeping promises, vindicating the wronged, redeeming the repentant.

- Neh 9:8: "you have kept your promise, for you are righteous" — 
  promise-keeping is an expression of righteousness
- Zec 8:8: "I will be their God, in faithfulness and in righteousness" — 
  covenant relationship characterised by righteousness
- Hos 2:19: "I will betroth you to me in righteousness and in justice" — 
  righteousness grounds the covenant bond
- Psa 103:17: "his righteousness to children's children" of those who fear him — 
  extends across generations within the covenant relationship
- 1Sa 12:7: "all the righteous deeds of the Lord that he performed for you" — 
  historical saving acts counted as righteousness
- Mic 6:5: "know the righteous acts of the Lord" — remembered through history
- Lam 1:18: "The Lord is in the right, for I have rebelled against his word" — 
  righteousness maintained even when Israel fails; the covenant standard stands
- Neh 9:33: "you have been righteous in all that has come upon us" — 
  God's righteousness affirmed even through judgment on the covenant people

**Inference:** God's righteousness is not abstract impartiality. It operates within
a covenant relationship — it is the quality that makes God reliable to his people,
faithful to his word, and consistent in his treatment of them. The righteous God
keeps his commitments even when his people do not.

---

### Inferred characteristic G4 — God's righteousness is the basis on which he acts to save and deliver

This is one of the most unexpected findings: righteousness in God is not primarily
a quality that condemns — it is repeatedly the ground for appeal for help and deliverance.

- Psa 31:1: "In your righteousness deliver me" — righteousness invoked as the basis for rescue
- Psa 71:2: "In your righteousness deliver me and rescue me"
- Psa 143:1: "in your righteousness answer me"
- Psa 143:11: "In your righteousness bring my soul out of trouble"
- Psa 119:40: "in your righteousness give me life"
- Psa 5:8: "Lead me, O Lord, in your righteousness"
- Psa 35:24: "Vindicate me, O Lord, my God, according to your righteousness"
- Psa 65:5: "you answer us with righteousness, O God of our salvation" — 
  righteousness expressed as answered prayer for salvation
- Isa 45:8: "let the clouds rain down righteousness...salvation and righteousness may bear fruit" — 
  righteousness descends alongside salvation
- Isa 46:13: "I bring near my righteousness...and my salvation will not delay" — 
  righteousness and salvation travel together
- Isa 51:5: "My righteousness draws near, my salvation has gone out" — 
  righteousness approaching means salvation arriving
- Isa 54:17: "their vindication from me, declares the Lord" — vindication as expression of righteousness
- Mic 7:9: "I shall look upon his vindication" — God's righteous vindication awaited
- 1Jo 1:9: "he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins" — 
  justice itself grounds forgiveness when sin is confessed

**Inference:** God's righteousness is the ground of confident appeal precisely because
it is reliable. The righteous God does not abandon his people arbitrarily. His 
righteousness — his consistent conformity to what is right — means he can be appealed 
to on the basis of that attribute. This is a striking finding: righteousness in God 
functions as a basis for salvation, not for condemnation alone. The two are inseparable
in the text.

---

### Inferred characteristic G5 — God's righteousness is revealed and proclaimed, not self-evident

The verses consistently note that God's righteousness must be made visible, declared,
proclaimed, and recognised — suggesting it is not automatically apparent and requires
active testimony.

- Psa 22:31: "proclaim his righteousness to a people yet unborn"
- Psa 35:28: "my tongue shall tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long"
- Psa 51:14: "my tongue will sing aloud of your righteousness"
- Psa 71:15: "your righteous acts...their number is past my knowledge"
- Psa 71:16: "I will remind them of your righteousness, yours alone"
- Psa 71:19: "Your righteousness, O God, reaches the high heavens"
- Psa 71:24: "my tongue will talk of your righteous help all the day long"
- Psa 97:6: "The heavens proclaim his righteousness, and all the peoples see"
- Psa 98:2: "revealed his righteousness in the sight of the nations"
- Psa 50:6: "the heavens declare his righteousness, for God himself is judge"
- Psa 88:12: righteousness is something that can be known — its absence in the realm of the dead is notable
- Psa 145:7: "shall sing aloud of your righteousness"
- Rom 1:17: "the righteousness of God is revealed" — requires revelation through the gospel
- Rom 3:21: "the righteousness of God has been manifested" — made visible
- Rom 3:25: "to show God's righteousness" — God demonstrates it through the cross

**Inference:** God's righteousness requires testimony. It is something that creatures 
proclaim, that the heavens declare, that the gospel reveals. The text treats this as 
necessary — not because God's righteousness is uncertain, but because creation needs 
to perceive and acknowledge what is true. The act of proclamation is itself a 
response to what God is.

---

### Inferred characteristic G6 — God's righteousness is the standard against which human unrighteousness is measured — and exposed

Several verses place God's righteousness and human unrighteousness in direct contrast,
where God's quality exposes the absence of the same quality in humans.

- Ezr 9:15: "you are just...we are before you in our guilt" — divine righteousness reveals human guilt
- Dan 9:7: "To you, O Lord, belongs righteousness, but to us open shame"
- Neh 9:33: "you have been righteous...but we have acted wickedly"
- Exo 9:27: "the Lord is in the right, and I and my people are in the wrong"
- Lam 1:18: "The Lord is in the right, for I have rebelled against his word"
- Jer 12:1: "Righteous are you, O Lord, when I complain to you" — 
  the speaker affirms God's righteousness even while questioning him
- Rom 3:5: "our unrighteousness serves to show the righteousness of God" — 
  human failing is the contrast that makes God's quality visible
- Jam 1:20: "the anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God" — 
  human anger is specifically named as unable to produce divine righteousness
- Rom 10:3: "ignorant of the righteousness of God, and seeking to establish their own" — 
  self-established righteousness is the alternative to submitting to God's

**Inference:** God's righteousness functions as a standard by which human conduct
is measured and found wanting. The contrast is structural throughout the OT 
(the people confess their unrighteousness against God's righteousness) and
made theologically explicit in the NT (God's righteousness is entirely distinct 
from and unreachable by human effort).

---

### Inferred characteristic G7 — God's righteousness is a defining characteristic of the Messiah / Righteous One

A specific cluster of verses applies righteousness to a coming figure —
using it as a title and a defining characteristic.

- Isa 53:11: "the righteous one, my servant, make many to be accounted righteous" — 
  righteousness is the Servant's defining quality enabling transfer to others
- Jer 23:5: "I will raise up for David a righteous Branch" — righteousness defines the coming ruler
- Jer 23:6 / Jer 33:16: "The Lord is our righteousness" — the Messiah named as righteousness itself
- Jer 33:15: "he shall execute justice and righteousness in the land"
- Isa 11:4: "with righteousness he shall judge the poor"
- Isa 11:5: "Righteousness shall be the belt of his waist" — defining, constant, worn close
- Zec 9:9: "righteous and having salvation is he, humble"
- Act 3:14: "the Holy and Righteous One" — title applied to Jesus
- Act 7:52: "the coming of the Righteous One, whom you have now betrayed and murdered"
- Act 22:14: "to see the Righteous One"
- Isa 24:16: "glory to the Righteous One" — receives universal praise
- 1Jo 2:1: "Jesus Christ the righteous" — qualifies him as advocate

**Inference:** In the messianic cluster, righteousness is not merely an attribute God
possesses — it becomes incarnate in a person. The Righteous One is righteous in a way
that enables others to become righteous (Isa 53:11). The title is not honorary;
it names the defining quality of this person's being and mission.

## Question C — Does it change between NT and OT?

### What is continuous

The OT and NT share the following about God's righteousness:

1. It is a foundational, permanent attribute — not situational *(OT: Psa 111:3, 145:17; NT: implicit in all NT usage)*
2. It is expressed through judgment — the righteous God judges rightly *(OT: Psa 7:11, 9:8; NT: 2Ti 4:8, Rev 15:3–16:7, Act 17:31)*
3. It is the ground for appeal — people call on God's righteousness for deliverance *(OT: Psa 31:1, 71:2, 143:1; NT: 1Jo 1:9)*
4. It is paired with salvation — righteousness and salvation travel together *(OT: Isa 45:21, 46:13, 51:6–8; NT: Rom 1:17, 3:21–26)*
5. It stands in contrast to human unrighteousness *(OT: Dan 9:7, Ezr 9:15; NT: Rom 3:5, 10:3)*

### What changes or deepens in the NT

**Change 1 — The righteousness of God becomes a gift given to humans through Christ**

The OT shows God's righteousness as his own attribute appealed to from outside.
The NT introduces something structurally new: God's righteousness is transferred 
to humans through Christ.

- Rom 3:22: "the righteousness of God through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe"
- Rom 3:24: "justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus"
- 1Cor 1:30: "Christ Jesus...became to us...righteousness and sanctification"
- 2Cor 5:21: "in him we might become the righteousness of God"
- Isa 53:11 anticipates this: "the righteous one...make many to be accounted righteous"
  but the NT makes the mechanism explicit.

**Change 2 — The tension between God's righteousness and his forgiveness is resolved**

In the OT, God is declared righteous even while forgiving — but the tension
between justice and mercy is noted rather than resolved.
The NT explicitly resolves it through the cross.

- Rom 3:25–26: "to show God's righteousness, because in his divine forbearance he had 
  passed over former sins...that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith"
  — the cross is the event that allows God to be simultaneously just and the one who justifies.
  This resolution is new. The OT affirms both qualities but does not show how they cohere.

**Change 3 — The Righteous One is identified and arrives**

The OT anticipates a Righteous One/Branch/Servant whose coming is foretold.
The NT identifies this person as Jesus and records his reception and rejection.

- Act 3:14: "the Holy and Righteous One" — identified as Jesus, whom the crowd denied
- Act 7:52: "the coming of the Righteous One, whom you have now betrayed and murdered"
- 1Pe 3:18: "the righteous for the unrighteous" — the transfer accomplished

**Change 4 — God's righteousness becomes the basis for universal judgment**

The OT references God judging "the world" in righteousness but primarily in Israel's context.
The NT makes this explicitly universal and eschatological.

- Act 17:31: "he will judge the world in righteousness by a man whom he has appointed" — 
  universal and future; a man (Christ) is the appointed instrument
- Rev 15:3: "Just and true are your ways, O King of the nations" — nations, not Israel only
- Rev 19:2: "his judgments are true and just" — in the context of final judgment

**What does not change**

God's righteousness as a character attribute — permanent, foundational, paired with
justice and faithfulness, expressed in action, proclaimed by creation and humans — 
is entirely consistent between OT and NT. The NT does not redefine what righteousness
in God means. It reveals what it does: how it is expressed toward humanity through Christ,
how it resolves the justice/mercy tension, and how it extends beyond Israel to all nations.

---

## Summary findings

**(a) Definition:** No verse in either testament defines God's righteousness explicitly.
The closest approach is the negative definition in Zep 3:5 ("does no injustice") and 
the structural pairings with justice, faithfulness, truth, and salvation.

**(b) Inferences:**
- G1: Foundational, permanent attribute — not situational
- G2: Expressed through judgments that conform to what is right
- G3: Inseparable from covenant faithfulness toward his people
- G4: The basis on which God acts to save and deliver — not only to condemn
- G5: Must be revealed, proclaimed, and testified to
- G6: The standard against which human unrighteousness is measured and exposed
- G7: Defining characteristic of the Messiah — becomes incarnate in the Righteous One

**(c) OT to NT change:**
- Continuous: permanence, judicial expression, basis for appeal, paired with salvation, contrast with humans
- New in NT: transferred to humans through Christ; justice/mercy tension resolved at the cross;
  the Righteous One identified and arrives; judgment scope made universal and eschatological

## Proposition test — righteousness as state, structural parallel, God-dependency (2026-05-09)

*Proposition under test: "Being righteous is a state not a characteristic — it is derived from what it represents. Its evidence of existence comes from what the object is or does. *Method: Each element of the proposition tested against actual verse evidence. Confirmations, resistances, and qualifications all recorded. Every claim attributed to specific verses.*

## ELEMENT 1: "Being righteous is a state, not a characteristic"

### What the distinction requires

A *characteristic* is a trait or quality among others — one feature of a person 
alongside other features. A *state* is a condition of being that governs how 
everything else in the person operates. The proposition claims righteousness is 
the latter.

### Testing against human verses

**Evidence that supports state rather than characteristic:**

H6662: The righteous person's *thoughts* are just (Pro 12:5), their *speech* gives 
life (Pro 10:11), their *desires* tend toward good (Pro 11:23), their *inner being* 
ponders how to answer (Pro 15:28), their *root* does not move (Pro 12:3), their *way* 
is known by God (Psa 1:6). The term governs thought, speech, desire, orientation, 
stability simultaneously. This is not one trait among others — it appears to be a 
governing condition from which multiple expressions flow.

G1342: Lot's *soul* is described as righteous (2Pe 2:8) — righteousness locates in 
the soul, not in a behaviour. Zechariah and Elizabeth were *both righteous before God* 
(Luk 1:6) — the state is assessed before God, not by one act.

G1343: "The new self, created...in true righteousness" (Eph 4:24) — righteousness is 
a quality of the *new self* as a whole, not one feature of it. "Trained in 
righteousness" (2Ti 3:16) suggests formation of the whole person, not acquisition 
of one trait.

H6666: Job "wore righteousness as a garment" (Job 29:14) — a garment covers and 
defines the whole person, not one part. Righteousness described as what David 
"walked before God in" (1Ki 3:6) — a comprehensive mode of being, not an 
isolated characteristic.

**Evidence that resists — where righteousness looks like one characteristic among others:**

Psa 112:4: "he is gracious, merciful, *and* righteous" — listed alongside other 
qualities, suggesting a characteristic among peers.

Tit 1:8: "upright, holy, and disciplined" — righteousness (upright) listed as one 
required trait among several for a church leader.

Luk 1:6: "righteous and devout" — two qualities named together.

1Ki 2:32: "more righteous and better than himself" — comparative use implies 
a measurable trait, not an absolute state.

**Assessment of Element 1:**

The proposition is substantially supported but requires qualification. The dominant 
usage treats righteousness as a *governing condition* from which multiple expressions 
flow — not as one trait among equals. However, the text does not consistently make 
this distinction explicit. Some verses list righteousness alongside other virtues 
in ways that read as characteristic rather than state.

The most precise claim the evidence supports: righteousness functions as a *primary 
governing orientation* whose evidence appears across the whole person — thought, 
speech, desire, conduct, stability. Whether this should be called a "state" rather 
than a "characteristic" is a conceptual distinction the text does not make in those 
terms — but the functional evidence points toward the former.

## ELEMENT 2: "It is derived from what it represents — its evidence of existence comes 
## from what the object is or does"

### Testing against human verses

This element claims righteousness is not directly observable as a quality — it is 
inferred from evidence. The state reveals itself through what the person *is* or *does*.

**Evidence supporting this:**

H6662: No verse says "righteousness looks like X internally." Instead:
- "the mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life" (Pro 10:11) — speech is the evidence
- "the thoughts of the righteous are just" (Pro 12:5) — thought pattern is the evidence
- "the righteous hates falsehood" (Pro 13:5) — inner aversion is evidence
- "the righteous who walks in his integrity" (Pro 20:7) — manner of life is evidence
- "he was righteous in his own eyes" (Job 32:1) — self-assessment, implying 
  righteousness is known through evaluation, not direct perception

G1342: Joseph's righteousness evidenced by merciful restraint (Mat 1:19); Cornelius 
evidenced by being "well spoken of by the whole Jewish nation" (Act 10:22); 
Abel evidenced by God commending him by accepting his gifts (Heb 11:4); 
Lot evidenced by distress over surrounding lawlessness (2Pe 2:8).
In each named case, righteousness is *inferred* from an action, a response, or 
a divine commendation — not stated as a directly visible quality.

G1343: "the fruit of righteousness" (Phili 1:11, Heb 12:11, 2Cor 9:10) — 
fruit is not the tree but what the tree produces. Righteousness is evidenced 
by what it yields. The state is interior; the evidence is exterior.

H6664G: "Righteousness shall be the belt of his waist" (Isa 11:5) — 
worn close and constantly, defining the posture, but the belt is not the body.
"The path of the righteous is like the light of dawn" (Pro 4:18) — the path 
is what the state of righteousness produces; the path is the evidence.

**Most significant evidence for this element:**

Mat 23:28: "outwardly appear righteous...but within full of hypocrisy" — 
this verse is critical. It confirms that righteousness is an interior state whose 
outward evidence can be counterfeited. The appearance is not the state. 
The state and its evidence are distinguishable. This is the clearest textual 
confirmation that righteousness is interior and that external behaviour is its 
evidence, not its substance.

Mat 25:37: The righteous serve others without knowing they are serving Christ — 
"Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you?" The state produces the action 
*without calculation*. The action is the evidence, but the actor is not performing 
for an audience. This is the clearest example of righteousness as a state that 
generates evidence unconsciously rather than strategically.

**Evidence that qualifies:**

Eze 18:5–9: "if a man is righteous and does what is just and right...he is righteous." 
Here the doing *defines* the being rather than merely evidencing it. The passage 
lists specific behaviours and concludes from them that the person *is* righteous. 
This is not purely "state produces evidence" — it reads more as "conduct constitutes 
the state." This is a significant qualification in Ezekiel's framework.

Deu 6:25: "it will be righteousness for us, if we are careful to do all this 
commandment" — obedience *produces* righteousness as a result. Here the conduct 
comes first and the state follows. This partially inverts the proposition's direction.

**Assessment of Element 2:**

Strongly supported overall, with important qualification from Ezekiel and 
Deuteronomy. The dominant picture — especially in Psalms, Proverbs, and the NT — 
is that righteousness is a primary interior state whose evidence appears in conduct, 
speech, and orientation. The Mat 23:28 and Mat 25:37 verses are particularly 
strong confirmations.

The qualification: Ezekiel and parts of Deuteronomy present conduct as constitutive 
of the state rather than merely evidencing it. This is a genuine tension within the 
corpus, not a minor variation.

---

## ELEMENT 3: "There is similarity in how it works between God and man"

### Testing against the God verses

The proposition claims the operational structure is the same in God and man — 
righteousness as a state evidenced by what the subject is or does.

**Evidence supporting similarity:**

For God, as for humans, righteousness is never directly defined. It is evidenced:

- By his judgments: "he judges the world with righteousness" (Psa 9:8) — 
  the judgment is evidence of the state
- By his acts: "the righteous deeds of the Lord that he performed for you" (1Sa 12:7) — 
  historical acts are evidence
- By promise-keeping: "you have kept your promise, for you are righteous" (Neh 9:8) — 
  covenant faithfulness is the evidence
- By his speech: "from my mouth has gone out in righteousness a word that shall not 
  return" (Isa 45:23) — speech patterns evidence the state
- By his inner disposition: "He loves righteousness and justice" (Psa 33:5) — 
  what God loves reveals what he is
- By paired qualities: righteousness and justice as foundation of his throne (Psa 89:14) — 
  the structural arrangement of his rule evidences the state

**Critical parallel — God's righteousness also resists direct definition:**

Neither God's righteousness nor human righteousness is ever directly defined.
In both cases, the state is evidenced by what the subject does and is. 
The structural parallel holds: the state is interior and primary; 
the evidence is external and secondary.

**Where the parallel holds most clearly:**

God does no injustice (Zep 3:5) — stated negatively, as with the human cases 
where righteousness is evidenced by what it excludes (Pro 13:5: hates falsehood).

God's righteousness produces specific outputs: saves, delivers, vindicates — 
just as human righteousness produces specific outputs: life-giving speech, 
stability, generosity. The logic is identical.

**Where the parallel breaks:**

God's righteousness is *permanent and self-grounded* (Psa 119:142, Isa 51:6–8, 
Job 37:23). It cannot be forfeited, abandoned, or compromised. Human righteousness 
can be forfeited (Eze 3:20, 18:24, 33:18) and in the NT is declared absent (Rom 3:10).

God's righteousness is the *source* from which others receive (Psa 24:5, 
"righteousness from the God of his salvation"; Isa 54:17 "their vindication from me"). 
Human righteousness is the *recipient* form of the same quality, not its origin.

**Assessment of Element 3:**

The operational structure is genuinely similar — in both cases righteousness is 
a primary state evidenced by what the subject is and does, never directly defined, 
and recognisable through its outputs. The proposition holds structurally.

The difference is ontological, not structural: God's righteousness is self-grounded, 
permanent, and the source. Human righteousness is derived, precarious in the OT, 
and absent in the NT until received from God. The *how it works* is parallel; 
the *what it is grounded in* is entirely different.

## ELEMENT 4: "In the human, it cannot be attained without God — taking God out 
## leaves the human unable to be in a state of righteousness"

### This is the most testable element. Testing systematically.

**NT evidence — explicit statements:**

Rom 3:10: "None is righteous, no, not one" — universal absence without qualification. 
This is the baseline NT statement. It does not say "attained with difficulty" — 
it says no human possesses it.

Rom 5:19: "by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous" — 
the only path to the state is through Christ's obedience, not through human effort.

Rom 3:24: "justified by his grace as a gift" — a gift requires a giver. 
Remove the giver and there is no gift.

Phili 3:9: Paul explicitly contrasts "a righteousness of my own that comes from 
the law" (which he counts as rubbish) with "that which comes through faith in 
Christ, the righteousness from God." There are two versions — one is self-generated 
and Paul names it as inadequate. The genuine state comes from God.

Gal 2:21: "if righteousness were through the law, then Christ died for no purpose" — 
the argument presupposes that righteousness *cannot* come through law. 
Remove God/Christ and no path to the state remains.

Gal 3:21: righteousness "would indeed be by the law" — but only "if a law had been 
given that could give life." It was not. Law cannot produce the state.

Eph 4:24: the new self is "created after the likeness of God in true righteousness" — 
the state belongs to the *new creation*, not to unreformed human nature.

**NT evidence — confirming the God-dependency in the OT names too:**

Gen 7:1: Noah is righteous "before me" — assessed by God, before God. 
The frame of reference is God. Remove God and the assessment has no ground.

Psa 1:6: "the Lord knows the way of the righteous" — the way exists because 
God knows and upholds it. The righteous person's path is defined in relation to God.

Eze 18:9: "walks in my statutes, and keeps my rules...he is righteous" — 
the statutes are God's. Remove God and his statutes and there is no standard 
against which righteous conduct is defined.

Mal 3:18: "the righteous...one who serves God" — serves *God* specifically. 
The definition of the state includes the God-relation. Remove God and the 
definition has no object.

Hab 2:4: "the righteous shall live by his faith" — faith directed toward God. 
Remove God and faith has no object.

**OT evidence that appears to resist — the strongest counter-test:**

The OT names persons as righteous without explicitly explaining their 
God-relationship in every case: Noah (Gen 6:9), David (1Sa 24:17), 
Job (Job 12:4), Abner and Amasa (1Ki 2:32). These appear as simple 
character descriptions.

**But testing deeper:**

Gen 6:9: Noah was righteous "before me" (Gen 7:1) — the second reference 
makes the relational frame explicit. God sees the righteousness.

Job 12:4: Job calls on God (12:4a — "I call to God and he answers me"). 
The calling on God immediately precedes the description of righteous and blameless.
The God-relation is present in the same verse.

1Sa 24:17: David is more righteous *because* he returned good for evil — 
but the context is David sparing Saul as the Lord's anointed (1Sa 24:6). 
The restraint is explicitly grounded in fear of the Lord.

The cases that appear to be purely moral descriptions consistently reveal, 
on closer reading, that the God-relation is either stated or immediately 
present in the context.

**The Isa 64:6 confirmation:**

"All our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment" — even the deeds 
that look righteous are corrupted. This is the OT anticipation of Rom 3:10. 
Without God's recognition and grounding, human righteousness is not merely 
absent — it is actively contaminated. The garment metaphor is striking: 
something is worn that *looks like* righteousness but is not.

**The Deu 9:4-6 confirmation:**

"Not because of your righteousness...not because of your righteousness or 
the uprightness of your heart." God explicitly denies that Israel's righteousness 
is the operative cause. They have it as an attributed quality, but it is 
insufficient as a ground for divine action. This does not say they lack 
righteousness — but it says their righteousness has no independent causal power 
before God.

**The Ezekiel tension:**

Eze 18 describes righteousness in terms of keeping God's statutes and rules. 
Remove God and the statutes disappear. Ezekiel's framework is entirely 
God-referenced — the standard is explicitly God's standard. The person who 
"walks in my statutes" is righteous (Eze 18:9). Remove "my statutes" and 
the framework collapses.

**Assessment of Element 4:**

Strongly confirmed by the NT — explicitly and categorically. 
Confirmed by the OT on close reading, though less explicitly.

The proposition "taking God out of the equation leaves the human unable to 
be in a state of righteousness" holds at multiple levels:

1. The *standard* against which righteousness is measured is God's 
   (Eze 18:9 — God's statutes; Psa 1:6 — God knows the way)
2. The *assessment* of whether a person is righteous is God's 
   (Gen 7:1 — righteous before God; 1Ki 8:32 — vindicated by God)
3. The *relational orientation* that constitutes the state is toward God 
   (Mal 3:18 — serves God; Hab 2:4 — lives by faith; 2Sa 23:3 — fears God)
4. The *source* of the state in the NT is explicitly God through Christ 
   (Rom 3:24, 5:19, Phili 3:9)
5. The *attempt* to achieve it without God produces a counterfeit 
   (Luk 18:9 — contempt; Mat 23:28 — hypocrisy; Phili 3:6 — rubbish)

## OVERALL VERDICT ON THE PROPOSITION

**Confirmed:** The proposition holds across the evidence when tested carefully.

**With three important qualifications:**

**Qualification 1 — "State not characteristic" requires nuance.**
The evidence strongly supports treating righteousness as a governing orientation 
rather than one trait among equals. But some verses do list it alongside other 
virtues. The more precise claim the text supports: righteousness is the *primary 
governing orientation* whose evidence permeates thought, speech, desire, and conduct. 
Calling it a "state" is defensible and useful, but should be held with awareness 
that the text itself does not use that category.

**Qualification 2 — The Ezekiel tension about conduct and state.**
Ezekiel's framework sometimes reads as conduct *constituting* the state rather 
than merely evidencing it (Eze 18:5–9). The full picture holds both: conduct 
is the evidence of the state, *and* the state must express itself in conduct 
to remain genuine. The relationship is bidirectional, not one-directional.

**Qualification 3 — The OT picture is more implicit than the NT about God-dependency.**
The OT names persons as righteous without always explaining how the God-relation 
grounds that righteousness. The NT makes the dependency explicit and categorical 
(Rom 3:10, 5:19, Phili 3:9). The OT evidence supports the same conclusion on 
close reading — but it requires reading, not just surface observation.

**The additional finding the test surfaces:**

The proposition, if confirmed, yields a significant inner-being finding:

Righteousness is structurally identical in God and in the righteous human — 
a primary state evidenced by what the subject is and does, never directly defined, 
recognisable through its outputs. The difference is not structural but ontological: 
God's righteousness is self-grounded, permanent, and the source. 
Human righteousness is derived, God-dependent at every point — in its standard, 
its assessment, its relational content, and its source. 

This means righteousness in the human is not merely *influenced* by God — 
it is *constituted* by the relationship with God. Remove that relationship 
and not only does the state disappear — the very framework within which 
righteousness means anything disappears with it.
