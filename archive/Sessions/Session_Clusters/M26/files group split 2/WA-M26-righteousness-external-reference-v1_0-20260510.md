## Part 1 — Testing the God-dependency finding against human sciences

### The finding under test

From the M26-A human righteousness analysis (WA-M26A-human-righteousness-by-term-v1_0-20260509.md), proposition test element 4:

> *"Righteousness in the human is not merely influenced by God — it is constituted by the relationship with God. Remove that relationship and not only does the state disappear — the very framework within which righteousness means anything disappears with it."*

The researcher raised a legitimate challenge: this is a significant finding. Before accepting it, it should be tested against what human experience independently recognises. We may be missing something.

---

### What human sciences study in this territory

The following fields engage directly with what the programme has been calling righteousness. These are described without importing them as programme evidence.

**Moral psychology** studies how humans develop and maintain moral character — whether virtue is a stable trait or situationally variable, and what grounds it. Key researchers include Jonathan Haidt (moral foundations theory — identifies fairness and sanctity as universal moral intuitions across cultures) and Lawrence Kohlberg (stages of moral development — describes a progression from rule-following to principled moral reasoning).

**Virtue ethics** (philosophy, rooted in Aristotle) treats righteousness-adjacent qualities — justice, integrity, practical wisdom — as stable inner states (*hexis*) that are developed through habituation and expressed in action. Aristotle's *dikaiosunē* (justice/righteousness — the same Greek word used in the NT) is structurally close to the Hebrew *tsad.diq* in its operational profile: a stable disposition evidenced by what the person does, never directly observable as an inner quality, requiring a *telos* (an external reference point for what the good is) to be coherent.

**Positive psychology** studies character strengths including fairness, integrity, and moral excellence as measurable human qualities. Peterson and Seligman's VIA character classification includes justice as a core virtue family.

**Developmental psychology** studies conscience formation and moral identity — how a person comes to identify themselves as a moral agent and act consistently from that identity.

---

### What these fields find — relevant to the test

**Finding 1 — Moral character requires an external reference point to be stable**

Moral psychology consistently finds that purely self-referential moral systems are unstable and tend toward rationalisation of self-interest. Research shows that people who ground their moral identity in something beyond themselves — community, transcendent values, a moral framework they regard as authoritative — show greater consistency and resilience in moral behaviour. The reference point does not have to be God, but it must be external to the self.

**Finding 2 — Virtue ethics explicitly requires a *telos***

Aristotle's framework is explicit: virtue requires a vision of the good toward which virtue is directed. Without an external reference point for what the good *is*, virtue becomes circular — I am good because I do what I think is good. The *telos* is the external standard that gives content to virtue. Remove the *telos* and the virtue has no direction.

Aristotle also treats *dikaiosunē* (justice/righteousness) as the most comprehensive of the virtues — not one trait among others but the virtue that encompasses all virtues in their social and relational expression. This aligns structurally with the programme's finding that righteousness functions as a *governing state* rather than one characteristic among peers.

**Finding 3 — Kohlberg's highest stage requires appeal to external, authoritative principles**

Kohlberg's Stage 6 (universal ethical principles) requires a person to appeal to principles they regard as binding independent of their own preference or social convention. Self-generated moral standards are classified at earlier, less developed stages. The most mature moral reasoning, in Kohlberg's framework, requires submission to something the agent did not create.

**Finding 4 — Character strengths are most stable within a meaning framework**

Positive psychology finds that character strengths are most consistently expressed and most resilient when embedded in a meaning framework larger than the self. Strengths that are exercised for purely self-regarding reasons tend to erode under pressure.

---

### The genuine challenge — cases that appear to resist the finding

Some individuals outside any theistic framework exhibit what looks like sustained moral integrity — the humanist tradition, Stoic philosophy, secular moral philosophy. These cases cannot be dismissed without examination.

**Examples that require accounting for:**
- The Stoic sage who grounds moral conduct in reason and natural law rather than divine command
- The secular humanist who grounds ethical behaviour in human dignity and universal principles
- Individuals who exhibit consistent moral integrity without any articulated God-relation

**What these cases actually show on closer examination:**

In each case, the reference point is external to the individual self — it is reason, natural law, human dignity, or universal principles — none of which the individual created or controls. The person is submitting their conduct to something they regard as authoritative and independent of their preferences.

What these cases do not establish is that the *content* of that external reference point is self-chosen or self-generated. They establish that the *structure* of dependency on an external reference point is universal — even in non-theistic frameworks. The disagreement is about what that reference point *is*, not whether one is required.

---

### Assessment — what the human sciences show about the finding

**The human sciences do not contradict the finding.** They confirm the structural dependency on an external reference point as a universal feature of stable moral character. No credible psychological, philosophical, or developmental framework produces a model in which sustained moral character is self-grounded.

**What the human sciences add:** The dependency structure may be more universal than the programme's scriptural finding alone shows. The Scripture analysis establishes that the reference point *must be God* for the state to be genuine. The human sciences establish that *some* external reference point is necessary across all frameworks. The two claims are consistent — they operate at different levels of specification.

**The unresolved question the human sciences surface:**

Is what individuals outside a theistic framework exhibit the same *state* the programme identifies from Scripture — or a functional approximation that shares some characteristics but lacks the constitutive God-relation that Scripture identifies as foundational?

This question cannot be resolved from Scripture alone or from human sciences alone. It is a theological and phenomenological question that sits at the intersection of both. It is appropriate for the tier review to flag it as an open question rather than resolve it prematurely.

---

### Conclusion for the programme

The God-dependency finding stands as a scriptural finding. The human sciences test does not overturn it. They do, however, sharpen the finding by revealing that:

1. The *structure* of dependency (on something external and authoritative) is universal
2. The *content* of that external reference point (God specifically) is what Scripture contributes
3. The apparent exceptions (secular moral integrity) share the structural dependency but differ on the content of the reference point
4. This distinction between structural dependency and content specificity is worth recording explicitly in the tier review findings

## Part 2 — Dictionary and etymological definitions

### Sources reviewed

- Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (OxfordLearnersDictionaries.com)
- Merriam-Webster Dictionary
- Dictionary.com
- Etymonline (Online Etymology Dictionary)
- Wiktionary
- Wikipedia (Righteousness article)
- OED (Oxford English Dictionary) — subscription required; surface-level entry only accessible

---

### Definitions recorded

**Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary:**
*"The feeling that what you are doing is morally acceptable or fair."*

Observation: This definition is notably subjective — it defines righteousness as a *feeling* rather than a state or quality. It also frames it entirely internally (the feeling belongs to the person). This is the weakest definition in the set and contrasts sharply with every other source. It also aligns with the counterfeit form of righteousness the NT identifies (Luk 18:9 — "trusted in themselves that they were righteous").

**Merriam-Webster:**
*"Acting in accord with divine or moral law: free from guilt or sin; morally right or justifiable; arising from an outraged sense of justice or morality."*

Observation: This definition has two layers — conformity to an external standard (divine or moral law) and freedom from guilt or sin. The external reference point is named first and explicitly. The phrase "arising from an outraged sense of justice" introduces an emotional/motivational dimension that the other definitions do not carry.

**Dictionary.com:**
*"The quality of being virtuous, honorable, or morally right. Being righteous means doing what is right — obeying the law or adhering to morals."*

Observation: This conflates the state (quality of being virtuous) with its expression (doing what is right). It does not distinguish between the two. It also treats law-obeying and moral adherence as equivalent, which the programme's scriptural analysis would not accept without qualification.

**Etymonline:**
*"Character of being righteous; purity of heart and rectitude of life; the being and doing right; conformity in character and conduct to a right standard."*

Observation: This older definition (from the Century Dictionary) is the richest. It names three distinct things simultaneously: (1) a character quality (purity of heart), (2) a life expression (rectitude of life), and (3) a conformity to an external standard (right standard). The sequence is significant — character precedes expression. This aligns structurally with the programme's finding that righteousness is a governing state that produces evidence.

The Century Dictionary also distinguishes *righteous* from *upright*, *rightful*, and *just* — noting that *righteous* specifically "carries up the idea of right to the standards, motives, and sanctions of religion." This is the only major English dictionary definition that explicitly identifies the religious reference point as constitutive of the word's meaning rather than merely contextual.

**Wiktionary — theological definition:**
*"Holiness; conformity of life to the divine law."*

Observation: Simple and direct. Divine law is the explicit external standard. This aligns with the Ezekiel material in the programme (Eze 18:9 — "walks in my statutes, and keeps my rules by acting faithfully — he is righteous").

**Wikipedia:**
*"Righteousness is the quality or state of being morally right or justifiable. The concept is rooted in religious or divine law and broadly encompasses moral correctness, justice, and virtuous living as dictated by a higher authority or set of spiritual beliefs."*

Observation: This definition explicitly roots the concept in religious or divine law. It also notes cross-religious universality — righteousness appears in Buddhism, Christianity, Confucianism, Hinduism, Islam, Jainism, Judaism, Taoism, and Zoroastrianism. The cross-cultural presence is analytically significant (see below).

---

### Etymology — what the word's history shows

**Old English:** *rihtwīs* — from *riht* (right) + *wīs* (manner, state, condition, way)

The compound is critical: *right* + *way/manner/condition*. The original Old English is not "doing right things" but "being in a right condition/manner/way." This is a *state* descriptor, not an action descriptor. The earliest form of the English word encodes the state-not-characteristic distinction the programme found from Scripture.

**Middle English:** *rightwisnesse*, *rightwisnes* — justice, conformity to divine or moral law

**16th century shift:** William Tyndale created the modern spelling *righteousness* when translating the Hebrew *tsedek* (צדק) and Greek *dikaios* (δίκαιος). The ending changed under influence of words like *bounteous* — but the semantic core remained.

**The Tyndale connection:** Tyndale used *righteousness* specifically to translate the Hebrew root *צדק (tsedek)* — which appears over 500 times in the Hebrew Bible — and the Greek *δίκαιος (dikaios)*. The English word was coined *for* these biblical concepts. It was not a pre-existing English concept that was then applied to Scripture — it was created to carry the scriptural concept into English. This means the English word's entire semantic history is biblical in origin.

---

### Analytical observations from the dictionary review

**Observation D1 — The word was made for Scripture**

The English word *righteousness* was coined by Tyndale in 1526 specifically to translate *tsedek* and *dikaios*. It has no semantic history independent of Scripture. Consulting English dictionaries for a definition of righteousness is therefore consulting secondary sources about the primary source. The programme's scriptural analysis is working directly with the concept's origin; the dictionaries are working with its English-language reception.

**Observation D2 — The oldest definitions encode state, not action**

The Old English *rihtwīs* (right + manner/condition) and the older dictionary definitions (Century Dictionary: "purity of heart and rectitude of life") consistently describe a *condition of being* before they describe conduct. The more modern and popular definitions (Oxford Learner's, Dictionary.com) tend to collapse this distinction and reduce righteousness to conduct or feeling. The etymological history supports the programme's finding that righteousness is primarily a state.

**Observation D3 — The external reference point is present in older definitions, absent in newer ones**

The Century Dictionary explicitly names religious standards and sanctions as constitutive. The Wiktionary theological definition names divine law. The Wikipedia definition names religious or divine law as the conceptual root. But the Oxford Learner's definition (the most widely consulted) removes the external reference entirely and replaces it with a subjective feeling. This progressive removal of the external reference point in popular usage is itself an analytically interesting cultural phenomenon — and may explain why the counterfeit forms of righteousness (self-assessed, self-referential) have become more culturally visible.

**Observation D4 — Cross-religious universality**

The Wikipedia entry notes that righteousness appears as a concept across Buddhism, Christianity, Confucianism, Hinduism, Islam, Jainism, Judaism, Taoism, and Zoroastrianism. In each tradition, it describes conformity to a standard that transcends the individual. This cross-religious presence confirms the human sciences finding: the structural dependency on an external reference point is universal. The traditions differ on what that reference point is — but all of them require one.

**Observation D5 — Righteousness behaves differently from other inner-being characteristics**

The researcher observed that righteousness appears to be integral to the fabric of inner being but behaves differently from other inner-being characteristics. The dictionary and etymological review supports this observation in an unexpected way: this is the only major inner-being characteristic whose English name was coined specifically to carry a scriptural concept. Words like *love*, *fear*, *hope*, *grief*, *anger* existed in English before they were applied to scriptural translation. *Righteousness* did not. It was purpose-built for the biblical concept.

This may be part of why it behaves differently: it is not a psychological category that was then found in Scripture — it is a scriptural category that was then given an English name. The concept is prior; the English word is derivative. This inverts the usual relationship between a word and its scriptural usage.

## Part 3 — Consolidated observations for tier review

The following observations are drawn from both parts of this debate and are offered for consideration during the M26 tier review (T5–T7). They are not Scripture-based findings — they are contextual and cross-disciplinary observations that may inform how the tier questions are framed and answered.

**For the tier review:**

1. The structural dependency on an external reference point (for human righteousness to exist as a stable state) is confirmed by both human sciences and dictionary/etymological analysis — not only by Scripture. This strengthens the finding's plausibility without changing its scriptural grounding.

2. The oldest English definitions of righteousness encode *state before conduct* — consistent with the programme's scriptural finding that the state is primary and conduct is its evidence.

3. The progressive loss of the external reference point in popular modern definitions (Oxford Learner's) may be analytically relevant to the NT's observation about counterfeit righteousness (Mat 23:28, Luk 18:9). The cultural drift toward self-referential definitions mirrors the scriptural warning about self-referential righteousness.

4. The cross-religious universality of righteousness as a concept (requiring an external reference point) is relevant to how the programme frames its findings for a general Christian audience — this is not a concept unknown outside Christianity; it is a concept whose *content* (God specifically) is the distinctive Christian contribution.

5. The etymological finding that *righteousness* was coined for Scripture (not pre-existing in English) supports treating it as a concept whose definition must be derived from Scripture rather than from general English usage. The programme is working with the concept at its source.

6. Aristotle's treatment of *dikaiosunē* as the most comprehensive of the virtues — not one among others but the encompassing virtue in relational expression — is a notable parallel to the programme's finding that righteousness functions as a governing state rather than one characteristic among peers. This parallel is worth noting but requires caution: Aristotle's framework is philosophical, not scriptural, and the concepts are related but not identical.
