## Analytical observations — H6662 *tsad.diq* — initial read (2026-05-09)

### How righteousness operates in the 159 verses

A close reading of all 159 verses reveals that *tsad.diq* consistently functions as a **label rather than a definition**. It names a person as belonging to a recognised category — *the righteous* — and the verse then describes what happens in relation to that category. The term itself rarely explains what righteousness is internally. What it does is generate a set of relational dynamics around the person so labelled.

**Notable finding:** No verse in this set defines or describes what righteousness *is* as an inner quality. The term is used, not explained. This is analytically significant and warrants further investigation.

---

### Operational groups emerging from the text

**Group 1 — Righteousness attracts divine action** *(~30 verses, heavy in Psalms and Proverbs)*  
God responds *to* the righteous specifically because they are righteous: protecting, upholding, delivering, blessing, rewarding, hearing prayer, watching over. The righteousness functions as the basis for God's engagement. Sub-patterns: God protects/upholds (Psa 37:17, 55:22, 5:12, 146:8, Pro 10:3, Psa 34:15); God delivers (Pro 11:8–9, 12:13, 12:21, Psa 34:19, 37:39, 37:25); God rewards/blesses (Pro 3:33, 10:6, 13:21–22, Psa 58:11, 97:11, 1Ki 8:32, 2Ch 6:23); God tests/watches (Psa 11:5, Jer 20:12, Job 36:7); God hears (Pro 15:29, Psa 34:15).

**Group 2 — Righteousness produces outward expression** *(~25 verses, concentrated in Pro 10–15)*  
The inner quality generates visible outputs — speech that gives life and wisdom, inner character qualities including generosity, care for animals, boldness, hatred of falsehood, and an orientation of joy and gratitude toward God. Sub-patterns: life-giving speech (Pro 10:11, 10:20–21, 10:31–32, 15:28, Psa 37:30); inner character qualities (Pro 12:5, 13:5, 21:26, 12:10, 12:26, 28:1, Psa 112:4, Hab 2:4); communal benefit (Pro 11:10, 11:30, Isa 3:10); orientation toward God (Psa 32:11, 33:1, 64:10, 97:12, 140:13, 68:3).

**Group 3 — Righteousness generates stable outcomes** *(~20 verses, predominantly Proverbs)*  
Material sufficiency, household stability, enduring legacy, blessed offspring. The person's standing as righteous produces measurable life conditions. Sub-patterns: material stability (Pro 15:6, 13:25, 10:16, Psa 37:16, 37:25); endurance of person or household (Pro 10:25, 10:30, 12:3, 12:7, Psa 112:6); legacy and offspring (Pro 11:21, 23:24, 20:7, 10:7).

**Group 4 — Righteousness makes the person a target** *(~20 verses, spread across Psalms, Proverbs, Prophets, Pentateuch)*  
Precisely because they are righteous, hostile forces act against them — the wicked plots, corrupt systems deprive them of justice, they are sold, afflicted, condemned, and killed unjustly. The righteousness provokes opposition. Sub-patterns: wicked attacks (Psa 37:12, 37:32, 34:21, 31:18, Hab 1:4, 1:13); systemic injustice (Amo 2:6, 5:12, Isa 5:23, Pro 17:15, 18:5, Deu 16:19, 17:26, Exo 23:7–8); vulnerable to being swept up in collective judgment (Gen 18:23–28, Eze 21:3–4, Lam 4:13).

**Group 5 — Righteousness as precarious personal standing** *(~8 verses, almost entirely Ezekiel)*  
The status can be abandoned, forfeited, or rendered ineffective. It is non-transferable. It must be maintained. Sub-patterns: can be abandoned (Eze 3:20, 18:24, 18:26, 33:18, Pro 25:26); non-transferable (Eze 18:20); cannot protect if violated (Eze 33:12, 33:13).

**Group 6 — Righteousness as legal/forensic category** *(~15 verses, Pentateuch, historical books, some Proverbs)*  
The term operates in judicial settings: innocent party, just cause, deserving acquittal, comparative moral status in a dispute. Sub-patterns: judicial innocence (Deu 25:1, Gen 18:25, 2Sa 4:11, Exo 23:7); just cause (Exo 23:8, Deu 16:19, 1Ki 8:32, Isa 29:21, 2Ki 10:9); comparative moral status (1Sa 24:17, 1Ki 2:32, Hab 1:13, Pro 18:17).

**Group 7 — Righteousness as community identity** *(~10 verses, mostly Psalms)*  
The righteous form a recognisable group — congregation, generation, tents, enrolled register — and act as witnesses to divine justice. Sub-patterns: named community (Psa 1:5, 14:5, 69:28, 118:15, 118:20, 141:5, 142:7); community as witness (Job 22:19, Psa 52:6, 58:10).

**Group 8 — Righteousness defines a life path** *(~7 verses)*  
A way, a path, a manner of walking. The righteous person has a recognisable trajectory that others can follow or observe (Psa 1:6, Pro 2:20, 4:18, Isa 26:7, Pro 12:26, Hos 14:9, Job 17:9).

---

### Structural observations

**Observation 1 — Ezekiel is anomalous.**  
The Ezekiel cluster (Group 5) speaks of righteousness as something dynamic and precarious — it can be turned from, forfeited, trusted in wrongly. This contrasts markedly with Proverbs and Psalms, where righteousness reads as a settled identity with stable outcomes. These may represent genuinely different operations of the same word rather than different angles on the same reality.

**Observation 2 — Groups 1 and 4 are structurally paired.**  
Righteousness both attracts divine favour and attracts human hostility simultaneously. The same quality that draws God's protective response draws opposition from the wicked and corrupt systems. This is a significant inner-being finding: *tsad.diq* operates in a field of tension, not in neutral space.

**Observation 3 — The term does not define itself.**  
No verse in this 159-verse set defines or describes what righteousness *is* as an inner quality. The term is consistently used to label a person and then describe what follows from that label — what God does, what the person produces, what enemies do to them, what outcomes emerge. The inner content of the quality remains unstated. This may be a key finding for the programme: *tsad.diq* assumes its own content rather than explaining it.

## NT/OT comparative analysis — human righteousness across all terms (2026-05-09)

### Structural finding — the terms do not overlap

The five terms divide cleanly by testament with no overlap:

The NT/OT comparison therefore operates across terms, not within them. What follows compares the OT adjective H6662 *tsad.diq* (the largest single group) with its NT counterparts G1342 *dikaios* and G1343 *dikaiosunē*.

---

### OT treatment — H6662 *tsad.diq*

1. **Assumed content.** The term labels a person as righteous but never defines what righteousness is. Its inner content is assumed, not stated. This is the most significant finding from the first read.

2. **Relational field.** Righteousness operates in a social and cosmic field simultaneously — attracting divine action (protection, deliverance, reward, hearing) and attracting human hostility (plotting, systemic injustice, killing). The same quality produces both responses.

3. **Stable identity.** The dominant picture in Psalms and Proverbs is of a person who *is* righteous as a defining characteristic — not primarily something done or achieved but something the person *is*, which then produces outcomes.

4. **Forensic dimension.** In legal/judicial contexts (Pentateuch, historical books) the term describes the innocent party in a dispute. Righteousness here is moral innocence before a human court.

5. **Precarious in Ezekiel alone.** Only in Ezekiel does the OT treat *tsad.diq* as a dynamic status that can be forfeited, abandoned, or rendered ineffective. This is isolated, not characteristic of OT usage overall.

6. **Communal and legacy.** Righteousness extends outward: offspring are blessed, cities benefit, memory endures. The righteous person is embedded in community and time.

7. **Material grounding.** Proverbs treats righteousness as producing tangible material outcomes: household stability, sufficient food, treasure, inheritance.

---

### NT treatment — G1342 *dikaios* and G1343 *dikaiosunē*

**Pattern 1 — Faith as the basis of righteousness** *(entirely absent from OT H6662)*  
The NT introduces a definition the OT never states: the righteous person is one who lives by faith. This appears as direct citation of Hab 2:4 in Rom 1:17, Gal 3:11, and Heb 10:38 — but the NT treats it as a governing principle. The OT *uses* Hab 2:4 as one verse among many; the NT reads it as the key to what righteousness is. Faith as definition: Rom 4:3, 4:5, 4:9, 4:11, 4:13, 4:22; Jam 2:23; Gal 3:6; Heb 11:7; Rom 9:30; 10:10.

**Pattern 2 — Righteousness as credited/imputed status** *(new NT category)*  
Righteousness can be credited, counted, reckoned to a person — a forensic declaration applied from outside, not a quality developed inwardly. The Abraham pattern ('counted to him as righteousness') appears six or more times in G1343. The OT forensic use describes innocence in a human dispute; the NT forensic use describes standing before God being declared rather than earned. These are structurally different operations.

**Pattern 3 — Righteousness as something pursued or produced** *(overlaps OT but reframed)*  
NT: pursue righteousness (1Ti 6:11, 2Ti 2:22); fruit of righteousness (Phili 1:11, 2Cor 9:10, Heb 12:11); trained in righteousness (2Ti 3:16). The OT produces outcomes from righteousness; the NT frames righteousness itself as something to be actively pursued. The difference in framing is significant: OT *tsad.diq* is what a person *is*; NT *dikaiosunē* is something a person *pursues* or *receives*.

**Pattern 4 — Righteousness with an eschatological dimension** *(new in NT)*  
The righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom (Mat 13:43); the righteous enter eternal life (Mat 25:46); the just are raised at resurrection (Luk 14:14); believers await the hope of righteousness (Gal 5:5); spirits of the righteous are made perfect (Heb 12:23). The OT speaks of the righteous enduring and their memory lasting; the NT explicitly links righteousness to resurrection, eternal life, and future glorification.

**Pattern 5 — Universal absence declared** *(entirely new in NT)*  
Rom 3:10: 'None is righteous, no, not one' — a categorical statement that all humans lack righteousness. The OT consistently assumes that some persons *are* righteous. The NT opens by declaring that none are — and then explains how righteousness can be received. This creates the problem to which the rest of the NT is the answer.

**Pattern 6 — Righteousness transferable through Christ** *(directly overturns Ezekiel)*  
Rom 5:19: 'by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous.' This introduces a corporate and representative dimension absent from H6662. Ezekiel explicitly states that righteousness is non-transferable (Eze 18:20 — 'the righteousness of the righteous shall be upon himself'). The NT directly reverses this: righteousness *is* transferable — through Christ's obedience. This is one of the most structurally significant differences across the two testaments.

**Pattern 7 — Outward appearance versus inner reality** *(new tension in NT)*  
Mat 23:28: 'outwardly appear righteous... but within full of hypocrisy.' Luk 18:9: 'trusted in themselves that they were righteous.' Luk 20:20: 'pretended to be sincere.' The NT introduces a distinction between *appearing* righteous and *being* righteous that is absent from H6662. The OT uses the label as corresponding to reality; the NT is alert to the gap between the external label and the actual inner state.

**Pattern 8 — Continuity with OT** *(what the NT preserves)*  
Despite the differences, the NT preserves significant continuity: righteous persons still attract divine attention (1Pe 3:12; Jam 5:16); they are still contrasted with sinners and the wicked (Mar 2:17, Mat 5:45, Act 24:15); named individuals are still called righteous (Joseph, Cornelius, Simeon, John the Baptist, Lot, Abel); righteousness is still expressed in deeds (1Jo 3:12; Rev 22:11; Act 10:35); and the righteous are still targets of unjust treatment (Jam 5:6; Mat 23:35).

### Questions this raises for the programme

1. If the OT never defines what righteousness *is* internally, and the NT opens by declaring that no one has it — where does the *content* of righteousness come from? Does it reside in the H6664G and H6666 noun terms rather than the adjective?

## CHARACTERISTIC 1 — Righteousness is an alignment with what is true and not harmful in speech

The mouth, tongue, and lips of the righteous are described in ways that reveal
an inner orientation in which speech is governed by what is genuinely good rather
than deceptive or destructive.

- Pro 10:11: "the mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life" — speech produces life, not harm
- Pro 10:20: "the tongue of the righteous is choice silver" — speech is precious, weighty, valuable
- Pro 10:21: "lips of the righteous feed many" — speech nourishes others
- Pro 10:31: "the mouth of the righteous brings forth wisdom" — speech produces wisdom
- Pro 10:32: "lips of the righteous know what is acceptable" — speech is discerning, fitting
- Pro 15:28: "the heart of the righteous ponders how to answer" — inner deliberation precedes speech; not impulsive
- Psa 37:30: "mouth of the righteous utters wisdom, and his tongue speaks justice" — wisdom and justice expressed in speech
- Pro 13:5: "The righteous hates falsehood" — inner aversion to what is false (implies speech governed by truth)

Inference: The state of righteousness includes an inner orientation toward truth and life in 
speech. Speech is deliberate, not reactive. The righteous person does not speak to harm, 
deceive, or gratify — they speak what nourishes, what is true, what is wise.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC 2 — Righteousness is an orientation toward others that extends care, not exploitation

Several verses reveal that righteousness extends concern outward — to neighbours, 
to the poor, to animals, and even to enemies.

- Pro 12:10: "whoever is righteous has regard for the life of his beast" — care extends even to animals
- Pro 12:26: "One who is righteous is a guide to his neighbor" — active orientation toward helping others
- Pro 21:26: "the righteous gives and does not hold back" — generosity without withholding, contrasted with craving
- Psa 37:21: "the righteous is generous and gives" — giving as a defining trait
- 1Sa 24:17: "you have repaid me good, whereas I have repaid you evil" — David called more righteous for returning good for evil
- Pro 12:10: the care for animals implies the orientation extends beyond what social obligation requires

Inference: The state of righteousness includes an outward-facing disposition — 
toward others' needs, toward giving rather than accumulating, toward guiding rather
than exploiting. It is oriented toward the good of others, not toward self-interest.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC 3 — Righteousness is a stable inner foundation, not a fluctuating emotional state

Multiple verses describe the righteous person as possessing an inner stability
that persists through difficulty, setback, and opposition.

- Pro 12:3: "the root of the righteous will never be moved" — deep inner rootedness
- Pro 12:12: "the root of the righteous bears fruit" — rootedness is productive, not passive
- Pro 10:25: "the righteous is established forever" — stable despite surrounding catastrophe
- Pro 10:30: "The righteous will never be removed" — permanence of standing
- Psa 112:6: "the righteous will never be moved; he will be remembered forever" — stability endures beyond death
- Pro 24:16: "the righteous falls seven times and rises again" — resilience; falls do not define the outcome
- Job 17:9: "the righteous holds to his way, and he who has clean hands grows stronger" — persistence and increasing strength

Inference: The state of righteousness is not fragile. It is characterised by deep inner
groundedness that survives adversity. The righteous person may fall but is not ultimately
overthrown. This is an inner stability — not absence of trouble but persistence through it.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC 4 — Righteousness is an alignment with justice as a governing inner value

Righteousness and justice are consistently paired or treated as inseparable.
The righteous person is oriented toward just outcomes — not merely for themselves
but as a governing value.

- Pro 12:5: "The thoughts of the righteous are just" — justice operates at the level of inner cognition
- Psa 37:30: "his tongue speaks justice" — justice expressed outwardly in speech
- Pro 21:15: "it is a joy to the righteous" when justice is done — inner delight in just outcomes
- Eze 18:5: "if a man is righteous and does what is just and right" — righteousness expressed as doing what is just
- Eze 18:9: "walks in my statutes, and keeps my rules by acting faithfully — he is righteous" — faithfulness to God's just standards
- Eze 23:45: "righteous men shall pass judgment" — the righteous are those competent and authorised to render just verdicts
- 2Sa 23:3: "When one rules justly over men, ruling in the fear of God" — righteous rule is just rule

Inference: Justice is not merely a product of righteousness — it is part of its inner 
constitution. The righteous person does not merely behave justly when required; 
justice has become a governing orientation of thought, speech, and action.

- Hab 2:4: "the righteous shall live by his faith" — the inner life of the righteous is characterised by faith/faithfulness

Inference: The state of righteousness in the OT is not separable from a relational
orientation toward God. It is not merely moral behaviour meeting an abstract standard.
God knows the righteous, tests them, hears them, loves them, and is present with them.
The righteous person fears God, serves God, lives by faith before God.
This is the most significant characteristic: righteousness is a state of being rightly
oriented toward God — and from that orientation the ethical qualities flow.

## CHARACTERISTIC 6 — Righteousness produces a recognisable inner emotional orientation

The righteous are not described as emotionally neutral. They experience joy,
delight in justice, and an orientation of gladness toward God.

- Psa 32:11: "Be glad in the Lord, and rejoice, O righteous" — gladness and rejoicing flow naturally from righteousness
- Psa 33:1: "Shout for joy in the Lord, O you righteous! Praise befits the upright" — joy and praise are fitting for the righteous
- Psa 64:10: "Let the righteous one rejoice in the Lord and take refuge in him" — rejoicing and refuge-taking are the righteous response
- Psa 68:3: "the righteous shall be glad; they shall exult before God" — exultation before God is characteristic
- Psa 97:12: "Rejoice in the Lord, O you righteous, and give thanks" — thanksgiving is natural to the state
- Psa 140:13: "the righteous shall give thanks to your name" — gratitude to God is characteristic
- Pro 21:15: "it is a joy to the righteous" when justice is done — inner alignment with just outcomes produces joy
- Psa 58:10: "The righteous will rejoice when he sees the vengeance" — justice being enacted produces joy
- Psa 52:6: "The righteous shall see and fear, and shall laugh at him" — appropriate emotional response to divine action

Inference: Joy, gladness, thanksgiving, and praise before God are not incidental 
to righteousness — they are characteristic of the state. The righteous person's inner
orientation toward God finds expression in these responses. Righteousness is not solemn
or grim; it is characterised by a fundamental joy grounded in the relationship with God
and in the alignment with what is just.

## CHARACTERISTIC 7 — Righteousness is a state of being willing to receive correction

- Pro 9:9: "teach a righteous man, and he will increase in learning" — receptivity to instruction
- Psa 141:5: "Let a righteous man strike me — it is a kindness; let him rebuke me" — the righteous person welcomes rebuke
- Eze 3:21: "the righteous person, when warned, heeds the warning and does not sin" — responsiveness to warning

Inference: The state of righteousness includes an inner disposition of teachability
and openness to correction. The righteous person is not self-sufficient or defensive.
They receive rebuke as a kindness, instruction as an increase. This implies an inner
humility — not an absence of conviction but a willingness to be corrected by truth.

## CHARACTERISTIC 8 — Righteousness is a state that generates opposition from outside

This is not merely what happens to the righteous — it is revealing about what
righteousness *is*. The wicked specifically target the righteous, implying that
righteousness represents something the wicked find threatening or condemning.

- Psa 37:12: "The wicked plots against the righteous and gnashes his teeth at him"
- Psa 37:32: "The wicked watches for the righteous and seeks to put him to death"
- Psa 34:21: "those who hate the righteous will be condemned"
- Psa 31:18: "speak insolently against the righteous in pride and contempt"
- Hab 1:4: "the wicked surround the righteous; so justice goes forth perverted"
- Hab 1:13: "the wicked swallows up the man more righteous than he"
- Amo 2:6: "they sell the righteous for silver" — economic exploitation of the righteous
- Amo 5:12: "afflict the righteous, who take a bribe, and turn aside the needy"
- Isa 5:23: "deprive the innocent of his right"
- Gen 18:23-28: the righteous would be unjustly destroyed alongside the wicked if swept up

Inference: Righteousness is a state that is recognisable and therefore targetable.
It is not hidden or passive — it is visible enough that the wicked identify it and
act against it. The opposition is not accidental. It implies that righteousness
represents a standard or presence that exposes and contrasts with wickedness.
The righteous person does not need to be provocative — their mere existence as
righteous constitutes a contrast the wicked cannot ignore.

## CHARACTERISTIC 9 — Righteousness is a state of personal integrity — inner and outer alignment

Several verses reveal that righteousness means the inner person corresponds to 
the outer life. There is no gap between what is inside and what is expressed.

- Pro 20:7: "the righteous who walks in his integrity" — integrity as the mode of the righteous life
- Job 12:4: "a just and blameless man" — righteousness paired with blamelessness
- Gen 6:9: "Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation" — righteousness and blamelessness together
- Eze 18:9: "walks in my statutes, and keeps my rules by acting faithfully" — faithful action, not just inward profession
- Eze 33:13: the righteous person who trusts in righteousness but "does injustice" forfeits the standing — actions must correspond
- Pro 18:17: "seems right, until the other comes and examines him" — righteousness is subject to testing; the appearance can be misleading

Inference: The state of righteousness requires inner-outer correspondence. It is not
merely an inward claim. The righteous person acts in a way that is consistent with
their inner state. Pro 18:17 is notable: it suggests that a claim to be righteous
can fail examination — implying that true righteousness survives scrutiny.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC 10 — Righteousness is a state that makes one deserving of justice

In legal contexts the righteous person is the one who *should* be vindicated —
whose cause is just, whose innocence merits acquittal.

- Deu 25:1: "acquitting the innocent and condemning the guilty" — righteousness = innocence deserving acquittal
- Exo 23:7: "do not kill the innocent and righteous" — righteousness = innocence that must be protected
- Exo 23:8: "subverts the cause of those who are in the right" — righteousness = having a just cause
- 1Ki 8:32 / 2Ch 6:23: "vindicating the righteous by rewarding him according to his righteousness" — righteous merits vindication
- Pro 17:15: "he who condemns the righteous" is an abomination — condemning the righteous is a category violation
- Deu 16:19: "subverts the cause of the righteous" — the righteous have a legitimate cause
- Isa 29:21: "turn aside him who is in the right" — having the right is characteristic of the righteous

Inference: In judicial usage, righteousness means having acted rightly such that one
deserves just treatment. The righteous person is not someone who is merely claiming 
innocence — they are someone whose case is genuinely sound. The corruption of the 
righteous person's cause by bribery is treated as a serious violation precisely 
because their cause was legitimate.

## WHAT THE VERSES DO NOT TELL US

Despite 159 verses, the following remain unstated:

1. **The inner mechanism by which the state is acquired.** No verse says how a person 
   becomes righteous. The state is assumed. Its origin is not described.

2. **The precise content of 'what is just and right'.** Ezekiel says the righteous person 
   'does what is just and right' (Eze 18:5) but does not define what that is in terms 
   of inner-being content — only in terms of statutes and rules.

3. **The relationship between righteousness and sin.** Ezekiel raises the possibility 
   of a righteous person sinning but does not explain how this is possible if righteousness 
   is a deep inner state. This tension is not resolved within H6662.

4. **Whether righteousness is a single unified state or a composite of qualities.** 
   The evidence suggests it produces multiple characteristics — but whether these are 
   effects of a single underlying state or components of a composite state is not clear 
   from these verses alone.

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-1 — Righteousness is a state grounded in faith, not legal performance

This is the single most explicit definitional statement across both testaments.
The NT does not merely use righteousness as a label — it identifies its ground.

- Rom 1:17: "The righteous shall live by faith" — faith is the defining inner orientation of the righteous person
- Gal 3:11: "The righteous shall live by faith" — same statement in a different argument: faith, not law, is the basis
- Heb 10:38: "my righteous one shall live by faith" — God identifies his righteous one by this quality
- Hab 2:4 (cited): the NT treats this OT verse as the governing principle for what righteousness is

- Rom 4:3 / Gal 3:6 / Jam 2:23: "Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness" — 
  belief itself is the act credited as righteousness; not deeds
- Rom 4:5: "his faith is counted as righteousness" — faith is the equivalent of righteousness before God
- Rom 4:9: "faith was counted to Abraham as righteousness" — faith as the ground of righteous standing
- Rom 4:11: "the righteousness that he had by faith while still uncircumcised" — 
  faith precedes and grounds the standing; the external sign follows
- Rom 4:13: "through the righteousness of faith" — faith is the channel
- Rom 4:22: "his faith was counted to him as righteousness"
- Heb 11:7: "heir of the righteousness that comes by faith" — righteousness is inherited through faith

Inference: The NT is explicit where the OT is silent. The inner ground of the state of 
righteousness is faith — trust in and orientation toward God. The righteous person is 
one who lives by this trust, not one who achieves standing through legal performance.
This is stated as a principle, not just attributed to Abraham alone.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-2 — Righteousness is a state that no human being possesses naturally

- Rom 3:10: "None is righteous, no, not one" — universal absence declared categorically

This is the starting point of the NT treatment. Before describing what righteousness 
is or how it is received, the NT establishes that it is universally absent.

Inference: The NT is working from a different premise than the OT. The OT treats some 
persons as righteous (Noah, Job, David relative to Saul). The NT declares the category 
empty. The state is real — but no one inhabits it naturally. This is not a minor 
qualification; it reframes everything that follows about how righteousness is received.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-3 — Righteousness is a state received from outside the self, not generated within

- Rom 3:24: "justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus" — 
  declared righteous freely, as a gift; not achieved
- Rom 5:17: "the free gift of righteousness" — righteousness is named explicitly as a gift
- Rom 5:19: "by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous" — conferred through another's obedience
- Eph 4:24: "the new self, created after the likeness of God in true righteousness" — 
  righteousness is a characteristic of the new creation, not of the unreformed self
- Phili 3:9: Paul explicitly contrasts "a righteousness of my own that comes from the law" 
  with "that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God" — 
  righteousness from God is distinct from self-generated righteousness
- Heb 11:7: "heir of the righteousness that comes by faith" — received, not earned
- Rom 9:30: "a righteousness that is by faith" — attained by receiving, not pursuing by law

Inference: The NT treats righteousness as something received from outside — from God, 
through Christ, as a gift. This is a structural difference from the OT where righteousness
is a state the person is described as having, with its origin unexamined.
The NT makes the origin explicit: it comes from God, not from the self.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-4 — Righteousness, when self-generated, is a distortion of the state

The NT specifically identifies what happens when a person treats righteousness 
as something they produce themselves — and treats this as a pathology, not a virtue.

- Luk 18:9: "trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and treated others with contempt" — 
  self-assessed righteousness produces contempt for others; it is a sign of the wrong inner state
- Mat 23:28: "outwardly appear righteous to others, but within you are full of hypocrisy and lawlessness" — 
  external righteousness label concealing internal lawlessness
- Phili 3:6: Paul describes his pre-conversion state as "as to righteousness under the law, blameless" — 
  then treats this as rubbish (Phili 3:8) compared to the righteousness from God
- Rom 10:3: "being ignorant of the righteousness of God, and seeking to establish their own" — 
  self-established righteousness is named as the alternative to submitting to God's righteousness

Inference: The NT is alert to a counterfeit version of righteousness — one that looks right 
externally but is grounded in self-assessment and produces pride and contempt.
The genuine state is not self-assessed; it is received. The counterfeit is self-generated 
and self-certified. The NT treats these as structurally opposite.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-5 — Righteousness is a state expressed in deeds but not produced by them

The NT preserves the OT link between righteousness and deeds while insisting 
that deeds do not produce the state.

- 1Jo 3:12: "his own deeds were evil and his brother's righteous" — Abel's deeds expressed his righteousness
- Rev 22:11: "the righteous still do right" — ongoing righteous conduct characterises the righteous person
- Act 10:35: "anyone who fears him and does what is right is acceptable to him" — doing right is associated with acceptability
- Jam 2:23: faith was counted as righteousness — but this follows James's argument that faith without deeds is dead
- Heb 11:33: "through faith conquered kingdoms, enforced justice" — faith expressed outwardly in just acts
- Mat 25:37: the righteous served others in need without self-awareness — deeds without calculation
- Tit 3:5: "not because of works done by us in righteousness" — works in righteousness do not produce salvation

Inference: The NT holds together two things the OT does not explicitly distinguish:
deeds express the state of righteousness, but they do not create it. The righteous person 
acts rightly — but they are not righteous *because* they act rightly. The ground is 
different from the expression.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-6 — Righteousness is a state with an eschatological completion

- Mat 13:43: "the righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father" — 
  righteousness has a future glorification; the state is not yet fully manifest
- Mat 25:46: "the righteous into eternal life" — the righteous are destined for eternal life
- Luk 14:14: "repaid at the resurrection of the just" — resurrection is the appointed reward of the just
- Gal 5:5: "we ourselves eagerly wait for the hope of righteousness" — righteousness has a future dimension still awaited
- Heb 12:23: "the spirits of the righteous made perfect" — the righteous are brought to completion; the state is not yet perfect

Inference: In the NT the state of righteousness has a future dimension entirely absent 
from the OT. The righteous person in the present age inhabits the state partially or 
provisionally — the full expression lies ahead. Righteousness is not merely a present 
ethical state but a trajectory moving toward glorification and completion.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-7 — Righteousness is an inner quality that can be pursued and grown

- 1Ti 6:11: "Pursue righteousness, godliness, faith, love" — active pursuit is commanded
- 2Ti 2:22: "flee youthful passions and pursue righteousness" — righteousness is in the direction of flight from passion
- 2Ti 3:16: "profitable for... training in righteousness" — scripture trains a person in righteousness; it is formable
- Eph 6:14: "having put on the breastplate of righteousness" — righteousness is something actively worn/maintained
- 2Cor 9:10: "increase the harvest of your righteousness" — righteousness is productive and can increase
- Phili 1:11: "filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ" — fruit implies growth over time
- Heb 12:11: "yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it" — 
  discipline produces righteousness as a fruit; it takes time and training
- Mat 5:20: "unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees" — 
  righteousness admits of degrees; it can be greater or lesser
- Mat 5:6: "hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied" — 
  an inner longing for righteousness is itself characteristic of the righteous person

Inference: The NT treats righteousness as something with an active dimension in 
the present — it can be pursued, grown, trained into, hungered for.
This is not in tension with characteristic NT-3 (received as a gift); rather, 
the received state has an active expression that develops. The gift is real but 
its expression is cultivated.

---

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-8 — Righteousness is a state of being in proper relationship with God

- Rom 5:1: "since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God" — 
  the righteous state produces peace with God; its absence means enmity
- Rom 5:9: "justified by his blood" — right standing produced through Christ's atoning work
- Rom 8:10: "the Spirit is life because of righteousness" — righteousness is the condition enabling the Spirit's life
- Eph 4:24: "created after the likeness of God in true righteousness" — 
  righteousness reflects the image of God; the righteous person corresponds to God
- Luk 1:6: "righteous before God, walking blamelessly in all the commandments" — 
  righteousness is defined relationally: before God, not merely before others
- Rom 2:13: "not the hearers of the law who are righteous before God, but the doers" — 
  righteous status is assessed before God, not before human observers

Inference: Righteousness in the NT is fundamentally a relational state — right standing 
before God. It is not merely ethical conformity measured against a standard. 
The relationship with God is the context in which righteousness exists, and 
the right standing produces peace in that relationship.

## CHARACTERISTIC NT-9 — Named righteous persons show righteousness as expressed in specific dispositions

The NT names specific individuals as righteous — and what is said about them reveals 
what the state looks like when inhabited.

- Mat 1:19: Joseph described as just — expressed in merciful restraint, not exposing Mary publicly
- Luk 2:25: Simeon described as righteous and devout — paired with waiting for consolation of Israel
- Mar 6:20: John the Baptist described as righteous and holy — Herod feared and protected him
- 2Pe 2:7-8: Lot described as righteous — expressed in being tormented by surrounding lawlessness;
  "his righteous soul" was distressed daily. Righteousness here produces a suffering inner reaction 
  to injustice — not neutrality.
- Act 10:22: Cornelius described as upright and God-fearing, well-spoken of by the Jewish nation — 
  righteousness is externally recognisable
- Heb 11:4: Abel commended as righteous through his offering — God accepted it; 
  the state was recognised by divine response

Inference: When named individuals are described as righteous, the state is characterised by:
mercy over punishment (Joseph); patient expectation and devotion (Simeon); 
holiness and prophetic courage (John); inner suffering over injustice (Lot);
fear of God and good reputation (Cornelius); a faithful offering recognised by God (Abel).
These are not identical expressions but they share a common orientation: 
toward God, toward others in mercy, away from injustice.

## WHAT THE NT VERSES DO NOT TELL US

1. **The precise inner experience of the state.** The NT describes the ground (faith),
   the source (God, Christ), the expressions (deeds, speech, orientation) — 
   but not the phenomenological quality of inhabiting the state.

2. **How faith itself operates.** Faith is named as the ground of righteousness 
   but its inner mechanism is not described within these verses.

3. **The relationship between the imputed state and the experienced state.** 
   The NT declares righteousness as a gift (Rom 3:24) and also commands pursuit 
   of it (1Ti 6:11). The relationship between the declared status and the 
   cultivated quality is not resolved within these verses alone.

## ELEMENT 1: "Being righteous is a state, not a characteristic"

### What the distinction requires

A *characteristic* is a trait or quality among others — one feature of a person 
alongside other features. A *state* is a condition of being that governs how 
everything else in the person operates. The proposition claims righteousness is 
the latter.

### Testing against human verses

**Evidence that supports state rather than characteristic:**

H6662: The righteous person's *thoughts* are just (Pro 12:5), their *speech* gives 
life (Pro 10:11), their *desires* tend toward good (Pro 11:23), their *inner being* 
ponders how to answer (Pro 15:28), their *root* does not move (Pro 12:3), their *way* 
is known by God (Psa 1:6). The term governs thought, speech, desire, orientation, 
stability simultaneously. This is not one trait among others — it appears to be a 
governing condition from which multiple expressions flow.

G1342: Lot's *soul* is described as righteous (2Pe 2:8) — righteousness locates in 
the soul, not in a behaviour. Zechariah and Elizabeth were *both righteous before God* 
(Luk 1:6) — the state is assessed before God, not by one act.

G1343: "The new self, created...in true righteousness" (Eph 4:24) — righteousness is 
a quality of the *new self* as a whole, not one feature of it. "Trained in 
righteousness" (2Ti 3:16) suggests formation of the whole person, not acquisition 
of one trait.

H6666: Job "wore righteousness as a garment" (Job 29:14) — a garment covers and 
defines the whole person, not one part. Righteousness described as what David 
"walked before God in" (1Ki 3:6) — a comprehensive mode of being, not an 
isolated characteristic.

**Evidence that resists — where righteousness looks like one characteristic among others:**

Psa 112:4: "he is gracious, merciful, *and* righteous" — listed alongside other 
qualities, suggesting a characteristic among peers.

Tit 1:8: "upright, holy, and disciplined" — righteousness (upright) listed as one 
required trait among several for a church leader.

Luk 1:6: "righteous and devout" — two qualities named together.

1Ki 2:32: "more righteous and better than himself" — comparative use implies 
a measurable trait, not an absolute state.

**Assessment of Element 1:**

The proposition is substantially supported but requires qualification. The dominant 
usage treats righteousness as a *governing condition* from which multiple expressions 
flow — not as one trait among equals. However, the text does not consistently make 
this distinction explicit. Some verses list righteousness alongside other virtues 
in ways that read as characteristic rather than state.

The most precise claim the evidence supports: righteousness functions as a *primary 
governing orientation* whose evidence appears across the whole person — thought, 
speech, desire, conduct, stability. Whether this should be called a "state" rather 
than a "characteristic" is a conceptual distinction the text does not make in those 
terms — but the functional evidence points toward the former.

## ELEMENT 2: "It is derived from what it represents — its evidence of existence comes 
## from what the object is or does"

### Testing against human verses

This element claims righteousness is not directly observable as a quality — it is 
inferred from evidence. The state reveals itself through what the person *is* or *does*.

**Evidence supporting this:**

H6662: No verse says "righteousness looks like X internally." Instead:
- "the mouth of the righteous is a fountain of life" (Pro 10:11) — speech is the evidence
- "the thoughts of the righteous are just" (Pro 12:5) — thought pattern is the evidence
- "the righteous hates falsehood" (Pro 13:5) — inner aversion is evidence
- "the righteous who walks in his integrity" (Pro 20:7) — manner of life is evidence
- "he was righteous in his own eyes" (Job 32:1) — self-assessment, implying 
  righteousness is known through evaluation, not direct perception

G1342: Joseph's righteousness evidenced by merciful restraint (Mat 1:19); Cornelius 
evidenced by being "well spoken of by the whole Jewish nation" (Act 10:22); 
Abel evidenced by God commending him by accepting his gifts (Heb 11:4); 
Lot evidenced by distress over surrounding lawlessness (2Pe 2:8).
In each named case, righteousness is *inferred* from an action, a response, or 
a divine commendation — not stated as a directly visible quality.

G1343: "the fruit of righteousness" (Phili 1:11, Heb 12:11, 2Cor 9:10) — 
fruit is not the tree but what the tree produces. Righteousness is evidenced 
by what it yields. The state is interior; the evidence is exterior.

H6664G: "Righteousness shall be the belt of his waist" (Isa 11:5) — 
worn close and constantly, defining the posture, but the belt is not the body.
"The path of the righteous is like the light of dawn" (Pro 4:18) — the path 
is what the state of righteousness produces; the path is the evidence.

**Most significant evidence for this element:**

Mat 23:28: "outwardly appear righteous...but within full of hypocrisy" — 
this verse is critical. It confirms that righteousness is an interior state whose 
outward evidence can be counterfeited. The appearance is not the state. 
The state and its evidence are distinguishable. This is the clearest textual 
confirmation that righteousness is interior and that external behaviour is its 
evidence, not its substance.

Mat 25:37: The righteous serve others without knowing they are serving Christ — 
"Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you?" The state produces the action 
*without calculation*. The action is the evidence, but the actor is not performing 
for an audience. This is the clearest example of righteousness as a state that 
generates evidence unconsciously rather than strategically.

**Evidence that qualifies:**

Eze 18:5–9: "if a man is righteous and does what is just and right...he is righteous." 
Here the doing *defines* the being rather than merely evidencing it. The passage 
lists specific behaviours and concludes from them that the person *is* righteous. 
This is not purely "state produces evidence" — it reads more as "conduct constitutes 
the state." This is a significant qualification in Ezekiel's framework.

Deu 6:25: "it will be righteousness for us, if we are careful to do all this 
commandment" — obedience *produces* righteousness as a result. Here the conduct 
comes first and the state follows. This partially inverts the proposition's direction.

**Assessment of Element 2:**

Strongly supported overall, with important qualification from Ezekiel and 
Deuteronomy. The dominant picture — especially in Psalms, Proverbs, and the NT — 
is that righteousness is a primary interior state whose evidence appears in conduct, 
speech, and orientation. The Mat 23:28 and Mat 25:37 verses are particularly 
strong confirmations.

The qualification: Ezekiel and parts of Deuteronomy present conduct as constitutive 
of the state rather than merely evidencing it. This is a genuine tension within the 
corpus, not a minor variation.

## ELEMENT 3: "There is similarity in how it works between God and man"

### Testing against the God verses

The proposition claims the operational structure is the same in God and man — 
righteousness as a state evidenced by what the subject is or does.

**Evidence supporting similarity:**

For God, as for humans, righteousness is never directly defined. It is evidenced:

- By his judgments: "he judges the world with righteousness" (Psa 9:8) — 
  the judgment is evidence of the state
- By his acts: "the righteous deeds of the Lord that he performed for you" (1Sa 12:7) — 
  historical acts are evidence
- By promise-keeping: "you have kept your promise, for you are righteous" (Neh 9:8) — 
  covenant faithfulness is the evidence
- By his speech: "from my mouth has gone out in righteousness a word that shall not 
  return" (Isa 45:23) — speech patterns evidence the state
- By his inner disposition: "He loves righteousness and justice" (Psa 33:5) — 
  what God loves reveals what he is
- By paired qualities: righteousness and justice as foundation of his throne (Psa 89:14) — 
  the structural arrangement of his rule evidences the state

**Critical parallel — God's righteousness also resists direct definition:**

Neither God's righteousness nor human righteousness is ever directly defined.
In both cases, the state is evidenced by what the subject does and is. 
The structural parallel holds: the state is interior and primary; 
the evidence is external and secondary.

**Where the parallel holds most clearly:**

God does no injustice (Zep 3:5) — stated negatively, as with the human cases 
where righteousness is evidenced by what it excludes (Pro 13:5: hates falsehood).

God's righteousness produces specific outputs: saves, delivers, vindicates — 
just as human righteousness produces specific outputs: life-giving speech, 
stability, generosity. The logic is identical.

**Where the parallel breaks:**

God's righteousness is *permanent and self-grounded* (Psa 119:142, Isa 51:6–8, 
Job 37:23). It cannot be forfeited, abandoned, or compromised. Human righteousness 
can be forfeited (Eze 3:20, 18:24, 33:18) and in the NT is declared absent (Rom 3:10).

God's righteousness is the *source* from which others receive (Psa 24:5, 
"righteousness from the God of his salvation"; Isa 54:17 "their vindication from me"). 
Human righteousness is the *recipient* form of the same quality, not its origin.

**Assessment of Element 3:**

The operational structure is genuinely similar — in both cases righteousness is 
a primary state evidenced by what the subject is and does, never directly defined, 
and recognisable through its outputs. The proposition holds structurally.

The difference is ontological, not structural: God's righteousness is self-grounded, 
permanent, and the source. Human righteousness is derived, precarious in the OT, 
and absent in the NT until received from God. The *how it works* is parallel; 
the *what it is grounded in* is entirely different.

## ELEMENT 4: "In the human, it cannot be attained without God — taking God out 
## leaves the human unable to be in a state of righteousness"

### This is the most testable element. Testing systematically.

**NT evidence — explicit statements:**

Rom 3:10: "None is righteous, no, not one" — universal absence without qualification. 
This is the baseline NT statement. It does not say "attained with difficulty" — 
it says no human possesses it.

Rom 5:19: "by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous" — 
the only path to the state is through Christ's obedience, not through human effort.

Rom 3:24: "justified by his grace as a gift" — a gift requires a giver. 
Remove the giver and there is no gift.

Phili 3:9: Paul explicitly contrasts "a righteousness of my own that comes from 
the law" (which he counts as rubbish) with "that which comes through faith in 
Christ, the righteousness from God." There are two versions — one is self-generated 
and Paul names it as inadequate. The genuine state comes from God.

Gal 2:21: "if righteousness were through the law, then Christ died for no purpose" — 
the argument presupposes that righteousness *cannot* come through law. 
Remove God/Christ and no path to the state remains.

Gal 3:21: righteousness "would indeed be by the law" — but only "if a law had been 
given that could give life." It was not. Law cannot produce the state.

Eph 4:24: the new self is "created after the likeness of God in true righteousness" — 
the state belongs to the *new creation*, not to unreformed human nature.

**NT evidence — confirming the God-dependency in the OT names too:**

Gen 7:1: Noah is righteous "before me" — assessed by God, before God. 
The frame of reference is God. Remove God and the assessment has no ground.

Psa 1:6: "the Lord knows the way of the righteous" — the way exists because 
God knows and upholds it. The righteous person's path is defined in relation to God.

Eze 18:9: "walks in my statutes, and keeps my rules...he is righteous" — 
the statutes are God's. Remove God and his statutes and there is no standard 
against which righteous conduct is defined.

Mal 3:18: "the righteous...one who serves God" — serves *God* specifically. 
The definition of the state includes the God-relation. Remove God and the 
definition has no object.

Hab 2:4: "the righteous shall live by his faith" — faith directed toward God. 
Remove God and faith has no object.

**OT evidence that appears to resist — the strongest counter-test:**

The OT names persons as righteous without explicitly explaining their 
God-relationship in every case: Noah (Gen 6:9), David (1Sa 24:17), 
Job (Job 12:4), Abner and Amasa (1Ki 2:32). These appear as simple 
character descriptions.

**But testing deeper:**

Gen 6:9: Noah was righteous "before me" (Gen 7:1) — the second reference 
makes the relational frame explicit. God sees the righteousness.

Job 12:4: Job calls on God (12:4a — "I call to God and he answers me"). 
The calling on God immediately precedes the description of righteous and blameless.
The God-relation is present in the same verse.

1Sa 24:17: David is more righteous *because* he returned good for evil — 
but the context is David sparing Saul as the Lord's anointed (1Sa 24:6). 
The restraint is explicitly grounded in fear of the Lord.

The cases that appear to be purely moral descriptions consistently reveal, 
on closer reading, that the God-relation is either stated or immediately 
present in the context.

**The Isa 64:6 confirmation:**

"All our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment" — even the deeds 
that look righteous are corrupted. This is the OT anticipation of Rom 3:10. 
Without God's recognition and grounding, human righteousness is not merely 
absent — it is actively contaminated. The garment metaphor is striking: 
something is worn that *looks like* righteousness but is not.

**The Deu 9:4-6 confirmation:**

"Not because of your righteousness...not because of your righteousness or 
the uprightness of your heart." God explicitly denies that Israel's righteousness 
is the operative cause. They have it as an attributed quality, but it is 
insufficient as a ground for divine action. This does not say they lack 
righteousness — but it says their righteousness has no independent causal power 
before God.

**The Ezekiel tension:**

Eze 18 describes righteousness in terms of keeping God's statutes and rules. 
Remove God and the statutes disappear. Ezekiel's framework is entirely 
God-referenced — the standard is explicitly God's standard. The person who 
"walks in my statutes" is righteous (Eze 18:9). Remove "my statutes" and 
the framework collapses.

**Assessment of Element 4:**

Strongly confirmed by the NT — explicitly and categorically. 
Confirmed by the OT on close reading, though less explicitly.

The proposition "taking God out of the equation leaves the human unable to 
be in a state of righteousness" holds at multiple levels:

1. The *standard* against which righteousness is measured is God's 
   (Eze 18:9 — God's statutes; Psa 1:6 — God knows the way)
2. The *assessment* of whether a person is righteous is God's 
   (Gen 7:1 — righteous before God; 1Ki 8:32 — vindicated by God)
3. The *relational orientation* that constitutes the state is toward God 
   (Mal 3:18 — serves God; Hab 2:4 — lives by faith; 2Sa 23:3 — fears God)
4. The *source* of the state in the NT is explicitly God through Christ 
   (Rom 3:24, 5:19, Phili 3:9)
5. The *attempt* to achieve it without God produces a counterfeit 
   (Luk 18:9 — contempt; Mat 23:28 — hypocrisy; Phili 3:6 — rubbish)

## OVERALL VERDICT ON THE PROPOSITION

**Confirmed:** The proposition holds across the evidence when tested carefully.

**With three important qualifications:**

**Qualification 1 — "State not characteristic" requires nuance.**
The evidence strongly supports treating righteousness as a governing orientation 
rather than one trait among equals. But some verses do list it alongside other 
virtues. The more precise claim the text supports: righteousness is the *primary 
governing orientation* whose evidence permeates thought, speech, desire, and conduct. 
Calling it a "state" is defensible and useful, but should be held with awareness 
that the text itself does not use that category.

**Qualification 2 — The Ezekiel tension about conduct and state.**
Ezekiel's framework sometimes reads as conduct *constituting* the state rather 
than merely evidencing it (Eze 18:5–9). The full picture holds both: conduct 
is the evidence of the state, *and* the state must express itself in conduct 
to remain genuine. The relationship is bidirectional, not one-directional.

**Qualification 3 — The OT picture is more implicit than the NT about God-dependency.**
The OT names persons as righteous without always explaining how the God-relation 
grounds that righteousness. The NT makes the dependency explicit and categorical 
(Rom 3:10, 5:19, Phili 3:9). The OT evidence supports the same conclusion on 
close reading — but it requires reading, not just surface observation.

**The additional finding the test surfaces:**

The proposition, if confirmed, yields a significant inner-being finding:

Righteousness is structurally identical in God and in the righteous human — 
a primary state evidenced by what the subject is and does, never directly defined, 
recognisable through its outputs. The difference is not structural but ontological: 
God's righteousness is self-grounded, permanent, and the source. 
Human righteousness is derived, God-dependent at every point — in its standard, 
its assessment, its relational content, and its source. 

This means righteousness in the human is not merely *influenced* by God — 
it is *constituted* by the relationship with God. Remove that relationship 
and not only does the state disappear — the very framework within which 
righteousness means anything disappears with it.
