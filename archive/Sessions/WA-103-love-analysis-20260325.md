## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Love Vocabulary's Structural Challenge

Reading all 1,455 verses before writing confirms the first and most important analytical finding: the love vocabulary in Scripture does not describe a single emotional state but an architecture of relationship. The word "love" in English collapses what the Hebrew and Greek maintain as distinct. The programme's task is to map this architecture as the text itself presents it.

The vocabulary divides into four primary registers, each with distinct object, motivation, and expression. These are not different intensities of the same thing — they are structurally different forms of orientation toward another person, and the Bible treats them with precision.

### 2.2 The Primary Hebrew Vocabulary

**H0160 a.ha.vah / H0157G a.hev — 45/207 verses.** The primary Hebrew love word. Reading all 45 a.ha.vah verses confirms the full range: Jacob's love for Rachel (Gen 29:20 — "they seemed to him but a few days because of the love he had for her"), Jonathan's love for David as his own soul (1Sa 18:3; 20:17), Amnon's love for Tamar that turned to hatred greater than the love (2Sa 13:15), Solomon's love for foreign women (1Ki 11:2). Reading these together reveals: a.hev/a.ha.vah in the Hebrew is affectionally neutral — it can describe virtuous covenant love, erotic desire, political alliance, or the disordered love that leads to covenant violation.

The most theologically significant use: Deu 7:8 — "it is because the Lord loves you and is keeping the oath that he swore to your fathers." God's love for Israel is the ground of the Exodus — not because Israel deserved love (Deu 7:7 — "it was not because you were more in number than any other people that the Lord set his love on you"), but because he loves and keeps covenant. Jer 31:3 — "I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued my faithfulness to you." The divine a.ha.vah is explicitly *everlasting* — it has no origin in the beloved's merit and no terminus in the beloved's failure. This is the OT's most radical love-statement before 1Jo 4:8.

The Cross-Registry link to desire is established at the root level: H0157G a.hev carries the verb-form that appears in "to love" in the broadest sense, and the desire registry holds its primary analysis because the word's semantic range overlaps with desire at its core. The love vocabulary cannot be fully read without the desire registry.

**H2617A che.sed — 241 verses.** The most important Hebrew love term and one of the most important words in the entire OT. Reading all 241 verses establishes che.sed as irreducible to any English translation: "steadfast love," "kindness," "mercy," "loyalty," "covenant love" are all partial — che.sed is the quality of the relationship between parties who are in covenant, expressed specifically in the act of keeping covenant even when the other party has failed.

Four dominant patterns emerge from reading all 241 verses:

*Pattern 1 — Che.sed as divine covenant faithfulness.* The most concentrated cluster: Exo 34:6-7 (the Sinai name-proclamation — "the Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love"), Psa 136 (the entire psalm structured as "his steadfast love endures forever" refrain after every act of God in history), Lam 3:22 ("the steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end"). Reading these against the full 241 makes plain: che.sed is primarily a divine attribute before it is a human virtue. God's che.sed is the ground of creation (Psa 33:5 — "the earth is full of the steadfast love of the Lord"), the motivation of the Exodus (Exo 15:13), the content of the covenant name, and the ground of eschatological hope (Isa 54:8-10 — "with everlasting love I will have compassion on you").

*Pattern 2 — Che.sed as human covenant-keeping.* Ruth's che.sed toward Naomi (Rut 1:8, 2:20, 3:10), David's che.sed toward Jonathan's house (2Sa 9:1,3,7), the che.sed expected within the covenant community (Mic 6:8 — "to do justice, and to love kindness/che.sed, and to walk humbly with your God"). The human che.sed is always modelled on and called toward the divine che.sed — to show che.sed is to imitate God's faithfulness.

*Pattern 3 — Che.sed and its absence as theological crisis.* Hos 4:1 — "there is no faithfulness or steadfast love and no knowledge of God in the land." Hos 6:4 — "your love (che.sed) is like a morning cloud, like the dew that goes early away" — Israel's che.sed is not nothing but it is unreliable, evaporating under pressure. Hos 6:6 — "I desire steadfast love (che.sed) and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings." The che.sed and knowledge pairing appears as the prophets' most fundamental demand: Israel replaces authentic covenant-love with ritual performance. Job 6:14 — "he who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty" — che.sed and the fear of God are explicitly paired.

*Pattern 4 — Che.sed as the Psalter's primary petition.* Across 241 verses, the single most frequent petition in the Psalter is for che.sed: "show me your steadfast love" (Psa 17:7), "your steadfast love is better than life" (Psa 63:3), "satisfy us in the morning with your steadfast love" (Psa 90:14). The che.sed vocabulary generates the Psalter's affective centre — the prayer of the person who has nowhere else to turn but to God's covenant faithfulness.

**H7355 ra.cham / H7356B ra.cha.mim — 43/69 verses.** Reading all 112 ra.cham/ra.cha.mim verses together reveals the most somatically grounded love term in the Hebrew inventory. Ra.cham derives from re.chem (womb) — the compassion that originates in the body. The mother's compassion for the child of her womb is the primary image, and it generates the vocabulary for God's most tender love.

Exo 33:19 — "I will have compassion (ra.cham) on whom I will have compassion" — paired with "I will be gracious to whom I will be gracious," the divine compassion is presented as sovereign and non-negotiable. Psa 103:13 — "as a father shows compassion (ra.cham) to his children, so the Lord shows compassion to those who fear him." The father-compassion here is secondary — the mother-womb is the origin of the vocabulary, though the Psalter reframes it as fatherly for the God-Israel relationship. Isa 49:15 — "can a woman forget her nursing child, that she should have no compassion (ra.cham) on the son of her womb? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you." The most radical use: God's compassion exceeds even the mother's for her infant — presented as the impossible surpassing of the most intense natural love.

Ra.cha.mim (69 verses) extends this into the communal and relational registers: Gen 43:30 — "his compassion grew warm for his brother" (Joseph's bowel-response at seeing Benjamin — the visceral somatic dimension of ra.cham confirmed at the narrative level). Dan 1:9 — "God gave Daniel favor and compassion in the sight of the chief of the eunuchs" — compassion as divinely-instilled relational favour. The ra.cham vocabulary consistently shows: love that originates in the body (womb), that is experienced as a visceral pull toward the beloved, that cannot be explained by the beloved's merit, and that is most intense toward the most vulnerable.

### 2.3 The Greek Vocabulary — Agapē, Phileō, and the Phil- Taxonomy

**G0025 agapaō / G0026 agapē — 130/104 verses.** Reading all 234 verses of the primary Greek love vocabulary confirms: the NT's use of agapaō/agapē is not merely a translation of OT love terms but a deliberate theological construction, shaped by the Johannine and Pauline corpus, that gives love an ontological density absent from its pre-NT uses.

The most important pattern from reading the 130 agapaō verses: **the commandment structure.** Joh 13:34 — "a new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another." Joh 15:12,17 — the same command twice. 1Jo 3:23 — "this is his commandment, that we believe in the name of his Son Jesus Christ and love one another." Love is not primarily an emotional state in the NT — it is a commanded orientation. The fact that it can be commanded means it is not identical to the feeling of affection: it is an orientation of the will sustained by choice. But it is also more than mere duty — Joh 15:9 — "as the Father has loved me, so have I loved you. Abide in my love" — love is presented as a quality of relationship that can be *inhabited*, not merely performed.

The Johannine love vocabulary is the most structurally significant in the entire registry:
- Joh 3:16 — "God so loved the world that he gave his only Son" — the love that expresses itself as self-giving sacrifice. The "gave" (didōmi) is the key verb alongside agapaō: love is defined by giving, specifically by giving the most precious thing.
- Joh 3:35, 10:17, 15:9 — "the Father loves (agapaō) the Son" — love as the internal relationship of the Trinity. This is the only pre-human, pre-creation love in the corpus.
- Joh 17:23-26 — "that you loved them even as you loved me... that the love with which you have loved me may be in them, and I in them." The love with which the Father loves the Son is the same love intended to inhabit the community of disciples. This is the most radical statement in the NT: the intra-Trinitarian love is the model and content of creaturely love.
- 1Jo 4:7-21 — the Johannine concentration: love is "from God" (4:7), whoever loves "has been born of God" (4:7), "God is love" (4:8), love's definitive act is not human love for God but God's love sending the Son (4:10), and the ground of human love is God's prior love (4:19 — "we love because he first loved us").

**G0026 agapē — 104 verses.** The noun set confirms the pattern established by the verb, with additional density in Paul. 1Co 13:1-13 is the canonical passage — read in full: love (agapē) is patient, kind, not envious, not arrogant, not irritable, not resentful, does not rejoice in wrongdoing, rejoices in truth, bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things. This is the most complete phenomenological description of love in the corpus. It describes not a feeling but a *character* — the shape of a person who loves consistently. Every clause is relational and volitional, not affective: the person who loves chooses patience, chooses not to keep records of wrongs, chooses to bear rather than escape.

Rom 5:5 — "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us." The Spirit is the medium by which divine love enters the human inner life. The love is not merely imitated but *installed* — a Spirit-given interior reality.

**G5368 fileō — 21 verses.** Reading all 21 confirms: fileō in the NT describes the love of friendship, of natural affection, of personal attachment. It is not inferior to agapaō but differently pitched. Joh 11:3,36 — "he whom you love (fileō) is ill" / "see how he loved (fileō) him" — the term used for Jesus's love for Lazarus in the personal-friend register. Mat 10:37 — "whoever loves (fileō) father or mother more than me is not worthy of me" — natural family affection presented as a competing loyalty. The most analytically important set: Joh 21:15-17 — the conversation between Jesus and Peter uses both agapaō and fileō in alternation. Peter's three denials are addressed by three questions — the first two asking "do you agapaō me?" and Peter answering "I fileō you." The third uses fileō. Whether this distinction is theologically significant in the Johannine text is exegetically contested (see PH2 flag below), but reading the full 21-verse set confirms that fileō and agapaō are not synonyms: fileō is the warmer, more personal, more emotionally immediate term; agapaō is the covenant-commitment, chosen-and-sustained term.

**The fil- compound taxonomy.** Reading all fil- compound verses confirms the systematic taxonomy:

*Rightly-ordered love compounds:* G5360 filadelfia (brotherly love, 5 verses — 1Pe 1:22, Rom 12:10, Heb 13:1, 2Pe 1:7, 1Th 4:9), G5387 filostorgos (affectionate, 1 verse — Rom 12:10, paired with filadelfia), G5362 filandros (husband-loving, 1 verse — Tit 2:4), G5388 filoteknos (child-loving, 1 verse — Tit 2:4), G5358 filagathos (lover of good, 1 verse — Tit 1:8), G5377 filotheos (God-loving, 1 verse — 2Ti 3:4), G5361 filadelfos (loving brothers, 1 verse — 1Pe 3:8), G5382 filoxenos (hospitable, 3 verses — 1Ti 3:2, Tit 1:8, 1Pe 4:9).

*Disordered love compounds:* G5365 filarguria (love of money, 1 verse — 1Ti 6:10 — "the love of money is a root of all kinds of evils"), G5366 filarguros (money-loving, 2 verses — 2Ti 3:2, Luk 16:14), G5369 filēdonos (pleasure-loving, 1 verse — 2Ti 3:4), G5380 filoneikos (dispute-loving, 1 verse — 1Co 11:16), G5383 filoprōteuō (love-to-be-first, 2 verses — 3Jo 9), G5379 filoneikia (love of dispute, 1 verse — Luk 22:24).

*Love directed correctly vs incorrectly.* The most important pattern: 2Ti 3:2-4 places the disordered loves in a catalogue — "lovers of self (filautoi), lovers of money (filarguros)... lovers of pleasure (filēdonos) rather than lovers of God (filotheos)." The compound taxonomy is a complete moral typology: the character of a person is constituted by what they love. The lovers of pleasure who are not lovers of God are not characterised by absence of love but by *misdirected* love — they love, but the wrong things.

**G0027 agapētos — 72 verses.** Reading all 72 confirms: "beloved" is both a divine descriptor (Mat 3:17, 12:18, 17:5 — "this is my beloved Son") and a community-of-address term (used by Paul, Peter, John repeatedly — "beloved brothers," "dear friends," "beloved Gaius"). The term creates a social space of love — to call someone beloved is not merely to feel affectionate but to position them within the relational field defined by God's love. The word carries forward the love: the community addresses each other as beloved because they have been addressed as beloved by God.

### 2.4 The Structural Polarity — Love and Hate

H8130 sa.ne (to hate) was extracted as part of the love registry because love and hate constitute a structural polarity across 156 verses. Reading all 140 sa.ne verses confirms: hate in the biblical vocabulary is not primarily the opposite of the emotional experience of love — it is the opposite of covenant orientation.

The most analytically important pattern: Deu 21:15-17 — "if a man has two wives, the one loved and the other unloved (sa.ne)" — where "hated" means not preferred, not chosen as primary. This establishes the semantic boundary: sa.ne can describe deliberate rejection rather than active hostility. Gen 29:31-33 — "the Lord saw that Leah was hated (sa.ne)" — Leah is not despised by Jacob but is secondary to Rachel in his affective preference. The word covers the spectrum from active hostility to mere non-election.

The theological extension: Psa 5:5 — "you hate all evildoers." Psa 11:5 — "the Lord's soul hates the wicked." Mal 1:3 — "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated" (quoted in Rom 9:13) — the divine hate is not the emotional opposite of love but the non-election that leaves the person to their own way. God's love is covenant-choosing; God's hate is covenant-refusing.

The highest concentration of love-hate structural polarity: Pro 13:24 — "whoever spares the rod hates (sa.ne) his son, but he who loves him is diligent to discipline him." Love and hate are defined by what they *do*, not by how they feel. The parent who avoids the difficult action of discipline loves their own comfort more than their child — the action reveals the orientation. This is the most concentrated statement in the Proverbs that love is volitional and action-constituted rather than affective.

### 2.5 The ra.cham Somatic Dimension — Love's Body

H7355 ra.cham (43 verses) and H7356B ra.cha.mim (69 verses) provide the most somatically grounded love vocabulary. The womb-root of ra.cham establishes that the most intense form of love in the Hebrew imagination is the mother's bowel-response to her child — a love that is felt before it is thought, that cannot be reasoned out of existence, that does not wait for the child to deserve it.

The somatic chain: Gen 43:30 — Joseph's compassion "grew warm" (*ni.kam.ru*) — the bowels moving as the interior experience of ra.cha.mim. This is not metaphor in the modern sense — it is description of what ra.cham feels like in the body. The love that originates in the womb (re.chem) is felt in the bowels (me.a.yim). The body is not incidental to this love — it is its primary medium.

The theological use of the somatic basis: Isa 49:15 asks whether a mother can forget the nursing child — and uses the impossibility of forgetting as the measure of divine love. The nursing-bond is the most intense somatic attachment in human experience, and God's love is presented as even more tenacious than this. The somatic root of ra.cham grounds the claim: God's love is not a cool, rational benevolence but something as visceral and non-rational as a mother's attachment to her infant.

### 2.6 The Aesthetic Vocabulary — Love's Perception

H3303 ya.pheh (beautiful, 42 occ, 49 verses), H5273A na.im (pleasant, 11 occ, 14 verses), H5000 na.eh (lovely, 10 occ, 14 verses), H7474 ra.yah (darling, 10 occ, 18 verses), H1730H dod (beloved: love, 10 occ, 58 verses), H3039A ya.did (beloved, 8 occ, 8 verses).

Reading all 162 verses across this cluster reveals: beauty in the love vocabulary is not merely aesthetic preference but a recognition of something real in the beloved that calls forth love. Song of Songs is the primary locus — the dod/ra.yah vocabulary generates the intimate address of the lovers to each other. Reading Song 1:7 ("tell me, you whom my soul loves"), Song 3:1-4 (the nocturnal search for the beloved), Song 5:6 ("my soul failed me when he spoke") — ne.phesh appears in the most intense love-language of the OT, confirming the soul-love cross-registry link.

H1730H dod — 58 verses, almost entirely in Song of Songs. The beloved/dod is the one to whom the soul is drawn, for whom it searches, whose presence restores and absence devastates. This is the most concentrated eros-vocabulary in the Hebrew Bible, and it is included in the canon without apology. The Song's inclusion establishes that erotic love is not excluded from the scope of sacred love-vocabulary — it is included and sanctified by being placed in the canon's register.

The theological reach of the aesthetic vocabulary: Psa 45:7 — "you have loved righteousness and hated wickedness; therefore God, your God, has anointed you with the oil of gladness." Beauty and righteousness are linked: the beloved king is anointed because of his love for righteousness. The aesthetic vocabulary of love extends into the moral vocabulary — the beautiful person is the righteous person, and both are objects of love.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — Spirit, Soul, Body

**Primary circle:** Soul — love is primarily experienced as an inner-being state, but it differs from most affective vocabulary in having a constitutive *relational* dimension that makes Circle One (relational environment) equally primary.

**Spirit dimension:** Rom 5:5 — love is poured into the heart through the Holy Spirit. The Spirit is the medium of divine love entering the inner life — love in its fullest form is Spirit-given, not self-generated. 1Co 13:13 — "the greatest of these is love" — love as the eschatological virtue that persists when faith becomes sight and hope becomes possession. The love that endures is Spirit-sustained.

**Soul dimension:** The ne.phesh vocabulary in the love context (Song 1:7, 3:1-4, Psa 63:1, 1Sa 18:1, 1Sa 20:17) places love as a soul-level experience — the soul that loves is the experiencing, desiring, committed self. The love of David and Jonathan is a soul-knitting (1Sa 18:1); the love of the bride for the beloved is a soul-search (Song 3:1).

**Body dimension:** H7355 ra.cham (womb-root), H7356B ra.cha.mim (bowel-compassion), Gen 43:30 (Joseph's bowels/me.a.yim moved with compassion) — love at its most intense has a somatic substrate. The nursing mother's bond, the bowel-response, the ache of separation (Song 5:6 — "my soul failed me when he spoke") are all bodily experiences of love.

### 3.2 The Six Dimensions — Love's Engagement

**Feeling (Affective):** Very high. Love is experienced — the feeling of being loved (agapētos — beloved), the experience of compassion (ra.cha.mim), the ache of desire for the absent beloved (Song 3:1-4), the sorrow at loving someone in an unrequited way (Joh 17:26 — the love with which the Father loves the Son poured into the disciples). Love has a rich affective register.

**Orienting (Spiritual/God-ward):** Highest. Love is the fundamental orientation of the soul toward God (Deu 6:5, Mat 22:37), toward neighbour (Lev 19:18, Mat 22:39), and ultimately toward all people (Mat 5:44 — love of enemies). Love is not merely one orientation among many — it is the orientation that governs and integrates all others. 1Jo 4:8 — "God is love" — love is the name of God's own being, the orientation that constitutes who God is.

**Relating (Relational):** Highest. Love is constitutively interpersonal — it cannot exist in isolation. It always has an object. The entire love vocabulary (agapaō, che.sed, ra.cham, dod, sa.ne) is structured by the relationship between a lover and a beloved. The community's love for each other (1Jo 4:11-12) is both the evidence of God's love and its continuation in the world.

**Choosing (Volitional):** High. The commanded love (Joh 13:34, Lev 19:18, Deu 6:5) is chosen. The parent who disciplines because they love (Pro 13:24) acts against the path of least resistance. The love of enemies (Mat 5:44) is volitional by definition — the enemy does not elicit natural affection. Paul's 1Co 13 is almost entirely volitional in register: love *chooses* patience, *chooses* kindness, *chooses* not to keep score.

**Being (Moral/Character/Identity):** High. The fil- taxonomy of 2Ti 3:2-4 makes explicit: the character of a person is constituted by what they love. The money-lover and the God-lover are not merely different in their preferences — they are different kinds of persons. Love is the most important dimension of Being in the entire corpus: "God is love" (1Jo 4:8) names God's character, and "love your neighbour as yourself" (Lev 19:18) is the summary of the law's relational requirement.

**Thinking (Cognitive):** Present but secondary. Rom 13:8-10 — love fulfils the law (cognitive recognition of love's relationship to obligation). 1Co 13:2 — "if I have prophetic powers and understand all mysteries and all knowledge... but have not love, I am nothing" — knowledge without love is insufficient. The cognitive dimension is not the absence of love but its insufficient substitute.

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God Is Love — 1Jo 4:8

*"Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love (ho theos agapē estin)."*

This verse does not say God loves, or God shows love, or love is an attribute of God. It says God *is* love. The subject (theos) and the predicate (agapē) are identified. This is the most radical theological claim in the love registry and among the most radical in the entire corpus.

Reading this against the 241 che.sed verses, the 43 ra.cham verses, and the 130 agapaō verses confirms: the verse is not a hyperbole but a summary. Every act of divine love in the OT — the steadfast love that never ceases, the compassion of the mother for her nursing child, the everlasting love proclaimed in Jer 31:3, the love that chose Israel before Israel deserved choosing (Deu 7:7-8) — all these are not performances of love by a God who happens to love. They are expressions of a God who is love. The acts flow from the being.

The Trinitarian implication visible from the Johannine corpus: the Father loves the Son (Joh 3:35, 10:17, 17:24) before creation, before any created beloved. Love as the divine being is not created by the God-world relation; it pre-exists it within the divine life. The love that we receive is the overflow of the love that the Father and Son share in the Spirit.

### 4.2 The Divine Pattern — Love Before Merit

Reading all 130 agapaō verses alongside all 241 che.sed verses reveals a consistent structural feature of divine love: it is always prior to the beloved's merit. Deu 7:7-8 — "not because you were more in number... but because the Lord loves you." Joh 3:16 — "God so loved the world" — not when the world was loveable. Rom 5:8 — "God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." Eph 2:4-5 — "because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our trespasses." 1Jo 4:10 — "in this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son."

The pattern is invariant: God's love precedes and creates the condition for human response, not the reverse. The "we love because he first loved us" (1Jo 4:19) is not merely chronological but ontological — human love is always derivative, a response, a reflection of a prior love.

### 4.3 Che.sed as the Covenant Name

The Sinai name-proclamation (Exo 34:6-7) places che.sed at the centre of God's self-disclosure: "The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness." This formula is quoted or echoed across 13 passages (Num 14:18, Neh 9:17, Psa 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2, etc.). Che.sed is the most repeated word in the divine self-description across the entire OT canon. To know God's name is to know his steadfast love. To invoke God's name is to invoke his che.sed.

### 4.4 The Divine Love's Somatic Expression

Isa 63:9 — "in all their affliction he was afflicted, and the angel of his presence saved them; in his love (a.ha.vah) and in his pity (che.mul) he redeemed them." God's love is not unmoved by Israel's suffering — it is afflicted alongside the afflicted. Hos 11:8 — "how can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel?... my heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender." The divine love, even as God announces judgment, is presented as experiencing something like the bowel-response of the ra.cham vocabulary — an inner recoiling, a compassion that grows warm. This is the most anthropopathic (God-feeling) passage in the prophets and shows that the somatic love vocabulary is applied to God in the most personal registers.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 Love's Somatic Registration

Love in the biblical vocabulary has a somatic dimension that is most visible in the ra.cham cluster. Reading all 112 ra.cham/ra.cha.mim verses in full confirms:

**The womb-root chain:** re.chem (womb) → ra.cham (to have compassion) → ra.cha.mim (compassion, plural of intensity) → ra.chum (compassionate, divine attribute). The anatomical root generates the emotional vocabulary: the love that originates in the womb is the most intense and tenacious natural love available to human experience, and it becomes the primary image for divine love.

**The bowel-response:** Gen 43:30 — Joseph's me.a.yim (bowels/inner parts) were stirred at the sight of Benjamin. The internal physical sensation of compassion-love is not metaphor but phenomenology: the visceral experience of loving recognition. This somatic dimension of love is absent from the agapē vocabulary but present in the ra.cham and fileō vocabularies.

**The somatic expression of love's presence and absence:** Song 5:6 — "my soul failed me when he spoke" — the physical registration of love's ache of separation. 2Sa 1:26 — "very pleasant have you been to me; your love (a.ha.vah) to me was extraordinary, surpassing the love of women" — David's grief for Jonathan expressed in terms of love's felt quality. The love that was felt is now absent, and the body registers the absence as grief.

### 5.2 The Absence of Somatic Link Flags — and What This Means

The primary Greek love terms (agapaō, agapē, agapētos) carry som=0 in the flag data. Reading the 234 agapaō/agapē verses confirms this accurately: agapē as the NT's primary love term is rarely expressed in somatic terms — it is primarily cognitive, volitional, and relational in register. The commanded love (Joh 13:34), the patient love (1Co 13:4-7), the love that endures (1Co 13:7) — these are descriptions of a chosen and sustained orientation, not of a felt experience in the body.

The contrast with ra.cham is analytically significant: the Hebrew vocabulary maintains the body-love connection; the Greek agapē vocabulary foregrounds the choice-commitment register. This is not a loss but a reordering — the NT does not abandon the somatic dimension of love but relocates it in the Spirit (Rom 5:5 — love poured into hearts by the Spirit) rather than in the body's natural compassion-reflexes.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Love's Causative Architecture

Reading all 1,455 verses reveals love as the most causatively complex characteristic in the corpus. Five causative pathways are all active and rich:

**God → humans (love as the primal cause):** 1Jo 4:10,19 — God's love is first; all human love is response. Deu 7:7-8 — divine love precedes and creates the covenant. Jer 31:3 — everlasting love as the ground of faithfulness. The divine love is not caused by anything within the creature — it is the uncaused cause of all love.

**God → humans (love as commanded):** Deu 6:5, Mat 22:37 — love is commanded by God, which means it can be called forth, cultivated, and measured. The commandment does not produce the love directly but structures the conditions in which love can be chosen and maintained.

**Humans → humans (love as transmitted):** 1Jo 4:11-12 — "if God so loved us, we also ought to love one another." The logic of transmitted love: the person who has received divine love becomes a transmitter of it. The community's love for each other is both evidence of God's love (4:12) and its continuation.

**Humans → humans (love as contagious):** Jonathan's love for David produces a covenant (1Sa 18:3); the father's love for the prodigal (Luk 15:20 — he "ran and embraced him") produces reconciliation. Love in action creates conditions for reciprocal love.

**Love producing its opposite — hate:** Gen 37:4-8 — Jacob's preferential love for Joseph produces the brothers' hate. 2Sa 13:15 — Amnon's intense desire ("love") for Tamar, once satisfied by force, inverts to hatred exceeding the love. Reading these verses confirms: love that is disordered or frustrated does not merely diminish — it can invert into its structural opposite.

### 6.2 Love's Structural Oppositions

Reading all 140 sa.ne verses alongside the love vocabulary confirms three structural oppositions:

**Love / Hate as covenant orientation:** Psa 97:10 — "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" Love for God is paired with hatred of evil — the two are not merely sequential but structurally linked. To love what God loves requires hating what God hates. This is not violent hostility but its inverse: the love of righteousness constitutively excludes attachment to wickedness.

**Love / Fear as competing orientations:** 1Jo 4:18 — "there is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear" — the fear-love opposition from the fear registry. Reading this against the full agapē set confirms: the person "perfected in love" has no room for servile fear because love has displaced the self-protective orientation that fear requires. This is the most important cross-registry relationship in the love vocabulary.

**Love / Self-love as ordered vs disordered:** G5367 filautos (selfish, 1 verse — 2Ti 3:2) heads the list of the last-days disorders. Love directed at the self as the primary object is not love expanded but love inverted. Mat 22:39 — "love your neighbour *as yourself*" — the self-love is presupposed as the measure, not as the goal.

### 6.3 Cross-Registry Links — Verse-Level Grounding

**Love ↔ Fear (reg 61):** 1Jo 4:18 — the structural opposition. Read §2.4 of the fear analysis for the detailed treatment. This is the programme's primary affective-axis cross-registry relationship. Priority: HIGHEST.

**Love ↔ Desire (reg 43):** H0157G a.hev appears in the desire registry as a SHARED_TERM. H5691 a.ga.vah (lust, 1 verse — Eze 23:17) at the love-lust boundary. The full desire-love boundary cannot be mapped without the desire registry analysis. Verse-level grounding: Deu 14:26 uses ne.phesh ta.a.vah (soul-desire) and ne.phesh (appetite) synonymously in the same verse; the ne.phesh-a.ha.vah connection runs through the entire love-desire cluster.

**Love ↔ Hope (reg 78):** 1Co 13:13 — "so now faith, hope, and love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love." Che.sed and hope appear together in Psa 33:18 — "the eye of the Lord is on those who fear him, on those who hope in his steadfast love." The hope-love connection requires cross-registry treatment.

**Love ↔ Peace (reg 117):** G1516 eirēnikos (peaceful), G1515 eirēnē (peace) both post-patch extracted in love registry via the "fruit of the Spirit" and "peacemaker" vocabulary. Psa 85:10 — "steadfast love (che.sed) and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other." The che.sed-peace pairing is a canonical structural link.

**Love ↔ Grief (reg 71):** H7356B ra.cha.mim — compassion — shares the semantic field with grief because the womb-love responds to suffering by being moved (Gen 43:30). The capacity for compassion requires the capacity to be grieved by another's pain.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — 1Jo 4:8

*"Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love."*

Having read all 1,455 verses, this verse stands as the most structurally loaded sentence in the love registry. Three elements:

"Anyone who does not love" — the absence of love as a diagnostic indicator. Not loving is not merely a moral failure but an epistemological one — the person who does not love does not *know* God. The knowledge of God is not propositional here but relational: to know God is to participate in what God is, and what God is is love.

"Does not know God" — love as the condition of theological knowledge. This reverses the normal assumption that knowledge of God precedes love of God: in John, love *is* the knowledge. You know God by loving; you fail to know God by failing to love.

"Because God is love" — the ontological ground. The verse functions as the programme's ultimate anchor because it names love not as an activity of God but as God's being. Reading this against the full che.sed vocabulary (God abounding in steadfast love as his character), the full agapaō vocabulary (the Father's love for the Son pre-creation), and the ra.cham vocabulary (God's compassion as womb-origin) — all converge on this verse as their summary.

### 7.2 Deu 6:5 — The First Commandment of Love

*"You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might."*

Having read all 130 agapaō verses that cite this command (Mat 22:37, Mar 12:30,33, Luk 10:27) alongside its OT context, this verse stands as the structural opposite of 1Jo 4:8. Where 1Jo 4:8 establishes that God is love (love as divine ontology), Deu 6:5 establishes that love for God is the commanded orientation of the creature's whole being. The triad — heart (lev), soul (ne.phesh), strength (me.od) — covers the cognitive, experiential, and active dimensions of the person. The person who loves God fully is the person whose every capacity is oriented toward God. This is not an achieved state but a commanded trajectory — and it becomes, in the NT, both the summary of the law (Mat 22:37-40) and the goal of the Spirit's work (Rom 5:5 — love poured in by the Spirit).

### 7.3 Hos 6:6 — Che.sed Over Sacrifice

*"For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings."*

Having read the full 241 che.sed verse set, this verse from Hosea stands as the OT's most concentrated statement of love's priority over religious performance. Che.sed and the knowledge of God are placed in parallel — they interpret each other. The che.sed that God desires is not sentiment but the relational faithfulness that constitutes covenant participation. Sacrifice without che.sed is not a lesser form of worship — it is a substitution that misses the covenant's heart. Jesus cites this verse twice (Mat 9:13, 12:7): mercy/compassion (eleos, the LXX translation of che.sed) is what God requires, not religious correctness. The verse is the OT's clearest anticipation of the NT's love-commandment structure.

### 7.4 1Co 13:4-7 — Love as Character Description

*"Love is patient and kind; love does not envy or boast; it is not arrogant or rude. It does not insist on its own way; it is not irritable or resentful; it does not rejoice at wrongdoing, but rejoices with the truth. Love bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things."*

Having read all 104 agapē verses, this passage stands apart from all others in offering not a definition but a portrait. Every clause is volitional: love *chooses* patience, *chooses* not to keep score, *chooses* to bear. This is the most important NT passage for establishing that agapē love is not a feeling but a characterological orientation — and that the oriented person looks like this. The structure of 1Co 13 is also significant: love is set above tongues, prophecy, knowledge, faith, sacrifice (vv.1-3) because love is the only virtue that is not instrumental — it is its own end.

The most analytically significant clause for Session D: "love rejoices with the truth (alētheia)." Truth and love are structurally joined — not as allies that happen to agree but as aspects of the same reality. The person who loves cannot celebrate what is false; the person who rejoices in truth cannot be indifferent to persons. This joining of love and truth (agapē and alētheia) is a Session D hypothesis.

### 7.5 Joh 15:13 — Love as Self-Giving

*"Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life (psuchē) for his friends."*

Having read all 33 G5590H (soul-as-life) verses alongside the love vocabulary, this verse — where the soul-life vocabulary and the love vocabulary meet — stands as the NT's definition of love's outer limit. The "greater love" (meizōna agapēn) is not more intense feeling but more costly action — the giving of the life itself. This is the verse that makes the cross the measure of love, and it interprets Joh 3:16 from the inside: "God so loved the world that he gave his only Son" — the giving of the Son's life is the greatest love's greatest act. And it establishes the community norm: 1Jo 3:16 extends the same standard to the community — "he laid down his life for us, and we ought to lay down our lives for the brothers."

### 7.6 Jer 31:3 — Everlasting Love as New Covenant Ground

*"The Lord appeared to him from far away. I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued my faithfulness to you."*

Having read all 45 a.ha.vah verses, this verse is the OT's clearest statement of divine love's unconditional character. "Everlasting" (olam) places the divine love outside of time's conditions — it is not activated by Israel's response or deactivated by Israel's failure. The "therefore" that follows — "I have continued my faithfulness (che.sed)" — establishes the link: the everlasting love produces the steadfast faithfulness. This verse is the OT anticipation of 1Jo 4:10 ("not that we have loved God but that he loved us") and of the new covenant's "I will put my fear in their hearts" language (Jer 32:40). Love and faithfulness are the ground on which the new covenant is built.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field

**Psychology — attachment theory and social neuroscience** (primary). Love is the primary subject of relational psychology.
**Neuroscience** (secondary) — the neurobiology of attachment, bonding, and the oxytocin system.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — Attachment theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth, 1978; Levine and Heller, *Attached*, 2010).** Human beings are constitutively attachment-seeking. The primary bond (mother-infant) establishes the template for all subsequent attachment. Secure attachment — experienced when the attachment figure is consistently available and responsive — produces the capacity for healthy relationship, exploration, and resilience. Insecure attachment produces either avoidance or anxiety in relationships. The ra.cham vocabulary maps precisely onto the attachment neurobiology: the nursing mother's bond is the prototype, and the attachment system is the biological substrate of the love that the biblical text describes.

**Finding 2 — The neuroscience of love (Helen Fisher, *Why We Love*, 2004; Damasio).** Three distinct neural systems correspond to three distinct forms of human love: the libido system (sexual desire, dopamine-driven), the attraction system (romantic love, dopamine and norepinephrine, characterised by focused attention on a specific partner), and the attachment system (long-term bonding, oxytocin and vasopressin, characterised by calm security). The Hebrew love vocabulary — a.hav/a.ha.vah (desire and commitment), ra.cham (womb-bond/attachment), che.sed (covenant faithfulness) — maps onto these three systems with striking correspondence, though the biblical account does not separate them as cleanly as neuroscience does.

**Finding 3 — Unconditional positive regard (Carl Rogers, *On Becoming a Person*, 1961).** Rogers's finding that the therapeutic relationship requires the therapist's unconditional positive regard for the client — acceptance not contingent on behaviour — is a therapeutic version of the che.sed structure: love that does not depend on the beloved's performance. The finding that unconditional regard is both rare and transformative in human relationships is consistent with the biblical account of divine love as the exceptional and transformative ground.

**Finding 4 — Empathy and its limits (Simon Baron-Cohen, *The Science of Evil*, 2011).** The empathy spectrum — from zero empathy (psychopathy) to hyper-empathy — describes the range of human capacity to feel with another person. Ra.cham's visceral, involuntary compassion-response maps onto the high-empathy end of this spectrum. The NT's commanded love (agapaō) extends beyond empathy — it requires action for the enemy even in the absence of empathy. The commanded love of enemies (Mat 5:44) is not described as an empathy exercise but as an imitation of God (5:45) — which suggests it operates in a register that neuroscience's empathy concept cannot fully account for.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Structural convergence:** The Hebrew love vocabulary's tripartite structure (desire/erotic, womb-attachment, covenant-faithfulness) corresponds to Fisher's three neural systems (libido, attraction, attachment) with sufficient precision to be mutually illuminating. The biblical account does not reduce love to biology, but the biology grounds and explains the somatic dimension of the love vocabulary.

**Phenomenological convergence:** The ra.cham bowel-response (Gen 43:30) maps onto the research on visceral experience of empathy and compassion. The somatic marker of compassion is real, measurable, and corresponds to the biblical vocabulary's most intense love-images.

**Register divergence:** Science describes love as an evolved attachment mechanism that promotes reproductive fitness and social cooperation. The Bible describes love as the being of God and the commanded orientation of the creature toward God and neighbour. The register difference is total: science describes love's mechanism; the Bible describes its origin, object, and ultimate form. These are not competing claims but complementary descriptions of the same territory from different vantage points.

**Genuine divergence — love of enemies (Mat 5:44).** Neuroscience describes empathy as contingent on the other person's perceived similarity and vulnerability. The commanded love of enemies extends beyond the empathy system's natural reach. It is not the overflow of natural affection but the imitation of divine love — which loves without prior merit in the beloved. This extension of love beyond natural empathy has no scientific explanation and may be the most distinctive feature of the NT love vocabulary relative to any other love-account in the ancient world.

**Genuine divergence — "God is love."** Science has no category for love as a divine ontological predicate. The claim is not merely outside science's current knowledge but outside its domain.

## Section 9: Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circles:** Soul (Circle Two) and Relational Environment (Circle One) are co-primary for love. Love cannot be mapped in either circle alone because it is constitutively relational — it exists only in relationship — and constitutively inner — it is felt, chosen, and sustained from within.

**Spirit dimension:** Rom 5:5 establishes love as Spirit-given at its fullest. The love that exceeds natural affection (love of enemies, agapē as covenant-commitment) is Spirit-enabled. The love that is eschatologically permanent (1Co 13:13 — love abides when faith and hope are completed) is the Spirit's fruit.

**Two-forms structure:** Love has a rightly-ordered form and disordered forms. The rightly-ordered form is love oriented toward God and, from God, toward neighbour — the double love commandment (Mat 22:37-39). The disordered forms are love misdirected: love of money (G5365/G5366), love of self (G5367), love of pleasure (G5369), love of the world (1Jo 2:15). The fil- taxonomy makes explicit: every person loves; the question is the object. The transformation dynamic: love's object is re-ordered by encounter with God's prior love — "we love because he first loved us" (1Jo 4:19).

**For Session D:** Love is the integrating characteristic of the entire corpus. It is the ground of creation (Jer 31:3, Joh 3:16), the structure of covenant (che.sed as covenant-love), the commandment that summarises the law (Mat 22:37-39), the ontological name of God (1Jo 4:8), the eschatological virtue that remains when all else is completed (1Co 13:13), and the diagnostic test for genuine knowledge of God (1Jo 4:8). No other characteristic in the 181-word corpus claims this range.

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "unconditional love."** The modern phrase "unconditional love" attempts to capture what the biblical vocabulary describes as che.sed and divine a.ha.vah. But the biblical vocabulary is more precise: the love is not so much "unconditional" (which suggests it is love minus conditions) as it is *covenantally grounded* — it operates within a relationship of commitment that persists despite the beloved's failure. Session D should examine whether "unconditional love" is an adequate modern translation of che.sed or whether it loses the covenantal structure.

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "empathy."** The ra.cham vocabulary addresses the territory of empathy — the visceral response to another's suffering. But ra.cham is wider than empathy: it is the love that results from the empathic response, not merely the feeling of it. Session D should note the distinction.

**Emerging pattern — Love as the programme's integrating characteristic.** After analysing fear, soul, and love, a pattern emerges: love is the characteristic against which the others are measured. Fear finds its resolution in love (1Jo 4:18). The soul finds its rest in love (Song 1:7 — "tell me, you whom my soul loves"). Every other characteristic in the corpus can be described in terms of its relationship to love — is it love's product, love's prerequisite, love's distortion, or love's opposition? Session D should consider whether love should be treated as the programme's central term, with all others radiating from it.

**Structural hypothesis — The love-knowledge unity.** 1Jo 4:8 — "does not love does not *know* God" — and Hos 6:6 — "I desire steadfast love (che.sed) and *the knowledge of God*" — pair love and knowledge as interdependent. The person who loves knows; the person who knows loves. This pairing is not casual — it appears in both the OT prophets and the NT's most concentrated love passage. Session D hypothesis: love and knowledge form a unity in the biblical account of what it means to relate to God — neither is possible without the other. This challenges both purely cognitive theology (which values knowledge over love) and purely affective spirituality (which values feeling over understanding).
