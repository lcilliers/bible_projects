## Section 1: Working Definition

Envy is the pain or resentment felt at another person's good — not merely the desire to have what they have, but the grievance that they have it. Where desire looks at an object and wants it, envy looks at another person and resents their possession of it. The distinction is significant: envy's target is not primarily the good but the person who holds it. It is a relational disturbance, not merely an appetitive one.

The biblical vocabulary sustains and deepens this definition while adding a dimension absent from most modern accounts: the positive pole. The Hebrew root *qana* and the Greek *zēlos* family both cover a semantic range running from destructive envy through competitive rivalry to legitimate zeal and holy jealousy. The same word that names the brothers' envy of Joseph names Elijah's passion for God. The same term that names the chief priests' envy of Jesus names Paul's divine jealousy for the church. This dual register is not a lexical accident — it reflects a theological anthropology in which the capacity for intense investment in another's status is morally neutral in its structure but morally decisive in its object and direction.

A further distinctive: God himself is described as jealous — not as a projection of human envy onto the divine but as a genuine characterisation of the divine response to covenant violation. The Lord whose name is *Jealous* (Exo 34:14) is the theological anchor that prevents any simple condemnation of the jealousy-register as inherently sinful.

**Core vocabulary families:**
- **Hebrew qa.na root:** H7065 qa.na (verb), H7067G/H qan.na (divine name/adjective), H7068 qin.ah (noun), H7072 qan.no (intensive adj)
- **Greek phthonos family:** G5355 phthonos, G5354 phthoneō — pure envy vocabulary
- **Greek zēlos family:** G2205 zēlos, G2206 zēloō, G2207 zēlōtēs, G3863 parazēloō
- **Peripheral:** G0329/G2200/G2204 (zeal-as-heat), G3788 ophthalmos (evil-eye), H7520 ra.tsad, G6041 zēleuō

## Section 2: Hebrew Vocabulary Survey

### 2.1 The Qa.na Root — The Dual-Register Structure

The qa.na root is the most structurally complex in this registry because it refuses to settle on either the negative or positive pole. Every form of the root carries both:

**H7065 qa.na** (verb, 34 occ, 29 verses) ranges across:
- Destructive human envy: Gen 26:14 (Philistines envy Isaac's prosperity); Gen 30:1 (Rachel envies Leah's children — "give me children or I shall die"); Gen 37:11 (brothers jealous of Joseph)
- Marital jealousy: Num 5:14/30 — the husband's spirit of jealousy over his wife, the most legally developed jealousy passage in the OT
- Positive human zeal: 1 Ki 19:10/14 (Elijah twice: "I have been very jealous for the Lord, the God of hosts") — zealous devotion at its most intense, in the context of abandonment and persecution
- Prohibited envy (wisdom literature): Psa 37:1 ("fret not yourself, be not envious of wrongdoers"); Psa 73:3 ("I was envious of the arrogant"); Pro 3:31; 23:17; 24:1/19 — the Proverbs cluster of prohibitions against envying the wicked
- Divine jealousy: Deu 32:21 ("they have made me jealous with what is no god"); 1 Ki 14:22; Zec 1:14; 8:2; Eze 39:25; Joe 2:18

**H7068 qin.ah** (noun, 43 occ, 42 verses) is the largest single verse set in the registry and the term that most clearly names both registers:

*Human destructive jealousy:*
- Job 5:2 — "jealousy slays the simple." The first OT statement of envy's lethal power.
- Pro 6:34 — "jealousy makes a man furious, and he will not spare when he takes revenge." Jealousy as the most dangerous emotional state — crueller than wrath (Pro 27:4: "who can stand before jealousy?")
- **Pro 14:30 ANCHOR** — "A tranquil heart gives life to the flesh, but envy makes the bones rot." The most anatomically precise inner-state description of envy in the OT — envy as a somatic process of decay from within. SOMATIC_INNER_LINK flag confirmed.
- Ecc 4:4 — "all toil and all skill in work come from a man's envy of his neighbour." The most sociologically penetrating observation: competitive human productivity is driven by envy. Envy is not merely personal vice but the engine of social rivalry.
- Song 8:6 — "jealousy is fierce as the grave." The comparison places jealousy alongside death as an absolute, inexorable force.
- Eze 35:11 — national envy (Edom's envy of Israel) as the ground of divine judgment

*Human positive zeal:*
- Psa 69:9 — "zeal for your house has consumed me" (cited in Joh 2:17 at the temple cleansing)
- Psa 119:139 — "my zeal consumes me because my foes forget your words"
- 2 Ki 10:16 — Jehu's "zeal for the Lord" (however problematic in context)
- Isa 9:7; 37:32 — "the zeal of the Lord will do this" — divine zeal as the engine of eschatological accomplishment

*Divine jealousy (largest sub-category):*
The Ezekiel corpus is the most concentrated: Eze 5:13; 16:38/42; 23:25; 36:5-6; 38:19; Zep 1:18; 3:8 — divine jealousy as the inner-state ground of covenant judgment. The formula "in my hot jealousy" / "in the fire of my jealousy" (Eze 38:19; Zep 1:18) directly links qin.ah to the fire metaphor (Deu 4:24 — "a consuming fire, a jealous God") — this is why the fire family entered the registry.

**H7067G / H7067H qan.na** — the divine jealousy name and adjective:

Exo 34:14 carries both H7067G (as a proper name: *"the Lord, whose name is Jealous"*) and H7067H (as an adjective: *"a jealous God"*). This verse is unique in the OT: jealousy is not merely an attribute but a *name* — the divine being is constitutively identified with jealousy. The other H7067H occurrences are the Decalogue (Exo 20:5; Deu 5:9), Deu 4:24 ("a consuming fire, a jealous God"), and Deu 6:15. These are the foundational covenant clauses — divine jealousy as the theological ground of the first and second commandments.

**H7072 qan.no** — the intensive form in covenantal judgment contexts: Jos 24:19 (Joshua's solemn warning at Shechem: "he is a jealous God, he will not forgive your transgressions") and Nah 1:2 ("the Lord is a jealous and avenging God"). Both are among the most uncompromising statements of divine judgment in the OT — jealousy here is the ground of absolute moral accountability.

### 2.2 H7520 Ra.tsad — The Envious Gaze

**H7520 ra.tsad** (1 occ) — "to watch with envy, to gaze with hatred." Psa 68:16: *"Why do you look with hatred, O many-peaked mountain, at the mount that God desired for his abode?"* The mountains watching Zion with envious hostility — the personified landscape enacting the gaze of the envious person. Ra.tsad names envy at its most elemental: the fixed, hostile, watching eye. This connects directly to the evil-eye vocabulary (G3788 ophthalmos) and anchors the eye-envy link in the Hebrew.

## Section 3: Greek Vocabulary Survey

### 3.1 Phthonos / Phthoneō — Pure Envy

**G5355 phthonos** (9 occ, 10 verses) is the closest Greek equivalent to the modern English "envy" — ill-will specifically at another's good fortune, with no positive pole. The word carries no redemptive register. Its NT corpus:

**Mat 27:18 / Mar 15:10 — the crucifixion verse:** Pilate knew the chief priests had delivered Jesus *"out of envy"* (dia phthonon). This is the most theologically momentous single use of a vice-word in the NT: envy is named as the direct cause of the crucifixion. The religious establishment's envy of Jesus's authority, popularity, and freedom was the proximate human motive for the cross. Phthonos here is not an abstract vice but a historical force producing the defining event of salvation history.

**Vice lists:** Rom 1:29 (phthonos among murder, strife, deceit); Gal 5:21 (works of the flesh — envy alongside drunkenness and orgies); 1 Ti 6:4 (envy among the diseased fruits of false teaching); Tit 3:3 ("passing our days in malice and envy"); 1 Pet 2:1 ("put away all malice and envy").

**Phil 1:15** — the most nuanced use: "some preach Christ from envy and rivalry, but others from good will." Phthonos in the service of gospel proclamation — envy of Paul's influence redirected into preaching. The content is right; the motive is envy. This raises the question of whether genuinely good outcomes can arise from envious motivation, and what moral significance attaches to the motive in such cases.

**Jas 4:5** — the most contested verse: "does the Scripture say 'He yearns jealously over the spirit that he has made to dwell in us'?" The divine yearning here uses phthonos-adjacent language for God's possessive, intense desire for the human spirit — the only NT text where divine jealousy is expressed in the phthonos register rather than the zēlos register.

**G5354 phthoneō** (verb, 1 occ) — Gal 5:26: the triad of conceited/provoking/envying names the social dynamics of envy with precision. Envy operates in a relational field: conceit generates provocation which generates envy in others. The three are a social chain, not three independent vices.

### 3.2 The Zēlos Family — Zeal and Jealousy

**G2205 zēlos** (48 occ, 18 verses) reproduces the dual register of Hebrew qana in Greek. The same term covers:

*Negative jealousy:* Act 5:17 / 13:45 — the religious establishment "filled with jealousy" at the apostolic movement's growth; 1 Cor 3:3 — jealousy and strife as marks of carnality; Gal 5:20 — jealousy in the works-of-the-flesh list; Jas 3:14/16 — "bitter jealousy and selfish ambition" as the roots of disorder and every vile practice.

*Positive zeal:* Joh 2:17 (citing Psa 69:9: "zeal for your house will consume me" — at the temple cleansing); Rom 10:2 (Israel's zeal for God, but not according to knowledge); 2 Cor 7:7/11 (the Corinthians' zeal in their repentance response); 2 Cor 9:2 (their readiness/zeal stirring others); 2 Cor 11:2 ("I feel a divine jealousy for you" — Paul's apostolic passion as a direct parallel to divine jealousy); Phil 3:6 (Paul's pre-conversion zeal as a persecutor).

**G2206 zēloō** (44 occ, 11 verses) similarly spans both registers: Act 7:9 (patriarchs jealous of Joseph — directly parallel to Gen 37:11 in the Hebrew); 1 Cor 13:4 ("love does not envy"); Jas 4:2 ("you covet and cannot obtain, so you fight"); and the positive: 1 Cor 12:31; 14:1/39 (earnestly desire the gifts); Rev 3:19 (be zealous and repent — the Laodicean call).

**G2207 zēlōtēs** — the person defined by zeal: Act 22:3 / Gal 1:14 (Paul's pre-conversion identity as a zealot for ancestral traditions); Tit 2:14 (a people zealous for good works); 1 Pet 3:13 (zealous for what is good). The word Simon the Zealot also belongs here (Luk 6:15, Act 1:13 — not in this export but contextually relevant). Zēlōtēs names the person for whom intense, exclusive commitment has become a defining identity.

**G3863 parazēloō** — to make jealous, provoke to jealousy (4 verses). The most theologically distinctive term in the NT envy vocabulary: Paul's argument in Romans 9-11 uses parazēloō as a deliberate divine strategy. Deu 32:21 (cited Rom 10:19) — God will make Israel jealous through those who are not a nation. Rom 11:11/14 — through Israel's trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles, and Paul's hope is that Gentile salvation will in turn provoke Israel to jealousy and thus save some. Jealousy becomes a redemptive mechanism: the same capacity for envy that destroyed Joseph's relationship with his brothers is redirected by God as a provocation toward salvation. 1 Cor 10:22 — "shall we provoke the Lord to jealousy?" — the same mechanism in reverse: idolatry awakens divine jealousy.

### 3.3 Zeal-as-Heat and the Evil Eye

**G0329 anazōpureō / G2200 zestos / G2204 zeō** — the inner-ardour family. 2 Ti 1:6 (fan into flame), Rev 3:15-16 (hot vs. lukewarm — the demand that inner zeal maintain temperature), Act 18:25 / Rom 12:11 (fervent in spirit). These terms contribute the somatic-intensity dimension of zeal: it is experienced as heat, and its absence is experienced as coldness or lukewarmness. The Laodicean letter is the most extended treatment — divine disgust at tepid zeal, demand for hot commitment.

**G3788 ophthalmos** (evil-eye verses only):
The evil-eye idiom (*ophthalmos ponēros*) is the Hebrew *ayin ra'ah* translated. In the NT's Jewish cultural context, the evil eye was the idiom for envy — the malignant, diminishing gaze that one person fixes on another's good. Key verses:

- Mat 20:15 — *"do you begrudge my generosity?"* (lit. "is your eye evil because I am good?"). The workers who received the same pay as those hired later: their envy at the owner's generosity is named as an evil eye. This is the most direct NT use of ophthalmos as an envy-idiom.
- Mar 7:22 — *ophthalmos ponēros* in the vice list alongside phthonos. The two envy terms appear in parallel — the evil eye and envy as cognate realities.
- Mat 6:22-23 / Luk 11:34 — *"if your eye is bad, your whole body will be full of darkness."* The bad/evil eye as a perceptual disorder that darkens the entire inner person. Envy functions here as a failure of moral perception: the person with the evil eye cannot see clearly because the envious gaze distorts their view of reality.

The ophthalmos evil-eye verses reveal envy's perceptual dimension: envy is not just a feeling but a way of seeing — or failing to see. The envious person looks at another's good and perceives it as a grievance. The inner darkness of the evil eye is the darkness of a self that cannot receive generosity, cannot celebrate another's blessing, and therefore cannot perceive reality rightly.

## Section 4: Structural Observations

### 4.1 The Dual Register — Structure, Not Ambiguity

The most important structural finding in this registry is that the dual register (envy/zeal) is not an ambiguity in the vocabulary but a single inner-state structure that can be oriented in opposite moral directions. The capacity for intense investment in another's status — feeling the weight of what another has, wanting to have it or wanting them not to have it — is the same capacity that underlies both destructive envy and holy zeal. What differs is not the intensity but the object and direction:

| Direction | Object | Orientation | Moral valence |
|-----------|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Human envy | Another person's good | Resentment of their advantage | Destructive |
| Human zeal | God's honour / righteousness | Passionate defence | Positive |
| Human rivalry | Another person's status | Competitive emulation | Variable |
| Divine jealousy | Covenant relationship | Possessive care for what is his | Holy |
| Parazēloō | Israel's salvation | Provocation toward return | Redemptive |

The structural insight: envy is not simply one of many vices but a distorted form of a capacity that, rightly ordered, is constitutive of holy zeal. The person who never feels the weight of another's advantage is also unlikely to feel the weight of God's honour. The eradication of the capacity would eliminate both the vice and the virtue.

### 4.2 GOD_AS_SUBJECT — The Theology of Divine Jealousy

The GOD_AS_SUBJECT flags on H7065, H7068, and G2206 (and implicitly throughout H7067G/H and H7072) make this one of the most theologically charged registries for the divine-inner-being question. Eleven Hebrew verses describe God as jealous with qin.ah (Eze corpus); the Decalogue clauses ground the entire moral structure of the covenant in divine jealousy; and the divine name El Qanna makes jealousy a proper-name attribute of God.

The theological stakes: if God is genuinely jealous — not metaphorically but as a real characterisation of his inner response to covenant violation — then jealousy cannot be intrinsically sinful. The human form is condemned in specific expressions (envy of another's prosperity, destructive rivalry) but the capacity itself is present in God. This parallels the wrath registry (where God's wrath is not simply a projection of human anger but a genuine divine attribute) and the joy registry (where God's joy is similarly presented as genuine).

Divine jealousy operates in a different moral universe from human envy, however: God's jealousy is the jealousy of one who has an absolute right to what he claims. The covenant partner who worships idols has genuinely violated a covenant. God's jealousy is the response of one whose legitimate claim has been dishonoured. Human envy, by contrast, is typically the resentment of one who has no claim on what another possesses. The moral difference is not in the emotional structure but in the legitimacy of the claim that grounds it.

### 4.3 The Crucifixion as the Historical Consequence of Envy

Mat 27:18 / Mar 15:10 (phthonos as the cause of the crucifixion) gives envy a unique place in the programme's inner-being vocabulary: no other single inner-state word is named as the direct human cause of the atonement event. Anger, fear, and guilt all play roles in the passion narrative, but phthonos is the explicit label that Pilate gives. The chief priests' envy of Jesus's authority, popularity, and freedom from their control drove the conspiracy. Envy here is not a private sin but a historically consequential force — the engine by which human resentment of divine goodness produces the ultimate act of violence.

This observation connects structurally to the Jas 4:2 pattern: "you desire and do not have, so you murder." The James passage generalises what the passion narrative concretises: envy, when it reaches its terminus, kills.

### 4.4 Envy's Somatic Dimension — Bones Rot

Pro 14:30 is the OT's most striking envy verse precisely because of its anatomical specificity: *"envy makes the bones rot."* The bones are the structural framework of the body — the deepest inner physical organ next to the viscera. Envy rotting the bones is a description of inner decay: the envious person is being consumed from the structural centre outward. This is not merely a metaphor for feeling bad; it maps onto the ancient anthropology in which the inner organs are the seat of life-force, and their decay represents the dissolution of personhood itself.

The contrast with the tranquil heart (*lev marpē* — a healing, gentle heart) is equally precise: the calm inner person gives *life to the flesh* — the undisrupted inner life radiates vitality outward. Envy and equanimity are here presented as physiologically opposed states with opposite somatic consequences.

The SOMATIC_INNER_LINK flag on both H7068 and G3788 (ophthalmos ponēros darkening the whole body) brackets the registry: at the OT end, envy rots the bones; at the NT end, the evil eye fills the whole body with darkness. Both describe envy's somatic totality — it is not a local emotional reaction but a whole-person condition.

### 4.5 The Social Ecology of Envy — Ecc 4:4 and the Labour Paradox

Ecc 4:4 is the most sociologically subversive verse in the registry: *"all toil and all skill in work come from a man's envy of his neighbour."* The Preacher's observation is that competitive human productivity — the engine of civilisation — is largely driven by envy. People work not merely to provide for themselves but to match, surpass, or be seen to surpass their neighbours. This is vanity — it is ultimately futile — but it is also the structural description of competitive social life.

The verse has no direct NT parallel but connects to Paul's parazēloō strategy (provoking Israel to jealousy through Gentile salvation). If envy is the engine of human striving, then the provocation of a productive jealousy can be a legitimate pastoral tool — not the cultivation of destructive envy but the redirection of the comparative impulse toward its proper object.

## Section 5: OT/NT Trajectory

**OT profile:** Envy/jealousy is embedded in the covenant structure from the beginning. The Decalogue's prohibition of other gods is grounded in divine jealousy; marital jealousy is given legal structure in Num 5; wisdom literature both diagnoses envy's destructive power (Pro 14:30; 27:4; Ecc 4:4) and prohibits envy of the wicked (Pro 3:31; 23:17; 24:1). The prophetic corpus develops divine jealousy most extensively — particularly Ezekiel, where qin.ah is the inner-state ground of nearly every major judgment oracle. The patriarchal narratives (Joseph, Rachel/Leah, Cain's implicit envy) provide the narrative embodiment of envy's social consequences.

**NT development:** Four shifts beyond the OT:

1. **Phthonos as the crucifixion cause.** The NT introduces the pure-envy term phthonos alongside the zēlos family, and immediately deploys it in the most theologically weighty context possible (Mat 27:18). Envy is not merely a social vice but a salvifically consequential force.

2. **The evil-eye idiom developed.** The ophthalmos ponēros passages (Mat 6:22-23; 20:15; Mar 7:22) make the connection between envy and perceptual darkness explicit. The NT's inner-being anthropology frames envy as a disorder of moral vision.

3. **Parazēloō as redemptive strategy.** Paul's deployment of provoked jealousy in Romans 9-11 is without OT precedent as a *deliberate pastoral mechanism*. God provoking Israel to jealousy through the Gentiles — using the capacity for envy as a lever toward salvation — is the most theologically creative deployment of the vocabulary in the NT.

4. **The zeal commanded.** The NT commands zeal positively and repeatedly (1 Cor 12:31; 14:1; Rev 3:19; Tit 2:14; 1 Pet 3:13) in a way the OT implicitly models (Elijah, Phinehas) but does not universalise. The NT democratises zeal as a required spiritual quality, not merely an exceptional one.

## Section 6: Canonical Verse Analysis

### §6.1 — Pro 14:30: Envy Rots the Bones

*"A tranquil heart gives life to the flesh, but envy makes the bones rot."* (Pro 14:30)

**Term:** qin.ah (H7068)

The anchor verse for this registry. The somatic precision is the analytical key: envy's destructive effect reaches the bones — the deepest structural layer of the body, the framework of life. The contrast with *lev marpē* (the gentle/healing heart, lit. the heart that is medicine) sets up the structural opposition at the centre of the registry: the inner state of the person determines their somatic vitality or decay. This is not a psychological observation in the modern sense but an anthropological one — the inner person and the bodily person are continuous, and the condition of one registers in the other.

### §6.2 — Exo 34:14: The Lord Whose Name Is Jealous

*"...for you shall worship no other god, for the Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God."* (Exo 34:14)

**Terms:** H7067G (divine name) and H7067H (divine adjective)

This is the only verse in Scripture where jealousy functions as a divine proper name. Not merely an attribute: *Qanna* is what God is called. The verse occurs in the covenant renewal after the golden calf — the context of covenant violation and restoration gives the divine jealousy its full weight. God's jealousy is the covenantal claim that will not release what is his, the passionate exclusivity that defines the relationship. Every subsequent prohibition of idolatry rests on this verse as its theological ground.

### §6.3 — Ecc 4:4: All Labour Driven by Envy

*"Then I saw that all toil and all skill in work come from a man's envy of his neighbour. This also is vanity and a striving after wind."* (Ecc 4:4)

**Term:** qin.ah (H7068)

The Preacher's diagnosis is the registry's sociological statement. Competitive productivity — toil and skill, the two dimensions of human work — is fuelled by envy. This is not merely a moral criticism but an anthropological observation about motivation. The Preacher does not call all work sinful; he names its hidden engine. The conclusion ("vanity and striving after wind") is not that work should stop but that the motive of envy renders it ultimately futile — work to surpass the neighbour serves no telos that outlasts the worker.

### §6.4 — Song 8:6: Jealousy Fierce as the Grave

*"Set me as a seal upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm, for love is strong as death, jealousy is fierce as the grave. Its flashes are flashes of fire, the very flame of the Lord."* (Song 8:6)

**Term:** qin.ah (H7068)

The most intense single verse in the registry. Jealousy is placed in parallel with death and Sheol as an absolute, overwhelming force — and then identified with divine fire (*shalhevet Yah* — "the very flame of the Lord"). The context is erotic love: jealousy here is the exclusivity of total love, the refusal to share what is wholly given. The verse does not condemn this jealousy; it names it as part of love's power. The flame of the Lord is not threatening but the identifying mark of love's intensity. This verse is the clearest evidence that qin.ah as an inner-state belongs on both sides of the moral register simultaneously: fierce, consuming, absolute — and here, holy.

### §6.5 — Mat 27:18: The Crucifixion and Envy

*"For he knew that it was out of envy that they had delivered him up."* (Mat 27:18)

**Term:** phthonos (G5355)

The anchor verse for the NT. Pilate's moral perception names the mechanism: *phthonos* — not theological objection, not legitimate legal concern, but envy of Jesus's standing, authority, and freedom. The chief priests saw Jesus drawing the people, operating with authority they could not match and could not control, and the inner response was phthonos — grief at his advantage, resentment of his freedom. The crucifixion is the terminal expression of that inner state.

The verse should be read alongside the Joseph typology that pervades the passion narrative: Gen 37:11 (brothers jealous of Joseph, sold him into Egypt) — Act 7:9 makes the typology explicit. The pattern repeats: the community's envy of the one marked by special divine favour drives him to apparent death, from which he returns vindicated.

### §6.6 — Rom 11:11 / 11:14: Jealousy as Redemptive Mechanism

*"Through their trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles, so as to make Israel jealous... in order somehow to make my fellow Jews jealous, and thus save some of them."* (Rom 11:11, 14)

**Term:** parazēloō (G3863)

Paul's most theologically developed deployment of the jealousy vocabulary. The argument: Israel's stumbling opened salvation to the Gentiles; Gentile salvation is intended to provoke Israel to jealousy; that jealousy is the mechanism of Israel's eventual return. The same inner-state capacity that produced the brothers' envy of Joseph becomes the instrument of God's restorative strategy. Jealousy, rightly provoked, does not destroy — it draws back. The envied party's flourishing becomes the irresistible pull on the envier.

This is the most counter-intuitive use of any inner-being vocabulary in the programme so far: an inner state typically categorised as destructive is here deployed as a salvific force. The moral valence is entirely determined by what the jealousy is oriented toward and what it drives the person to do.

### §6.7 — Mat 6:22-23: The Evil Eye and Inner Darkness

*"The eye is the lamp of the body. So, if your eye is healthy, your whole body will be full of light, but if your eye is bad, your whole body will be full of darkness."* (Mat 6:22-23)

**Term:** ophthalmos (G3788) — evil-eye register

The perceptual anthropology of envy. The bad/evil eye (*ophthalmos ponēros*) in the Jewish context is the idiom for envy. The verse frames envy as a failure of the perceptual organ — not simply a bad feeling but a distorted way of seeing. The person with the evil eye perceives another's good as a darkness-generating reality; the envious gaze reverses light and darkness so that another's blessing becomes the occasion of the envier's inner night.

The verse sits in the Sermon on the Mount's section on possessions (Mat 6:19-34) — the evil eye is specifically envy directed at prosperity, the inability to receive generosity (Mat 20:15 makes this explicit). Envy as perceptual darkness is the inner-state correlate of the anxiety about material provision (Matt 6:25-34): the two are different inner responses to the same threat of scarcity, one looking outward at another's abundance with resentment, the other looking forward at the future with anxiety.

## Section 7: Science Framework Interface

### 7.1 Envy as Social Comparison — Neuroscientific Resonances

The biblical definition of envy (pain at another's good fortune, with the element of resentment) maps closely onto what social psychologists term *malicious envy* — the form of envy motivated by wanting to pull the other down rather than bring oneself up (Lange & Crusius, 2015, distinguish malicious envy from benign envy). Phthonos in the NT vice lists corresponds to malicious envy; the Jas 4:2 chain (desire → murder) is its terminal expression.

The neural substrate of social comparison — particularly the role of the ventral striatum in processing information about others' rewards relative to the self (Fliessbach et al., 2007) — suggests envy has a specific neurological locus in the brain's reward circuitry. The pain of Pro 14:30 (envy rots the bones) may encode an accurate phenomenology: envy activates the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) — associated with pain processing — in response to another's advantage. The somatic-pain language of the biblical vocabulary may be tracking a real neurological event.

### 7.2 The Dual Register and Zeal — Approach vs. Avoidance

The positive zeal / negative envy dual register maps onto the psychological distinction between approach motivation (moving toward a valued goal with ardour) and avoidance motivation (moving away from another's superior position with resentment). Zelos as positive zeal is approach-oriented — the zealot moves toward God's honour, the church's good, the beloved's wellbeing. Phthonos and the negative qin.ah register are avoidance-structured — the envier moves away from the other's advantage by wishing its removal.

The biblical vocabulary's refusal to split these into separate words reflects a sophisticated observation: the motivational capacity underlying both is the same — intense responsiveness to comparative status. The difference is in the direction of orientation and the object of concern.

### 7.3 Pro 14:30 and Psychosomatic Research

The "bones rot" observation has potential resonance with research on chronic stress, inflammatory processes, and psychosomatic health outcomes. Sustained envy — as a form of chronic negative social comparison — is associated with elevated cortisol, inflammatory markers, and in longitudinal studies with poorer physical health outcomes (Smith & Kim, 2007). The biblical observation that envy produces somatic decay, while made in a pre-scientific idiom, appears to track a genuine correlation. Phase 2 should examine the growing literature on envy and physical health as a potential dialogue partner for the Pro 14:30 analysis.

### 7.4 The Evil Eye — Perceptual Bias Research

The ophthalmos ponēros (evil-eye / envy) passages frame envy as a perceptual distortion — the envious person cannot see clearly because their gaze is structured by resentment. This connects to research on *attentional bias* in emotional states: envious individuals show heightened attention to information about others' advantages and diminished attention to their own goods. The "darkness" of the evil eye is phenomenologically accurate: envy narrows and distorts perception toward the comparison object.

## Section 8: Concentric Circle Mapping and Session D Alignment

### 8.3 Session D Alignment Questions

1. **The dual-register question — one faculty or two?** Is the capacity for zeal and the capacity for envy the same inner-state faculty oriented in different directions, or are they genuinely distinct inner states that happen to share vocabulary? The programme's governing principle (structural categories emerge from data) suggests the former — the vocabulary does not split them. Session D must decide whether to treat them as one category with moral differentiation or as two distinct entries.

2. **Divine jealousy and divine wrath — how related?** The qin.ah and ḥēmah (wrath) pairing in Ezekiel and Zephaniah is persistent. The two appear as co-present divine inner states in judgment contexts. Is divine jealousy a form of divine wrath, or its independent ground? Session D synthesis across the wrath (178) and envy (056) registries should address this.

3. **The parazēloō redemptive strategy — implications for inner-being anthropology.** If God deliberately provokes jealousy as a salvific mechanism, what does this imply about the moral status of the capacity for envy? Does it suggest that envy, rightly oriented and provoked, is a feature of the inner-being's responsiveness to God rather than simply a vice to be eradicated?

4. **The crucifixion-as-envy-consequence (Mat 27:18) — cross-registry significance.** No other single inner-state term is named as the direct cause of the crucifixion. What does this imply for the envy registry's place in the programme's overall inner-being anthropology? Should envy be treated as the representative vice — the one whose terminal consequence is most clearly named?
