# WA-116 — Patience
## Phase 1 Analysis | Registry 116 | v4 Specification | 2026-03-09

## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** patience | **Registry ID:** 116 | **Source List:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-09 | **Spec:** Session B Instruction v4

The word patience provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word with high confidence. The qualification rests on three converging lines of evidence in the JSON. First, both primary Greek families — the MAKRO family (makrothumia, makrothumeō) and the HUPO family (hupomonē, hupomenō) — have meaning definitions that locate the state explicitly as internal: makrothumia is defined as "internal and external control in a difficult circumstance" and hupomonē as "a patient frame of mind." Second, the ARK root family in Hebrew generates a coherent inner-being signal through the adjective a.rekh ("slow to anger"), which in its verse set describes a quality of internal disposition in both God and human subjects (Exo 34:6, Pro 14:29, Pro 16:32). Third, the signal feature `god_as_subject` is present on six of the twelve terms, which is a high concentration, and indicates that the word set intersects significantly with inner states attributed to the divine, a pattern associated with inner-being vocabulary elsewhere in the Framework B word set.

The word set also includes terms that are contrast and adjacent terms rather than direct patience terms: la.ah ("to be weary, be impatient"), da.mam ("to be silent, still"), and the main body of aph ("nostril, anger"). These serve the inner-being analysis but are not patience terms in themselves. Their inclusion is warranted, and the qualification assessment is not weakened by their presence.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: ARK — o.rekh, a.rekh, a.rakh

The ARK root carries the image of physical length extended along a spatial or temporal axis. From this single physical image the family generates three terms that operate across measurement, time, and inner-being function. The core image is extension: something that goes on beyond what was expected, whether that is a wall, a day, or the restraint of anger.

**o.rekh (H0753)** is the noun form meaning "length" and accounts for by far the largest verse set in the family (90 verses). The overwhelming majority of those verses use it for physical measurement: the dimensions of the tabernacle, the temple courts, Noah's ark, the land boundaries, and the elaborate architectural measurements of Ezekiel's vision (Eze 40–48). The inner-being sense — meaning definition 2, "forbearance, self-restraint (of patience)" — is present in the dictionary entry but appears in only one verse in the recorded set: Pro 25:15 ("With patience a ruler may be persuaded, and a soft tongue will break a bone"). In that verse o.rekh does genuine inner-being work: patience as a quality that enables persuasion, functioning in the political-relational sphere. The verse reveals that when o.rekh is used in its patience sense, it is pointing to a social capacity, a capacity to extend the length of restraint long enough to achieve a relational outcome. The English translation "patience" for o.rekh in Pro 25:15 correctly identifies the sense, but the etymology is invisible: the Hebrew is calling patience a kind of length, and the metaphor is spatial. The verse set as a whole is dominated by non-patience senses and o.rekh is not itself a patience term for inner-being purposes, but it is the etymological source of the patience image that runs through the entire ARK family.

**a.rekh (H0750)** is the adjective form meaning "slow" in the compound "slow to anger." Its 15-verse set is entirely composed of patience-relevant or directly patience instances. The term is used almost exclusively in the compound arek aph, and its verse set is one of the most theologically concentrated in the entire word set. Ten of the fifteen verses apply a.rekh directly to God: Exo 34:6 names it as one of God's self-declared attributes ("slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love"), and this formulation or close variants of it recur in Num 14:18, Neh 9:17, Psa 86:15, Psa 103:8, Psa 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2, and Nah 1:3. The frequency of this formulation makes a.rekh one of the most repeated divine character attributes in the entire Hebrew lexicon. The remaining verses apply the same quality to humans in the wisdom tradition: Pro 14:29 links it to understanding ("Whoever is slow to anger has great understanding"), Pro 15:18 to the resolution of conflict, and Pro 16:32 to personal mastery ("Whoever is slow to anger is better than the mighty"). The adjective form, unlike the noun o.rekh, has effectively lost its spatial-measurement sense in practice. Its use is so consistently relational and dispositional that a.rekh functions as a dedicated character-quality term. God_as_subject is true for this term.

**a.rakh (H0748)** is the verb form, meaning "to be long, to prolong." Its 34-verse set is more mixed than a.rekh's but contains patience-relevant uses alongside time-duration uses. The dominant use in Deuteronomy is the Hiphil causative "prolong your days," which is covenantal and has no direct inner-being content (Deu 4:40, 5:16, 6:2, and multiple parallels). More relevant are Isa 48:9 ("For my name's sake I defer my anger; for the sake of my praise I restrain it for you"), where the Hiphil form describes God's deliberate prolonging or deferral of anger — a causative inner-being action — and Pro 19:11 ("Good sense makes one slow to anger"), where the same verb is used of human patience. Job 6:11 asks "what is my end, that I should be patient?" using a.rakh in a context of suffering and loss of hope. God_as_subject is true for this term, and causative_form_present is true: the Hiphil of a.rakh is used in Isa 48:9 for God deferring his own anger, which represents a causative inner-being action — God acts upon his own anger to prolong its restraint.

**Within-family verdict:** The three ARK terms are doing genuinely different work within a coherent root image. o.rekh is the etymological base and contributes one key patience verse (Pro 25:15) that reveals the social-relational dimension of the concept; it is not itself a primary patience term. a.rekh is the primary patience term of the family and the most concentrated divine-attribute term in the word set. a.rakh is the verb that carries the action dimension — the deliberate prolonging of restraint — and its causative form adds an active, volitional dimension to the patience concept that neither noun form possesses. The three terms are not consolidation candidates: they occupy distinct grammatical and functional positions within the same conceptual cluster.

### Root Family: APH — aph

**aph (H0639)** stands alone in its root family in this word set but is functionally inseparable from the ARK family because the patience concept in the OT is almost entirely constructed through the compound arek aph, "slow to anger" or literally "long of nostril." The term itself means "nostril, nose, face" in its primary physical sense, and "anger" as its derived inner-being sense. The word occurs approximately 278 times. The verse set in the source file records 219 verses, almost all of which use aph in the sense of anger, often specifically of divine anger. The patience-relevant subset is the "slow to anger" compound, which appears in roughly 12 verses in the recorded set.

The somatic link for aph is direct and structural: anger in Hebrew is not merely metaphorically connected to the nose — it is the nose. The flaring or extension of the nostril is the name of the inner state. This is the most transparent somatic naming in the entire patience word set. The ESV and virtually all English translations suppress this physical image entirely, rendering aph simply as "anger." The English "slow to anger" translates the Hebrew "long of nostril" and the spatial metaphor is doubly lost: neither "long" (which comes from a.rekh) nor "nostril" (which is aph) is visible in the translation.

God_as_subject is true and heavily evidenced: the largest sub-set of aph verses in the file involves God's anger (Exo 32:10-12, multiple Psalms, Lamentations, Ezekiel, Hosea, and others). The divine anger verses are not patience-relevant, but they establish the profile of aph as an attribute that God both possesses and restrains.

**Within-family verdict:** aph stands alone in the word set and is a contrast/adjacent term rather than a patience term. Its contribution to the patience word set is as the object of the ARK family's action: arek aph means "the nostril is extended" — patience, in Hebrew, is the extension (ARK) of the anger (APH). The two root families are functionally interlocking at the word-formation level.

### Root Family: CHKH — cha.khah

**cha.khah (H2442)** carries the meaning "to wait, wait for, await" with Qal and Piel stems. Its 13-verse set shows the term functioning in two distinct ways. In its more common use it denotes active, expectant waiting for a person or event: Psa 33:20 ("Our soul waits for the Lord"), Isa 8:17 ("I will wait for the Lord"), Isa 30:18 ("The Lord waits to be gracious to you"), Dan 12:12 ("Blessed is he who waits"). The waiting is consistently oriented toward an object: one waits for something or someone specific. The Piel form adds the senses of tarrying, waiting in ambush (Hos 6:9), and longing for (Job 3:21: "who long for death, but it comes not"). The longing-for sense connects cha.khah to the hope vocabulary, but the primary image is attentive readiness rather than hope's forward orientation.

The god_as_subject instance is Isa 30:18, one of the more striking verses in the set: "The Lord waits to be gracious to you." Here it is God who is waiting — waiting for the conditions under which grace can be shown. This is not quite the same as human patience under suffering; it is a disposition of withheld action that is itself a form of care. Whether cha.khah functions as a genuine inner-being term or primarily as a temporal-positional term (one occupies a waiting state relative to an expected event) is a question the verse set does not fully resolve.

### Root Family: YCHL — ya.chal

**ya.chal (H3176)** is defined as "to wait, hope, expect" with Niphal, Piel, and Hiphil forms, and the related word to.che.let identifies the hope derivative. Its 26-verse set is the longest OT individual-term set in the word set and is heavily concentrated in the Psalms (15 of the 26 verses). The Psalms usage consistently frames ya.chal in first-person address to God under conditions of distress or uncertainty: Psa 42:5, 42:11, 43:5 each use the refrain "Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation," Psa 130:5 links waiting and hoping explicitly ("I wait for the Lord, my soul waits, and in his word I hope"), and Psa 131:3 frames the same attitude as a communal call ("O Israel, hope in the Lord from this time forth and forevermore"). The Psalms use of ya.chal reveals it operating in conditions where the outcome is uncertain and the waiting is sustained by expectation rather than just patience under circumstance. Job 6:11 and 13:15 use ya.chal in the most difficult register: "Though he slay me, I will hope in him" — an extreme of sustained forward orientation under mortal threat.

The difference between ya.chal and cha.khah is visible in their verse sets. cha.khah tends toward attentive readiness for something expected to arrive; ya.chal tends toward an inner orientation of hope toward God in conditions of distress where the outcome is uncertain or threatened. cha.khah feels more positional (one is in a waiting state); ya.chal feels more volitional (one maintains hope). The two terms are not consolidation candidates: they carry different affective profiles even where their surface meanings overlap.

### Root Family: LAH — la.ah

**la.ah (H3811)** is the contrast term for patience in this word set. Its meaning — "to be weary, impatient, grieved, offended" — positions it as the state that patience overcomes or refuses. Its 18-verse set covers multiple registers of weariness and impatience: physical exhaustion (Gen 19:11, the men groping for the door), emotional depletion (Jer 20:9, "I am weary with holding it in"), and divine weariness (Isa 1:14, "I am weary of bearing them"; Jer 15:6, "I am weary of relenting"; Mic 6:3, "How have I wearied you?"). The causative Hiphil form is used in Job 16:7 ("God has worn me out"), establishing that impatience or weariness can be caused by another.

God_as_subject is true: in Isa 1:14 God declares weariness of Israel's feasts, and in Jer 15:6 God declares weariness of relenting, which is precisely the inverse of the patience vocabulary — God's patience reaching a limit. This creates a direct signal: the OT patience vocabulary includes God growing weary of restraining judgment, which is the termination of arek aph. The connection between la.ah and the ARK/APH complex is functional and not merely incidental.

### Root Family: DAM — da.mam

**da.mam (H1826)** carries the meaning "to be silent, still, wait; to be struck dumb; to die." Its 12-verse set places it at the edge of the patience word set: it is included as a waiting/stillness term rather than a patience term in the direct sense. The verse set includes the inner-being uses most relevant to patience: Psa 62:5 ("For God alone, O my soul, wait in silence, for my hope is from him") directly links da.mam with ya.chal in a single verse's context, and Psa 131:2 ("I have calmed and quieted my soul, like a weaned child with its mother") is one of the most concentrated inner-being stillness images in the Psalms. Lev 10:3 presents Aaron's silence as a response to the death of his sons before God — a silence that carries weight and restraint in the face of overwhelming circumstance. Lam 2:10 shows communal silence as a response to catastrophe.

The Hiphil causative form means "to make silent (cause to die)" — the same root covers both the inner state of stillness-waiting and the physical act of silencing by death. This etymological range is unusual and raises a question about whether the stillness of da.mam is always a chosen inner state or whether it sometimes describes a condition imposed from outside. Causative_form_present is true.

### Root Family: MAKRO — makrothumia, makrothumeō

The MAKRO root is Greek and means "long" (makro-) combined with "spirit/anger" (thumos), producing a structural parallel to the Hebrew arek aph ("long of nostril/anger"). This is not a coincidence of translation: the Greek term is theologically and etymologically equivalent to the Hebrew patience concept. Both construct patience as the lengthening or extension of the anger/spirit response.

**makrothumia (G3115)** is the noun form, occurring 14 times. Its verse set is almost entirely epistolary. The term appears in Gal 5:22 as a fruit of the Spirit, in Eph 4:2 alongside humility and gentleness, in Col 3:12 as part of the wardrobe of the new self, in 2Ti 4:2 as a disposition required for ministry, and in Heb 6:12 as a companion to faith in inheriting the promises. Two instances apply makrothumia to God: Rom 2:4 and Rom 9:22 both describe God's patience as the basis of his withholding of judgment, and 1Pe 3:20 attributes God's patience in the days of Noah to this term. 2Pe 3:15 instructs the readers to "count the patience of our Lord as salvation," the most theologically dense instance of the term. God_as_subject is true with strong evidential weight.

**makrothumeō (G3114)** is the verb form, occurring 9 times. Its verse set includes the most direct commands to patience in the NT: Mat 18:26 and 18:29 use it in the parable of the unforgiving servant ("Have patience with me"), 1Cor 13:4 in the great definition of love ("Love is patient"), 1Th 5:14 in the community instruction to "be patient with them all," and Jam 5:7–8 in the eschatological call to patience pending the Lord's coming. Luk 18:7 raises an exegetical question: "Will he delay long over them?" uses makrothumeō to describe a possible divine delay in giving justice — patience as delay in acting, which may be an unfavourable sense. The God_as_subject instance here is ambiguous: the question is whether God's patience (delay) works for or against his people.

**Within-family verdict:** makrothumia and makrothumeō are doing different work appropriate to their grammatical forms. The noun form is predominantly used to describe a character quality — something one has or possesses — and is the more theologically loaded term (Gal 5:22, the divine patience statements). The verb form is predominantly used for commands and descriptions of patience in action in specific relational situations. They are not consolidation candidates. The family as a whole shows makrothumia functioning as the primary NT patience term in the relational and interpersonal register, with its divine-attribute dimension strongly evidenced.

### Root Family: HUPO — hupomonē, hupomenō

The HUPO root combines "under" (hupo) with "remain" (menō), producing the image of remaining under pressure, staying put when circumstances press down. Where the MAKRO family constructs patience as the lengthening of the response (arek aph in Greek form), the HUPO family constructs it as the maintenance of position under load. These are two structurally distinct images for patience: extended restraint versus sustained endurance under pressure.

**hupomonē (G5281)** is the noun form, occurring 31 times in the verse set (source states about 32). Its verse set is the most extensive Greek term set in the word set and covers an unusually wide range of NT genres: Luke-Acts (Luk 8:15, 21:19), the Pauline corpus (Romans, Corinthians, Colossians, Thessalonians, Timothy, Titus), Hebrews, James, Peter, and Revelation. Revelation alone accounts for six instances (Rev 1:9, 2:2, 2:3, 2:19, 3:10, 13:10, 14:12), where hupomonē is consistently linked to persecution, martyrdom, and eschatological faithfulness. Rom 5:3–4 provides the most developed process account: "suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope" — placing hupomonē inside a chain of inner-being development that moves from affliction to hope. Heb 12:1 uses it of the race metaphor, and Jam 1:3–4 mirrors the Romans account, presenting steadfastness as a productive force that brings the person to completion. God_as_subject is false for this term: hupomonē is not attributed to God in the verse set.

**hupomenō (G5278)** is the verb form, occurring 16 times. Its verse set includes the Synoptic perseverance sayings (Mat 10:22, 24:13, Mar 13:13: "the one who endures to the end will be saved"), the Hebrews cluster (Heb 12:2, 12:3, 12:7), and the Luk 2:43 instance where Jesus "stayed behind" in Jerusalem — a non-inner-being use of the same verb that reveals the physical image of remaining in place still active in the Greek. 1Pe 2:20 raises a distinction within endurance: "if when you do good and suffer for it you endure, this is a gracious thing" — the moral quality of the situation affects whether the endurance has value. 1Cor 13:7 uses hupomenō as one of the four qualities of love: "love endures all things."

**Within-family verdict:** hupomonē and hupomenō are doing different work appropriate to their grammatical forms. The noun form carries the heavier theological weight and the process-account (Rom 5:3–4, Jam 1:3–4) is exclusively carried by the noun. The verb form handles the action and command instances (the endure-to-the-end sayings, the Hebrews examples). The HUPO family as a whole differs from the MAKRO family in both its etymology and its verse set profile: MAKRO is relational (patience toward persons), HUPO is circumstantial (endurance under conditions). This is the most important within-NT distinction in the word set.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

Three sets of within-family or cross-family comparisons reveal genuine functional differences.

**a.rekh (H0750) vs a.rakh (H0748) within ARK:** a.rekh is the adjective and functions exclusively as a character-quality descriptor. In its 15-verse set it is applied as a settled attribute — primarily to God, secondarily to the wise human. a.rakh is the verb and functions as an action descriptor: days are prolonged, anger is deferred, a person waits (Job 6:11). The critical difference is in Isa 48:9, where a.rakh is used for God's active deferral of anger: "I defer my anger; I restrain it for you." The verb introduces volitional agency into what the adjective describes as a settled disposition. a.rekh says what God is; a.rakh says what God does. These are not mere synonyms: one describes inner character, the other describes an inner-being action.

**cha.khah (H2442) vs ya.chal (H3176):** Both are waiting terms but with different inner profiles. cha.khah is attentive readiness — waiting in the sense of being positioned for something expected to arrive. Its instances are positional and often externally directed (one waits for a word, a person, a counsel). ya.chal is volitional hope — waiting in the sense of maintaining an inner orientation of expectation toward God in uncertain or threatening circumstances. The most telling contrast is in the Psalms concentration: ya.chal is the Psalms' signature inner-being patience term, and its use under conditions of suffering (Psa 42, 130, 131) and mortal threat (Job 13:15) indicates a term under greater pressure than cha.khah. cha.khah's use in Isa 30:18 (God waiting to be gracious) is probably the best evidence that its positional character is not merely passive: it can describe a purposive withholding.

**makrothumia (G3115) vs hupomonē (G5281):** These are the two primary NT patience terms and they function consistently differently across their verse sets. makrothumia is patience toward persons in relational situations: Eph 4:2 ("bearing with one another"), Jam 5:10 (the prophets' patience toward their persecutors), Luk 18:7 (patience toward God as he delays giving justice). hupomonē is patience under circumstances and pressures: Rom 5:3–4 (suffering produces endurance), Rev 13:10 and 14:12 (the endurance called for in persecution), Heb 12:1 (running a race). This distinction maps broadly onto the source file's own editorial note: "Distinguish hupomone (patient endurance under circumstances) from makrothumia (longsuffering patience toward people)." The verse sets confirm this distinction is real and operative throughout the NT corpus.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The testament_coverage field in the JSON meta is "both." All eight Hebrew terms are OT; all four Greek terms are NT.

**What the OT vocabulary does that the NT does not:** The OT vocabulary constructs the patience concept from below, through the physical image of the extended nostril. The etymology is spatial and bodily: patience is length (ARK) applied to anger (APH). The OT also builds patience directly into the divine character formula — arek aph is one of the most repeated self-declarations of God in the canon — and situates it in a covenantal frame where God's patience is the basis of Israel's continued existence. No NT term reproduces this covenantal-national framing. The OT also contributes waiting terms (cha.khah, ya.chal) that frame patience as an inner orientation under uncertainty, particularly in the Psalms. These terms describe an experiential inner state (one waits, one hopes) rather than a character quality. The OT patience vocabulary is thus split between character quality (ARK/APH family) and experiential inner state (CHKH, YCHL).

**What the NT vocabulary does that the OT does not:** The NT vocabulary names patience as a productive inner state, one that generates outcomes. Rom 5:3–4 and Jam 1:3–4 both describe hupomonē as producing character (dokime) and bringing the person to completion (teleios). No OT term in the word set carries this developmental or generative account. The NT also places both primary patience terms (makrothumia as fruit of the Spirit in Gal 5:22, hupomonē in the eschatological endurance passages of Revelation) inside explicitly theological and eschatological structures that have no OT parallel within this word set. makrothumia's appearance as a fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22) raises a question not present in the OT: whether NT patience is understood as something infused from outside (a Spirit-gift) rather than cultivated as a disposition.

**The most significant OT/NT difference observed in the verse sets:** The OT patience vocabulary centres on restraint — specifically the restraint of anger, both human and divine. The Hebrew terms that do the most patience work (a.rekh, arek aph) are constructions about what one does not do: one does not let the nostril flare, one holds length, one defers. The NT patience vocabulary centres on endurance — specifically the sustained maintenance of a state under external pressure. The Greek terms that do the most patience work (hupomonē especially) are constructions about what one continues to do: one remains, one stays under, one bears. The OT patience is primarily restrained reaction; the NT patience is primarily sustained action. Whether this difference is a genuine theological development or a reflection of different situational pressures (covenant violation in the OT, persecution in the NT) is not determinable from the verse records alone.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

Four data quality issues from the JSON affect this analysis.

The compound arek aph ("slow to anger"), listed in the registry header as a STEP term, returned no results in STEP and has no word analysis block in the source file. The patience concept it names is covered by its component terms a.rekh (H0750) and aph (H0639), and the analysis has proceeded on that basis. The absence of a compound-term block means there is no occurrence count or verse set specifically for the compound form. The 12 or so "slow to anger" verses in the aph verse set and the full a.rekh verse set cover the same material, but it is possible that some occurrences of the compound were missed.

aph (H0639) has a verse set of 219 entries against a stated count of 278 occurrences. The verse set is dominated by anger/wrath uses and the patience-relevant subset (the "slow to anger" instances) amounts to approximately 12 of the 219 verses. The analysis of aph's patience-relevant function is based on this sub-set. The majority of the verse set is not analytically relevant to patience and has been noted but not used in the Section 3 comparative analysis.

o.rekh (H0753) has 90 verses against a stated count of 95. The verse set is overwhelmingly composed of physical measurement uses (tabernacle, temple, Ezekiel measurements), with a single patience-relevant verse (Pro 25:15). The analysis treats o.rekh as etymologically significant but not a primary patience term. Any additional patience-relevant o.rekh instances in the five unrecorded verses would not change this assessment but could add nuance.

hupomonē (G5281) has 31 verse records against a stated count of about 32. The single missing verse is minor and not expected to affect the analysis. Rev 13:10 is recorded as one entry covering two source lines.

## Section 6 — Open Questions

1. [ROOT: a.rekh] Does the concentration of a.rekh in the divine-attribute formula ("slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love") indicate that patience in the OT is primarily a theological attribute from which human patience is derived, or does the wisdom tradition's application of the same adjective to human subjects (Pro 14:29, 15:18, 16:32) indicate a parallel anthropological strand?
   *Phase 2 flag: The model will need to decide whether the divine and human patience attributions share a single inner-being function or whether God's arek aph and human arek aph describe structurally different states that merely share a label.*

2. [TERM: aph] The primary patience concept in the OT is built on the somatic term for nostril/anger — patience is literally "long of nostril." Does the suppression of this physical image in English translation affect the inner-being analysis, and is there evidence in other OT texts that the nostril image was understood as a live bodily metaphor rather than a dead one?
   *Phase 2 flag: The body_inner_expression signal requires the model to account for what happens to inner-being analysis when the naming term is a body part. If anger is the nostril and patience is the extension of the nostril, is the nose a somatic site of the patience state or merely a linguistic convention?*

3. [TERM: la.ah] la.ah describes God reaching the limit of patience (Isa 1:14, Jer 15:6: "I am weary of relenting"). Does the OT word set imply that God's patience has a structural limit, and if so, how does this interact with the NT portrait of divine makrothumia as withheld judgment pending universal repentance (2Pe 3:9, 3:15)?
   *Phase 2 flag: The god_as_subject signal on la.ah requires the model to address whether divine weariness and divine patience are opposite poles of a single inner-being capacity or whether they represent different inner states that happen to converge at the limit point.*

4. [TERM: cha.khah] Isa 30:18 applies cha.khah to God: "The Lord waits to be gracious to you." Does God's waiting constitute a form of patience in the same inner-being sense as human waiting, and what is the inner-being character of waiting that is itself a form of care?
   *Phase 2 flag: The god_as_subject signal on cha.khah requires the model to assess whether waiting-for can function as an inner-being state in God, and whether it is structurally different from the restraint model (arek aph) or the endurance model (HUPO) present elsewhere in the word set.*

5. [TERM: a.rakh] In Isa 48:9, a.rakh is used causatively: God "defers" his anger, acting upon his own inner state to prolong its restraint. Does this constitute an inner-being causative action (one party causing a change in their own inner state) or does the causative apply only between different persons?
   *Phase 2 flag: The causative_form_present signal on a.rakh raises a model-architecture question: whether the inner-being model permits self-causation (one's own inner state being deliberately modified by one's own action) as distinct from other-causation.*

6. [TERM: da.mam] The Hiphil of da.mam means "to make silent (cause to die)." The same root covers deliberate inner stillness (Psa 131:2), waiting in God (Psa 62:5), and death-producing silence. Does da.mam's inner-being register depend entirely on context, or is there a structural link between the stillness required for patience and the silencing associated with death or devastation?
   *Phase 2 flag: The causative_form_present signal on da.mam raises the question of whether the model should treat da.mam as a single inner-being term with a wide range or as two functionally distinct uses that share a root without sharing an inner-being function.*

7. [OT/NT] The OT patience vocabulary centres on restrained reaction (not letting the nostril flare); the NT patience vocabulary centres on sustained endurance (remaining under pressure). Does this difference indicate that the two Testaments are describing the same inner-being state seen from different angles, or are they describing two functionally different inner states that English and Greek tradition have collapsed under the single label "patience"?

8. [TERM: makrothumeō] In Luk 18:7, makrothumeō describes a possible divine delay in giving justice to his elect — a use where patience may function against the interests of those waiting. Does the god_as_subject instance of makrothumeō in Luk 18:7 represent the same inner-being state as the relational patience commanded to humans elsewhere, or does divine patience-as-delay constitute a structurally different state?
   *Phase 2 flag: The god_as_subject signal on makrothumeō requires the model to determine whether divine patience in the verb form (delaying action) is the same inner state as human patience in the verb form (forbearing toward a person), or whether the same term covers two functionally distinct states that happen to share the same name.*

10. [OT/NT] makrothumia appears as a fruit of the Spirit in Gal 5:22. Is NT patience understood in that verse as a state produced by the Spirit working in the person, and if so, does this change the inner-being model's account of how patience is acquired or sustained in comparison to the OT wisdom tradition's cultivation model (Pro 14:29, 16:32)?

11. [OT/NT] The HUPO family (hupomonē, hupomenō) consistently associates patience with suffering, persecution, and trial in its NT verse set (Rom 5:3–4, Jam 1:3–4, Rev 13:10, 14:12, Heb 12:1–3, 1Pe 2:20). Is this situational concentration unique to the NT, and does the OT word set show any comparable cluster of patience terms in suffering contexts, or does the OT situate patience primarily in the everyday relational sphere (arek aph) and the Psalmic interior (ya.chal)?
