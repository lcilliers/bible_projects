## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** grief | **Registry ID:** 071 | **Source list:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-08 | **Version:** v5

This assessment covers the ten terms in Part 2 only. The testament_coverage for this part is OT_only. On the basis of the verse records in this file, grief provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word with high confidence across most of Part 2's terms. The KAV family (ke.ev, makh.ov, ka.av) provides the clearest inner-being evidence: the meaning blocks for ke.ev and ka.av explicitly name both mental and physical pain as sub-meanings, and the verse records for makh.ov place it consistently in contexts where a person's own suffering is the referent. ## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### ATSAV — a.tsav (H6087A) and ats.tse.vet (H6094)

The ATSAV root carries a core image of physical or inner pain that has been shaped or imposed — the related word cluster includes both e.tsev (toil/pain), o.tsev (pain/idol), and also a.tsav in the sense of shaping or fashioning something. This etymology suggests the root covers the experience of being worked upon, pressed, or pained — whether through labour or through inner distress. The two terms in this part divide along verb/noun lines with different signal feature profiles.

**a.tsav (H6087A)** is the verb, with approximately 16 occurrences and 15 verse records — a representative sample. The verb covers a wide range: being grieved or distressed inwardly (Gen 45:5; 1Sa 20:3; Neh 8:10–11), being caused pain or displeasure (1Ki 1:6), being hurt by an action (Ecc 10:9), and grieving another person. The meaning block lists Qal (to hurt, pain), Niphal (to be pained, grieved), Piel (to vex, torture), Hiphil (to cause pain), and Hithpael (to feel grieved, be vexed).

The most theologically significant verse in the entire Part 2 set is Isa 63:10: "they rebelled and grieved his Holy Spirit; therefore he turned to be their enemy." Here a.tsav in the Hiphil appears with the Holy Spirit as the one who is grieved — the only verse in Part 2 where a grief term is applied to the Holy Spirit as the one who receives the grief. This is flagged in the cross-registry links and is the most notable god_as_subject instance in Part 2, though technically the Spirit is the object here rather than the agent.

Other god_as_subject instances for a.tsav show humans as the agent: Psa 78:40 — "how often they grieved him in the desert" — with Israel's rebellion as the cause. Gen 6:6 uses the closely related emotion of God's regret ("it grieved him to his heart") — an instance where the grief-adjacent state is attributed to God himself, not merely caused to God by humans. The phrase "to his heart" in Gen 6:6 places the inner state in a specific inner faculty.

The causative form (Hiphil) is present and functions in two directions: humans grieving God (Isa 63:10; Psa 78:40), and humans being instructed not to be grieved (Neh 8:10–11, though these are Niphal passives). The verse set for a.tsav shows the emotion operating interpersonally — between David and Jonathan (1Sa 20:3; 1Sa 20:34), between David and the people (2Sa 19:2), between Israel and God (Psa 78:40; Isa 63:10).

The anger-adjacent uses are real: Gen 34:7 shows the sons of Jacob being "indignant and very angry" where a.tsav contributes to the anger field; 1Sa 20:34 shows Jonathan "in fierce anger" while also "grieved for David" — a verse where grief and anger are simultaneous. This places a.tsav at the boundary of the grief and anger word sets, consistent with the cross-registry flag for that connection.

**ats.tse.vet (H6094)** is the noun for pain, hurt, injury, sorrow, or wound, with approximately 5 occurrences and 5 verse records. The meaning block — "pain, hurt, injury, sorrow, wound" — covers both physical and inner registers without distinguishing them by sub-definition. The verse records show ats.tse.vet functioning across those registers: Job 9:28 refers to all the speaker's suffering as ats.tse.vet; Psa 16:4 names the sorrows of those who run after other gods; Psa 147:3 uses the image of God binding up the wounds/sorrows of the brokenhearted; Pro 15:13 states that by "sorrow of heart the spirit is crushed," which is the most explicit inner-being usage in the sample.

The somatic_link flag is true for ats.tse.vet and the god_as_subject flag is true. Psa 147:3 shows God as the one who heals the inner pain — "he heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds (ats.tse.vet)." The image here treats inner sorrow as something that can be bound up like a physical wound. Pro 10:10, the remaining verse, uses the term in a context of trouble caused by another person's winking of the eye.

**Verdict within ATSAV:** a.tsav and ats.tse.vet are doing differentiated work. a.tsav is the active, relational verb: grief that is felt, caused, and exchanged between persons, including between persons and God. ats.tse.vet is the noun of the resulting state or wound — the pain as a condition that can be accumulated, named, and healed. The two terms are not consolidation candidates; they occupy the expected verb/noun distribution of the same root. The anger-boundary of a.tsav is a genuine complication requiring Phase 2 attention.

### YGN — ya.gon (H3015)

ya.gon is a standalone noun in Part 2 — its root family label is YGN, distinguishing it from the YGH family (tu.gah, ya.gah) despite the shared consonantal root. The related word list connects ya.gon to ya.gah (to suffer, grieve) and tu.gah (grief, heaviness), so the families share etymology but are treated separately in the source data.

ya.gon means grief, sorrow, or anguish. With approximately 14 occurrences and 14 verse records, it has effectively complete coverage of its occurrence set — the most fully represented term in Part 2. This makes its verse distribution a reliable indicator of how the term functions.

The verse records cluster around several distinctive patterns. First, ya.gon appears frequently in contexts of reversal: Est 9:22 contrasts sorrow turned into gladness; Isa 35:10 and 51:11 (parallel verses) describe the ransomed of the Lord returning to Zion with joy while "sorrow and sighing shall flee away." Jer 31:13 announces that God "will give them gladness for sorrow." This pattern — sorrow as the baseline condition that is explicitly replaced by joy — is more consistent across ya.gon's verse set than in any other term in Part 2.

Second, ya.gon appears in self-locating laments. Psa 13:2 — "how long must I have sorrow in my heart all the day" — places ya.gon explicitly in the heart. Jer 8:18 — "grief is upon me; my heart is sick within me" — pairs ya.gon with heart-sickness, locating the sorrow in the inner person. Jer 45:3 — "the Lord has added sorrow to my pain" — presents ya.gon as something that can be added to existing pain, giving it a discrete and measurable quality.

Third, the god_as_subject flag is true. The pattern in Isa 35:10, 51:11, and Jer 31:13 involves God as the agent who removes or replaces sorrow — not as the one who experiences it, but as the one who acts upon it in the lives of others. The somatic_link flag is true: Psa 31:10 — "my life is spent with sorrow, and my years with sighing; my strength fails because of my iniquity, and my bones waste away" — shows ya.gon directly connected to physical diminishment (strength failing, bones wasting).

**Verdict:** ya.gon is a well-defined inner-being term for sorrow as a sustained, named condition — one that can be located in the heart, that consumes life and strength over time, and that is explicitly positioned as the state that God's redemptive action replaces. Its complete verse coverage gives this verdict confidence. There is no consolidation candidate for ya.gon in Part 2; its closest partners (tu.gah, ya.gah) belong to the YGH family and are assessed separately.

### YGH — tu.gah (H8424) and ya.gah (H3013)

The YGH family groups two terms that share root consonants with ya.gon but are assigned separate root family labels in the JSON. The related words for both tu.gah and ya.gah list ya.gon alongside each other, confirming the etymological connection across all three terms.

**tu.gah (H8424)** is the noun, meaning grief, heaviness, or sorrow. With approximately 4 occurrences and 3 verse records, it has very small data — 75% coverage of a small set. The somatic_link flag is true. The three verses show tu.gah functioning in two distinct contexts. Psa 119:28 — "my soul melts away for sorrow; strengthen me according to your word" — is the most direct inner-being use: sorrow causes the soul itself to melt, and the person appeals to God's word as the counteragent. Pro 10:1 and 17:21 both present tu.gah as the sorrow of a parent caused by a foolish child — a relational use where one person's behaviour produces grief in another.

The image of the soul melting for sorrow (Psa 119:28) is the most distinctive feature of tu.gah in this small sample. The verb "melt" (davah or equivalent in that context) applied to the soul points to a liquefying, dissolving image of inner grief — grief that undermines the inner person's stability.

**ya.gah (H3013)** is the verb, meaning to afflict, grieve, suffer, or cause grief, with approximately 8 occurrences and 8 verse records — complete coverage of the stated count. The meaning block lists Niphal (grieved, grieving as participle), Piel (to grieve), and Hiphil (to cause grief, cause sorrow), making ya.gah one of the causative-form-present terms in Part 2. The god_as_subject flag is true.

The verse records place ya.gah primarily in lament and judgment contexts. Lam 1:12 is the most striking: "Is it nothing to you, all you who pass by? Look and see if there is any sorrow like my sorrow, which was brought upon me, which the Lord inflicted on the day of his fierce anger." Here the Hiphil of ya.gah is used with God as subject — "the Lord inflicted" the sorrow — making this a clear causative instance where God imposes grief. Lam 3:32–33 then complicates this: "though he cause grief, he will have compassion according to the abundance of his steadfast love; for he does not afflict from his heart or grieve the children of men." This pair of verses presents God as the one who causes grief (ya.gah, Hiphil) but not from his own desire — the grief-causing is presented as purposive and compassionate rather than arbitrary.

Lam 1:4–5 shows ya.gah used of persons under judgment — "her virgins have been afflicted, and she herself suffers bitterly" and "the Lord has afflicted her for the multitude of her transgressions." The agent is again God, the object is Jerusalem, and the mechanism is covenant-judgment. Job 19:2 — "how long will you torment me and break me in pieces with words?" — shows ya.gah used by a human agent (Job's friends) as the source of grief, one of the few non-divine causative instances in the sample.

**Verdict within YGH:** tu.gah and ya.gah function as noun and verb of a grief family with a particular character: sorrow that dissolves or unmakes the inner person (tu.gah, Psa 119:28) and grief that is caused upon a person by an external agent — God in judgment, enemies in words (ya.gah). The two terms are not consolidation candidates; they occupy distinct grammatical and functional roles. The causative dimension of ya.gah — especially the Lamentations cluster where God causes grief as an act of covenant judgment — is a significant theological feature requiring Phase 2 attention. Relationship to ya.gon across the YGN/YGH boundary is a cross-family question that also requires investigation.

### KAV — ke.ev (H3511), makh.ov (H4341), and ka.av (H3510)

The KAV family is the most developed in Part 2, containing a noun (ke.ev), a compound-noun/abstract (makh.ov), and a verb (ka.av). All three share related words with each other — the KAV cluster is one of the tightest root families in the grief word set. The meaning ranges across the family consistently name both mental and physical dimensions of pain.

**ke.ev (H3511)** is the noun for pain (mental and physical) or sorrow, with approximately 6 occurrences and 6 verse records — complete coverage. The meaning block does not separate physical and mental pain into sub-definitions, presenting them as a single undivided referent. The somatic_link flag is true. The verse records show ke.ev functioning in three ways: as the speaker's own pain that will not be assuaged (Job 16:6), as the visible suffering of another recognised in silence (Job 2:13 — seven days' silence at the sight of Job's ke.ev), as distress that grows worse when suppressed (Psa 39:2), as the grief of heart and breaking of spirit (Isa 65:14 — "cry out for pain of heart and wail for breaking of spirit"), and as unceasing pain in a lament addressed to God (Jer 15:18).

The cluster of ke.ev in Job (2:13; 16:6) is notable: in both cases the pain is seen and witnessed but not resolved. Job 2:13 describes observers sitting silently in the face of pain so great that nothing can be said. This is ke.ev as witnessed suffering — pain that others acknowledge but cannot address. Isa 65:14 places ke.ev in the heart ("pain of heart"), making it explicitly inner.

**makh.ov (H4341)** is a noun for pain or sorrow with physical and mental sub-definitions explicitly distinguished in the meaning block: "1a) pain (physical); 1b) pain (mental)." With approximately 16 occurrences and 9 verse records, it is at about 56% coverage — a moderate sample. The god_as_subject and somatic_link flags are both true. The verse records show makh.ov in theologically prominent contexts. Exo 3:7 — "I know their sufferings (makh.ov)" — places God as the one who sees and knows the pain, paralleling the o.ni pattern from Part 1. 2Ch 6:29 — "each knowing his own affliction and his own sorrow" — presents makh.ov as individually known and personally owned by the sufferer. Psa 69:26 — "they recount the pain of those you have struck down" — shows makh.ov applied to those who are struck by God. Jer 30:15 — "your pain is incurable" — presents makh.ov as permanent and beyond healing.

The pairing of makh.ov with God knowing it (Exo 3:7) and God striking those who bear it (Psa 69:26) creates a pattern in which this term for pain has a consistently theological framing: the pain is known to God and often inflicted or permitted by God. Lam 1:18 — "see my suffering" — presents makh.ov as visible and appeal-worthy, parallel to o.ni from Part 1.

**ka.av (H3510)** is the verb for being in pain (physical and mental), being sorrowful, and — in the Hiphil — causing pain, hurting, or marring. It has approximately 8 occurrences and 8 verse records — complete coverage. All three signal features are true: god_as_subject, somatic_link, and causative_form_present. The Qal meaning block explicitly subdivides into "to be in pain (physical)" and "to be in pain (mental)," making ka.av one of the few terms in the grief word set where the source data itself articulates the physical/mental distinction within the same verb.

The verse records for ka.av cover several registers. Gen 34:25 uses ka.av for the bodily pain of circumcision — unambiguously physical. Job 14:22 — "he feels only the pain of his own body, and he mourns only for himself" — pairs ka.av with mourning, and places pain in "his own body" as the immediate referent. Pro 14:13 — "even in laughter the heart may ache, and the end of joy may be grief" — places ka.av in the heart, naming the inner ache that persists beneath surface joy. Job 5:18 — "he wounds, but he binds up; he shatters, but his hands heal" — presents God in both the causing and the resolving of pain, making ka.av part of a generation-resolution pair for the same agent.

Eze 13:22 introduces a causative use with a human agent: God states "I have not grieved him" (ka.av, negative Hiphil), distinguishing what God has caused from what false prophets have done. Eze 28:24 uses the Hiphil in the sense of a brier hurting Israel — a physical causative in a metaphorical frame.

**Verdict within KAV:** The three terms are doing distinct work within the same root. ke.ev is the noun of pain as it is witnessed — the suffering that others see and sit silently with. makh.ov is the noun of pain as God knows it and as the sufferer personally owns it — a more theological and covenantal framing. ka.av is the verb of pain both experienced and caused — the active dimension of pain as event, whether in the body or in the heart. All three are genuine inner-being terms. None is a consolidation candidate for the others. The generation-resolution pair in Job 5:18 (God wounds and heals using the same root) and the explicit heart/body distinction in Pro 14:13 and Job 14:22 are significant Phase 2 signals.

### KAS — ka.a.s (H3708B) and ka.as (H3708A)

The KAS family presents the most complex analytical situation in Part 2. Both terms are listed with identical occurrence counts (approximately 26 each), near-identical related word lists, and the source data explicitly cross-references them: H3708A notes "also spelled ka.a.s (H3708B)" and vice versa. The data_quality_flags mark both as CONSOLIDATION_CANDIDATE. Despite this, they are retained as separate entries per the source classification and are analysed accordingly here, with the consolidation question flagged for Phase 2.

**ka.a.s (H3708B)** is glossed as vexation and carries meanings of anger, vexation, provocation, and grief. The god_as_subject and somatic_link flags are true. The verse sample is only 4 records against 26 occurrences — approximately 15%, the thinnest coverage in Part 2. From the four verses: Job 5:2 presents vexation as something that kills — "vexation kills the fool." Job 6:2 treats ka.a.s as a measurable weight — "oh that my vexation were weighed." Job 10:17 — "you increase your vexation toward me" — places God as the agent increasing the vexation directed at Job. Job 17:7 — "my eye has grown dim from vexation, and all my members are like a shadow" — is the somatic_link evidence: grief/vexation causing physical deterioration of the eye and the body.

**ka.as (H3708A)** is also glossed as vexation and carries meanings of anger and vexation. The god_as_subject and somatic_link flags are true. With 21 verse records against 26 occurrences, its coverage at approximately 81% is representative. The verse records show ka.as operating across three identifiable registers.

First, provocation of divine anger: Deu 32:19; 1Ki 15:30; 1Ki 21:22; 2Ki 23:26; Eze 20:28 — all describing Israel's provocations that kindled God's wrath. In these verses, ka.as functions as the noun of provocation-that-causes-anger, with God as the one who is provoked.

Second, personal grief and inner distress: 1Sa 1:16 — Hannah's "great anxiety and vexation"; Psa 6:7 — "my eye wastes away because of grief"; Psa 31:9 — "my eye is wasted from grief; my soul and my body also"; Pro 17:25 — "a foolish son is a grief to his father." In these verses, ka.as names an inner condition of distress that has visible physical effects (wasting eye) and that is located in "my soul and my body."

Third, wisdom contexts in Ecclesiastes where ka.as describes the vexation or sorrow that comes with knowledge and toil: Ecc 1:18; 2:23; 5:17; 7:3; 11:10. Ecc 7:3 — "sorrow is better than laughter, for by sadness of face the heart is made glad" — is the most distinctive: here ka.as (sorrow/sadness) is presented as having a positive inner effect on the heart.

The somatic_link evidence for ka.as is concentrated in the Psalms verses: Psa 6:7 and Psa 31:9 both show the eye wasting from grief, and Psa 31:9 explicitly extends to "my soul and my body also" — one of the clearest statements in Part 2 that an inner state is simultaneously affecting both.

**Verdict within KAS:** The CONSOLIDATION_CANDIDATE flag is well-founded. The verse sets for ka.a.s and ka.as appear to share the same semantic territory — inner distress as vexation, grief, and provocation — with ka.a.s having too few verses to assess independently. Whether they should be treated as two entries for the same lexeme or as two genuinely distinct forms with different distributional histories is a question the 15% coverage of ka.a.s cannot resolve. The analytical priority here is to note that both terms occupy the boundary between grief and anger in a way that is distinctive within Part 2: ka.as specifically names a state that can be directed toward God as provocation and that can also be a personal inner grief state, and the verse records support both uses without apparent contradiction. The Ecclesiastes wisdom use of ka.as — where sorrow itself can benefit the heart — is a notable sub-register not present in any other term in this part.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-Terms Do Different Work

Three root families in Part 2 contain two or more terms where the verse sets reveal meaningful differences.

**ATSAV family: a.tsav vs. ats.tse.vet.** a.tsav is the active verb of grieving and being grieved — it operates interpersonally (humans grieving each other, humans grieving God's Spirit) and shows anger-adjacent uses. ats.tse.vet is the noun of the wound or pain that results — a condition that accumulates, can be healed by God, and can crush the spirit. The difference is not merely grammatical: a.tsav clusters around the action of grieving in relational contexts, while ats.tse.vet appears in contexts of existing suffering whose remedy is in view. Both are genuine inner-being terms doing different work.

**YGH family: tu.gah vs. ya.gah.** tu.gah is the noun of sorrow as a condition — in particular the dissolving, unmanning image of Psa 119:28 where the soul melts for sorrow. ya.gah is the verb of grief as something caused or inflicted, prominently by God in covenant-judgment (Lam 1:5; 1:12; 3:32–33). The difference is between sorrow as a state felt by the person and grief as an event caused upon the person. These differences are genuine and are not labelling distinctions.

**KAV family: ke.ev vs. makh.ov vs. ka.av.** The three terms distinguish themselves along three axes. ke.ev is pain as it appears to observers — suffering so great that others sit in silence. makh.ov is pain as it is known to God and owned by the individual — a more covenantal and theological framing. ka.av is pain as active event — the aching of the heart in a specific moment, the body being hurt, and the agent who causes or resolves the pain. These distinctions are genuine, though the relatively small verse samples for ke.ev (6) and ka.av (8) limit confidence in the verdict. The fact that ka.av explicitly carries both physical and mental sub-definitions in its meaning block, while ke.ev and makh.ov present pain without that formal distinction, is a genuine lexical difference regardless of verse sample size.

**KAS family: ka.a.s vs. ka.as.** From the available verse records, no meaningful functional difference between ka.a.s and ka.as has been established. The four verses for ka.a.s are insufficient to compare against ka.as's 21-verse set. The apparent difference is a data artefact rather than a proven semantic distinction. The question of whether these are genuinely two distinct terms or a single lexeme with two Strong's labels remains open and is the primary consolidation decision for this family.
