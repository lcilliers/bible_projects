# WA-002 — agony — Phase 1 Analysis

**Word:** agony  
**Registry ID:** 002  
**Source List:** Missing Inner Being Words  
**Category:** Affective/Emotional  
**Date:** 2026-03-06  
**File Version:** 2  

This word qualifies under the inner being definition. agonia (G0074) names an intense interior struggle or anguish as its sole NT sense (Luke 22:44, Gethsemane), making it the most direct inner-being term in the cluster. The Hebrew chul (H2342I) and its nominal derivatives chi.lah/chil (H2427B/A) designate writhing anguish experienced by persons — an experiential state that is simultaneously somatic and inner-being, inseparable in the Hebrew frame. odunaō (G3600) covers both bodily and mental torment and is used of grief, distress, and anguish in distinctly inner-being contexts. The word cluster as a whole reveals a consistent pattern: agony in the biblical vocabulary is named through the somatic — writhing, twisting, birth-pangs, the touchstone's test — and this somatic vocabulary consistently names inner experience, not merely bodily sensation. The terms qualify because they name states that are experienced by the inner person, not only conditions observed on the body.

## Term-by-Term Analysis

### agonia — a struggle — G0074 — Greek

**What this term means**

agonia is a feminine noun occurring once in the NT. Its primary classical meaning is athletic contest or struggle for victory, then gymnastic exercise, then — by LSJ's third sub-definition, with clear attestation from the fourth century BC onward — inner agony or anguish of the mind, particularly in contexts of fear (en phobō kai pollē agonia — in fear and great agony). The NT meaning (agony, anguish, anxiety) represents the inner-being end of this semantic trajectory. The related words — agōn (fight), agōnizomai (to struggle), and its prefixed compounds (to struggle against, contend, conquer, struggle alongside) — confirm that the root is fundamentally about contest, striving, and exertion, and that agonia names the experienced inner dimension of that struggle at its most intense.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Dative Singular Feminine. The dative case indicates agonia is the condition in which the subject exists — 'being in agony' (en agonia). This is the dative of state or circumstance, situating agonia as an ongoing interior condition at the moment of Luke 22:44, not a momentary sensation but a sustained condition of the person during prayer.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'agony' is a direct borrowing from the Greek and so carries the word's full semantic weight in this context better than most English renderings of biblical terms. However, English 'agony' has largely lost the contest-struggle dimension of the Greek root. For Luke 22:44, the contest sense is not merely etymological background — it is active: the Gethsemane scene is explicitly a struggle, and agonia names both the inner anguish and its character as a contest being waged. English 'agony' does not surface this.

**Core verse observations**

Luke 22:44 is the only NT occurrence and the verse is remarkable for its density of inner-being content. Being in agonia, Jesus prays more earnestly — the agony intensifies rather than interrupts the inner orientation of prayer. The verse then notes that his sweat became like great drops of blood falling to the ground. This somatic sign (whether literal or metaphorical in Luke's account) functions as the external register of an interior condition named by agonia. The verse locates the highest-intensity use of this term in the context of a human-divine encounter, where the person's inner life is under extreme strain. The causal structure — being in agonia, therefore praying more earnestly — shows agonia as a condition that drives rather than suppresses inner-being activity.

### chi.lah — agony — H2427B — Hebrew

**What this term means**

chi.lah is a feminine noun meaning pain, agony, sorrow, writhing, or anguish. Its sub-definitions name two dimensions: writhing of fear and anguish. It is the feminine nominal form of the chul root, paired with the masculine chil (H2427A). The related words cluster shows the same root covers dance (ma.chol, me.cho.lah), sand (chol), and structural-military terms (chel, che.lah, rampart and bulwark) alongside anguish (chal.cha.lah) — the chul root's semantic range across motion, physical formation, and emotional writhing is one of the most striking root profiles in this cluster.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular feminine absolute. The feminine form (chi.lah) may carry a slightly more intense or experiential quality than the masculine chil in its specific usage contexts, though this cannot be established from the single provided verse. The absolute state indicates a standalone nominal use rather than a construct chain.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'agony' or 'pain' renders the overall sense but entirely conceals the writhing-motion that is embedded in the root. chi.lah names a pain that is expressed through physical twisting and writhing — the somatic expression is built into the word itself, not added by context. The same root that means to dance (whirling, twisting in joy) means to writhe in agony (twisting in pain): the physical motion is the same; the inner state is opposite. English cannot render this within a single word.

**Core verse observations**

Job 6:10 is the sole provided verse. Job states that this would be my comfort — he would even exult in pain unsparing, because he has not denied the words of the Holy One. This is a striking inner-being verse: chi.lah (pain, writhing) is presented not merely as a condition to be endured but as a paradoxical source of comfort and exultation when held alongside fidelity. The inner-being significance is not the agony itself but the orientation of the person within it — the refusal to deny, the finding of comfort in unsparing pain. This verse shows chi.lah as a condition that tests and reveals the inner person's alignment with God's word.

### chil — agony — H2427A — Hebrew

**What this term means**

chil is the masculine nominal form paired with chi.lah (H2427B), with identical meaning (pain, agony, sorrow, writhing, anguish; writhing of fear; anguish) and the same occurrence count (7). The source file notes them as alternate forms. The shared related-words cluster is identical to chi.lah. chil's verse set is more extensive in the provided data and reveals the dominant contextual register for this root: agony as of a woman in labour, used to describe national or communal inner anguish upon hearing catastrophic news.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular masculine absolute. As a standalone masculine noun, chil functions in the verse set consistently as the subject of verbs of seizing or taking hold — anguish seized them, pangs seized her — placing it as an active, invading force that overtakes the person rather than a passive condition.

**What the English translation conceals**

The birth-labour comparison is the dominant English rendering vehicle for chil in the verse set, but this partially conceals the fact that chil is not a birth-specific term. It names the writhing anguish itself; the woman-in-labour comparison is a simile applied to the term, not a definitional feature of it. English readers reading 'pain as of a woman in labor' may associate the term exclusively with birth, whereas chil names the quality of the anguish (writhing, seizing) that birth-labour exemplifies.

**Core verse observations**

Psa 48:6 is the clearest individual inner-being verse: trembling took hold of them, anguish (chil) as of a woman in labour. The anguish arises in response to seeing the power and presence of God — it is a response of the inner person to a divine encounter, the same structure as agonia in Luke 22:44.

Jer 6:24 shows hands falling helpless alongside chil taking hold — the physical posture (helpless hands) externalises the inner anguish, parallel to Luke 22:44's somatic sign.

Jer 50:43 applies the same structure to the king of Babylon — hearing a report, hands falling helpless, chil seizing him as pain of a woman in labour. This shows chil as an inner-being response to information received — the anguish is triggered by hearing, not by physical injury.

Mic 4:9 is pointed: 'Now why do you cry aloud? Is there no king in you? Has your counselor perished, that pain seized you like a woman in labor?' The question challenges the inner source of the agony — the king and counselor are inner-being capacities (leadership, wisdom) whose failure or absence produces the agony.

### sha.vats — agony — H7661 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

sha.vats is a masculine noun meaning cramp, agony, or anguish, with the meaning explicitly stated as uncertain in sub-definition 1a. It occurs once in the biblical text. Its related words are strikingly incongruous with its meaning: mish.be.tsot (filigree, ornamental gold work), sha.vats the verb (to weave or embroider), and tash.bets (checkered or embroidered pattern). The semantic connection between the noun sha.vats (cramp/agony) and the verb sha.vats (to weave) is not explained in the source file and cannot be resolved from the data alone.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular masculine absolute. The STEP anchor-token artifacts (me, the) preceding the heading indicate the anchor occurrence appeared in a possessive or definite construct; the meaning section confirms this is not a factor in the term's semantic content.

**What the English translation conceals**

The single English translation ('anguish has seized me') renders the term adequately for its context. The uncertainty in the meaning section means English may be translating by context rather than by lexical definition. The weaving/cramp root connection — if genuine — would suggest sha.vats names a kind of cramping or contraction analogous to the tightening of woven threads, which would give the term a somatic-physical specificity English 'anguish' does not convey.

**Core verse observations**

2Sa 1:9 shows sha.vats in its only occurrence: the mortally wounded Amalekite servant of Saul asks to be killed because anguish has seized him yet his life still lingers. The term names a condition between living and dying — the anguish (cramp/agony) that makes continued life unbearable. This is an extreme inner-being verse in the sense that sha.vats names a state of torment so intense that it generates the will to die. The inner-being dimension is the orientation of the will toward death as preferable to the agony of continued existence.

### odunaō — be anguished — G3600 — Greek

**What this term means**

odunaō is a verb meaning to grieve, be anxious, be in agony, or be anguished; in the passive: to be in agony, be tormented, or be distressed. The meaning section explicitly covers both bodily and mental pain — making it the most clearly dual-register term in the Greek set. The related noun odunē (anguish) is its only related word in the source file. The LSJ confirms the active sense (to cause pain or suffering, where 'being corrected in one's judgement causes pain') alongside the passive sense of experiencing pain. The anchor form is Present Middle Indicative 2nd Singular — addressing a single person who is currently in the state of being anguished.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The Present Middle Indicative names a current, ongoing state from the subject's own perspective. The 2nd singular addresses the subject directly — 'you are in anguish' — which in Luke 16:24-25 is Abraham's assessment of the rich man's condition. The middle voice nuance suggests the subject is both experiencing and, in some sense, in the grip of the anguish as a condition native to or involving the self.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'distressed', 'in anguish', and 'sorrowful' across the four verses distribute what Greek uses a single verbal root to cover. The English translations fail to show that the same inner-being state — odunaō — is present in Mary's anxious grief over the lost Jesus (Luk 2:48), the rich man's torment in Hades (Luk 16:24-25), and the disciples' sorrow at Paul's farewell (Act 20:38). English conceals the continuity across these registers.

**Core verse observations**

Luk 2:48 shows odunaō as the word for Mary's parental anguish in searching for the lost Jesus — an inner-being state of distress arising from relational loss and fear.

Luk 16:24 and 16:25 show odunaō as the condition of the rich man in Hades — in anguish in the flame, contrasted with Lazarus being comforted. The explicit contrast between anguish and comfort is itself an inner-being structure: both are states of the person rather than external conditions.

Act 20:38 shows odunaō as the grief of the Ephesian elders at Paul's farewell — a relational inner pain arising from the prospect of permanent separation. This is the most purely psychological-relational use of the term in the set.

### odin — labor — G5604 — Greek

**What this term means**

odin is a feminine noun meaning birth pang or spasm of travail; plural: the birth throes used metaphorically for catastrophic preliminary events before a catastrophe (eschatological register). The meaning section also notes an extended meaning derived from Hebrew: a stringent band, snare, or noose — applying to the phrase ōdines thanatou (pangs of death / cords of death) in Acts 2:24, where the LXX translator appears to have confused Hebrew hebel ('pang') with hebel ('cord'). The LSJ confirms the primary sense as the physical pangs of childbirth, extended to metaphorical travail of the mind, and used in the genitive of the fruit of the mind's travail (the product of intellectual labour).

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Genitive Plural Feminine. The genitive plural indicates odin is a partitive or qualifying noun ('birth pains of') rather than the primary subject in its anchor occurrence. This suggests the term typically qualifies another noun — the beginning of birth pains, the bonds of death — rather than standing alone as the named experience.

**What the English translation conceals**

The rendering 'birth pains' or 'labor pains' adequately translates the primary sense but loses the metaphorical range. The Acts 2:24 instance is particularly concealed: English 'pangs of death' reads as emotional-experiential, whereas the meaning note reveals the Hebrew-LXX confusion between pang and cord — making 'loosing the pangs/cords of death' a physical-binding image as much as an experiential one.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Matt 24:8 and Mark 13:8 use odin in an eschatological-metaphorical register, describing preliminary catastrophes before the end as 'the beginning of birth pains.' The inner-being dimension of the agony is present but indirect — the term is a simile vehicle for the character of events rather than a name for the inner state itself.

1Th 5:3 extends the birth-pang simile to the suddenness and inescapability of eschatological destruction — again using the agony of birth as a figure for something other than inner experience.

Act 2:24 is classified ambiguous because the Hebrew-LXX meaning shift between pang and cord makes the referent uncertain from the English text and the meaning note alone.

### che.vel — pain — H2256B — Hebrew

**What this term means**

che.vel is a masculine noun (9 occurrences) meaning pain, sorrow, travail, or pang; sub-definitions: pains of travail and pains/pangs/sorrows. Its related words reveal a remarkable root polysemy: the same root covers the verb cha.val in three distinct senses (to pledge, to destroy, to be in labour), the nouns cho.vel (pilot) and chib.bel (mast), che.vel as destruction and as cord, and tach.bu.lah (counsel). The co-presence of cord, travail-pain, destruction, and pilot/mast under one root suggests the root may originally have designated binding, tension, or the pulling-tight of a rope, from which both pain-of-contraction (birth pang) and cord-binding derive.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common plural masculine absolute. The plural form is the standard form for this term in its pain/pang sense — it typically names pangs as a category rather than a single pang, consistent with its verse set showing repeated or intensifying pain experiences.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'pangs', 'pains', and 'sorrows' used across the verse set do not surface the cord-tension root that may underlie the term. If the root does derive from the image of tightening or binding, then che.vel names pain as a gripping or contracting force — a pain that seizes and holds — which English 'pang' partially preserves (pang from prong, something that pierces) but does not capture completely.

**Core verse observations**

Job 21:17 shows God distributing che.vel (pains) in his anger — divine agency in the dispensing of inner-being anguish, locating the source of the pain in divine moral response to wickedness.

Jer 13:21 applies che.vel to Judah: pangs will take hold of you like those of a woman in labour — the familiar birth-pang simile applied to an inner state of anticipated devastation at the prospect of being ruled by those one has taught to be enemies.

Jer 49:24 shows Damascus: anguish and sorrows (che.vel) have taken hold of her as of a woman in labour. Panic, anguish, and sorrows cluster together — che.vel is one item in a sequence of inner-being states triggered by a single crisis.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Hos 13:13 is ambiguous: birth pangs come for a son who is described as unwise — at the right time he does not present himself at the opening of the womb. The agony imagery is applied to a spiritual-moral failure to respond at the critical moment. Whether che.vel names the inner agony of the crisis or the external birth circumstance is genuinely ambiguous.

### gi.ach — to burst/come out — H1518 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

gi.ach is a verb (7 occurrences, 5 provided) meaning to burst forth, draw forth, or bring forth (Qal) and to break forth (Hiphil). An Aramaic cognate means 'to strive'. Its related words are proper nouns (Gihon, Giah) — rivers or places named for the bursting-forth of water. The term's inclusion in the agony cluster reflects the birth-imagery connection: the bursting-forth of the child from the womb is an image of the same violent emergence that agony drives toward. gi.ach itself does not name an inner-being state; it names the action of violent emergence that birth-agony produces.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb qal infinitive construct, naming the action in its bare verbal form without person or tense assignment. This is consistent with the term's role as a process-descriptor — the bursting-forth itself — rather than an attributed experience.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'to burst forth' adequately captures the primary sense. The Aramaic cognate 'to strive' is not surfaced in English and adds a nuance of active exertion that connects gi.ach more closely to agonia's contest-struggle root.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Job 38:8 applies gi.ach to the sea bursting from the womb at creation — a cosmic-creative register where the birth-bursting image is applied to the origin of the sea itself. This amplifies the birth-agony connection in the cluster's semantic background.

Psa 22:9 applies gi.ach to God drawing the psalmist from the womb — a reference to birth used to ground trust established at the moment of emergence. The inner-being dimension is trust, not agony.

Judg 20:33 and Eze 32:2 are peripheral — military ambush and the dragon-king imagery respectively.

### basanizō — to torture: anguish — G0928 — Greek

**What this term means**

basanizō is a verb (12 occurrences) meaning to torture or torment; passive: to be tortured, tormented, or in pain. Its etymological origin is the basanos — the Lydian touchstone used to test the purity of gold by rubbing against it. From this physical test, the meaning extends to examination by questioning, then to torture by physical examination, then to torment and affliction in the NT. The meaning range in the NT covers: tormenting by disease (Mt 8:6), tossing by waves (Mt 14:24), making headway painfully against the wind (Mk 6:48), and the agony of birth (Rev 12:2). The passive participle is the anchor form, naming a person characterised by being in a state of torment.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Verb Present Passive Participle Nominative Singular Neuter. The present passive participle indicates an ongoing state of being tormented — the subject is characterised as one who is currently and continuously subjected to basanizō. The neuter nominative singular is unusual for a human referent and may indicate the source file's anchor occurrence is the neuter subject of Rev 12:2 (the woman's agony, abstracted).

**What the English translation conceals**

The touchstone etymology is entirely absent from English 'to torture' or 'to torment'. The touchstone tests what is genuine — it reveals the true nature of the material by applying pressure and friction. This testing-by-affliction dimension means basanizō carries a latent inner-being significance beyond torture: it is the pressure that reveals what is inside. English has no single word that covers both the testing and the torment.

**Core verse observations**

Mat 8:6 applies basanizō to the centurion's paralysed servant suffering terribly — the servant is basanizomenos, lying in a condition of ongoing torment. This is the primary inner-being use: the servant's suffering is located in his person, not merely his limbs.

Rev 12:2 applies basanizō to the woman in the apocalyptic vision crying out in birth pains and the agony of giving birth. The term is here at the intersection of literal birth-agony and eschatological-symbolic register — both dimensions are active.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Mat 14:24 applies basanizō to a boat beaten by waves — peripheral; no human inner-being referent. Mark 6:48 applies it to disciples making headway painfully against the wind — peripheral in the same way.

### chul — to twist: writh in pain — H2342I — Hebrew

**What this term means**

chul is a verb (56 occurrences) with the most expansive meaning range in the cluster. The root covers: to dance, twist, writhe, fear, tremble, travail, be in anguish, be pained. Its stem range is the widest of any term in this word: Qal (to dance, twist, writhe, whirl); Polel (to dance, writhe in travail, bear, wait anxiously); Pulal (passive: to be made to writhe, be brought forth); Hophal (to be born); Hithpolel (whirling participle, writhing-suffering torture participle, waiting longingly); Hithpalpel (to be distressed). The Hithpolel participle 'writhing, suffering torture' and 'waiting longingly' in the same stem is a remarkable pairing — both torture and longing are expressed through the same reflexive-intensive form of twisting. chul is the root from which chi.lah, chil, chal.cha.lah, ma.chol, and me.cho.lah all derive.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb qal perfective first singular either gender — 'I writhed' or 'I twisted' in a completed action. This first-person perfective places chul at its most personal and experiential in the anchor occurrence. However, the full stem range shows the term operates across many inflectional registers from impersonal cosmic events to intimate personal confession.

**What the English translation conceals**

No single English word covers the chul root's full range. English uses dance, writhe, tremble, travail, whirl, and be in anguish for different occurrences of the same root. This conceals the fundamental insight that the Hebrew root treats all these as one motion — twisting and turning — experienced variously as joy (dance), birth-effort (travail), terror (tremble), or inner agony (writhe in pain). The Hithpolel's combination of torture-suffering and longing-waiting under one stem is completely hidden in English translation.

**Core verse observations**

Job 15:20 shows the wicked man writhing in pain all his days — chul as the characterising ongoing inner-being condition of the person who lives in wickedness; the agony is not circumstantial but constitutive of the wicked life.

Isa 13:8 shows a cluster of inner-being states triggered by crisis: pangs and agony (chul) seize them, anguish takes hold, faces aflame — chul is embedded in a sequence of inner responses to catastrophic news.

Isa 26:17-18 extends this into a corporate-national inner-being image: like a pregnant woman who writhes and cries out in her pangs when near to giving birth, so were we before you, O Lord — chul applied to the corporate experience of a nation before God, the agony producing no deliverance ('we gave birth to wind'). The inner-being agony without productive outcome is itself named.

Jer 5:3 is significant: God struck them but they felt no anguish (chul); they were consumed but refused correction; their faces are harder than rock. The absence of chul — the failure to writhe in inner anguish in response to divine discipline — is itself a sign of inner-being hardening. This verse reveals chul as a morally appropriate inner response to judgement; its absence is a diagnostic of a damaged inner person.

Mic 4:10 shows Zion commanded to writhe and groan like a woman in labour — the command to chul suggests that the anguish is not merely an involuntary reaction but a directed inner movement appropriate to the situation, pointing toward promised rescue.

Deu 2:25 shows peoples trembling and in anguish (chul) at hearing the report of Israel — the inner-being response to revelation of divine power, parallel to chil in Exo 15:14 and Psa 48:6.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Psa 77:16 applies chul to the deep trembling at God's presence — cosmic-nature register, the chul-twisting applied to creation rather than persons.

Isa 23:4-5, Isa 54:1, Isa 66:7-8, Jer 51:29, Eze 30:16, Joe 2:6, and Zec 9:5 all use chul in extended registers: personified cities, nations, the land, and cosmic creation writhing under divine judgement or catastrophic news. Each extends the core inner-being meaning into collective or cosmic domains, amplifying the scale of the anguish without changing its character.

## Root and Stem Patterns Across the Term Set

The Hebrew term set is dominated by a single root family: chul/chi.lah/chil/chal.cha.lah constitute the core of the agony vocabulary, with 56+7+7 occurrences between the verb and its nominal derivatives — the largest occurrence mass in the cluster by far. This root's semantic range (dance, birth-travail, writhing in pain, trembling, longing) is unusual in that it unites motion categories across the emotional spectrum under a single root image of twisting or turning. The che.vel cluster (cord, travail-pain, pledge, destruction) may share a comparable root logic of binding-tension. sha.vats, with its weaving-related words, may also embed a tension or contraction image, though this is uncertain. The Greek terms present a different profile: agonia derives from athletic contest, basanizō from a testing-stone, and odunaō from a pain-root with no clear physical image in its etymology. The dominant pattern across the full term set is the use of somatic movement and pressure vocabulary to name inner states: twisting, bursting, being pressed on a touchstone, bearing down in birth. The inner-being and the somatic are not separated in this vocabulary — the physical motion is the vehicle through which inner agony is named and known.

## Data Quality Notes

The most significant gap is basanos, which appears in the source file's primary term list (with Matt 4:24 and Luke 16:23 cited) and in the STEP Bible Suggestion as a key term, and as a related word under basanizō — but has no independent term block, Strong's reference, or verse list in the STEP section. Luke 16:23 is specifically identified as important for understanding the full range of inner suffering, yet the term that carries that verse has no analysable data. This is a meaningful absence for the inner-being analysis.

chi.lah has 7 attested occurrences but only 1 verse provided (Job 6:10). Given the importance of this feminine form for the agony cluster, the missing 6 verses represent a significant gap — they may contain further inner-being material.

gi.ach has 5 of 7 attested occurrences; the 2 missing verses are unlikely to contain inner-being material given the peripheral character of the 5 provided, but this cannot be confirmed from the source file.

chil (H2427A) and chi.lah (H2427B) are treated as alternate forms with shared occurrence counts. It is unclear whether the 7 occurrences of each are the same 7 verses or 7 distinct verses per form — the verse lists provided for each are different, which suggests they are separate occurrence sets, but the source file's notation is ambiguous.

chalchalah appears in the primary term list as a primary Hebrew term (Isa 21:3, Nah 2:10, Ezek 30:4 cited) but has no independent term block in the STEP section; it appears only as a related word under chi.lah, chil, and chul. Its primary term list citation means it was assessed by the analyst as important, but the STEP data for it is absent.

The basanizō Strong's number is listed as G0928H rather than G0928 — the H suffix is unexplained.

## Open Questions

1. WORD-LEVEL: Does basanos have a separate term block in the STEP data that was omitted from the source file? The primary term list citations (Matt 4:24, Luke 16:23) suggest substantive data exists.

2. basanizō: The touchstone etymology introduces a testing-by-pressure dimension — does this 'testing that reveals what is inside' function as an inner-being concept in any of the 12 NT occurrences not fully represented in the source file?

3. chul (H2342I): Jer 5:3 identifies the absence of chul in response to divine discipline as a sign of inner-being hardening. Does this diagnostic pattern — the failure to writhe as a marker of inner damage — appear elsewhere in the prophetic corpus? Phase 2 question.

4. chul (H2342I): The Hithpolel stem pairs 'writhing, suffering torture' with 'waiting longingly' — these are opposite experiential states under the same reflexive-intensive form. What determines which meaning is active in a given occurrence, and is the waiting-longingly sense attested as an inner-being state elsewhere in the prophetic corpus?

5. chi.lah: The 6 missing verses for this term may contain inner-being material. Can the full verse set be recovered and does it change the classification profile?

6. sha.vats: The related words (filigree, weaving, checkered pattern) are semantically incongruous with cramp/agony. Is sha.vats a genuine agony term or a STEP cluster artifact where two distinct roots share a transliteration?

7. WORD-LEVEL: The chul root treats dance, birth-travail, writhing in pain, trembling, and longing as a single motion concept. Does this root unity, in which somatic motion names opposite inner states, represent a pattern in how the Hebrew vocabulary accesses inner agony through bodily expression — and is this pattern present in other terms outside this cluster?

8. agonia: The Gethsemane verse (Luke 22:44) presents the only NT occurrence of this term, in a context that also involves a divine-human encounter, intensified prayer, and a possible haematidrosis sign. Does the uniqueness of this occurrence limit generalisation of agonia as an inner-being category, or is the verse's singular intensity precisely what makes it definitional? Phase 2 question.

9. WORD-LEVEL: The birth-labour image (odin, chil, chi.lah, che.vel, chul, basanizō in Rev 12:2) is the single most pervasive vehicle for agony language across both Testaments. Does this image carry inner-being significance beyond its descriptive function — specifically, does the productive outcome of birth (new life following agony) shape the inner-being meaning of these terms in their contexts? Phase 2 question.

10. odin (G5604): Acts 2:24 uses odin of the pangs/cords of death loosed by resurrection. The LXX confusion between hebel-pang and hebel-cord introduces an ambiguity between experiential agony and physical binding. Does the resurrection context assign inner-being significance to this ambiguity — specifically, that death's agony is also death's bondage? Phase 2 question.
