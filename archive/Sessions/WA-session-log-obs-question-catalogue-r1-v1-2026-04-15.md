## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

---

### Pair 001

**[OBS]** Grace is defined as the disposition of a greater party toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice to give rather than withhold, arising from within the giver rather than from the quality of the recipient.

**Q:** What is grace, structurally and relationally, as a disposition — and what determines whether it is extended or withheld?

---

### Pair 002

**[OBS]** Grace as received is described as a relational position: the experience of standing before one with authority and discovering they are disposed toward you. This creates real outcomes — access, protection, standing, the sense of being known and held.

**Q:** What does receiving grace actually produce in the relational and inner-being condition of the recipient?

---

### Pair 003

**[OBS]** In its deepest biblical expression, grace as received is what a human being discovers in relationship with God — not mere tolerance, but active, personal goodwill that precedes any action on the recipient's part.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence distinguish between mere tolerance and active, personal goodwill as two distinct modes of divine disposition toward humanity?

---

### Pair 004

**[OBS]** Moses' standing before God is grounded in being known by name — the prior personal knowledge is the entire basis of his standing, not his actions or petitions.

**Q:** What is the stated basis of Moses' standing in favour with God, and does the text ground it in action, petition, or something prior to both?

---

### Pair 005

**[OBS]** The gaze of the greater party is described as the location of the gift: to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention.

**Q:** How does the biblical text spatially or somatically locate the reality of being in grace — where, bodily or relationally, does favour reside?

---

### Pair 006

**[INT]** The second form of grace — sustained inner condition — is distinguished from the act of receiving grace. It is an ongoing state, a sphere one inhabits, not a transaction that concludes.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence support a distinction between the act of receiving grace and the ongoing condition of standing in grace as two different aspects of the same reality?

---

### Pair 007

**[OBS]** Romans 5:2 is cited as naming grace spatially — as something one stands in — and as forward-oriented, not static.

**Q:** What spatial or directional language does the biblical text use to describe the ongoing condition of being in grace, and what does that language imply about grace as a state?

---

### Pair 008

**[OBS]** The third form of grace is specific capacity — a concrete gift that equips the inner person for a particular function. Examples: the word of grace builds (Acts 20:32); grace enables apostolic effort (1 Cor 15:10).

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence describe grace as functioning as an enabling endowment — a concrete capacity given to a specific person for a specific function?

---

### Pair 009

**[OBS]** The fourth form of grace is inner character quality — the gracious disposition expressed in speech, attention to others, and the way one holds power without using it to extract.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence describe a form of grace that is a character quality of the person formed by grace, as distinct from grace received or grace as standing?

---

### Pair 010

**[OBS]** Proverbs 31:30 is cited as naming the fear of the Lord as the inner ground from which lasting gracious character grows — distinguishing genuine grace from its performed imitation.

**Q:** Does any biblical text name the inner ground or source from which genuine gracious character grows, and does it distinguish this from performed or imitated grace?

---

### Pair 011

**[INT]** The four forms of grace — received, sustained condition, specific capacity, character quality — are described as the same grace working through the inner person in sequence: from spirit level through relational standing to capacity to outward character.

**Q:** Is there a discernible sequence or flow in the way grace moves through the inner person — and does the data support a model in which the four forms are stages of a single movement rather than independent phenomena?

---

### Pair 012

**[OBS]** Grace has a stated direction: it flows inward and then back out. The full inner-being picture requires all four faces.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence suggest a directional movement to grace within the inner person — inward reception followed by outward expression?

---

### Pair 013

**[OBS]** The originating source of grace is stated to be divine character. Grace enters human experience from above and works outward from there.

**Q:** What does the biblical evidence identify as the originating source of grace — and does it locate that source in divine character, human response, relational circumstance, or elsewhere?

---

### Pair 014

**[OBS]** Even the capacity to seek grace — the posture of earnest appeal — is described as something God gives, not something the human being generates independently.

**Q:** Does any biblical text describe the human capacity for earnest appeal or supplication as itself a gift of grace rather than a human-generated act?

---

### Pair 015

**[OBS]** Grace (*charis*) and joy share a common Greek root: *charis* derives from *chairo* (to rejoice). The root connection is described as structural, not incidental — signalling that grace and joy are bonded at the level of language.

**Q:** What is the etymological relationship between the Greek words for grace and joy — and does the text treat this as structurally significant or incidental?

### Pair 016

**[INT]** Grace is described as the disposition of one who gives with gladness — and receiving grace produces joy as its natural inner response. The root etymology encodes a characterisation of the giver's inner state, not just the act.

**Q:** Does the etymology of *charis* carry information about the inner state of the giver — specifically that grace is given with gladness — or does it only name the act of giving?

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

---

### Pair 017

**[OBS]** The natural enemy of grace is identified as transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on exchange, where what is received is proportional to what has been earned.

**Q:** What does the study identify as the primary structural opposite or enemy of grace — and what inner-being logic does that opposite operate on?

---

### Pair 018

**[OBS]** The negation of grace is not hostility but the closed logic of merit: you get what you deserve, no more. Where this logic dominates the inner life, it shuts down both receiving grace and extending it.

**Q:** Does the closed logic of merit function as an active barrier to both receiving and extending grace — and if so, how does the text describe that barrier's operation?

---

### Pair 019

**[OBS]** The Greek *acharistos* (ungrateful, graceless) names the person who has received grace but has not registered it as grace. Ingratitude is described as an inner failure to recognise what has been given.

**Q:** Does the grace vocabulary include a term that names the failure mode of the recipient — and what inner condition does that term describe?

---

### Pair 020

**[OBS]** What feeds grace in a person is primarily the experience of having received it undeservedly and knowing it.

**Q:** What does the study identify as the primary inner-being condition that cultivates or sustains the capacity to extend grace to others?

---

### Pair 021

**[OBS]** Supplication — earnest, vulnerability-based appeal — is described as itself a form of grace-awareness: it acknowledges that what is needed cannot be demanded, only asked for.

**Q:** What inner-being posture does the act of supplication express — and does the study treat that posture as already a form of recognising grace?

---

### Pair 022

**[OBS]** The body enacts the inner posture of the grace-seeker: prostration, raised hands, fasting, weeping. The whole body is brought into alignment with what the soul is doing.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence show the body participating in the inner act of seeking grace — and if so, what forms does that somatic participation take?

---

### Pair 023

**[OBS]** What diminishes grace is the loss of the recognition that it is freely given: self-sufficiency closes the space through which grace moves, as does contempt — placing others below the level where they deserve consideration.

**Q:** What inner-being conditions or orientations does the study identify as diminishing or closing off the movement of grace?

---

### Pair 024

**[OBS]** The first structural feature of grace's inner-being work: grace does not resolve weakness — it inhabits it. Paul's request for relief is denied; grace is the answer instead. Weakness is not the obstacle to grace but its most active theatre.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence describe grace as resolving or removing conditions of weakness — or does it operate differently in relation to weakness?

---

### Pair 025

**[INT]** The inner-being transformation grace produces is not from weakness to strength but from weakness-as-problem to weakness-as-the-place-where-grace-operates. This is a reframing, not a removal.

**Q:** What kind of transformation does grace produce in the person experiencing weakness — does it change the condition, or change the person's relationship to the condition?

---

### Pair 026

**[OBS]** The second structural feature: grace produces fear before it produces peace. The initial inner response to unexpected divine favour is not comfort but the weight of recognition — creaturely recoil before the magnitude of what is being given.

**Q:** What does the biblical evidence identify as the first inner response to receiving unexpected divine favour — and is that response comfort, fear, or something else?

---

### Pair 027

**[INT]** Grace settles into peace as the inner person adjusts to the new condition of standing-in-favour. Peace is a subsequent state, not the immediate response.

**Q:** Is peace the immediate or the subsequent inner-being effect of receiving grace — and what is the sequence the text describes?

---

### Pair 028

**[OBS]** The third structural feature: grace produces mourning before joy. Zechariah 12:10 describes an eschatological outpouring of a spirit of grace producing mourning and weeping as the first inner response.

**Q:** Does any biblical text describe mourning as a first fruit of grace rather than its absence — and what accounts for that sequence?

---

### Pair 029

**[INT]** The mourning in Zechariah 12:10 is not mourning in the absence of grace but mourning as grace's first fruit — grief-laden recognition of what grace has cost in the inner person who has fully received it.

**Q:** What is the relationship between mourning and grace in Zechariah 12:10 — is the mourning a response to the absence of grace, or a response produced by grace itself?

---

### Pair 030

**[OBS]** Grace produces stability that is not earned — a standing in relationship that holds even when circumstances do not support it. This is not passive; it is forward-directed.

**Q:** What is the nature of the stability grace produces in the inner person — is it circumstantial, earned, or of a different kind?

---

### Pair 031

**[OBS]** From the stability grace produces comes the capacity to extend grace outward: to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt.

**Q:** Does the study identify a causal connection between receiving grace and the capacity to extend it to others — and if so, what is the mechanism?

---

### Pair 032

**[OBS]** The extension of grace to others flows not from discipline but from a softened inner orientation — a tenderness of heart that is itself the work of grace.

**Q:** What is the inner-being source of the capacity to extend grace outward — is it discipline, decision, or an inner transformation that precedes the act?

---

### Pair 033

**[OBS]** The grace-agency paradox is named explicitly: the person formed by grace works genuinely and effortfully — yet the work is simultaneously the operation of grace rather than the person's own achievement. Grace does not produce passivity; it reconstitutes agency.

**Q:** Does the biblical evidence hold together genuine human effort and the priority of grace without resolving the tension — and if so, how does it do so?

---

### Pair 034

**[OBS]** Grace does not originate in human character. Scripture consistently places the divine disposition as the source. Human grace is downstream of what has been received.

**Q:** Does the study find any evidence for grace originating within human character — or does the data consistently place the divine disposition as prior and causal?

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

---

### Pair 066

**[OBS]** Grace in Scripture is carried by two root families: Hebrew centred on *chen* (favour, grace) and Greek built from *charis* (grace, favour, gift). Together they account for thirteen terms in the research inventory, five of which are primary.

**Q:** How many term families carry the grace vocabulary in Scripture — and how many terms are in the research inventory, with how many designated primary?

---

### Pair 067

**[OBS]** Eleven of the thirteen terms appear in neighbouring word studies as well. This high degree of sharing signals that grace is not an isolated concept but operates in close relationship with adjacent characteristics, particularly supplication, mercy, and covenant-love.

**Q:** What does the degree of vocabulary overlap between grace and adjacent characteristics — measured by shared terms — suggest about grace's relationship to those characteristics?

---

### Pair 068

**[OBS]** The range of English translations for *charis* is exceptionally broad: grace, favour, gift, thanks, blessing, benefit, credit, kindness. This breadth reflects that *charis* names not just a disposition but the full arc from disposition through act to the response the act evokes.

**Q:** What does the breadth of English translations for *charis* indicate about the semantic range of the term — and does it carry only the disposition, or the full relational arc?

---

### Pair 069

**[OBS]** The root etymology of *charis* (*chairo* — to rejoice) connects grace and joy at the lexical level. The New Testament pairing of "grace and peace" should be read against this background: grace is the glad-giving disposition that produces peace as the inner-being condition of its recipients.

**Q:** How does the etymology of *charis* inform the reading of the New Testament epistolary greeting "grace and peace" — and does the etymology change the meaning of the pairing?

---

### Pair 070

**[OBS]** The term *charis* moves through at least four distinct domains: state of favour, the gift itself, gracious commission, and response of gratitude. These four domains correspond exactly to the four-face semantic architecture described in Section 1.

**Q:** Does the semantic range of *charis* independently map onto the four-form structure of grace identified in Section 1 — or is that mapping imposed by the analysis?

---

### Pair 071

**[OBS]** In classical Greek, *charis* carried the sense of that which gives pleasure or charm — the quality in a person or thing that produces delight in an observer. This is the Hebrew *chen* sense carried into Greek.

**Q:** What does the classical Greek meaning of *charis* contribute to understanding the term — and how does it relate to the Hebrew *chen*?

---

### Pair 072

**[OBS]** What the New Testament adds to the classical sense of *charis* is theological weight: a God who holds this disposition toward humanity unilaterally and without obligation — transforming the classical reciprocal grace-economy into the unidirectional grace of the gospel.

**Q:** What is the difference between the classical Greek understanding of *charis* and the New Testament's use of it — and what theological shift does that difference represent?

### Pair 073

**[OBS]** The somatic connections of *charis* in the verse evidence: the heart as the seat of its reception and transformation; the mouth as the organ of its expression; the flesh as the domain into which it enters most fully.

**Q:** Which specific organs or bodily locations does the verse evidence associate with *charis* — and what function does each organ serve in relation to grace?

### Pair 074

**[OBS]** *Charizō* (the verbal form) has three primary senses: to give freely as a favour, to forgive or cancel a debt, and — in a juridical sense — to hand over a person to the demands of others. The same word names both God graciously giving all things and Pilate handing Jesus over under pressure.

**Q:** What are the distinct senses of *charizō* — and does any single Greek term carry both the grace-giving sense and its structural corruption simultaneously?

### Pair 075

**[INT]** The term *charizō* is structurally neutral in its form — the giving-from-authority structure can be inhabited by genuine grace or its corruption. The inner-being distinction is entirely in the disposition of the giver.

**Q:** Does the form of *charizō* distinguish between genuine grace-giving and its corruption — or is the distinction located elsewhere?

### Pair 076

**[OBS]** *Acharistos* (ungrateful, graceless) names the person who has received grace but has not registered it as grace. Ingratitude is not merely a moral failing; it is the inner-being condition that closes off the grace dynamic by misidentifying what is happening.

**Q:** What does the term *acharistos* contribute to the study of grace — and does the vocabulary treat ingratitude as a moral failing, an inner-being condition, or both?

### Pair 077

**[OBS]** *Charisma* (gift) names the particularisation of grace into a defined endowment given to a specific person for the benefit of others. The distinction between *charis* and *charisma* is the distinction between the river and a particular channel it cuts.

**Q:** How does *charisma* relate to *charis* — and does the distinction between them map onto any inner-being or functional difference?
