## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** grief | **Registry ID:** 071 | **Source list:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-08 | **Version:** v5

This assessment covers the nine terms in Part 1 only. On the basis of the verse records in this file, grief provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word with high confidence for a subset of its terms. Several terms — including o.ni (H6040), qa.dar (H6937), cha.lah (H2470I), and ma.rar (H4843) — appear consistently in contexts where an inner state is named, appealed to, or attributed to a person experiencing distress. The verse records show these terms functioning as descriptions of a person's condition, used in lament, petition, and narrative of suffering. The a.vel/a.val family (AVL) is the strongest inner-being candidate in this part: both terms name an identifiable inner state with associated outward expression, and the verb a.val occurs in first-person and third-person narrative contexts with grief as a clearly internal experience that produces visible behaviour.

Two terms qualify more weakly in isolation. ra.ah (H7451C) at 684 occurrences is primarily a term for objective evil, calamity, or harm that befalls someone from outside; while the experience of ra.ah can produce inner distress, the term itself does not name the inner state. a.mel (H6001A) is a noun denoting a labourer or sufferer and appears in grief-related contexts only marginally in its verse sample. Both are retained per the source list classification but are flagged as requiring closer evaluation in Phase 2. mo.rah (H4786) occurs only once in the OT and its single verse record concerns bitterness produced in another person, leaving its inner-being contribution undetermined.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### ONI — o.ni (H6040)

The root behind o.ni carries the core image of being pressed down or humbled — its related word cluster includes a.nav (poor/humble), an.vah (gentleness), and a.ni (afflicted), pointing to a semantic field in which lowness, poverty, and suffering under pressure are closely related. o.ni itself is the noun form meaning affliction, poverty, or misery, with the two sub-definitions (affliction and poverty) indicating that the term moves between material and experiential registers.

The verse records show o.ni functioning almost exclusively as the object of divine seeing and hearing. Across the sample, God is consistently the one who sees, hears, looks upon, or delivers the person from their o.ni (Gen 16:11; Gen 29:32; Gen 31:42; Exo 4:31; Deu 26:7; 1Sa 1:11; 2Ki 14:26; Neh 9:9). This pattern gives o.ni a distinctive quality: it is the condition that is seen from outside, and in particular the condition that is visible to God in a way that calls for his response. Psa 31:7 makes this explicit — "you have seen my affliction; you have known the distress of my soul" — where o.ni is paired with the distress of the soul and both are presented as objects of God's knowledge.

The Psalms verses (Psa 9:13; Psa 25:18; Psa 31:7; Psa 88:9; Psa 107:10; Psa 107:41; Psa 119:50; Psa 119:92; Psa 119:153) cluster around petition: the afflicted person appeals to God to see, consider, look on, or remember the o.ni. This makes o.ni less a term that describes the inner experience in detail and more the term that names the condition presented to God in lament. In Lamentations (Lam 1:3; 1:7; 1:9; 3:1; 3:19), o.ni becomes the noun of Jerusalem's corporate state — exile, abandonment, and the memory of prior dignity — which is then appealed to as the ground of divine response.

The Job verses (Job 10:15; 30:16; 30:27; 36:8; 36:15; 36:21) introduce a different function: o.ni as what confines or characterises the sufferer's present existence. "Days of affliction have taken hold of me" (Job 30:16) and "my inward parts are in turmoil" (Job 30:27) show o.ni appearing alongside visceral inner experience, though the term itself remains the label for the condition rather than the sensation.

The related words connect o.ni firmly to fasting (ta.a.nit) and to the act of afflicting oneself (a.nah), which the JSON cross-registry note flags as linking o.ni to the humility word set. The proximity of o.ni to the poverty-humility cluster is a genuine feature of the root and should be investigated in Phase 2.

**Verdict:** o.ni is doing specific inner-being work: it names the condition of the person-under-affliction as the object that is presented to God, appealed to in lament, and remembered as the ground for divine response. It is not a near-synonym of the AVL mourning terms. It stands alone in this part and has no consolidation candidate here.

### QADAR — qa.dar (H6937)

The core image of this root is darkness. The verb means to be dark, to mourn, or to grow dark, and its related word cluster — darkness (qad.rut), the adverb mournfully (qe.do.ran.nit), and the proper names Kedar and Kidron — confirms that the darkening is both literal and figurative. What is notable is that the same root covers both atmospheric darkening (the heavens grow dark, the stars are obscured) and the human experience of going about in grief.

The verse records split along this line. In the cosmic or prophetic contexts, qa.dar describes God darkening the heavens (Eze 32:7–8) or the sun and moon being darkened (Joe 2:10; Joe 3:15; Mic 3:6), and in 1Ki 18:45 the heavens grew black with storm clouds. In the personal lament contexts, qa.dar describes the sufferer going about in a darkened state that is explicitly not the darkness of physical shadow: Job 30:28 states "I go about darkened, but not by the sun," which is the clearest marker that the term is operating as an inner-being word in that verse. The Psalms instances (Psa 38:6; Psa 42:9; Psa 43:2) show the verb in the participial form "go about mourning," paired with bodily prostration (Psa 38:6 — "utterly bowed down and prostrate") and with crying out to God because of oppression.

The Hiphil causative form is present: "to cause to mourn" and "to darken" (Hiphil). In Eze 31:15, God causes mourning over the cedar's fall. In Eze 32:7–8, God darkens the heavens as an act of judgment. The causative moves in both directions — causing the inner state of mourning and causing literal darkness — with the verse set not always distinguishing clearly between them.

The signal features here are significant: god_as_subject is true (God acts as the one who darkens and causes mourning), somatic_link is true (Psa 38:6 pairs the verb with physical prostration), and causative_form_present is true. All three appear in this single-term family.

The English translation obscures the root's unified image by sometimes rendering qa.dar as "be dark" (the literal sense) and sometimes as "mourn" (the interior sense), which prevents readers from seeing that the same word describes both the darkening sky and the darkened person. The Hebrew term carries both simultaneously, and the image functions as a bridge between outer and inner registers.

**Verdict:** qa.dar is doing genuine inner-being work as a term for grief-as-darkening, but its verse set spans both atmospheric and personal domains. The term is not a near-synonym of any other term in this part. Phase 2 needs to examine whether the dual register (outer darkness / inner mourning) is a deliberate poetic feature or whether the verse sample in this part has over-represented the cosmological uses.

### CHALAH — cha.lah (H2470I)

*This root family also appears in Part 3 (cho.li, H2483). Root-level conclusions here are provisional until Session D cross-part synthesis is complete.*

The CHALAH root carries a primary image of weakness or becoming weak — illness, bodily weakness, and grieving are presented by STEP as expressions of the same basic condition of becoming unable to function as normal. The meaning block for cha.lah lists Qal, Piel, Niphal, Pual, Hithpael, Hiphil, and Hophal stems, making it one of the most conjugationally rich terms in this part. Notably, the same root covers making oneself sick (Hithpael), being made sick by another (Hophal), and causing grief (Hiphil sub-definition 4).

The verse records in Part 1 for cha.lah are nine in number, representing approximately 15% of the stated occurrence count of around 60 — a thin sample. From what is present, cha.lah appears in three types of context. First, in expressions of someone's grief about another person's condition (1Sa 22:8 — "none of you is sorry for me"; Amo 6:6 — "not grieved over the ruin of Joseph"). Second, in descriptions of a wound or blow as grievous (Jer 14:17 — "a very grievous blow"; Jer 30:12 — "your wound is grievous"; Nah 3:19 — "your wound is grievous"), where the term applies to the severity of an external injury but with language that bridges physical and inner registers. Third, in the theologically distinctive use in Isa 53:10 — "he has put him to grief" — where God is subject and the Suffering Servant is the object, with cha.lah as the verb denoting what God causes to happen to the servant. This is the clearest god_as_subject instance in the cha.lah sample.

The somatic link is marked true, which the Jeremiah and Nahum wound-language supports. The causative form is marked true, with the Hiphil covering "to make sick," "to make sore," and "to grieve." The presence of all three signal features on cha.lah makes it a high-priority term for Phase 2 investigation, though the thin verse sample here limits what can be observed.

**Verdict:** cha.lah is doing inner-being work at the intersection of physical weakness and inner grief, with the causative dimension and the Isa 53:10 use being particularly significant. Because the verse sample is only 15% of the total occurrence count, the verdict here is descriptive rather than analytical. Full assessment requires the broader verse set in Phase 2. Root-level synthesis with cho.li (Part 3) must await Session D.

### MAR — mo.rah (H4786) and ma.rar (H4843)

The MAR root carries a core image of bitterness. The related word cluster shared by both terms is almost identical: bitter (mar), myrrh (mor), bitterness (me.ri.rut, ma.ror, mor.rah), gall (me.re.rah, me.ro.rah), and the place name Marah. The image of bitterness here has both a physical referent (the bitter taste of gall, of undrinkable water) and an inner referent (bitterness of grief, of experience). The two terms in this part divide along these lines.

**mo.rah (H4786)** occurs approximately once in the OT. Its single verse record (Gen 26:35) describes Esau's Hittite wives making life bitter for Isaac and Rebekah. The noun is glossed "bitterness, grief" but in this instance is used for a relational experience produced by another person's behaviour — life made bitter by the presence of others. Because only one verse is present, mo.rah's inner-being status cannot be assessed beyond noting that it names a condition of experienced bitterness with a relational cause.

**ma.rar (H4843)** is a verb with fourteen occurrences, and its verse sample of thirteen records is the most complete in this part. The verb ranges across several uses. In Exo 1:14, the Egyptians made the Israelites' lives bitter with hard service — a causative use where the subject imposes bitterness on another. In Ruth 1:20, Naomi says the Almighty "has dealt very bitterly with me," attributing the experience of bitterness to God's direct action. In Job 27:2, Job accuses God of having "made my soul bitter." In Isa 38:17, the speaker says it was for his welfare that he "had great bitterness." These verse records show ma.rar functioning in inner-being contexts where the soul, the life, or the person's experience is described as made bitter by an agent — primarily God or an oppressing power — or as an inner condition the speaker has inhabited.

Two outlier verses complicate the picture. Dan 8:7 and Dan 11:11 use the Hithpalpel of the root to describe a military commander being "enraged" — a meaning close to fury rather than grief, and one of the sub-definitions in the TWOT note flags "to be strong, strengthen" as an alternative meaning. This suggests that ma.rar at its edges overlaps with the anger word set.

Zec 12:10 is among the most theologically significant verses in the Part 1 set: "they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child, and weep bitterly over him, as one weeps over a firstborn." Here ma.rar appears alongside mourning language in an eschatological lament context, with bitter weeping as the outward expression of the inner state.

The god_as_subject flag is true for ma.rar: Ruth 1:20 ("the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me") and Job 27:2 ("the Almighty, who has made my soul bitter") both name God as the one who causes the bitter state. The causative_form_present flag is true: the Hiphil "to make bitter, embitter" covers the action of one party imposing bitterness on another.

**Verdict within MAR:** mo.rah and ma.rar share a root but are doing different work in the verse sets. mo.rah is a rare noun naming experienced bitterness; ma.rar is a verb covering the experience of bitterness as both a state and an action. The soul-directed use of ma.rar (Job 27:2 — "made my soul bitter") and the Hithpalpel reflexive form (to embitter oneself) make it a genuine inner-being term. mo.rah, given a single verse, is a consolidation candidate with ma.rar pending further evidence. The anger-edge of the Hithpalpel (to be enraged) must be addressed in Phase 2.

### RAAH — ra.ah (H7451C)

ra.ah is the largest-occurrence term in this part at approximately 684 instances, and only 78 verses are recorded — approximately 11% of the occurrence count. This is the most significant SMALL_VERSE_SAMPLE situation in Part 1, and all verdicts here must be treated as provisional.

The core meaning of ra.ah covers evil, misery, distress, and injury across three sub-definitions: (a) evil/misery/distress, (b) evil/injury/wrong, (c) ethical evil. This range places ra.ah at the broad end of Hebrew vocabulary for badness and harm — it is not a grief word in the focused sense that o.ni or ma.rar are. What includes ra.ah in the grief word set is its frequent appearance in contexts where people experience distress (the "day of trouble," "calamity," "disaster") and where that experience has an inner dimension.

The verse records show ra.ah operating primarily as a noun or adjective for something that comes upon a person from outside: disaster that God brings (Jer 11:11; Jer 19:3; 1Ki 21:21), harm that enemies intend (1Sa 23:9; 1Sa 24:9), trouble that a city finds itself in (Neh 1:3; Neh 2:17). The god_as_subject flag is true: across a very large number of verses, God is the one who brings the ra.ah — Exo 32:14 ("the Lord relented from the disaster"), 2Sa 17:14 ("the Lord might bring harm upon Absalom"), Dan 9:14 ("the Lord our God is righteous in all the works that he has done"). This is not God experiencing grief but God as the agent who appoints or withdraws calamitous experience for others.

A smaller subset of verses uses ra.ah for the inner experience of the one who suffers: Psa 88:3 ("my soul is full of troubles"), Num 11:15 ("that I may not see my wretchedness"), Jon 4:6 (Jonah's discomfort). These verses suggest that ra.ah can name what is being felt, not only what is being imposed. Whether this inner-experiential use is representative of a genuine sub-register within ra.ah or whether it reflects incidental usage in what is predominantly an external-event term cannot be determined from 11% of the verse set.

The English translation renders ra.ah as disaster, harm, evil, trouble, calamity, and hurt depending on context, but these are all renderings of the same word. The semantic width of the term — which English requires multiple words to handle — is a notable feature of ra.ah and one that may affect how it functions alongside narrower grief terms in context.

**Verdict:** ra.ah's inner-being contribution in this part is real but peripheral: the term names the situation of badness/calamity/distress that produces grief rather than the grief itself. At 684 occurrences, a fuller verse set may reveal a more developed inner-experiential register. The current sample is insufficient for a firm verdict. Phase 2 must supply a fuller verse set before ra.ah's placement in the grief word set can be assessed with confidence.

### AMEL — a.mel (H6001A)

The AMEL family as represented in Part 1 consists of a single term: a.mel, a noun meaning labourer, sufferer, or wretched one. The root a.mal covers toiling with effort or trouble, and the related word cluster includes the verb a.mal (to toil / trouble), the noun a.mal (trouble), and the proper name Amalek. The noun a.mel sits at the intersection of the labour and suffering senses of the root, denoting the person who endures hard toil and who may be wretched as a result.

The verse records are four in number. Judg 5:26 is a warrior context where a.mel appears in its workman sense (the mallet of workmen — a physical tool). Job 3:20 uses it in a grief-adjacent context: "why is light given to him who is in misery, and life to the bitter in soul" — where a.mel as sufferer or wretched one sits alongside "bitter in soul," giving it an inner-being dimension. Job 20:22 refers to the hand of "everyone in misery" turning against the prosperous wicked man. Pro 16:26 uses the form in the labour sense: a worker's appetite drives him.

None of the four verses show a.mel clearly functioning as an inner-being word on its own. Its inclusion in the grief word set appears to rest on the Job 3:20 context, where "him who is in misery" is a person whose condition is then given an inner description ("the bitter in soul") by the following phrase. a.mel names the person in that state, not the state itself.

**Verdict:** a.mel is a thin entry in the grief word set on the basis of this verse sample. It denotes the person who suffers or toils rather than naming the inner condition of suffering. It is not a near-synonym of any other term in this part. Its inner-being qualification requires a broader verse set and fuller review in Phase 2.

### AVL — a.vel (H0057) and a.val (H0056)

The AVL root is the clearest and most developed mourning root in Part 1. Its two terms divide by grammatical category: a.val is the verb (to mourn, lament) and a.vel is the noun and adjective (mourning; one who mourns). The related words confirm a tight family: a.vel, a.val, and e.vel (mourning as an abstract state) are the three primary forms of the same root.

**a.vel (H0057)** covers mourning in three distinguishable senses: mourning for the dead, mourning because of calamity, and the rites of mourning. In its noun function it also denotes the mourner as a social role (one who comforts mourners, Job 29:25). The verse records show a.vel in several settings. Gen 37:35 has Jacob going down to Sheol mourning — mourning as the ongoing state of a person who cannot be comforted. Psa 35:14 describes the psalmist going about in mourning as though for a friend or brother, with bowing down as the physical posture. Est 6:12 shows Haman going home "mourning and with his head covered" — mourning with a visible external sign. Isa 57:18 and 61:2–3 are theologically significant: in 61:2–3, the Lord's anointed is sent to "comfort all who mourn" and to "give them a beautiful headdress instead of ashes, the oil of gladness instead of mourning" — where a.vel names the state that is to be replaced by gladness, and the replacing involves specific physical signs (ashes, oil).

The somatic_link flag is true for a.vel: the verse records consistently attach mourning to bodily expression — covered head (Est 6:12), bowing down (Psa 35:14), ashes (Isa 61:3). Mourning in the a.vel sense is an inner state that is both expressed in and partially constituted by its bodily practices.

**a.val (H0056)** is the verb with approximately 39 occurrences and 31 verse records — a representative sample at 79% coverage. The verb covers mourning in first and third person, for the death of individuals (Gen 37:34 — Jacob tears garments and mourns; 2Sa 13:37 — David mourns for Absalom; Dan 10:2 — Daniel mourns three weeks), for communal sin or calamity (Ezr 10:6 — Ezra mourns over the faithlessness of the exiles; Neh 1:4 — Nehemiah mourns upon hearing of Jerusalem's state), and in extended figurative use where land, gates, the vine, and the earth itself are said to mourn (Isa 3:26; Isa 24:4, 24:7; Isa 33:9; Jer 4:28; Jer 12:4; Hos 4:3; Amo 1:2).

The god_as_subject flag is true for a.val: Eze 31:15 states "I caused mourning" with God as the agent who produces mourning over the fallen cedar — a figurative use where mourning is caused across the natural world by God's action. The causative_form_present flag is true: the Hiphil of a.val means "to mourn, cause to mourn," and the Eze 31:15 verse illustrates it. The somatic_link flag is true: mourning in the verb form is also regularly shown in bodily behaviour — Gen 37:34 includes tearing garments and putting on sackcloth; 2Sa 14:2 describes putting on mourning garments and not anointing with oil.

The figurative extension of a.val to land, gates, and the vine is a distinctive feature. These uses are explicitly marked in the STEP meaning block as figurative (inanimate objects mourning). The Hebrew understanding of mourning as something that can be attributed to creation as well as persons is carried by this verb in ways that a.vel (the noun) does not extend as far.

One theologically notable instance: 1Sa 15:35 — "Samuel grieved over Saul. And the Lord regretted that he had made Saul king over Israel." Here a.val (Samuel's grief) and the Lord's regret appear in the same verse as two parallel responses to Saul's rejection, and 1Sa 16:1 has God directly addressing Samuel's ongoing a.val ("How long will you grieve over Saul?"). This is not god_as_subject in the sense of God experiencing grief through a.val, but it shows human grief for a leader alongside God's own response, with God instructing the human mourner to redirect.

**Verdict within AVL:** a.vel and a.val are doing differentiated work within the same root. a.val is the active process of mourning — the verb of entering and sustaining the state. a.vel names the state itself and the social role of the mourner. Both are genuine inner-being terms, with the noun carrying the social and ritual dimensions and the verb carrying the experiential and figurative dimensions. They are not consolidation candidates — they are the expected noun-verb pair of the same root and should both be retained. The figurative extension of a.val to inanimate subjects is a distinctive feature that Phase 2 should examine: does the earth mourning (a.val) represent a genuine cosmological use or a rhetorical figure?

## Section 3 — Where Sub-Terms Do Different Work

Three root families in Part 1 contain more than one term where the verse sets reveal meaningful differences.

**MAR family: mo.rah vs. ma.rar.** mo.rah is a noun that appears once, in a context where bitterness is produced in one person by another's behaviour (Gen 26:35). ma.rar is a verb with a much fuller verse set covering: bitterness imposed on a person by an oppressor (Exo 1:14), bitterness attributed to God's action on the soul (Job 27:2; Ruth 1:20), the speaker's own experience of great bitterness (Isa 38:17; Isa 22:4), and the eschatological mourning of bitter weeping (Zec 12:10). The difference is not only grammatical: ma.rar shows the soul as a specific location of bitterness (Job 27:2 — "made my soul bitter") and shows the experience as something that can be directed, attributed to an agent, and expressed outwardly in weeping. mo.rah lacks verse records to show any of these features. Whether mo.rah and ma.rar occupy genuinely separate semantic space or whether mo.rah is effectively a rare form variant of the same condition cannot be determined from one verse.

**AVL family: a.vel vs. a.val.** As discussed in Section 2, a.vel and a.val function as the noun and verb pair of the same mourning root. The verse sets reveal a genuine functional distinction: a.vel clusters around the social role of the mourner and the mourning state as a recognisable condition (Jacob going down mourning, the mourner who comforts others), while a.val shows mourning as a process entered into (Daniel mourns for three weeks) and as something that can be caused, instructed to stop, or attributed to creation. The figurative extension to inanimate objects belongs entirely to a.val, not a.vel. These differences are genuine, not labelling distinctions.

**The single-term families** (ONI, QADAR, RAAH, AMEL) have no internal sub-term comparison to make.
