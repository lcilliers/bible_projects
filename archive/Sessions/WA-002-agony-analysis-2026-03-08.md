## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

The word agony provisionally qualifies under the inner being definition with moderate-to-high confidence, subject to the exclusion of three terms that are borderline or peripheral to inner experience (discussed in Section 5).

The qualification rests on a core of seven terms — chul (H2342I), chil (H2427A), chi.lah (H2427B), chal.cha.lah (H2479), agonia (G0074), odunaō (G3600), and basanos (G0931) — whose meanings centre on an intense interior state of suffering, distress, or overwhelming pain that may or may not involve physical expression. The Hebrew CHUL-family terms use the image of writhing or twisting as their root, which orients the word set toward an experience that is felt bodily but is not exclusively physical. The NT term agonia appears in a supreme inner-being context — the Gethsemane account — and its verse set is correspondingly thin (one occurrence), while odunaō and basanos supply additional NT coverage. The remaining three terms (sha.vats H7661, ta.cha.lu.im H8463, and gi.ach H1518) raise qualification questions addressed in the data quality section.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### CHUL Root Family (chul, chil, chi.lah, chal.cha.lah)

The CHUL root carries a core image of twisting, whirling, or writhing — a physical motion abstracted into the language of inner experience. The verb chul (H2342I) is the root form and the most expansive term in the family, occurring about 56 times. Its meaning block spans an unusual semantic range: to dance, to twist, to writhe in pain, to tremble, to be in anguish, to travail, to give birth. The breadth of this range is not accidental — the same twisting motion underlies all of these senses. Dancing and writhing in pain share a root image; so do giving birth and trembling with fear. The CHUL root does not distinguish between the motion of joy and the motion of terror at the level of etymology; that distinction is supplied entirely by context.

The two noun forms derived from this root — chil (H2427A, masculine) and chi.lah (H2427B, feminine) — both occur about 7 times and carry the narrowed meaning: pain, agony, sorrow, writhing, anguish. Their meaning blocks are identical, and the source file itself states that H2427A also means H2427B. In practice these appear to be grammatical variants of the same noun — a masculine-feminine pair — rather than two semantically distinct words. Both are concentrated in the sub-sense of writhing with fear or anguish; they do not carry the birth-travail sense as prominently as the verb. The chil verses cluster around national catastrophe and military dread: the inhabitants of Philistia tremble (Exo 15:14), anguish seizes armies and kings (Jer 6:24; Jer 50:43), and the prophetic image of a woman in labour is consistently used to describe the inner effect of hearing devastating news. This places chil/chi.lah as the noun form that names the inner state produced when overwhelming threat arrives.

The intensified reduplicative noun chal.cha.lah (H2479, about 4 occurrences) appears to represent the same root at an elevated register. Its meaning block adds terror and trembling to the pain-writhing cluster, and its verse set is specifically eschatological or oracular: anguish filling the loins (Isa 21:3), knees trembling and faces growing pale (Nah 2:10), anguish descending on nations under divine judgment (Eze 30:4, 30:9). The body parts named in these verses — loins, knees — are the same anatomical sites associated with weakness and collapse under extreme duress. chal.cha.lah does not simply intensify the pain meaning; it concentrates it into a specific register of overwhelming dread that disables the body.

**Verdict on CHUL family:** The four members of this family are doing genuinely different work. chul is the generative verb with the widest semantic range, encompassing motion (dance, whirl), biological process (birth), and inner state (anguish, writhing fear). chil and chi.lah are the noun forms that name the inner state alone, stripped of the birth and dance senses, and concentrated in the experience of anguish arising from threat or catastrophe. chal.cha.lah is the intensified noun of the same root, reserved for the most extreme register and consistently linked to specific somatic effects (loins, knees, facial pallor). The three noun forms are not consolidation candidates; their verse sets occupy different registers and contexts.

The English translation does not consistently reveal these distinctions. "Anguish," "pain," "pangs," and "writhing" are all used across the family without marking which Hebrew term is in view, and the birth-travail sense of chul in some verses (Isa 66:7-8; Mic 4:10) obscures its inner-state sense in others.

### CHAVEL Root Family (che.vel)

The noun che.vel (H2256B) belongs to a root that also carries the meanings of cord, pledge, destruction, and labour. The pain sense — pains of travail, pangs, sorrows — is one semantic stream within a wide root family. The inner-being sense of che.vel is specifically the pang or spasm of agony, modelled consistently on birth pangs: Jer 13:21, Jer 49:24, and Hos 13:13 all use the childbirth metaphor. Unlike the CHUL family, which uses birth travail as an image of generalised anguish, che.vel stays closer to the physical spasm itself — the seizure of pain that takes hold suddenly. Job 21:17 is significant: it states explicitly that God distributes pains in his anger, placing che.vel in a causative relationship with divine agency.

**Verdict on CHAVEL family:** Single term. che.vel adds to the word set the sense of a sudden seizing pang rather than a sustained writhing. Its inner-being qualification rests primarily on the metaphorical uses in Jeremiah and Hosea; the Job passage alone does not establish it as an inner-being term.

### CHALA Root Family (ta.cha.lu.im)

ta.cha.lu.im (H8463) means diseases. Its connection to agony in this word set is contextual rather than etymological: it appears in contexts where God either inflicts sickness as judgment or heals it, and in 2Ch 21:19 it is associated with a death described as occurring in great agony. The meaning block does not define an inner state; it names a physical condition. The God-as-subject and causative flags are both true for this term (Deu 29:22 explicitly states that God made the land sick), but the inner-being connection depends on reading suffering consequent on disease as an inner state.

**Verdict:** ta.cha.lu.im is a borderline term. Its primary meaning is physical illness. The connection to agony as an inner-being word is indirect.

### Standalone Term: sha.vats

sha.vats (H7661) occurs once (2Sa 1:9) and its meaning block explicitly notes that its meaning is uncertain. The verse — a dying man describing anguish that has seized him while his life still lingers — places it in a clear inner-being context, and the gloss "cramp, agony, anguish" is consistent with the CHUL-family range. However, its related words (filigree, weaving, checkered patterns) share no semantic connection to agony. The single occurrence prevents verse-set analysis.

**Verdict:** sha.vats is a thin, isolated term. The verse context qualifies it, but the etymology does not support it and the meaning uncertainty is stated in the source.

### Standalone Term: gi.ach

gi.ach (H1518) means to burst forth, draw forth, or bring forth. It is a physical action verb. The verse set contains no example where gi.ach names an inner state: it describes water bursting out of the sea (Job 38:8), a river in flood (Job 40:23), men bursting out of ambush (Judg 20:33), a child drawn from the womb (Psa 22:9), and Pharaoh compared to a bursting dragon (Eze 32:2). None of these uses address agony as an inner-being experience. gi.ach appears in this word set because it participates in the birthing-imagery cluster that runs through several agony terms, not because it names an inner state.

**Verdict:** gi.ach does not qualify as an inner-being term for agony on the basis of its verse set. It contributes imagery but not inner-being vocabulary.

### AGON Root Family (agonia)

agonia (G0074) is the Greek cognate of the English word agony. It occurs once in the NT (Luk 22:44) and carries a semantic range that moves from athletic contest and struggle to the mental and emotional state of extreme distress. The LSJ entry shows the word in classical use for the agony of the mind — in fear and great anguish — well before its NT occurrence. In Luke 22:44 it is used for Jesus in Gethsemane. The verse explicitly links the inner state to a physical sign: sweat falling like great drops of blood. This makes agonia the only NT agony term where both god_as_subject and somatic_link are true simultaneously in a single verse. The rarity (one occurrence) means the verse functions as a definitive example rather than a pattern.

**Verdict:** agonia is a high-qualification inner-being term. Its single occurrence is paradigmatically significant for inner-being analysis, and its classical semantic history as a term of mental anguish reinforces the inner-being reading.

### BASAN Root Family (basanizō, basanos)

The BASAN root originates in the touchstone — a stone used to test the purity of metals. From this physical testing sense, the Greek developed meanings of examination by torture, suffering of disease, and torment. Both terms in this family carry this trajectory.

basanos (G0931) is the noun form (about 3 occurrences): torment, severe pain, torture. In Luke 16:23 and 16:28 it names the suffering of the rich man in Hades. In Matt 4:24 it describes the pains of the sick. The Hades usage is particularly important for the word set: basanos is the term used to describe post-mortem inner experience of torment — a use that has no OT parallel in this word set.

basanizō (G0928) is the verb (about 12 occurrences). Its verse set divides into three functional clusters: (1) physical suffering of disease — a paralysed servant (Mat 8:6), physical torment of illness; (2) physical agitation — a boat beaten by waves (Mat 14:24), disciples making headway against the wind (Mar 6:48); (3) spiritual/demonic torment — demons fearing torment by Jesus (Mat 8:29; Mar 5:7; Luk 8:28), Lot's soul tormenting itself over lawless deeds (2Pe 2:8), eschatological torment in Revelation (Rev 9:5; Rev 11:10; Rev 14:10; Rev 20:10). The Revelation usage places basanizō at the most extreme end of the agony spectrum — eternal, unrelenting torment — and in 2Pe 2:8 the verb is applied to a righteous man tormenting his own soul, which makes it explicitly an inner-being term.

The causative_form_present flag is true for basanizō: it consistently takes a tormentor and a tormented, establishing agony as something that can be inflicted by one agent on another.

**Verdict on BASAN family:** basanos and basanizō are doing different work. basanos names the state of torment (noun, condition). basanizō names the act of tormenting or the experience of being tormented (verb, process). The verse sets are meaningfully distinct. The Revelation verses for basanizō are heavily eschatological; the basanos verse set in Luke 16 is specifically post-mortem. These are not consolidation candidates.

### ODUN Root Family (odunaō)

odunaō (G3600) is the verb form of the odunē family (the noun odunē appears only in the related words list, not as a separate term in this word set). Its meaning covers both physical and mental pain; the passive form — to be in agony, be tormented, be distressed — is the NT usage. The four NT occurrences span: Mary's distress over losing Jesus (Luk 2:48), the rich man's anguish in flames (Luk 16:24–25), and Paul's companions' sorrow at his departure (Act 20:38). The last occurrence is striking: Act 20:38 uses odunaō for grief at anticipated separation, which is categorically different from the fire-torment context of Luk 16:24–25. The causative flag is true on the basis of the LSJ entry, which shows an active causative use.

**Verdict:** odunaō is an inner-being term that straddles the boundary between agony and grief. Its NT verse set places it at a lower intensity than agonia or basanizō in some contexts (Act 20:38) and at extreme intensity in others (Luk 16:24–25). The term appears to function as the general inner-agony verb where the other NT terms are more specific.

---

### ODIN Root Family (odin)

odin (G5604) is the Greek birth-pang word. Its meaning block includes both literal birth pangs and metaphorical uses — eschatological birth pangs preceding catastrophe (Mat 24:8; Mar 13:8), the pangs of death (Act 2:24), and sudden destruction likened to labour pains coming on a woman (1Th 5:3). The NT uses are uniformly metaphorical for events or processes, not for individual inner experience. The somatic link is true — the birth-pang image is inherently bodily — but in the NT verse set odin is never applied to an individual's inner state directly; it always describes a collective or eschatological process.

**Verdict:** odin overlaps with the CHUL family's birth-travail dimension in Hebrew but occupies a different register in the NT — it is primarily a metaphor for event-level suffering rather than an individual inner-being term.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

The most significant within-family differentiation is in the CHUL Hebrew family.

chul (H2342I) versus chil/chi.lah (H2427A/B): The verb chul spans dance, birth, and anguish indifferently. The noun forms chil and chi.lah strip out the dance and birth senses and name only the resulting inner state. This is not merely a grammatical difference. chul in its anguish uses tends to appear in descriptive or prophetic narrative (Jer 5:3; Eze 30:16; Zec 9:5), where the writhing is something happening to people or lands. chil and chi.lah tend to appear in more direct descriptions of the inner state as something that seizes or grips a person: anguish has taken hold of us (Jer 6:24), anguish seized him (Jer 50:43), pain seized you (Mic 4:9). This is a genuine functional difference: chil/chi.lah name the state as felt from the inside; chul describes the visible or observable process of writhing.

chal.cha.lah (H2479) versus chil/chi.lah: chal.cha.lah is not simply an intensified chil. The verses reveal a consistent anatomical specificity absent from chil: loins filled with anguish (Isa 21:3), anguish in all loins (Nah 2:10). The loin is the site of both physical power and reproductive capacity; anguish located there has a specific somatic and cultural weight. chal.cha.lah appears in contexts of prophetic vision of national destruction, not in contexts of individual experience. The difference between chil and chal.cha.lah is not only intensity but register: chil is the agony of individuals under threat; chal.cha.lah is the overwhelming dread of collective catastrophe experienced as paralysis of the body's core.

Within the BASAN family, basanos and basanizō differ by the noun-verb distinction noted in Section 2. More significantly, basanos in the Luke 16 passage names the ongoing condition of post-mortem torment as a state (Luk 16:23: being in torment), while basanizō names the process of tormenting (Rev 14:10: he will be tormented). The verb carries an external agent more readily than the noun.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The word set has both OT and NT terms, so this section is required.

The OT vocabulary of agony is dominated by the CHUL family and is overwhelmingly somatic: the inner state is named through the image of a body writhing, twisting, trembling, or seized by convulsive pain. Birth travail is the governing metaphor across the OT verse set, and it is applied to nations, cities, kings, and individuals alike. The OT vocabulary does not distinguish between agony as an experience of the individual inner being and agony as a collective or national condition; chul and its noun forms apply to both without differentiation.

The NT vocabulary introduces two functions not present in the OT portion of this word set. First, agony is named in an eschatological and post-mortem context in ways the OT terms do not. basanos and odunaō in Luke 16 describe a person experiencing torment after death, in a named afterlife location (Hades). The OT agony terms describe suffering in historical, embodied situations; the NT terms extend the category to post-mortem inner experience. Second, agonia at Luk 22:44 applies agony to the inner state of Jesus in Gethsemane — a figure who is simultaneously fully human and, in the Lukan narrative, the subject of divine attention (an angel strengthens him). This use has no direct parallel in the OT word set, where god_as_subject is true for terms (ta.cha.lu.im, che.vel) where God is the agent who inflicts agony on others, not the one experiencing it.

The single most significant difference between the OT and NT verse sets is this: the OT uses agony language primarily to describe what happens to people under divine judgment or historical catastrophe (nations writhing, cities trembling, individuals seized by dread), while the NT uses agony language to describe what happens inside individuals — in their soul, in the afterlife, or in extremis — and introduces the post-mortem dimension that is absent from the OT agony vocabulary in this word set.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

Three terms in the JSON raise qualification concerns that affect the completeness of the analysis.

gi.ach (H1518) does not function as an inner-being term in any of its listed verses. Its presence in the word set appears to arise from its connection to birth imagery rather than from a direct agony meaning. The analysis in Section 2 treats it as a non-qualifying term; the word set's inner-being picture is not diminished by its absence.

ta.cha.lu.im (H8463) names physical diseases, not inner states. Its verse set does contain one occurrence where death from disease is described as occurring in great agony (2Ch 21:19), and the root verb cha.la is used in the Deu 29:22 verse in a causative form attributed to God. However, the meaning block itself does not describe an inner state; the agony connection is contextual. This limits what can be said about ta.cha.lu.im's inner-being contribution.

sha.vats (H7661) occurs once, with explicitly uncertain meaning. The verse context (2Sa 1:9) supports an agony reading, but the related words (filigree, weaving) and the stated uncertainty prevent confident analysis. The single occurrence also means no verse-set pattern can be identified.

The chil/chi.lah consolidation question (see JSON data quality flags) is a structural issue for Phase 2 rather than an analytical gap. For the purposes of this analysis they have been treated as a masculine-feminine noun pair from the same root and analysed together. If they are confirmed as a single lexical item in Phase 2, the term count for the CHUL family reduces from four to three.

The basanizō verse set in the source file is split under two sub-headings (to torture: anguish; to torture: torture). The combined verse set reveals a single verb covering multiple semantic domains; this has been treated as one term throughout. The full verse set (12 occurrences) is richer than either sub-heading alone would suggest.
