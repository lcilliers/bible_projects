## Pass 1 — Meaning and Semantic Range

### G5483 charizō — to give grace (24 occurrences)

**Mounce:** to give grace; to forgive, cancel (a debt); to grant; to hand over into custody.

**LSJ analysis:** The classical LSJ entry for χαρίζομαι (the middle, which is the dominant NT form) shows three main registers: (1) to show favour or kindness, to gratify, oblige; (2) to grant, give freely, bestow — specifically as gift without obligation of return; (3) to forgive, remit a debt or offence. The classical range makes the structure clear: charizō names the act of giving from a position of favour — where the giving is the outward expression of an inner disposition of goodwill. The term does not name the disposition itself (that is charis) but the act that the disposition produces.

**Sense structure:**
- Primary sense: to give freely / to bestow graciously — the act of free bestowal (Rom 8:32: freely gave us all things; 1Cor 2:12: things freely given)
- Secondary sense: to forgive / to cancel — the act of releasing from a debt or offence (Eph 4:32; Col 2:13; 2Cor 2:7,10)
- Tertiary sense: to grant / to hand over — juridical giving, including into custody (Lk 23:25: handed over Jesus to their will; Acts 3:14: granted Barabbas)

**Semantic tension:** The same term names both the most generous act (giving the Son, Rom 8:32) and the most juridical act (handing over to custody, Acts 27:24). The inner-being content is determined by context — the disposition of free giving can express itself in bestowal, in forgiveness, and in juridical grant. This range reveals that charizō names the mode of giving (freely, from goodwill) not the content of the gift.

**Session C check:** Word study Section 1 states grace involves "freely granting, releasing, and bestowing." Confirmed. The juridical custody sense is not mentioned — minor gap, not significant for the inner-being analysis.

**Cross-registry flags from Pass 1 — charizō:**

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD018]: charizō carries both the giving sense and the forgiving sense as primary senses of the same term — not as metaphorical extension but as direct lexical range. This is not the forgiveness-as-consequence-of-grace relationship (that is PH2-068-001): it is the deeper question of whether giving and forgiving are the same inner-being act from the giver's side. When God forgives, is God doing the same inner-being thing as when God gives? Does the forgiveness cancel a debt (juridical act) or does it freely give again to one who forfeited (dispositional act)? The answer shapes how the grace-forgiveness relationship is understood at Session D. Registry 64 (forgiveness) and Registry 68 (grace) share this term's semantic range. The question for Session D: is forgiving a subspecies of giving, or a distinct act that grace makes possible?

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD019]: The tertiary sense of charizō — handing over to custody — appears in Lk 23:25 (Barabbas released, Jesus handed over) and Acts 27:24 (Paul granted to those sailing with him). In both cases the juridical giving is an act of authority. The same term that names God's free giving of all things (Rom 8:32) names Pilate's handing over of Jesus. The inner-being contrast is stark: both uses name a giving from authority, but one is grace and one is its opposite. For Session D: does charizō's range across grace and juridical custody illuminate anything about the inner-being conditions under which authority gives freely vs. gives in response to external pressure? This may connect to the C20 power-authority cluster (DIM-068-SD003).

### G5485 charis — grace (241 occurrences)

**Mounce:** grace, the state of kindness and favour toward someone, often with a focus on a benefit given to the object; by extension: gift, benefit; credit; words.

**LSJ analysis — this is the most semantically rich term in the registry:**

The LSJ entry reveals a structure the NT usage inherits but transforms:

*Objective sense (I):* outward grace, beauty, attractiveness — the quality of a person or thing that draws favour. This is chen in Hebrew: the gracious quality that generates goodwill in the observer. This sense persists in the NT in a minor key (Lk 4:22: "words of grace" — gracious speech quality).

*Subjective sense (II) — two poles:*
- From the giver: grace, kindness, goodwill toward another (II.1) — this is the dominant NT theological sense
- From the receiver: sense of favour received, thankfulness, gratitude (II.2) — this is the charis-as-gratitude sense, almost entirely absent from the NT theological usage but present in the classical world

*Concrete sense (III):* a favour done or returned, a boon — charis as the concrete gift, the specific benefit conferred. This sense underlies Paul's usage of charis for the collection (2Cor 8:6,7,19) and for spiritual gifts (Rom 12:6).

*Gratification/delight sense (IV):* χάρις as pleasure, delight — opposed to λύπη (grief) and πόνος (toil). This sense is rare in the NT but illuminates the root: χαίρω (to rejoice, be glad). Grace and joy share a root.

**The root connection χάρις / χαίρω is analytically significant:**
The LSJ makes explicit that charis derives from chairo (to rejoice, be glad). Grace and joy are not merely theologically related — they share a lexical root. This means that at the deepest semantic level, grace carries the note of gladness: it is favour that gladdens, the disposition of one who gives with delight. The NT theological sense strips away the reciprocal gratitude dimension (grace is unconditional) but retains the joy-root — grace as the glad giving of the greater toward the lesser.

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD020]: charis and chairo share a lexical root (χαρ-). Grace is the noun of the same root as joy/rejoicing. This is not merely a word-family connection — it is a semantic identity: grace as the disposition that rejoices in giving; grace received as that which produces joy in the recipient. For Session D: does the joy registry (if one exists) or rejoicing (Reg 132, which shares G5485 as its one xref term) encode this root-level connection? Is there a structural inner-being relationship between grace and joy that the root reveals — grace as the ground of joy, joy as the inner-being response to grace? The shared term G5485 in the xref signal with rejoicing (Reg 132) is not incidental — it is the root connection made visible in the data.

**NT semantic range of charis — four faces visible in the corpus:**

1. **Grace as divine disposition** — the settled inner character of God toward humanity (Eph 2:8, Rom 5:8). The most theologically weighty sense. Not a single act but a standing orientation.

2. **Grace as relational standing** — the condition of being in favour (Rom 5:2: "this grace in which we stand"). Grace as sphere rather than act. The human is not the agent but the inhabitant.

3. **Grace as gifted enablement** — grace as operative power equipping the person for specific function (Rom 12:6: "gifts that differ according to the grace given to us"; 1Cor 15:10: "his grace that is with me"). Here grace becomes the capacity-conferring donation.

4. **Grace as gracious quality of speech/conduct** — the chen sense carried into Greek (Lk 4:22; Col 4:6: "let your speech always be gracious"). Grace as the character quality of the gracious person.

**Internal tension:** Faces 1 and 3 create a theological tension that is analytically important. Grace as divine disposition (face 1) is God's standing orientation — it is not an act. Grace as gifted enablement (face 3) is a specific conferral that produces a capacity in the recipient. Are these the same thing? Or is face 3 the specific application of face 1 — divine disposition expressing itself in a concrete gift of capacity? This tension maps onto the inner-being question: is grace something the person receives once (standing) or continuously (enabling)?

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD021]: The four semantic faces of charis reveal a potential inner-being architecture: divine disposition (face 1) → relational standing created in the recipient (face 2) → specific capacity gifted into the person (face 3) → gracious character quality expressed outward (face 4). This is a possible sequential inner-being model — grace received at the spirit level becomes standing at the soul level becomes capacity in the person becomes character expressed through conduct. For Session D: does the programme's data across multiple registries support this sequential model? This may be the deepest structural question the grace registry raises for Session D.

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD022]: charis means "words of grace" in Lk 4:22 (gracious speech quality) and Col 4:6 (speech seasoned with grace). ### G5487 charitoō — to favour (2 occurrences)

**Mounce:** to give graciously, to show acts of kindness by freely giving; one highly favoured.

**LSJ:** χαριτόω — to show grace to any one; passive: to have grace shown one, to be highly favoured.

**Sense structure:** A causative/factitive form of charis — "to grace someone," to make them an object of grace. Only two NT uses:
- Lk 1:28: kecharitōmenē (perfect passive) — Mary as the one who has been graced, the perfected state of having received grace
- Eph 1:6: "to the praise of his glorious grace, which he has freely given us in the Beloved"

**Analytical observation:** The perfect passive participle in Lk 1:28 is theologically dense. The perfect tense names a completed action with ongoing results: Mary is not merely "currently receiving grace" but "stands in a state of having been graced." This is the clearest grammatical encoding of grace-as-standing (charis face 2) in the NT. The term does not appear in secular Greek — it is a biblical coinage. This indicates that the theological concept of being made the object of divine grace required a new word.

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD023]: charitoō is a biblical coinage — it does not appear in classical Greek. The need to create a new word for "to grace someone" suggests that the concept of being made the specific object of divine favour, in the full sense that Scripture encodes, exceeded what existing vocabulary could carry. For Session D: does the absence of this concept in classical Greek vocabulary reveal something about the uniqueness of the biblical inner-being claim about grace? Is the specifically biblical inner-being reality of standing-in-favour (as opposed to receiving-a-favour) a concept that required new language? This may be relevant to how Session D situates the grace registry relative to classical concepts of favour and benefaction.

### H2580 chen — favour (69 occurrences)

**Lexical structure:** No Mounce entry (Hebrew). The term is a noun from the root חנן (chanan — to be gracious, to show favour). Primary meaning: grace, favour — the goodwill disposition of one toward another, creating a relational bond that gives access and standing.

**Semantic range across 69 occurrences:**
- The find-favour idiom (מצא חן — matsa chen): "to find favour in the eyes of X" — the standard formula for discovering that someone is disposed favourably toward you (38 occurrences, approximately). This is the relational-standing sense.
- chen as quality of gracious speech/conduct: "gracious words" (Pro 22:11); charm/attractiveness (Pro 31:30). This is the character-quality sense.
- chen in divine-human contexts: God's chen toward Moses (Exo 33), the outpoured Spirit of grace (Zec 12:10). The theological weightiest uses.

**Root family note:** H2580 (chen) shares the CHEN root with: H2587 (chan.nun — gracious), H2603A (cha.nan — to be gracious), H8467 (te.chin.nah — supplication), H2594 (cha.ni.nah — favour), H2600 (chin.nam — for nothing/gratuitously). The root encompasses both the disposition (chen, chanan) and the plea that appeals to it (tachanum, techinah) and the structural logic of uncaused giving (chinnam — for nothing). This is a semantic family, not just a word family.

The full expression of grace is presented as future. What has been received is real but partial; the eschatological promise is "immeasurable riches." This is analytically significant: grace has a future orientation built into its inner-being structure — receiving grace now points forward to its fullness.

### ANCHOR: Rom 8:32 [group 5470-001 — charizō: divine giving]

*"He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?"*

**Annotation:** The verse is structured as an argument from greater to lesser: if God did not withhold the supreme gift (the Son), the inference is that no lesser gift will be withheld. The word "spare" (ouk epheisato) carries OT resonance — the same word used of Abraham not sparing Isaac (Gen 22:12,16, LXX). Grace here is not a disposition stated abstractly but an act narrated concretely: the not-sparing of what was most precious as the paradigm case of free giving. The phrase "with him graciously give us all things" (sun autō ta panta charisetai) is total: grace is not partial bestowal but the disposition that, having given everything, gives everything through the lens of that everything.

**Five standing questions:**

1. *Other inner-being states implied:* The "not sparing" implies sacrifice — the giver gives what costs. This connects to the Reg 123 (pride) shared anchor: the giver of grace does not hold back even what is most precious, the opposite of the self-preserving orientation of pride. Also: "for us all" implies inclusion — grace is directed toward a community, not merely an individual. The inner-being experience of being included in "us all" without distinction is itself a grace-constituted identity.

2. *Sequence or relationship encoded:* The argument structure encodes: divine self-giving (past, supreme) → inference of continuing grace (present and future). Grace is not a past event recalled — it is an ongoing orientation inferred from the supreme past act.

3. *Multiple registries implicated:* The verse implicates sacrifice (not sparing), gift/giving (all things), and the logic of inference (how will he not). The "all things" connects to blessing (Reg 194, cooccurrence 7 verses) — everything given in Christ as the scope of blessing. The "not spare" connects to sacrifice and to the cost-of-love question (Reg 103, love, cooccurrence 14 verses).

4. *Conspicuous absence:* The verse says nothing about the recipient's condition or merit — "for us" without qualification. The worthiness or unworthiness of the recipients is structurally absent. This is the non-merit logic stated by silence: the argument rests entirely on what God did, not on anything about "us."

5. *Structural inner-being relationship for Session D:* The verse encodes the inner logic of grace-as-inference: because the supreme gift was given, the lesser gifts follow necessarily. This is the inner-being assurance of grace — not a promise that might be kept but an inference from what has already happened. For Session D: is this inference-structure a pattern across the NT grace corpus? Do recipients of grace develop an inner-being confidence not based on their own standing but on the logic of what God has already done?

SESSION D POINTER [DIM-068-SD029]: Rom 8:32 encodes grace as inference — "how will he not also..." The supreme act of grace (not sparing the Son) becomes the ground of inner-being assurance about all other grace. This is a structural inner-being claim: once the supreme gift is received, all subsequent grace is not hoped for but inferred. The inner-being state this produces is not mere hope but confidence — a settled assurance derived from logic, not from feeling. ### ANCHOR: Eph 4:32 [group 5470-002 — charizō: human forgiving]

They are not presented as three separate commands but as a unified inner-being posture — the tender heart produces the kind conduct and the forgiving act. The grounding clause "as God in Christ forgave you" does not merely set the standard but names the source: the human capacity for forgiveness flows from having been forgiven. The sequence is divine forgiveness received → inner heart softened → kindness and tenderheartedness expressed → forgiveness extended.

**Five standing questions:**

The inner-being sequence is explicit: grace received changes the inner person (softens the heart) which then changes conduct. The hard heart that cannot forgive is the inner-being condition that has not fully received the forgiveness it has been given.

4. *Conspicuous absence:* The verse says nothing about whether the person being forgiven deserves it or has repented. "Forgiving one another" is not conditioned on the other person's repentance. This mirrors the divine pattern (not sparing, unconditional). The inner-being act of forgiveness is not reactive to the other's change but expressive of one's own received grace.

5. *Structural inner-being relationship:* The verse encodes the inner-being transmission of grace: received grace produces a changed inner person who then extends grace to others. This is the grace-to-kindness-to-forgiveness pipeline operating within the inner life. For Session D: is this pipeline — grace received → inner softening → grace extended — a programmatic inner-being sequence that the C17 cluster as a whole encodes? ### ANCHOR: Luk 1:28 [group 5471-001 — charitoō: bestowing grace]

*"And he came to her and said, 'Greetings, O favored one, the Lord is with you!'"*

**Annotation:** The perfect passive participle kecharitōmenē names Mary's condition before the angel speaks a word about what she will do. The grace precedes the call. She is not "the one who will be favoured" but "the one who has been and stands favoured." The accompanying statement — "the Lord is with you" — identifies the ground and the content of the grace: divine presence is what grace gives. Being in favour with God means God is present to you in a particular way. The verse also opens with "Greetings" (chairē — rejoice!), the chairo root: grace announced as joy.

**Five standing questions:**

1. *Other inner-being states implied:* The angel immediately says "do not be afraid" (Lk 1:30) — implying that Mary's inner response to the greeting was fear. Grace announced produces fear as the initial inner response before it produces receptivity. The fear is the creaturely recognition of the weight of what is being said. This connects to the grace-fear relationship — not fear instead of grace but fear as the first inner response to grace before it settles into peace.

2. *Sequence or relationship encoded:* Grace precedes calling (she is already kecharitōmenē before the announcement of her role). Grace-as-prior → announcement → fear → reassurance → receptivity ("let it be to me according to your word", Lk 1:38). The inner-being sequence from grace announcement to acceptance passes through fear.

3. *Multiple registries implicated:* Fear (likely Reg 105 or equivalent — not in cooccurrence data but implied by "do not be afraid"). Calling (Reg 19, cooccurrence 11 verses — Mary being called). Surrender/yielding (Reg 180, shared anchor Eph 2:8 — the posture of "let it be to me"). Also: "the Lord is with you" as divine presence — connects to anointing (Reg 6, cooccurrence 7 verses) and blessing (Reg 194, cooccurrence 7 verses).
