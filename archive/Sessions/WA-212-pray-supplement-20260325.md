## §2.8 — The Intercession Vocabulary (G1793 entugchanō, G1783 enteuxis)

### G1793 entugchanō — The Intercession Verb

Reading all five verses:

**Act 25:24** — "all who are present with us, you see this man about whom the whole Jewish people petitioned (entugchanon) me, both in Jerusalem and here, crying that he ought not to live any longer." This is the only non-prayer use in the set: entugchanō used for legal petition to a human authority. It is bleed from the pray vocabulary — the same word's secular register (petition to a magistrate). It does not belong with the prayer analysis, though it clarifies the word's root meaning: to fall in with someone, to approach someone on a matter, to bring a case before.

The remaining four verses are the programme's most concentrated intercession theology:

**Rom 8:27** — "the Spirit intercedes (entugchanei) for the saints according to the will of God." Already in the anchor verse analysis (§4.3 of the primary document). With the verb now present in the registry, the anchor verse is complete: entugchanō names the specific activity of the Spirit's prayer-within-the-person — not a general helping but a specific intercessory address to the Father on behalf of each person who prays.

**Rom 8:34** — "Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died — more than that, who was raised — who is at the right hand of God, who indeed is interceding (entugchanei) for us." The two divine intercessors appear in the same chapter, separated by seven verses: Spirit (v.27) and Christ (v.34), both using entugchanō. The significance of the pairing: the Spirit intercedes within the person (v.26 — "the Spirit helps us in our weakness"); Christ intercedes at the Father's right hand. The interior intercession (Spirit within) and the heavenly intercession (Christ at the throne) are the two poles of a single intercession reality encompassing the person from within and from above.

Christ's intercession is described as the ongoing activity of the resurrected and exalted Lord — it is not a past completed act but the present tense of his eternal life. The right hand position is not static honour but active intercession. This is the most concentrated statement in the NT of what the ascended Christ is currently doing.

**Heb 7:25** — "he is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession (entugchanein) for them." The most expansive intercession statement in the corpus. Three elements: "to the uttermost" — the scope of salvation is defined by the scope of the intercession; "always lives" — the intercession is perpetual and grounded in the resurrection life; "through him" — the access to God is through the interceding Christ. The Hebrews theology: Christ's session is intercession, and the intercession is the ground of unlimited salvation.

**Rom 11:2** — "Do you not know what the Scripture says of Elijah, how he appeals (entugchanei) to God against Israel?" The advocate/accuser polarity within a single term. Elijah intercedes against Israel — entugchanō used for accusation before God. The word's semantic range covers both directions: to intercede for (Rom 8:27,34; Heb 7:25) and to intercede against (Rom 11:2). The OT background is the prosecutor in the heavenly court (cf. Job 1-2, Zec 3:1 — the satan as accuser). Paul's point: even as Elijah accused Israel, God preserved a remnant. The negative intercession sets up the positive: God is not bound by the accuser's case.

**The structural insight from reading all five:** The entugchanō vocabulary establishes that intercession has a direction, not just a content. The Spirit intercedes for; Christ intercedes for; Elijah intercedes against. The advocate and the accuser use the same word. This means that intercession is the practice of bringing a case before God on behalf of another — whether as advocate or as accuser — and the gospel is that the two divine intercessors are permanently on the side of the believer (Rom 8:31-34).

### G1783 enteuxis — The Intercession Noun

**1Ti 2:1** — "I urge that supplications (deēseis), prayers (proseuchas), intercessions (enteuxeis), and thanksgivings (eucharistias) be made for all people."

The NT's most systematic prayer taxonomy. The fourfold classification:
- **deēseis** (G1162 — already extracted): specific petition for a need
- **proseuchas** (G4335 — already extracted): general prayer-address to God
- **enteuxeis** (G1783 — now extracted): intercession — prayer on behalf of another
- **eucharistias**: thanksgiving

The enteuxis category names the specifically intercessory dimension of prayer as a distinct type — not a subset of petition or praise but its own mode: the prayer that is entirely directed toward someone else's benefit. The phrase "for all people" (hyper pantōn anthrōpōn) immediately following the fourfold taxonomy names the scope of intercession: unrestricted by category, ethnicity, or standing. The universal scope of intercession is the NT's most democratic prayer statement — everyone is to be prayed for.

**1Ti 4:5** — "for it is made holy by the word of God and prayer (enteuxis)." Food sanctified by the word and enteuxis. Here enteuxis = the prayer of blessing/thanksgiving over a meal. This is enteuxis in its most domestic application: the table prayer. The word used for the Spirit's intercession within the person (G1793 entugchanō) and for the fourfold prayer taxonomy (G1783 enteuxis) is here applied to the blessing before eating. The most theologically elevated and the most domestically ordinary forms of intercession share the same vocabulary root — intercession reaches from the Spirit's inarticulate groanings to the family blessing over food.

## §2.9 — Tongues-Prayer (G1100 glōssa)

Reading all 46 glōssa verses establishes a clear distinction between primary pray vocabulary (~17 verses) and bleed (~29 verses). The analysis treats only the primary pray cluster.

### The Bleed Verses (not primary pray vocabulary)

Physical tongue: Mar 7:33,35 (healing of the deaf-mute's tongue), Luk 1:64 (Zechariah's tongue loosed), Luk 16:24 (Dives requesting water on his tongue), Act 2:26 (Psa 16:9 citation — "my tongue rejoiced").

Speech-ethics: Rom 3:13 (tongues used to deceive), Jam 1:26 (bridling the tongue), Jam 3:5,6,8 (the tongue as fire and poison), 1Pe 3:10 (keep the tongue from evil).

Language/nation groups: Rev 5:9; 7:9; 10:11; 11:9; 13:7; 14:6; 16:10; 17:15 (every tongue/language/nation — the eschatological multitude vocabulary). Rom 14:11; Phi 2:11 (every tongue shall confess — eschatological confession).

These bleed categories are not prayer vocabulary: they are physical organ, speech ethics, and language-group classification. PH2-212-008 flags these for Phase 2 sub-set tagging.

### The Primary Pray Vocabulary (~17 verses)

**Act 2:3-4** — "divided tongues as of fire appeared to them and rested on each one of them. And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance." Pentecost: the fire-tongues are a visual sign accompanying the Spirit-filling, and the speech-tongues (glōssai) are the Spirit's own utterance through the person. The Pentecost tongues are identified as known languages by the hearers (v.11 — "we hear them in our own tongues") — the Acts Pentecost pattern differs from the 1 Corinthians pattern (where tongues require interpretation and are unintelligible to outsiders).

**Act 10:46** — "they were hearing them speaking in tongues and extolling God." The Cornelius household: tongues as the evidence of Spirit-filling, alongside the praise of God. The two activities are simultaneous — tongues and extolling God are not sequential but concurrent. Tongues here is the Spirit-filling evidence vocabulary; its function is to demonstrate that the Holy Spirit has been poured out on the Gentiles (v.47).

**Act 19:6** — "the Holy Spirit came on them, and they began speaking in tongues and prophesying." Ephesians: same Spirit-filling pattern. Tongues + prophecy as the paired Spirit-manifestations.

**1Co 12:10,28,30** — tongues as one of the spiritual gifts in the body of Christ. Listed alongside healing, miracles, prophecy, discernment. The gift-list context: tongues is a distributed gift, given to some but not all (v.30 — "do all speak with tongues?"). The 1 Corinthians pattern distinguishes tongues as one gift among many, governed by order and interpretation.

**1Co 13:1,8** — "if I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am a noisy gong." "As for tongues, they will cease." The love-chapter situates tongues: speaking in tongues without love is emptied of value. Tongues will cease — they are a gift for the present age, not the age to come. The cessation of tongues is the most contested exegetical point in the corpus — it generates a PH2 flag (see §10 below).

**1Co 14:2-39** — the tongues-prayer chapter. The most concentrated and analytically rich cluster:

14:2 — **"one who speaks in a tongue speaks not to men but to God"** — the definitional statement. The directionality of tongues-prayer is unambiguous: it is addressed exclusively to God, not to the congregation. This is what distinguishes it from prophecy: prophecy speaks to men; tongues speaks to God. Tongues is prayer by definition — the speech-act that is entirely and exclusively Godward.

14:4 — "the one who speaks in a tongue builds up himself (heauton oikodomeō)." Tongues-prayer has a personal edification function — it builds the inner person of the one who prays. This is the Spirit-prayer's personal dimension: what happens in the person's spirit when they pray in tongues is described as construction (oikodomeō — the same verb used for building the church). The personal inner life is built up by spirit-prayer.

14:13 — "one who speaks in a tongue should pray (proseuchesthō) that he may interpret." Tongues-speaker directed to use proseuchomai to ask for the interpretation gift — prayer addressing the limitation of tongues in community. The tongues-speaker is not passive; they are directed to pray for what is needed to make their tongues-prayer useful for others.

14:14 — the anchor verse's companion: "if I pray in a tongue, my spirit prays but my mind is unfruitful." Already analysed in §7.3. With glōssa now in the registry: the tongue is the physical instrument through which the spirit's prayer is expressed. The spirit's prayer uses the bodily tongue as its vehicle while the mind (nous) remains unengaged with the content.

14:18 — "I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you." Paul's personal testimony. He practices tongues-prayer extensively — more than the entire Corinthian church combined. This testimony is given in the context of regulations limiting tongues in public worship. The implication: Paul's extensive tongues practice is private, personal prayer, not public performance. He values it for his own inner life while carefully regulating its communal expression.

14:39 — "do not forbid speaking in tongues." The closing directive balances the regulations of the chapter: tongues is not to be prohibited. The regulat and the permission stand together — order without prohibition, freedom without chaos.

### The Two-Modes of Prayer Confirmed by Glōssa

With G1100 glōssa now extracted, the two-modes structure of prayer established in §7.3 is fully confirmed by the vocabulary:

**Spirit-mode (praying in/with the pneuma):** The tongue (glōssa) as the instrument of spirit-prayer. 1Co 14:2 — "speaks not to men but to God." 1Co 14:14 — "my spirit prays." The spirit-mode bypasses the cognitive-formative faculty and speaks directly to God.

**Mind-mode (praying with the nous):** 1Co 14:15 — "I will pray with my mind also." The mind-prayer uses articulate, comprehensible language addressed to God. It is intelligible to others and builds up the congregation as well as the person.

The two modes are the pray vocabulary's most concrete expression of the WA-184 two-modes structure (spirit/soul, pneumatikos/psuchikos). Prayer is the activity where that structural distinction becomes most practically visible — the person can pray in one mode or the other, and Paul's instruction is to do both.

## §2.10 — Earnest Dawn-Seeking (H7836 sha.char)

Reading all 12 sha.char verses: the primary pray vocabulary is approximately 6 verses; approximately 6 are bleed (general diligence: Pro 11:27 seeking good, Pro 13:24 diligent discipline, Job 24:5 donkeys seeking food, Pro 7:15 woman seeking lover, Pro 1:28 seeking Wisdom too late). PH2-212-009 flags these for Phase 2 sub-set tagging.

The primary pray vocabulary:

**Psa 63:1** — "O God, you are my God; earnestly I seek you (sha.char); my soul thirsts for you; my flesh faints for you, as in a dry and weary land where there is no water." The most important sha.char verse and the programme's most somatically complete prayer verse.

The sha.char root means to seek at dawn — to make the first movement of the day toward God, before anything else. The title situates this as David's prayer in the wilderness of Judah — the actual desert, where water is absent. The metaphor and the reality coincide: the physical thirst (flesh faints for water) and the spiritual thirst (soul thirsts for God) are the same condition. The body that is thirsty and the soul that longs for God are expressing the same inner movement outward.

Three registers of total-person seeking: the soul (ne.phesh — desire-thirst), the flesh (ba.sar — bodily faintness), the dawn-seeking (sha.char — the spirit's first orientation of the day). All three converge on the same act: the earnest seeking of God. This is the sha.char vocabulary's most concentrated expression of the spirit-to-Spirit framing: the whole person — body, soul, spirit — oriented toward God at dawn.

**Isa 26:9** — "my soul yearns for you in the night; my spirit within me earnestly seeks you (sha.char)." The soul (ne.phesh) and the spirit (ru.ach) are paired — two dimensions of the inner person both directed toward God. The night-context and the dawn-root (sha.char) together: the seeking begins in the night-before-dawn, the reaching toward God that precedes the actual rising of light. Prayer as the inner person's orientation before the day begins.

**Psa 78:34** — "when he killed them, they sought him; they repented and sought God earnestly (sha.char)." The crisis-generated sha.char — earnest seeking that arises from confrontation with mortality and judgment. Not the joyful dawn-seeking of Psa 63 but the urgent turning-back seeking of a people under God's hand. Sha.char covers both the voluntary first-movement of the day and the compelled returning under pressure.

**Job 8:5** — "if you will seek God (sha.char) and plead (cha.nan) with the Almighty for mercy." Sha.char paired with H2603A cha.nan (already extracted): the earnest seeking and the imploring for grace as simultaneous acts. This pairing is analytically significant — the sha.char seeking and the cha.nan imploring are not sequential but co-present. The earnest seeker is also the humble supplicant.

**Hos 5:15** — "until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face; in their distress they will earnestly seek (sha.char) me." The eschatological sha.char — the future turning of Israel toward God. "Seek my face" (ba.qash pa.nay — the seeking of the divine face from the desire registry's ba.qash vocabulary) is parallel to sha.char. The eschatological prayer is earnest seeking in distress — the sha.char driven by the recognition of need and guilt.

**Pro 8:17** — "I love those who love me, and those who seek me diligently (sha.char) find me." Wisdom speaking: the sha.char directed at Wisdom receives a response — the diligent seeker finds. While this is Wisdom vocabulary rather than directly prayer vocabulary, the parallel to Hos 5:15 and Psa 63:1 is clear: the one who earnestly seeks finds. The seeking that is sha.char is the seeking that is answered.

**The sha.char pattern:** Dawn-seeking is the prayer vocabulary's posture of first-orientation. Before the day's activities and claims begin, the spirit turns toward God — not because the spirit has resolved all difficulties or achieved a settled peace but because the spirit's first movement is Godward. Psa 63:1 is in a desert; the flesh is fainting; the seeking is nonetheless earnest. This is the sha.char vocabulary's most important inner-being insight: the earnest seeking of God is not produced by favourable circumstances but by the spirit's orientation regardless of circumstances.

## Section 11: Session D Observation Log (Supplement)

**Additional Session D observation — The two divine intercessors.**
Rom 8:27 (Spirit) and Rom 8:34 (Christ) in the same chapter, seven verses apart, using the same verb (entugchanō): the Spirit intercedes within the person from weakness; Christ intercedes from the Father's right hand in power. Together they constitute a complete intercession structure — the person is held in prayer from within (Spirit's inarticulate groaning) and from above (Christ's perpetual high-priestly intercession). This pairing should be examined in Session D as the pray registry's most important theological contribution to the programme's overall anthropology: the person who cannot pray adequately (v.26 — "we do not know what to pray for") is the person who is nevertheless most completely prayed for.

**Additional Session D observation — Tongues-prayer as the most direct spirit-to-God speech.**
1Co 14:2 — "speaks not to men but to God" — is the most explicit directional statement in the pray vocabulary. Tongues-prayer is exclusively and entirely Godward. Combined with 14:14 (my spirit prays) and Rom 8:26 (groanings too deep for words): the pray vocabulary presents a spectrum of spirit-level communication, from the structured but insufficient prayer of the unguided person, through the Spirit's own inarticulate groaning, to the person's own spirit-prayer in tongues. All three are at the spirit level; all three bypass or transcend the mind's formative capacity; all three are addressed directly to God.

**Additional Session D observation — Sha.char as the dawn-posture of prayer.**
The prayer vocabulary contains a time-signature: sha.char (dawn-seeking), the three-times-daily pattern (Dan 6:10 — morning, noon, evening via Psa 55:17), and the continuous prayer (1Th 5:17 — pray without ceasing). These are not competing instructions but a structure: the day begins with sha.char (earnest first-orientation), is punctuated by structured prayer, and is sustained by continuous inner orientation (si.ach). The pray vocabulary describes not just what prayer is but when and how it structures the inner life's relationship to time.
