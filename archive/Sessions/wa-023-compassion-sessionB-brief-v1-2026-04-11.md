## 2. Meaning Findings

**Primary semantic picture:** The compassion vocabulary in Scripture divides into four root families, each carrying a distinct mode of compassion:

1. **RACHAM/SPLANCHN — visceral, somatic compassion.** The Hebrew womb-root (*ra.cham/re.chem*) and Greek bowel-root (*splanchnizō/splanchnon*) name compassion as a physically-located inner movement from the body's deepest interior. The etymological direction is unusual: the abstract (compassion, *rachamim*) is named after the concrete (womb, *rechem*), not vice versa. This means the somatic grounding is constitutive of the word's meaning, not a later metaphorical overlay.

2. **CHESED/CHANNUM — covenantal, characterological compassion.** The *chesed* family describes compassion as a settled disposition of loyal relationship — a character quality that persists through time and underlies all of God's dealings with his people. The *chan.nun* adjective names the divine character attribute from which compassion flows.

3. **SYM-PATHĒ — participatory, solidarity compassion.** The Greek *sym-* compounds (*sumpatheō*, *sumpaschō*, *sumpathēs*, *metriopatheō*) name compassion as genuine entry into shared experience. This family is concentrated in the NT epistles and introduces a Christological ground: Christ sympathises because he has lived in the conditions of human weakness.

4. **ELEEIN/CHUS — relational, responsive compassion.** *Eleēmosunē* (almsgiving — the institutionalised form of compassion), *eleeinos* (the pitiable condition that calls for compassion), *chus* (pity as the instinctive sparing impulse). This family names compassion at the level of its social and relational expression.

**Significant semantic tensions:**

- *Chus* (to pity) appears *most frequently in prohibition contexts* — "your eye shall not pity." In 14 of 24 occurrences God or the law commands the withholding of pity. This establishes that compassion is the *default inner orientation* from which justice must actively deviate — a major architectural observation about the moral inner life.
- H2617B *che.sed* (shame) names the negative semantic pole of the *chesed* root — covenantal faithfulness and shame/disgrace share a root. The same character quality that at its best is steadfast love can become disgrace when violated.
- *Splanchnizō* spans four semantic domains (physical suffering, spiritual destitution, hunger, relational/moral contexts) — unified not by the type of need but by the visceral, inward, movement-producing quality of the response.
- *Eleēmosunē* represents a semantic development from classical Greek *pity/mercy* to LXX/NT *almsgiving* — compassion institutionalised as its normative social form.

**Senses underrepresented or missing from Session C:**
- The eschatological dimension of *sumpaschō* (Rom 8:17 — suffering-with-Christ as condition of glorification)
- The four-domain breadth of *splanchnizō*
- The etymological direction of the RACHAM root (abstract from concrete)
- The *eleēmosunē* semantic development (pity → almsgiving)

## 3. Divine Dimension Summary

**Dominant pattern:** God models, enacts, and in judgment temporarily withdraws the compassion he calls human beings to exercise. Five stages identifiable across the corpus:

1. **Character** (chan.nun, che.sed): Compassion as God's settled attribute before any act — the Exod 34:6 formula.
2. **Act** (splanchnizō, oikteirō, ni.chum, chem.lah): God acts from that character — moved, stirred, showing pity.
3. **Withdrawal** (chus in Ezek repeatedly; cha.ni.nah in Jer 16:13): Temporary judicial withdrawal — compassion suspended, not destroyed.
4. **Demand** (che.sed in Hos 6:6; Mic 6:8): God requires the same compassion from human beings.
5. **Correspondence** (cha.sad in 2 Sam 22:26): God mirrors the human's compassionate disposition.

**Key divine-dimension findings:**

- The divine withdrawal of pity (chus) is stated seven times in Ezekiel, each in God's first person. The repetition underscores that real compassion (not reflex sentiment) is being suspended — it is felt, weighty, deliberate.
- Hos 11:8 presents God's inner life as *deliberative* — the ni.chum warmth contends with and overcomes the judgment impulse. This is not compassion as a static attribute but as a dynamic inner event.
- Isa 54:8 establishes a temporal asymmetry: judgment is a *moment*; steadfast love is *everlasting*. Compassion is the more fundamental divine characteristic.
- G4697 *splanchnizō* is used in the Gospels almost exclusively of Jesus — the gut-level compassion is the distinctive mark of his earthly ministry. In one parable (Luke 10:33) the Samaritan is the *splanchnizō* subject, making compassion the defining quality of the one who correctly answers "who is my neighbour?"

**Divine-human relationship:** The human characteristic is *derivative and demanded* — received from God as a model, commanded by God as a requirement, and grounded by Jesus's incarnation as an achieved capacity (Heb 4:15).

**Eschatological dimension:** Rom 8:17 (*sumpaschō*) — suffering-with-Christ as condition of glorification. Jas 5:11 (*polusplanchnos*) — God's deep compassion is the final perspective on suffering and endurance. Both underrepresented in Session C.

## 4. Somatic Signature

**Primary somatic location: the body's interior spaces.**

The womb (RACHAM family), the bowels/gut (SPLANCHN family), and the heart (ni.chum/chan.nun contexts) are all interior organs. Compassion in this vocabulary originates *inside out* — from the body's most interior spaces outward to action.

This sub-pattern spans the divine-human boundary: both God and humans are described as *moved within* by compassion using the same body-language.

## 5. Spirit-Soul-Body Provisional Classification

**Classification: Soul-body interface**

Compassion originates at a somatic level (womb, gut, heart) but is felt and experienced as an inner-being orientation before producing outward action. It is neither purely spiritual (though God can produce and command it) nor purely bodily (the physical stirring is the vehicle of an inner-being movement). The soul-body interface captures the way compassion is simultaneously felt as a bodily reality and experienced as an inner-being orientation.

**Caveat:** The CHESED/CHANNUM vocabulary (characterological compassion) has minimal somatic evidence and sits more naturally at the *soul level* (moral character) without strong body-interface signals. The registry may require a two-classification model: somatic compassion (RACHAM/SPLANCHN) = soul-body interface; characterological compassion (CHESED) = moral character (soul level).

**Confidence: Medium.**

**Christological note:** G4834 *sumpatheō* (Heb 4:15) — Christ's capacity to sympathise was *acquired* through incarnate human experience. This suggests that in its fullest form, compassion requires embodiment. This has potential implications for the spirit-soul-body framework beyond this registry.
