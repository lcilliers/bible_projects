# WA-073 | Guilt | Phase 1 Analysis

**Registry ID:** 073
**Source List:** High Confidence
**Date:** 2026-03-07
**Phase:** 1 — Linguistic and Contextual Data

---

## 1. Term Overview

The guilt word set is structurally different from most in the programme: where words like distress or anger have a spatial or emotional metaphor at their root, guilt in the biblical vocabulary is fundamentally a legal and relational category. The term inventory covers 16 entries, but the analytical centre of gravity rests on a small cluster of Hebrew terms (a.sham, a.von, cha.ta) and one pivotal Greek term (suneidēsis) that together define the inner-being dimensions of guilt across both Testaments.

The word set also has two significant missing terms — enochos (G1777, guilty) and katakrima (G2631, condemnation) — which are primary terms in the source file's own classification but lack STEP blocks. Their absence is noted throughout and creates a gap specifically in the NT legal-guilt register.

## 2. Root Family Analysis

### 2a. The a.sham Root Cluster

The a.sham root (H0816/H0817/H0818/H0819) is the most tightly integrated cluster in the guilt set. Four distinct words share the root, each covering a facet of the guilt experience:

**a.sham H0816** (verb, to be guilty, 35x) functions as the verb of guilt-recognition. The most distinctive feature of this verb is that many of its Levitical instances use the pattern: a person performs an action — often without knowing it was wrong — and then realises their guilt (Lev 5:2–4: he becomes unclean... and it is hidden from him... when he comes to know it, and realises his guilt). The Niphal (to suffer punishment) and Hiphil (to declare guilty) extend the stem range to cover external judgment, but the Qal's primary use is for the inner moment of guilt-recognition. Gen 42:21 is the most significant CORE verse: the Joseph brothers, confronted with distress they cannot escape, say to one another — in truth we are guilty (a.shem adjective, H0818) concerning our brother, in that we saw the distress of his soul when he begged us. The verb a.sham thus names a retrospective inner process: the recognition of one's own wrongdoing, sometimes precipitated by its consequences.

**a.sham H0817** (noun, guilt offering, 46x) is the cultic term built on the same root. The most exegetically significant CORE instance is Isa 53:10, where the Servant's soul (nefesh) is described as making an offering for guilt (a.sham). This is the only occurrence in the entire a.sham cluster where the guilt-offering is internal — located in the Servant's own inner person — rather than an external animal. It connects the cultic vocabulary directly to the inner-being question of how guilt is borne and addressed.

**ash.mah H0819** (noun, guiltiness, 19x) is the abstract noun for the guilt-state itself. Its most concentrated CORE cluster is Ezra's confession in Ezr 9:6–15, where ash.mah names the accumulated national guilt that Ezra cannot bear to lift his face before God because of — our guilt (ash.mah) has mounted up to the heavens. The word here names a quantity of guilt that has a weight and a height, and it produces the somatic shame response of averted eyes and bent body. The Ezra passage is the clearest OT example of guilt as an experienced inner burden with physical expression.

**a.shem H0818** (adjective, guilty, 3x) is the smallest entry but contains one of the richest single verses in the entire word set: Gen 42:21, where the brothers' self-recognition — in truth we are guilty — is both the named state and the explanation of their present distress. The verse is explicit about the connection between past guilt, the victim's inner distress (they saw the distress of his soul), and the present suffering of the guilty parties. Guilt is retrospective, relational, and causally connected to present experience.

**Within-root differentiation:** The four a.sham entries show genuine differentiation in verse context, not lexical overlap. H0816 (verb) clusters around the act of incurring or recognising guilt. H0817 (noun) clusters around the cultic remedy for guilt. H0819 (noun) clusters around the accumulated weight of guilt. H0818 (adjective) names the person in the guilt-state. Together they cover the full cycle: incurring guilt → being in the guilt-state → the weight of that state → the cultic/Christological address of it.

### 2b. The a.von Root

a.von (H5771G, 233x) is the largest term in the Hebrew guilt set and arguably the most semantically rich word in the entire programme to date. Derived from a.vah (to twist, to pervert), it names iniquity as the twisting or perversion of what is right, and simultaneously carries three semantic layers in many of its occurrences: the act of wrongdoing, the guilt-state resulting from it, and the punishment/consequence of that state.

The three-layer structure is visible in paradigmatic verses:

- Lev 5:17 (if anyone sins... though he did not know it, then realises his guilt, he shall bear his iniquity) — here the realisation of guilt (Qal recognition) and the bearing of iniquity (as both the state and its consequence) are both present.
- Psa 38:4 (my iniquities have gone over my head; like a heavy burden, they are too heavy for me) — the iniquity is named as a weight over the head and a burden too heavy for the body.
- Isa 53:5, 6 (he was crushed for our iniquities... the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all) — the iniquity is transferred as a weight from the many to the Servant; here a.von is simultaneously the act, the guilt, and the consequential penalty.

The Psalms CORE cluster (Psa 25:11; 31:10; 32:2, 5; 38:4, 18; 40:12; 51:2, 5, 9; 130:3) is the deepest OT evidence for guilt as an inner-being state. Psa 38 is the most extended: the psalmist's iniquities produce physical deterioration (strength fails, loins are filled with burning, my bones have no soundness), social isolation (friends stand aloof), and an inner moral self-assessment (I am sorry for my sin). The three-layer structure of a.von is fully expressed here: the act, the guilt that produces somatic symptoms, and the anticipated punishment.

The Ezekiel individual-responsibility cluster (Eze 18:17–20, 30) is notable for the way it uses a.von in explicit personal moral accountability: the soul who sins shall die; the son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father. This passage represents a deliberate theological correction of the collective iniquity-transmission framework (Exo 20:5) — a.von here becomes the possession of the individual inner person, not an inherited corporate burden.

### 2c. The cha.ta Root

The cha.ta verb (H2403, 238x) is the primary OT verb for sinning. Two features of its verse set are analytically significant:

**The confession sequence.** The cha.ta verb appears repeatedly in first-person confession formulas: I have sinned (Exo 9:27; Num 22:34; 2Sa 12:13; Psa 41:4; Dan 9:5; Neh 1:6). The formula is a named inner-being act — the verbal acknowledgement of one's guilt before God or another person. David's I have sinned against the Lord in 2Sa 12:13 is the canonical OT example of an individual's verbal guilt-confession, and Nathan's response (the Lord has put away your sin) establishes the relational framework: confession directed to God produces forgiveness. The cha.ta confession formula is thus a sequence: sin → guilt-awareness → named confession → divine response.

**The causative Hiphil.** The Hiphil form (to cause to sin, to lead into sin) appears extensively in the Kings narrative with Jeroboam as subject: he made Israel to sin. This usage creates a category of derivative guilt — the guilt of the one who leads others into sin. The verse set for the Jeroboam pattern has been classified PERIPHERAL because no inner guilt state of Jeroboam is named; the verses are historical-narrative rather than inner-being focused. However, the Hiphil usage is significant for Phase 2: it raises the question of how the biblical vocabulary handles transferred or induced guilt as distinct from owned guilt.

**The Ezekiel individual accountability text** (Eze 18:4, 20: the soul who sins shall die) uses cha.ta to assert that sinning is the act of the individual soul (nefesh) — not a collective or inherited state. The verb here is applied directly to the nefesh, making cha.ta one of the few sin-verbs that is explicitly indexed to the inner person.

### 2d. The Greek Legal Cluster: aitia, aitios, and the missing terms

**aitia (G0156, 20x)** and **aitios (G0159, 5x)** function primarily in the legal-guilt register. Their most significant CORE concentration is in the Johannine passion narrative: Pilate's repeated pronouncement I find no guilt (aitia / aitios) in him (Joh 18:38; 19:4, 19:6; Luk 23:4, 23:14, 23:22; Act 13:28). In these verses, legal guilt as an objective verdict is explicitly denied in the case of Jesus. This creates an important structural asymmetry in the guilt vocabulary: the Greek legal terms (aitia, aitios) are used to proclaim innocence, not guilt, in the most prominent NT applications. The legal register that these terms represent — external verdict, objective charge — is employed by Pilate's declarations to frame Jesus as the one who carries no legal guilt even as he bears the guilt of others.

**enochos (G1777)** and **katakrima (G2631)** are absent from the source file's STEP blocks. This is a significant data gap. The source file's own note identifies katakrima (Rom 8:1, no katakrima in Christ Jesus) as the theological resolution of guilt, and enochos (Mat 5:21-22) as the primary NT legal liability term. Their absence means the inner legal register of NT guilt is represented only by the aitia/aitios trial narrative cluster and cannot be cross-referenced to the Pauline doctrinal resolution at the level of verse data in Phase 1.

### 2e. Conscience: suneidēsis

**suneidēsis (G4893, 30x)** is the most important NT inner-being term in the guilt set and has no direct Hebrew equivalent in this word set. The word means shared knowledge — an inward knowing that monitors one's own moral state and actions. Its verse set is entirely inner-being focused and covers the full range of conscience states: clear/good (Act 23:1; 24:16; 1Ti 1:5; 2Ti 1:3; Heb 13:18), weak (1Cor 8:7, 10, 12), defiled (Tit 1:15; 1Pe 3:21), seared (1Ti 4:2), evil (Heb 10:22), cleansed from dead works by Christ's blood (Heb 9:14).

The function of suneidēsis in the NT guilt vocabulary is to name the inner monitoring faculty that registers guilt. Unlike a.von (which names the guilt-weight as a reality the person carries) or a.sham (which names the moment of guilt-recognition), suneidēsis names the persistent internal organ of moral self-assessment. Rom 2:15 is the most significant CORE verse: the law is written on the heart, while the conscience also bears witness, and conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse. Here suneidēsis functions as an inner judiciary — the internal voice that prosecutes or acquits.

The Hebrews cluster (Heb 9:9, 14; 10:2, 22) is the most theologically developed use of suneidēsis in the NT: the blood of Christ purifies the conscience from dead works (Heb 9:14); worshippers whose sins have been truly dealt with would no longer have consciousness of sins (Heb 10:2); hearts sprinkled clean from an evil conscience (Heb 10:22). Hebrews treats the conscience as the inner site where the guilt-problem is finally addressed — a cult that cannot cleanse the conscience (Heb 9:9) fails at the level of inner-being; the sacrifice of Christ succeeds because it reaches the conscience.

The 1Ti 4:2 seared conscience is a notable outlier: a conscience that has been cauterised to the point of no longer registering guilt. This is a deteriorated state — the inner moral-monitoring faculty has been disabled by repeated suppression or rejection of its voice.

### 2f. chin.nam and the category of unjust accusation

**chin.nam (H2600, 32x)** is structurally unusual in the guilt set: it is not a guilt term but its opposite — the term for groundlessness, for being without cause. Its inclusion in the guilt set reflects the way the biblical vocabulary handles the question of false guilt or unjust accusation. The CORE verses reveal that chin.nam is used for the accusation of the innocent (those who hate me without cause, Psa 69:4; princes persecute me without cause, Psa 119:161) and for the theological question of whether suffering is deserved (Job 1:9: does Job fear God for no reason? Job 2:3: he still holds fast his integrity, although you incited me against him to destroy him without reason). The Job cluster is significant: the entire book of Job turns on the question of whether Job's suffering is chin.nam — for no cause — or whether it is the consequence of guilt. The satan's challenge and God's vindication frame this as the central question of the narrative.

1Sa 25:31 is particularly significant: Abigail asks David to have no cause of grief or pangs of conscience for having shed blood without cause — here chin.nam is directly connected to conscience (and anticipated guilt) that would arise from a causeless act. The phrase pangs of conscience renders the Hebrew colloquially; the underlying text refers to the stumbling of the heart. This is the closest the OT comes to naming anticipated guilt as a motivator for restraint.

## 3. Verse-Level Patterns

### 3a. The confession sequence

The guilt word set reveals a structural confession sequence that runs throughout the OT individual guilt narratives:

1. Act (cha.ta, a.von — the sin or iniquity)
2. Inner-weight (a.von as burden, ash.mah as mounting accumulation)
3. Recognition (a.sham — realising guilt; a.shem — being in the guilt-state)
4. Named confession (I have sinned, cha.ta in first-person formula)
5. Divine response (forgiveness, lifting/removing of a.von via na.sa)

This sequence is clearest in Psa 32 (I acknowledged my sin, I did not cover my iniquity, I said I will confess my transgressions, and you forgave the iniquity of my sin) and in 2Sa 12:13 (David said to Nathan, I have sinned against the Lord — Nathan: the Lord has also put away your sin). The sequence is not merely theological: it traces a movement through inner states, from the weight of unacknowledged guilt to the relief of forgiveness.

### 3b. a.von as somatic burden

The a.von verse set in Psalms shows guilt producing physical symptoms: Psa 31:10 (strength fails because of my iniquity; my bones waste away), Psa 38:4–18 (bones have no soundness; loins filled with burning; I am utterly bowed down and prostrate; my heart throbs; my strength fails). This is the most concentrated somatic guilt evidence in the entire word set. The body registers the guilt that the nefesh carries. The inner moral state is not confined to the soul but pervades the body — a direct connection between guilt-as-inner-state and physical deterioration.

### 3c. Ezra's confession as a model of embodied communal guilt

Ezr 9:6–15 stands as the most developed portrayal of guilt as a named inner experience in the OT. Ezra's body is physically prostrated (he sat appalled until the evening sacrifice, Ezr 9:4–5); he describes himself as ashamed and unable to lift his face; he names the guilt as having mounted up to the heavens; he is unable to stand before God because of the guilt. The passage is unique in the OT for its sustained, first-person description of the phenomenology of guilt — the way guilt is experienced in the body and the soul of the one carrying it, even when it is primarily a corporate/national guilt for which Ezra himself is not personally responsible.

## 4. Open Questions

**OQ-073-01 — The absence of an OT equivalent to suneidēsis**
The NT introduces suneidēsis — the conscience as a persistent internal faculty of moral self-monitoring — as the primary inner-being instrument through which guilt is registered, maintained, and addressed. The OT guilt word set has no direct equivalent term. The closest OT functionality is the a.sham recognition pattern (realising guilt when confronted with evidence) and the somatic guilt symptoms in Psa 38 and Ezr 9 (physical and emotional consequences of carried guilt). But neither of these is a named inner faculty — they are states or events, not a persistent monitoring organ. **Flag for Phase 2:** What does the emergence of suneidēsis as an explicit inner-being faculty in the NT imply about the development of the inner person's relationship to moral knowledge? Is the conscience a new anthropological category in the NT, or does it make explicit what the OT presupposed?

**OQ-073-02 — a.von's three semantic layers: guilt as act, state, and consequence simultaneously**
The structure of a.von uniquely encodes act, guilt-state, and punishment/consequence as a single semantic field. This means that in the OT understanding of guilt, the categories that Western moral philosophy and jurisprudence tend to separate (the wrong act, the subjective guilt, the objective penalty) are lexically fused. The person who commits iniquity is simultaneously in the iniquity, guilty of the iniquity, and carrying the consequence of the iniquity, all in a single Hebrew word. **Flag for Phase 2:** What does this semantic fusion imply for the Framework B understanding of guilt as an inner state? Does the OT refuse to locate guilt purely in the inner person because it understands guilt as ontological (bound up with one's condition before God and its consequences) rather than psychological?

**OQ-073-03 — na.sa: the same root covers both bearing and removing guilt**
na.sa (to lift) is used for bearing iniquity as a weight (the guilt-carrier model, Lev 5:1; Isa 53:12) and for lifting/removing iniquity as forgiveness (the guilt-remover model, Psa 32:5; Mic 7:18; Zec 3:4). The Hebrew conceptualises guilt-removal as the same physical act as guilt-bearing, but with the direction reversed: one person lifts the guilt onto themselves so that another is relieved of the weight. Isa 53:11 combines both: the Servant bears the iniquities and makes many to be accounted righteous. **Flag for Phase 2:** Does the na.sa model of guilt-transfer and guilt-lifting provide the conceptual framework that the NT draws on for atonement, and does it illuminate how the Pauline language of Christ bearing sin (2Cor 5:21; Gal 1:4) connects to the OT inner-being experience of guilt as a carried weight?

**OQ-073-04 — The missing terms: enochos and katakrima**
The absence of STEP blocks for enochos (G1777) and katakrima (G2631) in the source file means that the NT legal-guilt register (being held in, liable to judgment) and the NT condemnation-resolution register (no katakrima in Christ, Rom 8:1) are not represented at the verse-analysis level in Phase 1. The source file itself identifies katakrima in Rom 8:1 as the theological resolution of guilt — the most important NT resolution statement. **Flag for Phase 2:** When the full enochos and katakrima verse sets are accessible, how do they interact with the suneidēsis data (conscience cleansed, Heb 9:14) to form the NT's complete inner-being guilt-resolution framework?

**OQ-073-05 — Gen 42:21 as an intersection of guilt and distress**
The Joseph brothers' confession in Gen 42:21 is the only verse in the entire guilt word set that explicitly names both guilt (a.shem — in truth we are guilty) and the victim's inner distress (they saw the distress of his soul — using tsa.rah, the CORE distress term from Registry 051). The verse also names the causal chain: their guilt arises from what they did to Joseph's soul, and their present distress is explicitly connected to that past guilt. **Flag for Phase 2:** Does the intersection of guilt and distress in Gen 42:21 illuminate a structural link between the two registries — guilt as the source of distress, distress as the experience that confronts the guilty — and does this link have implications for the Framework B model of how inner states relate to one another?

**OQ-073-06 — OT/NT register difference: guilt as weight versus guilt as indwelling power**
In the OT, guilt (a.von) is consistently imaged as a weight carried by the person — something borne, accumulated, and potentially transferred or lifted. The dominant metaphors are vertical (guilt mounting up, too heavy, going over the head) and burdens carried on the body. In Paul's letters, hamartia is imaged as an indwelling power that rules, enslaves, seizes opportunity, deceives, and dwells in the members (Rom 7:8–23). The OT guilt weight is external to the person's will even if it is borne internally; the Pauline sin-power is a principle within the person that acts against the person's own will and knowledge. **Flag for Phase 2:** Does the shift from guilt-as-weight to sin-as-indwelling-power represent an anthropological development — a deeper penetration of the guilt-category into the inner person — or is it a theological reframing that does not alter the underlying inner-being structure?

**OQ-073-07 — The seared conscience (1Ti 4:2) as the deteriorated guilt-faculty**
The seared conscience in 1Ti 4:2 describes the end-state of a moral faculty that has been repeatedly suppressed or rejected. The conscience no longer registers guilt because it has been cauterised. This is a directional deterioration: the NT describes the conscience as capable of being good, weak, defiled, evil, and seared — representing a spectrum from functional clarity to complete dysfunction. The OT has no direct equivalent to this deterioration spectrum because it lacks the term suneidēsis. The closest OT concept is the hardening vocabulary (qa.shah, cha.zaq in Exodus and Isaiah), but that hardening is associated with God's action or national apostasy rather than individual moral faculty deterioration. **Flag for Phase 2:** Does the seared conscience represent a distinctive NT anthropological category for the inner person whose capacity to register guilt has been permanently damaged — and does this have implications for how the programme understands the relationship between ongoing sin and inner-being capacity?

**OQ-073-08 — The Isa 53 guilt matrix: a.von, a.sham, and na.sa in a single passage**
Isa 53 brings together three of the primary guilt terms in a single text: a.von (iniquity, vv. 5, 6, 11), a.sham (guilt offering, v. 10), and na.sa (bear, v. 12). The Servant's soul makes a guilt offering (a.sham, H0817), he bears the iniquity of all (na.sa + a.von, v. 11), and the three-term cluster creates the most concentrated guilt vocabulary in the OT outside the legal sections of Leviticus. The passage appears in the inner-being programme because it locates the guilt-address within the Servant's nefesh (soul). **Flag for Phase 2:** Does the convergence of the guilt vocabulary in Isa 53 around the nefesh of the Servant provide the OT basis for the NT's Christological application — and how does it connect to the NT's use of hamartia (2Cor 5:21: he made him to be sin who knew no sin) and katakrima (Rom 8:1: no condemnation in Christ)?

## 6. Summary Assessment

Guilt in the biblical vocabulary is built on a fundamentally different conceptual foundation from distress: where distress is spatial (narrowing, pressing), guilt is relational-ontological (standing before God in an altered condition resulting from wrongdoing). The Hebrew terms locate guilt as a state the person is in (a.shem, ash.mah), a weight the person carries (a.von in Psa 38), and a recognition event that triggers a response (a.sham in Lev 5 and Gen 42). The remedial vocabulary (na.sa, a.sham offering) operates by transfer and lifting — guilt is moved from one bearer to another.

The NT introduces a new inner-being term (suneidēsis, conscience) that has no OT equivalent and names the faculty through which guilt is registered rather than the guilt-state itself. This represents the most significant structural difference between the OT and NT guilt vocabularies: the OT describes guilt as a state and a burden; the NT identifies the inner organ that monitors and maintains awareness of that state, and then describes what it looks like for that organ to be purified, wounded, defiled, or destroyed.

The most open and significant question for Phase 2 is the relationship between a.von's three-layer semantic structure (act, guilt, consequence as a unified whole) and the NT's resolution language — particularly the katakrima of Rom 8:1 and the conscience-cleansing of Heb 9:14. If the OT cannot separate guilt from its consequence, the NT's claim that the consequence is removed while the person continues to exist requires an account of how the ontological guilt-weight is lifted without the soul being destroyed. The na.sa imagery of Isa 53 is the bridge — but whether it fully answers the inner-being question is the work of Phase 2.
