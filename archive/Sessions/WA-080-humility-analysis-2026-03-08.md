## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** humility | **Registry ID:** 080 | **Source list:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-08 | **Version:** Session B v5

The word *humility* provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word on the basis that the primary terms in this set describe a disposition of the inner person — a posture of lowliness, meekness, or self-subordination — rather than a purely external behaviour or physical state. The Hebrew noun terms a.na.vah / an.vah and the adjective a.nav operate consistently as descriptions of an inward orientation that shapes how the person stands before God and relates to others. The Greek terms tapeinofrosunē and tapeinos, particularly in the NT compound, explicitly locate the state in the mind or thinking: tapeinofrosunē means literally "lowly-mindedness," naming humility as a mental disposition. The verb sha.phel (to be made low / to abase) sits at the boundary: it is used extensively for external abasement and divine judgement, but several verses also apply it to the inner state of being humbled. The adjective praus (meek / gentle) and its noun prautēs describe a quality of character that operates in relational and moral registers. Confidence: **moderate-high** for the noun and adjective terms; **moderate** for sha.phel, which spans inner and physical-social abasement more broadly than the noun terms.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: ANAV
**Terms:** a.na.vah (H6038), an.vah (H6037), a.nav (H6035)

The ANAV root generates a cluster of terms covering the inner experience of meekness, lowliness, and affliction. The root's basic image appears to be that of being pressed down or bowed under weight — the same root system connects to *a.nah* (to afflict, to be occupied), *a.ni* (afflicted), and *o.ni* (affliction), suggesting that the inner disposition of humility in the Hebrew mind is entangled at the root level with the experience of being pressed down by external circumstances. The three terms cover distinct lexical categories: a.nav is an adjective (humble, meek, poor); a.na.vah and an.vah are nouns (humility, gentleness, meekness). The two nouns are listed in the source as alternate spellings of each other and share identical related-word clusters and occurrence counts (approximately 7 each).

Within a.nav's verse set, the adjective operates across two distinct registers. In several Psalms verses (Psa 9:12, 10:12, 10:17, 22:26, 69:32, 76:9, 147:6) the word describes persons who are humble in the sense of being poor, needy, and afflicted — people who are low in social standing and depend entirely on God for rescue. In other verses (Num 12:3, Psa 25:9, 37:11, 149:4, Pro 3:34, Isa 11:4) the same term describes a moral or spiritual disposition — the meek as those whom God teaches and to whom he gives his way. The beatitude antecedent (Psa 37:11: "the meek shall inherit the land") and Num 12:3 (Moses described as the meekest of all men) both belong to this second register. The question of whether these are genuinely two senses of the term or one integrated concept in which social lowliness and inner disposition are inseparable cannot be resolved from the verse sample alone.

The two nouns a.na.vah and an.vah appear in contexts that lean more consistently toward the inner disposition register. In a.na.vah's verse set, Pro 15:33 and 18:12 present humility as the precondition for honour, and Pro 22:4 presents it as the condition for divine reward — all three suggesting an ongoing character state. Zep 2:3 calls the community to *seek* humility alongside righteousness, indicating that humility is something that can be pursued and cultivated, not merely a label for the socially low. The overlap verse in both noun entries — 2Sa 22:36 / Psa 18:35 ("your gentleness made me great") — applies the noun to God as the subject, describing God's posture or action toward the psalmist.

**Verdict:** The three ANAV terms are not fully synonymous and appear to do different work. a.nav covers the broadest range, spanning social lowliness, affliction, and spiritual meekness. The two nouns (a.na.vah / an.vah) are consolidation candidates — they share near-identical data, and their verse sets differ in reference only, not in semantic function. Within the noun pair, an.vah appears in more explicitly royal and martial contexts (Psa 18:35 and 45:4), while a.na.vah's verses cluster around wisdom, reward, and prophetic call. This difference is present but marginal. A verdict on whether these are genuinely distinct terms requires fuller verse review in Phase 2.

### Root Family: SHAPHAL
**Term:** sha.phel (H8213)

The SHAPHAL root generates terms for being low in a spatial and social sense: *she.phel* (poverty), *sha.phal* (low), *shiph.lah* (humiliation), *shiph.lut* (idleness). sha.phel itself is a verb with both an intransitive Qal form (to be or become low) and an active Hiphil form (to lay low, humiliate, make low, set in a lower place). The causative form is therefore explicitly present in the source data: sha.phel can describe either the state of being low or the act of causing another to be brought low.

The verb's verse set is dominated by contexts of divine judgement and the reversal of pride. In Isaiah, sha.phel appears in sustained sequences describing God's action against the proud and exalted — all that is lofty being brought low, cities and nations being cast to the dust (Isa 2:9, 2:11, 2:12, 2:17, 5:15, 10:33, 13:11, 25:11, 25:12, 26:5, 29:4, 32:19). The subject in most of these verses is either God directly or an implied divine agent. In the Psalms subset (Psa 18:27, 75:7, 113:6, 147:6), sha.phel similarly appears in the context of God's sovereign activity — putting down one and lifting up another. The few Proverbs occurrences (Pro 16:19, 25:7, 29:23) apply the root to the condition of being lowly in spirit as something positively valued: "he who is lowly in spirit will obtain honour" (29:23). These Proverbs verses are the only contexts in the verse set where sha.phel or its cognates describe an internal disposition as a positive quality rather than an external condition produced by divine or human action.

God is consistently the acting subject of sha.phel's causative function in the verse set — it is God who brings the proud low, God who exalts the humble and casts the wicked down. Eze 17:24 and 21:26 make this explicit in summary declarations. 1Sa 2:7 ("he brings low and he exalts") names it as a characteristic divine action.

**Verdict:** sha.phel occupies a distinct position in the humility word set. It is primarily a verb of action and result rather than a noun of disposition. Its inner-being qualification rests on the Proverbs occurrences, where lowliness of spirit is explicitly valorised, but the bulk of its verse set describes external abasement — of cities, nations, the proud, and the arrogant — as the result of divine judgement. It is not a near-synonym of the ANAV terms; it describes the condition that results when pride is broken, not the inner posture that precedes it.

### Root Family: TSANA
**Term:** tsa.nu.a (H6800B)

tsa.nu.a is an adjective meaning "modest," occurring only once in the source (Pro 11:2: "with the humble is wisdom"). Its related word *tsa.na* (to be humble) is listed but has no word analysis block of its own in the source. The single occurrence and minimal meaning block ("adj — modest") make any root-level analysis very limited.

**Verdict:** tsa.nu.a stands alone in its family for the purposes of this analysis. The single verse establishes a wisdom connection (humility paired with wisdom over against disgrace), consistent with the ANAV noun set in Proverbs. Whether tsa.nu.a describes a meaningfully distinct quality from a.nav (modest vs meek / humble) cannot be assessed from one verse. This term requires fuller investigation in Phase 2.

### Root Family: PRAUS
**Terms:** prautēs (G4240), praus (G4239)

The PRAUS family consists of an adjective (praus — meek, gentle) and its noun (prautēs — gentleness, meekness). The LSJ entry for praus indicates the word's range in classical Greek extends from mildness of weather and sound through the gentleness of tamed animals to the moral quality of persons who are mild or gentle. The NT appropriation narrows and intensifies this range. The STEP definition explicitly frames praus as "power under control" — a quality of dealing with people in a kind manner with humility and consideration.

In the NT verse set, praus appears in Mat 5:5 (the beatitude of the meek) and Mat 21:5 (the king entering Jerusalem mounted on a donkey — cited as fulfilment of Zep 9:9 and connected to a.nav in the OT). These two Matthew occurrences situate praus within a royal and eschatological frame. Mat 11:29 attributes praus to Jesus self-referentially ("I am gentle and lowly in heart"), making the word a Christological descriptor. The 1Pet 3:4 verse associates praus with a "gentle and quiet spirit" that is "precious in God's sight," locating it as an inner quality. The 1Ti 6:11 verse places praus in a virtue list alongside righteousness, godliness, faith, and love.

prautēs's verse set (Tit 3:2, Jam 1:21, Jam 3:13, 1Pe 3:15) consistently places the noun in relational and ethical contexts: receiving the word with meekness, showing wisdom in meekness, giving a defence with gentleness. Jam 3:13 is notable in that it explicitly connects prautēs with wisdom — a parallel to Pro 11:2 (tsa.nu.a with wisdom) and the ANAV Proverbs cluster.

God is the subject of praus in Mat 21:5 in the sense that the verse describes Jesus — who is presented as king — as the meek one. This is not God the Father as subject, but the verse's theological significance is that meekness becomes a royal, Christological attribute.

**Verdict:** praus and prautēs are performing different grammatical functions (adjective vs noun) but represent the same root and are not distinct semantic entities. The terms are near-synonyms at the root level; their difference lies in grammatical deployment. The adjective praus tends to appear in characterisation contexts (who someone is), while the noun prautēs tends to appear in manner contexts (how something should be done). This is a genuinely meaningful difference in function within a unified semantic field.

### Root Family: TAPEINOS
**Terms:** tapeinofrosunē (G5012), tapeinos (G5011)

The TAPEINOS family is distinguished in the NT word set by its compound morphology. tapeinos is an adjective (lowly, humble) with a classical Greek range spanning physical lowness, social abasement, downcast spirits, and — in the NT — the positive moral quality of humility. tapeinofrosunē is a compound noun (tapeinos + phrosunē, from phrēn — mind, thinking) that explicitly locates humility in the thinking or mind. The related word *frēn* (thinking) is noted in the source, making this etymology explicit.

The verse set for tapeinos places the term in several distinct contexts. Mat 11:29 uses it in Jesus's self-description ("I am gentle and lowly in heart"), locating tapeinos specifically in the *heart* (kardia), giving it an inner-faculty location. Luk 1:52 uses tapeinos in the social reversal register (bringing down the mighty and exalting the humble). 2Cor 7:6 uses it to describe the downcast or despondent — God comforts the tapeinos, where the word appears to mean those who are downcast rather than those who are humble by disposition. 2Cor 10:1 uses it in Paul's ironic self-description as one who is "humble when face to face" — possibly suggesting timidity rather than virtue. Jam 4:6 and 1Pe 5:5 both cite the tradition "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" (from Pro 3:34), applying tapeinos in its positive disposition sense.

tapeinofrosunē's verse set spans Paul's letters and 1 Peter. In Eph 4:2 it appears alongside gentleness and patience in a list of relational virtues. In Phil 2:3 it is explicitly defined in cognitive terms: "count others more significant than yourselves" — the only verse in the entire word set that offers a concrete cognitive description of what humility involves as an inner act. Col 3:12 places it in a clothing metaphor alongside compassion, kindness, meekness, and patience. 1Pe 5:5 commands communal "clothing" with humility toward one another. The two Colossians verses (Col 2:18, 2:23) are anomalous — neither contains an obvious occurrence of tapeinofrosunē in the humility sense; both appear in a context warning against false asceticism that mimics humility.

**Verdict:** tapeinos and tapeinofrosunē are performing distinct work within the TAPEINOS family. tapeinos covers a range from downcast/despondent (2Cor 7:6) through social lowliness (Luk 1:52) to positive moral disposition (Mat 11:29, Jam 4:6). tapeinofrosunē is more specialised: it consistently names the inner disposition or quality of mind that produces humble conduct, and Phil 2:3 provides the only explicit cognitive definition in the entire word set. The difference between a person being described as tapeinos and a person being said to act from tapeinofrosunē is analogous to the difference between "he is humble" and "he acted from a humble mind."

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

**ANAV family — a.nav vs a.na.vah / an.vah:**
a.nav (H6035) and the noun pair a.na.vah / an.vah show a functional difference in their verse sets. a.nav's verse set includes a substantial cluster of verses where the person described as a.nav is primarily poor, needy, and afflicted rather than exhibiting a spiritual disposition (Psa 9:12, 10:12, 22:26, Job 24:4, Amo 2:7). The nouns a.na.vah and an.vah do not appear in this socio-economic poverty register at all — their verse sets deal consistently with the disposition or state of humility as a virtue, reward condition, or royal quality. The two registers are semantically distinct: one describes what a person *is* (poor and pressed down), the other describes what a person *does or cultivates* (humility as a practice). Whether this difference is inherent in the root's polysemy or in the distinction between adjective and noun usage cannot be decided from this data set alone.

**TAPEINOS family — tapeinos vs tapeinofrosunē:**
As noted in Section 2, tapeinos shows greater range: it describes the downcast (2Cor 7:6), the socially low (Luk 1:52), and the humble as virtue (Mat 11:29). tapeinofrosunē is consistently the inner disposition term. The most significant single difference in the verse sets: tapeinos is used by Paul to describe himself ironically in 2Cor 10:1 in a context where the meaning may shade into timidity or social smallness rather than virtue, whereas tapeinofrosunē is never used ironically in the verse set. The compound appears to have a more stabilised positive valence.

**PRAUS family:**
No significant positional differences in meaning between praus and prautēs. The difference is grammatical function (adjective vs noun), which produces contextual differences in use (characterisation vs manner) but not semantic divergence.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

*Testament coverage is "both"; this section is required.*

**What the NT introduces that has no direct OT equivalent in this word set:**

The NT compound tapeinofrosunē has no Hebrew equivalent in the source file. The OT noun terms (a.na.vah / an.vah) name humility as a state or disposition but do not etymologically embed the *mind* or *thinking* as the location of the state. tapeinofrosunē, by compounding tapeinos with phrosunē (mind, thinking), makes the cognitive dimension of humility explicit. Phil 2:3 then provides a definition of what that cognitive act is: counting others more significant than oneself. This cognitive framing — humility as a mental operation performed toward others — is absent from the OT verse set in this word group.

**What the NT does differently with shared concepts:**

The OT verse set for a.nav places the humble person consistently in a posture of being acted upon by God: God leads the humble (Psa 25:9), God saves the humble (Psa 76:9), God lifts up the humble (Psa 147:6), God adorns the humble with salvation (Psa 149:4). The humble person in the OT verse set is predominantly the *object* of divine action. In the NT verse set, by contrast, humility is more consistently a practice to be performed or cultivated: Eph 4:2 commands it, Phil 2:3 defines it, Col 3:12 calls believers to put it on, and 1Pe 5:5 calls for mutual clothing with it. The shift is from the humble person receiving divine favour to the humble person performing an inner act.

**What is present in the OT but absent from the NT:**

The OT verse set includes the social abasement register of a.nav (the poor, the needy, the afflicted as a class) and the judgement register of sha.phel (the proud being brought low by divine action). Neither register appears in the NT verse set for this word group. The NT terms do not describe humility as a state of socioeconomic poverty or as the result of divine judgement on pride.

**Most significant single OT/NT difference:**

The OT verse set presents humility primarily in terms of what God does to and for the humble — his sovereign activity of exalting the low and bringing down the proud — whereas the NT verse set presents humility primarily in terms of what the believer is called to do and how it is to be practiced. The direction of agency differs.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

**a.na.vah and an.vah (H6038, H6037) — consolidation candidates:** These two terms are listed as alternate spellings of each other and share identical related-word clusters, occurrence counts, and largely overlapping meaning blocks. Their verse sets are distinct (a.na.vah: 2Sa 22:36, Pro 15:33, 18:12, 22:4, Zep 2:3; an.vah: Psa 18:35, 45:4), which could reflect different versional assignments of the same underlying word form rather than genuine lexical distinctness. This limits the confidence of any analysis that treats them as distinct terms. Phase 2 must check whether these Strong's numbers represent a genuine lexical split or a textual variant classification.

**tsa.nu.a (H6800B) — thin data:** One occurrence and one verse. No word analysis block for the related word tsa.na. Any analysis of this term's function is necessarily provisional. The related word cluster points to a family that may have more data, but it is not developed in the source.

**sha.phel (H8213) — verse set composition:** 22 of approximately 36 verses are recorded. The verse set as provided is dominated by prophetic judgement contexts, particularly from Isaiah (14 of 22 verses). Wisdom, narrative, and other registers are proportionally under-represented relative to what a fuller verse review might show. Register assessments for sha.phel should be treated as provisional.

**tapeinofrosunē (G5012) — Col 2:18 and Col 2:23:** These two verses are listed under tapeinofrosunē in the source but do not contain the word "humility" in the ESV text as recorded. Both belong to a passage warning against a form of false asceticism or self-imposed religious rigour. Whether tapeinofrosunē appears in the Greek text of these verses or whether they are contextual references requires verification in Phase 2.

**Cross-registry note:** The ANAV root family overlaps significantly with the affliction/poverty word cluster, and the sha.phel judgement register overlaps with pride and arrogance vocabulary. Cross-registry links have been noted in the JSON but are not analysed here; that belongs in Session D or Phase 2 cross-word synthesis.

7. [TERM: tsa.nu.a] With only one verse available (Pro 11:2), is tsa.nu.a doing work that is semantically distinct from a.nav in a wisdom context, or is it a near-synonym operating in the same conceptual space?

9. [TERM: praus] The LSJ entry for praus connects its semantic range to tamed animals — specifically, strength that has been brought under control — and the STEP definition frames it as "power under control." Does this meaning distinguish praus from the ANAV terms in a theologically significant way, and if so, what does the inner-being model need to do with the distinction?
   > *Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to determine whether the "controlled power" dimension of praus represents a different inner-being quality than the lowliness or meekness of the ANAV and TAPEINOS families, or whether these are descriptions of the same state from different angles.*

10. [TERM: tapeinofrosunē] Phil 2:3 offers the only explicit cognitive definition in the word set — "count others more significant than yourselves." Is this verse prescribing the cognitive act that constitutes tapeinofrosunē, or describing the result that tapeinofrosunē produces — and what is the difference for the inner-being model?

11. [TERM: tapeinos] In 2Cor 7:6, God "comforts the tapeinos" — where the term appears to describe the downcast or despondent rather than those humble by virtue. Does tapeinos in this verse belong to the humility word set, or is it functioning closer to a word like dejection or lowness of spirit?

12. [OT/NT] The OT verse set positions the humble person primarily as the recipient of divine action (God leads, saves, exalts, adorns the humble), while the NT verse set positions humility primarily as a practice to be performed by the believer. Does this represent a theological development in how the relationship between divine action and human inner posture is understood, or does the difference reflect genre and context rather than theological content?

13. [OT/NT] tapeinofrosunē etymologically embeds phrosunē (mind / thinking) in its construction, making the cognitive location of humility explicit — a feature absent from the OT noun terms a.na.vah and an.vah. Is this cognitive explicitness a NT lexical development or does it reflect a continuity with OT inner-being thought that the noun terms simply do not encode lexically?

[TERM: tapeinofrosunē] In Col 2:18 and 2:23, where tapeinofrosunē appears in contexts describing what the ESV records as "asceticism" and "severity to the body," is the word being used in a positive sense (true humility), a negative sense (false or self-made humility), or a neutral descriptive sense — and does this usage affect the meaning range of the term?
