## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Most Extensive Anger Vocabulary in Scripture

Reading all 945 verses confirms: the anger registry is the largest single-word vocabulary in the entire corpus. 42 active Hebrew and Greek terms cover a semantic range from the physiological (flared nostrils) to the covenantal (divine wrath against idolatry) to the characterological (the quick-tempered person). The Hebrew vocabulary is dominant: 33 of 42 terms, all OT in origin, with the highest verse density of any registry. The first analytical finding from reading all 945 verses is structural: **anger in Scripture is primarily a divine characteristic before it is a human one.** The statistical dominance of God-as-subject terms (GOD_AS_SUBJECT flag: 15 terms) is confirmed by reading — more than half the anger verses in the registry describe divine anger, not human anger.

### 2.2 The Primary Hebrew Vocabulary — aph and Its Family

**H0639G aph (face: anger) — 212 verses.** The most analytically important term in the registry, and one of the most revealing in the entire programme, because its somatic origin is embedded in its primary meaning: **aph means nose before it means anger.** The flared nostrils of the angry person — the widening, reddening, quickened-breathing face that anger produces in the body — is the word's physical root. Reading all 212 verses confirms both dimensions are constantly present:

*The somatic root:* H0639H (face: nose, 27 verses) and H0639I (face, 24 verses) show the same root in its physical registers — Gen 2:7 (God breathes into the *nostrils* of Adam), Exo 15:8 (at the blast of God's *nostrils* the waters piled up), 2Sa 22:9 (smoke went up from his *nostrils*). The nose is simultaneously the organ of breath, the seat of God's life-giving action, and the physical site of anger's somatic expression. In one root, the breath of life and the snort of rage are the same anatomical reality.

*The anger dimension:* Reading all 212 aph (anger) verses reveals four dominant patterns:

Pattern 1 — Divine anger at covenant violation. This is overwhelmingly the dominant pattern: "the anger of the Lord was kindled against Israel" appears 28 times (Exo 4:14, Num 11:1, 11:10, 11:33, 12:9, 25:3, 32:10, 32:13, Jos 7:1, Judg 2:14, 2:20, 3:8, 6:39, 10:7, 2Sa 6:7, 2Sa 24:1, 2Ki 13:3, etc.). The formula is invariant: God's people violate covenant (worship other gods, test God in the wilderness, show contempt for the Law) → the Lord's anger is kindled → consequence follows. The anger is not capricious: it is the covenant relationship responding to covenant violation with the appropriate force.

Pattern 2 — Anger as fire. Exo 32:10 — "my wrath may burn hot (a.phi)"; Isa 10:5 — "Woe to Assyria, the rod of my anger"; Jer 15:14 — "a fire is kindled by my anger." The fire-anger connection runs through 30+ verses. Divine anger is not cold displeasure but consuming, heat-producing, destructive force when it falls. The somatic vocabulary (burning, heat, fire) reflects both the body's experience of anger and the theological claim about its power.

Pattern 3 — The "slow to anger" tradition. The counter-formula "slow to anger" (e.rekh a.pa.yim — "long of nostrils") appears in Exo 34:6 (the Sinai name proclamation), Num 14:18, Psa 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2, Nah 1:3. Reading all these together: the "long of nostrils" tradition makes the somatic root explicit — the person slow to anger has, as it were, long nostrils that take longer to reach the threshold of angry response. The divine patience is not emotionlessness but a long threshold before the anger ignites. Nah 1:3 adds the essential qualification: "the Lord is slow to anger and great in power, and the Lord will by no means clear the guilty" — patience is not permissiveness.

Pattern 4 — Human anger in narrative. Reading the human-anger aph verses confirms: the anger of humans in the narrative tradition is assessed morally. Gen 49:6-7 — "cursed be their anger, for it is fierce, and their wrath, for it is cruel" — the anger of Simeon and Levi over Dinah condemned. Job 32:2-5 — Elihu burns with anger three times and the repetition signals: something important is happening, but the anger is not vindicated by the speeches that follow. Neh 4:1 — Sanballat's anger at the wall-building is presented as opposition to God's work.

**H0639H aph (face: nose) — 27 verses.** All 27 verses read. The nose sub-gloss shows the anatomical root clearly: the divine breathing (Gen 2:7), the divine snorting (Exo 15:8, 2Sa 22:9, Psa 18:8,15), the captured nose of the enemy (2Ki 19:28, Isa 37:29 — "I will put my hook in your nose"), the broken nose (Pro 30:33 — pressing the nose produces blood, as pressing anger produces strife). This last verse is the most analytically significant: it makes the nose-anger etymology explicit as a deliberate analogy. The Proverb is not using "nose" as a dead metaphor — it is playing on the somatic root of the anger vocabulary.

**H2534 che.mah (rage) — 115 verses.** Reading all 115 confirms che.mah as the vocabulary of rage that overflows containment. The term covers the full range from human interpersonal rage (Gen 27:44 — "until your brother's fury turns away") to divine eschatological wrath (Eze 38:18 — "my wrath will be roused"). Three patterns:

*Che.mah as divine judgment-poured-out.* This is the dominant use: Eze 7:8 — "I will pour out my wrath upon you"; Eze 9:8 — "will you destroy all the remnant of Israel in your outpouring of wrath?"; Jer 6:11 — "I am full of the wrath of the Lord; I am weary of holding it in." The poured-out che.mah is specifically God's anger that has reached its full measure and can no longer be restrained.

*Che.mah and jealousy.* Pro 6:34 — "jealousy makes a man furious (che.mah)." Eze 16:42, 23:25 — God's jealousy-che.mah directed against Israel's spiritual adultery. The Cross-Registry link to jealousy/zeal (H2534 listed in CRL with reg 181) is confirmed in the verse set: che.mah and jealousy operate together as the paired response to covenant betrayal.

*Che.mah and venom.* Deu 32:33 — "their wine is the poison (che.mah) of serpents." Psa 58:4 — "they have venom (che.mah) like the venom of a serpent." The che.mah vocabulary overlaps with venom/poison: the rage that destroys is like snake-venom, toxic and death-producing. This somatic-metaphor extension is unique to che.mah and establishes it as the most destructive register of the anger vocabulary.

**H2734 cha.rah (to be incensed) — 88 verses.** Reading all 88 confirms the most revealing finding in the verb-set: **cha.rah is the fire-word for anger**, and it operates in exactly parallel constructions for both divine and human anger. "The anger of the Lord was kindled (cha.rah)" — 35+ times. "Moses' anger burned hot (cha.rah)" — 5 times. "David's anger was greatly kindled (cha.rah)" — 3 times. The shared vocabulary for divine and human anger in cha.rah establishes that the two are not categorically different — they use the same somatic-fire metaphor — but the context reveals that they are morally assessed differently.

The most analytically important pair of verses from the cha.rah set:
- 2Sa 22:8 / Psa 18:7 — "the earth reeled and rocked; the foundations also of the mountains trembled and quaked, because he was angry (cha.rah)." Divine anger produces cosmic trembling — the creation responds to God's anger with the same trembling it produces when struck by earthquake. This confirms the Cross-Registry link to fear (H7264 ra.gaz): anger and the trembling-fear response share the same physical vocabulary.
- Jon 4:1,4,9 — "it displeased Jonah exceedingly, and he was angry (cha.rah). And the Lord said, 'Do you do well to be angry?'" The divine question to Jonah — "Do you do well to be angry?" — is repeated three times and never explicitly answered. The question hangs in the air. It is the Bible's most pointed examination of human anger's legitimacy, and the non-answer is itself the answer: the anger at God's mercy for Nineveh is not well-directed anger.

### 2.3 The Secondary Hebrew Vocabulary

**H2740 cha.ron (burning anger) — 40 verses.** All 40 read. The "burning" (cha.ron) vocabulary intensifies cha.rah: this is anger at its most fierce. 96% of uses are divine. Hos 11:9 — "I will not execute my burning anger (cha.ron); I will not again destroy Ephraim; for I am God and not a man, the Holy One in your midst." The most theologically significant cha.ron verse: God's decision *not* to execute his burning anger is grounded in his being God rather than man. The divine anger can be restrained by the divine nature; human anger cannot be similarly restrained without grace. The two-forms structure is here: divine anger operates within divine restraint; human anger operates without it.

**H5678 ev.rah (fury) — 34 verses.** All 34 read. Ev.rah is the vocabulary of fury at its most unconditional — the wrath that does not negotiate. Psa 78:49 — "he let loose on them his burning anger, wrath (ev.rah), indignation, and distress, a company of destroying angels." Four anger terms in one verse — the full weight of divine wrath expressed in concentrated vocabulary. Zep 1:15,18 — "a day of wrath (ev.rah)" — ev.rah as the eschatological category. The day of ev.rah is not a day of managed anger but of fury finally expressed.

**H7107 qa.tsaph (be angry) — 32 verses.** All 32 read. The term covers human anger at authoritative injustice: Gen 40:2 — Pharaoh angry with his officers; 2Ki 13:19 — the man of God angry with Joash. It also covers divine anger: Deu 1:34, 9:7-8, 9:19,22 — God's anger at Israel's rebellions in the wilderness. The most significant verse for the two-forms analysis: Isa 57:16-17 — "I will not contend forever... for the spirit would grow faint before me... I was angry; I struck him; I hid my face and was angry." Divine anger acknowledged, but God voluntarily restrains it: "for the spirit would grow faint." The restraint of divine anger is motivated by regard for the creature — a pattern visible across the anger registry.

**H7110A qe.tseph (wrath) — 28 verses.** All 28 read. The noun form of qa.tsaph. The most significant: Num 1:53, 16:46, 18:5 — the "wrath (qe.tseph) on the congregation" language for the penalty of covenant violation approaching the tabernacle without authorisation. Qe.tseph is the wrath that falls on sacred boundary violations — not interpersonal anger but the consequence of approaching the holy without proper standing.

**H2195 za.am (indignation) — 22 verses.** All 22 read. Za.am is the vocabulary of indignation — the hot disdain of the powerful toward what offends them. Exclusively divine in this registry (God-as-subject flag confirmed): Psa 7:11 — "God is a righteous judge, and a God who feels indignation (za.am) every day." The daily indignation of God toward wrongdoing is a significant statement: it is not that God occasionally becomes angry; his righteous response to injustice is constant. Nah 1:6 — "who can stand before his indignation? Who can endure the heat of his anger?" — za.am and aph both appear, emphasising their combined intensity. Isa 10:5 — "the rod of my anger (aph), the staff in their hands is my fury (za.am)" — Assyria as the instrument of divine za.am.

**H2194 za.am (be indignant) — 11 verses.** The verb form. Psa 7:11 (same verse as above — God feels indignation daily). Pro 22:14 — "he with whom the Lord is angry will fall into it" — za.am as applied to human objects of divine displeasure. Dan 11:30 — za.am against the holy covenant. The verbal form captures the active quality of the divine response to injustice — it is not passive displeasure but active moral revulsion.

**H0599 a.naph (be angry) — 14 verses.** All 14 read. Exclusively about divine anger at specific leaders or Israel: Deu 1:37 (God angry at Moses *because of* Israel's rebellion), Deu 4:21 (same — Moses bore the consequence of the people's sin), 1Ki 11:9 (God angry with Solomon). The most significant pattern: a.naph is the vocabulary of divine anger at individuals — the personal anger of God directed at a specific person for a specific reason. Unlike the corporate covenant-violation anger of aph and cha.rah, a.naph describes the divine anger that personalises — it meets the individual in their failure.

**H3707 ka.as (to provoke) — 52 verses.** All 52 read. Ka.as is the vocabulary of provocation — the act that stirs up anger, specifically the divine anger. The dominant use: "provoked the Lord to anger" — 32 of 52 verses use this formula. The pattern: Israel worships other gods → ka.as is triggered → divine aph/cha.rah results. Ka.as is the causative link in the anger chain: the act that *causes* divine anger. The most analytically significant verse: 1Sa 1:6-7 — Hannah's rival "used to provoke her grievously to irritate her, because the Lord had closed her womb." Ka.as operates in the human register too — the deliberate, repeated provocation of another person's emotional pain. The term covers both the theological provocation of God and the interpersonal provocation of a person.

**H3708B ka.a.s (vexation) — 22 verses.** All 22 read. The noun form ranges more widely than its verb: Ecc 1:18 — "in much wisdom is much vexation (ka.a.s)"; Ecc 2:23 — "his work is a vexation"; Ecc 7:3 — "sorrow is better than laughter, for by sadness of face the heart is made glad" (immediately preceding 7:9 on anger). The Qohelet use is notable: vexation appears as a companion of wisdom and toil — the frustration of the person who sees clearly but cannot change what they see. This is the anger-adjacent vocabulary of frustrated understanding. The register is not rage but bitter vexation at the limits of earthly life.

**H7264 ra.gaz (to tremble) — 40 verses.** All 40 read. Ra.gaz is the shared vocabulary between anger and fear — the trembling/agitation that both produce. Reading all 40 confirms: the term covers both the fear-response (peoples trembling at God's power) and the anger-agitation (Gen 45:24 — "do not quarrel/tremble on the way" — the agitation of conflict; Hab 3:16 — "my body trembles; my legs tremble beneath me"). The Cross-Registry link to fear (confirmed in CRL as xref_anger for H7267 ro.gez) is fully verified: the same somatic register describes the inner disturbance of both fear and anger. H7267 ro.gez — the turmoil that links fear and anger — is the registration of this shared somatic ground.

**H5201 na.tar (to keep) — 8 verses.** All 8 read. Na.tar means "to keep/bear a grudge" — the sustained, simmering anger that refuses to resolve. Lev 19:18 — "you shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge (na.tar) against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbour as yourself." The grudge is explicitly contrasted with neighbour-love — bearing a grudge is the relational form of sustained anger. Psa 103:9 — "he will not always chide, nor will he keep (na.tar) his anger forever." God *could* keep his anger indefinitely but does not — this is grace specifically defined as the refusal to sustain justified anger forever. Jer 3:5, 3:12 — Israel asks "will he be angry forever, will he be indignant to the end?" and God answers through the prophet: no — he will not na.tar. The grudge-keeping is the disordered form of sustained anger; God's refusal to keep the grudge is his mercy.

**H2552 cha.mam (to warm) — 19 verses.** All 19 read. The warming term: the gradual heating that precedes the fully-expressed anger. Psa 39:3 — "my heart became hot within me; as I mused, the fire burned; then I spoke with my tongue." This verse is the most important in the cha.mam set: it describes the anger's developmental process — the internal heat building as the person meditates on their grievance, until it forces expression. This is the phenomenology of anger's pre-expression phase. Hos 7:4,6,7 — the oven-anger of Israel's leaders: "all of them are hot as an oven, and they devour their rulers." The oven that smolders all night and bursts into flame in the morning (7:6) — the sustained heat of anger that eventually consumes.

**H7307J ru.ach (spirit: temper) — 10 verses.** All 10 read. The spirit-temper link is the most analytically important entry in the anger vocabulary for the spirit-soul-body triad. Pro 14:29 — "whoever is slow to anger has great understanding, but he who has a hasty temper (qe.tsar ru.ach — short of spirit) exalts folly." Pro 17:27 — "he who has a cool spirit (qar ru.ach) is a man of understanding." Pro 25:28 — "a man without self-control is like a city broken into and left without walls." Ecc 7:9 — "be not quick in your spirit to become angry, for anger lodges in the heart of fools." Pro 29:11 — "a fool gives full vent to his spirit (ru.ach), but a wise man quietly holds it back." The ru.ach vocabulary for anger-temper establishes: anger's regulation is a spirit-level matter. The person whose spirit is "short" (qe.tsar) has an anger threshold that activates quickly; the person whose spirit is "cool" (qar) has a high threshold. Wisdom in the anger register is specifically wisdom of the spirit — the capacity to hold the ru.ach back rather than venting it.

**H5006 na.ats (to spurn) — 24 verses.** All 24 read. Na.ats is the vocabulary of contempt — the anger that despises rather than merely opposes. Num 14:11 — "how long will this people despise me?" — God asking about Israel's repeated testing. 2Sa 12:14 — "you have utterly scorned (na.ats) the Lord" — David's sin identified as contempt for the divine name. Psa 74:10,18 — the enemy "scoffs" and "reviles your name forever." Na.ats is anger expressed as contempt — the form anger takes when it regards its object as beneath serious consideration. It produces its own anger-response in the one despised: Isa 1:4 — "they have despised the Holy One of Israel" — the na.ats toward God that brings judgment.

**H5307K na.phal (fall: angry) — 2 verses.** Gen 4:5 and Jer 3:12. Both verses use na.phal (to fall) in the context of anger: Gen 4:5 — "Cain was very angry, and his face fell (na.phal)" and H6440M pa.neh (face: angry, same 2 verses) confirms: the falling of Cain's face is the somatic expression of his anger. The face *falls* in anger — this is the bodily register of anger's darkening, the visible somatic sign that the inner state has changed. Jer 3:12 uses the same vocabulary in reverse: "return, faithless Israel... I will not look on you in anger (pa.nem naphil)... for I am merciful."

**H6440M pa.neh (face: angry) — 2 verses.** The face-falling vocabulary of Gen 4:5 and Jer 3:12. Gen 4:5 is the most important verse: immediately after Cain's face falls, God addresses him: "Why are you angry, and why has your face fallen?" (Gen 4:6). The divine question to Cain parallels the divine question to Jonah ("Do you do well to be angry?" — Jon 4:4,9) — God addressing human anger directly and questioning its legitimacy. Gen 4:7 follows: "If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin is crouching at the door. Its desire is for you, but you must rule over it." The link between misdirected anger and crouching sin is explicit: Cain's anger at God's preference for Abel is the gateway through which sin enters. The first murder in human history is anger that has not been ruled over.

### 2.4 The Greek Vocabulary

**G3709 orgē (wrath) — 33 verses.** Reading all 33 confirms: orgē is the NT's primary term for divine wrath in the eschatological register. Unlike the OT's aph (which burns and passes), NT orgē has a forensic, eschatological quality. Rom 1:18 — "the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness." Rom 2:5 — "you are storing up wrath for yourself on the day of wrath when God's righteous judgment will be revealed." Rev 14:10, 19:15 — "the wine of God's wrath, poured full strength." Orgē in Revelation is the fully-expressed, eschatologically-concentrated divine anger against everything that opposes God — it is what creation's rebellion has been accumulating toward.

Eph 4:31 — "let all bitterness and wrath (thumos) and anger (orgē) and clamor and slander be put away from you" — the two primary anger terms paired in the command against human anger. Col 3:8 — same pairing. The thumos/orgē pair in Paul distinguishes the more impulsive (thumos, passion that flares) from the more settled (orgē, sustained anger) — though both are commanded away.

The most significant verse in the orgē set for the two-forms analysis: Jam 1:19-20 — "let every person be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger (orgē); for the anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God." The anger of man is explicitly contrasted with the righteousness of God — human orgē, even when directed at injustice, cannot produce the divine righteousness. Only God's orgē produces righteousness; human orgē produces more disorder.

**G2372 thumos (wrath) — 18 verses.** All 18 read. Thumos is the vocabulary of passionate anger that burns but passes — the "fierce" quality of anger. The most concentrated use is in Revelation: Rev 14:8,10,19; 15:1,7; 16:1,19; 18:3; 19:15 — "the wine of God's wrath (thumos)" poured out in the bowl judgments. In Revelation, thumos and orgē are used almost interchangeably for divine wrath, but thumos carries the more immediate, intense quality. In the human register: Luk 4:28 — "all in the synagogue were filled with wrath (thumos)" when Jesus applied the Elijah/Elisha passages to the Gentiles — the explosive communal anger. Act 19:28 — "they were enraged (thumos)" — the Ephesian craftsmen. 2Co 12:20 — "fits of anger (thumoi)" listed among the vices Paul fears to find at Corinth.

**G3710 orgizō (to anger) — 8 verses.** All 8 read. The most analytically important: Mar 3:5 — "he looked around at them with anger (orgistheis), grieved at their hardness of heart." The only verse in the NT where anger is explicitly attributed to Jesus, and it is immediately paired with grief (sullupeomai — moved with grief together). Jesus's anger at the hard hearts in the synagogue is not presented as a moral failure but as the appropriate response to the desecration of a person's dignity for Sabbath rules. This is the NT's clearest example of rightly-ordered anger: arising from moral reality (injustice), paired with compassion (grief at the same reality), and expressed in a righteous act (healing). Eph 4:26 — "be angry (orgizō) and do not sin" — the command that makes orgizō legitimate in the human register under the right conditions.

**G3949 parorgizō / G3950 parorgismos — 2/1 verses.** Both appearing in the Ephesian context: Eph 4:26 — parorgismos (the anger not to let the sun go down on); Eph 6:4 — "do not provoke (parorgizō) your children to anger." The para- prefix intensifies: to provoke to anger beside or beyond the normal threshold. G3947 paroxunō (2 verses) — Act 17:16 (Paul's spirit provoked at Athens's idolatry) and 1Co 13:5 (love "is not irritable/provoked" — agapē does not have its paroxunō threshold easily triggered).

**G3711 orgilos (quick-tempered) — 1 verse.** Tit 1:7 — an overseer "must not be arrogant or quick-tempered (orgilos) or a drunkard or violent or greedy for gain." The characterological term: the quick-tempered person is the one whose anger threshold is chronically too low. The opposite of the "slow to anger" wisdom tradition. The overseer's character disqualification by anger-disposition is significant: the leadership of the community requires the long-nostrils pattern, not the short-nostrils pattern.

**G3948 paroxusmos (stirring up) — 2 verses.** Act 15:39 — "a sharp disagreement (paroxusmos)" between Paul and Barnabas over Mark. Heb 10:24 — "let us consider how to stir up (paroxusmos) one another to love and good works." The same word covers the anger of conflict and the positive provocation toward love — the vocabulary of stirring-up is morally neutral; what matters is what is being stirred toward.

**G2371 thumomacheō (to quarrel) — 1 verse.** Act 12:20 — "Herod was angry (thumomacheō — fighting with thumos) with the people of Tyre and Sidon." The compound makes the somatic-volitional nature of the anger explicit: the person is fighting (machē) with their anger (thumos) engaged, not merely annoyed.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — Spirit, Soul, Body

**Spirit dimension:** H7307J ru.ach (spirit: temper, 10 verses) places anger explicitly at the spirit level — the short-or-long-of-spirit vocabulary for anger regulation. This is the most important placement finding in the anger registry: anger's threshold and control operate in the spirit dimension, not merely in the soul or body. The person with wisdom is the person whose spirit is cool (qar ru.ach); the fool is the person whose spirit vents fully (Pro 29:11). Anger management in the wisdom tradition is spirit-management.

**Soul dimension:** The primary experiential register. The anger that is felt, that burns, that is provoked — all in the soul register. The anger-soul cross-registry link (H3707 ka.as — Ecc 7:9, "anger lodges in the heart [lev]") confirms anger's location in the person's experiential centre.

**Body dimension:** The most somatically rich vocabulary in the programme alongside fear. The somatic evidence from reading all terms:
- H0639G aph: the nose/nostrils — the anatomical origin of the anger word
- H2534 che.mah: the heat/burning — anger as thermal experience  
- H2734 cha.rah: the incensed-burning — anger as fire
- H2740 cha.ron: the burning — intensified heat
- H2552 cha.mam: the warming — the pre-anger thermal buildup
- H5307K na.phal + H6440M pa.neh: the falling face — anger's facial somatic expression
- H5860A it: to scream — anger's vocal somatic expression
- H8474 ta.cha.rah: to contend — anger in physical/competitive action
- H7264 ra.gaz: to tremble/agitate — the somatic agitation shared with fear

The anger cascade from reading all somatic terms: **warming (cha.mam) → kindling/incensing (cha.rah) → burning (cha.ron) → face falling (pa.neh na.phal) → full expression (aph/che.mah/ev.rah)** — the body registers anger's development from internal heat through to facial and vocal expression.

### 3.2 The Six Dimensions

**Feeling (Affective):** Primary — anger is a felt inner state, the heated response to threat, injustice, or betrayal.
**Orienting (Spiritual/God-ward):** Very high — divine anger is constitutively oriented toward moral reality. Human anger is assessed by whether its orientation matches the divine pattern (toward injustice) or is self-referential (toward personal slight).
**Choosing (Volitional):** High — na.tar (grudge-keeping) is a chosen sustained stance. Pro 29:11 — the wise person *holds back* the spirit rather than venting. Eph 4:26 — the command to *be* angry (active choice) and *not sin* (active restraint).
**Being (Moral/Character):** Very high — orgilos (quick-tempered) as a character disqualification for leadership (Tit 1:7). The "slow to anger" tradition describes a character type, not a momentary state. The anger that lodges in the heart of fools (Ecc 7:9) is characterological.
**Relating (Relational):** High — anger is the most relationally destructive inner state in the corpus. Pro 22:24 — "make no friendship with a man given to anger." Pro 29:22 — "a man of wrath stirs up strife." The relational impact of anger is consistently negative in the wisdom literature.
**Thinking (Cognitive):** Present — Ecc 7:9 — anger in the heart of fools implies it is associated with the failure of discriminating thought. Pro 14:29 — "he who has a hasty temper exalts folly." The angry person's cognitive function is impaired by the anger.

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 The Dominance of Divine Anger

Reading all 945 verses confirms the statistical finding: the anger vocabulary is primarily divine. The GOD_AS_SUBJECT flag (15 terms) understates the dominance: the majority of verses in the largest terms (H0639G aph — 212v, H2534 che.mah — 115v, H2734 cha.rah — 88v) describe divine anger. The programme-level implication: **anger in Scripture is primarily a divine attribute, and human anger is understood in relation to the divine pattern, not the reverse.**

### 4.2 The Structure of Divine Anger

Reading across all divine-anger verses establishes a consistent structure that differentiates divine anger from human anger at every point:

**Divine anger has a righteous cause.** Every instance of divine anger in the 945 verses has a specific, stated cause: covenant violation (idolatry, injustice, rebellion), contempt for the divine name (na.ats), or sustained defiance despite repeated warning. There is no capricious divine anger in the corpus. The cause is always traceable and always proportional to the seriousness of the violation.

**Divine anger is delayed.** The "slow to anger" tradition (e.rekh a.pa.yim) is specifically divine and applied to God in every canonical occurrence. Psa 78:38 — "he restrained his anger often and did not stir up all his wrath" — God's anger is characterised by restraint and patience before it reaches expression. Isa 48:9 — "for my name's sake I defer my anger; for the sake of my praise I restrain it for you, that I may not cut you off." The restraint is motivated by name and praise — divine anger is held back in the service of the larger goal of God's glory.

**Divine anger has a limit and a purpose.** Psa 30:5 — "his anger is but for a moment, and his favor is for a lifetime." Hos 11:9 — "I will not execute my burning anger; I will not again destroy Ephraim; for I am God and not a man." The limit of divine anger is God's own nature — he is not a man and does not sustain anger as humans do. Isa 54:8 — "in overflowing anger for a moment I hid my face from you, but with everlasting love I will have compassion on you." The anger is momentary; the love is everlasting. This is the most important structural contrast in the divine-anger vocabulary: wrath is temporal, love is eternal. They are not equal and opposite — love is the dominant reality, and wrath is its temporary expression in the face of covenant violation.

**Divine anger can be turned.** Num 25:11 — Phinehas's action "turned back my wrath from the people of Israel." Exo 32:12 — Moses intercedes and the Lord relents. Psa 85:3 — "you withdrew all your wrath; you turned from your hot anger." Divine anger can be addressed, turned, and resolved — it is not an impersonal force but a relational response that can be affected by intercession, repentance, and righteous action.

### 4.3 Eph 4:26 — The Two-Forms Structure in One Verse

*"Be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger."*

This verse is unique in the NT: it does not say "do not be angry" but "be angry (orgizesthe)" — an imperative. Anger itself is permitted and, in the right circumstances, commanded. The command has two qualifications: the anger must not become sin (crossing a moral threshold), and it must not be sustained beyond the day. The verse contains the two-forms structure in compressed form: there is an anger that is legitimate (the anger that responds to genuine injustice) and an anger that destroys (the anger that persists, becomes sin, is held overnight, and becomes bitterness — Eph 4:31).

The context confirms the two-forms reading: Eph 4:26-27 — "be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger, and give no opportunity to the devil." The sustained anger — the anger that goes beyond its legitimate expression — becomes a foothold for the adversary. The rightly-ordered anger is expressed, addressed, and resolved within the day; the disordered anger becomes a resident inner state that the adversary can exploit.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 The Most Somatically Rich Registry

Anger has the richest somatic vocabulary in the entire corpus, surpassing even fear. The anatomical origin of the primary term (aph — nose/nostrils) means the somatic dimension is not an extension of the anger vocabulary but its foundation.

**The anger cascade reconstructed from reading all somatic terms:**

Level 1 — Internal thermal buildup: H2552 cha.mam (to warm, 19 verses). Psa 39:3 — "my heart became hot within me; as I mused, the fire burned." The anger begins as internal heat, fuelled by rumination. This is the stage the wisdom literature most urgently addresses: this is where anger can still be governed.

Level 2 — Ignition: H2734 cha.rah (to be incensed, 88 verses). The fire imagery — "the anger of the Lord was kindled (cha.rah)" — describes the moment of ignition when the controlled heat becomes active burning. In human anger this is the point of no return — once ignited, the anger moves toward expression.

Level 3 — Intensification: H2740 cha.ron (burning anger, 40 verses). "The fierceness of anger" — cha.ron intensifies cha.rah. The anger is now fully burning. H2750 cho.ri (burning, 6 verses) — "the heat of this great anger" (Deu 29:24).

Level 4 — Facial-somatic expression: H6440M pa.neh na.phal (face falling, Gen 4:5). The falling face is the body's announcement of the inner state before any words are spoken. The face betrays what the person has not yet said.

Level 5 — Physiological arousal: H0639G aph (the flared nostrils, 212 verses). The nostrils flare, the breathing quickens, the face flushes. This is the full somatic expression of anger at the body level.

Level 6 — Vocal expression: H5860A it (to scream, 1 verse — 1Sa 25:14 — the young man shouting in anger). The anger's vocal register — screaming, shouting, the raised voice of anger.

Level 7 — Physical action: H8474 ta.cha.rah (to contend, 2 verses), H7264 ra.gaz (to tremble/agitate, 40 verses). The anger moving into action — contending, fighting, the agitated body in conflict.

**The pro.30:33 somatic summary:** "For pressing milk produces curds, pressing the nose (aph) produces blood, and pressing anger (aph) produces strife." The word for "pressing anger" uses the same word (aph) as "pressing the nose" — the Proverb makes the somatic etymology explicit as a deliberate teaching tool. Anger, when pressed (sustained, accumulated, expressed without restraint), produces social bleeding as surely as pressing the nose produces physical bleeding.

### 5.2 God's Somatic Anger Vocabulary

The most striking feature of the anger vocabulary's somatic dimension: the same somatic vocabulary used for human anger is applied to God. Exo 15:8 — "at the blast of your nostrils (aph) the waters piled up." 2Sa 22:9 / Psa 18:8 — "smoke went up from his nostrils; devouring fire from his mouth." Hab 3:16 — "I hear, and my body trembles (ra.gaz)." Hos 11:8 — "my heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender" (cha.mam vocabulary in divine compassion). The biblical writers use the full somatic register to describe divine emotional states without apparently fearing the anthropomorphism. The somatic vocabulary is the *best available language* for divine inner states because it is the most concrete and experientially grounded language available to embodied creatures.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Anger as the Most Destructive Relational Force

Reading all 945 verses confirms: anger is the inner characteristic most consistently presented as relationally destructive when expressed without governance. Pro 22:24 — "make no friendship with a man given to anger, nor go with a wrathful man, lest you learn his ways and entangle yourself in a snare." Pro 29:22 — "a man of wrath stirs up strife, and one given to anger causes much transgression." Ecc 7:9 — "anger lodges in the heart of fools."

The relational destruction of anger has four modes from reading the verse set:
- **Relational severing:** The anger that ends friendship (Pro 22:24), that turns love to hatred (2Sa 13:15 — in grief registry too), that separates Jonathan from Saul (1Sa 20:34 — Jonathan rose in fierce anger and ate no food).
- **Relational contagion:** The anger that provokes answering anger (Pro 15:1 — "a harsh word stirs up anger"). Deu 20:8 — the fearful soldier's fear is contagious to the army; similarly, the angry person's anger kindles answering anger in the community.
- **Relational injustice:** The anger that produces wrongful action — the anger that kills (Gen 4:8 — Cain's murder following anger), the anger that destroys (the anger of Simeon and Levi condemned in Gen 49:6-7).
- **Relational provision for evil:** Eph 4:26-27 — the sustained anger that gives the adversary a foothold. Anger held overnight becomes the entry point for malevolent spiritual influence.

### 6.2 The Causative Chain

**Injustice/violation → anger (human):** The anger that is morally grounded — David's anger at Nathan's story (2Sa 12:5), Jesus's anger at the synagogue's hard hearts (Mar 3:5), Paul's spirit provoked at Athens's idolatry (Act 17:16 — paroxunō).

**Provocation → anger:** H3707 ka.as (52 verses) — the deliberate provocation that stirs up anger. The causative vocabulary is rich: the rival who provokes Hannah (1Sa 1:6), the people who provoke God (Judg 2:12, 1Ki 16:33, 2Ki 17:11, etc.).

**Anger → sin:** Gen 4:5-8 — anger → face falling → the crouching sin → murder. The causal chain from misdirected anger to violence is the first such chain in human history.

**Anger → strife:** Pro 15:18 — "a hot-tempered man stirs up strife." Pro 29:22 — anger causes much transgression. The relational cascade: one person's anger provokes another's, producing the social phenomenon of strife.

### 6.3 Cross-Registry Links — Verse-Level Grounding

**Anger ↔ Humility (reg 80 — SEMANTIC_OPPOSITION, G3709 orgē):** The orgē-humility opposition confirmed in Eph 4:31-32 — "let all... wrath (thumos) and anger (orgē)... be put away from you" immediately followed by "be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another." The anger vocabulary and the humility/kindness vocabulary are structural opposites in the same Ephesian passage.

**Anger ↔ Indignation (reg 1 — SHARED_TERM, H2194/H2195 za.am):** Za.am appears in both registries. The verse-level grounding: Psa 78:49 — "he let loose on them his burning anger (aph), wrath (che.mah), indignation (za.am), and distress" — four anger-register terms in one verse, confirming the registry overlap.

**Anger ↔ Jealousy/Zeal (reg 181 — SEMANTIC_OVERLAP, H2534 che.mah):** Pro 6:34 — "jealousy makes a man furious (che.mah)." Eze 16:42 — "I will satisfy my wrath (che.mah) on you, and my jealousy shall depart from you." The che.mah-jealousy pairing is consistent across the verse set.

**Anger ↔ Fear (H7264 ra.gaz — SHARED_TERM):** 2Sa 22:8 / Psa 18:7 — the earth trembles (ra.gaz) because God is angry (cha.rah). The same somatic term covers the trembling-fear response and the trembling-anger agitation. This is the deepest shared vocabulary between the two registries.

**Anger ↔ Spirit (H7307J — SHARED_TERM):** The ru.ach-temper vocabulary (10 verses) establishes that anger's regulation is a spirit-level matter. The Cross-Registry link (xref_spirit for H7307G,H,J and H7308) is confirmed at the verse level.

**Anger ↔ Love (Jam 1:20 — SEMANTIC_OPPOSITION):** "The anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God." Read against 1Co 13:5 — love "is not irritable (paroxunō)." The anger that does not produce righteousness is structurally opposed to the love that seeks the other's good. The Eph 4:31-32 passage makes this explicit — all anger-register words are commanded away, and the love-register words (kind, tenderhearted, forgiving) replace them.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — Eph 4:26

*"Be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger."*

Having read all 945 verses, this verse stands as the most structurally precise anger verse in the corpus. It does not prohibit anger. It permits and arguably commands it. But it hedges the permission with two conditions that define the legitimate form: the anger must not cross into sin, and it must not be held overnight.

The first condition — "do not sin" — implies a threshold between anger that is morally appropriate (responding to genuine injustice, mirroring the divine anger at covenant violation) and anger that has become sinful (self-righteous, disproportionate, vengeful, contemptuous). The verse does not specify where the threshold lies — that is wisdom's task. But it establishes that the threshold exists.

The second condition — "do not let the sun go down" — implies that anger's legitimate expression has a time limit. Anger held overnight becomes bitterness, becomes a grudge (na.tar), becomes the resident inner state that the adversary can exploit (v.27). The wisdom of the verse is practical: express the anger, address its cause, resolve it within the day. The anger that cannot be resolved within the day is becoming something other than anger.

Reading this verse against Gen 4:5-7 (Cain's face-falling and the crouching sin), Jon 4:1-9 (Jonah's anger and God's question "do you do well to be angry?"), and Mar 3:5 (Jesus's anger paired with grief at the hard heart) — Eph 4:26 synthesises the two-forms structure that runs across the whole registry: there is anger that is appropriate and anger that destroys, and the difference is its cause, its expression, and its duration.

### 7.2 Gen 4:5-7 — The Anatomy of Disordered Anger

*"Cain was very angry, and his face fell. The Lord said to Cain, 'Why are you angry, and why has your face fallen? If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin is crouching at the door. Its desire is for you, but you must rule over it.'"*

Having read all 945 verses, this is the first and structurally most important anger narrative in the corpus. Five elements:

The somatic expression — face fell (na.phal) — before any speech or action. The body announces the anger before the person acts.

The divine address — God speaks to Cain's anger directly, as he will later speak to Jonah's. The pattern: God meets human anger not with condemnation but with a question and an alternative.

The diagnosis — "if you do well." The anger's misdirection is identified: Cain is angry at God's preference rather than at his own offering's quality. The anger is self-referential rather than morally grounded.

The warning — sin is "crouching at the door." The anger that has been stoked and not addressed becomes the threshold through which a greater evil enters. The crouching sin waits for the unaddressed anger to give it an opening.

The command — "you must rule over it." The anger-as-crouching-sin must be mastered, governed, overruled. This is the most direct statement in the OT about anger's governance — it is not suppressed or denied but *ruled over*.

### 7.3 Mar 3:5 — The Anger of Jesus

*"And he looked around at them with anger, grieved at their hardness of heart, and said to the man, 'Stretch out your hand.' And he stretched it out, and his hand was restored."*

Having read all 8 orgizō verses, this is the only explicit attribution of anger to Jesus in the Gospels. Three elements are analytically decisive:

The anger is present. Jesus is angry. The text does not qualify or explain it away.

The anger is paired with grief. Sullupeomai — "grieved together with." The anger and the grief are simultaneous and mutually informing. The hardness of heart that refuses healing on the Sabbath produces two responses in Jesus: moral indignation (anger) and compassion-pain (grief). The anger is not coldly righteous; it is grieving.

The anger produces righteous action — healing. The anger does not turn inward, does not produce destructive speech, does not persist. It is expressed through the compassionate act that the hard hearts were trying to prevent.

This verse is the NT's clearest portrait of what rightly-ordered anger looks like in practice: grounded in genuine injustice (a person denied healing for religious correctness), paired with grief for the situation, expressed in righteous action, and resolved in the act of healing. The two-forms structure applied: this is anger that does not sin.

### 7.4 Jon 4:1-4,9 — God's Question to Human Anger

*"Do you do well to be angry?" ... "I do well to be angry, angry enough to die."*

Having read all 88 cha.rah verses, this exchange between God and Jonah stands as the most sustained divine examination of human anger in the corpus. Jonah's anger at God's mercy for Nineveh is presented without resolution — God asks the question three times; Jonah never gives the right answer, and the book ends in the air of the unanswered question.

The question "do you do well to be angry?" is not rhetorical — it genuinely asks Jonah to evaluate his anger against a moral standard. The standard is implicit: the divine anger's righteous cause (covenant violation, injustice, contempt for the name). Jonah's anger fails this test: it is anger at God's mercy, anger that something evil was not destroyed, anger motivated by wounded expectations rather than genuine moral outrage.

The contrast with divine anger across the whole register is stark: God's anger at Nineveh was delayed, purposive, and — on Nineveh's repentance — withheld (Jon 3:10). Jonah's anger is immediate, self-referential, and absolute ("angry enough to die"). The divine and human anger vocabularies converge in their somatic-fire register but diverge completely in their moral structure.

### 7.5 Psa 78:49 and Nah 1:6 — The Full Weight of Divine Anger

*"He let loose on them his burning anger, wrath, indignation, and distress, a company of destroying angels." / "Who can stand before his indignation? Who can endure the heat of his anger? His wrath is poured out like fire, and the rocks are broken into pieces by him."*

Having read all the multi-term anger passages, these two verses represent the fullest expression of divine anger in the corpus. Psa 78:49 stacks four anger terms: cha.ron aph (burning anger), ev.rah (wrath/fury), za.am (indignation), tsa.rah (distress). The divine anger is not a single faculty — it is a compound of every intensity and register of anger, deployed together.

Nah 1:6 sets up the rhetorical impossibility: no one can stand before the divine anger. The rocks are broken. The fire pours out. This is not anger as frustrated preference — it is the eschatological force of the Creator's moral response to sustained evil, poured out with the force that made and can unmake creation.

Reading these verses against the "slow to anger" tradition and the Hos 11:9 limit ("for I am God and not a man, the Holy One in your midst") confirms the structure: divine anger is maximally serious in its expression *and* maximally restrained in its deployment. Both are true and necessary together.

### 7.6 Hos 11:8-9 — The Divine Self-Restraint

*"How can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel?... My heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender. I will not execute my burning anger; I will not again destroy Ephraim; for I am God and not a man, the Holy One in your midst, and I will not come in wrath."*

Having read all 40 cha.ron verses and the full ra.cham/compassion vocabulary, this passage stands at the intersection of the anger and love registries — the only place in the corpus where divine anger and divine compassion are explicitly held in tension within the same speech. God announces the anger (cha.ron), then immediately revokes its execution. The reason: "my heart recoils (nehepak libby — my heart turns over); my compassion grows warm (cha.mam — the same warming vocabulary as the anger's buildup)." The same thermal vocabulary that describes anger's buildup (cha.mam) describes compassion's stirring. Both anger and compassion heat the divine heart from the inside. The love wins — not because the anger was unjustified but because "I am God and not a man." The divine nature holds the anger within the larger frame of everlasting love. This is the clearest statement in the OT of the two-forms relationship between divine anger and divine love: they coexist, the love is larger, and the decision not to destroy is the divine mercy asserting itself against the divine wrath.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field

**Neuroscience and psychology** — both are primary for anger. The neuroscience of anger (amygdala, the threat-appraisal system, the stress response) and the psychology of anger (functions, regulation, expression, clinical dimensions) are equally relevant.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — The dual-process model of anger (LeDoux, *The Emotional Brain*):** The fast pathway (thalamus → amygdala) produces the immediate anger response before cognitive appraisal. The slow pathway (thalamus → cortex → amygdala) allows appraisal and contextualisation. The biblical wisdom literature's "slow to anger" tradition maps precisely onto this: the wise person has a longer slow-pathway that intercepts the fast-pathway response before it produces action. The short-of-spirit person has an inadequate slow-pathway.

**Finding 2 — The function and dysregulation of anger (clinical psychology broadly):** Anger is a biologically adaptive response to threat and injustice — it mobilises action, signals boundary violations, motivates social correction. This functional account is consistent with the biblical recognition that anger can be appropriate (Eph 4:26 — "be angry"). The clinical literature's account of anger dysregulation — chronic, disproportionate, or displaced anger — maps onto the biblical account of the anger that sins, the anger that lodges in the heart of fools.

**Finding 3 — Emotional granularity (Feldman Barrett, *How Emotions Are Made*):** The ability to distinguish fine-grained emotional states (distinguishing anger from contempt, from irritation, from moral outrage) produces better emotion regulation. The biblical anger vocabulary's granularity — distinguishing aph, che.mah, cha.rah, cha.ron, za.am, na.tar, ka.as — is a form of emotional granularity built into the language. The culture that uses these terms learns to distinguish the forms of anger; the culture that collapses them into one word loses the capacity to see the distinctions the wisdom literature draws.

**Finding 4 — Physiological effects of chronic anger (Sapolsky, *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*):** Sustained anger produces elevated cortisol, cardiovascular strain, and immune suppression. The na.tar (grudge-keeping) that the Bible prohibits and the grief it produces are exactly what clinical research identifies as physically harmful sustained states.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Mechanistic convergence:** The thermal, somatic phenomenology of the anger vocabulary (warming → kindling → burning → expression) corresponds to the physiological account of anger's development: sympathetic nervous system activation, epinephrine release, cardiovascular arousal, facial expression, and finally motor action.

**Structural convergence:** The distinction between functional anger (appropriate response to genuine threat or injustice) and dysfunctional anger (disproportionate, chronic, displaced) is present in both the biblical and scientific accounts. Eph 4:26's two-forms structure (anger that does not sin vs anger that becomes bitterness) anticipates the clinical distinction between adaptive and maladaptive anger.

**Register divergence:** Science describes anger as a threat-appraisal response shaped by evolution, attachment history, and neural architecture. The Bible describes divine anger as the righteous response of the Holy One to covenant violation and injustice — a response that has moral weight, not merely biological function.

**Genuine divergence — the anger of God.** The attribution of anger to God — as a genuine inner response, not a metaphor — has no scientific parallel. The scientific study of anger is the study of a creaturely neurological state. The divine anger of the biblical account is something categorically different that uses the same vocabulary. The register divergence is total: science studies the mechanism; the Bible describes its ultimate referent.

**Genuine divergence — rightly-ordered anger at evil.** Science has no normative account of what anger *should* be oriented toward. The biblical account has a very precise normative account: anger should mirror the divine anger — oriented toward genuine injustice and covenant violation, slow to ignite, expressed proportionately, resolved within the day, never sustained into bitterness. The science describes the mechanism; the Bible describes the standard.
