# Seven Principles for the Sound Interpretation of a Biblical Verse
### A Methodological Synthesis from Historical and Modern Biblical Scholarship

---

## A note on method (and an honest caveat)

There is no universally agreed list of "seven principles." The activity of careful interpretation is genuinely stable across the discipline, but different traditions organise it differently:

- The **medieval *Quadriga*** read every text in four senses — literal, allegorical, moral (tropological), and anagogical — captured in the mnemonic attributed to Augustine of Dacia (13th c.): *littera gesta docet, quid credas allegoria, moralis quid agas, quo tendas anagogia.*
- The **Antiochene school** (Theodore of Mopsuestia, John Chrysostom) prioritised the literal-historical sense; the **Alexandrian school** (Origen, Clement) favoured allegory.
- **Modern critical scholarship** distributes the work across a family of "criticisms" — textual (lower), source, form, redaction, tradition, narrative, rhetorical, and canonical.
- **Reader-response and postmodern** approaches relocate meaning toward the reader and the act of reading, contesting the assumption that a single recoverable authorial meaning governs the text.

The seven principles below are therefore a **deliberate synthesis** of the *grammatical-historical*, *historical-critical*, and *canonical-theological* mainstreams. They are sequenced roughly as a workflow: from establishing the words on the page (exegesis) to discerning their contemporary force (hermeneutics proper). Where scholars genuinely disagree, this is flagged rather than smoothed over.

## The two test verses

- **Old Testament — Isaiah 7:14**: *"Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign: Behold, the young woman (Heb. הָעַלְמָה, ha-‘almah) shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel."*
- **New Testament — John 1:1**: *Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος* — *"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."*

Both were chosen because each exercises all seven techniques *and* sits on a real scholarly fault line, which makes them honest teaching cases rather than tidy ones.

---

## Principle 1 — Establish the Text (Textual Criticism)

**The method.** Before a verse can be interpreted, the interpreter must determine, as far as the manuscript evidence allows, what it actually says. This is "lower criticism": the comparison of manuscripts, ancient versions, and citations to recover the earliest recoverable reading. Guiding maxims include *lectio difficilior potior* (the harder reading is likelier original, because scribes tend to smooth difficulties) and the weighing of witnesses by date, geography, and text-type rather than mere counting.

**Key voices.** For the Hebrew Bible: Emanuel Tov, *Textual Criticism of the Hebrew Bible* — working from the Masoretic Text, the Septuagint (LXX), the Samaritan Pentateuch, and the Dead Sea Scrolls. For the New Testament: Bruce Metzger, *The Text of the New Testament* and *A Textual Commentary on the Greek New Testament*, behind the critical apparatus of the Nestle-Aland / UBS Greek editions.

**Isaiah 7:14.** The Masoretic Text reads *ha-‘almah*. The great Qumran scroll **1QIsaᵃ** confirms the Hebrew consonantal tradition here, so the *Hebrew* text is secure. The interpretive drama enters at the level of **translation**, not text: the LXX renders *‘almah* with Greek *παρθένος* (parthenos, "virgin"). That is a versional decision the textual critic must register and the lexical step (Principle 2) must then weigh.

**John 1:1.** The text of 1:1 is remarkably stable across the manuscript tradition. The honest illustration of textual criticism in this passage actually lies a few verses down at **John 1:18**, where witnesses divide between *μονογενὴς θεός* ("the only begotten God / the unique one, himself God") and *μονογενὴς υἱός* ("the only begotten Son"). The choice has direct christological weight — a clean example of how establishing the text precedes interpreting it.

> *Note on perspective:* textual criticism is the least confessionally divided of the seven steps; conservative and critical scholars largely share method and apparatus here.

---

## Principle 2 — Analyse the Original Language (Lexical, Grammatical, Syntactical)

**The method.** Words carry meaning *in use*, governed by grammar and syntax, not by etymology or by importing every possible sense at once. James Barr's *The Semantics of Biblical Language* (1961) is the standard warning against two recurring fallacies: **etymologising** (deriving present meaning from a word's roots) and **illegitimate totality transfer** (loading a word's every possible meaning into a single occurrence). The classic Protestant statement of disciplined language work is Milton S. Terry's *Biblical Hermeneutics* (1883), the grammatical-historical method.

**Isaiah 7:14.** The crux is the semantic range of *‘almah*. It denotes a young woman of marriageable age; it does **not** technically specify virginity, for which Hebrew has *betulah* (and even *betulah*'s precision is debated). The LXX's choice of *parthenos* is therefore an interpretive narrowing. This single lexical fact is the hinge of centuries of dispute (developed under Principle 6).

**John 1:1.** Two grammatical features carry the theology. First, *πρὸς τὸν θεόν* ("with God") connotes relationship and orientation — the Word is distinct from, and in company with, God. Second, in *θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος* the predicate noun *theos* is **anarthrous** (lacks the article) and precedes the verb. E. C. Colwell's 1933 rule observed that definite predicate nouns preceding the verb often drop the article, which argues against rendering it "a god." But Daniel Wallace (*Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics*, 1996) and others refine this: the construction is best read as **qualitative** — the Word shares the *nature* of God without being numerically identical to "the God" (ho theos) of the previous clause.

> *Note on perspective:* the rejection of the indefinite "a god" rendering (associated with the New World Translation) is near-universal among Greek grammarians; the *qualitative-vs-definite* nuance is the live scholarly question.

---

## Principle 3 — Reconstruct the Historical-Cultural Context

**The method.** A verse was written by someone, to someone, in a setting, about an occasion. The interpreter reconstructs author, date, audience, and the political, social, religious, and geographical world assumed by the text. This is the engine of the historical-critical method and of "Introduction" scholarship.

**Isaiah 7:14.** The oracle is set in the **Syro-Ephraimite crisis** (c. 735–732 BCE). Rezin of Aram (Syria) and Pekah of Israel (Ephraim) pressured King **Ahaz of Judah** to join an anti-Assyrian coalition and threatened to depose him. Isaiah confronts the frightened king and offers a *sign*. The name *Immanu-El*, "God with us," is a pointed reassurance to a specific king in a specific siege — the historical horizon must be honoured before any later horizon is added.

**John 1:1.** The Fourth Gospel is generally dated to the late first century CE and traditionally associated with Ephesus. The Prologue's *Logos* presupposes a layered milieu: Greek philosophy (the Stoic and Heraclitean *logos* as cosmic rational principle), Hellenistic Judaism (Philo's mediating *Logos*), the Aramaic Targumic *Memra* ("Word of the LORD"), and above all the Hebrew "word of the LORD" by which creation occurs in Genesis 1. The author writes into a world where "Word" already resonated on several frequencies at once.

---

## Principle 4 — Read in Literary Context

**The method.** The unit of meaning is rarely the isolated verse; it is the verse within its paragraph, the paragraph within its book, and the book within its argument. The hermeneutical maxim "a text without a context is a pretext" circulates widely (its exact origin is uncertain, so I cite it as a common saying, not as a specific scholar's coinage).

**Isaiah 7:14.** The verse belongs to the sign-narrative of **Isaiah 7:1–17** and to the cluster of "Immanuel" passages running through chapters 7–9 (the child of 9:6, "for to us a child is born"). Read in context, the sign first concerns deliverance from the *two kings* Ahaz fears (7:16: "before the boy knows...").

**John 1:1.** The verse opens the **Prologue (1:1–18)**, a tightly structured overture that frames the entire Gospel. Its first words, *Ἐν ἀρχῇ* ("In the beginning"), form an unmistakable **inclusio with Genesis 1:1**, signalling new creation. The Prologue introduces themes — light, life, witness, glory, "the world knew him not" — that the narrative will then dramatise.

---

## Principle 5 — Identify the Genre (Literary Form)

**The method.** Different literary forms operate by different rules; reading poetry as if it were chronicle, or apocalyptic as if it were legal prose, produces error. **Hermann Gunkel** pioneered *form criticism* (identifying *Gattungen*, "types," and their *Sitz im Leben*, "setting in life"). **Robert Lowth**'s *Lectures on the Sacred Poetry of the Hebrews* (1753) established the analysis of Hebrew **parallelism** (synonymous, antithetic, synthetic). Gordon Fee and Douglas Stuart's *How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth* organises the entire interpretive task by genre.

**Isaiah 7:14.** The verse is a **prophetic sign-oracle** — a divinely offered *’ot* (sign) embedded in confrontational prophetic speech. Sign-oracles characteristically point beyond themselves and can carry both an immediate referent and a larger trajectory, which is precisely why the fulfilment question (Principle 6) is so contested.

**John 1:1.** The verse opens what many scholars regard as a **hymn or hymnic prologue** (the hymn hypothesis is debated, not settled), set within the larger genre of the Gospel — an ancient *bios* (life) with a distinctly theological purpose stated in 20:31. Reading 1:1 as soaring theological poetry, not flat prose, conditions how its compressed clauses are weighed.

---

## Principle 6 — Interpret Canonically and Theologically (the Analogy of Scripture)

**The method.** Within the believing traditions, Scripture is read as a coherent whole: *scriptura sui ipsius interpres* ("Scripture is its own interpreter," a Reformation principle associated with Luther) and the *analogia fidei* / *analogia Scripturae* ("analogy of faith / of Scripture"), whereby clearer passages illuminate obscurer ones. **Brevard Childs**'s *canonical approach* (*Introduction to the Old Testament as Scripture*, 1979) reads each text in its final canonical shape. Intertextuality and biblical theology operate here.

**Isaiah 7:14.** This is the great test case. **Matthew 1:23** quotes the verse — in its LXX form (*parthenos*) — and applies it to the virgin conception of Jesus. Scholarship divides:
- A **near-fulfilment / historical** reading: the sign concerned a child in Ahaz's own time (candidates include Hezekiah, Isaiah's own son, or the child of 8:1–4), guaranteeing deliverance within a generation.
- A **messianic / direct-prediction** reading: the verse looks beyond Ahaz to the virgin birth of the Messiah.
- A **dual / typological ("near-far") fulfilment** reading: an initial eighth-century referent that prefigures a fuller, later realisation.

Critical and confessional scholars weight these differently, and one's view of *‘almah* (Principle 2) and of how Matthew uses the OT largely determines the outcome. This is a place to *present the options*, not to adjudicate them as though settled.

**John 1:1.** Canonically the verse reverberates with Genesis 1 (creation by divine speech), the personified **Wisdom** of Proverbs 8 (present "at the beginning"), and the high-christology hymns of Colossians 1:15–20 and Hebrews 1:1–3. Read across the canon, "the Word was God" became a load-bearing text for Nicene trinitarian theology — distinguishing the Word from "the God" he is "with," while affirming shared deity.

---

## Principle 7 — Bridge from "What It Meant" to "What It Means" (Hermeneutics and Application)

**The method.** The final move distinguishes the text's original sense from its present significance. **Krister Stendahl** (article "Biblical Theology, Contemporary," *Interpreter's Dictionary of the Bible*, 1962) framed this as the difference between *what it meant* and *what it means*. **E. D. Hirsch** (*Validity in Interpretation*, 1967) drew the parallel distinction between fixed authorial **meaning** and changing **significance**. **Hans-Georg Gadamer** (*Truth and Method*, 1960) described interpretation as a *fusion of horizons* between the text's world and the reader's. On the application side, **Walter Kaiser** (*Toward an Exegetical Theology*, 1981) advocates "principlising" — extracting the abiding principle to re-apply responsibly.

**Isaiah 7:14.** *What it meant*: God pledged his presence ("God with us") to a terrified king amid a real geopolitical threat. *What it means*: read forward through Matthew and the church's confession, it grounds the conviction that God is decisively "with us" in the incarnation — and, principlised, it speaks to divine presence in human crisis.

**John 1:1.** *What it meant*: the eternal, divine Word who was the agent of creation became the subject of the Gospel's narrative. *What it means*: it anchors a theology of incarnation — that the transcendent God is knowable because the Word "became flesh" (1:14) — with continuing force for how communities understand revelation and the person of Christ.

> *Note on perspective:* this step is where confessional and purely historical-critical readers most openly part company — the former treating contemporary appropriation as a legitimate goal of interpretation, the latter often restricting the task to recovering original meaning.

## Summary

| # | Principle | Isaiah 7:14 | John 1:1 |
|---|-----------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Establish the text | Hebrew secure (1QIsaᵃ); LXX renders *‘almah* as *parthenos* | 1:1 stable; apparatus drama at 1:18 (*theos*/*huios*) |
| 2 | Original language | *‘almah* = young woman, not strictly "virgin" (*betulah*) | Anarthrous, qualitative *theos*; *pros ton theon* = relationship |
| 3 | Historical context | Syro-Ephraimite crisis (c. 735–732 BCE), King Ahaz | Late 1st c. CE; Greek/Jewish *Logos* milieu |
| 4 | Literary context | Sign-narrative 7:1–17; Immanuel cluster (chs. 7–9) | Prologue 1:1–18; inclusio with Genesis 1:1 |
| 5 | Genre | Prophetic sign-oracle | Hymnic prologue within a Gospel (*bios*) |
| 6 | Canonical / theological | Matthew 1:23; near / messianic / dual-fulfilment debate | Genesis 1; Prov 8; Col 1; Heb 1; Nicene christology |
| 7 | Meaning → significance | "God with us" in crisis → incarnation | Divine creating Word → theology of incarnation |
