# PASS 1 — CATALOGUE APPLICATION

---

## Section 1 Questions — Word Characteristic Summary

---

**Q001: What is the structural disposition of the word — where does it originate?**

ANSWER. Love is an orientation of attachment and self-giving — directed always toward an object. It does not originate in the human person: "we love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19). Divine love is the originating source; human love is secondary before it is primary.

---

**Q002: What determines whether the word is extended or withheld?**

ANSWER. The direction of love is determined by its object — whatever the person has chosen to attach to most deeply. Extension of love toward God, neighbour, and enemy is commanded and volitional. Withholding or misdirection follows from prior attachment to wrong objects (darkness, money, self, pleasure).

---

**Q003: What are the distinct modes of operation of the word in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Three modes held simultaneously: (1) feeling — warm attachment, longing, tenderness; (2) settled disposition — stable faithfulness and loyalty independent of momentary feeling; (3) choice — something that can be commanded, cultivated, trained, and directed. The biblical vocabulary holds all three together without collapsing one into another.

---

**Q004: What does the word produce in the inner being of the recipient?**

ANSWER. Stability of character — patience, kindness, freedom from envy and arrogance. Freedom from anxiety about self (the self is no longer the primary concern). Openness toward those who make no natural claim on affection. In the person toward whom divine love is directed: identity constituted by being-loved (*agapētos* — beloved, the primary designation before any description of conduct).

---

**Q005: What does the word produce in the relational position of the recipient toward another?**

ANSWER. Openness toward enemies and outsiders. Inversion of status competition: where status competition produces hierarchy, love produces a race toward servanthood. Access and belonging in community.

---

**Q006: What does the word reveal about the disposition of God toward the human being?**

ANSWER. Love is named as what God *is* — not merely what God does (1 John 4:8, 16). The divine love is unilateral, prior, directed toward the undeserving world (John 3:16), particular (Galatians 2:20 — "who loved me"), and constitutive of the Son's identity (Matthew 3:17). It is irreducibly relational and personal.

---

**Q007: What does the word reveal about the basis on which God's disposition toward a person is established?**

ANSWER. Not on the basis of merit — directed toward the world "in its estrangement" (John 3:16). God's love precedes human response (1 John 4:19). The *agapētos* designation is given before any description of conduct (Romans 1:7 — "loved by God and called to be saints").

---

**Q008: Where, somatically or relationally, does the word locate the reality of being in favour?**

ANSWER. The heart is the primary address — love is received there (Romans 5:5), commanded there, written there (Proverbs 3:3), can be concealed there as hatred (Leviticus 19:17). The seal upon the heart and the arm (Song of Songs 8:6) places love simultaneously at the affective centre and the site of action and identity.

---

**Q009: Does the word describe a one-time act or an ongoing condition — and how are these distinguished?**

ANSWER. Both. God's love is a completed and permanent prior act (1 John 4:19 — "he first loved us"). It is also an ongoing poured-in condition (Romans 5:5). Human love is both a single directional commitment and an ongoing disposition of faithfulness — chesed's steadfastness means it persists through changing circumstances.

---

**Q010: What spatial or directional language is used to describe the ongoing condition the word produces?**

ANSWER. Love is described as the governing orientation from which everything else flows. Directionality is central — love is always toward someone. Song of Songs 8:6 — the seal upon the heart and arm. Peace as the inner state produced: "he will quiet you by his love" (Zephaniah 3:17 — spatial stilling).

---

**Q011: Does the word produce enabling capacity in the inner being — and if so, what kind?**

ANSWER. Yes — love poured into the heart by the Spirit (Romans 5:5) enables human love as such. Love is the enabling ground of the whole moral and relational life. The capacity for love of enemies (Matthew 5:44) requires divine enabling since the command cannot be addressed to the emotions alone.

---

**Q012: What character quality does the word produce in the inner being?**

ANSWER. The stable dispositions of 1 Corinthians 13: patience, kindness, freedom from envy and arrogance, freedom from self-assertion. These are not momentary states but the inner life organised away from self-assertion. Chesed's faithfulness — the refusal to let circumstances determine the conclusion.

---

**Q013: How does the word express itself in relation to others?**

ANSWER. As self-giving at cost (John 15:13 — laying down one's life). As covering of offences (Proverbs 10:12). As servanthood and honour-giving (Romans 12:10). As community formation — the publicly legible mark of discipleship (John 13:35). As obedience (John 14:15 — love of Christ expresses itself as obedience).

---

**Q014: What inner condition or orientation is identified as the ground of genuine expression of the word?**

ANSWER. Prior receipt of divine love — "we love because he first loved us." The inner person rightly oriented toward God (Matthew 22:37 — whole-person integration). Received forgiveness (Luke 7:47 — forgiven much → loves much).

---

**Q015: Is there a discernible sequence or movement in the way the word operates through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Yes: divine love as prior act → poured into the heart by the Spirit → constitutes identity and standing → produces stable inner dispositions → expresses outward in self-giving, servanthood, community. The 1 John 4:19 causal sequence is the programme's most concentrated statement of this movement.

---

**Q016: What direction of movement does the word follow within and through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Inward first — received from its divine source, poured into the heart. Then outward — toward God, neighbour, enemy, community. Then, at its fullest, toward the beloved at great cost (John 15:13). The word also has a depth dimension: it persists below conscious attention — the heart continues its orientation toward the beloved even in sleep (Song of Songs).

---

**Q017: What does the word identify as its originating source?**

ANSWER. Divine love — explicitly and consistently. 1 John 4:8 — God *is* love. Romans 5:5 — poured in by the Spirit. 1 John 4:19 — he first loved. The divine source is not merely the model but the enabling ground of all human love.

---

**Q018: What does the word reveal about the origin of the human capacity to seek it or exercise it?**

ANSWER. The human capacity for love as such is grounded in the prior divine act. 1 John 4:19 — the verb has no object ("we love" not "we love him") — meaning divine love is the ground of the entire human capacity for love, not merely of love directed back toward God. Love is received before it is exercised.

---

**Q019: Does the word share an etymological root with another inner-being characteristic — and if so, what is that characteristic?**

ANSWER. Multiple root-family connections: RACHAM root spans love, compassion, and mercy. DOD root is shared with goodness and desire. TOV root connects love and goodness. AGAV root connects love and desire. The vocabulary has the highest term-sharing ratio in the programme (0.701) — reflecting love's structural position at the centre of the inner-being vocabulary.

---

**Q020: What does the word produce as a natural inner response in the one who receives it?**

ANSWER. Peace — "he will quiet you by his love" (Zephaniah 3:17). Freedom from anxiety. Stability. Identity (the beloved is constituted as beloved). Joy (love heads the fruit-of-the-Spirit list, joy follows immediately). Also awe — the Father's declaration of love for the Son (Matthew 3:17) constitutes identity and calling.

## Section 2 Questions — Word Impact Description

---

**Q021: What inner-being logic or orientation functions as the structural opposite of the word?**

ANSWER. Hatred — a structural contrary, not merely an absence. Named as the constitutive opposed inner disposition. Both love and hatred operate first in the heart before becoming visible in conduct. Both can be caused in another (causative stems parallel in the Hebrew vocabulary).

---

**Q022: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to receive the word?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop merit-logic as a barrier to receiving love explicitly. The closest is the observation that divine love is directed toward the undeserving world — merit is simply not a precondition. The barrier to receiving love is not merit-logic but the wrong ordering of loves (loving darkness rather than light, John 3:19).

---

**Q023: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to extend the word to others?**

PARTIAL. Not developed directly. The enemy-love command (Matthew 5:44) is the most pointed evidence that the word is extended regardless of merit — but the merit-logic as a specific barrier to extension is not developed in the same way as for forgiveness.

---

**Q024: What is the inner-being condition of the person who has received the word but failed to recognise it?**

PARTIAL. The study does not name a specific failure-recognition term. The nearest is the person who loves the darkness (John 3:19) — not failure to recognise love received, but failure of direction. The person who has Demas's trajectory: love for the present world already established, other loyalties displaced.

---

**Q025: What inner-being experience cultivates the capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. Prior experience of being loved — the causal sequence of 1 John 4:19. Received forgiveness (Luke 7:47). The practice of covenant faithfulness over time. Love can be deepened by community formation and is the subject of apostolic prayer (1 Thessalonians 3:12 — prayer that love may increase and abound).

---

**Q026: What inner-being posture does the act of earnest appeal express?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop a seeking-love posture in the same way as grace-seeking or forgiveness-seeking. The nearest is the act of directing the whole inner person toward God (Matthew 22:37 — heart, soul, and mind). But appeal to divine love is not developed as a distinct inner posture here.

---

**Q027: What somatic forms does the inner act of seeking the word take?**

PARTIAL. Seeking love as a distinct act is not developed. The somatic expressions of love in the study are primarily expressions of love extended (Luke 15:20 — running, embracing, kissing; Luke 7:38 — tears, hair, anointing) rather than love sought.

---

**Q028: What inner-being orientation closes off the reception of the word?**

ANSWER. Love of darkness (John 3:19) — the inner state from which evil works proceed, named as the cause not the symptom. Misdirected attachment (self-love, money-love, pleasure-love) hollows love out. Prior attachment to wrong objects proves stronger than the relational bond it displaces (Demas — love of the present world).

---

**Q029: What relational orientation toward others diminishes the operation of the word?**

ANSWER. Status competition and self-promotion — the inner logic that love inverts. Hatred — the deliberate concealment of enmity in the heart (Leviticus 19:17). Accumulation of goods and pursuit of approval (diminishes love's outward expression progressively).

---

**Q030: What is the relationship between the word and conditions of weakness in the inner being?**

PARTIAL. Not developed as a structural feature equivalent to grace's inhabiting of weakness. The closest is Isaiah 49:15 — God's love compared to a mother's love for a nursing child, surpassing even that when it fails. Love toward the vulnerable and suffering is present (Luke 15:20) but weakness as the theatre of love is not made explicit.

---

**Q031: What kind of inner-being transformation does the word produce — does it change the condition or the orientation?**

ANSWER. Both. The stable dispositions of 1 Corinthians 13 are a transformed character. The identity-constituting act of being named *agapētos* is a transformation of the person's fundamental self-understanding. The whole moral and relational life is reorganised: "where it is rightly ordered, everything else in the inner life tends toward its proper form."

---

**Q032: What is the first inner-being response to receiving the word unexpectedly?**

PARTIAL. Not developed as a distinct moment in the way grace generates fear and forgiveness generates awe. The nearest is the prodigal's father scene (Luke 15:20) — but this is the giver's response, not the receiver's immediate first response. The son's response (rehearsed speech, being silenced by embrace) is present but not analysed as a first inner response.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. The study does not examine the first inner response of the person who unexpectedly discovers they are loved. This is a gap.

---

**Q033: What is the sequence of inner-being states as the word takes hold in the person?**

ANSWER. Implied throughout: divine love received → identity constituted → stable dispositions formed → outward expression in conduct. The Romans 5:5 — Romans 5:1–5 sequence also implies: grace → peace → endurance → character → hope, with love poured in at the base.

---

**Q034: In the eschatological context, what is the first inner-being response to the full expression of the word?**

ANSWER. The new covenant vision (Hosea; Jeremiah 31; Zephaniah 3:17) — love producing rest and inner stillness ("he will quiet you by his love"), and universal knowledge of God as the eschatological condition in which love will be the inner condition of all.

---

**Q035: What is the relationship between mourning and the full reception of the word?**

PARTIAL. Not developed directly for love. Lamentations 3:22 is cited — the counter-claim of chesed asserted precisely when circumstances suggest it has ceased. Mourning as a context in which love is asserted rather than felt is present but not developed as mourning produced by love received.

---

**Q036: What quality of inner-being stability does the word produce?**

ANSWER. Chesed's steadfastness — not a stable emotion but a stable orientation that does not let circumstances determine its conclusion. The stable dispositions of 1 Corinthians 13. Love persists below conscious attention — the heart continues its orientation toward the beloved even in sleep.

---

**Q037: What orientation does the stability produced by the word carry?**

ANSWER. Forward — love is the ground of hope (faith-love-hope triad, 1 Thessalonians 5:8). The eschatological vision of love as universal inner condition is the forward reach of present love. Love also orients outward — toward God, neighbour, enemy simultaneously.

---

**Q038: What capacity toward others does the word produce in the inner being of the one who has received it?**

ANSWER. Capacity for enemy-love (Matthew 5:44 — commanded to the will). Capacity for community formation. Capacity for servanthood and honour-giving. Capacity for self-giving at great cost (John 15:13). The capacity is not self-generated but enabled by received love.

---

**Q039: What is the inner-being source of the capacity to extend the word to others — discipline or transformation?**

ANSWER. Transformation — love poured in by the Spirit (Romans 5:5), prior to and enabling human effort. The verb in 1 John 4:19 is causal: the prior divine act is the ground of the entire human capacity. Apostolic prayer for love to increase and abound (1 Thessalonians 3:12) suggests the source is ongoing reception, not accumulated discipline.

---

**Q040: How does the word relate to genuine human effort — does it displace it, supplement it, or reconstitute it?**

ANSWER. Reconstitutes it. Love is commanded — it is volitional, can be directed, trained, cultivated. The command to love enemies addresses the will. But the ground of the capacity is received divine love. The paradox is present though not named as explicitly as in grace (1 Corinthians 15:10).

---

**Q041: What does the word reveal about the source of its human expression — generated from within or received?**

ANSWER. Received before given — stated as the programme's broadest finding. The 1 John 4:19 verb without object makes the point maximally: divine love enables human love as such, not only love directed back toward God.

## Section 3 Questions — Verse Evidence

---

**Q042 type: What is the structural relationship between two elements named in a verse?**

ANSWER (1 John 4:19). Prior divine love is the cause; human love is the effect. The causal sequence is unambiguous and the verb has no object.

---

**Q043 type: What does the verse explicitly rule out as the cause of the outcome?**

ANSWER (John 3:16; Romans 1:7). Merit — love directed toward the world in its estrangement. The *agapētos* designation precedes conduct, not follows it.

---

**Q044 type: What category does the verse assign to what is given?**

ANSWER (Romans 5:5). A substance poured in — delivered by the Spirit to the heart. Not a commandment or model but a gift of a different order.

---

**Q045 type: What prior condition does the access-vocabulary imply?**

ANSWER (John 3:19; Galatians 2:20). Prior condition: estrangement from God, misdirected attachment, the fallen person. Love moves toward what does not merit it.

---

**Q046 type: Does the verse describe the outcome as a completed event or a continuing state?**

ANSWER (1 John 4:8; Romans 5:5). Both. God *is* love — permanent quality, not event. Love poured in is both a completed act and an ongoing indwelling condition.

---

**Q047 type: What is the context in which the word operates, and what alternative was present?**

ANSWER (Matthew 5:44). The context is the presence of enemies and persecution. The alternative is the natural response of hatred or avoidance. Love is commanded precisely where the natural response would be otherwise.

---

**Q048 type: In what mode is the word conveyed — relational address or impersonal provision?**

ANSWER. Both — love is relational in its deepest expressions (Galatians 2:20 — "who loved me," singular and personal) and also given as substance by the Spirit (Romans 5:5 — poured into hearts). The personal and the pneumatic are both present.

---

**Q049 type: What condition does the verse identify as the location where the word is most fully operative?**

ANSWER. Love's fullest operation is named in self-giving at the point of greatest cost (John 15:13). Also in conditions of failure — love covers offences (Proverbs 10:12), the father runs to the returning son who has squandered everything (Luke 15:20).

---

**Q050 type: What is the relationship between the word and the condition in which it operates?**

ANSWER. Love does not require favourable conditions — chesed's steadfastness is asserted against circumstances, not because of them (Lamentations 3:22). Love toward enemies operates precisely where conditions would justify the opposite.

---

**Q051 type: What reason does the verse give for the extension of the word?**

ANSWER (John 3:16; Galatians 2:20; 1 John 4:19). The reason is in the giver, not the recipient — divine initiative, prior and unilateral. No merit in the recipient is named as basis.

---

**Q052 type: Where does the verse locate the reality of the word in the giver?**

ANSWER. The heart (Romans 5:5 — poured into our hearts). The inner being of God — 1 John 4:8: God is love. The Spirit as the deliverer.

---

**Q053 type: What practical outcome does the word produce for the recipient?**

ANSWER. Identity constituted (*agapētos* — beloved). Peace (Zephaniah 3:17). Stable character (1 Corinthians 13). Community membership and belonging (John 13:35). Calling (Romans 1:7 — "loved by God and called to be saints").

---

**Q054 type: What inner state does the verse identify as the first response to the announcement of the word?**

PARTIAL. Not examined as a distinct moment. The nearest is the prodigal son's return — but the father's response is developed, not the son's first inner state on being embraced.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** Same gap as Q032. The first inner response of the one who is loved — particularly unexpectedly — is not examined.

---

**Q055 type: What comes first — establishment of standing or commission?**

ANSWER (Romans 1:7; Matthew 3:17). Belovedness precedes calling — "loved by God and called to be saints." The Father's declaration of love for the Son grounds his identity and calling. Standing as beloved is constitutive prior to commission.

---

**Q056 type: What does the verse identify as the concrete content of the word given?**

ANSWER. John 3:16 — the gift of the Son. Romans 5:5 — love itself, poured in. Zephaniah 3:17 — rest, quieting. The content ranges from the supreme act of giving to the inner condition of peace.

---

**Q057 type: What spatial language does the verse use?**

ANSWER. Into the heart (Romans 5:5 — poured in). Seal upon the heart and arm (Song of Songs 8:6). Written on the heart (Proverbs 3:3). The heart is the primary spatial location.

---

**Q058 type: Does the verse distinguish between means of entry and the condition itself?**

PARTIAL. Romans 5:5 identifies the Spirit as the means of delivery; the heart as the location of indwelling. But the entry/condition distinction is not developed as explicitly as in the grace study's Romans 5:2 analysis.

---

**Q059 type: Does the verse describe the present condition as carrying a forward orientation?**

ANSWER. Faith-love-hope triad (1 Thessalonians 5:8) maps love to the present inner orientation alongside faith and hope's forward reach. Eschatological love as universal inner condition (new covenant vision) is the forward horizon.

---

**Q060 type: What does the verse identify as the ground of identity?**

ANSWER. Being loved — *agapētos* is the primary designation, before conduct. Matthew 3:17 — the Father's declaration of love constitutes the Son's identity. Romans 1:7 — belovedness logically precedes calling.

---

**Q061 type: How does the verse handle co-presence of genuine human effort and prior divine initiative?**

ANSWER. Matthew 22:37 — love is commanded to the whole person, engaging all faculties. 1 John 4:19 — the capacity is entirely grounded in prior divine act. The tension is held: the command is real, the enabling ground is prior.

---

**Q062 type: In what form does the verse present the word?**

ANSWER. As commandment (Matthew 22:37). As prior divine act (1 John 4:19). As poured-in substance (Romans 5:5). As covenant faithfulness (Lamentations 3:22). As bodily act (Luke 15:20; Luke 7:38). Multiple forms.

---

**Q063 type: What effect does the word in its expressed form produce in those who receive it?**

ANSWER. Peace (Zephaniah 3:17). Identity (Matthew 3:17). Freedom and calling (Romans 1:7). Transformation of inner life (1 Corinthians 13 stable dispositions). Community belonging (John 13:35).

---

**Q064 type: Does the verse describe the word's work as present only or toward a future?**

ANSWER. Both. Eschatological horizon is developed: universal knowledge of God, love as the inner condition of all (new covenant vision). Zephaniah 3:17 — eschatological rejoicing. Faith-love-hope triad.

---

**Q065 type: Does the verse distinguish a performed version from genuine version?**

ANSWER. John 3:19 — loving darkness named as inner cause, not surface conduct. Hosea 6:6 — God desires the inner disposition (chesed) not the external act (sacrifice). The distinction between genuine orientation and performed ritual is explicit.

---

**Q066 type: What inner orientation does the verse identify as the source of genuine expression?**

ANSWER. The rightly ordered inner person — whole-person orientation toward God (Matthew 22:37). The prior experience of being loved. Fear of the Lord as love's ground is implied in Hosea 6:6 (chesed and knowledge of God as the two required inner orientations).

---

**Q067 type: What is the sequence of somatic and verbal response — which comes first?**

ANSWER (Luke 15:20). Five physical acts in sequence before any words — saw, felt compassion (inner), ran, embraced, kissed. The bodily movement precedes speech. Luke 7:38 — tears, hair, anointing: bodily acts without speech; Jesus' verbal verdict comes only afterward.

---

**Q068 type: What does the recipient's experience reveal about their inner state?**

ANSWER (Luke 7:38; Luke 15 implied). Luke 7:38 — all bodily resources given; no words. The inner love is expressed entirely through the body. Luke 7:47 — Jesus names her love as the evidence and its cause as received forgiveness. The inner state is read from the bodily evidence.

---

**Q069 type: What structural condition establishes the recipient as having no basis for the word received?**

ANSWER. John 3:16 — the world in its estrangement. Matthew 5:44 — enemies and persecutors. Galatians 2:20 — love directed to the individual sinner ("who loved me"). No natural claim, no merit, no prior faithfulness.

---

**Q070 type: Does the word operate within or across existing boundaries?**

ANSWER. Consistently across boundaries — enemy-love command (Matthew 5:44) crosses the natural boundary of reciprocal affection. John 3:16 — the world as love's object. The *agapē* vocabulary's defining movement is toward what does not merit it.

---

**Q071 type: What is the logical structure of the argument?**

ANSWER (1 John 4:19). Premise: God first loved. Conclusion: we love. The causal sequence is the structure of the entire New Testament love vocabulary. Song of Songs 8:6 argument: love strong as death → absolute claim → divine origin.

---

**Q072 type: What inner-being state does the argument structure produce in the recipient?**

ANSWER. Freedom from anxiety (the self is no longer primary concern). Stability — the settled qualities of 1 Corinthians 13. Identity — the person knows they are loved before they have earned it.

---

**Q073 type: What is the relationship between the divine act and the human act — illustrative or causal?**

ANSWER. Causal — stated as the programme's consistent finding. "We love because he first loved us" is causal, not illustrative. The model is also present (John 13:34 — "as I have loved you"), but the ground is causal.

---

**Q074 type: Where does the verse locate the inner quality from which the act flows?**

ANSWER. The heart (Matthew 22:37; Romans 5:5; Song of Songs 8:6). The inner parts — *splanchnon* (bowels, visceral compassion, Luke 15:20). The whole inner person in Matthew 22:37's tripartite formula.

---

**Q075 type: Does the verse describe the inner quality as decided or as changed in the person?**

ANSWER. Both — commanded to the will (Matthew 22:37 — volitional), and poured in as substance (Romans 5:5 — received). The two are not contradictory in the study's treatment.

---

**Q076 type: What does the form of address say about how the act has affected identity?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* — beloved — is the primary identity designation before conduct (Romans 1:7). Matthew 3:17 — "my beloved Son" — constitutes the Son's identity. The form of address as identity-constitution is developed more fully for love than for any other word in the programme.

---

**Q077 type: What does the grammatical form reveal about the nature of the act?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* — perfect passive in sense though adjectival in form: one who has been loved and remains in that state. The *agapaō* verb's range of objects encodes the word's directionality. The no-object form of 1 John 4:19 encodes the breadth of the enabling ground.

---

**Q078 type: What word opens the greeting — and what is its relationship to the primary term?**

PARTIAL. The love study does not develop greeting/salutation scenes. The nearest equivalent is *agapētos* as the opening designation in letters (Romans 1:7) — belovedness as the first thing named about the recipients.

---

**Q079 type: What is the first movement described — inner or outward?**

ANSWER (Luke 15:20). The inner compassion (*splanchnon*) comes first — the father "felt compassion" before he ran. The inner movement is prior; the outward acts follow and express it.

---

**Q080 type: What do the somatic acts share structurally?**

ANSWER (Luke 15:20; Luke 7:38). Both scenes involve the abandonment of ordinary social restraint — dignity is abandoned in Luke 15:20; all bodily resources given in Luke 7:38. The somatic acts enact the excess of love that cannot contain itself. What they share: they are not proportionate to social norms.

---

**Q081 type: What is the relationship between the inner act and the somatic acts?**

ANSWER. The inner is prior — compassion before running (Luke 15:20), love before anointing (Luke 7:38). The somatic acts are the necessary external form of an inner state that cannot remain interior. They are not performances but inevitabilities.

---

**Q082 type: Does the verse present the word and the capacity to seek it as a single movement or separate acts?**

ANSWER. The capacity to love is itself the gift (Romans 5:5; 1 John 4:19) — not separate from the love given but its enabling ground. The word and the capacity for the word arrive together.

---

**Q083 type: What does the verse identify as the source of the capacity to seek/exercise the word?**

ANSWER. God — prior divine love as enabling ground of all human love (1 John 4:19, verb without object).

---

**Q084 type: What is the first inner and somatic outcome described?**

ANSWER (Luke 15:20; 1 John 4:19). Inner: love produced by prior divine love. Somatic: Luke 15:20 — the running and embrace. Romans 5:5 — love in the heart as the first named outcome of the Spirit's gift.

---

**Q085 type: What triggers the mourning and in what sequence?**

PARTIAL. Not developed for love in the same way as grace/forgiveness. Lamentations 3:22 — chesed asserted in a context of affliction and mourning — is the nearest: love as counter-claim in the context of suffering, not mourning produced by love.

## Section 4 Questions — Original Language Vocabulary

---

**Q086: What is the range of translation equivalents?**

ANSWER. *Agapē/agapaō*: love, beloved, charity. *Chesed*: steadfast love, lovingkindness, mercy, faithfulness, goodness, kindness. *Ahavah*: love (all registers). *Phileō*: love (warm affection), friendship. The range across languages is very broad — no single English word covers the vocabulary.

---

**Q087: Does the translation range cluster around one aspect or span multiple?**

ANSWER. Spans multiple: disposition (*chesed* as character quality), act (self-giving), feeling (*phileō* affection), command (*agapaō*), eschatological noun (*agapē* as what can be measured and poured). No single dimension captures the full range.

---

**Q088: What is the root etymology — does it connect the word to another characteristic?**

ANSWER. Four root families: AHAV, CHESED, RACHAM, DOD. RACHAM spans love, compassion, mercy and connects to *rechem* (womb) — embodied intimacy. DOD connects to desire and goodness. TOV connects to goodness. AGAV connects to desire. Multiple connections.

---

**Q089: What does the root etymology reveal about the inner state associated with the act?**

ANSWER. RACHAM/womb etymology: love as bodily-intimate, instinctive, prior to merit — the mother's attachment to the child she carries. DOD in Song of Songs: love as intense longing, delight, intoxication. CHESED: love as faithful orientation, not dependent on feeling. The etymology reveals that multiple inner states are encoded within the same vocabulary family.

---

**Q090: How many distinct semantic domains does the term operate in?**

ANSWER. Five for *agapaō*: love of God/Christ (commanded); divine love toward the person (prior act); love of neighbour/enemy; mutual community love; disordered love toward wrong objects. Three for *chesed*: character quality, human covenant faithfulness, divine steadfast love. The vocabulary is the broadest in the programme.

---

**Q091: What was the dominant sense in pre-biblical usage?**

ANSWER. *Agapaō* in classical Greek had a broader, less theologically defined sense of preferential love or regard. *Phileō* was the more common classical term for love/friendship. The New Testament's elevation of *agapaō* over *phileō* as the theological primary is itself a linguistic shift.

---

**Q092: What relational economy did the classical usage assume?**

ANSWER. *Phileō* vocabulary assumed reciprocal friendship — love based on shared character and mutual benefit. *Agapaō*'s theological elevation breaks from this by encoding love toward enemies and the undeserving — a unidirectional economy where merit is not the basis.

---

**Q093: What change in the relational economy does the term undergo?**

ANSWER. From reciprocal (classical phileō economy — love between equals, based on shared virtue) to unidirectional (New Testament agapē — love toward enemies, the world in its estrangement, the individual sinner). The shift is theologically programmatic.

---

**Q094: Which bodily organs or locations does the verse evidence associate with the term?**

ANSWER. Heart (primary — Romans 5:5, Matthew 22:37, Song of Songs 8:6, Proverbs 3:3). Inner parts/*splanchnon* — bowels, visceral compassion (Luke 15:20). Arm — site of action and identity (Song of Songs 8:6). Body in all its faculties (Matthew 22:37 — heart, soul, and mind as tripartite integration).

---

**Q095: What are the distinct senses of the verbal form — do they all operate in the same relational direction?**

ANSWER. *Agapaō*: five registers (see Q090). Not all in the same direction — love can be misdirected (John 3:19 — loved darkness; 2 Timothy 3:2 — disordered philo- compounds). The verb is structurally neutral as to object — direction determines moral character.

---

**Q096: Does the verbal form distinguish the disposition of the giver — or is it structurally neutral?**

ANSWER. Structurally neutral as to moral quality — direction determines everything. Same conclusion as *aphiēmi* and *charizō* in the other studies. The moral character of the act is entirely in the object chosen.

---

**Q097: Where is the moral distinction between uses located — in the act or in the giver?**

ANSWER. In the object of the act — what is loved. The study is explicit: disorder is substitution, not absence of love's energy. Disordered people love intensely, but toward the wrong objects.

---

**Q098: What does the negative form name — and how is it defined relative to the positive?**

ANSWER. *Sane* (H8130) — hatred, the structural contrary. 156 occurrences. Both love and hatred have causative stems — both can be caused in another. Hatred is not merely love's absence but its active contrary, with its own constitutive properties. The moral character of hatred also depends on its object (Psalm 97:10 — "hate evil" is commanded).

---

**Q099: Does the negative form describe a moral failing, an inner condition, or both?**

ANSWER. Both — and the moral character depends on the object. Hatred of the neighbour in the heart (Leviticus 19:17) is a moral failing and an inner condition. Hatred of evil (Psalm 97:10) is commanded. The negative form is as morally complex as the positive.

---

**Q100: How does the particularised form relate to the primary form?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* (beloved) — the particularised form names the person who is the object of the word, not a narrowed channel of it (contrast *charisma* in the grace study). *Philos* (friend) — names the relational form love takes in friendship, linked to knowledge and mutual disclosure (John 15:15).

---

**Q101: What is the distribution of the primary terms?**

ANSWER. *Agapaō*: 344 occurrences (NT). *Agapē*: 133 occurrences (NT). *Agapētos*: 79 occurrences. *Chesed*: 261 occurrences (OT). *Phileō*: 52 occurrences (NT). *Ahavah*: 38 occurrences. Total active terms: 138. The vocabulary is the largest in the programme.

---

**Q102: What does the coinage of a new lexical form suggest?**

PARTIAL. No newly coined term identified in the love vocabulary equivalent to *charitoō*. However, the New Testament's elevation of *agapaō* over *phileō* as the primary theological term for love is itself a kind of semantic re-coining — investing an existing term with new theological weight.

---

**Q103: What is the difference between identity-constituting and simple reception?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* encodes identity-constitution — being named as beloved before any description of conduct. This is the love study's equivalent of *charitoō*'s identity-constituting function, though through an adjectival designation rather than a coined verb.

---

**Q104: What does grammatical aspect encode about the nature of the act?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* — the state of being loved as the recipient's ongoing identity. Romans 5:5 — love poured in as completed act with continuing presence. Song of Songs 8:6 — seal encoding permanence.

---

**Q105: What is the frequency of the primary term and dominant syntactic form?**

ANSWER. *Chesed* — 261 occurrences, dominant in the covenant-faithfulness formula ("his steadfast love endures forever"). *Agapaō* — 344 occurrences, dominant in commandment form (love God, love neighbour) and divine-love-toward-person form. Both forms are widely distributed across the canon.

---

**Q106: What bodily faculty does the dominant idiom associate with the term?**

ANSWER. The heart — primary locus across both testaments. The tripartite heart-soul-mind formula of Matthew 22:37. The womb etymology of RACHAM grounding the bodily intimacy of love. The *splanchnon* (visceral inner parts) of Luke 15:20.

---

**Q107: How widespread is the somatic language — is it literal or metaphorical?**

ANSWER. Very widespread — the study calls it primary evidence, not illustration: "the body is how love becomes visible in the world." Luke 15:20 (running, embracing, kissing), Luke 7:38 (tears, hair, anointing), Isaiah 49:15 (womb and nursing body doing theological work). Treated as literal participation.

---

**Q108: Does the term carry more than one semantic register?**

ANSWER. Yes — multiple registers within each term family. *Agapaō* five registers. *Chesed* three levels. *Ahav* three registers. The multi-register character of the vocabulary is one of its defining features.

---

**Q109: Does the term distinguish between genuine and performed expressions?**

ANSWER. Hosea 6:6 — inner disposition (chesed) vs external act (sacrifice). John 3:19 — the direction of love as moral verdict. The vocabulary distinguishes between love as inner orientation and its performed substitutes.

---

**Q110: What terms share the same root — and what does the shared root suggest?**

ANSWER. RACHAM root — love, compassion, mercy as a three-way family structure: love as broadest orientation, compassion as love responding to suffering, mercy as love overriding just consequence. DOD root — love and desire, suggesting intimate longing as one register of love. TOV root — love and goodness, suggesting content of what love gives.

---

**Q111: What does the shared root between the disposition and the seeking of it imply?**

PARTIAL. The love vocabulary does not develop a seeking-love root-structure equivalent to *chen/chanan/tachanum*. The nearest is the RACHAM root spanning both the divine disposition and its outward expression, but seeking-love as a distinct act is not developed through shared root.

---

**Q112: To whom is the primary adjectival form applied?**

ANSWER. *Agapētos* — applied first to Christ (Matthew 3:17, Father's declaration), then to believers (Romans 1:7). The distribution is Christ-first, believers-as-participation. Applied to Abraham as God's friend (James 2:23) — extending the designation across covenant history.

---

**Q113: What does the adverbial form contribute to the economic logic?**

PARTIAL. No distinct adverbial form equivalent to *chinnam*. The nearest is the no-object verb of 1 John 4:19 — the breadth of "we love" without specified object makes the economic logic explicit: divine love is the ground of all human love, not merely of love returned.

---

**Q114: What does the adverbial or qualifying form say about the conditions under which the term operates?**

ANSWER. Love operates regardless of merit — enemy-love command (Matthew 5:44), love toward the world in estrangement (John 3:16). The conditions under which love does not operate are misdirection toward wrong objects, not absence of an object.

---

**Q115: What does the plural or intensive form indicate?**

ANSWER. *Rachamim* (plural of racham) — not multiple acts of compassion but the intensive form of the single compassionate disposition at its most concentrated. Parallel to *tachanum* in the grace study.

---

**Q116: What somatic expressions are associated with the term?**

ANSWER. Running, embracing, kissing (Luke 15:20). Tears, hair, repeated kissing, anointing (Luke 7:38). Womb and nursing body (Isaiah 49:15). Heart, inner parts, arm (Song of Songs 8:6). The somatic repertoire is the richest in the programme.

---

**Q117: Does the term describe a speech act, a whole-person act, or both?**

ANSWER. Whole-person — Matthew 22:37 (heart, soul, mind). Luke 15:20 (body before speech). Love that exists only as inner state without outward expression is incomplete — John 14:15 (love expresses as obedience). Both inner and outer are required.

---

**Q118: Does any usage present the capacity as internally generated or externally given?**

ANSWER. Externally given — explicitly and consistently (Romans 5:5, 1 John 4:19). The study's broadest structural finding: love is received before it is given. The capacity for love as such is externally given, not generated from within.

**Q130: What is the relationship between this word and human will?**

**Q138: Does any verse anchor both this word and another — revealing dual membership?**

## Forgiveness Extension Questions (F-001 to F-014) applied to Love

---

**F-001: Does the word have a stated outer limit — a condition under which it cannot operate?**

ANSWER. The outer limit of love is named as its misuse — when love is directed toward darkness (John 3:19), the love-capacity is still operative but directed wrongly. There is no equivalent to the unforgivable sin for love. However, love *as commanded* cannot reach the person whose inner person is finally closed — Matthew 22:37 addresses those who can hear the command.

---

**F-002: Can the core act-structure be directed toward a wrong object?**

ANSWER. Yes — this is the study's central diagnostic. The philo- compound taxonomy maps this systematically: the same root applied to God names virtue; applied to money, pleasure, and self names corruption. Disorder is substitution — the same inner capacity of attachment misdirected. This is love's most distinctive feature not present in grace or forgiveness.

---

**F-003: What is the structural relationship between the word's vertical and horizontal axes?**

ANSWER. The vertical and horizontal axes are structurally integrated and co-required. Matthew 22:37–39 — love of God and love of neighbour as the two commandments from which all others hang. John 13:35 — horizontal love as the public evidence of vertical discipleship. 1 John 4:20 — "whoever does not love his brother whom he has seen cannot love God whom he has not seen." The horizontal is the necessary expression of the vertical; the vertical is the source of the horizontal.

---

**F-004: Is the primary term grammatically restricted to a specific actor as subject?**

ANSWER. No — uniquely, love is commanded both as human-toward-God (Matthew 22:37) and as God-toward-human (1 John 4:19), and as human-toward-neighbour and human-toward-enemy. The term is not restricted to divine or human subject. Contrast with *sālach* (forgiveness — divine only) and *channun* (grace — predominantly divine predicate).

---

**F-005: What is the mechanism of administration — and what is the relationship between outward mechanism and inner reality?**

ANSWER. Love is given by the Spirit — poured into hearts (Romans 5:5). The mechanism is pneumatic, not covenantal-ritual as in forgiveness. The inner reality is the love itself; the Spirit is the means of delivery. The body is also a mechanism — bodily acts of love are not separate from love but its necessary external form.

---

**F-006: Does the word operate unconditionally — or does it have stated conditions?**

ANSWER. The divine love toward humanity is stated as unconditional — prior to and independent of merit (1 John 4:19; John 3:16). Human love is commanded conditionally in that it requires the whole-person orientation (Matthew 22:37) and is shaped by its object. The capacity for love is conditioned by receipt of divine love (Romans 5:5). No conditionality on the divine side; commanded form on the human side.

---

**F-007: Is the word a single act or a compound of distinct component acts?**

ANSWER. The study describes love as operating simultaneously as feeling, disposition, and choice — three components that are not identical but are held together by the same word. It is not sequential (first feeling, then disposition, then choice) but simultaneous. Whether these are distinct component acts or dimensions of a single act is a question the study raises but does not fully resolve.

---

**F-008: What does the word make possible in a relationship that would otherwise be closed?**

ANSWER. Enemy relationship made possible (Matthew 5:44). Community formation across natural social divisions (John 13:35). The whole moral and relational life rightly ordered where love governs (Section 1). Love covers offences (Proverbs 10:12) — absorbing the cycle of accumulating injury.

---

**F-009: Does the word function as a prerequisite for another spiritual act or practice?**

ANSWER. Yes — as the governing orientation from which everything else flows. Hosea 6:6 — God desires chesed above sacrifice. Matthew 22:37–40 — all the law and prophets hang on the two love commandments. Love is not merely one virtue but the ground of the whole moral life. The obedience-love nexus (John 14:15) makes love the source of obedience, not a separate act alongside it.

---

**F-010: Are the downstream effects proportional to the degree of the word received?**

ANSWER. Implied by 1 John 4:19 — the prior divine love is total, and the human response is enabled by it entirely. The love study does not develop a quantitative proportionality equivalent to Luke 7:47's forgiven-much/loves-much logic. But love growing and abounding through prayer (1 Thessalonians 3:12) implies that more received leads to more extended.

---

**F-011: Does the word share vocabulary with adjacent characteristics — or occupy an isolated space?**

ANSWER. Maximum sharing — highest term-sharing ratio in the programme (0.701). 11 of 13 grace terms shared; multiple root families shared with compassion, mercy, desire, goodness. Love is the relational hub through which most of the inner-being vocabulary passes — the structural opposite of forgiveness's isolated lexical space.

---

**F-012: Is the word named as a divine possession or attribute — distinct from an act God performs?**

ANSWER. Yes — 1 John 4:8: God *is* love. Not merely that God loves (acts) but that love is what God is. This is a stronger claim than any other word in the programme makes. Love is the divine essence before it is a divine act.

---

**F-013: What practices or disciplines feed the capacity to extend the word?**

ANSWER. Apostolic prayer (1 Thessalonians 3:12 — prayer for love to increase and abound). Practice of covenant faithfulness over time — chesed deepened by circumstances that would justify its withdrawal. Received forgiveness (Luke 7:47). Community formation. Being loved names the foundational feed.

---

**F-014: Is the word a terminal state or a transitional one?**

ANSWER. Terminal in the programme's hierarchy — love is "the most foundational characteristic in the entire vocabulary of the human inner life." It is the governing orientation, not a threshold to something beyond itself. However, it generates joy, peace, hope, and community — suggesting it is both terminal (the goal) and generative (producing other states from within itself).
