## The problem, deeper

Your verdict on the M38 essay yesterday: *"the facts seem to be correct, the style and language is not fluent. There are numerous sentences that just not make sense. It clearly is a different engine that compiled the prose from say M11. It seems that AI took each sentence from the findings and tried to construct a sentence of it, often not finding itself into the holistic context."*

This is the single most important critique of the M38 experiment. Costs and timing can be tuned; an essay that reads as sentence-by-sentence assembly is a quality failure that no amount of process refinement compensates for. If we don't fix this, the analytical phase produces analytically correct prose that nobody enjoys reading — which means nobody reads it.

I want to be careful here, because there are at least three things that could be causing the sentence-by-sentence quality and they require different fixes:

**Hypothesis A: The author skipped the holistic-reading layer.**
Good essayists read everything first, form a mental model of what the material is about, then write. The M38 essay I drafted went straight from "look at the findings dump" to "write the essay". The integration never happened in my mind before the prose started flowing. The result was: I wrote sentences faithful to individual findings, but I didn't compose the argument as an argument first.

**Hypothesis B: The underlying findings are themselves sentence-by-sentence in character.**
The Phase D Stage A and Stage B findings were generated by Sonnet 4.6 via API, one prompt at a time, with no holistic context across prompts. Each finding was answered in isolation. If you compress 189 individually-answered prompts together, the union doesn't flow as integrated prose because each was composed independently. The essay drawn from this material can only be as integrated as the material it draws from.

**Hypothesis C: The chapter-by-chapter Session C approach atomises authoring too early.**
Each chapter in the Session C workflow loads only its own evidence. The chapter 4 author doesn't see chapter 3's findings or chapter 5's findings. They write their chapter without knowing what the other chapters say. The result is competent per-chapter prose that doesn't add up to an integrated book.

These three hypotheses aren't mutually exclusive. They may all be operating. The refinements that follow address all three.

The diagnostic that will tell us which is dominant: take the existing findings to chat unchanged, run them through the new Phase E.0 vision document workflow, and see if chat-authored prose with a vision document produces fluent argument. If yes, hypotheses A and C were dominant and the findings are fine. If no, hypothesis B is dominant and the findings need regeneration.

## The model

### Two layers of prose

Prose writing has two layers that operate sequentially:

**Layer 1 — Composition.** What is the argument? What is the dominant arc? Where are the surprises? Which verses carry the most weight? What is the partition between things that look similar but are actually distinct? What is the single sentence that summarises the whole?

This layer is mental and integrative. The output is a model, not text. The model lives in the author's mind (or in a written planning document) and shapes every sentence that follows.

**Layer 2 — Sentence-by-sentence writing.** How do I write this paragraph? Which verse goes here? How does this paragraph open? What's the topic sentence? How does this paragraph end so the next one can begin?

This layer is generative and produces text. It's where most authors spend most of their time, but the quality of layer 2 is entirely shaped by whether layer 1 happened first.

The M38 essay (and probably the M38 findings underneath) skipped layer 1. The Session C workflow as currently designed has no place for layer 1 — each chapter run jumps straight to layer 2.

### What good layer 1 work looks like

When an essayist does layer 1 well, the resulting essay has features you can detect from the outside:

- **Topic sentences carry the argument.** You can read just the first sentence of each paragraph and follow the case. (Compare the M11 essay opening "Scripture treats the recovery of right standing between God and the person …" with any paragraph from the M38 essay middle.)
- **The dominant arc is named explicitly somewhere.** The essay says, somewhere near the opening, "here is the integrated reality these characteristics describe", and then the rest of the essay unfolds it.
- **The partition is respected.** Where the material doesn't integrate (M38 has six receptive characteristics and one vocational one — that's a real partition), the essay names the partition rather than smoothing it.
- **Surprises are surfaced as surprises.** The essay knows what's expected and what's unusual, and treats unusual things as unusual rather than burying them in the flow.
- **Strongest verses appear at strongest points.** The essay knows which verses do the most work and uses them at the moments when the most work needs to be done.

These features are layer 1 outputs that get expressed in layer 2 writing. You can't get them by writing harder at layer 2. You have to do layer 1 first.

## Concrete proposals

### A. Phase E.0 — Cluster vision document

A new sub-phase between the end of Phase D and the start of chapter authoring. One call to AI chat (or API), with all the cluster's findings in context, producing a 500–700 word internal reflection.

**Path:** `Sessions/Session_Clusters/{CODE}/wa-cluster-{CODE}-vision-v1-{date}.md`

**Status:** internal — never published. It's a planning document that informs the chapter authors.

**Prompt structure (sketch):**

> You are about to draft a published study of {cluster_name}, a family of {N} characteristics in Scripture's account of inner life. Before writing any chapters, read all the findings below and answer five questions in writing. This is not the published essay. This is your internal model of what the cluster is, which you will use to compose every chapter.
>
> 1. **The dominant arc.** What is the integrated theological reality these {N} characteristics together describe? State it in one sentence. Then expand to one paragraph.
>
> 2. **The partition.** Where in this cluster do the characteristics not align into one uniform pattern? Are there sub-groups of characteristics that work differently from the rest? Name the partition explicitly. If there is no partition (all characteristics align uniformly), say so.
>
> 3. **The surprises.** Reading the findings, what surprised you? What is here that you would not have predicted from the cluster's title or from a passing knowledge of the subject? Name three surprises. (If there are fewer than three, name as many as the evidence supports.)
>
> 4. **The strongest verses.** Across all {N} characteristics, what are the five verses that carry the most theological weight? These are the verses you will quote at the most important moments of the essay. Name them.
>
> 5. **The single-sentence summary.** If a reader came to you and asked "in one sentence, what is this study about?", what would you say? Write it.

**Expected output structure:**

```markdown
# {Cluster name} — cluster vision (internal)

## 1. The dominant arc
[one sentence]
[one paragraph]

## 2. The partition
[one paragraph naming the partition, or naming its absence]

## 3. The surprises
- Surprise 1: [...]
- Surprise 2: [...]
- Surprise 3: [...]

## 4. The strongest verses
1. [verse + role in the argument]
2. [verse + role in the argument]
3. [verse + role in the argument]
4. [verse + role in the argument]
5. [verse + role in the argument]

## 5. The single-sentence summary
[one sentence]
```

This document then becomes context for every chapter run. Each chapter's input now includes a reference to the vision document at the top: "Load the vision document. You don't restate it in your chapter, but you know what the dominant arc is, what the partition is, and which verses carry the most weight. Use this to know where your chapter sits in the whole."

### B. Chapter authoring with vision context

Each chapter run loads three things together (was three; now four with vision):

1. Shared style and method (`wa-sessionc-cluster-style-method-v1_1-20260512.md`)
2. Chapter instruction (e.g. `wa-sessionc-cluster-ch4-instruction-v1_0-20260512.md`)
3. Chapter input (e.g. `wa-cluster-{CODE}-ch4-input-v1-{date}.md`)
4. **Cluster vision document** (new — `wa-cluster-{CODE}-vision-v1-{date}.md`)

The chapter instruction is modified to declare: "Read the vision document first. Do not repeat it in your prose. Use it to know which findings are load-bearing in the whole essay, which verses are the strongest across the cluster, and where the partition lies."

The chapter author now operates with the cluster's argument in their head before composing any sentence. Layer 1 is done; layer 2 can do its job.

### C. Phase E.2 — Cross-chapter consistency pass

After all chapters are drafted, before final assembly. One call (chat or API) loads:

- The cluster vision document
- All drafted chapters

And returns a per-chapter edit list:

**Path:** `Sessions/Session_Clusters/{CODE}/wa-cluster-{CODE}-consistency-edits-v1-{date}.md`

**Prompt structure (sketch):**

> You are reviewing seven drafted chapters of a published study for consistency. Read all chapters. Identify:
>
> 1. **Duplications.** The same point made in two places. Name where. Recommend which place keeps it.
> 2. **Contradictions.** A claim in one chapter that conflicts with a claim in another. Name them.
> 3. **Voice drift.** Places where the tone slips from the agreed register. Name them.
> 4. **Forbidden vocabulary.** Any of "cluster", "sub-group", "VCG", "finding", "tier", "characteristic code" appearing in prose.
> 5. **Argument gaps.** Places where one chapter assumes a claim that another chapter is supposed to establish but doesn't.
> 6. **Connecting work missed.** Places where a chapter could have linked to another chapter's framing but didn't.
>
> Output per-chapter edit list. Each edit is: location (chapter + section), issue, recommended fix.

**Expected output structure:**

```markdown
# {Cluster name} — consistency review (internal)

## Chapter 2
- §2.3 "Healing wholeness" — duplicates §3 divine-pattern claim. Recommended: trim §2.3 paragraph 3, keep §3 version.
- §2.5 "Priestly mediation" — uses "this characteristic operates at the meta-level". Forbidden vocabulary ("meta"). Recommended: replace with "this characteristic operates at the institutional level".

## Chapter 4
- §4.1 — claim about "soul-level location" without quoting a verse. Recommended: add Mat 16:25 or Psa 35:9.

[etc.]
```

Each chapter author then applies the edits in a quick follow-up pass. The consistency pass is the only place in the workflow where all chapters are read together; it's the single place where cross-chapter coherence can be confirmed.

### D. Phase E.3 — Integration pass (the closing observation)

After consistency edits, before final DOCX render. One call loads:

- Vision document
- All revised chapters

And writes the closing observation that ties the whole book together. This is where the dominant arc, the partition, and the strongest verses get their final integrated treatment. The closing observation goes at the end of the last chapter (or as its own short section before the appendices, depending on the existing convention).

The M11 essay's "A closing observation" section is the model. That section was written with the whole book in context. The Phase E.3 call replicates this conscious closing.

### E. Division of labour: CC, API, chat

This is the place to be explicit about who does what:

| Sub-phase | Workload | Where it happens | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| E.0 Vision | Read all findings → write 500-word reflection | **Chat (Opus)** | Holistic thinking is chat's strength; one-shot read of large context; no orchestration overhead |
| E.1 Chapter authoring (×7) | Write one chapter from input + vision | **Chat (Opus)** per chapter | Per-chapter authoring needs your guidance and iteration; chat-paced is the right speed |
| E.2 Consistency | Read all chapters → produce edit list | **Chat (Opus)** | Same reasoning as E.0 |
| E.2 Edit application | Apply edits to chapters | **CC** | Mechanical; cheap |
| E.3 Integration | Write closing observation | **Chat (Opus)** | Composition; chat's strength |
| E.4 Assembly + DOCX render | Combine chapters into single file; render | **CC** | Deterministic |

API is absent from this workflow. The verdict from M38 was clear: Sonnet via API does not produce the prose quality the study needs. Opus in chat does. The cost trade-off (chat subscription is already paid) makes this dominant.

### F. The quality test for prose

A simple test you can apply after Phase E to know whether the refinement worked:

1. **Read the first sentence of every paragraph in chapters 2–7.** Does this sequence carry the argument? If yes, topic sentences are doing their job. If no, layer 1 wasn't done well enough.
2. **Find the dominant-arc sentence.** Is there a sentence somewhere in chapter 1 (or in the closing observation) that names the dominant arc explicitly? If yes, E.0 worked. If no, the vision didn't transfer to the prose.
3. **Find the partition treatment.** Where in the essay is the partition named? Is it named clearly or buried in qualifying clauses? If it's named clearly, the vision document held; if buried, the chapter author didn't trust the partition.
4. **Spot the strongest verses.** Are the five strongest verses (from the vision document) actually quoted at the most important moments? Or do they appear in passing? If at important moments, layer 1 transferred. If in passing, the chapter authors didn't know they were the strongest.

This is a 10-minute test after Phase E completes. It tells you whether the prose refinement succeeded or whether something needs reworking.

## Open uncertainties

1. **Whether the vision document concept actually transfers.** I'm proposing it because it addresses the diagnosis. It's not battle-tested. M36 will be the first test. If after M36 the prose still reads as sentence-by-sentence, the diagnosis was wrong (or partly wrong).

2. **Whether 500–700 words is the right length for the vision document.** Too short and the chapter authors don't get enough framing; too long and it competes with the published essay for the reader's attention even though it's internal. The number is a guess. Calibrate after M36.

3. **Whether the consistency pass should produce edit suggestions or full revisions.** Edit suggestions preserve chapter-author voice; full revisions could be uneven. The proposal is edit suggestions, but if the chapter authors are inconsistent in voice anyway, full revisions might be cleaner.

4. **What happens to the M38 essay.** Three options: (a) leave it as is — it's published but flagged as v1; rerun under refined workflow for v2 if you want; (b) redo it now as the proof-of-concept for the refinement; (c) wait until M36 has run under refinement, then decide retrospectively.

5. **Whether the findings need regeneration.** Hypothesis B above. The refinement above doesn't fix Hypothesis B if it's dominant. The diagnostic — running M38's findings through the refined Phase E to see if chat-authored prose with vision context produces fluent argument — is the test.

6. **How chapter 1 fits.** Chapter 1 is meant to open the study; it's typically written last (per M01 convention) because it depends on the other chapters. The vision document does some of what chapter 1 does (states the dominant arc). The question: does chapter 1 just become a polished version of the vision, or does it serve a different purpose?

## Questions for your review

1. **Does the two-layer prose model (composition then sentence) match how you think good essays get written?** If not, what's missing?

2. **The five questions in the vision document — are they the right five?** Specifically: dominant arc, partition, surprises, strongest verses, single-sentence summary. Anything to add (e.g. "the danger of the cluster" — the wrong reading the essay must steer away from)? Anything to drop?

3. **Should chapter authors see other chapters they haven't yet drafted?** Currently they don't (per M01 precedent). With vision-document framing, they don't need to — but if they did, it would let chapter 4 reference chapter 3's specific framing. Trade-off: more context per call, harder to manage. The proposal is "no" (vision document is enough); worth checking your view.

4. **The consistency pass — chat or API?** Chat is the same Opus that authors chapters; API gives you a fresh perspective. Trade-off: chat preserves voice; API might catch what the author missed. The proposal is chat; worth checking.

5. **Closing observation — at end of last chapter, or as its own short closing section?** M11 has its own section. M01 doesn't (closing observation embedded in chapter 8 / appendices). Personal preference.

6. **M38 essay disposition** — leave it, redo it now, or redo it later? Recommendation is "redo it later, after M36 has validated the refinement". Worth confirming.

7. **Quality test for prose** — do the four test questions feel like the right diagnostic? Or would you check different things?
