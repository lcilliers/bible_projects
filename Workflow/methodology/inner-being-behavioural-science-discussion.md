# The Inner Being: A Behavioural-Science Discussion

*A conversation exploring how behavioural science describes the inner being — its faculties, anchors, characteristics, and whether it is better understood as parts or as process. Framed deliberately from a behavioural-science perspective, not from scripture or from a Bible-study programme.*

---

## 1. What faculties would we expect the inner being to have?

**A note on the word "faculties."** Behavioural science largely abandoned the older "faculty psychology" idea — the notion that the mind divides into a fixed set of discrete, organ-like faculties. Modern psychology and cognitive science talk instead about *processes*, *systems*, or *functions*, and there is no single agreed master list. What follows is a synthesis, with each part's organising framework flagged, and genuine disagreements marked rather than smoothed over.

The most durable high-level scheme is the **"trilogy of mind"** — cognition, affect, and conation (knowing, feeling, willing). It traces to 18th-century philosophy (often attributed to Kant and Moses Mendelssohn) and still structures much of psychology. But it omits several things modern science treats as central — self, sociality, consciousness — so those are added below.

**1. Cognitive faculties (knowing / thinking).** From the information-processing tradition: perception, attention, working and long-term memory (the latter usually split into episodic, semantic, procedural), learning, language, reasoning and problem-solving, judgement and decision-making, imagination / mental simulation, and metacognition. Dual-process theory (Kahneman; Stanovich/Evans) overlays a fast automatic "System 1" and a slow effortful "System 2."

**2. Affective faculties (feeling).** From affective science: discrete emotions, mood (longer, diffuse states), and core affect (the raw valence/arousal substrate — Russell). The *nature* of emotion is contested: Ekman's **basic-emotions** view (a handful are hardwired and universal) versus Barrett's **constructed-emotion** theory (emotions assembled from core affect plus learned concepts). Panksepp's affective neuroscience proposes seven primary systems (seeking, rage, fear, lust, care, panic/grief, play). Emotion *regulation* is usually treated as a faculty in its own right.

**3. Conative faculties (willing / striving / motivation).** Homeostatic drives (hunger, thirst); psychological needs (Self-Determination Theory's autonomy, competence, relatedness; or Maslow's hierarchy, which is more empirically contested); goals; volition and the sense of agency; and self-control / self-regulation. Approach and avoidance systems sit here.

Those three are the classic trilogy. The following are domains the trilogy under-represents but that contemporary science treats as core:

**4. The self and identity.** Self-awareness, self-concept, self-esteem, sense of agency and body-ownership, and the autobiographical narrative giving continuity over time. (Social/personality psychology; consciousness research.)

**5. Social and moral faculties.** Theory of mind / mentalizing, empathy, the attachment system (Bowlby/Ainsworth), social cognition, and a moral faculty — moral reasoning plus moral emotions like guilt and shame, sometimes loosely called conscience.

**6. Personality / temperament.** Not a faculty so much as the stable dispositional patterning across the above. The dominant empirical model is the **Big Five / OCEAN**; alternatives exist (HEXACO adds honesty-humility).

**7. Consciousness / awareness.** The capacity for subjective experience itself, plus arousal and wakefulness — the most philosophically unsettled (the "hard problem").

**Two caveats.** First, these categories overlap heavily (decision-making is cognitive *and* affective *and* conative); many researchers resist calling them separate "faculties" at all. Second, the boundaries and even the existence of some items are live debates (emotion theory, Maslow, the modularity question).

## 2. Why is there no clear "anatomy" of the inner being?

This is a genuine feature of the field, not a gap. There is no agreed anatomy of the inner being, for good reasons.

**How we come to know each.** Anatomy is *observed* — you can open a body and point to where an arm stops. The inner being is *inferred*. Nobody has seen a memory or held an instance of attention. We watch behaviour and self-report, then posit invisible machinery to explain the patterns. The "parts" of the inner life are theoretical constructs proposed because they earn their keep, not found objects like bones.

**No physical individuation.** An arm is bounded by where flesh ends. "Attention" has no edge; it's a label drawn around a cluster of phenomena, and the boundary could be drawn elsewhere to yield a workable alternative. Functions can be cut many ways depending on purpose.

**A levels problem.** The same mental phenomenon can be described by what it accomplishes, how it does it as a process, or what neural tissue carries it out — the computational, algorithmic, and implementational levels (David Marr). None is the privileged "true" description, so even the *grain* of a parts-list is a choice.

**The historical attempt.** People once tried to build exactly this anatomy. **Faculty psychology** treated the mind as discrete organ-like powers, and **phrenology** (Franz Gall, early 1800s) tried to map them to skull bumps. Its collapse is part of why the field is now wary of clean part-lists: intuitive divisions of the mind don't reliably correspond to real divisions in the system.

**But not a free-for-all.** The brain does show genuine functional specialisation — damage to specific regions reliably knocks out specific capacities (the hippocampus and laying down new memories; particular cortical areas and aspects of language). Nature has *some* joints. The trouble is they rarely line up with folk categories: the hippocampus isn't "memory" — memory is distributed across many systems.

**The live fork underneath.**
- **Natural kinds:** psychological categories are real divisions waiting to be discovered (as oxygen was real before being named). A correct anatomy exists in principle.
- **Instrumentalist / pragmatic:** categories like "emotion" or "attention" are useful tools for prediction and intervention, not discoveries of pre-existing parts. Barrett's work on emotion leans this way, as does much contemporary thinking that prefers processes and dynamic systems to components.

**A quiet assumption worth surfacing.** The expectation that the inner being *should* decompose into parts the way a body does is itself an intuition borrowed from physical objects. It's possible the inner life simply isn't the kind of thing that has parts in that sense — that "made up of" transfers cleanly to bodies but only loosely to minds. Whether that's a temporary limit of the science or a permanent feature of the subject is not settled.

So the honest answer to "what does the inner being consist of?" is that behavioural science offers several competing maps, each useful for different purposes, none claiming to be *the* anatomy — and a real disagreement about whether a true anatomy is even the right thing to look for.

## 3. What key anchors does modern science use to discuss the inner being?

In the absence of fixed *parts*, the field orients around fixed *reference points*. There are two kinds, doing different jobs.

### The epistemic anchors — what makes the inner being studiable

- **Observable behaviour and physiology.** The bedrock left from behaviourism (Watson, Skinner): any claim about an inner state must cash out in something measurable — behaviour, task performance, reaction times, physiological signals (heart rate, cortisol, skin conductance), self-report. The state is inferred; the anchor is the observation.
- **The construct, defined by how it's measured.** Because you can't point at "anxiety," it's anchored to an operational definition and a measurement instrument, then tested for validity (the psychometric tradition; Cronbach and Meehl on construct validity). This is why so much of psychology runs on validated scales and tasks.
- **The brain and body itself.** Cognitive neuroscience uses neural and bodily measures as objective referents — the implementational anchor. Caution: neural specialisation is real but rarely matches folk categories.

### The organising frameworks — the maps laid over those anchors

- **The trilogy of mind** (cognition, affect, conation) still silently structures subfields and textbooks.
- **The information-processing model** anchors the cognitive side: input → encoding → storage → retrieval → output — the mind as something like a computer (a historically specific metaphor).
- **Core affect** anchors emotion at a low level: a two-dimensional space of valence (pleasant–unpleasant) and arousal (high–low) — Russell's circumplex. It works as an anchor precisely because it sits *below* the contested question of how many discrete emotions exist.
- **RDoC — the Research Domain Criteria framework** (US NIMH, early 2010s, under Thomas Insel) is the field's most deliberate recent attempt to lay down anchor *domains* for the inner life. Built as an alternative to the symptom-based DSM, it anchors research to functional domains studied across levels (genes, circuits, behaviour, self-report). Its domains: negative valence systems, positive valence systems, cognitive systems, social process systems, and arousal/regulatory systems — with sensorimotor systems added later (around 2019; date approximate). The NIMH RDoC matrix is the primary source. This is the closest thing to "the constructs modern science uses to anchor discussion of the inner being."
- **Levels of analysis** (Marr's three levels) is a meta-anchor — any inner phenomenon can be described by what it accomplishes, how it processes information, or what tissue implements it. Less a map of the inner being than a map of how to discuss it without talking past one another.

**Caveats.** None of these is *the* anchor; they coexist, and which one a researcher reaches for depends on subfield and purpose. And the live disagreement runs underneath all of it — whether these anchors track real joints in nature or are useful instruments. RDoC is itself partly a bet that the *old* anchors (DSM categories) were the wrong ones, which shows how unsettled the choice remains.

## 4. Does the inner being have different characteristics?

"Characteristic" carries two distinct meanings, and the field has solid things to say under each.

### Reading one: characteristics as properties of the inner being as a whole

Claims about how the inner life behaves as a system (which the field commits to more readily than to a fixed anatomy):

- **Dynamic** rather than static — better described as ongoing process than standing structure.
- **Largely non-conscious** — much operates outside awareness; conscious experience is the minority case (implicit memory, automaticity, Kahneman's System 1). *How much* is unconscious is debated.
- **Embodied** — bodily states feed directly into thought and feeling (Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis; embodied cognition). A research programme with critics, not settled fact.
- **Integrated in experience yet distributed in mechanism** — subjectively unified, mechanically spread across many systems with no central headquarters (the "binding problem," the unity of consciousness).
- **Plastic and developmental** — changes across the lifespan and reshapes with experience (neuroplasticity).
- **Situated** — states and even apparent traits shift with context (the person-situation debate; Mischel, 1968).

### Reading two: characteristics as individual differences

The ways one person's inner being differs from another's. In psychology "characteristics" is close to a technical synonym for *traits*. The dominant map is the **Big Five** (openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, neuroticism), with **HEXACO** a serious rival adding honesty-humility. Five-Factor Theory (McCrae and Costa) distinguishes *basic tendencies* (underlying, substantially heritable dispositions) from *characteristic adaptations* (goals, habits, self-concepts that develop as tendencies meet a particular life and culture).

The two readings pull opposite ways: reading one describes what is *common* to all inner lives (universal properties); reading two describes what *varies* between them. Both answers are "yes," but they are two separate yeses. The disputes flagged earlier (basic vs. constructed emotion; natural kinds vs. instruments) sit mostly underneath reading two, where you assert that specific traits are real, stable things.

## 5. Could the inner being be a series of cause-and-effects — processes that start, do something, and morph into something else, with distinct patterns?

This isn't a fallback from "no parts" — it's one of the main recognised alternatives. A whole strand holds that the inner being is better understood as *process* than *structure*, and the three sub-questions map onto three real traditions.

**"A series of cause and effects."** In philosophy of mind this is **functionalism**: a mental state *is* its causal role — what causes it, how it interacts with other states, what behaviour it produces (Putnam, 1960s). Appealing because it sidesteps the anatomy problem — you describe by causal role, not by pointing at a part. The standing criticism: pure causal-role definitions struggle to capture the *felt* quality of experience (the qualia objection).

**"Processes that start, do something, and morph into something else."** The richest set of homes:
- **William James's stream of consciousness** (1890) — the inner life is a flow, not a string of discrete states, and we distort it by chopping it into parts. (Your phrasing nearly restates his.)
- **Dynamical systems theory** applied to mind (van Gelder; Thelen & Smith, 1990s) — the inner being modelled as a system moving through a "state space" over time; change *is* the primary object of description.
- **Predictive processing / active inference** (Friston's free-energy work; Clark, *Surfing Uncertainty*, 2016) — the whole inner life as one continuous loop of predicting sensory input and updating on error. Honest flag: critics argue it's so general it risks being hard to falsify — a powerful organising idea rather than settled fact.

**"Are the patterns distinct and different from other patterns?"** The sharpest question, because pure flow struggles to explain why experience comes in recognisable, repeatable kinds. The elegant bridge from dynamical systems is **attractors** — stable patterns a system tends to fall into and return to, even in constant motion. A mood or emotion isn't a part but a temporary stable pattern in an ever-flowing system, distinct from others the way a whirlpool is distinct from the river without being a separate object *from* it. This gives distinct, identifiable patterns *without* fixed parts.

But the fork returns: whether those patterns are *really* distinct (natural kinds) or imposed for convenience (instrumentalism) is the live disagreement. A process ontology doesn't settle it; it relocates it from "are the parts real?" to "are the patterns real?"

**A counterweight.** Not everyone gives up parts. The **new mechanism** school (Bechtel, Craver, 2000s onward) argues a mechanism is *parts plus their organised activities* — so "cause and effect" and "parts" aren't rivals; the inner being is components *doing things in organised sequence*. The clean either/or is softened by a third position: organised processes performed by parts.

**Where this lands:** the framework being circled is real and respectable — a *process / dynamical* view of the inner being — whose signature move is to replace "what is it made of?" with "what patterns does it stabilise into, and how do they transform?" Its competitors are functionalism (roles), mechanism (organised parts), and the older structural view.

## 6. The study, and the diagnosis

**The observation.** Describing the inner workings of the inner being is elusive; at best, scientists are grappling to come to grips with it. The study in question describes the inner being using the words of scripture that relate to it, through deep analysis of verses, allowing the verses to describe how it works. Over roughly six months, repeated attempts to bring the verses into logical units failed. The conversation began from the belief that a *faculties* lens would help — until that lens began to feel like a distraction.

**The diagnosis.** The repeated failure to form logical units may not be a failure of effort or method. It may be *information about the subject matter*. If sorting something into clean units keeps failing in a specific way — terms bleed across boundaries, the same word does different work in different verses, a grouping clean in one passage collapses in the next — that is exactly what you'd predict if the thing doesn't decompose into parts. A part-sorting method applied to a non-part subject fails in precisely that boundaries-won't-hold way. The frustrating experience is consistent with the conclusion the conversation reached independently: the inner being resists clean partition.

If so, the faculties lens wasn't merely unhelpful but *miscalibrated* — "faculties" is the most part-committed frame there is. It presupposes the answer (discrete powers) and asks the verses to fill boxes that may not correspond to anything the text is doing. Sensing it as a distraction is well-founded.

**The sounder move already in hand.** "Allowing the verses to describe how it works" is the same move the more honest science reaches for — let categories emerge from the material rather than imposing a grid. The difficulty may be that "bring the verses into logical units" quietly reintroduced the grid: *units* is a part-word, and the instinct to group is the instinct to find the parts.

**The shift to test (offered as a hypothesis against the corpus, not a claim about the data):** stop asking *what part or category does this word name?* and start asking *what does this word do in the verse?* — what brings the state about, what it produces, what it acts on, what it turns into. This functional/relational and process framing has two things going for it here:
- It likely matches what the verses give anyway — biblical language, Hebrew especially, tends to be concrete, verbal, and relational rather than abstract and taxonomic; it describes the heart *doing* things more than defining what the heart *is*.
- It lets distinct patterns emerge (recurring movements, recurring cause-and-effects) without forcing them into sealed compartments — patterns that can shade into each other, as the text often lets them.

**Three honest caveats.**
1. This is a structural observation about *why a method might be failing*, not a claim about the actual data — the researcher is far better placed to check whether the verses misbehave in the part-resisting way described.
2. The bridge between any behavioural-science framing and scripture is the researcher's to build; there are several legitimate stances on whether the two should inform each other at all. No mapping is assumed.
3. Scripture may resist systematisation for reasons unrelated to ontology — it isn't trying to be a psychology, and some "failure to form units" could be the text simply having different purposes than a descriptive framework wants from it.

**A sharpening question.** When the attempts at logical units failed — was the work grouping *words* (clustering vocabulary into categories), or mapping *relationships and movements* (what leads to what)? If the former, the diagnosis fits cleanly and the latter is the unexplored door. If it was already the latter and still failed, something more interesting is going on, and the story isn't simply "parts versus process."
