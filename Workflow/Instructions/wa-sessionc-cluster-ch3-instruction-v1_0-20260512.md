## 1. What this chapter does

Chapter 3 tells the story of **how Scripture attributes these characteristics to God**. It is the first chapter where the study turns to the analytical evidence in earnest, and it does so through a single unifying lens — the divine pattern. The chapter answers:

- **What does Scripture say about God in this family of characteristics?** Where each characteristic is named as God's own attribute, named as something he gives, named as something he commands or commends, named as something he opposes when claimed autonomously.
- **What is the cluster-wide divine-image pattern?** The arc that runs across most or all of the family — typically a pattern in which each characteristic has a divine version that grounds the human version, and the human characteristic is an imaging of the divine.
- **Where does the divine treatment vary per characteristic?** Each characteristic has its own particular relationship to its divine source — some are characteristic of God himself first and the human person second; some are characteristically creaturely with the divine treatment muted or absent.

The chapter has two distinct structural parts: a **cluster-wide spine** that tells the unifying story, followed by **per-characteristic variation paragraphs** that note where each characteristic departs from or refines the cluster-wide pattern. This is the chapter that establishes the family's standing under God before the deep-dives on inner topography (Ch 4), motion (Ch 5), relationships (Ch 6), and the outside view (Ch 7) begin.

The chapter input file lays out the structure as a single AI-WRITE zone covering both the spine and the per-characteristic paragraphs:

2. **Per-characteristic variation paragraphs (~100–250 words each).** One short paragraph per non-BOUNDARY characteristic, in the order they appear in the cluster. Each names where this characteristic's divine treatment differs from the spine.

Total target: **~1500–2500 words.**

Both parts go into a single AI-WRITE zone; you structure the prose internally (the spine flowing into the per-characteristic paragraphs).

## 3. The cluster-wide spine — what to write

**Open** with the cluster's primary divine claim — typically the divine-image pattern itself: each characteristic in this family is first God's own attribute, then the human person's image of that attribute. Anchor the opening with a key verse that holds the strongest divine-attribute claim in the family.

**Develop** the spine across 3–6 paragraphs. Move through the cluster's divine treatment by *theme*, not by characteristic — for example:

- *God's own possession of these attributes.* The verses where God is named as the source — possessor of wisdom, of understanding, of knowledge, of counsel, of word — and what that possession looks like (unsearchable, eternal, faithful, creating).
- *The giving of these attributes to the human person.* The verses where God places these capacities in the inner being — Bezalel filled with the Spirit for wisdom, Solomon asked and was given understanding, Daniel received interpretation. The pattern of divine bestowal.
- *Christological consolidation.* Where the NT names Christ as the embodiment of one or more of these attributes (Christ the wisdom of God, the Word, etc.). The way the family's pattern reaches its NT focal point in him.
- *Divine opposition to autonomous human claim.* The verses where humanly-claimed wisdom or knowledge or planning is named as folly, opposed by God, brought low. The shadow side of the family.
- *Eschatological orientation.* Where the family points to the fullness yet to come — the earth filled with the knowledge of God, the renewal of the mind, the Word that does not pass away.

Not every cluster will have evidence on every theme. Use what the evidence carries. Apply the **silence principle from shared style §6** — where Scripture is silent on a divine-pattern aspect that the reader would expect (expectation-then-absence), name the silence; routine absence is not surfaced.

**Quote key verses verbatim throughout.** The spine is the chapter's primary verse-citation zone — claims about God should be densely supported.

---

## 4. The per-characteristic variation paragraphs — what to write

After the spine, write one short paragraph per non-BOUNDARY characteristic. Each paragraph answers, in plain English:

- **Does this characteristic follow the cluster-wide pattern?** If yes, name it briefly and move on — do not restate the spine.
- **Where does it depart?** What is distinctive about this characteristic's divine treatment compared to the family as a whole?
- **If Scripture is silent on God's possession of this characteristic, and that silence is meaningful**, name it. (E.g. for M15-F: "Meditative inner activity is the one characteristic in this family largely silent on God's own meditation. The silence is itself a finding — meditation is named as the characteristically creaturely mode within the family of cognitive characteristics, the inner response to what God has given.")

The variation paragraph should cite **at least one key verse** that holds the departure or that anchors the silence-as-finding.

The paragraphs are short — 100–250 words. Do not duplicate spine content. The variation paragraphs assume the reader has just read the spine.

---

## 5. Length

**Total chapter: ~1500–2500 words.** Descriptive guidance, not a cap. The spine takes 600–1500 words; the variation paragraphs take 100–250 each (so 800–2000 words across all characteristics for the M15 9-characteristic case, of which one or two will be near the high end and several near the low end).

Length never constrains comprehensiveness. If the family's divine treatment is rich, the spine takes the space it needs. If a characteristic's divine treatment is unremarkable, its variation paragraph is brief.

## 6. Citation rules for this chapter

Chapter 3 has the **highest citation density** of any chapter except the per-SG deep dives. Specifically:

- **The cluster-wide spine quotes at least five key verses verbatim** — drawn from the divine-pattern key verses listed in the input (typically VCG01/VCG02 anchors across the characteristics).
- **Each per-characteristic variation paragraph quotes at least one key verse verbatim**, unless the paragraph is naming a silence-as-finding (in which case the citation may be a verse the silence comments on, or no citation if the silence-finding is itself the entire paragraph).
- **At least one quoted verse must name God explicitly as possessor or giver of one of the family's characteristics** — the foundational divine-image evidence.

Citations use standard biblical references. Never use VCG codes, tier codes, or finding-ids in the prose.

## 7. How to read the evidence in the input

The input gives you:

- **Brief characteristic profiles** (cross-chapter scaffold).
- **Cluster-wide T0 findings (synthesis).** The strongest cross-cutting divine-pattern claims — your spine's backbone.
- **Per-characteristic T0 findings.** For each non-BOUNDARY characteristic, the analytical record's T0 findings — the divine-pattern observations specific to that characteristic. Drive the variation paragraphs.
- **Per-characteristic T0 silences.** Where Scripture is silent on a divine-pattern aspect for the characteristic, named explicitly. Apply the silence principle (shared §6).
- **Divine-pattern key verses (per characteristic, typically VCG01–VCG02 anchors).** The verses that most directly carry the divine treatment.

You weave from these. The spine draws primarily on the cluster-wide T0 and the strongest cross-characteristic verses. The variation paragraphs draw primarily on each characteristic's own T0 evidence.

---

## 8. Avoiding overlap with later chapters

- **Where each characteristic lives in the person** is the subject of Chapter 4. Do not unfold inner topography here. You may name location briefly when it serves a divine-pattern point ("the Spirit gives wisdom into the heart of Bezalel"), but the chapter's centre is the divine pattern.
- **How each characteristic works** (movement, formation, transformation) is the subject of Chapter 5. Where the divine pattern shows movement (God gives, the person receives), that is in scope for Chapter 3 — but it is the giving, not the mechanics of the human reception, that belongs here.
- **How characteristics relate to each other** is the subject of Chapter 6. Do not catalogue inter-characteristic relationships here. Where the cluster-wide divine pattern depends on the family operating together (the family of divine attributes imaged as a unity in the human person), name that briefly without unfolding the pairings.
- **The view from outside Scripture** is Chapter 7. No clinical-science framing here.

The general principle: Chapter 3 is about **the family under God**. Adjacent material that supports that frame is in scope; adjacent material that drives toward a different chapter's purpose is not.

The cluster's BOUNDARY characteristic is **not treated in this chapter**. The chapter input file omits it. The divine pattern, by the structure of the analytical record, does not run through the BOUNDARY terms.

## 11. Quality bar

Chapter 3 passes the quality bar when:

1. The spine opens with the cluster's primary divine claim and quotes a key verse that holds it.
2. The spine moves thematically (not characteristic-by-characteristic) and covers the major divine-pattern themes present in the cluster.
3. At least five key verses are quoted verbatim across the spine.
4. Each non-BOUNDARY characteristic has a variation paragraph.
5. Each variation paragraph names how the characteristic departs from (or follows) the spine and quotes at least one verse (or is itself a silence-as-finding paragraph with appropriate framing).
6. Meaningful silences (per shared style §6) are named; routine absences are not.
7. No content has crossed into chapters 4–7 territory.
8. No jargon from the shared instruction's avoid-list appears in the prose.
