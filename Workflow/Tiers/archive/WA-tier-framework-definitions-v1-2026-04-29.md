## Governing Question

*What are the inner workings of the human being as described in Scripture — characterising and grouping the inner characteristics, mapping their interrelationships, describing the spirit-soul-body boundary, and documenting the relational interfaces with God, other humans, and other spiritual beings?*

---

## The Role of the Tier Framework

The tier framework defines the **complete scope of analytical enquiry** for every inner-being characteristic studied in the programme. The tiers ensure that analysis is consistent, deep, and broad across all words — they function as analytical prompts, not as a checklist.

Not every word will have content in every tier. Some tiers will be richly populated for a given word; others may be sparse or silent. The framework defines what *could* be explored — the evidence determines what *is* found.

The framework has two layers:
- **First-tier areas** — the major analytical domains (defined in this document)
- **Second-tier components** — the specific elements within each first-tier area (to be defined in subsequent working documents)

---

## Epistemological Foundation

Before the analytical tiers, the framework rests on an epistemological foundation that applies across all tiers:

**Scripture is the primary and governing source.** What Scripture says about a characteristic within any tier is the finding. If Scripture is silent on an aspect, that silence is noted — it is not filled by other sources.

**Human sciences serve as interpretive lenses and light sanity checks.** The disciplines of psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, linguistics, and hermeneutics illuminate and organise what Scripture describes. They help ensure coherence and completeness. They do not add content.

**Original language analysis** — Hebrew and Greek lexical and semantic study — is the primary tool for establishing what a word means and how it operates. This is a sub-discipline of the Scripture source, not a separate source.

*Note: A full human science treatment of any single tier represents a lifetime of scholarly work. The programme's engagement with human science frameworks is selective and sanity-checking in nature, not exhaustive.*

## The Tier Structure

### T1 — Definition
**What is this characteristic?**

T1 establishes the definition of the characteristic — nothing more and nothing less. It is the orientating tier. It must be completed before the analytical tiers begin, because all subsequent analysis depends on knowing what is being studied.

A definition has three elements:

**Name** — What is this characteristic called, and what does the name itself signal about its nature? This includes the primary Hebrew and Greek terms and what their root meanings reveal at the definitional level.

**Kind** — What category of inner-being phenomenon is this? This is the dimension classification — the assignment of the characteristic to one or more of the programme's 10 inner-being dimensions: Emotion (Positive), Emotion (Negative), Cognition, Volition, Moral Character, Relational Disposition, Vitality / Existence, Transformation, Agency / Power, Dependence / Creatureliness. The primary dimension is identified and justified from the evidence.

**Boundary** — What is this characteristic not? The structural opposite of the characteristic is identified. The boundary defines the characteristic by contrast — it prevents the definition from being so broad as to be meaningless.

*Scope boundary: T1 does not ask where the characteristic is located, how it operates, what it produces, how it engages the will, how it relates to others, or how it develops. Those questions belong in T2–T7. T1 asks only: what is it?*

---

### T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**Where is this characteristic located within the constitution of the human person?**

T2 maps the characteristic onto the constitutional structure of the human person — identifying where it is seated, where it is expressed, and how it moves across constitutional boundaries.

A critical clarification from the debate: **constitutional locations are not the same as physical organs.** The body and its physical structures — including the brain — are interface layers between the constitutional reality and the physical world. The characteristic may be interfaced through the body but is not necessarily housed there. Cognition, for example, is not housed in the brain — the brain is the physical connector through which cognition engages the physical world. The cognitive faculty itself exists at a constitutional level that transcends the body.

**Constitutional locations under examination:**

*Spirit* — The deepest level of the human person. The primary site of encounter with God. The seat of spiritual perception and discernment. Scripture locates certain characteristics here specifically — a willing spirit, a contrite spirit, a hardened spirit.

*Soul* — The life-principle of the person. The seat of emotional and volitional experience in its most personal expression. Often the level at which the person experiences the characteristic most directly and consciously.

*Heart* — Distinct from both spirit and soul in Scripture. The heart is the integrating centre of the person — the seat of knowing, willing, feeling, and moral awareness together. Many characteristics are explicitly located here.

*Mind* — Distinct in Scripture from the brain. The mind is the faculty of thought, understanding, and discernment at the soul level. It is the interface between cognition and conscious experience.

*Body* — The physical interface layer. The body connects the constitutional person to the physical world through the five physical senses. Characteristics that have somatic expression — bodily posture, physical gesture, physiological response — are mapped here.

*DNA and Generational Constitution* — The inherited dispositional layer. Scripture explicitly names iniquity and disposition passing across generations. DNA — and the broader generational constitution — carries inherited tendencies toward certain emotional, volitional, and relational patterns. This is a physical carrier of constitutional reality, not merely a biological phenomenon.

*T2 also maps:*
- The direction of movement of the characteristic across constitutional levels
- The interface points between the physical senses and their inner-being analogues
- The inner-outer boundary — how inner constitutional reality surfaces in outward expression

*Scope boundary: T2 asks where the characteristic is located and how it moves constitutionally. It does not ask what capacities the person brings to engage with it (T3), or how it operates relationally (T5), or how it develops over time (T6).*

### T3 — The Inner Faculties
**Through which inner capacities does this characteristic operate — and what does it reveal about those faculties?**

T3 examines the characteristic through the lens of the human person's active inner capacities — the faculties through which every characteristic is received, processed, expressed, and chosen. These faculties are not locations (T2) — they are active capacities the person brings to every inner-being encounter.

A critical clarification: the inner faculties are not the same as the characteristics being studied. They are the constitutional equipment through which the person engages with characteristics. Volition, for example, is itself a faculty — the capacity to choose — and it both shapes and is shaped by every other characteristic the person encounters.

**The inner faculties under examination:**

*Perception — the inner senses*
The capacity to receive and register inner and outer reality beyond the physical. The physical senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell) have inner-being analogues in Scripture:
- Inner hearing — spiritual receptivity — *he who has ears to hear*
- Inner sight — the eyes of the heart — *that the eyes of your heart may be enlightened*
- Inner taste — direct experiential knowledge — *taste and see that the Lord is good*
- Inner touch — the sense of presence, contact, and reality
- Inner smell — *a fragrant offering* — perceiving the quality of what is offered

Beyond these five analogues, Scripture and human experience suggest a sixth faculty of perception — intuition, spiritual discernment, knowing beyond rational process — which may be seated in the spirit. The relationship of this sixth perception to the five inner-sense analogues is a live question for the programme.

*Cognition — knowing and understanding*
The capacity to know, understand, remember, discern, and hold truth. A foundational clarification: cognition is not housed in the brain. The brain is the physical interface through which cognition engages the physical world. The cognitive faculty itself transcends the body — Scripture shows the dead knowing, remembering, and recognising (Luke 16). Knowing in Scripture is frequently located in the heart, not the brain. The renewed body at resurrection does not create new cognition — it reconnects existing cognition to a renewed physical interface.

*Affect — feeling and emotional experience*
The capacity to experience inner states — emotion, feeling, affective response. This is the emotional faculty through which the characteristic is felt and experienced. It is distinct from cognition (knowing about the characteristic) and from volition (choosing in relation to the characteristic).

*Volition — the capacity to choose*
The God-given capacity to choose. Scripture is clear that this capacity distinguishes the human person from other creatures. Volition is constitutional — it is not a characteristic but a faculty. Three aspects of volition are analytically distinct:
- Volition as capacity — the ability to choose itself, given by God
- Volition as interaction — choosing one characteristic shapes or excludes others (choosing love excludes hate; choosing grace produces mercy)
- Volition as constrained — the capacity to choose is bounded by DNA, physical constitution, formative history, and circumstance

*Moral evaluation — conscience, awareness, and inner witness*
The capacity to assess against a standard — right/wrong, good/bad, true/false. This is not a single thing. Scripture identifies several distinct elements:
- Conscience — the inner faculty that bears witness to moral reality
- Awareness — the perceptual capacity to register moral quality
- The law written on the heart — the inner standard against which evaluation occurs
- Shame and guilt — the affective responses to moral evaluation

These are related but distinct. Conscience is the faculty. Shame and guilt are responses. The law on the heart is the standard. A complete examination of how this characteristic engages the moral-evaluative capacity distinguishes between these elements.

*T3 also examines:*
- How this characteristic engages, enables, bypasses, or transforms each of these faculties
- Whether the characteristic operates primarily through one faculty or across several
- What the characteristic reveals about the nature and limits of each faculty it engages

*Scope boundary: T3 asks what inner capacities the person brings and how the characteristic engages them. It does not ask where the characteristic is located (T2), how it operates relationally toward others (T5), or how it develops over time (T6).*

### T5 — Relational Interfaces
**How does this characteristic operate across the relational interfaces of the human person?**

T5 examines the characteristic through the three relational interfaces named in the governing question: the interface with God, the interface with other humans, and the interface with other spiritual beings.

The governing question establishes that the human person exists in structured relationship — not as an isolated individual but as a being constitutionally oriented toward God, other humans, and the wider spiritual reality. Every inner-being characteristic has some operation across these interfaces — either it is received through them, expressed through them, shaped by them, or it shapes them.

**Relational interfaces under examination:**

*Divine interface — God to human and human to God*
How does this characteristic operate in the relationship between God and the human person? This includes:
- The direction of operation — does it originate with God and move toward the human, or with the human toward God, or both?
- What it reveals about God's disposition toward the human person
- What it reveals about the basis on which God extends or withholds it
- How the human person seeks, receives, and responds to it in the context of this relationship
- Its expression in worship, supplication, covenant, and liturgical contexts

*Human interface — person to person*
How does this characteristic operate between human persons? This includes:
- Whether and how the person who has received it extends it to others
- What relational orientations enable or block its extension
- Whether it operates differently within existing relationships versus across relational boundaries
- Its social and community dimension

*Spiritual beings interface*
How does this characteristic operate in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial? This interface is currently not evidenced in the question catalogue and represents a gap in the current analytical framework. It is retained as a named interface because the governing question explicitly includes it.

*T5 also examines:*
- The boundaries within which the characteristic operates — covenantal, social, relational
- The direction of the characteristic's operation across each interface
- Whether the characteristic crosses or respects existing relational boundaries

*Scope boundary: T5 asks how the characteristic operates across relational interfaces. It does not ask where it is constitutionally located (T2), through which inner faculties it operates (T3), or how it develops over time (T6).*

### T6 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**How does this characteristic shape the person over time?**

T6 examines the characteristic as a formative force — asking what it does to the person across time, through what process and mechanism, in what direction, and toward what ultimate end.

This is distinct from T3 (which asks what inner capacities are engaged) and T5 (which asks how the characteristic operates relationally). T6 asks about the developmental arc — the person before, during, and after the characteristic has done its work.

**Areas under examination:**

*Nature of transformation*
Does this characteristic change the person's condition, or does it change the person's orientation to their condition? This is a fundamental distinction — some characteristics transform what the person is; others transform how the person relates to what they are.

*Sequence and process*
Is there a discernible sequence of inner states through which the characteristic moves the person? What is the mechanism of change — is it discipline, transformation, encounter, or something else?

*Suffering and affliction as formative context*
What is the relationship between this characteristic and conditions of weakness, suffering, and affliction? Does the characteristic emerge from suffering, deepen through it, or operate despite it?

*Formation and sanctification*
How does this characteristic shape the person in the longer arc of formation — moral formation, character development, sanctification? What does it produce in the person over years rather than moments?

*Eschatological trajectory*
What is the ultimate direction of this characteristic — toward what end does it point? Does it carry a forward orientation — does the present experience of it anticipate a future fullness? What does the characteristic look like in its ultimate or eschatological expression?

*Scope boundary: T6 asks how the characteristic shapes the person over time. It does not ask where it is located (T2), through which faculties it operates (T3), or how it connects to other characteristics (T7).*

---

### T7 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics
**How does this characteristic relate to adjacent inner-being characteristics?**

T7 maps the characteristic within the wider landscape of the inner person — identifying connections, dependencies, sequences, overlaps, and distinctions with other characteristics. The governing question explicitly names this as a purpose of the programme: mapping the interrelationships.

No characteristic exists in isolation. Every characteristic has structural relationships — some characteristics precede others, some produce others, some exclude others, some co-occur consistently, some share vocabulary at the root level. T7 makes these relationships visible.

**Areas under examination:**

*Co-occurrence patterns*
Which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently in the same verses as this characteristic? What does the pattern of co-occurrence reveal about structural affinity?

*Sequential relationships*
Does this characteristic precede, follow, or accompany other characteristics? Is there a directional sequence — does one produce the other, or do they arise together?

*Causal and constitutive relationships*
Does this characteristic produce another characteristic, or is it produced by one? Is the relationship causal (one causes the other) or constitutive (one is part of what the other is)?

*Vocabulary and root sharing*
Which adjacent characteristics share vocabulary — individual terms or root architecture — with this characteristic? What does shared vocabulary reveal about conceptual relationship?

*Distinctions*
Where apparent overlap exists between this characteristic and an adjacent one, how are they distinguished? What is the precise boundary between them?

*Shared verse anchors*
Does any verse function as a primary anchor in both this characteristic's study and another characteristic's study? What does dual anchoring reveal about the relationship between them?

*Dimensional sharing*
How many of this characteristic's confirmed analytical dimensions are shared with another characteristic or cluster? Is there any characteristic that shares all confirmed dimensions?

*Scope boundary: T7 asks how this characteristic relates structurally to other characteristics. It does not ask how it operates within the person (T2, T3) or how it develops over time (T6).*

---

### T8 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**Where does the knowledge of this characteristic come from — and how is it derived with integrity?**

T8 is qualitatively different from T1–T7. The analytical tiers (T1–T7) define the body of understanding — the substantive areas that together constitute a complete characterisation. T8 defines the epistemological foundation — the sources and disciplines from which understanding in T1–T7 is derived and validated.

T8 does not stand alongside T1–T7 as a peer analytical area. It underlies all of them. Every finding in T1–T7 is grounded in and tested against the sources and disciplines of T8.

**T8 has three components:**

*T8.1 — Lexical and semantic analysis*
The original language vocabulary of the characteristic — Hebrew and Greek terms, root meanings, grammatical forms, semantic range, canonical development across the Testaments, LXX bridging, and NT coinage. This is the primary linguistic evidence base from which all T1–T7 findings are drawn.

*T8.2 — Verse and literary interpretation*
How the characteristic functions within its verse evidence — the word's role in the sentence, the logical structure of the argument, the literary form (narrative, psalm, prophecy, wisdom, epistle), the contextual setting (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological), and the interpretive method applied to read the evidence with integrity.

*T8.3 — Human science frameworks*
The interpretive lenses drawn from psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, and related disciplines. These frameworks illuminate and organise what Scripture describes. They serve as sanity checks — ensuring that the characterisation is coherent, that nothing obvious is missed, and that the findings can be communicated in terms that connect to wider understanding of the human person. They do not add content to T1–T7; they help ensure the reading of Scripture is complete and coherent.

## Key Clarifications Embedded in this Document

The following clarifications emerged from the debate and are embedded in the tier definitions above:

1. **T1 is the definition tier only** — name, kind, boundary. Narrow and bounded. Does not move into any other tier.

2. **The body is an interface layer, not the seat of characteristics.** Constitutional locations are faculties and their interface points, not physical organs. The brain interfaces cognition with the physical world but does not house it.

3. **Cognition transcends the body.** Scripture shows the dead knowing and remembering. The renewed body reconnects existing cognition — it does not create it.

4. **DNA and generational constitution are constitutional locations in T2.** Scripture names iniquity passing across generations. Inherited disposition is a constitutional reality, not only a biological one.

5. **The inner senses are analogues to the five physical senses** — and possibly a sixth. They belong in T3 as perceptual faculties, not in T2 as locations.

6. **Volition has three distinct aspects**: capacity (God-given), interaction (choosing one shapes others), and constraints (DNA, formation, history).

7. **Moral evaluation is not a single thing** — it is a cluster: conscience (faculty), shame and guilt (responses), the law on the heart (standard).

8. **T3 replaces and absorbs T3 and T4** from the earlier framework — moral conscience and volition are both inner faculties, not separate analytical tiers.

9. **The third relational interface (other spiritual beings) is a confirmed gap** in the current question catalogue.
