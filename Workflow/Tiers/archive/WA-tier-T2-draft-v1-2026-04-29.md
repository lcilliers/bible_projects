## Full Text

### T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

T2 maps a characteristic onto the constitutional structure of the human person — identifying where it is seated, where it is expressed, and how it moves across constitutional boundaries. The question is not simply where the characteristic is located but what that location reveals, and what Scripture is doing when it connects a characteristic to a specific constitutional element.

A foundational distinction governs the whole of T2. Constitutional locations are not physical organs. The body and its structures — including the brain — are the design layer through which the constitutional person interfaces with the physical world. A characteristic may be expressed through the body without being housed there. Cognition is not located in the brain — the brain is the physical connector through which cognition engages the physical world. The cognitive faculty itself exists at a constitutional level that transcends the body: Scripture shows the dead knowing, remembering, and recognising (Luke 16), and the renewed body at resurrection reconnects existing cognition to a renewed interface rather than creating it afresh. This distinction holds across all constitutional locations. T2 maps the constitutional reality; the body is the interface through which that reality operates in the physical world.

**Spirit.** The deepest level of the human person and the primary site of encounter with God. The seat of spiritual perception and discernment. Scripture locates certain characteristics explicitly at the spirit level — a willing spirit, a contrite spirit, a hardened spirit (Psalm 51; Ezekiel 36). T2 asks whether this characteristic is named as a spirit-level phenomenon and what that location reveals about its nature and its relationship to the divine encounter.

**Soul.** The seat of the faculties and their expression into the characteristics of the innermost personal experience. The soul is the constitutional home of the person's active inner life — broader and deeper than any single faculty or characteristic. T2 asks whether this characteristic is named as a soul-level phenomenon and what that location reveals. The soul is not reducible to emotion or to volition; it is the constitutional level at which the person's inner life in its fullness operates.

Heart and mind are the two named subsets of the soul visible in the current evidence:

*Heart.* The integrating centre of the person within the soul — the seat of knowing, willing, feeling, and moral awareness held together. Many characteristics are explicitly located in the heart: "I have hidden your word in my heart" (Psalm 119); "love the Lord your God with all your heart" (Deuteronomy 6). T2 asks whether this characteristic is located in the heart and what that location reveals about its integrative function in the person.

*Mind.* The faculty of thought, understanding, and discernment within the soul — distinct in Scripture from the brain. The mind is the interface between cognition and conscious experience: "be transformed by the renewing of your mind" (Romans 12). T2 asks whether this characteristic engages or is located in the mind and what that reveals.

Heart and mind are the named soul-subsets present in the current evidence. The possibility exists that Scripture identifies other soul-subsets not yet named in the programme's vocabulary. T2 does not force every soulish characteristic into either heart or mind. Unnamed locations within the soul are held open and noted where they appear.

**Body.** The physical design layer of the human person. Its constitution, structure, and parts are the design print through which the soul interfaces with the physical world. DNA is the foundational design print of the body — defining what the body is constitutionally and carrying that design forward generationally.

Scripture explicitly links specific body parts to inner-being characteristics. T2 examines the significance of each such link. When Scripture associates a characteristic with a body part — the eyes as a window to the soul, the bowels as the seat of compassion in Hebrew anthropology, the hands as the instrument of action, the knees as the posture of submission — T2 does not treat the reference as rhetorical decoration. It asks what the reference is doing.

Three analytical anchors guide this examination:

*Significance.* What is Scripture doing by naming this body part in relation to this characteristic? The reference may be emphatic — the body part gives vividness and weight to a soul-level reality without participating in it directly. It may be functional — the body part genuinely participates in the characteristic, as when the ear is the channel through which faith enters ("faith comes by hearing," Romans 10:17). It may be expressive — the body gives outward utterance to an inner reality, as when lifted hands make intercession visible or tears make grief communicable. It may be indicative — the body part signals the inner state without causing it, as when a downcast face indicates grief. It may be mediating — the body part is the channel through which the characteristic is received from the outer world into the inner person. These distinctions matter because they determine whether the body is participating in the characteristic or only displaying it.

*Direction.* Which way does the relationship between the characteristic and the body run — and what is the consequence? The soul expresses through the body (disgust producing nausea — a soul characteristic with a real physical consequence); the body receives and feeds back to the soul (hearing the word through the physical ear as the entry point for faith); or both directions operate simultaneously. Direction is not abstract. The consequence is real and in some cases irreversible. Chronic anxiety produces measurable physical damage. Prolonged grief depletes the body. The direction of the relationship carries the weight of its consequences.

*Deposit.* Does sustained or repeated operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body? Chronic patterns of inner-being activity — sustained fear, habitual anger, prolonged sorrow — may produce lasting physical change and leave a deposit in the body's design, including what is carried forward generationally. Scripture's language of iniquity passing to the third and fourth generation points toward a constitutional transmission that may have a physical dimension. T2 notes where the evidence points toward such deposit. The developmental consequence of that deposit — what it produces in the person and in those who come after — is examined in T6 (Formative and Developmental Dimension), which holds the forward arc. T2 holds the constitutional mechanism.

**Scope boundary.** T2 asks where this characteristic is located within the human constitution and what Scripture is doing when it links the characteristic to constitutional elements and body parts. It does not ask through which active inner faculties the person engages with the characteristic — that is T3. It does not ask how the characteristic operates relationally toward others — that is T5. It does not ask how the characteristic shapes the person over time — that is T6, except where T2 identifies the constitutional mechanism that T6 then examines for its developmental consequence.
