## Purpose of this session

Continuation of the tier framework debate. Starting point was the T1 preliminary second-tier document (WA-obslog-t1-second-tier-prelim-v1-2026-04-28.md). The session moved into a fundamental structural reexamination of T1–T4, producing significant clarifications to the tier framework.

## Key debate and thinking

### T1 as a mirror of T1–T7

Observation: The T1 second-tier components produced in the preliminary document were themselves a miniature version of T2–T7. T1.1 (origin) looked like T2. T1.7 (conditions of reception) looked like T3 and T4. T1.5 (what it produces) looked like T6.

This revealed a structural problem: T1 as originally framed was not a peer of T2–T7 — it was trying to hold everything.

Question posed: If T1 is scrapped completely and we focus only on T2–T7, what would be genuinely missing?

Answer reached: Three things are genuinely missing without T1:
1. Classification and naming — what dimension does this characteristic belong to?
2. Origin and source — where does it come from?
3. Structural form — is it an act, disposition, condition, or quality?

But even these three were examined further. Origin was identified as not belonging to definition. Structural form was identified as part of classification.

### T1 reset — Definition only

Resolution: T1 is radically narrowed to the definition tier only.

T1 — Definition: What is this characteristic?
- Name — what it is called and what the name signals
- Kind — what category of inner-being phenomenon it is — dimension classification
- Boundary — what it is not — its structural opposite

Everything else that was in T1 migrates to the tier where it genuinely belongs.

Researcher confirmation: T1 scope must be narrow. It should not move into the other tiers.

### T3 and T4 — the structural problem

Researcher observation: T3 (moral conscience) and T4 (volition and agency) drop three concepts into the framework — moral, volition, agency — as if they are self-evident. But they are not self-evident. Questions raised:

- What is moral conscience — does it exist as a single thing, or is it a collection of characteristics with a specific role?
- What is volition exactly?
- What is agency, and is it distinct from volition?
- Are there other categories being missed?

### Volition — three distinct aspects identified

Researcher unpacked volition as having three distinct aspects:

1. Volition as capacity — the God-given ability to choose. Constitutional. Distinguishes humans from other creatures. Not a characteristic but a faculty.

2. Volition as interaction — choosing one characteristic shapes or excludes others (choosing love excludes hate; choosing grace results in mercy). This is volition operating across characteristics — touches T7.

3. Volition as constrained — the capacity to choose is bounded by DNA, physical constitution, formative history, circumstance.

Key insight: Volition is not simply an analytical lens applied to other characteristics. It is a foundational faculty that underlies all inner-being activity.

### Moral — not a single thing

Debate on what moral conscience actually is. Scripture speaks of:
- The heart that knows right and wrong
- The conscience that bears witness
- Shame and guilt as inner responses
- The law written on the heart

Conclusion: These are not the same thing. Conscience may be the faculty. Shame and guilt may be the responses. The law written on the heart may be the standard. Moral is a cluster of faculties, responses, and standards — not a single thing.

### The physical senses and inner-being analogues

Researcher raised: the physical senses (sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell) have inner-being analogues in Scripture. The sixth sense — intuition, perception beyond the five physical senses — may be seated in the inner-being reality.

Claude identified the scriptural inner-sense analogues:
- Hearing — spiritual receptivity (he who has ears to hear)
- Sight — the eyes of the heart
- Taste — taste and see that the Lord is good
- Touch — sense of presence and contact
- Smell — fragrant offering

Question raised: where does the sixth sense sit — spirit faculty, soul faculty, or integration of all five inner analogues?

### Cognition beyond the brain

Researcher insight: Cognition — knowing, remembering, understanding — is not housed in the brain. The brain is the physical interface between the cognitive faculty and the physical world. The faculty itself exists at a level that transcends the body.

Evidence: Scripture shows the dead know, remember, and recognise (Luke 16). The renewed body does not create new cognition — it reconnects existing cognition to a renewed physical interface. Knowing in Scripture is frequently located in the heart, not the brain.

Implication for T2: Constitutional locations are not physical organs but faculties and their interface points. The body — including DNA — is an interface layer, not the seat of the characteristic.

### DNA as a constitutional location

Identified as a missing location in the current T2 framework. DNA carries:
- Inherited disposition — tendencies toward certain patterns
- Generational material — iniquity passing to third and fourth generation (Scripture)
- The physical expression of the image of God

Proposed T2 locations now include:
- Spirit
- Soul
- Heart — distinct from both spirit and soul in Scripture
- Mind — distinct in Scripture
- Body — as physical interface
- DNA / generational constitution — as inherited dispositional layer

### T3 and T4 reconstituted — The Inner Faculties

The emerging grouping from the debate: T3 and T4 should be combined and reframed as a single tier covering the inner faculties — the active capacities the person brings to every characteristic.

These faculties appear to be:
- Perception — inner senses including analogues to physical senses and beyond
- Cognition — knowing, understanding, discerning — not brain-housed but constitutionally present
- Affect — feeling, emotional experience, inner response
- Volition — capacity to choose, bounded by constitution and history
- Moral evaluation — conscience, awareness, inner witness against a standard

What unifies these: they are all capacities the person brings — not locations, not relational operations, not developmental processes. They are the active inner life of the person through which every characteristic is received, processed, expressed, and chosen.

Proposed reframing:
T3 — The Inner Faculties
Scope: Through which inner capacities does this characteristic operate — and what does it reveal about those faculties?

Researcher confirmation: Yes. Prepare a document for the tier 1 framing/definition/description. Genuine clarifications have been made to aid discovery through Scripture.

## Current conclusions and decisions

### Revised Tier Framework (working version after Session 2)

| Tier | Title | Scope | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| T1 | Definition | What is this characteristic — name, kind, boundary | Reset and confirmed |
| T2 | Constitutional Location and Boundaries | Where it is located — spirit, soul, heart, mind, body, DNA | Expanded — DNA added |
| T3 | The Inner Faculties | Through which inner capacities does this characteristic operate | Reconstituted — replaces T3 and T4 |
| T4 | [Absorbed into T3] | | Pending formal resolution |
| T5 | Relational Interfaces | Operation across relational interfaces — God, other humans, other spiritual beings | Unchanged |
| T6 | Formative and Developmental Dimension | How it shapes the person over time | Unchanged |
| T7 | Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics | Connections, dependencies, sequences, distinctions | Unchanged |
| T8 | Evidential and Methodological Foundation | Scripture primary, human sciences as sanity check | Unchanged |

### Key clarifications made in this session

1. T1 is the definition tier only — name, kind, boundary. Narrow and bounded.
2. T2 locations include DNA and generational constitution — not just spirit/soul/body.
3. Cognition is not brain-housed — the brain is a physical interface only.
4. The body and its physical senses are interface layers, not the seat of characteristics.
5. T3 and T4 are reconstituted as a single Inner Faculties tier.
6. The inner senses (analogues to the five physical senses, and possibly a sixth) are a legitimate line of enquiry within T3.
7. Volition has three distinct aspects: capacity, interaction across characteristics, and constraints.
8. Moral is not a single thing — it is a cluster of faculties, responses, and standards.
