## Science sections

### Section 1 — Hope as psychological construct

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / clinical psychology — hope research

**Glosses covered:**
hope (elpis), hope (miq.veh), hope (se.ver), hope (to.che.let), hope (tiq.vah), to hope/expect (elpizo), to hope/expect (elpizō), untroubled (alupos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Snyder's hope theory (*The Psychology of Hope*, 1994) defines hope as the perceived capacity to find pathways to desired goals (pathway thinking) combined with the motivation to use those pathways (agency thinking). Hope is thus not a passive wish but an active cognitive-motivational state. Hope is a significant predictor of academic achievement, athletic performance, physical health outcomes, and resilience across the lifespan. Clinical hope interventions have demonstrated efficacy in depression and anxiety treatment. Herth's hope research (in palliative care contexts) shows that hope remains possible even in terminal illness — the object and timescale of hope shift but hope itself persists.

**Emergence — what causes hope to arise:**
Hope arises from: the identification of a valued goal; the belief that pathways to the goal exist (pathway thinking); and the belief that one has the capacity to initiate and sustain movement along those pathways (agency thinking). It is also socially transmitted — hope is sustained and renewed through relationships with others who hold and model hope. The cluster's waiting vocabulary (tiq.vah — cord, thread, hope — the thing that holds despite stretching) suggests that hope involves a sustained, active attachment to the expected outcome, not merely a cognitive judgment.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hope to diminish:**
Hope diminishes when: pathways to valued goals are blocked or perceived as non-existent; agency beliefs are undermined by failure or learned helplessness; the goal itself is lost or abandoned; or the social network that sustains hope is removed. Hopelessness (the specific absence of hope) is one of the two core cognitive features of depression (alongside helplessness) in Beck's cognitive theory, and is among the strongest predictors of suicidal ideation. Hope can be protected against despair by: the flexibility to reframe goals when specific pathways are blocked; secure relational support; and — in the theological context — the grounding of hope in the reliability of God's promises rather than in the visibility of pathways.

**Location — where hope is seated:**
Primarily cognitive and motivational — hope is a thought-pattern and motivational orientation, not primarily a felt emotion. It has emotional consequences (the felt experience of hope as energising, of hopelessness as depleting) but is constituted by cognitive-motivational content. The tiq.vah vocabulary (cord/thread/hope) suggests a somatic-metaphorical dimension — hope as the line that holds, that keeps the person attached to the expected outcome even when circumstances would break the connection.

**External influences:**
*Relational support:* Hope is socially sustained — being in community with others who hold hope is among the most reliable hope-maintaining factors. *Prior experience:* A history of pathway-finding and successful agency builds hope-capital; a history of inescapable failure depletes it. *Cultural and theological:* Communities with robust hope traditions (eschatological hope, covenant promise) sustain hope under conditions that would otherwise produce hopelessness. *Circumstances:* Objectively hopeless situations (terminal diagnosis, imprisonment, famine) test hope most severely; the fact that hope is maintained in such contexts is evidence that its grounds are not merely circumstantial.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich. Hope psychology is well-developed in positive and clinical psychology.

### Section 2 — Expectation, anticipation, and forward orientation

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology — prospective cognition

**Glosses covered:**
eager expectation (apokaradokia), expectation (prosdokia), expectation (mab.bat), to look for (prosdokaō), to aspire (oregō), to aspire (filotimeomai)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Expectancy theory (Vroom, 1964) in motivational psychology posits that motivation is a function of three variables: expectancy (if I try, will I succeed?), instrumentality (if I succeed, will I get the reward?), and valence (do I value the reward?). Prospective cognition research (Szpunar) examines the mental simulation of future scenarios — how the imagined future shapes present motivation. Apokaradokia (eager, outstretched-head expectation) names a posture of straining forward — the physical metaphor of the body leaned toward the anticipated event.

**Emergence — what causes eager expectation to arise:**
Eager expectation arises from: a highly valued anticipated outcome; strong belief in its arrival; and the felt tension of not-yet-having what is valued and believed-for. The physical metaphor of apokaradokia (head stretched forward as if looking around a corner) captures the whole-body orientation of intense anticipation.

**Reduction and absence:** Expectation ceases with the arrival of the expected event, with the abandonment of the expectation, or with the collapse into despair when the expected event does not arrive. The tension of sustained expectation without arrival is one of the most psychologically demanding states — it requires both the maintenance of conviction and the tolerance of delay.

**Location — where expectation is seated:**
Cognitive and motivational — forward-directed mental simulation combined with motivational orientation toward the anticipated outcome. The bodily metaphor of apokaradokia gives it a somatic expression — the posture of one who is leaning toward what they are waiting for.

**External influences:**
*Prior fulfilment:* The reliability of past expectation-fulfilment shapes the confidence of present expectation. Communities with a tradition of God's faithfulness to his promises sustain expectation more durably. *Social:* Shared expectation (eschatological community anticipation) is more robust than individual anticipation in isolation.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Expectancy and prospective cognition research provide frameworks; the small gloss count limits scope.

### Section 3 — Longing, desire, and motivational orientation

**Primary field:** Motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
longing (epipothēsis), longing (epipothia), longing (ka.leh), longing (ta.a.vah), longed for (epipothētos), to long (ka.saph), to long for (epithumeō), to long for (epipotheō), to long for (sha.aph)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan, *Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behaviour*, 1985) identifies three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — whose satisfaction generates intrinsic motivation and genuine desire. Desire psychology more broadly distinguishes appetite (biological drives), wanting (mesolimbic dopamine system — Berridge's wanting/liking distinction), and longing (the specifically human, value-laden form of desire oriented toward personal goods). The cluster's longing vocabulary (epipothēsis — intense yearning; sha.aph — to gasp after, to pant) names the most intense form of desire — not appetite or craving but the whole person oriented toward an intensely valued other or good.

**Emergence — what causes longing to arise:**
Longing arises from: the perception of something as genuinely desirable and not yet possessed; the established attachment to what is longed for (longing typically follows love — you long for what you already love); and the experience of distance or absence that activates the longing. The panting/gasping vocabulary (sha.aph) reflects the physiological dimension of intense longing — desire so strong it changes the body's breathing.

**Reduction and absence — what causes longing to diminish:**
Longing diminishes with: the attainment of the longed-for object (fulfilled longing); the loss of the belief that the object is attainable (longing collapsing into grief or resignation); and habituation (prolonged presence reducing the intensity of the desire). Biblical longing for God (Psalms 42, 63) is characterised by a longing that does not diminish with partial encounter but is intensified by it.

**Location — where longing is seated:**
Emotional, motivational, and somatic. Longing is felt in the body (the panting, the aching of unfulfilled desire) and oriented in the whole person toward the longed-for object. It is not purely cognitive — it involves the arousal and motivational systems as well as the evaluative and cognitive systems.

**External influences:**
*Attachment:* Longing follows from love — you long for what you are attached to. The strength of the attachment determines the intensity of the longing. *Separation:* Longing is activated by distance from the beloved object — presence satisfies, absence intensifies. *Cultural:* The objects that are culturally valued shape what is longed for. Spiritual traditions that cultivate desire for God cultivate longing as a spiritual orientation.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Motivational psychology and desire research provide solid frameworks.

### Section 4 — Patient waiting and endurance

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
to wait (cha.khah), to wait: hope (ya.chal), to wait: wait (ya.chal), waiting (ya.chil)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Delay of gratification research (Mischel — the marshmallow test; follow-up studies by Mischel and colleagues) demonstrates that the capacity to wait for a greater future reward is a significant predictor of life outcomes — academic achievement, social functioning, health. Self-regulation capacity (Baumeister and Vohs) underlies waiting — it requires the active suppression of the immediate preference in favour of the future good. Patience research (Schnitker, *Is patience a virtue?*, 2012) distinguishes interpersonal patience (waiting for people), life-hardship patience (enduring difficult circumstances), and daily-hassle patience — and associates patience with wellbeing, hope, and positive emotion.

**Emergence — what causes patient waiting to arise:**
Patient waiting arises from: confidence in the reliability of the expected outcome (hope); adequate self-regulatory capacity; the community and relational support that sustains waiting under delay; and the character formation that has built tolerance for temporal frustration. Ya.chal (to wait with hope) suggests that biblical waiting is not passive endurance but active, hope-laden orientation toward the expected arrival.

**Reduction and absence — what causes patience to diminish:**
Patience breaks under: very long delays without signal of progress; the erosion of confidence in the expected outcome's arrival; the depletion of self-regulatory resources; and the absence of community that sustains the waiting person. Impatience is associated with low self-regulatory capacity, high present-orientation (Zimbardo time perspective), and the absence of hope.

**Location — where patient waiting is seated:**
Primarily volitional and emotional — it requires both the will to sustain the wait and the emotional management of the frustration that delay produces. It has a cognitive dimension (the maintained belief in the expected outcome) and a character dimension (the stable disposition to endure delay without abandoning the expectation).

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Cultures with strong future-orientation and long time horizons cultivate patience more readily. Cultures of immediate gratification inhibit it. *Community:* Shared waiting — waiting together in community — is more sustainable than solitary waiting. The communal eschatological waiting of the covenant community sustains individual hope in ways that individual waiting alone cannot.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Delay of gratification and patience research are well-developed; the gloss count is small.

## Outside-science inventory

- **hope (tiq.vah) in its eschatological register** — hope for resurrection, for the new creation, for the final fulfilment of God's covenant promises is a theological category. Science addresses forward orientation and hope in relation to achievable human goals; it does not address hope that is grounded in the resurrection of Christ and oriented toward events that transcend history. The eschatological quality of biblical hope — hoping for what cannot yet be seen, on the basis of what has been done in Christ — is outside science's descriptive reach.

**Observation:** M18 has minimal outside-science content except at the eschatological edge. The psychology of hope, expectation, longing, and patient waiting are well covered by science. The theological distinctive is the object and ground of biblical hope — not human possibility but divine promise; not probability but covenant reliability.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Positive and clinical psychology (hope — Section 1); prospective cognition (Section 2); motivational psychology (Section 3); self-regulation research (Section 4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Snyder's two-component model (pathways + agency) illuminates the structure of biblical hope — the pathways dimension (God will make a way) and the agency dimension (I will trust and act). The hopelessness-depression link illuminates the cluster's spiritual despair vocabulary and why the loss of hope is so devastating. Mischel's delay of gratification research illuminates why patient waiting is associated with character strength and why it is cultivable.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science grounds hope in visible or probable pathways; biblical hope is explicitly hope for the unseen (Romans 8:24 — hope that is seen is not hope). The grounding of hope in God's character and covenant promise — rather than in the probability of the hoped-for outcome — is a genuine register divergence. Hopelessness in the science account is a cognitive distortion; hopelessness in the biblical account can be an appropriate response to every possible human outcome while remaining inconsistent with hope in God.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** Herth's palliative care research (hope in terminal illness) may surface the closest science analogue to eschatological hope — the phenomenon of hope that persists when all human pathways are closed. This convergence point is worth noting in Session B.
