## Science sections

### Section 1 — Pride as emotional state

**Primary field:** Social psychology / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
pride (kauchēma), pride (kauchēsis), pride (ge.ah), pride (ga.a.vah), pride (ga.on), pride (ge.vah), pride (ro.hav), pride (sha.chats), proud (ge), proud (ge.eh), proud (ra.hav), to boast (kauchaomai), to boast: boast (ha.lal)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Tracy and Robins (*The Psychological Structure of Pride*, 2007) identified pride as a distinct emotion with two facets: authentic pride (achievement-based, produced by specific accomplishment — "I did something great") and hubristic pride (global self-evaluation — "I am great"). These two facets have different antecedents, different expressions, different correlates, and different downstream consequences. Authentic pride is associated with achievement motivation and prosocial behaviour; hubristic pride is associated with narcissism, aggression, and interpersonal problems. The two-facet model is directly relevant to a cluster that spans legitimate boasting (kauchaomai applied to God's works) and self-glorifying pride (ge.ah, ga.on in negative registers).

**Emergence — what causes pride to arise:**
Pride arises from the appraisal of a positive outcome as self-caused — the attribution of a good result to one's own actions or character. Authentic pride is triggered by specific achievement; hubristic pride by global self-inflation independent of specific performance. The social signal function of pride (Tracy and Robins) suggests it arises partly to communicate status and claim recognition from others — an inherently social emotion. Boasting (the expressive behaviour associated with pride) arises when pride seeks social validation rather than remaining internal.

**Reduction and absence — what causes pride to diminish:**
Authentic pride diminishes as the achievement recedes into the past or as new challenges require fresh effort. Hubristic pride is more stable and more resistant to revision — it is a trait-level self-evaluation rather than a state response. Pride is appropriately reduced by failure, by recognition of one's dependence on others, and by encounters with genuine greatness that dwarf the self. Absence of pride capacity is associated with depression and with shame-based self-evaluation (in which no achievement registers as worth valuing).

**Location — where pride is seated:**
Primarily emotional and cognitive, with somatic and postural expression (upright posture, expanded chest, head tilted back — Tracy and Robins have documented the universal pride expression cross-culturally). Pride is felt as an inner warmth or expansion associated with positive self-evaluation. Its somatic expression is the opposite of shame's shrinking and concealment. The height vocabulary in Section 3 (go.vah, qo.mah, rom) reflects this physical-metaphorical dimension — pride lifts the self up.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* The expression and legitimacy of pride vary significantly across cultures. Collectivist cultures suppress individual pride display; individualist cultures normalise and reward it. The biblical context draws on honour-based social norms where some forms of pride display are expected while others are condemned as transgressive. *Parenting:* Parenting that attributes positive outcomes to the child's stable character (rather than specific effort) is associated with hubristic rather than authentic pride development. *Circumstances:* Achievement contexts that provide frequent positive feedback inflate hubristic pride; contexts that provide honest evaluation of specific performance support authentic pride. *Hereditary:* Narcissism (the trait form of hubristic pride) has moderate heritability.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Pride research has developed substantially since Tracy and Robins' two-facet model.

### Section 2 — Arrogance, haughtiness, and narcissism

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — narcissism research

**Glosses covered:**
arrogance (fusiōsis), arrogance (za.don), arrogant (huperēfanos), arrogant (zed), be arrogant (hupsēlofroneō), be conceited (tufoō), be haughty (huperfroneō), be proud (zud), haughtiness (gav.hut), self-willed (authadēs), selfish (filautos), intemperate (akratēs), bold man (tolmētēs), braggart (alazōn), boasting (alazoneia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Narcissism research has expanded substantially since the publication of the Narcissistic Personality Inventory (Raskin and Hall, 1979). Twenge and Campbell (*The Narcissism Epidemic*, 2009) document rising narcissism rates in Western cultures. The distinction between grandiose narcissism (overt, exhibitionistic, domineering) and vulnerable narcissism (covert, hypersensitive to criticism, shame-prone) is important — the cluster's vocabulary appears predominantly grandiose. Haughtiness and arrogance involve the cognitive component of narcissism (the belief in one's superiority) without necessarily the full clinical syndrome. Self-will (authadēs — self-pleasing, refusing to be constrained by others) adds a volitional dimension beyond mere self-evaluation.

**Emergence — what causes arrogance and haughtiness to arise:**
Arrogance arises from a combination of elevated self-evaluation and diminished regard for others — a hierarchical self-placement that ranks the self above others in worth or capability. Psychodynamically it can arise as a defensive compensation for underlying shame (vulnerable narcissism using grandiosity to avoid shame exposure). Developmentally, it is associated with parenting that gives excessive unconditional praise without appropriate reality-testing. Culturally it can be reinforced in contexts that elevate certain groups and give them permission to look down on others.

**Reduction and absence — what causes arrogance to diminish:**
Grandiose arrogance is among the most treatment-resistant personality presentations — it resists the vulnerability required for psychological change precisely because vulnerability is what it defends against. It is reduced by: genuine encounters with human limitation (illness, failure, loss); relationships in which the person is genuinely known and valued without their performance; and spiritual or philosophical frameworks that relativise human greatness. Absence of any pride and self-regard is not healthy — the positive pole is appropriate self-knowledge and dignity, not self-negation.

**Location — where arrogance is seated:**
Primarily cognitive (the self-evaluation schema) and relational (the comparative ranking of self above others). It has motivational consequences (dominance-seeking, resistance to authority) and somatic expression (the upright, expanded, sometimes contemptuous posture of haughtiness).

**External influences:**
*Parenting:* Inconsistent parenting that alternates excessive praise with withdrawal produces narcissistic personality organisation. Parenting that communicates the child's unconditional importance without performance grounding also produces grandiosity. *Cultural:* Societies that reward self-promotion, individual achievement, and status display foster arrogance more than those that value humility and collective orientation. *Circumstances:* Power and status without accountability reliably produce arrogance — the psychological effects of power (Keltner's power paradox) include reduced empathy and increased self-focus.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich. Narcissism is one of the most researched personality constructs of the last four decades.

### Section 3 — Status, dominance, and social hierarchy

**Primary field:** Evolutionary psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
height (go.vah), height (ma.rom), height (qo.mah), height (rum), high (hupsēlos), high (ga.vo.ah), majesty (ge.ut), domineering (shal.lit), imperious (shal.le.tet), be first (archō), to exalt (rum), to rise up (ga.ah), to inflate (fusioō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Evolutionary psychology treats status-seeking as a fundamental human motivation — social hierarchies are universal, and position within them has direct consequences for reproductive success, resource access, and physical safety (Barkow). The prestige-dominance distinction (Henrich and Gil-White) separates status based on freely conferred respect (prestige) from status imposed through intimidation and force (dominance). The cluster's imperious and domineering vocabulary maps onto the dominance pole. The physical height metaphors (high, exalted, towering) reflect a cross-cultural association between physical height and status — documented in perception research.

**Emergence — what causes dominance-seeking to arise:**
Dominance-seeking arises from the motivation to secure status, resources, and influence within social hierarchies. It is amplified by: competitive contexts where resources or recognition are scarce; personality traits (high dominance, low agreeableness); and experiences of threat to existing status. Keltner's power paradox research shows that the acquisition of power reliably reduces empathy and increases self-focus — power changes the psychology of those who hold it.

**Reduction and absence — what causes dominance to diminish:**
Dominance decreases when power is removed or shared, when accountability structures impose limits on the exercise of power, and when communities develop norms that sanction domineering behaviour. The biblical material's consistent critique of domineering leadership and its model of servant authority represents a counter-cultural resistance to the naturalisation of dominance as the legitimate form of power.

**Location — where the status drive is seated:**
Motivational and social — the drive for status is primarily relational (it requires others to rank against) and behavioural (it produces actions oriented toward securing and displaying superior position). The height metaphors are somatic in their origin — the link between physical elevation and psychological status is embodied.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Dominance as a personality trait has moderate heritability. Testosterone levels influence competitive dominance behaviour. *Cultural:* Hierarchical cultures normalise and valorise dominance; egalitarian cultures restrain it through social norms. *Circumstances:* Competitive resource environments and social contexts that reward dominance over cooperation select for domineering behaviour.

**Coverage assessment:** Good coverage from evolutionary psychology and social psychology of power and status.

### Section 4 — Self-promotion and boasting as social behaviour

**Primary field:** Social psychology — impression management

**Glosses covered:**
to boast: rave madly (ha.lal), self-chosen (authairetos), voluptuous (a.din)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Impression management (Goffman, *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life*, 1959) analyses how individuals actively construct and manage their public presentation. Self-promotion is one of five impression management tactics (Jones and Pittman) — the strategy of highlighting one's abilities and achievements to be seen as competent. Self-enhancement bias — the tendency to view oneself more positively than objective evidence warrants — is one of the most robust findings in social psychology. Boasting as social behaviour has received attention in relation to social media behaviour and narcissism amplification in digital contexts.

**Emergence — what causes self-promotion to arise:**
Self-promotion arises from the motivation to be perceived positively by others — to claim and secure a social identity that is valued in the community. It is amplified in competitive contexts where one's status relative to others is consequential, and in cultures that reward individual achievement and display.

**Reduction and absence — what causes self-promotion to diminish:**
Self-promotion is reduced by: cultural norms that sanction boasting (modesty norms); secure social identity that does not depend on others' approval; and genuine humility that no longer needs external validation. It is absent in those with low social motivation (avoidant personalities) and — paradoxically — in those with sufficient social security to present themselves without defence.

**Location:** Social and motivational — located in the relationship between the self and its audience, rather than in an internal emotional state.

**External influences:** Cultural norms around modesty and boasting are the primary shapers. Social media environments that reward self-display have been shown to increase self-promotion as a practised behaviour.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Impression management and self-promotion are well-understood phenomena; the specific gloss count here is small.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology and positive psychology (pride — Section 1); clinical psychology/narcissism research (Section 2); evolutionary psychology and social psychology (Sections 3–4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Tracy and Robins' two-facet model illuminates the distinction the cluster may carry between legitimate boasting (kauchaomai in God's works) and self-glorifying pride (ge.ah, ga.on). The power paradox research (Keltner) illuminates the systematic psychological effects of pride-elevation — it is not merely a feeling but a distorting cognitive orientation that reduces empathy and increases self-focus. Narcissism research illuminates the characterological depth of the cluster's arrogance vocabulary — it is not a mood state but a stable self-orientation.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science distinguishes healthy pride (authentic, achievement-based) from pathological pride (hubristic, narcissistic) and treats only the latter as problematic. The biblical material's critique of pride is more thoroughgoing — it is addressed to the creatureliness of the creature before God, not merely to the social dysfunctionality of narcissism. Even legitimate achievement pride becomes disordered when it forgets the giver of all good gifts. This is a genuine register divergence, not merely a matter of degree.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The shame-as-underlying-vulnerability in narcissism (vulnerable narcissism) may surface a connection between M07 and M08 that the biblical pass should test — whether the cluster's arrogance vocabulary carries any signal of the shame it defends against.
