## Science sections

### Section 1 — Honesty, deception-resistance, and truthfulness

**Primary field:** Social psychology / moral psychology

**Glosses covered:**
true (alēthēs), true (alēthinos), truth (alētheia), truth: certain (e.met), truth: faithful (e.met), truth: true (e.met), be truthful (alētheuō), to deal (sha.qar — negative: to deal falsely), sound (haplous), sound (she.ma), straightforward (na.kho.chah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Levine's truth-default theory (*Duped*, 2019) proposes that humans have an evolved default toward believing others — deception is the exception, not the baseline. DePaulo's naturalistic studies of everyday lying demonstrated that most people lie one to two times per day, usually for social lubricating or self-protective purposes. The moral psychology of honesty (Bok, *Lying*, 1978; Ariely, *The (Honest) Truth About Dishonesty*, 2012) examines the conditions under which people are honest and the rationalisations that accompany dishonesty. The straightforward and sound vocabulary (haplous — single-eyed, undivided; na.kho.chah — straight, direct) names an inner disposition of truthfulness rather than a behavioural output.

**Emergence — what causes honesty and truthfulness to arise:**
Honesty arises from: a strong moral identity in which honesty is central (Aquino and Reed); a community of accountability that makes dishonesty costly; the internal motivation to maintain coherence between inner conviction and outward expression (authenticity — Kernis); and — in the biblical context — the relationship with a God who is true, which grounds the human calling to truthfulness in the character of God rather than in social utility. Truth-default theory suggests that the baseline is toward honesty, and that deception requires active effort to maintain.

**Reduction and absence — what causes honesty to diminish:**
Honesty is reduced by: situations where dishonesty is perceived as low-risk and high-benefit; the suppression of moral self-monitoring through self-justification; social contexts that normalise certain forms of deception (white lies, impression management); and the progressive erosion of the truthfulness disposition through habitual small deceits. Ariely's research shows that most dishonesty occurs in small, self-justifiable amounts — the slippery slope of incremental dishonesty.

**Location — where truthfulness is seated:**
Primarily characterological and cognitive — truthfulness is a stable disposition of character (the commitment to honest expression) with cognitive and volitional dimensions (recognising truth, choosing to express it). The soundness vocabulary (haplous — undivided, whole) suggests an integration of the inner and the outer that is a whole-person quality rather than a behavioural practice in isolation.

**External influences:**
*Community:* Truthful communities produce truthful individuals — the social environment that models, expects, and rewards honesty is the primary external shaper. *Parenting:* Parental modelling of honesty and honest feedback to children cultivates honesty as an internalised value. *Cultural:* Cultures and professions differ in their honesty norms. *Developmental:* The capacity for deception develops in early childhood; the capacity for principled commitment to honesty develops through moral development in middle childhood and adolescence.

**Coverage assessment:** Good coverage. Honesty and deception research are well-developed fields.

### Section 2 — Faithfulness, reliability, and trust

**Primary field:** Social psychology / organisational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
faithful (pistos), faithful (e.mun), faithfulness (e.mu.nah), faithfulness (o.men), be faithful (a.man), sure (a.ma.nah), to confirm (bebaioō), confirmation (bebaiōsis), irrevocable (ametameletos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Trust research (Rotter — interpersonal trust scale, 1967; Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman — integrative model of organisational trust, 1995) distinguishes dispositional trust (general inclination to trust others) from situation-specific trust (trust in a particular person or institution). The three components of trustworthiness (Mayer and colleagues): ability, benevolence, and integrity — reliability requires all three. Commitment psychology (Rusbult's investment model) examines the conditions under which people maintain faithfulness even when alternatives are attractive. Irrevocability (ametameletos) captures the unconditional quality of genuine covenant commitment.

**Emergence — what causes faithfulness to arise:**
Faithfulness arises from: internalised commitment (the decision to be reliable regardless of circumstance); strong relationship investment that makes faithfulness the path of least cost; community accountability that monitors and reinforces commitment; and — in the theological context — the experience of God's own faithfulness as both model and motivating ground. Faithfulness to specific commitments (promises, covenants, relationships) develops through repeated experience of both making and receiving reliable commitments.

**Reduction and absence — what causes faithfulness to diminish:**
Faithfulness is undermined by: alternative attractions that make the cost of faithfulness high; the weakening of commitment (through gradual emotional disengagement or through specific betrayal); and the absence of accountability structures that maintain the commitment. The irrevocability vocabulary (ametameletos — without change of mind) names the character of commitment that cannot be reversed — in the theological context this is a divine attribute; in human character it describes the person whose commitment is not conditional on circumstances.

**Location — where faithfulness is seated:**
Primarily volitional and characterological — faithfulness is a sustained commitment of the will. It is not primarily an emotion (it can be maintained when feeling has faded) and not merely a behaviour (it requires inner commitment, not just compliant action). Reliability is also cognitive — the person who is faithful has accurate self-knowledge of their commitments and tracks them over time.

**External influences:**
*Relationship history:* Experiences of faithfulness received cultivate the capacity to be faithful. Betrayal in early significant relationships undermines the template for faithfulness. *Cultural:* Cultures with strong honour norms around promise-keeping have different faithfulness profiles than low-commitment cultures. *Circumstances:* Periods of high temptation, high cost, or weakened self-regulatory capacity test faithfulness most severely.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Trust and commitment research provide solid frameworks.

### Section 3 — Integrity, uprightness, and moral consistency

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / virtue psychology

**Glosses covered:**
integrity (tom), integrity (tum.mah), upright: right (ya.shar), upright: straight (ya.shar), sincerity (eilikrineia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Integrity psychology (Palanski and Yammarino, *What Does the Concept of Integrity Hold Together?*, 2009) identifies integrity as consistency between words and actions, and between espoused and enacted values. The VIA character strengths framework (Peterson and Seligman) includes honesty/authenticity as a core character strength that encompasses integrity. Uprightness (ya.shar — straight, right, without deviation) describes the person whose path and character are not crooked — a consistency metaphor that is central to the Hebrew wisdom literature's moral vision.

**Emergence — what causes moral integrity to develop:**
Integrity develops through: the habituation of honest, consistent conduct over time (Aristotelian character formation); strong moral identity (Aquino and Reed); community accountability; and the deliberate practice of alignment between inner conviction and outward action. Sincerity (eilikrineia — clearness as of sunlight, without wax filling cracks) names a purity of motivation — acting from genuine concern rather than mixed or self-serving motives.

**Reduction and absence — what causes integrity to diminish:**
Integrity is eroded by: incremental compromises; the rationalisation of inconsistency; impression management that substitutes performance for genuine character; and social environments that reward results regardless of the means. The absence of integrity (hypocrisy — M14) is the specific counterfeit that the cluster's integrity vocabulary implicitly defines itself against.

**Location — where integrity is seated:**
Characterological — a whole-person disposition toward consistency between inner values and outward conduct. It is expressed in behaviour but is grounded in character — it cannot be maintained as a performance without an inner correspondent.

**External influences:** Moral community membership and parental modelling are the primary external shapers. Sustained accountability relationships that notice and name inconsistency are among the strongest supports for integrity.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Integrity and character research are well-developed in positive psychology and virtue ethics research.

## Outside-science inventory

- **faithfulness (e.mu.nah) in its covenantal/theological register** — where e.mu.nah names God's own faithfulness to covenant (the rock-solid reliability of God who cannot lie and whose promises are certain), it is a theological category. Human e.mu.nah as a covenantal response mirrors the divine faithfulness — a relational structure that science addresses only as a human trust phenomenon without its theological grounding.

**Observation:** M13 has minimal outside-science content. Honesty, faithfulness, reliability, and integrity are human phenomena with good science coverage. The theological distinctive is the grounding of truthfulness and faithfulness in the character of God — truth as the character of the one who is Truth, faithfulness as the human echo of the covenant-keeping God.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology and moral psychology (honesty — Section 1); social and organisational psychology (faithfulness — Section 2); positive and virtue psychology (integrity — Section 3).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Truth-default theory (Levine) illuminates why the biblical material can treat truthfulness as the baseline from which deception deviates — humans are naturally oriented toward trust and truth. The three-component trust model (ability, benevolence, integrity) illuminates the cluster's faithfulness vocabulary — what it means to be genuinely trustworthy rather than merely compliant. Integrity psychology illuminates the consistency structure of the cluster's uprightness vocabulary.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats honesty and faithfulness as social phenomena — valuable because they enable cooperation and social functioning. The biblical material treats truth as grounded in the character of God — not merely instrumentally valuable but ontologically grounded. Truth is not a social convention but a reality; God's faithfulness is not merely reliable behaviour but the expression of who he is.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The incremental dishonesty research (Ariely) may surface a dimension of the cluster's honesty vocabulary that the biblical pass should test — does the cluster name the small, self-justifiable departures from truth, or only gross falsehood? The irrevocability vocabulary (ametameletos) may have no direct science analogue and may need to be addressed primarily from the theological side.
