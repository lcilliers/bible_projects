## Science sections

### Section 1 — Shame as emotional and psychological state

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — shame research

**Glosses covered:**
shame (aischunē), shame (entropē), shame (bu.shah), shame (bosh.nah), shame (bo.shet), shame (ke.lim.mah), shame (ke.lim.mut), be ashamed (aischunō), be ashamed (bosh), be ashamed (cha.pher), shameful (aischros), to cause shame (entrepō), to shame (cha.sad)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Shame psychology is a major clinical field. Lewis (*Shame and Guilt in Neurosis*, 1971) made the foundational distinction between shame (the whole self is bad) and guilt (the behaviour was bad). Tangney and Dearing (*Shame and Guilt*, 2002) developed the most widely used measurement instruments and demonstrated that shame is associated with negative outcomes (depression, anger, externalisation of blame) while guilt is associated with more adaptive responses (remorse, reparative action). Brown's research (*Daring Greatly*, 2012) has brought shame into popular consciousness as a barrier to vulnerability and authentic connection. Shame is increasingly recognised as a transdiagnostic factor in psychopathology — it underlies or amplifies depression, anxiety, eating disorders, addiction, and social phobia.

**Emergence — what causes shame to arise:**
Shame arises when the self — not merely a behaviour — is negatively evaluated, either by the self or in relation to perceived others' evaluation. The key conditions are: the person's self-concept is implicated in the failure or exposure; the failure is visible or imagined to be visible to others who matter; and the person lacks the internal resources to separate their worth from the specific failure. Shame is triggered by: public exposure of perceived inadequacy; failure to meet internalised standards; and — in honour-shame cultures specifically — any act that reduces the person's standing in the community's eyes. The cluster's vocabulary for being ashamed includes both the inner state and the outer exposure — shame is both felt and seen.

**Reduction and absence — what causes shame to diminish:**
Shame reduces through: empathy and acceptance from others (being seen in the shame and not rejected — Brown's vulnerability research); the separation of the self from the specific failing (guilt-like repair rather than shame-based global self-condemnation); and the development of self-compassion (Neff). Shame is particularly resistant to resolution through reassurance alone — surface reassurance without genuine empathic connection tends to drive shame deeper. Absence of appropriate shame (shamelessness) is associated with narcissistic and antisocial personality structures and is clinically significant as a deficit of self-monitoring.

**Location — where shame is seated:**
Shame is primarily experienced as a self-directed emotional state — it is felt in the self as a collapse or exposure of the self. It is highly social in its constitution (requiring real or imagined others whose gaze is felt) but experienced as profoundly internal (the feeling of being small, exposed, wanting to hide or disappear). Somatically, shame produces characteristic postures: gaze aversion, physical shrinking, hunched shoulders, blushing, concealment gestures. It is less acute and arousing than fear but more penetrating — it reorganises the self's sense of its own worth.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* The shame/guilt distinction maps onto honour-shame versus guilt-innocence cultural frameworks (Piers and Singer; Triandis). Collectivist cultures and honour cultures have more extensively shame-based moral regulation; individualist cultures are more guilt-based. The biblical material spans both frameworks. *Parenting:* Shame-based parenting (attacking the child's character rather than addressing specific behaviour) produces chronic shame proneness. Warm, authoritative parenting that separates the child's worth from their behaviour produces a guilt-based conscience rather than a shame-based one. *Developmental:* Shame capacity emerges in the second year of life alongside self-consciousness — it requires a sense of self before it can involve self-evaluation. *Hereditary:* Shame proneness has some heritable components via temperamental sensitivity and nervous system reactivity.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich coverage. Shame is one of the most clinically significant and well-researched emotions in contemporary psychology.

### Section 2 — Disgrace, dishonour, and social degradation

**Primary field:** Social psychology — stigma and status research

**Glosses covered:**
disgrace (qi.qa.lon), dishonor (qa.lon), dishonour (atimia), be humiliated (ka.lam), to dishonor (atimazō), to dishonor (kataischunō), to dishonor (qa.lah), to abase (sha.phel), to disgrace (paradeigmatizō), to reject (exoutheneō), reviling (loidoria), to curse/revile (kakologeō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Stigma research (Goffman, *Stigma*, 1963) analyses how socially discrediting attributes are managed and their consequences for identity and interaction. Status degradation research (Garfinkel's degradation ceremonies) examines the social rituals by which a person is formally reduced in standing within a community — a process that the cluster's public dishonour and disgrace vocabulary reflects. Social rejection and ostracism research (Williams, Leary) documents the severe psychological consequences of being cast out or marked as dishonoured. Cross-cultural honour-shame research (Nisbett, Cohen) identifies societies where public status is the primary moral currency — dishonour as social death.

**Emergence — what causes disgrace and dishonour to arise:**
Social disgrace arises from a perceived violation of community norms that is publicly known and publicly judged. Unlike private shame (felt by the self), social disgrace is conferred by the community — it is a verdict delivered by others. The key elements are: a failure or violation that the community considers status-degrading; public knowledge of the failure; and community response that demotes the person's standing. To disgrace (paradeigmatizō — to make an example of) and to abase (sha.phel) describe the active infliction of social degradation by an agent with power over the other.

**Reduction and absence — what causes disgrace to be lifted:**
Social disgrace is reduced by: public vindication (the community revises its judgment); rehabilitation (the disgraced person demonstrates worthiness through subsequent conduct); and reconciliation (the community accepts the person back into standing). In honour-shame cultures, the restoration of honour requires a public act of rehabilitation — private redemption is insufficient when the shame was publicly inflicted. The absence of disgrace-sensitivity is associated with psychopathy (no concern for social standing) and with secure identity that does not depend on community approval.

**Location — where disgrace is seated:**
Primarily relational and social — located in the community's judgment and its expression, not primarily in the individual's inner state (though it produces profound inner consequences). Disgrace is enacted publicly and is visible; its effects are felt internally. The cluster's vocabulary includes both the social act (to disgrace, to dishonour) and the resulting state (dishonour, disgrace), reflecting this dual location.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* The power of disgrace is maximally determined by cultural context. In honour-shame cultures (most of the ancient world, including the biblical material's contexts), disgrace is a catastrophic social sanction — more feared than physical punishment in some contexts. In guilt-innocence cultures, inner conviction matters more than social standing. *Circumstances:* Public exposure is necessary for disgrace as distinct from private shame. Social contexts in which reputation is the primary social currency make disgrace more powerful.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Stigma, honour-shame, and status degradation research provide solid frameworks.

### Section 3 — Innocence, purity, and the absence of shame

**Primary field:** Moral psychology

**Glosses covered:**
innocence (niq.qa.von), filth (ni.dah), indecency (aschēmosunē), bluntness (qe.ha.von), defect (te.val.lul)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
This section names the contrast states to shame — what shame attaches to (defect, filth, indecency) and what shame's absence looks like (innocence). Moral purity research (Haidt) addresses the purity dimension: disgust-sensitivity shapes what is experienced as contaminating or defiling, which in turn shapes what triggers shame. Innocence (niq.qa.von) represents the state of being without the marks that trigger shame — a condition of moral and social integrity.

**Emergence — what causes the perception of defect or indecency to arise:**
The perception of defect or indecency is shaped by the moral framework of the community — what counts as a blemish, an impurity, or an improper exposure is culturally determined, though core disgust categories (bodily violation, sexual exposure) are cross-culturally consistent. The cluster's filth and indecency vocabulary reflects both the biological disgust substrate and its moral-cultural elaboration.

**Reduction and absence:** Innocence — freedom from the conditions that trigger shame — is maintained through moral integrity and compliance with purity norms. Its loss marks the transition into shame-exposed territory.

**Location:** Moral and social — these are evaluative categories that describe states of the person in relation to community standards, not primary emotional states in themselves.

**External influences:** Cultural determination of purity norms is primary; universal disgust sensitivity provides the biological substrate.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. A smaller and more heterogeneous section. Moral psychology provides the primary framework.

### Section 4 — Social silencing and exclusion

**Primary field:** Social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
mute (afōnos), to muzzle (fimoō), to throw away (apoballō), mutilation (katatomē), mutilation (mish.chat)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Social exclusion research (Williams, *Ostracism*; Leary, *The Curse of the Self*) documents that being silenced, excluded, and treated as non-existent activates the same neural pain circuits as physical harm. Silencing as a social control mechanism has been studied in organisational psychology (voice suppression — Detert, Edmondson) and in political psychology. Mutilation — physical marking of the excluded person — extends the social exclusion into the body, making the person's outsider status visible and permanent.

**Emergence — what causes social silencing and exclusion to arise:**
Silencing and exclusion arise as social control mechanisms deployed by those with power against those who threaten, embarrass, or violate the group's norms. They can also be instruments of punishment for disgrace — the disgraced person is treated as non-existent (ostracism) or physically marked (mutilation). The extremity of the mutilation vocabulary in this section suggests punishments that extend shame into permanent bodily inscription.

**Reduction and absence:** Social silence ends when the silenced person is granted voice by those with power to restore it, or when power dynamics shift. Ostracism ends through readmission to the community.

**Location:** Relational and social in origin; experienced as profound internal pain (the psychological weight of being treated as non-existent).

**External influences:** Power differentials and social hierarchies determine who can silence whom; cultural norms determine what warrants silencing.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Social exclusion research is well-developed; the mutilation vocabulary has thinner direct science engagement.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Clinical psychology (shame research) for Section 1; social psychology (stigma and status degradation) for Section 2; moral psychology for Sections 3–4.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The shame/guilt distinction (Lewis, Tangney) illuminates potential differences within the cluster's vocabulary — terms that name the whole-self condemnation of shame versus terms that name the behaviour-focused response of guilt. The honour-shame cultural psychology research is directly applicable to the biblical material's social world. Brown's vulnerability research illuminates why the cluster's shame vocabulary is so extensive — shame is one of the most powerful inhibitors of relationship and authentic community.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats shame primarily as a pathological or maladaptive state to be reduced. The biblical material includes categories of appropriate shame (shame at genuine sin and failure) and the loss of appropriate shame (shamelessness as a spiritual condition). The distinction between healthy shame-as-signal and destructive shame-as-identity is present in both frameworks but framed differently: science focuses on the psychological damage; the biblical material focuses on the moral and relational reality to which shame may appropriately point.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The transdiagnostic function of shame (as an amplifier of depression, anxiety, addiction, and other conditions) may surface connections within the programme's other clusters that the biblical pass should test. The somatic dimension of shame (physical postures — hiding, shrinking, averting the gaze) may be present in the cluster's vocabulary and worth identifying specifically.
