## Science sections

### Section 1 — Justice perception and moral reasoning

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / social psychology — justice research

**Glosses covered:**
just (dikaios), just (endikos), justice (dikaiokrisia), righteousness (dikaiosunē), righteousness (tse.deq), righteousness (tse.da.qah), righteousness (euthutēs), righteous (tsad.diq), righteous act (dikaiōma), to justify (dikaioō), to justify (tsa.deq), uprightness (yo.sher), uprightness (ye.sha.rah), uprightness (me.shar), upright (ne.kho.chah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Kohlberg's stages of moral development (*The Philosophy of Moral Development*, 1981) proposed that moral reasoning develops through pre-conventional, conventional, and post-conventional stages. Gilligan's critique (*In a Different Voice*, 1982) identified a care-based moral orientation alongside Kohlberg's justice-based orientation. Haidt's moral foundations theory (*The Righteous Mind*, 2012) proposes six moral foundations — care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation, and liberty/oppression — with justice and fairness as foundational. Tyler's procedural justice research demonstrates that people care as much about whether processes are fair as whether outcomes are fair. Lerner's just-world theory addresses the psychological need to believe that the world is fundamentally just.

**Emergence — what causes the justice sense to arise:**
The human sense of justice arises early and robustly — infant research (Hamlin, Wynn, Bloom) shows that infants as young as six months prefer helpers over hinderers and show proto-moral responses to fairness. Cross-cultural research confirms that fairness intuitions are universal, though their expression varies. Justice perception is triggered by the observation of rule violation, harm to others, or unequal distribution — the perception of moral imbalance activates both emotional (indignation) and cognitive (reasoning about what should happen) responses.

**Reduction and absence — what causes the justice sense to diminish:**
The justice sense is suppressed by moral disengagement (Bandura — mechanisms by which people disconnect moral standards from conduct: moral justification, euphemistic labelling, displacement of responsibility). It is also diminished by in-group/out-group dynamics — justice intuitions apply more readily to in-group members than to out-group members. Cynicism about the possibility of justice (learned injustice) can suppress the justice sense by making it feel futile.

**Location — where the justice sense is seated:**
Moral psychology identifies justice perception as both emotional and cognitive. The emotional dimension (indignation, satisfaction) arises rapidly and automatically (System 1 — Kahneman); the cognitive dimension (reasoning about rights, duties, proportionality) is slower and more deliberate (System 2). Haidt's social intuitionist model proposes that moral emotions precede moral reasoning — we feel the injustice first and reason about it second. The justice sense is therefore primarily affective-evaluative, with cognitive elaboration following.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Moral development research (Kohlberg, Piaget) traces the increasing sophistication of justice reasoning from childhood to adulthood. Family and community modelling of fair treatment is among the strongest developmental influences. *Cultural:* Cultures vary in which moral foundations are most salient — some emphasise harm and fairness (Western liberal cultures), others emphasise loyalty, authority, and purity more heavily (collectivist cultures). *Circumstances:* Experience of injustice — being treated unfairly, witnessing oppression — powerfully activates the justice sense. The experience of justice received produces satisfaction and gratitude.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Moral psychology and justice research are well-developed fields with direct relevance to this vocabulary.

### Section 2 — Punishment, retribution, and accountability

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / legal psychology

**Glosses covered:**
avenging (ekdikos), to avenge (ekdikeō), vengeance (ekdikēsis), condemnation (dikē), condemnation (katakrisis), to condemn (katadikazō), to condemn (katakrinō), to judge (she.phat), judgment (she.phot), accountable (hupodikos), cause/charge (aitia), causer (aitios)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Carlsmith and Darley's research on punishment motives (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 2002) distinguishes retributive punishment (the offender deserves to suffer) from consequentialist punishment (punishment deters and rehabilitates). Retributive intuitions are powerful and appear cross-culturally. Fehr and Gächter's altruistic punishment research (*Nature*, 2002) demonstrates that people will pay a personal cost to punish unfair actors — even strangers, even when there is no future benefit — establishing punishment as a genuine prosocial mechanism, not merely self-interested. The accountability vocabulary maps onto legal psychology's study of how people reason about responsibility and blame attribution.

**Emergence — what causes the desire for punishment and accountability to arise:**
The punishment impulse arises when a moral violation is identified and attributed to a blameworthy agent. It is strengthened when the violation is severe, when the agent acted with intent, and when the agent has not been held accountable. The retributive impulse specifically — that wrongdoing creates a moral debt that must be paid — appears to be a universal moral intuition rather than a culturally specific one.

**Reduction and absence:**
The punishment impulse is reduced when the offender is punished (proportionally and visibly), when the offender genuinely repents and makes restitution, or when the victim forgives. Mercy — the suspension of deserved punishment — requires a conscious override of the retributive intuition and is psychologically costly. This structure (retribution as default; mercy as deliberate override) maps onto the programme's theology of judgment and grace with notable precision.

**Location — where retributive justice is seated:**
Both emotional (the felt satisfaction of seeing wrongdoing punished) and cognitive (reasoning about proportionality, intent, and desert). The emotional dimension — what Haidt calls moral elevation's counterpart, moral disgust and indignation — drives the initial punishment impulse; the cognitive dimension moderates its expression.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Retributive versus restorative justice traditions vary by culture and legal system. *Developmental:* Children show retributive punishment intuitions very early, suggesting a developmental-universal basis. *Circumstances:* Proximity to the victim and severity of the violation strengthen punishment intuitions.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Legal and moral psychology provide strong coverage.

### Section 3 — Moral evaluation and attribution

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / social cognition

**Glosses covered:**
to count (logizomai), to deem worthy (axioō), to value (a.rakh), to act unjustly (a.val), be wicked (ra.sha), impartial (adiakritos), to repay (antapodidōmi)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Social cognition research addresses how people form judgements about others' character and actions. Attribution theory (Heider, Weiner) addresses how people assign responsibility for outcomes — the fundamental attribution error (Ross) identifies the pervasive tendency to over-attribute behaviour to character and under-attribute to situation. Logizomai (to count, to reckon, to credit) is used in Paul's theology of imputed righteousness — the accounting metaphor maps onto social cognition research on moral bookkeeping.

**Emergence — what causes moral evaluation to arise:**
Moral evaluation is automatic and immediate — people form impressions of others' character from minimal information and are highly resistant to revising them. The evaluation of acts (rather than persons) requires more deliberate processing. The impartial (adiakritos) ideal — judging without partiality — is a deliberate override of the in-group/out-group bias that naturally shapes moral evaluation.

**Location:**
Primarily cognitive and social. Moral evaluation is a property of the perceiver's judgment, not of the object judged — though the content of the evaluation refers to the object. It is relational in that it constitutes a social positioning of the evaluated relative to the evaluator.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Social cognition provides coverage; the theological use of logizomai in Romans exceeds social cognition's scope.

### Section 4 — Righteousness as moral character

**Primary field:** Virtue psychology / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
Righteousness [God] (tse.deq), righteousness (tse.deq — as character attribute)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Peterson and Seligman's VIA (Values in Action) classification of character strengths (*Character Strengths and Virtues*, 2004) identifies justice as one of six virtue categories (alongside wisdom, courage, humanity, temperance, and transcendence). Aristotle's virtue ethics — mediated through contemporary virtue psychology — treats justice as a stable character disposition rather than a rule-following behaviour. The psychological research on just behaviour (as distinct from justice perception) addresses character traits that reliably produce fair treatment of others.

**Emergence — what causes the character virtue of justice to arise:**
Virtue psychology follows Aristotle's account: virtues are habits formed through repeated practice and shaped by formation in a community of practice. The just character is formed through repeated acts of just dealing, in a community that values and models justice. Moral identity research (Aquino, Reed) shows that people whose self-concept is organised around moral traits are more reliably morally consistent.

**Location — where virtue is seated:**
Virtue psychology locates virtues in the character — a stable dispositional tendency that shapes perception, emotion, and action across situations. Virtues are not merely cognitive beliefs but integrated character dispositions. For the programme, this is significant: righteousness as a character virtue is an inner-being phenomenon — seated in the person's habitual orientation — not merely a description of their actions.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Virtue psychology is relevant; the theological dimension of righteousness as God's own character attribute is outside science.

## Outside-science inventory

- **Justification (dikaiōsis) in its Pauline theological register** — imputed righteousness, forensic divine declaration — is a theological category without science parallel. Science addresses moral evaluation; the theological claim is that God declares the guilty righteous on the basis of Christ's righteousness.
- **Righteousness as eschatological status** — the righteous at the last judgment — is a theological category.
- **God's righteousness (tse.deq) as his own character attribute** — divine moral integrity and covenant faithfulness — exceeds what virtue psychology addresses for human character.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Moral psychology (justice research, Kohlberg, Haidt) for Sections 1 and 2; social cognition for Section 3; virtue psychology for Section 4.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The dual-process account of moral judgment (automatic emotional intuition followed by slower reasoning) illuminates why righteousness/justice is simultaneously a felt response and a reasoned judgment in the biblical material. Retributive punishment intuitions illuminate the cluster's judgment and condemnation vocabulary — these are not merely culturally contingent but represent deep moral intuitions. Virtue psychology's characterisation of justice as a stable character disposition illuminates why the cluster's righteous vocabulary describes persons (the righteous) as well as acts.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The theological category of imputed righteousness — being declared righteous without being righteous in character — runs directly counter to virtue psychology's account, which locates righteousness in stable character. The forensic/relational distinction in Paul's theology has no science parallel. This is among the most significant science-theology divergences in the programme.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The just-world theory may surface as relevant to how the cluster's vocabulary functions in lament contexts — where the just world belief is challenged by observed injustice and cried out to God.
