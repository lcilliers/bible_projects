## Science sections

### Section 1 — Anger and rage as emotional states

**Primary field:** Clinical and social psychology — emotion theory

**Glosses covered:**
anger (parorgismos), be angry (a.naph), be angry (qa.tsar), be displeased (be.esh), rage (za.aph), rage (che.ma), rage (che.mah), vexation (ka.a.s), vexed (za.eph), to anger (orgizō), to anger (parorgizō), to be incensed (cha.rah), to boil (zid), to enrage (za.aph), to enrage (re.gaz), to fall: angry (na.phal), quick-tempered (orgilos), hot (zestos), burning anger (cha.ron), scorching (zal.a.phah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Anger is among the most studied of the basic emotions. Averill's social constructionist account (*Anger and Aggression*, 1982) treats anger as a socially scripted syndrome — a culturally shaped response to perceived wrongdoing or frustration. Berkowitz's cognitive-neoassociationistic model emphasises aversive stimulation as the primary trigger. Tavris (*Anger: The Misunderstood Emotion*, 1982) challenged the catharsis hypothesis, demonstrating that expressing anger typically amplifies rather than reduces it. The trait-state distinction (Spielberger) separates dispositional anger-proneness from situational anger episodes. The somatic vocabulary of this cluster — boiling, burning, scorching — maps onto the heat metaphor that anger research has documented cross-culturally (Lakoff and Kövecses).

**Emergence — what causes anger to arise:**
Anger emerges from two primary sources: frustration (the blocking of a goal or expected outcome — Dollard's frustration-aggression hypothesis, 1939, updated by Berkowitz) and perceived injustice or wrongdoing directed at the self or others. Appraisal theories (Lazarus, Smith and Ellsworth) specify that anger is produced by appraising an event as negative, caused by another agent, and deserving of blame — a blame attribution is essential to anger as distinct from mere frustration. Anger also arises from physical aversive stimulation (pain, heat, crowding) even without a clear blaming target. The biblical heat/burning vocabulary suggests that the cluster captures both the appraisal dimension (blame, wrongdoing) and the arousal dimension (somatic heat) of anger.

**Reduction and absence — what causes anger to diminish:**
Anger diminishes through: resolution of the offending situation (the wrong is addressed or the frustration relieved); reappraisal (the event is reconsidered and the blame attribution modified); forgiveness (the debt is released — links to M11 Repentance); and temporal decay (anger naturally attenuates over time if not maintained by rumination). Importantly, venting anger typically increases rather than reduces it — Tavris's finding counters the popular catharsis model. Empathy for the offender reliably reduces anger. Physical cooling (cold environments, reduced arousal) attenuates anger intensity. Absence of anger in provocation contexts may indicate emotional detachment, secure attachment, or virtuous self-regulation — or suppression, which carries its own costs.

**Location — where anger is seated:**
Anger is primarily emotional and cognitive, with pronounced somatic accompaniments. Neurologically, it involves the amygdala (rapid threat/offence appraisal), the anterior cingulate cortex (conflict monitoring), and hypothalamic activation of the sympathetic nervous system. Physiologically, anger produces increased heart rate, elevated blood pressure, muscle tension, and heat — exactly the heat-metaphor vocabulary the cluster deploys. The somatic dimension is genuine, not merely figurative: anger is a hot, high-arousal state. Cognitively, it is constituted by the appraisal of wrongdoing and the attribution of blame. The cluster's thermal vocabulary (burning, scorching, boiling) sits at the intersection of the somatic and metaphorical dimensions of anger — both are real.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Trait anger and aggression have moderate heritability (approximately 40–50% from twin studies). Temperamental reactivity — how quickly and intensely the anger system activates — is partly innate. *Parenting:* Harsh, inconsistent, or abusive parenting is among the strongest predictors of elevated trait anger and aggression in adulthood. Children model parental anger regulation patterns directly. Authoritative parenting (warm but consistent boundaries) predicts better anger regulation. *Circumstances:* Poverty, chronic frustration, social injustice, and powerlessness all elevate situational and trait anger. Crowding, heat, and physical discomfort are reliable situational anger amplifiers. *Cultural:* Cultures vary significantly in anger norms — what triggers it, how it is expressed, and whether it is legitimate. Honour cultures (including the ancient Near Eastern context of much of the biblical material) have significantly lower thresholds for anger at perceived insult to status or dignity.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich and well-developed science coverage. This is one of the most studied emotional states in psychology.

### Section 2 — Indignation and moral anger

**Primary field:** Moral psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be indignant (za.am), indignation (aganaktēsis), indignation (za.am), to provoke (paroxunō), to provoke (ka.as), to provoke (ma.rar), to provoke (tsur), to provoke/irritate (erethizō), vexer (tsa.rah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Moral anger — indignation at injustice to others or violation of moral norms — is distinct from personal anger in moral psychology. Haidt's social intuitionist model identifies moral anger (indignation) as a distinct moral emotion with a different elicitor profile from self-directed anger: it is triggered by norm violation rather than personal frustration. Third-party punishment research (Fehr and Gächter) shows that humans reliably experience and act on indignation even at personal cost, suggesting it serves a social function in norm enforcement. Justice sensitivity (Schmitt) identifies stable individual differences in the tendency to respond to injustice with indignation. Aristotle's concept of righteous anger (orge) — anger proportionate to genuine wrongdoing — has been operationalised in contemporary virtue psychology.

**Emergence — what causes moral anger to arise:**
Moral anger arises from the perception that a moral norm has been violated — harm has been done, fairness has been breached, or an authority structure has been transgressed (Haidt's moral foundations). Unlike personal anger, it does not require that the self be the victim. The presence of a blameworthy agent is essential: perceived injustice without a responsible agent produces sadness or despair rather than indignation. Moral anger is amplified by identification with the victim and by the perceived severity and intentionality of the violation. Provocation terms in this section (paroxunō, ka.as, erethizō) suggest the external stimulus that triggers the moral anger response — the wrongdoing that elicits it.

**Reduction and absence — what causes moral anger to diminish:**
Moral anger diminishes when the wrongdoing is addressed — when justice is done, the norm is restored, or the wrongdoer is held accountable. Reappraisal of the severity or intentionality of the violation reduces it. Third-party forgiveness (releasing the moral debt on behalf of the victim) can resolve moral anger in the observer. Absence of moral anger in the face of genuine injustice is associated in the literature with moral disengagement (Bandura) — the mechanisms by which moral self-sanctions are deactivated — and with low justice sensitivity.

**Location — where moral anger is seated:**
Primarily moral-cognitive and emotional, with less somatic intensity than personal rage. Moral anger involves the same emotional substrate as personal anger (amygdala, autonomic arousal) but is more heavily mediated by the prefrontal cortex's moral reasoning and appraisal functions. It is experienced as righteous indignation rather than hot rage — a distinction the cluster's vocabulary partially reflects in the differentiation between burning rage (Section 1) and indignation terms (this section). The relational dimension is significant: moral anger is inherently other-directed and socially embedded.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Moral development (Kohlberg, Haidt) shapes what triggers indignation and at what stage of moral reasoning it is grounded. Children develop capacity for third-party moral anger across middle childhood. *Cultural:* Moral norms vary cross-culturally, so the specific triggers for moral indignation vary accordingly — though some (harm, fairness, care for the vulnerable) show cross-cultural consistency. *Individual differences:* Justice sensitivity is a stable individual difference trait. High empathy predicts higher moral anger at harm to others. *Circumstances:* Exposure to systemic injustice — living in unjust social structures — can either heighten moral anger (in those who perceive it) or habituate it (in those who normalise it through familiarity).

**Coverage assessment:** Good and growing. Moral psychology has given increasing attention to the moral emotions, including indignation, since the early 2000s.

### Section 3 — Jealousy and rivalry

**Primary field:** Social psychology / evolutionary psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be jealous (qa.na), jealous (qan.na), jealous (qan.no), jealousy (qin.ah), rivalry (eritheia), dispute (antilogia), dispute-loving (filoneikos), quarrel (eris), quarrel (logomachia), strife (mats.tsah), strife (mats.tsut), strife (riv), to quarrel (thumomacheō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Jealousy is motivationally distinct from anger: it arises specifically from perceived threat to a valued relationship or resource (Parrott and Smith, 1993). Evolutionary psychology treats jealousy as an adaptation to protect valuable social bonds and resources — mate-guarding research (Buss) shows cross-cultural consistency in jealousy elicitors, with sex differences in the relative salience of sexual versus emotional infidelity. Social comparison theory (Festinger) underlies rivalry: people evaluate their standing against relevant others, and perceived disadvantage in valued domains produces competitive hostility. Envy (related but distinct — coveting what another has) is treated separately from jealousy (protecting what one already has) but the Hebrew qin.ah carries both dimensions.

**Emergence — what causes jealousy and rivalry to arise:**
Jealousy arises when a valued relationship, status, or resource is perceived as threatened by a third party. The precondition is that the threatened possession is valued: jealousy does not arise over things the person is indifferent to. Rivalry arises from social comparison in competitive domains where relative standing matters. Scarcity amplifies both: when resources, status, or affection are perceived as limited, competition for them intensifies. The biblical term qin.ah carries a strong intensity of passionate attachment — jealousy here is not a mild discomfort but a fierce response to the threat of losing what is cherished.

**Reduction and absence — what causes jealousy and rivalry to diminish:**
Jealousy diminishes when the threat to the valued relationship or resource is resolved or perceived as unthreatening. Security in the relationship (secure attachment) reduces jealousy sensitivity. Rivalry diminishes when competition ceases, when relative standing is no longer salient, or when values shift away from competitive domains. Absence of jealousy can reflect either security and trust, or emotional detachment from what others have. The biblical material treats divine jealousy (qin.ah applied to God) as a positive attribute — the passionate protectiveness of covenant — which science does not adjudicate but which produces a genuine divergence from the pathologising tendency of clinical accounts of human jealousy.

**Location — where jealousy and rivalry are seated:**
Jealousy is primarily emotional and relational — it is inherently about the threat to a bond or a possession within a social context. It involves affective components (threat, possessiveness, hurt), cognitive components (monitoring for rival, appraising threat), and motivational components (protective action toward the valued object). Rivalry is more behavioural and motivational — oriented toward competitive action. Both are social emotions in that they require a relational context for their existence; they cannot arise in isolation.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Some jealousy-proneness has heritable components via attachment style and temperamental sensitivity. Evolutionary psychology treats the jealousy mechanism itself as an evolved, species-typical response. *Attachment and parenting:* Anxious attachment (rooted in inconsistent early caregiving) significantly elevates jealousy in adult relationships. Secure attachment reduces jealousy reactivity. *Cultural:* Cultures vary substantially in what triggers jealousy and how it is expressed. Cultures with strong honour norms show higher jealousy sensitivity around insults to status. *Circumstances:* Scarcity, competition for limited goods, and social hierarchies that make relative standing visible all increase rivalry. The ancient Israelite context — including land, progeny, and divine favour as scarce and competed-for goods — provides a specific circumstantial backdrop for the cluster's jealousy and rivalry vocabulary.

**Coverage assessment:** Good coverage. Jealousy has a well-developed evolutionary and social psychology literature. The overlap between qin.ah as human jealousy and as divine passionate protectiveness is the key divergence point.

## Outside-science inventory

- **wrath (orgē) in its eschatological register** — orgē as divine judgment at the end of the age is a theological category; science addresses anger as a human emotional state, not eschatological wrath.
- **wrath (qe.tsaph / qe.tseph)** — where these terms carry covenantal judgment meaning (God's wrath as the righteous response to covenant violation), they are outside science's descriptive scope. Science addresses moral anger as a human phenomenon but not divine wrath as a category of holy response to covenant breach.
- **jealousy (qin.ah) applied to God** — divine jealousy as the passionate protectiveness of the covenant-keeping God is a theological category that science addresses only at the experiential edge (the phenomenology of encountering a jealous God). The ontological and covenantal claims are outside science's scope.

**Observation:** The outside-science content for M02 is concentrated at the divine-attribute end of the cluster. Human anger, indignation, jealousy, and rivalry are richly covered by science. The theological distinctive is the application of anger and jealousy categories to God — as righteous, covenantally grounded responses — which science does not adjudicate.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Clinical and social psychology (emotion theory) for personal anger; moral psychology for indignation; evolutionary and social psychology for jealousy and rivalry.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The appraisal structure of anger (blame attribution, perceived wrongdoing) illuminates the cluster's consistent association of anger with provocation and injustice. The heat/arousal physiology illuminates the thermal vocabulary (burning, scorching, boiling). The distinction between trait anger (dispositional) and state anger (situational) illuminates differences between anger as a character description and anger as a specific episode. The evolutionary account of jealousy as protective of valued bonds illuminates why qin.ah carries positive valence when applied to God's protection of covenant.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science pathologises anger more readily than Scripture does — the clinical literature treats much anger as disordered, whereas Scripture consistently presents certain anger as righteous and appropriate. The catharsis model's failure (venting increases anger) is consistent with some biblical counsel on anger, but the biblical framework also includes righteous anger as a legitimate response that is not merely to be managed. Divine wrath as a category is the deepest divergence — science has no analogue for holy wrath as a morally appropriate, ontologically grounded response to evil.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The role of rumination in sustaining anger (Nolen-Hoeksema applied to anger) may surface gaps in how the cluster's sustained-wrath vocabulary is classified. Jealousy's attachment dimension (anxious versus secure attachment predicting jealousy sensitivity) may illuminate differences in the cluster's jealousy terms that the biblical pass should test.
