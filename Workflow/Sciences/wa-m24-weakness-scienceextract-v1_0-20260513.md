## Science sections

### Section 1 — Physical illness, pain, and bodily weakness

**Primary field:** Health psychology / medicine

**Glosses covered:**
be sick (noseō), be sick (da.vah), be sick (nush), illness (nosos), illness (de.vay), disease (mad.veh), disease (ta.cha.lu.im), sickness (cho.li), weak (asthenēs), weak: ill (asthenēs), weakness (asthenēma), weakness: ill (astheneia), weakness: weak (astheneia), be weak: ill (astheneō), be weak: weak (astheneō), half-dead (hēmithanēs), poison (rosh), severe (qa.sheh), crippled (na.kheh), smitten (ne.kheh)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Health psychology (Ogden, *Health Psychology*, 2012) addresses the psychological dimensions of illness experience — how people appraise, cope with, and adapt to physical disease. The biopsychosocial model (Engel, 1977) challenged purely biomedical accounts of illness by showing that psychological and social factors both contribute to and shape the experience of disease. Leventhal's illness representations model identifies the cognitive schemas (illness identity, cause, timeline, consequences, controllability) that shape how patients experience and manage illness. The experience of being 'half-dead' (hēmithanēs) maps onto the clinical literature on severe depletion and near-death states.

**Emergence — what causes illness and bodily weakness to arise:**
Physical illness arises through biological causes — infection, injury, genetic vulnerability, physiological deterioration. Health psychology adds that psychological factors (stress, hopelessness, social isolation) significantly influence the onset, severity, and recovery trajectory of physical illness. The HPA axis dysregulation produced by chronic psychological stress directly suppresses immune function, increasing susceptibility to illness (Segerstrom and Miller, *Psychological Bulletin*, 2004). Bodily weakness as a subjective experience arises from the gap between the demands the person faces and the physical resources they possess.

**Reduction and absence — what causes physical weakness to diminish:**
Physical recovery depends on adequate rest, nutrition, treatment, and time. Psychological factors significantly influence recovery — patients who feel hopeful, socially supported, and efficacious recover faster. The social environment of care (the presence of a caring other) has measurable physiological effects on recovery. The absence of illness and weakness is associated with physical health, adequate resources, rest, and the absence of sustained psychological stress.

**Location — where physical weakness is seated:**
The body — specifically in the musculoskeletal, immunological, neurological, and metabolic systems. However, the experience of weakness is also affective (the feeling of being unable to function), cognitive (the assessment of one's reduced capacity), and relational (the changed social position that illness produces — dependency, vulnerability to others' care or neglect).

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Disease vulnerability has significant genetic components. *Social:* Social determinants of health (poverty, access to healthcare, social support, discrimination) are among the strongest predictors of illness outcomes — structural social factors shape who gets sick and who recovers. *Circumstances:* Environmental factors — nutrition, safety, exposure to pathogens and toxins — are primary. *Developmental:* Adverse childhood experiences increase the risk of chronic illness in adulthood through sustained HPA axis dysregulation.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Health psychology provides direct coverage. The cross-reference to M01's somatic vocabulary is noted — illness weakness has a different character from fear-induced weakness, though both have somatic expression.

### Section 2 — Psychological suffering and distress

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — suffering and trauma

**Glosses covered:**
afflicted (a.ni), affliction (o.ni), to afflict (a.nah), to suffer (ya.gah), to languish (da.ev), to pine (duv), failing (kil.la.von), breaking (she.ver), breaking (shib.ba.ron)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Herman's *Trauma and Recovery* (1992) established the clinical framework for understanding psychological suffering following overwhelming experience. Van der Kolk (*The Body Keeps the Score*, 2014) demonstrated that trauma is stored in the body as well as in narrative memory — psychological suffering has somatic as well as cognitive dimensions. Frankl's logotherapy (*Man's Search for Meaning*, 1946) identified meaning as the primary resource that enables the human being to endure severe suffering without being destroyed by it. The terms 'languishing' and 'pining' map onto the clinical picture of low-grade chronic distress that is neither acute crisis nor diagnosed disorder.

**Emergence — what causes psychological suffering to arise:**
Psychological suffering arises from the experience of loss, injustice, helplessness, humiliation, or trauma — events that overwhelm the person's existing coping resources and sense of safety. The 'breaking' vocabulary (she.ver, shib.ba.ron) suggests the shattering of a prior intact state — psychological suffering as a rupture of what was whole. Languishing (da.ev) arises from sustained low-grade distress without resolution — the gradual depleting effect of unrelenting difficulty.

**Reduction and absence — what causes psychological suffering to diminish:**
Suffering reduces when the cause is addressed, when meaning is found within the experience (Frankl), when social support provides a relational buffer, and when the person's narrative of the suffering is processed to a point of integration. Trauma-informed care emphasises safety, connection, and empowerment as the three primary conditions for healing. The complete absence of suffering is associated in positive psychology with certain protected circumstances, but moderate suffering — when it produces growth — is distinguished from destructive suffering.

**Location — where psychological suffering is seated:**
Psychological suffering is simultaneously emotional (the felt experience of distress), cognitive (the narrative and meaning-making dimension), somatic (suffering is held in the body — tension, chronic pain, physiological dysregulation), and relational (suffering changes one's social position and relationships). Van der Kolk's work has established that there is no clean separation between psychological and physical suffering — they are aspects of a single integrated response.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Psychological resilience and vulnerability to distress have moderate heritability. Prior trauma sensitises the stress response system, increasing vulnerability to subsequent suffering. *Developmental:* Adverse childhood experiences are among the strongest predictors of psychological suffering in adulthood. *Social:* The availability of relational support — someone who witnesses and validates the suffering — significantly shapes whether suffering is integrated or compounded. Cultural frameworks that provide meaning for suffering (religious, philosophical, communal narrative) reduce its destructive potential. *Circumstances:* Genuine loss and injustice are the primary triggers.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Trauma psychology and clinical suffering research are well-developed and directly relevant.

### Section 3 — Vulnerability, poverty, and social weakness

**Primary field:** Social psychology / community psychology

**Glosses covered:**
poor (penēs), poor (a.nav), lacking (chas.sir), dearth (bats.tsa.rah), oppression (me.ru.tsah), plunder (harpagē), plunder (skulon), to harm (adikeō), to harm (kakoō), to hurt (blaptō), to hurt (a.tsav), to oppress (katadunasteuō), to oppress (kataponeō), to oppress (na.ga.s), to distress (tsa.rar)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Mullainathan and Shafir's scarcity research (*Scarcity*, 2013) demonstrates that poverty produces a cognitive tax — the mental bandwidth consumed by financial scarcity reduces cognitive capacity for other decisions and increases short-term thinking. Walker and Smith's relative deprivation theory addresses the psychological effects of perceived injustice and inequality. Oppression research (Freire, Bulhan) addresses the psychological effects of systemic oppression on the oppressed — including internalised oppression, learned helplessness, and the erosion of dignity. The cluster's oppression vocabulary is extensive and precise — these terms describe the experience of structural powerlessness as an inner-being reality.

**Emergence — what causes vulnerability and poverty to arise:**
Social weakness arises through structural disadvantage — lack of resources, social exclusion, and the exercise of power by others in ways that harm. Poverty arises through systemic factors as well as individual circumstances. Oppression arises when those with power use it to exploit, constrain, or harm those without it. The cluster's vocabulary consistently describes the experience of the one being harmed, not the one harming — this is a vocabulary from below.

**Reduction and absence — what causes social weakness to diminish:**
Social weakness diminishes through access to resources, social power, or protection. Justice — the rectification of structural disadvantage — is the primary systemic reducer. Relational advocacy — someone with power acting on behalf of the powerless — is the relational reducer. The biblical pattern of God as defender of the poor and oppressed maps precisely onto the psychological research on how the presence of a powerful advocate transforms the experience of the vulnerable.

**Location — where vulnerability is seated:**
Social vulnerability is primarily structural (located in the relationship between persons and social systems) but is experienced internally as a combination of material deprivation (physical), emotional distress (humiliation, fear, grief), cognitive burden (scarcity bandwidth), and relational marginalisation. There is no single inner location — vulnerability penetrates the whole person.

**External influences:**
*Social structures:* The primary driver — poverty and oppression are produced by social arrangements, not individual psychology. *Developmental:* Children raised in poverty experience cognitive, emotional, and social developmental effects that compound across the life course. *Cultural:* What counts as poverty and vulnerability is culturally relative; the experience of social marginalisation is universal.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Social psychology and community psychology provide coverage; the cluster's biblical vocabulary is extensive in this domain.

### Section 4 — Fatigue, sloth, and physical depletion

**Primary field:** Health psychology / motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be sluggish (a.tsel), be weary (la.ah), sluggish (a.tsel), sluggishness (ats.lah), sluggishness (ats.lut), to slacken (ra.phah), to faint (apopsuchō), to faint (ya.eph), to expire (ekpsuchō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Baumeister's ego depletion model (*Psychological Science*, 1998) proposed that self-control draws on a limited resource that is depleted through use — explaining why sustained effort increases the likelihood of subsequent failure of will. (Note: the replication record for ego depletion is mixed; the general phenomenon of mental fatigue is well-established even if the resource-depletion mechanism is debated.) Fatigue research distinguishes physical fatigue (muscular depletion) from mental fatigue (cognitive) from emotional fatigue (compassion fatigue, burnout — Maslach). Sloth as a character disposition (sluggishness) is addressed in personality psychology as low conscientiousness and low need for achievement.

**Emergence — what causes fatigue and sloth to arise:**
Physical fatigue arises from sustained output without adequate recovery. Mental fatigue arises from sustained cognitive demand. Emotional fatigue (burnout) arises from sustained high-stakes relational demand without replenishment. Sloth as a disposition arises from a combination of low motivation, avoidance of discomfort, and insufficient development of the capacity for sustained effort — often with developmental and temperamental roots.

**Reduction and absence — what causes fatigue to diminish:**
Rest and recovery are the primary reducers of physical and mental fatigue. Emotional replenishment — relational support, meaning restoration, reduced demand — reduces burnout. Sloth as a disposition responds to motivational interventions, meaningful goals, and the development of self-regulatory habits.

**Location — where fatigue is seated:**
Physical fatigue is musculoskeletal and metabolic. Mental fatigue is neurological — sustained cognitive activity depletes prefrontal resources. Emotional fatigue is affective and relational. The fainting vocabulary (apopsuchō, ya.eph) suggests the most extreme depletion — the person at the limit of their capacity to continue.

**External influences:**
*Circumstances:* Sustained demand without adequate resources is the primary driver. *Developmental:* The capacity for sustained effort (conscientiousness, self-regulation) is partly heritable and partly formed through developmental experience. *Cultural:* What counts as appropriate rest and work varies significantly by culture and historical context.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Fatigue research is well-developed; sloth as a dispositional characteristic has thinner coverage.

### Section 5 — Labour, travail, and effortful struggle

**Primary field:** Occupational psychology / health psychology

**Glosses covered:**
labor (ōdin), labor (odin), labour[er] (a.mel), toil (e.tsev), trouble (a.mal), to crush (da.kha), to crush (da.khah), to crush (ka.tat), to crush (ra.tsats), to seize (harpazō), to put to death (nekroō), to wither (na.vel), to blind (tufloō), blind (tuflos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Occupational psychology addresses the psychology of work and effortful labour — Hackman and Oldham's job characteristics model identifies what makes work meaningful and what makes it merely effortful. The psychology of chronic adversity and the productive dimension of suffering (post-traumatic growth — Tedeschi and Calhoun) are relevant to the travail vocabulary here. Birth-pang language (ōdin/odin — labour pains) has a specific semantic in Paul's letters where suffering produces new life — a developmental model of productive suffering that has psychological analogues in post-traumatic growth research.

**Emergence — what causes effortful struggle to arise:**
Effortful struggle arises when meaningful goals require sustained high-cost effort, when circumstances impose unavoidable hardship, or when resistance and opposition must be overcome. The crushing and seizing vocabulary describes suffering that is imposed rather than chosen — the person as object of force rather than agent of effort.

**Reduction and absence:**
Effortful struggle is resolved when the goal is achieved or abandoned, when circumstances change, or when the person's resources are depleted. Post-traumatic growth research notes that the experience of struggle itself, when it does not destroy the person, often produces increased capacity, depth, and relational quality that would not have developed without it.

**Location — where effortful struggle is seated:**
Simultaneously physical (muscular effort, physiological cost), emotional (distress of sustained demand), cognitive (motivational maintenance under difficulty), and relational (who is with the person in their struggle). The crushing vocabulary places this firmly in the somatic domain — physical force applied to the body as a metaphor for overwhelming experience.

**External influences:**
*Circumstances:* The primary driver — struggle is produced by the demands imposed by circumstances. *Social:* Whether the struggle is borne alone or with others significantly shapes its psychological impact. *Cultural:* Cultures that value productive suffering and frame difficulty as formative produce different responses to effortful struggle than those that frame suffering as purely negative.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Occupational psychology and post-traumatic growth research provide coverage; the crushing and imposed-suffering vocabulary exceeds occupational psychology's scope.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Health psychology (Section 1); trauma psychology (Section 2); social and community psychology (Section 3); motivational and occupational psychology (Sections 4 and 5).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The biopsychosocial model illuminates why the cluster's vocabulary integrates physical, emotional, and social weakness rather than treating them separately. Social determinants of health illuminate the oppression and poverty vocabulary — these are not merely metaphors; they describe actual social conditions with documented psychological effects. Post-traumatic growth illuminates why the biblical account of suffering can be simultaneously negative (genuine pain) and productive (genuine growth).

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats weakness and suffering as problems to be solved or at least managed. Scripture introduces the category of redemptive suffering — weakness as the location of divine power (2 Cor 12:9). This inversion — that weakness may be the condition for encountering divine strength — has no direct science parallel, though post-traumatic growth comes closest at the experiential level.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The compassion fatigue dimension (Section 4) may surface in the verse evidence for those whose weakness arises from sustained care for others. The social vulnerability vocabulary may produce a significant sub-group in Session B — the poor, oppressed, and afflicted as a distinct inner-being posture rather than merely a social description.
