## Purpose

This document is the cluster science review for M01. It is produced as a pre-analytical asset — before the cluster's Session B analysis begins — and is the standing reference for T7.3 prompt responses during Phase 8 of Session B. It does not constitute the biblical analysis; it holds the scientific lens alongside which the biblical pass will be read.

The document is structured according to how human science categorises the territory covered by this cluster's gloss list, not according to the programme's sub-group structure (which is determined by the biblical evidence). Where the science groupings and the programme's eventual sub-groups align, that alignment is noted in Session B. Where they cut the material differently, that divergence is itself analytically significant.

## Science sections

### Section 1 — Threat detection and fear response

**Primary field:** Neuroscience — affective neuroscience

**Glosses covered:**
fear (yir.ah), fear (fobos), fear (deos), fear (me.go.rah), fear (me.gu.rah), fear (mo.rah), fear (mo.ra), dread (pa.chad), dread (pach.dah), terror (e.mah), terror (bal.la.hah), terror (be.a.tah), terror (chit.tah), terror (chat.chat), terror (chit.tit), terror (cha.tat), terror (ma.gor), terror (me.chit.tah), terror (ma.a.ra.tsah), terror (tiph.le.tset), fearful (foberos), terrified (ekfobos), dreadful (a.ruts), timid (deilos), timidity (deilia), to fear (fobeō), to fear (ya.re), to fear (de.chal), to fear (za.chal), to fear (ya.gor), to fear (ra.hah), to fear: revere (ya.re), to frighten (ptoeō), to frighten (pturomai), afraid (emfobos), afraid (ya.re), fearing (ya.gor)

**Landmark findings and key researchers or frameworks:**

This is the core research domain of fear psychology. The amygdala-centred model of fear processing (LeDoux, *The Emotional Brain*, 1996; *Anxious*, 2015) established that fear responses can be initiated by subcortical pathways that bypass conscious appraisal — a rapid, automatic threat-detection system. This architecture distinguishes fear as a survival mechanism from fear as a conscious experience, a distinction the biblical vocabulary does not always observe but which becomes analytically relevant when the cluster's terms are examined for their somatic versus cognitive register.

Affective neuroscience (Panksepp, *Affective Neuroscience*, 1998) identifies FEAR as one of seven primary emotional systems, hard-wired and cross-species, associated with specific subcortical circuitry. Öhman's work on preparedness theory demonstrates that certain threat stimuli (predators, snakes, angry faces) activate fear circuitry more readily than others — a finding relevant to the cluster's concentration of terms for specific terror objects.

The distinction between fear (present, definite threat) and anxiety (anticipated, diffuse threat) is foundational in this field (Barlow, *Anxiety and Its Disorders*, 2002) and maps onto a distinction visible within the cluster's gloss list: terms for acute threat response (this section) versus terms for anticipatory distress (Section 3).

**Coverage assessment:** This is the most science-rich section of the cluster. Extensive coverage across neuroscience, experimental psychology, and clinical research.

### Section 2 — Physiological arousal and somatic fear expression

**Primary field:** Physiology / psychophysiology

**Glosses covered:**
trembling (entromos), trembling (tromos), trembling (ze.va.ah), trembling (cha.red), trembling (cha.ra.dah), trembling (ra.ad), trembling (re.a.dah), trembling (re.tet), to tremble (cha.rad), to tremble (a.rats), to tremble (ra.gaz), to tremble (ra.ad), to shudder (pa.lats), to shudder (sa.ar), shuddering (pal.la.tsut), shuddering (sa.ar), quivering (rag.gaz), quivering (rog.zah), staggering (pu.qah), faint (dav.va), to be dismayed (cha.tat), panic (re.tet), tossing (ne.dud)

**Landmark findings and key researchers or frameworks:**

The somatic vocabulary of fear in this cluster is unusually dense — a concentration of terms for trembling, shuddering, quivering, fainting, and physical collapse that maps directly onto the psychophysiology of the fight-flight-freeze response. Cannon's early description of the sympathetic nervous system's role in emergency arousal (*Bodily Changes in Pain, Hunger, Fear and Rage*, 1915) remains foundational. The autonomic arousal sequence — elevated heart rate, muscular tension, tremor — is well-described physiologically and corresponds to the biblical vocabulary's detail.

James-Lange theory (William James, 1884) proposed that emotional experience follows rather than precedes somatic response — a position that gives the somatic vocabulary independent significance. Contemporary somatic marker theory (Damasio, *Descartes' Error*, 1994) similarly argues that bodily states are constitutive of emotional experience, not merely expressive of it. Both frameworks make the cluster's somatic vocabulary analytically significant rather than merely illustrative.

The freeze response — distinct from fight or flight — has received increased attention in trauma research (Porges, polyvagal theory). Terms in this cluster for staggering, fainting, and immobility may map onto this freeze dimension rather than the arousal dimension.

**Coverage assessment:** Good coverage. Psychophysiology and somatic research provide direct analogues to this vocabulary. The density of somatic terms in M01 is itself a finding — it exceeds what is typical in ancient Near Eastern emotional vocabulary compilations.

### Section 3 — Anxiety, anticipatory dread, and worry

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — anxiety research

**Glosses covered:**
anxiety (de.a.gah), anxiety (sar.ap.pim), be distressed (ademoneo), be distressed (ke.ra), be timid (deiliaō), hardship (stenochoria), hardship (te.la.ah), pressure (thlipsis), pressure (a.qah), be perplexed (aporeō), be perplexed (diaporeō), be perplexed (she.vash)

**Landmark findings and key researchers or frameworks:**

Anxiety research distinguishes anticipatory and sustained distress from the acute fear response of Section 1. Beck's cognitive model of anxiety (Beck and Emery, *Anxiety Disorders and Phobias*, 1985) identifies threat appraisal and perceived inability to cope as the cognitive architecture of anxiety — a structure that can be compared with the biblical terms in this section, several of which carry a cognitive dimension (perplexity, being at a loss) alongside the affective one.

Worry as a cognitive process has been mapped by Borkovec and colleagues (Generalised Anxiety Disorder research, 1980s–2000s): worry is verbal-linguistic, repetitive, and future-oriented — a sustained cognitive engagement with possible threat. The Hebrew terms for anticipatory anxiety (de.a.gah, sar.ap.pim) may carry this cognitive-anticipatory quality rather than the acute arousal of Section 1.

Barlow's unified model of anxiety (*Anxiety and Its Disorders*, 2002) treats anxiety as a distinct emotional state from fear — both adaptive but with different temporal profiles and neural substrates. This distinction is useful for Session B classification of cluster terms.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate coverage. The gloss count for this section is smaller than Sections 1 and 2. Science coverage for anxiety is rich and well-developed; the cluster's contribution here is proportionally smaller.

### Section 4 — Awe, wonder, and reverential fear

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion / positive psychology — psychology of awe

**Glosses covered:**
be awe-struck (ekthambeo), astonished (ekthambos), bewilderment (tim.ma.hon), amazement (thambos), to be astounded (ta.mah), to dismay (ba.hal), to dismay (be.hal), horror (za.a.vah), horror: destroyed (sham.mah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers or frameworks:**

Awe is recognised as a distinct emotion that crosses the positive-negative boundary: it can be elicited by both sublime beauty and terrifying vastness. Keltner and Haidt's foundational paper on awe (2003) identifies two core features — perceived vastness and a need for accommodation (the existing mental structures do not fit the experience). This framework gives awe a cognitive as well as affective dimension.

Empirical research on awe has accelerated since 2015 (Stellar, Gordon, Piff and colleagues at UC Berkeley). Awe reliably produces self-diminishment, slowed time perception, prosocial orientation, and openness to new information. These downstream effects are of potential relevance when the biblical vocabulary of reverential fear before God is compared with the science account: the phenomenological profile partially overlaps.

The psychology of religion has addressed reverential fear under the category of the numinous experience (following Otto, *Das Heilige*, 1917 — not a science source, but a framework that the psychology of religion has operationalised in Hood's Mysticism Scale and related measures). Fear of God as a religious emotion has an experiential dimension that falls within science's scope; its theological content (the appropriateness of such fear given who God is) does not.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate and growing. Awe research is one of the more active areas of positive psychology. The overlap with Section 1 (horror terms appear in both sections) is a genuine ambiguity in the source groupings — noted here for Session B.

**Flag for Session B:** Several glosses in this section (ba.hal, be.hal, za.a.vah, sham.mah) carry strong negative weight (horror, dismay) and may sit between Sections 1 and 4 depending on the verse evidence. The science grouping places them here because of their overlap with the awe/overwhelming register, but the biblical analysis should determine their primary classification independently.

### Section 5 — Constraint, coercion, and external pressure

**Primary field:** Behavioural science / social psychology — stress and coercion research

**Glosses covered:**
coercion (o.tser), constraint (mu.tsaq), terrible (a.yom), ruthless (a.ruts), wave (mish.bar), burning (cho.ri)

**Landmark findings and key researchers or frameworks:**

These glosses name fear-inducing external realities rather than internal fear states. They describe the quality of the threatening agent or force, not the inner response of the subject. Science addresses this territory through stress research (Lazarus and Folkman, *Stress, Appraisal, and Coping*, 1984) — where external stressors are assessed for controllability and threat magnitude — and through social psychology of coercion and power (French and Raven's power bases; Milgram's obedience research).

Perceived uncontrollability and unpredictability are the primary psychological dimensions that determine whether a stressor produces fear and anxiety (Seligman's learned helplessness model). The terms in this section for overwhelming external force (waves, burning, ruthless force) invoke the uncontrollable and unpredictable dimensions that science identifies as maximally stress-inducing.

**Coverage assessment:** Thinner than Sections 1–3. These glosses are at the outer edge of science's descriptive reach for this cluster — science addresses how humans respond to such forces, but the glosses themselves describe the forces rather than the responses. A short section in the review is appropriate; a thin honest section is preferred over padding.

## Outside-science inventory

The following glosses, or registers of glosses, fall outside human science's descriptive scope for this cluster:

- **fear (ptoēsis)** — used in contexts of eschatological fear (the terror of the Day of the Lord as a divine event); science addresses human fear experience but not its eschatological framing.
- **terror (chag.ga)** — terror as a divine instrument of judgment; the theological category of God deploying fear as a covenantal response is outside science's scope.
- **distress (me.tsar)** — in covenantal distress contexts where the distress is the consequence of covenant violation; the relational-covenantal structure is not a scientific category.
- **to fear: revere (ya.re)** — the reverential/worship register of ya.re, where fear names the appropriate posture before the holy God, is a theological category. Science addresses awe and the phenomenology of numinous experience (Section 4 above), but the normativity of such fear — that it is right and fitting — is outside science's descriptive reach.

**Observation:** The outside-science content for M01 is proportionally smaller than for clusters whose core content is theological (e.g. M43 Prophecy, M38 Salvation). Fear as a human experience is richly covered by science. The theological distinctive of this cluster is not that fear falls outside science, but that the *object* and *normativity* of certain fears (fear of God as right response to covenant relationship) are theological categories that science does not adjudicate.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

These notes are prepared to assist the analyst during Phase 8 of Session B when T7.3 prompts are reached. They are provisional — the biblical pass may surface findings that modify or extend them.

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Neuroscience (affective neuroscience) — specifically the amygdala-centred model of fear processing and the fight-flight-freeze response. Clinical psychology (anxiety research) is the major secondary field for the anticipatory dimension of the cluster.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Science is likely to illuminate the somatic density of the cluster's vocabulary (Section 2) — the degree to which fear is embodied in Scripture aligns with somatic marker theory and the psychophysiology of arousal. The distinction between acute fear and anticipatory anxiety (Sections 1 and 3) may illuminate differences between cluster sub-groups once the biblical pass establishes them. The awe/reverential-fear dimension (Section 4) may illuminate how certain fear-of-God terms produce the downstream effects science identifies (self-diminishment, openness, prosocial orientation).

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The most likely divergence point is the normativity of fear. Science treats fear as adaptive or maladaptive depending on context — a functional assessment. Scripture treats certain fears as categorically right (fear of God) and others as categorically destructive (fear that drives out trust). Science has no category equivalent to the commanded fear that is simultaneously a virtue. Reductive divergence is likely if the cluster's theology of fear is mapped onto clinical constructs of anxiety management.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The freeze response dimension (Porges, polyvagal theory) may be underrepresented in the cluster's current term groupings — worth testing in verse evidence whether any terms carry a dissociative/freeze rather than arousal register. The cognitive dimension of worry (Borkovec) may surface gaps in how the cluster's anxiety terms are classified.
