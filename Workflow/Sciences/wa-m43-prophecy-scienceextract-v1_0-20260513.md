## Science sections

### Section 1 — Discernment and perception

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / psychology of perception

**Glosses covered:**
discernment (diakrisis), insight (aisthēsis), insight (sokh.le.ta.nu), to interpret (diermēneuō), to interpret (hermēneuō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Perceptual psychology addresses the mechanisms of pattern recognition and the detection of meaningful signals in complex environments. Expert perception research (Chase and Simon — chunking in chess experts) demonstrates that expertise involves perceptual reorganisation — experts perceive meaningful patterns that novices cannot detect. Insight research (Bowden and Jung-Beeman) addresses the cognitive mechanisms of the 'aha' moment — sudden perception of a solution that was not visible through incremental analysis. Discernment (diakrisis — the ability to distinguish and judge) maps onto the cognitive psychology of discrimination learning and signal detection theory (d-prime — the capacity to distinguish signal from noise). Interpretation (hermēneuō, diermēneuō) maps onto the cognitive psychology of meaning-making and translation.

**Emergence — what causes discernment to arise:**
Discernment arises through the combination of developed perceptual expertise, attentive engagement with the situation, and the absence of cognitive distortions that would bias perception. In the theological register, discernment (diakrisis) is specifically the capacity to distinguish spirits and spiritual realities — a category of perception that is not merely cognitive expertise but a spiritual gift.

**Location — where discernment is seated:**
Cognitively, discernment is located in the evaluative and discriminative capacities of the mind. In the programme's framework, discernment is a capacity of the spirit as well as the mind — the spirit's capacity to perceive spiritual reality.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Cognitive psychology of perception provides partial coverage; discernment as a spiritual capacity is primarily outside science.

### Section 2 — Spiritual perception and religious experience

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion

**Glosses covered:**
prophecy (profēteia), prophecy (ne.vu.ah), prophesying (ne.vu.ah), sign: miraculous (ot), spiritual (pneumatikos), spiritual (logikos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
James's *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) provides the foundational phenomenological account. Hood's Mysticism Scale operationalises the numinous experience for empirical study. Taves's *Fits, Trances, and Visions* (1999) addresses the historical and psychological study of religious experience including prophetic states. Research on glossolalia and altered states in religious contexts (Malony and Lovekin; Oesterreich on possession) addresses the psychology of prophetic experience from the outside. Spiritual (pneumatikos) in its Pauline register names a whole mode of existence and perception — the spiritual person who is animated and enabled by the Spirit of God — which psychology addresses only at the experiential edge.

**Emergence — what causes prophetic/spiritual perception to arise:**
From a psychology of religion perspective, prophetic experiences arise in the context of intense religious formation, altered states, heightened expectation, and socially legitimised prophetic roles. The theological account is fundamentally different: prophetic perception arises through the Spirit of God granting perception of divine reality — a category of causation that psychology does not address.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Psychology of religion addresses the phenomenology of prophetic experience; the theological claim (that prophecy is genuine divine communication) is outside science entirely.

### Section 3 — Image, symbol, and mental representation

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology — mental imagery

**Glosses covered:**
image (eikōn), image (tse.lem)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Kosslyn's mental imagery research (*Image and Brain*, 1994) addresses the neural and cognitive mechanisms of mental image generation and manipulation. Symbolic representation research (Deacon — *The Symbolic Species*) addresses how symbols function as representations of absent realities. Image (eikōn — image, likeness, representation) and image (tse.lem — image, used in Gen 1:26–27 of the imago Dei) both have cognitive psychology coverage for their representational function; their theological uses substantially exceed this.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Mental imagery research provides coverage for the representational dimension; the imago Dei is a theological category.

## Outside-science inventory

This cluster has the highest proportion of outside-science content in the programme:

- **Prophecy as divine communication** — the theological claim that the prophet speaks God's word is outside science entirely. Psychology addresses the phenomenology of prophetic states; the ontological claim is a theological matter.
- **Revelation as God disclosing himself** — divine self-disclosure as a category of knowledge acquisition is outside science. Human religious experience is addressable; God's actual self-revelation is not.
- **Spiritual (pneumatikos) in Paul's theology** — the spiritual person as one in whom the Spirit of God dwells and operates — is a theological category.
- **Image (tse.lem) in the imago Dei register** — humanity as the image of God, bearing God's likeness as a creational gift — is a theological category of anthropology.
- **Sign: miraculous (ot)** — the miraculous sign as a divine act that authenticates the divine message — is a theological category.

**Observation:** M43 is the cluster where the programme's methodology is most stretched. The cluster's entire vocabulary is oriented toward realities that science does not address as human phenomena — divine communication, divine self-disclosure, the Spirit's work of illumination. The science review is necessarily minimal; the programme's analytical work in Session B will be almost entirely theological.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Psychology of religion (religious experience — James, Hood) for Section 2; cognitive psychology of perception and imagery for Sections 1 and 3.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Expert perception research illuminates the discernment concept — genuine discernment involves a formed capacity to perceive meaningful patterns rather than naive impression. Psychology of religion illuminates the human phenomenology of prophetic experience — what people report experiencing in prophetic states, even if it cannot adjudicate the theological claims.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The deepest divergence in the programme. Prophecy and revelation as divine realities are entirely outside human science's descriptive scope. This is not a failure of science but a recognition of its domain boundaries.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** Whether the cluster's vocabulary describes primarily the experience of the prophet/perceiver or the content of what is perceived will be the key Session B analytical question. The programme's interest in inner-being may be served primarily by the former.
