## Science sections

### Section 1 — Wellbeing, flourishing, and subjective prosperity

**Primary field:** Positive psychology — wellbeing research

**Glosses covered:**
goodness (agathōsunē), abundance (mar.veh), abundance (rov), greatness (ge.dul.lah), greatness (go.del), greatness (mar.bit)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Diener's subjective wellbeing research (*Psychological Bulletin*, 1984) defines wellbeing as the combination of positive affect, low negative affect, and high life satisfaction — the subjective evaluation of one's life as going well. Seligman's PERMA model (*Flourish*, 2011) extends wellbeing beyond subjective happiness to include Positive emotion, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, and Achievement — a multidimensional account of flourishing. Goodness (agathōsunē — moral goodness, generosity) maps onto the VIA character strengths (kindness, generosity) and their wellbeing correlates. The abundance vocabulary (mar.veh, rov — abundance, multitude, greatness) spans both quantitative abundance and qualitative greatness — the quality of a life that is full rather than merely large.

**Emergence — what causes flourishing and abundance to arise:**
Psychological flourishing arises when the conditions for need satisfaction are met — relationships, meaningful activity, basic security, and growth. The positive psychology research consistently shows that flourishing is not produced by wealth above a threshold (Easterlin paradox) but by the quality of relationships, meaning, and engagement. Goodness (agathōsunē) as a character quality produces flourishing both in the person who possesses it and in those around them — virtue psychology establishes that character strengths are positive-sum: their exercise benefits both the virtuous person and the community.

**Reduction and absence — what causes flourishing to diminish:**
Flourishing diminishes through the deprivation of relational connection, meaningful activity, basic security, or the absence of growth. Material scarcity below a threshold reduces flourishing through the cognitive tax of scarcity (Mullainathan, Shafir); above the threshold, additional material abundance does not produce proportional increases in wellbeing.

**Location — where flourishing is seated:**
Flourishing is a property of the whole person's life — it cannot be localised to a single faculty or system. It is experienced as a felt quality of aliveness, engagement, and rightness — an inner-being state that is simultaneously emotional (positive affect), cognitive (the evaluation of life as going well), relational (embedded in meaningful relationships), and motivational (engaged and directed). For the programme's framework, goodness (agathōsunē) as a Spirit-fruit is an inner-being quality that produces flourishing as its natural expression.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Relationships are the single strongest predictor of subjective wellbeing across cultures and populations (Diener and Oishi). *Cultural:* The definition of flourishing varies by cultural context — different cultures emphasise different components of the PERMA model. *Circumstances:* Basic security (food, shelter, safety) is a prerequisite; above this threshold, material circumstances add little to flourishing.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Positive psychology wellbeing research is directly relevant to this section's vocabulary.

### Section 2 — Material wealth and the psychology of money

**Primary field:** Behavioural economics / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
riches (ke.vud.dah), substance (hon), wealth (cho.sen), to gain (kerdainō), luxury (ta.a.nug), to prosper (da.shen)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Kahneman and Deaton's research (*PNAS*, 2010) established that emotional wellbeing increases with income up to approximately $75,000/year (in 2010 dollars) and plateaus above this — the Easterlin paradox at the individual level. Kasser's materialism research demonstrates that strong financial goals are negatively correlated with wellbeing even when achieved. Thaler's behavioural economics (mental accounting, endowment effects) addresses the psychological distortions in how people value and manage money. Luxury (ta.a.nug — delight, luxury, softness) maps onto the psychology of hedonic consumption and the hedonic treadmill — the adaptation of pleasure to new levels of consumption that drives pursuit of ever-greater luxury.

**Emergence — what causes the pursuit of wealth to arise:**
The pursuit of wealth arises from: basic security-seeking (wealth as a buffer against deprivation); social status-seeking (wealth as a marker of social standing); the conditioning of desire toward material objects by cultural norms; and the genuine utility of financial resources for meaningful goals. The problem identified by both the biblical cluster and the behavioural economics research is when wealth becomes an end in itself rather than a means — producing the accumulation dynamic without satisfaction.

**Reduction and absence — what causes the pursuit of wealth to diminish:**
The pursuit of wealth is reduced by: genuine material security (removing the scarcity-driven motivation); the reorientation of goals toward intrinsic rather than extrinsic values (Kasser); gratitude and sufficiency practices (recognising what one has as adequate); and the experience that wealth does not deliver the wellbeing it promises.

**Location — where wealth-orientation is seated:**
The pursuit of wealth is primarily cognitive and motivational — it is a goal structure and a valuation system. It has an identity dimension (wealth as a source of self-worth) and an emotional dimension (the anxiety of insufficiency that drives accumulation, and the pleasure of acquisition that reinforces it). The psychology of material prosperity as an inner-being orientation is more about what wealth does to the inner being than what it is in itself.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Consumer capitalism creates cultural conditions that systematically elevate material goals. Cultures with strong communal sharing norms and sufficiency values produce less wealth-oriented persons. *Developmental:* Childhood material deprivation can produce adult over-valuation of financial security. *Social:* Social comparison with wealthier peers amplifies material aspiration.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Behavioural economics and materialism research provide strong coverage.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Positive psychology (wellbeing and flourishing — Diener, Seligman) for Section 1; behavioural economics and materialism research for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** PERMA model illuminates why biblical flourishing is multidimensional — it is not merely wealth but the comprehensive goodness of life in right relationship. Kasser's materialism research illuminates the biblical concern about wealth as a spiritual danger — not because material goods are evil but because misplaced ultimate trust in them destroys the relational and meaning-based foundations of genuine flourishing.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The covenantal account of abundance — that material prosperity is God's covenantal gift and its absence is covenantal discipline — introduces a vertical theological dimension that behavioural economics cannot address. Science addresses wealth's effects on wellbeing; theology addresses its source and its proper place in a life ordered toward God.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The cluster is small; the verse evidence may show more diverse registers than the gloss list suggests. Whether abundance appears primarily as material reality or as metaphor for spiritual richness will be a key Session B analytical question.
