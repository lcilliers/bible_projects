## Science sections

### Section 1 — Temptation and self-control failure

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / self-regulation research

**Glosses covered:**
temptation/testing: temptation (peirasmos), to test/tempt: tempt (peirazō), stumbling block (skandalon), stumbling block (proskomma), stumbling (proskopē), to cause to stumble (skandalizō), to stumble (ptaiō), to strike (proskoptō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Baumeister and Muraven's self-control research (*Psychological Science*, 1998) addresses temptation as a situation where immediate desires conflict with long-term values and goals. The ego depletion model proposes that resisting temptation depletes a common resource, making subsequent resistance harder (though the replication record for this specific mechanism is mixed, the general phenomenon of self-control fatigue is robust). Hofmann's ecological momentary assessment research (*Psychological Science*, 2012) collected real-time reports of desire and temptation across daily life — finding that people experience multiple desires and temptations daily, and that resistance success varies with strength of desire, current self-regulatory resources, and social context. The stumbling block (skandalon — a snare that causes falling) maps onto the psychology of environmental cues that trigger desire — temptation environments and situational triggers.

**Emergence — what causes temptation to arise:**
Temptation arises when a desired object is present or made salient by environmental cues, and when the desire conflicts with existing values or commitments. Temptation is intensified by: prior depletion of self-control resources; proximity to the desired object; social modelling of the tempted behaviour; and the framing of the desired object as forbidden (psychological reactance can amplify desire for prohibited objects). Stumbling blocks (skandalon) are environmental or social factors that place the tempted person in a context that makes falling more likely — a social/situational rather than purely internal phenomenon.

**Reduction and absence — what causes temptation to diminish:**
Situation management is the primary effective strategy — removing the stumbling block or avoiding the tempting environment is more effective than resisting strong desire in the presence of cues. Commitment devices (pre-committing to abstinence before desire is activated) are more effective than relying on in-the-moment resistance. Social accountability and relational support for self-control also reduce temptation susceptibility. The implementation intention (if-then plans — Gollwitzer) is among the most evidence-based self-regulation strategies.

**Location — where temptation is seated:**
The experience of temptation is affective-motivational — it is felt as a pull toward the desired object combined with awareness of its conflict with values. It has a cognitive dimension (the evaluation of the conflict) and a somatic dimension (appetitive desire as bodily arousal). The stumbling block vocabulary places the external situational trigger in the analysis alongside the internal desire — temptation is not purely internal but an interaction between inner desire and outer situation.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Peer contexts that normalise the tempted behaviour dramatically increase temptation susceptibility. Social accountability that makes values visible to others increases resistance. *Developmental:* Delay of gratification capacity develops through childhood and is shaped by parental practice and family environment (Mischel). *Cultural:* Cultural norms determine which behaviours are tempting (they must be simultaneously desired and culturally available) and which have effective social constraints.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Self-control and temptation research is well-developed.

### Section 2 — Testing, evaluation, and the psychology of trial

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
test (dokimē), test (peira), tested (dokimos), testing (dokimion), to test (dokimazō), to test/tempt: testing (peirazō), to test/tempt: try (peirazō), to attempt (epicheireō), to try (peiraō), fight (agōn), not stumbling (aproskopos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Performance under pressure research (Beilock, *Choke*, 2010) addresses why high-stakes evaluation contexts impair performance — choking under pressure arises when conscious attention is redirected to automated processes, disrupting them. Test anxiety research is well-established — evaluation-related anxiety impairs performance through attentional distraction and working memory depletion. Dokimazō (to test, to prove, to approve through testing) carries a specifically positive connotation — testing that demonstrates quality — which maps onto the psychology of proving rather than merely evaluating. Tested (dokimos — approved, having passed the test) represents the positive outcome of testing.

**Emergence — what causes the experience of being tested to arise:**
Testing arises when performance, character, or commitment is evaluated under conditions that reveal its true quality. The test reveals rather than creates — the theological account of divine testing (as in Abraham's testing) is that the test discloses what is already present. The psychological parallel is assessment's function: evaluation reveals capacity that exists prior to the test.

**Reduction and absence:**
Test anxiety is reduced by adequate preparation, autonomy-supportive evaluation contexts, and a growth mindset (seeing the test as an opportunity for learning rather than a verdict on fixed capacity). Not stumbling (aproskopos) suggests the state of one who has navigated the testing environment without falling — the positive outcome of the section's testing vocabulary.

**Location — where the testing experience is seated:**
Testing as an event is situational; the response to testing is internal — primarily cognitive (appraisal of the test as threat or challenge) and emotional (anxiety or engaged focus). The growth mindset research distinguishes challenge appraisal (the test is an opportunity — produces performance-enhancing arousal) from threat appraisal (the test will reveal my inadequacy — produces anxiety).

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Experience of manageable challenge and success in prior tests builds the capacity to face subsequent testing with confidence. *Social:* Evaluation contexts that are perceived as supportive reduce test anxiety; those perceived as threatening increase it.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Performance and test anxiety research provide coverage; the theological category of divine testing (that God tests in order to develop and prove, not in order to discover what he does not know) is outside science.

### Section 3 — Persuasion and social influence

**Primary field:** Social psychology — persuasion research

**Glosses covered:**
to persuade (peithō), to sift (siniazō), to endure (anechō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Cialdini's persuasion research (*Influence*, 1984) identifies the six principles of social influence. Petty and Cacioppo's elaboration likelihood model (1986) distinguishes central route persuasion (careful consideration of arguments) from peripheral route persuasion (responding to cues and heuristics without deep processing). To sift (siniazō — used in Luke 22:31 of Satan sifting Peter as wheat) suggests an evaluative process through which the true character is separated from what is external — an image combining testing and temptation.

**Emergence — what causes susceptibility to persuasion to arise:**
Persuasion susceptibility increases when: the person is cognitively depleted (peripheral route processing increases); the persuader is perceived as credible and likeable; the message is framed in terms of social norms and consensus; and the person has not yet formed a committed position. Endurance (anechō — to hold on, to put up with) is the perseverance dimension of resisting persuasion or bearing difficulty — it requires sustained commitment.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Persuasion research provides partial coverage; the theological dimensions of these terms (divine sifting, bearing with one another) exceed the psychology of social influence.

## Outside-science inventory

- **temptation by Satan/spiritual adversaries** — science addresses temptation as an internal and social phenomenon; the theological category of demonic temptation as an external personal assault is outside science's scope.
- **testing by God as a divine pedagogical act** — God testing his people in order to form and develop their faith is a theological category. Science addresses testing as human evaluation; divine purposive testing is outside its scope.
- **dokimazō in its discernment register** — the proving and approving of what is excellent (Phil 1:10) as a spiritual capacity is a theological category.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Self-control and temptation research for Section 1; performance and test anxiety research for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The situation management principle illuminates why Scripture so consistently warns about placing oneself in contexts of temptation rather than assuming the capacity for in-the-moment resistance. The challenge vs. threat appraisal distinction illuminates why the same difficult circumstances can be either faith-forming or faith-destroying depending on the person's inner orientation.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The deepest divergence: divine testing as purposive and formative is the opposite of the science account of testing as evaluation of existing capacity. The theological claim is that God tests in order to produce what is not yet fully formed — testing as developmental rather than merely revelatory.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The semantic range of peirasmos (both temptation and trial) in different verse contexts — whether the testing is internal desire or external circumstance — will be an important Session B disambiguation.
