## Science sections

### Section 1 — Obedience, compliance, and deference to authority

**Primary field:** Social psychology — obedience research

**Glosses covered:**
to keep: obey (sha.mar), to keep: careful (sha.mar), to keep: guard (sha.mar), to keep: look at (sha.mar), disobedience (apeitheia), disobedience (parakoē), to disobey (apeitheō), to ignore (parakouō), rebellion (parapikrasmos), rebellion (me.rad), rebellion (me.red), rebellion (me.ri), rebellious (mar.dut)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Milgram's obedience experiments (*Obedience to Authority*, 1974) remain the most influential demonstration of the power of legitimate authority to produce obedience — 65% of ordinary participants delivered apparently lethal electric shocks under authoritative instruction. Cialdini's compliance research (*Influence*, 1984) identifies the six principles of social influence (reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity). Brehm's reactance theory (*A Theory of Psychological Reactance*, 1966) addresses disobedience and rebellion as responses to perceived threats to freedom — when people feel their autonomy is being constrained, they react against the constraint. Note that sha.mar (to keep, guard, observe) in the biblical material is primarily positive — the careful, attentive keeping of God's commands — which has a different psychological character from mere compliance.

**Emergence — what causes obedience to arise:**
Obedience arises through the combination of legitimate authority, proximity to the authority figure, the graduated nature of the compliance request, and the diffusion of personal responsibility. In the biblical context, covenantal obedience arises from the relationship of trust and love within the covenant — sha.mar as keeping implies attentive care rather than mere compliance. Disobedience arises when the authority's legitimacy is questioned, when the cost of compliance is appraised as too high, or when reactance against constraint overcomes the tendency to comply.

**Reduction and absence — what causes disobedience and rebellion to diminish:**
Rebellion reduces when the perceived constraint is removed, when the authority is restored to legitimate standing, or when the psychological reactance is resolved through a freely chosen compliance. The biblical vocabulary of rebellion (parapikrasmos, me.ri) suggests a hardened pattern — not mere single episodes of disobedience but an established orientation of resistance. The transition from rebellion to willing obedience is a significant biblical theme addressed in M45 (Transformation).

**Location — where obedience and disobedience are seated:**
The social psychology of obedience locates it primarily in the social situation — obedience is a relational response to authority, not simply an inner-state phenomenon. However, sha.mar as careful keeping implies an inner attentive orientation — the person who guards and observes is in a state of alert, engaged faithfulness. This inner attentiveness is relevant to the programme's interest in inner-being characteristics. Disobedience and rebellion are seated in the will and the heart (the inner being's orientation of resistance).

**External influences:**
*Social:* Legitimate authority structures, peer conformity pressure, and graduated compliance sequences are the primary social situational drivers. *Developmental:* Patterns of authority-relationship developed in childhood (parental authority, school authority) shape adult obedience/disobedience styles. Secure parenting that explains the reasons for rules (rather than demanding unquestioning compliance) produces more internalised, willing obedience in adulthood. *Hereditary:* Conscientiousness and agreeableness (Big Five) — both with moderate heritability — predict obedience to social norms. *Cultural:* High power-distance cultures (Hofstede) show greater obedience to authority as a cultural norm.

**Coverage assessment:** Good for obedience generally. The sha.mar dimension (attentive, careful keeping) is less well covered by the compliance literature, which focuses on obedience as social response rather than as inner attentiveness.

### Section 2 — Stubbornness, hardness of heart, and cognitive rigidity

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / personality psychology

**Glosses covered:**
hardening (pōrōsis), hardness (sklērotēs), hardness of heart (sklērokardia), stubbornness (qe.shi), stubbornness (she.ri.rut), be insensitive (ta.phash), to harden (pōroō), to become callous (apalgeō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Cognitive rigidity research addresses the tendency to maintain fixed patterns of thought and response in the face of contradicting information. Psychopathy research (Hare — Psychopathy Checklist) identifies callousness (reduced empathy and emotional responsiveness) as a core feature of antisocial personality — becoming callous (apalgeō) maps directly. Research on moral hardening addresses the progressive diminution of moral sensitivity through repeated moral disengagement — each act of moral compromise makes the next easier (the slippery slope of moral desensitisation, Walker). Hardness of heart (sklērokardia) as a clinical description of reduced emotional and moral responsiveness has parallels in the alexithymia literature — reduced capacity to identify and process emotions.

**Emergence — what causes hardness and stubbornness to arise:**
Hardness arises through: (1) repeated moral disengagement and the progressive habituation of conscience; (2) defensive hardening in response to perceived threat or repeated injury (emotional armour); (3) temperamental rigidity amplified by experience; (4) in the Pauline/OT theological register, divine judicial hardening as a response to persistent rejection of truth (Rom 9; Exod). The becoming-callous (apalgeō) term suggests a process — gradual desensitisation rather than sudden change.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hardness to diminish:**
Cognitive rigidity responds to challenge and new information in safe contexts — people can become less rigid when they feel psychologically secure. Callousness is harder to treat clinically — it is among the most treatment-resistant personality features. The theological account of divine transformation (M45) provides the most radical account of hardness reduction: the removal of the heart of stone and its replacement with a heart of flesh (Ezek 36:26) — a theological category that exceeds what clinical psychology offers.

**Location — where hardness is seated:**
Primarily in the heart (the Hebrew and Pauline term) — which in the programme's framework is the inner being's central integrating faculty. Hardness of heart is a hardening of the whole person's orientation — reduced responsiveness in affect, perception, and will simultaneously. Neurologically, the callousness dimension has coverage in reduced amygdala responsiveness to others' distress — a measurable neural correlate. But the full biblical category of hardness of heart exceeds any single neural location.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Childhood trauma can produce defensive emotional hardening as a survival adaptation. Repeated moral compromise progressively hardens moral sensitivity. *Hereditary:* Callous-unemotional traits have significant heritability (approximately 50%). *Circumstances:* Sustained adversity and betrayal can produce hardening as an emotional self-protection response.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. The clinical literature on callousness and cognitive rigidity provides coverage; the theological category of hardness of heart exceeds clinical scope.

### Section 3 — Lawlessness, rebellion, and moral defiance

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / developmental psychology

**Glosses covered:**
lawlessness (anomia), lawlessness (paranomia), to rebel (parapikrainō), to rebel (ma.rad), to restrain (a.tsar), to turn aside: depart (sur), to turn aside: remove (sur), to turn aside: turn aside (sur)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Moral development research (Kohlberg, Piaget) addresses how moral reasoning develops and how lawbreaking relates to moral stage. Antisocial behaviour research (Patterson — coercive family process model) traces the developmental pathways from childhood oppositional behaviour through adolescent delinquency to adult antisocial conduct. Turning aside (sur) — departing from the prescribed path — maps onto the psychological research on moral lapse and drift (the gradual departure from values without a single decisive moment of rebellion).

**Emergence — what causes lawlessness to arise:**
Lawlessness arises from: (1) moral development failure (insufficient formation of moral reasoning); (2) situational influences that override existing moral commitments; (3) prior moral disengagement that has progressively reduced the hold of moral norms; (4) ideological rejection of the law's legitimacy. The turning-aside vocabulary suggests a gradual drift rather than a decisive rebellion in some cases — the cumulative effect of small departures.

**Location — where moral defiance is seated:**
Primarily cognitive (rejection of the law's authority or legitimacy) and volitional (the settled choice to act contrary to it). The will is the primary location — lawlessness as a settled orientation of the inner being against the governing norm.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Coercive family processes, peer delinquency, and inadequate moral formation are among the strongest developmental predictors of lawlessness. *Social:* Community norms, peer influence, and institutional structures significantly shape moral compliance. *Circumstances:* Situations that remove social accountability and provide impunity increase lawless behaviour.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Developmental and moral psychology provide coverage for the behavioural dimensions; the theological register of rebellion against God as covenant violation is outside science.

## Outside-science inventory

- **Obedience/disobedience to God in its covenantal register** — the theological category of covenant faithfulness versus covenant rebellion is outside science's descriptive scope. Science addresses authority compliance; the biblical account adds that the authority in question is the Creator and Covenant Lord, whose commands are expressions of his own character and the creature's good.
- **Hardening by God (pōroō, Exod hardening)** — divine judicial hardening as a response to persistent rejection is a theological category.
- **Lawlessness (anomia) in its eschatological register** — the man of lawlessness, lawlessness as a cosmic power — is a theological category.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology of obedience (Milgram, Cialdini) for Section 1; clinical psychology of callousness and rigidity for Section 2; moral development research for Section 3.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Milgram's findings illuminate why obedience to authority can be either servile compliance or genuine submission, and why the distinction matters — the quality of inner orientation (sha.mar as attentive keeping versus mere rule-following) is psychologically significant. The callousness research illuminates hardness of heart as a progressive process — the desensitisation model maps onto the biblical pattern of accumulating moral hardening.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The covenantal frame fundamentally reshapes the obedience cluster — obedience to God is not mere authority compliance but the creature's rightly-ordered response to the Covenant Lord within a relationship of love. The social psychology of obedience (situational compliance regardless of inner orientation) captures the outer behaviour but misses the inner-being dimension that the programme's framework is specifically examining.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The sha.mar dimension (attentive, caring observance) may not have an adequate science category and may require a programme-specific characterisation in Session B. Whether hardening appears as a gradual process or a sudden state in the verse evidence is a finding to watch for.
