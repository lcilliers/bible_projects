## Science sections

### Section 1 — Psychology of evil and moral harm

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
bad: harmful (ra), be evil (ra.a), to do evil/harm (kakopoieō), to ruin (sha.chat), devastation (she.ma.mah), be desolate: destroyed (sha.mem)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Baumeister's *Evil: Inside Human Violence and Cruelty* (1997) challenges the assumption that evil is radical otherness, arguing that most harm is done for mundane instrumental reasons — people harm others to get what they want, not from the sadistic enjoyment of harm itself. Zimbardo's situationist account (*The Lucifer Effect*, 2007) emphasises the role of situational factors in producing evil behaviour — ordinary people commit extraordinary harm under specific social conditions (the Stanford Prison Experiment, Milgram's obedience findings). Hannah Arendt's 'banality of evil' concept (operationalised psychologically by Milgram) captures the same insight: evil often emerges not from exceptional malice but from the suspension of moral judgment under institutional and social pressure. The devastation and ruin vocabulary in this section moves beyond individual harm toward collective and structural destruction.

**Emergence — what causes evil conduct to arise:**
Baumeister identifies four primary roots of evil: instrumentality (harming others to obtain desired outcomes), threatened egotism (violence in response to perceived insult or status threat), idealism (harm committed in service of a purportedly good cause), and sadism (pleasure in causing pain — the rarest form). Situational factors (Zimbardo) — deindividuation, diffusion of responsibility, obedience to authority, gradual escalation — reliably produce evil behaviour in ordinarily non-evil persons. Moral disengagement (Bandura) describes the cognitive mechanisms by which people disconnect their moral standards from their harmful conduct.

**Reduction and absence — what causes harmful and evil conduct to diminish:**
Situational interventions — reducing anonymity, clarifying personal responsibility, introducing moral exemplars who resist group pressure — are among the most effective reducers. Individual moral identity (Aquino, Reed) — where being a moral person is central to the self-concept — buffers against situational evil. Social accountability structures (law, cultural norms, community oversight) constrain harmful behaviour. Empathy for potential victims is the most reliable internal inhibitor of harmful conduct.

**Location — where evil conduct is seated:**
This is analytically complex. Baumeister's account locates evil primarily in social situations and cognitive processes (moral disengagement). Psychodynamic accounts locate it in unconscious drives and defences. The cluster's vocabulary (to do evil, to ruin, to harm) describes acts and their effects — they are primarily descriptions of conduct rather than of inner states. The inner-being dimension of evil — as an orientation, a character trait, or a settled disposition — is the programme's specific interest, which the verse evidence will need to address rather than the science review.

**External influences:**
*Situational:* Zimbardo's situationist emphasis argues that situation is more powerful than character in producing evil. *Developmental:* Psychopathy and antisocial personality disorder have significant hereditary components (approximately 50%); callous-unemotional traits in childhood are among the strongest predictors of adult antisocial conduct. Childhood abuse, neglect, and exposure to violence significantly increase the risk of perpetrating violence. *Cultural:* Cultures and institutions that normalise harm, dehumanise out-groups, or grant impunity for harmful conduct dramatically increase rates of evil behaviour.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Psychology of evil is a defined field; the cluster is small. The outside-science content is proportionally large.

### Section 2 — Idolatry and substitute attachment

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion / attachment theory

**Glosses covered:**
idol (eidōlon), idol (a.tsav), idol's temple (eidōleion), idolater (eidōlolatrēs), idolatrous (kateidōlos), idolatry (eidōlolatreia), sacrificed to idols (eidōlothutos), to detest (shiq.qets), refuse (pig.gul), refuse (ra.mut)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Granqvist and Kirkpatrick's attachment theory of religion (*Psychology of Religion and Spirituality*, 2008) addresses God as an attachment figure — people relate to God with the same attachment strategies (secure, anxious, avoidant) they bring to human relationships. Idolatry, from this perspective, represents the attachment of ultimate concern to a substitute attachment figure — an object, person, or institution that is not God but is treated as if it were. Tillich's theological concept of idolatry as misplaced ultimate concern (operationalised psychologically by Pargament as the attachment of sacred significance to non-sacred objects) provides the psychological-theological bridge. The detesting vocabulary (shiq.qets, pig.gul — the language of revulsion and refuse) maps onto the psychology of disgust and moral purity.

**Emergence — what causes idolatry to arise:**
Psychologically, idolatry arises when the need for a reliable ultimate attachment figure is met by a substitute — a finite object, person, status, or achievement that is treated as if it possessed infinite worth and reliability. The substitute provides real (if temporary) comfort and orientation; its failure to deliver permanent security perpetuates the cycle of seeking a more adequate substitute. The revulsion vocabulary (a.tsav — idols that cause grief/pain; pig.gul — refuse, detestable thing) reflects the psychological reality of the attachment object's ultimate inadequacy.

**Reduction and absence — what causes idolatry to diminish:**
Psychologically, idolatry reduces when the substitute is seen clearly as finite and inadequate — when it fails to deliver what it promised. The therapeutic equivalent is the grief process that follows the loss of an idealised object. For the theological account, idolatry reduces through the reorientation of ultimate attachment toward God — what the prophets call 'returning' and M45 (Transformation) addresses.

**Location — where idolatry is seated:**
Primarily relational and cognitive — it is an attachment orientation (relational) combined with an appraisal of worth (cognitive). The disgust response to idols (shiq.qets) adds an emotional-somatic dimension — revulsion is a visceral, bodily response. The cluster sits at the intersection of attachment, cognition, and somatic disgust.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Idolatry is culturally shaped — what objects are available to serve as substitute attachments varies enormously by cultural context. *Developmental:* Insecure attachment in childhood is associated with more pronounced substitute-attachment dynamics in adulthood (Granqvist). *Social:* Communal idolatry — shared devotion to a substitute attachment object — is reinforced by social consensus and ritual.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Attachment theory of religion provides the most direct science coverage; direct idolatry psychology research is limited.

## Outside-science inventory

- **shade (re.pha.im)** — the dead as an underworld category; the theological anthropology of the dead in Sheol is outside science's descriptive scope.
- **abomination (to.e.vah)** in its covenantal-boundary register — what violates the holiness of God's covenant community — is a theological category. Science addresses disgust responses but not covenant holiness.
- **evil as spiritual reality** — the theological claim that evil is not merely human harm but a spiritual force (connected to the enemy and to sin as a power) is outside science's descriptive scope.
- **wickedness (ra.sha) as a relational status before God** — not merely as a behavioural pattern but as a standing before the holy God — is a theological category.

**Observation:** M27 has the highest proportion of outside-science content of any cluster to this point, exceeding M21 (Prayer). The cluster's theological core — evil as a spiritual reality, abomination as covenant violation, idolatry as the rejection of God — is not accessible to human science. The science review is proportionally smaller as a result.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Moral psychology (psychology of evil — Baumeister, Zimbardo) for Section 1; psychology of religion (attachment theory — Granqvist, Kirkpatrick) for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The situationist account of evil illuminates why the cluster's vocabulary describes evil conduct as arising from ordinary conditions rather than requiring exceptional malice. Moral disengagement mechanisms illuminate how the cluster's wicked characters sustain harmful conduct without apparent self-condemnation. Attachment theory illuminates the idolatry vocabulary — idols as substitute attachment figures that fail.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The cluster's deepest divergence: evil as a spiritual force, as a property of the cosmos under the enemy's influence, and as a posture before God cannot be reached by science's horizontal account of human harm. The theological vocabulary of abomination and refuse is not primarily psychological disgust but covenant violation — a different category.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The cluster is small. Whether the verse evidence shows evil as primarily external circumstance or internal character orientation is a finding Session B will need to determine — science cannot pre-determine this.
