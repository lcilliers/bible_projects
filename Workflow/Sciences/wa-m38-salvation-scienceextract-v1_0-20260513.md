## Science sections

### Section 1 — Relief, rescue, and the psychology of being saved

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / positive psychology / psychology of religion

**Glosses covered:**
salvation (soteria), salvation (sōtēria), salvation (ye.shu.ah), saving (sōtērion), savior (sōtēr), to save (sōzō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Psychology of religion addresses conversion and salvation experiences — the phenomenology of what people report experiencing in relation to salvation. William James's *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) remains foundational — his analysis of the 'twice-born' religious type describes the experience of moving from a divided, troubled self to a unified, at-peace self through religious transformation. Pargament's work on religion and coping identifies salvation-related experiences as among the most psychologically significant — they produce relief, reorientation, and the restoration of meaning. Trauma recovery research (Herman) provides an analogue framework: rescue from danger produces a specific pattern of relief, disorientation, gratitude, and the need for narrative integration of what occurred. The experience of being saved — regardless of its theological content — has an identifiable psychological structure.

**Emergence — what causes the salvation experience to arise:**
The salvation experience arises when the person perceives that they have been delivered from a state of danger, lostness, or condemnation — and that the deliverance came from outside themselves. The relief component requires prior awareness of threat; the gratitude component requires attribution of deliverance to a benefactor; the reorientation component requires integration of the new status into the person's self-understanding. Conversion experiences (James's 'twice-born') are preceded by a period of unease, moral conflict, or existential crisis that is resolved in the salvation event.

**Reduction and absence — what causes the salvation experience to diminish:**
The felt reality of salvation can diminish through doubt (M20), through the failure to integrate the experience into a stable ongoing narrative, or through the loss of the relational and community context that sustains it. Assurance — the sustained confidence that one is saved — is addressed in both theological (Westminster Confession) and psychological (attachment security) frameworks.

**Location — where the salvation experience is seated:**
The salvation experience is whole-person: relief is somatic (the parasympathetic release from sustained threat arousal); reorientation is cognitive (new self-understanding and world-understanding); gratitude is emotional and relational; the peace that follows is affective. It is among the most comprehensive inner-being state-changes describable in human experience.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Conversion and salvation experiences are almost always socially embedded — they occur in the context of religious community, relationship, and proclamation. *Developmental:* The theological content of what salvation means is entirely formed through community and tradition. *Circumstances:* Crisis and existential distress are consistent preconditions for salvation experiences — the awareness of need is the psychological precondition for the experience of rescue.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin but genuine. Psychology of religion and trauma recovery research provide coverage for the experiential dimension. This is the only section possible for this cluster — all other content is outside science.

## Outside-science inventory

The core content of M38 is comprehensively outside human science:

- **Propitiation (hilasmos, hilastērios)** — the theological concept of a sacrifice that satisfies divine wrath and restores relationship with God — is a theological category without science parallel. Science addresses neither divine wrath nor its satisfaction.
- **Atonement (kip.pu.rim — also M10)** — the covering/removal of sin through sacrificial substitution — is a theological category.
- **Redemption (pa.dah)** — the ransoming of the captive through the payment of a price — is a theological category. The psychological experience of being released from captivity (M36 Section 2) provides a partial experiential analogue; the theological content (Christ as ransom) is outside science.
- **Free gift (dōrea, dōrēma)** — salvation as pure gift, unearned and unearnable — is a theological category. Gift psychology (Mauss) addresses the social dynamics of giving; the theological claim that God gives salvation as pure grace is outside science.
- **Propitious/gracious (hileōs)** — the character attribute of God that makes propitiation possible — is a theological category.

**Observation:** M38 is the programme's most theologically concentrated cluster. The science review for this cluster is necessarily modest — a single section covering the experiential edge of what is primarily a theological reality. This is not a failure of the science review; it is an accurate reflection of the cluster's nature. The programme's analytical work in Session B will be primarily theological, with the science contributing only to the characterisation of the human experience of receiving salvation.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Psychology of religion (salvation and conversion experience — James, Pargament) and trauma recovery research (relief and rescue phenomenology).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The phenomenology of rescue and relief illuminates what the inner-being experience of salvation feels like — the somatic release, the cognitive reorientation, the gratitude and peace. This is genuine science contribution, even if it addresses only the human side of what is primarily a divine act.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The deepest divergence in the programme. Salvation as God's act of rescuing sinners from judgment through the substitutionary death of Christ is a theological reality that science cannot evaluate. Psychology addresses the human experience of salvation; the ontological content — that the rescue is real, that the judgment is real, that Christ's death accomplished what it claims — is outside science entirely.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The cluster is small (13 terms) and concentrated. The inner-being dimension most likely to surface in Session B is the assurance/doubt dynamic — the experience of salvation as settled or as uncertain — which links to M20 (Doubt) and M19 (Trust).
