The cluster publication pipeline currently lives in the analytical phase, not the editorial phase. The combined chapter drafts plus appendices are working documents whose purpose is to surface the analytical findings in readable form — not to be the final, polished, publication-ready editions for general distribution.

**Why:** 2026-05-29 — researcher named this directly after the M38 ch3 v1 → v2 rewrite. v1 was over-engineered in pursuit of editorial polish, which produced mechanical prose that fit rules rather than serving meaning. v2 was a quality improvement but the deeper point is that v1 was acceptable for finding-surfacing purposes. "Putting too much pressure on editorial perfection may create forced errors."

**How to apply:**
- **Write each chapter once.** Apply the per-chapter scaffolding (input + chapter instruction + style guide) and the reverse audit + self-review discipline. That's enough.
- **Watch for specific failures** that compromise finding-surfacing: mechanistic openings, forcing observations the verses don't carry, analytical jargon leaking into prose, dropping content to fit prose flow.
- **Don't agonise over editorial polish.** Don't rewrite for natural rhythm or fluent voice unless prose is so bad it obscures findings. Reserve v2 rewrites for chapters where v1 actually fails to surface findings clearly or contains substantive errors.
- **Trust the working-document phase.** All 46 clusters need their findings surfaced before any editorial perfection cycle. Compress the timeline; don't optimise the wrong axis.
- **"Forced errors" risk.** Pressing for editorial polish in the analytical phase risks: dropping findings to fit prose flow, manufacturing observations for cohesion, skipping evidence that doesn't fit a constructed narrative. Finding-surfacing must not be compromised for fluency.

The editorial phase is later — after all clusters have their first-pass combined documents. At that point, with the corpus complete, considered editorial revision can happen.
