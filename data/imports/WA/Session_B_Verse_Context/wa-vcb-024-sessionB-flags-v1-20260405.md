### The analytical question

archō carries a dual semantic range that is not a translation accident — it is a structural feature of the Greek term. The same word means:

**(a) Inceptive:** the moment at which an inner state or expression begins — sorrow begins (Mar 14:33), faith begins (Mat 14:30), denial begins (Mar 14:71), repentance begins. archō marks the threshold, the inception-point of inner-being movement.

**(b) Ruling:** the exercise of authority over others — "rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them" (Mar 10:42); "the root of Jesse will arise to rule the Gentiles; in him will the Gentiles hope" (Rom 15:12).

These are not two unrelated homographs. They share etymological and conceptual ground. The question the verse corpus raises is: **Is the connection between beginning/initiating and ruling/authority theologically significant for the inner being?** Put differently: does the one who can begin (initiate, originate, set in motion) share something essential with the one who rules (governs, has authority over)?

### Relevant verse evidence

**Inceptive group (2782-001) — selected anchors and key verses:**

- Mar 14:33 — *"began to be greatly distressed and troubled"* — archō marks the inception of Jesus's Gethsemane anguish. The beginning of inner-being suffering is the moment it crosses the threshold from potential to actual.
- Mar 14:71 — *"began to invoke a curse on himself and to swear, 'I do not know this man'"* — archō marks the inception of Peter's denial-under-oath. The beginning is the point of no return.
- Luk 3:8 — *"do not begin to say to yourselves, 'We have Abraham as our father'"* — archō marks the inception of a self-deceiving inner movement. The warning is precisely against the beginning — the moment false security takes root.
- 1Pe 4:17 — *"judgment to begin at the household of God"* — archō applied to the inception of divine judgment. The beginning-point of judgment is itself a statement about order, priority, and the structure of divine authority.

**Ruling group (semantic extension within 2782-001):**

- Mar 10:42 — *"rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their great ones exercise authority over them"* — archō used in the ruling sense, immediately followed by Jesus's inversion: greatness = servanthood. The ruler-who-lords-it-over contrasted with the one who begins from below.
- Rom 15:12 — *"the root of Jesse will arise to rule the Gentiles; in him will the Gentiles hope"* — archō as messianic rule, with hope as the inner-being consequence. The arising/ruling of the Messiah is the ground of the Gentiles' inner-being reorientation.

### Suggested Session B exploration

**Primary question:** Does the semantic convergence of archō — inception and ruling — reveal something about how authority operates within the inner being? Specifically:

1. **Authority as initiation:** Is the capacity to *begin* something (to bring into being, to inaugurate) a form of inner authority? The person who can begin — whose inner state crosses the threshold into action, expression, or decision — exercises a form of self-rule. archō in its inceptive sense may be naming the moment of inner sovereignty over one's own states and responses.

2. **The beginning as a moral threshold:** The inceptive use consistently names morally or spiritually significant beginnings — the beginning of sorrow, denial, repentance, false confidence. These are not neutral starting points; they are points of inner-being commitment. The beginning locks in a direction. This structural feature of archō may illuminate how the soul exercises or forfeits authority over its own course.

3. **The ruler who begins vs. the ruler who lords it over:** Mar 10:42–45 uses archō in the ruling sense, then immediately defines true greatness as servanthood. The contrast is between authority that begins from power over others and authority that begins from service to others. The two semantic ranges of archō are brought into explicit tension in this passage.

4. **Messianic rule and inner-being hope (Rom 15:12):** The arising-to-rule of the Messiah produces hope in the Gentiles. The connection between the exercise of legitimate authority and the inner-being capacity for hope is worth tracing: how does the existence of just rule create the inner conditions for hope?

**What this implies for the programme's understanding of the inner being:**
The convergence of archō's two senses raises the possibility that *authority within the soul* is fundamentally connected to *initiation* — the capacity to begin, to set in motion, to inaugurate an inner movement. If so, the seat of inner authority may be identifiable not by what the person controls, but by what they originate. This is a structural insight that could reshape how the programme characterises the inner-being architecture of authority across the full cluster.
