## 1. The Discovery

During VCB-019 flag resolution, the researcher identified that Gen 27:44 — *"stay with him a while, until your brother's fury turns away"* — had been set aside in the Session B analysis of H2534 (*che.mah*, fury) in the anger registry (Registry 4, VCB-001). The verse was not missing from the corpus; it was classified as not inner-being relevant and placed in the set-aside list.

The researcher's observation: *ad* in Gen 27:44 frames fury as a time-bounded, changeable inner-being state. This is a genuine inner-being claim about the nature of fury — not merely that it exists, but that it has a natural limit and can subside. This dimension was not captured in any of the three Session B groups for H2534, nor in the groups for H0639G (*aph*), where Gen 27:45 — the adjacent verse carrying the same insight — was also set aside.

**Observation (researcher):** "That is actually quite frightening."

Subsequent investigation confirmed:
- Gen 27:44 (H2534) and Gen 27:45 (H0639G) — both set aside
- Est 2:1 (*"when the anger of King Ahasuerus had abated"*) — also set aside for H2534
- The three Session B groups for H2534 do not name fury's transience as a dimension
- The Session B analysis built on a systematically incomplete Verse Context corpus

Subsequent re-evaluation of VCB-019 flags confirmed the drift pattern: Claude AI had applied a filter tighter than the governing instruction requires, systematically setting aside verses where inner-being content is carried indirectly — through temporal framing, structural relationships, physical signs of inner states, or liturgical acts originating in inner devotion.

## 2. The Nature of the Error

### 2.1 What the error is

**Filter drift** — a systematic tightening of the relevance filter below the standard established in the governing instruction. Specifically:

The governing instruction (§3.3) states: *"Where the filter decision is genuinely uncertain: retain. The cost of a missed inclusion is higher than the cost of retaining an uncertain verse."*

The governing instruction (§3.5) states that a grammatical particle passes the filter if it *"qualifies the scope of an inner-being condition"* — establishing time-bounds, limits, or turning points on an inner state meets this criterion.

The governing instruction (§3.4) states that an act of expression passes the filter *"if the act plausibly originates in an inner state"* — covenant liturgical acts meet this criterion.

The actual classification behaviour was: setting aside verses where inner-being content is carried through temporal framing, physical signs, or liturgical acts — treating these as "context" rather than the term's inner-being engagement. This is a misapplication of the filter, prioritising directness of inner-being naming over the instruction's explicit retention preference.

### 2.2 What the error is not

This is not a misunderstanding of what the inner being is. The filter correctly identified inner-being material in the majority of verses. The error is at the margin — the borderline cases that the instruction says must be retained were being set aside. The cost of this error accumulates precisely at the boundary where the most nuanced and structurally significant inner-being data lives.

### 2.3 Why it is structurally significant

The verses that are set aside at the margin are often the verses that carry the most analytically important content about the inner being — not its most intense or visible manifestations, but its structural characteristics: its temporariness, its reversibility, its relationship to divine action, its embodied signs, its liturgical expressions. These are the dimensions that distinguish a full characterisation of an inner-being state from a partial one.

The Gen 27:44 example is paradigmatic: the most dramatic expressions of fury (fury at its peak, fury driving violent action, fury as a settled disposition) were all correctly retained and grouped. The one dimension that was missed — fury as a state with a natural limit — is precisely the dimension that would have been invisible in the retained corpus. It required looking at a verse where fury is already subsiding, or is expected to subside, to see it.

## 3. Known Scope of the Problem

### 3.1 Confirmed in VCB-001 (anger registry)

- H2534 (*che.mah*): Gen 27:44 and Est 2:1 set aside — fury's transience unrecorded
- H0639G (*aph*): Gen 27:45 set aside — anger's turning-away unrecorded
- Both are large-corpus terms (H2534: 115 verses, H0639G: 212 verses) in the anger registry
- VCB-001 ran under poor conditions: three rejected extract versions, context window collapse mid-session, recovery session
- Session B for anger is complete and built on this incomplete foundation

### 3.2 Confirmed in VCB-019 (current batch)

Five of twelve flags in VCB-019 were revised following re-evaluation:
- DF-001 (H5704 *ad*): 13 verses retained — temporal boundaries on anger, fury, guilt, endurance, spiritual renewal
- DF-003 (H2939 *te.em*): 3 verses retained — inner-being endpoint governs physical act's duration
- DF-004 (H2953 *te.phar*): 2 verses retained — physical signs of inner-being verdicts
- DF-009 (H0370 *a.yin*): 5 verses retained — interrogative directing toward inner capacities
- DF-012 (H4635 *ma.a.re.khet*): 9 verses retained — covenant liturgical acts

This represents a 42% revision rate among flags — materially higher than would be expected if the filter had been correctly applied throughout.

### 3.3 Unknown scope across remaining batches

The programme is approximately 80% complete (81 of 181 registries). The scope of filter drift across all completed batches is unknown. It cannot be assessed without systematic validation of the set-aside verse populations across all classified terms.

## 4. What Was Lost

### 4.1 At the term level

For H2534 (*che.mah*) specifically, the following inner-being dimension is absent from the current Session B analysis:

**Fury as a time-bounded, changeable inner state.** The current groups capture: (1) divine fury as consuming fire spending itself, (2) fury transforming countenance and driving extreme action, (3) habitual fury as settled disposition. What is missing: the general characteristic that fury — whether human or divine — has a temporal horizon, can subside, and is not a permanent condition of the person. This is a structural claim about the nature of the inner state, not merely a situational observation.

### 4.2 At the registry level

The anger registry analysis (Registry 4, Session B complete) was built on a Verse Context corpus with unknown but non-zero rate of missed inclusions at the borderline. The groups produced are accurate within their scope — but the scope is incomplete. The programme's characterisation of anger as an inner-being state is therefore also incomplete.

### 4.3 At the programme level

The inner being as characterised across all completed Session B analyses rests on Verse Context classifications produced by a filter that was systematically tighter than the governing instruction requires. The dimension most likely to be missing is: **the structural and temporal characteristics of inner-being states** — how they change, subside, intensify, are bounded, are signalled outwardly, and are enacted liturgically. The most visible, intense, and directly named inner-being content was correctly captured. The relational, structural, and transitional dimensions were systematically under-captured.

## 5. Implications by Pipeline Stage

### 5.1 Verse Context (current stage)

**Immediate action taken:** VCB-019 flags revised; batch ready for patch construction. Filter discipline corrected for remaining batches.

**Ongoing risk:** The corrected filter applies from VCB-019 onward. Batches VCB-001 through VCB-018 were classified under the drifted filter and have not been reviewed. Their set-aside verse populations may contain missed inclusions.

**Magnitude estimate:** If 5–15% of set-aside verses across completed batches are borderline cases that should have been retained, and the programme has classified approximately 45,000–55,000 verses across VCB-001 to VCB-018, then the affected verse count is plausibly in the range of several hundred to several thousand verses. This is a rough estimate only — actual scope requires validation.

### 5.2 Session B (analysis stage)

**Registry 4 (anger):** Session B complete. The analysis rests on an incomplete Verse Context corpus. The fury-transience dimension is demonstrably absent. Other structural dimensions of anger may also be absent.

**Registries where Session B is complete:** All registries in VCB-001 through approximately VCB-018 that have been processed through Session B. Their analyses are accurate within their scope but that scope is incomplete.

**Registries not yet at Session B:** Not yet affected at Session B level — but their Verse Context classifications carry the same risk.

### 5.3 Session D (synthesis stage)

**Not yet begun.** Session D synthesis for C01 (Anthropological Core) was noted as the nearest cluster to triggering. If Session D proceeds on analyses built on incomplete Verse Context data, the synthesis will reproduce and amplify the incompleteness.

**Critical implication:** Session D is designed to synthesise cross-registry patterns. The structural and temporal dimensions of inner-being states — precisely what was under-captured — are the dimensions most likely to produce cross-registry patterns of programme-level significance. Fury's transience is not a local fact about *che.mah*; it is potentially a characteristic of the anger cluster, and possibly a principle about how inner-being states in general operate. If this dimension is absent from the underlying data, Session D cannot discover it.

## 7. Considerations and Possible Future Steps

### 7.1 Immediate — no further action required before completion

The corrected filter applies from VCB-019 onward. The remaining ~20% of registries will be classified under the corrected standard. No immediate interruption to the programme is required.

### 7.2 After all 181 registries are classified — validation design

Before Session D synthesis begins, a structured validation of the Verse Context set-aside populations should be conducted. The design question is what a meaningful validation looks like given the corpus size.

**Possible approaches:**

**(A) Targeted term validation** — select the most analytically significant terms from each completed batch (the large-corpus anchor terms, the terms with the highest set-aside rates, the terms that carry the most structurally important inner-being content) and re-inspect their set-aside populations specifically for temporal, transitional, physical-sign, and liturgical dimensions. This is feasible and targeted but may miss systematic errors in lower-profile terms.

**(B) Pattern-based validation** — identify the specific linguistic patterns most associated with the drift (temporal connectors bounding inner states, physical features as signs of inner verdicts, liturgical acts with covenantal origins, interrogatives directing toward inner-being subjects) and scan all set-aside populations programmatically for verses matching these patterns. This surfaces candidates for re-inspection without requiring full re-reading of every set-aside verse.

**(C) Anger registry re-run** — given that the anger registry is the confirmed affected case with a completed Session B, a targeted re-inspection of the set-aside populations for the anger registry's largest terms (H0639G, H2534, H2734, H2740) and a supplementary Verse Context patch for any newly retained verses, followed by a Session B addendum for the anger registry, would address the most concrete known gap.

**(D) Full re-classification of VCB-001 through VCB-018** — complete re-run of all classified batches under the corrected filter. This is the most thorough option but is resource-intensive and would delay Session D significantly.

**Researcher consideration:** The choice between these approaches depends on the programme's tolerance for residual uncertainty in the Session D synthesis versus the cost of extended validation. Options A and B together may represent the best balance — targeted and systematic without requiring a full re-run.

### 7.3 Session B — anger registry specific action

Given that the fury-transience dimension is demonstrably absent from the anger registry Session B, and given that this dimension is analytically significant for the programme's characterisation of anger as a whole, consideration should be given to:

- A supplementary Verse Context patch for H2534 and H0639G reinstating the confirmed borderline verses (Gen 27:44, Gen 27:45, Est 2:1, and any others identified in validation)
- A Session B addendum for the anger registry addressing the structural/temporal dimension of anger that is currently unrepresented
- A note in the Session D synthesis pointer for Cluster C07 (anger) flagging the incompleteness of the current Session B analysis

### 7.4 Governing instruction — no change required

The governing instruction (v2.4) already states the correct standard in §3.3 and §3.5. The problem was not in the instruction but in its application. No instruction change is required.

**However:** consideration should be given to adding an explicit note in a future version of the governing instruction documenting this discovery — specifically naming the categories of inner-being content most at risk of filter drift (temporal/structural/transitional dimensions) and the correction applied from VCB-019 onward. This would make the learning visible to any future instance of Claude AI working within this programme.

### 7.5 Session D — deferred but flagged

Session D synthesis should not begin for any cluster until the validation described in 7.2 has been completed for the registries in that cluster. The fury-transience dimension is a programme-level pattern that may surface across multiple registries and clusters — it should not be reconstructed from an incomplete foundation.

## 8. Characterisation of the Finding

This is a material discovery. It is not a catastrophic failure — the programme's core data is substantially sound and the analytical work of Session B is accurate within its scope. But it is not a minor error either.

**What makes it material:**

1. It is systematic, not random. The drift operated in a consistent direction — against retention of borderline cases — and affected a specific category of inner-being content (structural/temporal/transitional dimensions).

2. It affects the most analytically significant data. The structural characteristics of inner-being states — how they change, subside, are bounded, are signalled, and are enacted — are exactly what cross-registry synthesis in Session D is designed to discover. These are not peripheral data points; they are the data that would generate programme-level findings.

3. It has already propagated into Session B. The anger registry Session B is complete and its incompleteness is confirmed. Other completed Session B analyses carry unknown levels of the same incompleteness.

4. It was not detectable without the specific challenge that surfaced it. The error was invisible in the retained corpus — the retained verses were correctly classified. The error was only visible by looking at what was missing, which required a specific and knowledgeable challenge from the researcher.

5. It establishes a validation requirement before programme-level synthesis can be trusted.

**What makes it recoverable:**

1. The correct standard is already in the governing instruction.
2. The corrected filter applies from VCB-019 onward — the remaining 20% will be clean.
3. The affected data (set-aside verse populations) is preserved and available for re-inspection.
4. The Session B group structures are accurate within their scope — supplementary patches can add missing dimensions without requiring full re-analysis.
5. The most important anger registry terms are large-corpus and well-documented — their set-aside populations can be inspected systematically.
