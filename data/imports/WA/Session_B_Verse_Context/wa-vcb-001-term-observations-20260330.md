**Grouping rationale:** Two groups — inner orientation toward detestable objects (heart/soul/eyes directed toward them) vs. inner character formed by attachment to them (becoming like what is loved). - Session B should note the progression visible across these terms: *orientation of gaze/desire* → *delight* → *conformation of character*. This may be a structural observation about how abomination as a characteristic enters and shapes the inner person.
- The high set-aside count (17 of 26) reflects the term's primary cultic-external register. The inner-being content is theologically rich but statistically sparse relative to the total corpus.

### H8262 shiq.qets (mti_term_id: 246) | 6 verses | 6 relevant

**Filter yield:** 100%. Every verse is relevant — an unusually high yield. The verb form consistently engages the inner person's disposition of revulsion and its effects.

**Grouping rationale:** Three groups — commanded inner revulsion (toward external objects), self-directed inner defilement (making oneself detestable through transgression), and divine non-revulsion (God does not abhor the afflicted). Three directions of the same inner dynamic.

- Psa 22:24 is the most theologically rich verse: it establishes that divine moral character does not include revulsion toward suffering per se. This is potentially important for Session B's treatment of what the abomination characteristic *excludes* — God's inner orientation toward the afflicted is explicitly not abhorrent, regardless of the person's condition.
- The Levitical commands (Lev 11:43, 20:25 — do not make yourselves detestable) name an inward-reflexive dynamic: violation of God's commands has a corrupting effect on the inner person's own moral character. This is consistent with the *shiq.quts* observation about conformation — the inner person is shaped by what it does.
- Session B should examine the relationship between *shiq.qets* (verb, action of detesting/making detestable) and *she.qets* (noun, the detestable thing) — they form a complementary pair.

### H8441 to.e.vah (mti_term_id: 250) | 112 verses | ~35 relevant | multiple borderlines

**Filter yield:** ~31%. The largest corpus in the registry. The high set-aside count reflects the term's dominant cultic-legal and historical usage. The inner-being relevant verses are concentrated in Proverbs, Jeremiah (conscience passages), and Ezekiel (inner orientation and self-recognition passages).

**Grouping rationale:** Five groups — the maximum threshold. Consolidation was considered and rejected: the five groups represent genuinely distinct inner-being engagements (divine moral assessment of character, worship from corrupted inner condition, failure of inner shame, inner grief/self-loathing, divine inner abhorrence). Each involves a different aspect of how abomination operates in or around the inner person.

**Pre-analytical observations:**
- *To.e.vah* is the most analytically rich term in this registry. Its 112 verses span the broadest range of inner-being engagements and reveal a complex, multi-dimensional characteristic.
- **The Proverbs cluster (Group 1)** is uniquely valuable: it consistently locates abomination in the inner life — thoughts (Pro 15:26), heart (Pro 11:20, 16:5, 26:25), dispositions (Pro 13:19), character (Pro 3:32). This is the most explicit inner-being engagement of any term in the registry. Pro 15:26 ("the thoughts of the wicked are an abomination to the Lord") is the analytical anchor: it asserts that the inner cognitive-moral life itself, not merely its outward expression, is what God regards as abominable.
- **The conscience failure group (Group 3)** — Jer 6:15 and 8:12 — is analytically significant and possibly unique in the biblical corpus: the inability to feel shame over abomination is itself a characteristic of moral corruption. The conscience has become unresponsive. This may be one of the most important observations for Session B: abomination as a characteristic involves not only the presence of detestable inner states but the *absence* of appropriate inner response to them.
- **The inner grief/self-loathing group (Group 4)** shows the restorative dynamic: Eze 9:4 (those who sigh and groan over abominations are preserved), Eze 36:31 (self-loathing as part of restoration). Inner moral grief in response to abomination is the beginning of the opposite movement. Session B should note the structural relationship between Groups 3 and 4 — they represent opposite conditions of the inner person in relation to abomination.
- **The worship group (Group 2)** — Pro 15:8, Pro 28:9 — establishes that the inner moral condition of the worshipper determines whether their worship is abominable. This positions abomination not merely as a characteristic of the person but as a filter through which their religious acts are evaluated.
- **Cross-registry note visible at Verse Context:** The Proverbs uses of *to.e.vah* appear to build a coherent inner taxonomy: abominable thoughts (Pro 15:26) → abominable heart (Pro 16:5, 26:25) → abominable character (Pro 3:32) → abominable way of life (Pro 15:9). Session B should examine whether this represents a progression or a set of simultaneous aspects.
- The five-group structure suggests *to.e.vah* may require special handling in Session B — it is not a simple characteristic but a multi-level inner-being concept. This term alone may justify a more extended analytical treatment than most.

**Flags:** High analytical priority for Session B. Recommend treating this term as the anchor term for the registry's Session B analysis.

**Pre-analytical observations:**

## Registry 001 — Cross-term observations

**Pattern 1 — Multi-term convergence on pride and heart:**
Pro 16:5 (*to.e.vah* — arrogant heart is abomination), Amos 6:8 (*ta.av* — God abhors the pride of Jacob), Jer 49:16 (*tiph.le.tset* — pride of heart as source of deception). Multiple terms in this registry independently reach the same inner-being characteristic: pride/arrogance of heart as what God most directly finds abominable. This convergence should be noted in Session B as potentially one of the registry's core analytical findings.

**Pattern 2 — The conscience dynamic:**
*To.e.vah* Group 3 (Jer 6:15, 8:12 — no shame) paired with *to.e.vah* Group 4 (Eze 9:4, 36:31 — grief and self-loathing). The registry contains both the failure of inner moral responsiveness to abomination (no shame) and the presence of inner moral responsiveness to it (sighing and groaning). These are opposite inner conditions. The contrast is available to Session B as a structural observation about the characteristic.

**Pattern 3 — Orientation, delight, conformation:**
Visible across *shiq.quts* (H8251): Eze 20:7 (eyes feast on), Isa 66:3 (soul delights in), Hos 9:10 (became like what they loved). Three sequential moments of the inner dynamic of idolatrous attachment. Not a finding but an observation for Session B to examine.

**Pattern 4 — Commanded revulsion as inner disposition:**
Multiple terms include commands to hold certain inner dispositions: *shiq.qets* (Deu 7:26 — utterly detest), *ta.av* (Deu 23:7 — do not abhor the Edomite). These establish that in the biblical framework, inner dispositions of revulsion and acceptance are considered commandable — they are not merely spontaneous emotions but appropriate or inappropriate inner orientations that can be governed. This may be significant for Session B's treatment of inner-being characteristics as moral rather than merely psychological.

**Pattern 5 — Abhorrence as directional:**
*Za.am* and *ta.av* both show that the inner disposition of moral revulsion can be rightly or wrongly directed. Mic 3:9 (detesting justice), Amo 5:10 (abhorring truth-telling) show inversion — the capacity is present but misdirected. Pro 119:163 shows the properly directed form. This structural observation may be important for Session B's treatment of how inner characteristics can be corrupted or misdirected rather than simply present or absent.
