The term set is the largest in the Phase 1 series so far — 29 terms across Hebrew and Greek, covering the full spectrum from the physical-somatic root (aph: nostrils) through the thermal inner state (che.mah: heat/rage; cha.rah: to be incensed) to the settled dispositional noun (orgē: wrath as temperament) and the volatile passion-noun (thumos: soul-passion). ### orgizō — to anger — G3710 — Greek

**What this term means**

orgizō is a verb (8 occurrences) meaning to be angry or enraged, to feel and express strong displeasure and hostility, ranging from petty human anger to the righteous anger of God. The active form means to provoke to anger or irritate; the middle/passive means to be angry, indignant, enraged. The LSJ range covers making someone angry, growing angry, and being in a state of anger — the middle passive form is described as more frequent, suggesting that in classical usage anger was typically treated as something one is in rather than something one does. The related words cluster — orgē (wrath), orgilos (quick-tempered), parorgizō (to anger alongside), parorgismos (anger, provoked state) — is a tight root family naming the noun, the disposition, the causative, and the compound provocation form of the same inner-being state.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Verb Present Middle Imperative 2nd Plural — specifically the imperative of Eph 4:26: be angry. The middle imperative is a command to enter into the angry state as a subject with full personal involvement in it. This is the most theologically significant grammatical form in the entire anger cluster: the imperative mood applied to a middle-voice anger verb means that anger is not merely permitted but commanded, and the subject is the one both acting and being acted upon by their own anger. The present tense indicates ongoing or repeated action. Eph 4:26 is the only NT imperative of orgizō.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'anger' and 'be angry' do not convey the classical Greek sense of orgē/orgizō as a natural impulse or propensity — a disposition, a settled inner bent of the person. The LSJ makes clear that the primary sense of orgē is temperament or mood before it narrows to anger; orgizō is the activation of that disposition. English treats anger as an emotion-event; Greek treats it as the arousal of a dispositional orientation. The distinction is significant: orgē/orgizō describes a category of inner-being disposition, not merely an episodic emotional reaction.

**Core verse observations**

Eph 4:26 is the structural verse for the entire anger cluster: be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger (parorgismos). Two things are commanded — the permission/command to be angry (orgizō, present middle imperative) and the prohibition on letting the day end while the provoked anger state (parorgismos) persists. The verse distinguishes the rightful arousal of anger from the sin of retaining it — anger as a legitimate inner-being response, anger's retention as the dangerous transition.

Mat 5:22 reveals the moral gradation of anger in Jesus's teaching: everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment. The present participle (being angry) combined with the liability for judgment shows that orgizō describes a sustained inner orientation toward another person, not a momentary flash. The verse is often contrasted with Eph 4:26 — the two together define the moral boundaries of anger: righteous anger does not persist; anger directed at a person in sustained hostility incurs judgment.

Mar 3:5 is the only verse combining Jesus's anger with grief: he looked around at them with anger, grieved at their hardness of heart. orgē and lupē (grief) appear as simultaneous inner states — anger at wrong and grief at the condition causing it. This pairing is the NT's fullest picture of righteous anger as a complex inner-being state with both a moral-reactive dimension (anger at injustice) and a relational-compassionate dimension (grief for the person).

Luk 15:28 is the elder son refusing to enter the celebration — he was angry. The orgizō here describes a sustained state that produces a behavioural refusal: the inner anger becomes an outer boundary of exclusion. The father then goes out and entreats him, revealing the relational damage that retained anger creates.

### parorgizō — to anger — G3949 — Greek

**Data quality note**

The term block in the source file contains orgizō data rather than parorgizō data. The two verses provided (Rom 10:19, Eph 6:4) are confirmed parorgizō occurrences. parorgizō is a para-prefixed compound of orgizō meaning to provoke to anger alongside or further — the prefix implies an anger that is triggered by or directed toward a specific agent.

**Core verse observations**

Eph 6:4 is the primary inner-being verse: fathers, do not provoke your children to anger. The causative force of parorgizō is clear — the parent's action produces the child's anger state. The relational context is formative: the verse addresses the anger-generating power of authority relationships, specifically the capacity of parental conduct to produce a sustained provoked state in a child. The verse thus identifies anger as something that can be induced and formed in another person through the patterns of authority relationships — a relational mechanism of inner-being state formation.

Rom 10:19 cites Moses's statement that God will provoke Israel to jealousy and anger through a foolish nation — divine use of the parorgizō mechanism as a covenantal instrument.

### orgē — wrath — G3709 — Greek

**What this term means**

orgē is a noun (35 occurrences) meaning wrath, anger, the feeling and expression of strong displeasure and hostility; ranging from petty human anger to God's righteous anger. The meaning section notes two primary registers: anger/indignation/wrath as inner state; and vengeance/punishment as its enacted consequence. The LSJ grounds the term in natural impulse or propensity, hence temperament, disposition, mood — the classical sense places orgē at the level of character formation, not momentary reaction. The related words cluster is identical to orgizō — they share the same root family.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Genitive Singular Feminine — used in a qualifying or partitive construction. This is appropriate to orgē's primary NT function: it regularly appears as a qualifying noun (the wrath of God, day of wrath, children of wrath) naming the character of a state or the nature of a day or relationship.

**What the English translation conceals**

'Wrath' in English has moved almost entirely into divine/judicial register. orgē covers both settled human anger as a disposition (Eph 4:31: let all bitterness and wrath and anger be put away) and divine judicial anger (Rom 1:18). English 'wrath' tends to sound exclusively divine; when applied to human inner states it sounds archaic. The LSJ's primary sense — natural impulse, temperament — is entirely absent from standard English translations.

**Core verse observations**

Eph 4:31 lists orgē alongside thumos, bitterness, clamour, slander, and malice as states to be put away — the pairing of orgē (settled disposition) and thumos (passionate outburst) in the same list confirms they are distinguishable states within the anger domain.

Col 3:8 parallels Eph 4:31: put away anger (orgē), wrath (thumos), malice, slander, and obscene talk. The order orgē before thumos may reflect the dispositional–explosive sequence: the settled state generates the outburst.

Jam 1:19-20 is the wisdom summary of the cluster: slow to speak, slow to anger (orgē), for the anger of man does not produce the righteousness of God. The genitive construction (anger of man) sets human orgē against the righteousness of God — not merely a prohibition on anger but a statement about what human anger is constitutionally incapable of producing.

Mar 3:5 (with orgizō above) provides the definitive inner-being picture of Jesus's anger.

Rom 12:19 commands leaving vengeance to the wrath of God (orgē theou) — placing orgē in the divine register while implicitly freeing the human inner-being from the burden of carrying wrath-as-vengeance.

### thumos — wrath — G2372 — Greek

**What this term means**

thumos is a noun (18 occurrences) meaning wrath, fury, anger, rage; a state of intense displeasure based on real or perceived wrong. The meaning section notes a primary sense of the soul or mind, and hence a strong passion or emotion of the mind — thumos is explicitly placed at the soul level before the specific anger sub-sense is reached. The LSJ is extraordinary in its range: soul/spirit as the principle of life, feeling, and thought; breath and life (thumos departing at death); spirit and strength; desire and appetite; mind, temper, and will; spirit and courage; the seat of anger; the heart as seat of joy, grief, fear, and love; and seat of thought. thumos in Homer and classical Greek is the whole inner life of a person — the term reaches its fullest inner-being breadth in the entire Phase 1 series.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Genitive Singular Masculine. Like orgē, thumos regularly functions as a qualifying noun in genitival constructions. The masculine gender (compared to orgē's feminine) is a grammatical observation only, but thumos's dominance in the Revelation eschatological passages (wine of God's wrath, bowls of wrath) may reflect its more visceral and intense sense.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'wrath' applied to thumos loses the soul/spirit primary sense entirely. thumos is not merely passionate anger — it is the vital, animating soul-principle expressing itself at maximum intensity. The related word makrothumōs (patiently, long-suffering, from makro + thumos = long-souled) reveals the inner-being depth: patience/long-suffering in Greek is the stretching of the thumos across time. English 'patience' contains none of this. Similarly thumomacheō (to quarrel) names the thumos fighting itself out in conflict.

**Core verse observations**

Gal 5:20 lists thumos among the works of the flesh alongside enmity, strife, jealousy, fits of anger, rivalries, dissensions, and divisions — thumos in the plural (fits of anger) names the episodic outburst form.

Eph 4:31 and Col 3:8 pair orgē and thumos as states to be put away — the dispositional (orgē) and the volatile (thumos) together constitute the full anger domain.

2Cor 12:20 lists thumos in a community-pathology catalogue — the same pattern as eritheia in the ambition entry; the inner state generating communal disorder.

Heb 11:27 uses thumos for the anger of the king (Pharaoh) that Moses did not fear — human political rage as a form of thumos.

Rev 14:10, 16:19, 19:15 use the compound thumos tēs orgēs theou (the fury of the wrath of God) — the two Greek anger nouns combined in an eschatological intensification; the volatile passion (thumos) of the settled wrath (orgē) of God.

### aph — face / nose / anger — H0639 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

aph is a noun (278 occurrences) with three interlocked meanings: nostril/nose, face, anger. It is the most fundamentally somatic term in the anger cluster — anger is located in the physical organ of the nose and face, expressed through flared nostrils, hot breath, and the physical appearance of the face. The related words — a.naph (to be angry, from the same root) and a.na.phah (heron, presumably named for its nose-like beak) — confirm that the nose is the primary physical referent. STEP divides the term into three sub-entries (H0639G anger, H0639H nose, H0639I face) — all three have the same occurrence count (278) and identical related words, confirming they are anchor-sense divisions of a single term.

**What the root reveals**

The dual form (aph dual = nostrils, literally the two nostrils) is used in some anger contexts — anger expressed through both nostrils equally. The singular form covers nose as physical organ and anger as the inner state expressed through it. The idiom slow to anger is literally long of nose (erek aph) — a person whose nostrils take a long time to flare. Hot anger is literally burning of nostrils. This is the most direct inner-being to somatic-expression connection in the Hebrew anger vocabulary.

**Core verse observations**

Gen 4:5-6 is the foundational inner-being diagnostic: Cain's face fell — and God's question why are you angry, and why has your face fallen immediately links the inner anger state (cha.rah) to its somatic expression (the fallen face, aph as facial register). The face and the inner state are read together.

Exo 32:10-19 is the paradigm case of the anger of Moses: God's wrath burns hot (aph); Moses intercedes; Moses's own anger then burns hot when he sees the calf (v.19, aph). The same term covers God's anger and Moses's anger — the parallel is deliberate. The physical expression (throwing and breaking the tablets) follows immediately on the inner burning.

Exo 34:6 is the self-description of God: slow to anger (erek aph) — long of nose. This is the repeated divine character formula across the OT (Num 14:18; Psa 86:15; 103:8; Jon 4:2; Joel 2:13) and represents the theological counter-image of anger: the restraint of the inner burning through a long-of-nose disposition.

Pro 14:17, 15:1, 15:18, 16:32, 19:11, 22:24, 29:22, 30:33 form the Proverbs wisdom cluster on anger management. Pro 16:32 (whoever is slow to anger is better than the mighty) places the long-of-nose disposition above military conquest as a form of inner power. Pro 30:33 (pressing the nose produces blood; pressing anger produces strife) is the only physiological metaphor for the anger-to-strife causation sequence.

Job 36:13 is a critical inner-being diagnostic: the godless in heart cherish anger (aph). The cherishing of anger — making it a possession held and maintained — is named as a disposition of the godless heart.

### che.mah — rage — H2534 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

che.mah is a feminine noun (127 occurrences) meaning heat, rage, hot displeasure, indignation, anger, wrath, poison, bottles. The range is striking: the same term covers the thermal sense (heat, fever), the toxicological sense (venom, poison), and the anger sense (burning rage). The Aramaic equivalent is che.ma (H2528). The related words reveal the root cluster: chem.ah (curd, dairy fat — the creamy thick substance), heat (che.mah alt), and ya.cham (to conceive — sexual heat). The semantic overlap of thermal heat, venom, and rage in a single term is one of the strongest inner-being root observations in the cluster: anger in Hebrew is not merely metaphorically like heat or poison — the same root word names all three.

**What the root reveals**

The heat-venom-rage convergence in che.mah reveals how the Hebrew conceptual field of anger works: anger is an inner thermal state that is also, in its more intense forms, toxic — it operates like a poison in the system of the inner person and in the community. Psa 58:4 (venom like the venom of a serpent) and Deu 32:33 (poison of serpents) use the literal poison sense; Eze 3:14 (I went in bitterness in the heat of my spirit) applies the thermal sense to prophetic inner experience; Pro 6:34 (jealousy makes a man furious — che.mah — and he will not spare when he takes revenge) shows the connection between che.mah and the explosive, unrestrained response. The poison sense adds a further inner-being dimension: retained che.mah is internally toxic.

**Core verse observations**

Est 1:12 through 7:10 is the richest human narrative illustration of che.mah in the Old Testament: Ahasuerus's anger burning within him at Vashti's refusal; his anger abating after the decree (2:1); Haman filled with fury at Mordecai's non-obeisance (3:5); Haman again filled with wrath (5:9); the king rising in wrath from the feast (7:7); the king's wrath abating at Haman's execution (7:10). The narrative documents the full cycle of che.mah: trigger, filling, persistence, explosion, and resolution.

2Ki 5:12 shows Naaman's che.mah at the apparently trivial cure prescribed by Elisha — turned and went away in a rage. The trigger is perceived indignity; the inner state is che.mah; the response is departure. The narrative then shows che.mah reversed when Naaman obeys and is healed.

Psa 76:10 is theologically distinctive: the wrath (che.mah) of man shall praise you — God's ability to redirect and use human anger is stated. This is the only OT statement that che.mah, the heat-rage of human beings, can serve divine purposes.

Eze 3:14 locates che.mah in the prophet's own spirit: I went in bitterness in the heat of my spirit (be.cha.mat ru.ach). The compound uses both che.mah and ruach in a single construction — the heat of the spirit. This is the closest the OT comes to an explicit statement that che.mah is a spirit-level inner state.

### cha.rah — to be incensed — H2734 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

cha.rah is a verb (90 occurrences) meaning to be hot, furious, burn, become angry, be kindled. The stem range — Qal (to burn/kindle anger), Niphal (to be angry with/be incensed), Hiphil (to burn/kindle causatively), Hithpael (to heat oneself in vexation) — covers both the arising of anger and the self-directed heating of anger. The Hithpael is particularly significant: to heat oneself in vexation describes a reflexive inner process of stoking anger — the person as both agent and object of the anger-kindling.

**What the root reveals**

The related words — cha.ron (burning anger), cho.ri (burning), cha.rar (to scorch), and ta.cha.rah (to contend/heat oneself) — form a fire-root cluster that treats anger as combustion. The root is kinetic and progressive: kindling (cha.rah) generates burning anger (cha.ron) which becomes scorching (cha.rar). The Hithpael (heat oneself) and ta.cha.rah (heat oneself in vexation) show that the fire can be self-stoked — anger is not only a reactive state but can be cultivated through internal heating.

**Core verse observations**

Gen 4:5-6 links cha.rah (Cain's anger) to the face falling — the kindling of inner anger registers immediately in the somatic indicator of the countenance. God's diagnostic question (why are you angry?) uses cha.rah to name the inner state before addressing the face as its somatic expression.

Jon 4:1, 4:4, 4:9 is the fullest cha.rah narrative: Jonah is angry (vexed/incensed) at God's mercy toward Nineveh; God asks do you do well to be angry?; Jonah insists yes, angry enough to die. The Hithpael self-heating is present here — Jonah is actively maintaining and intensifying his vexation. The divine question do you do well to be angry? is the theological framework question for all human anger: is this anger righteous or self-generated?

Psa 37:1, 37:7-8 and Psa 24:19 (fret not yourself) use cha.rah in the Hithpael to address the self-heating of anger/envy — do not heat yourself at evildoers. The command targets the reflexive, self-directed stirring-up of anger as a distinctive inner-being discipline.

2Sa 22:8 and Psa 18:7 use cha.rah for God's anger expressed in cosmic earthquake — the earth reeled because he was angry. The theophanic extension of cha.rah into cosmic register confirms that the same kindling-of-inner-fire that applies to human anger applies to divine anger.

### qa.tsaph — be angry — H7107 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

qa.tsaph is a verb (35 occurrences) meaning to be displeased, be angry, fret oneself, be wroth. Qal: to be wroth, full of wrath, furious. Hiphil: to provoke to wrath. Hithpael: to put oneself in a rage, anger oneself. Related to qe.tseph (wrath, H7110A) and the rare homonym qe.tseph (splinter/broken twig, H7110B). The potential etymological link — wrath as the snapping of something — is not stated in the source data and should not be pressed without further evidence.

**Core verse observations**

The verse set is predominantly divine anger in judicial-historical contexts. For human anger: Exo 16:20 (Moses angry at those who kept manna), Lev 10:16 (Moses angry at Eleazar and Ithamar), Num 31:14 (Moses angry with commanders), 1Sa 29:4 (commanders angry with David), 2Ki 5:11 (Naaman angry at Elisha's cure), 2Ki 13:19 (Elisha angry at Joash), Est 2:21 (eunuchs angry and seeking to kill the king). The concentration on Moses as the human qa.tsaph subject is notable — Moses's anger is a recurrent narrative thread and is consistently related to failure of obedience or trust.

Isa 57:16-17 is the theological anchor: for I will not contend forever, nor will I always be angry (qa.tsaph) — for the spirit would grow faint before me. God's own restraint of qa.tsaph is grounded in care for the creature: unlimited sustained anger would destroy what God has made. This is the closest the OT comes to a theological limit placed on divine anger from the inside of God's own nature.

### ev.rah — fury — H5678 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

ev.rah is a feminine noun (34 occurrences) meaning outpouring, overflow, excess, fury, wrath, arrogance. The meaning range covers overflow/excess/outburst, arrogance, and overflowing rage or fury. The root connection to a.var (to pass over/cross, H5674) and to the ford/crossing vocabulary (ma.a.var, e.ver — side/beyond) is linguistically significant: ev.rah is anger that has overflowed its banks, crossed its boundary, become an excess. The connection between arrogance and overflowing rage in the same word is a critical inner-being observation — both are states of going beyond one's proper limits.

**Core verse observations**

Gen 49:7 is Jacob's curse on Simeon and Levi: cursed be their anger (aph) for it is fierce, and their wrath (ev.rah) for it is cruel. The pairing of aph (anger as somatic state) and ev.rah (fury as overflow/excess) in the same colon defines ev.rah as the intensification of anger beyond restraint.

Amo 1:11 is diagnostic for the inner-being persistence dimension: Edom's anger tore perpetually and he kept his wrath forever. The keeping/maintaining of ev.rah is the persistent inner-being state that makes Edom's judgment inevitable. The verb na.tar (to keep, guard) appears here — anger guarded like a possession becomes a defining inner-being orientation.

Pro 22:8 (the rod of his fury will fail) and Pro 14:35 (the king's wrath falls on one who acts shamefully) place ev.rah in the practical wisdom register of anger's social consequences.

### za.am — indignation — H2195 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

za.am is a masculine noun (22 occurrences) meaning anger, indignation. Its primarily divine-anger register distinguishes it from the more balanced human-divine distribution of aph and che.mah. The only clearly human inner-being occurrence is Jer 15:17: you had filled me with indignation — the prophet's own inner state of being filled with za.am. The meaning section gives no sub-definitions, suggesting a relatively tight semantic range.

Jer 15:17 is the only clear human-subject occurrence in the verse set: the prophet is filled with za.am — divine indignation has been poured into him as prophetic inner content. The filling of the inner person with divine anger as a prophetic experience is an unusual inner-being datum.

### cha.ron — burning anger — H2740 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

cha.ron is a masculine noun (41 occurrences) meaning anger, heat, burning of anger. The meaning section states it is always used of God's anger — making it the most exclusively divine-register anger term in the Hebrew cluster. It is related to cha.rah (to be incensed) and shares the fire/heat root cluster. The anger sense of cha.ron is always intense and judicial; it is never used as a neutral observation about divine disposition but always in the context of covenant violation or national sin.

**Core verse observations**

The cluster is dominated by the idiom charon aph (burning of nostrils) — the compound of the burning-anger noun with the nostril/anger noun. This compound appears throughout Exodus, Numbers, Deuteronomy, Jeremiah, and Lamentations as the standard formula for divine anger at covenant violation. It is the intensified form of the aph metaphor, combining the somatic (nostrils) with the thermal (burning) in a single concentrated expression.

Hos 11:9 is the most important cha.ron verse: I will not execute my burning anger; I will not again destroy Ephraim; for I am God and not a man, the Holy One in your midst. The restraint of cha.ron is grounded in God's divine nature versus human nature — the burning anger is real, but God's being-as-God means it does not execute as human burning anger would. This is the OT's most theologically direct statement about the restraint of divine inner anger.

### ru.ach: temper — H7307J — Hebrew

**What this term means**

This is sub-sense J of the vast ruach entry (389 occurrences) — the specific use of ruach to name temper and anger as inner dispositions. The meaning section identifies sub-sense 1c3 as temper/anger alongside impatience, patience, and agitation. The anchor form and anchor verse are specifically the temper/anger use. The verse set provided (Judg 8:3; Pro 14:29, 17:27, 25:28, 29:11; Ecc 7:9, 10:4; Mic 2:7) is a concentrated Proverbs-Ecclesiastes wisdom cluster on the management of the temper.

**What the root reveals**

The ruach-as-temper connection grounds anger explicitly at the spirit level — temper is a spirit-condition, not merely an emotional event. The compound in Eze 3:14 (be.cha.mat ru.ach — in the heat of the spirit) shows that che.mah and ruach work together to name the full inner-being anger state: the thermal content (che.mah) of the spirit (ruach).

**Core verse observations**

Pro 17:27 states that the one who has knowledge restrains his words and has a cool spirit (qar ru.ach). Cool spirit is the positive inner-being counterpart to the hot-temper vocabulary — the temperature of the ruach as a measure of its anger disposition.

Pro 25:28 compares a man without self-control to a city without walls — the ru.ach that gives vent to itself is a broken-down inner fortification. This is the most architecturally precise inner-being image for uncontrolled anger in the cluster.

Pro 29:11 states: a fool gives full vent to his spirit (ru.ach) but a wise man quietly holds it back. The inner-being discipline of containing the ru.ach — not suppressing it but holding it back strategically — is the anger-management wisdom of Proverbs.

Ecc 7:9 provides an inner-location statement: anger lodges in the heart of fools (be.cheq ke.si.lim). The word lodges (ya.nu.ach, from the root for rest/dwelling) means that anger makes a permanent dwelling in the heart of the fool — it does not pass through but settles.

### ka.a.s / ka.as — vexation — H3708 — Hebrew

These two nouns (ka.as H3708A and ka.a.s H3708B, 26 occurrences) name the vexation/grief/frustration state that is the subdued or inward-turned form of anger. The Job cluster (Job 5:2, 6:2, 10:17, 17:7) places vexation as an inner-being state that can kill the fool (5:2), weighs on the spirit (6:2), wears down the eyes (17:7). The Ecclesiastes cluster (1:18, 2:23, 5:17, 7:3, 11:10) associates vexation with the toilsome futility of life under the sun — anger turned inward becomes the vexation that characterises the qohelet's inner experience. Ecc 11:10 commands: remove vexation (ka.as) from your heart — naming it as a resident inner-being state that can and should be evacuated.

### na.tar — to keep — H5201 — Hebrew

na.tar in the anger cluster (10 occurrences) carries the idiomatic sense of keeping or guarding anger — maintaining a grudge. The two primary inner-being verses are Lev 19:18 (you shall not bear a grudge — lo ta.tor — against your neighbour) and Psa 103:9 (he will not keep his anger forever). na.tar applied to anger names the active inner process of maintaining and protecting anger as a possession — the opposite of the release that forgiveness involves. The link to Lev 19:18 connects this term directly to the love-your-neighbour command and to the forgiveness/tolerance project.

### paroxunō — to provoke — G3947 — Greek

paroxunō is a verb (2 occurrences) meaning to sharpen, to incite or stir up, to irritate, to provoke anger. ### na.ats — to spurn — H5006 — Hebrew

## Root and Stem Patterns Across the Term Set

The Hebrew anger vocabulary organises around three primary root clusters. The first is the fire cluster: cha.rah (to kindle/be incensed), cha.ron (burning anger), cha.mam (to warm), che.mah (heat/rage/venom), all expressing anger as an inner thermal process that kindles, burns, and can scorch. The second is the overflow cluster: ev.rah (fury as overflow/arrogance), a.var (to pass over/be angry), the ford and crossing vocabulary — anger as something that crosses its proper boundary. The third is the agitation cluster: ro.gez (turmoil/trembling), re.gaz (to enrage), ra.gaz (to tremble) — anger as inner shaking that connects to physical trembling.

The Greek anger vocabulary organises differently. orgē and orgizō name anger as a dispositional temperament — anger as a settled inner orientation that is aroused. thumos names anger at the level of the soul/spirit — the whole inner-life force expressing itself at maximum intensity. The parorgiz- compound family names the relational provocation mechanism — anger triggered by a specific agent. The paroxunō sharp-point family names anger as an inner sharpening/irritation process.

Together, the Hebrew and Greek terms reveal that anger in Scripture is simultaneously a somatic state (nostrils, face, heat, trembling), a spirit-level condition (ru.ach, thumos, paroxunō in the spirit), a dispositional orientation (orgē as temperament, erek aph as disposition), and a relational event (provocation, keeping/guarding, giving full vent). No single English word covers all four dimensions.

## Open Questions

1. **WORD-LEVEL: orge vs thumos distinction.** The source list correctly identifies orgē (settled disposition) and thumos (passionate outburst) as distinct. Eph 4:31 and Col 3:8 consistently list them together. Does the biblical data require a systematic distinction between anger as dispositional orientation and anger as volatile episode — or do the NT authors treat these as a single inner-being state at different intensity levels?

2. **aph:** The slow-to-anger formula (erek aph, long of nose) appears in the divine self-description at least eight times across the OT (Exo 34:6, Num 14:18, Psa 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Neh 9:17, Jon 4:2, Joel 2:13). Does the consistent use of the aph/nose idiom in the divine self-description carry theological weight for the imago dei dimension of anger management — that being slow to anger is a specifically God-like inner-being quality to cultivate?

3. **cha.rah/Hithpael:** The self-heating of anger (heating oneself in vexation, Hithpael) and the command not to fret/heat yourself (Psa 37:1, 37:7-8) identify a specific inner-being pathology: the deliberate stoking of anger through rumination. Is this pattern — anger that is cultivated rather than merely reactive — addressed consistently across the anger cluster vocabulary, and is it associated with specific consequential outcomes in the text?

4. **na.tar (to keep/guard anger):** Lev 19:18 prohibits na.tor (guarding a grudge) in the same command as lo tiqom (do not avenge). Does the grudge-keeping prohibition in Lev 19:18 provide a direct inner-being link between the anger entry and the forgiveness/tolerance project — specifically identifying retained anger (na.tar) as the inner-being precondition for both vengeance and the failure of reconciliation?

5. **che.mah / heat-venom-rage:** The che.mah root covers heat, venom/poison, and rage in a single term. Does the toxicological sub-sense (venom, poison) appear to function as a deliberate metaphor for retained anger in any of the attested verses — anger held internally becoming corrosive to the person and community around them?

6. **thumos / LSJ breadth:** In classical Greek thumos names the whole inner life — soul, spirit, desire, courage, thought, emotion. Its NT restriction to the anger/passion register is a narrowing of a much broader term. Does the NT anger usage of thumos retain any trace of the broader classical sense (the whole animating inner force) — or do the NT occurrences treat the anger/passion sub-sense as semantically independent from the wider classical range?

7. **Ecc 7:9 / anger lodging in the heart of fools:** The statement that anger lodges permanently in the heart of fools identifies a long-term inner-being formation outcome of habitual anger. Is this the anger entry's contribution to the transformation/neuroplasticity research — that sustained anger becomes a resident inner-being state with formation consequences?

8. **WORD-LEVEL: righteous anger.** The data includes two clear cases of commanded or justified anger: Eph 4:26 (be angry and do not sin), Mar 3:5 (Jesus angry at hardness of heart), Jon 4 (Jonah's anger interrogated rather than simply condemned), and Nehemiah's anger at injustice (Neh 5:6). Does the biblical data consistently distinguish righteous anger from unrighteous anger by the nature of its object (injustice, sin, hardness of heart) and its duration (does not persist into the night, Eph 4:26) — or are these distinctions present in some texts but not others?

9. **parorgizō (Eph 6:4) and induced anger:** The command not to provoke children to anger through parental authority identifies anger as something that can be induced and formed in another person through relational patterns. Does the biblical data elsewhere address the mechanism by which formative relationships produce persistent inner states — and is the anger addressed in Eph 6:4 presented as a passing reaction or as a settled disposition?

10. **DATA GAP: mēnis, enkotēma, cholos.** Three Greek terms in the STEP related word list have no term blocks. mēnis in particular is a classical Greek term of considerable weight (Homer's mēnis is the opening word of the Iliad — the wrath of Achilles). If these terms have NT occurrences they may need to be recovered from independent STEP searches before Phase 2 synthesis.
