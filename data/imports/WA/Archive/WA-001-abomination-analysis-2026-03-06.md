# WA-TBC — abomination — Phase 1 Analysis

**Word:** abomination  
**Registry ID:** TBC  
**Source List:** Missing Inner Being Words  
**Category:** Moral/Conscience  
**Date:** 2026-03-06  
**File Version:** 1  

This word provisionally qualifies as BORDERLINE under the inner being definition. The primary Hebrew term to.e.vah (H8441) and its Greek counterpart bdelugma (G0946) do name a moral-evaluative category with genuine inner-being dimensions — conscience, moral orientation toward God, and the internal disposition that makes a person or practice repugnant — but both terms are predominantly deployed to designate external acts, cultic objects, and prohibited practices rather than internal states. Key exceptions establish the inner-being case: Proverbs applies to.e.vah directly to thoughts (Pro 15:26), heart-dispositions (Pro 16:5, Pro 11:20), inner moral states (Pro 26:25), and the experience of conscience in relation to evil (Pro 13:19). Luke 16:15 grounds bdelugma in what God perceives of the human heart. The word is best understood as a relational moral category that frequently has inner-being referents without being primarily an inner-being state.

### bdeluktos — abominable — G0947 — Greek

**What this term means**

bdeluktos is an adjective meaning detestable or abominable. It is used to describe persons or things that are morally disgusting. The LSJ attests it across LXX, NT, and first-century AD literature. Its single NT occurrence applies it to persons who profess knowledge of God while their works deny him, making their person rather than merely their acts abominable.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Adjective Nominative Plural Masculine, functioning as a predicate description of persons. This is significant: bdeluktos is not applied to acts or objects in its NT occurrence but to persons — those whose inner profession and outer conduct are contradictory. The adjective form means the entire person is characterised as abominable, not merely one deed.

**What the English translation conceals**

The English 'abominable' adequately renders the adjective sense, but it does not surface the sensory-disgust root embedded in the bdel- stem, which the related noun (bdelugma) and verb (bdelussomai) share. This root carries a visceral, near-physical quality of repulsion that the abstract English 'abominable' does not convey.

**Core verse observations**

Tit 1:16 shows bdeluktos applied to persons who hold a profession of God-knowledge that is contradicted by their works. The verse locates the abominableness in the gap between inner claim and outer action — the inner profession and the external works are in contradiction, and the total person is assessed as detestable. This is the closest this term comes to a direct inner-being referent.

### bdelugma — abomination — G0946 — Greek

**What this term means**

bdelugma is a neuter noun meaning an abomination, detestable thing, or abominable thing. Its meaning range covers two distinct registers: a cultic-eschatological register (the 'abomination of desolation' standing in the holy place, following Daniel) and an ethical-relational register (idolatry with its pollution, moral abomination before God). The LSJ notes its use in the LXX for what is an abomination to the Egyptians and for the 'abomination of desolations' as an idol. Both registers are active in the NT.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Noun Accusative Singular Neuter, serving as the direct object in its anchor occurrence. The neuter gender is standard for abstract moral-categorical nouns of this type in Greek and does not carry additional inner-being significance.

**What the English translation conceals**

'Abomination' in English functions as a near-technical term, neutralised by familiarity. The Greek bdelugma retains its connection to the bdel- disgust-root, which also underlies bdeluktos and bdelussomai. This root cluster conveys revulsion with a physical-sensory dimension that English does not preserve. Additionally, English 'abomination' is used for both the eschatological referent (Matt 24:15) and the moral-evaluative referent (Luk 16:15) without distinguishing them; in the STEP data these are sub-definitions 1 and 2 respectively.

**Core verse observations**

Luke 16:15 is the clearest inner-being verse for this term. Jesus states that what is highly esteemed among humans is an abomination before God — and this immediately follows the statement that God knows the human heart. The verse explicitly grounds the abomination-assessment in God's knowledge of interior states, making inner-being orientation (what a person esteems, exalts, values) the referent of the abomination.

Rev 21:27 contrasts those whose character is detestable or false with those written in the Lamb's book of life. The contrast implies that the abominable quality belongs to the person's orientation and deeds, with the eschatological consequence being exclusion from the new Jerusalem.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Rev 17:4 and Rev 17:5 use bdelugma in an apocalyptic-symbolic register, depicting Babylon as a systemic moral entity whose abominations encompass corporate evil rather than individual inner states. These verses contextually amplify the word's moral gravity by showing bdelugma applied to concentrated systemic corruption — they extend the inner-being meaning into a cosmic register.

Matt 24:15 and Mark 13:14 use bdelugma in the cultic-eschatological register of Daniel's 'abomination of desolation'; these have no inner-being referent and are peripheral to the Framework B analysis.

### to.e.vah — abomination — H8441 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

to.e.vah is a feminine noun (117 occurrences) meaning a disgusting thing, abomination, abominable. Its meaning operates in two clearly distinguished registers given in the source file: a ritual sense (unclean food, idols, mixed marriages) and an ethical sense (wickedness in general). This dual register is crucial: the same term designates both a cultic category (what is ritually off-limits) and a moral category (what is ethically repugnant). The related words point to verbal forms ta.av (to abhor, H8581/H8374), indicating that to.e.vah names the object or quality that provokes the action of abhorring.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular feminine absolute. This is a nominal form — to.e.vah names a quality or category of thing, not an action or state experienced by a subject. This is important for inner-being classification: the term primarily characterises acts, objects, or persons as belonging to the category of what is morally repugnant, rather than naming an inner state of the person who abominates. However, the Proverbs corpus shifts this pattern significantly.

**What the English translation conceals**

The English 'abomination' is adequate as a translation of the term's primary sense but conceals two things. First, it does not distinguish the ritual and ethical registers — in English both read identically. Second, the English does not capture the relational character of to.e.vah: the term almost always specifies whose abomination it is, either 'to the Lord' or 'to the Egyptians' or 'to the righteous', making it a relational-evaluative category (what X finds repugnant) rather than an objective property of the thing described. This relational structure is partially visible in English but is more systematically present in the Hebrew usage.

**Core verse observations**

Pro 26:25 is the most directly inner-being verse for this term: it states there are seven abominations in his heart — locating to.e.vah inside the person's inner life as a quality of the heart's content.

Pro 16:5 describes everyone arrogant in heart as an abomination to the Lord, grounding the moral assessment in an inner disposition (arrogance of heart) rather than an external act.

Pro 15:26 describes the thoughts of the wicked as an abomination to the Lord — thoughts being the clearest inner-being referent in the entire to.e.vah corpus.

Pro 11:20 identifies those of crooked heart as an abomination, again grounding the category in inner moral orientation.

Pro 12:22 applies it to lying lips, which reflects an inner disposition of deceit expressed externally — the border between inner state and external act.

Pro 13:19 shows that turning away from evil is an abomination to fools, revealing that the fool's inner orientation (desire) is incompatible with moral transformation.

Pro 28:9 applies it to the prayer of one who turns his ear from the law — showing the inner act of deliberate non-hearing producing a state of abomination before God.

Pro 29:27 shows a bidirectional structure: the unjust are an abomination to the righteous, and the straight are an abomination to the wicked — revealing that to.e.vah describes the relational-evaluative response of persons with opposing inner orientations.

Eze 9:4 is significant in the larger corpus: the mark is placed on the foreheads of those who sigh and groan over the abominations — describing persons with an inner-being response of grief and moral distress toward corporate evil.

Eze 36:31 describes the people loathing themselves for their iniquities and abominations — self-directed moral revulsion as an inner state.

Eze 6:9 states that survivors will be loathsome in their own sight for the evils and abominations they have committed — again a self-directed inner experience.

Deu 25:16 applies to.e.vah to those who act dishonestly — a behavioural characterisation that implies inner moral disposition.

Jer 6:15 and Jer 8:12 describe those who committed abomination but felt no shame and did not know how to blush — the absence of moral shame and the blunting of conscience as inner-being states related to the abomination category.

Eze 18:24 shows the righteous person who turns to abominations as losing the record of all previous righteous deeds — abomination is here used of a fundamental inner re-orientation, a turning away that is moral and total.

Eze 16:50 states that the Sodomites were haughty and committed an abomination — again pairing an inner disposition (haughtiness) with the abomination category.

Pro 3:32 identifies the devious person (not merely their act) as an abomination to the Lord, linking the entire moral orientation of a person to the category.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Pro 11:1 is ambiguous: dishonest weights are named an abomination, but the verse's contrast with God's delight in just weights implies an inner dimension of integrity. The term names the act, but the relational structure implies an inner valuation.

Pro 15:8 is ambiguous: the sacrifice of the wicked as abomination is contrasted with the prayer of the upright as acceptable — the contrast implicates inner disposition, but the term nominally refers to the act.

Psa 88:8 is extended: the psalmist describes being made a horror to companions by God's action — a social-relational register with individual emotional resonance (isolation, being shut in), but the term characterises the social status rather than an inner state.

Deu 32:16, Isa 1:13, and Jer 44:4 operate in a divine-relational register — God's response to Israel's acts described in abomination language — which amplifies the ethical dimension of the term but applies it to acts and practices rather than inner states.

### shiq.quts — abomination — H8251 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

shiq.quts is a masculine noun (28 occurrences) meaning a detestable thing or idol, abominable thing, abomination, or detested thing. The meaning section does not subdivide into ritual and ethical registers as to.e.vah does; shiq.quts is predominantly used of foreign gods and idols as detestable objects. It belongs to the same root family as shiq.qets (to detest) and she.qets (detestation), forming a tight semantic cluster around the concept of ritual uncleanness and cultic repugnance.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular masculine construct, indicating that shiq.quts typically appears in a construct chain — naming an abomination as belonging to or associated with a referent (e.g., 'the abomination of the Sidonians'). This construct usage confirms the predominantly external, object-oriented character of the term.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'abomination' translates both to.e.vah and shiq.quts without distinction, concealing that shiq.quts is a narrower term more consistently restricted to cultic-object reference than to.e.vah. The English reader cannot determine which Hebrew concept is in use.

**Core verse observations**

Isa 66:3 is the primary inner-being verse for shiq.quts: the verse states that those who perform religious acts with idolatrous inner orientation find that their soul delights in their abominations. The self-chosen orientation of the soul toward what God calls abominable is named explicitly here.

Eze 11:21 states that those whose hearts go after their detestable things and abominations will have their deeds brought upon their heads — the heart's orientation toward shiq.quts is the determining factor.

Hos 9:10 describes Israel becoming detestable like the thing they loved — identity transformation through attachment, showing that inner orientation toward the abominable reshapes the person's own moral character.

### za.am — be indignant — H2194 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

za.am is a verb (12 occurrences) meaning to denounce, express indignation, or be indignant. In Qal: to have indignation, be angrily indignant, be defiant, be abhorrent, or express indignation in speech, including to denounce or curse. In Niphal: to show indignation or anger. The meaning range spans an internal emotional-moral state (to feel indignant) and an external speech act (to denounce, curse). The term is less frequently translated as 'abomination' in the English versions and the source file's inclusion of it in this word cluster reflects the semantic overlap between intense moral repugnance and the action it produces.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb qal imperfective first singular either gender. The Qal imperfective positions the term as an ongoing or habitual inner state — 'God feels indignation every day' (Psa 7:11) — rather than a completed act. This is significant: za.am in its primary Qal usage describes an enduring interior orientation of moral displeasure, which places it closer to an inner-being category than most of the nominal terms in this cluster.

**What the English translation conceals**

The English rendering 'be indignant' captures the emotive dimension but the Hebrew za.am also includes the speech-act of denouncing or cursing (1a3 in the meaning section), which English 'indignant' does not convey. The term thus spans inner moral experience and its vocal expression in a way English requires two separate words to render.

**Core verse observations**

Psa 7:11 states that God is a righteous judge and a God who feels indignation every day — attributing za.am to God as a continuous inner moral-evaluative state directed toward wickedness. This verse models the inner-being dimension of the term: moral indignation as an ongoing orientation.

Pro 24:24 shows that one who calls the wicked righteous will be abhorred by nations — the relational-social dimension of indignation expressed as corporate repugnance.

Pro 25:23 connects a backbiting tongue to angry looks, linking moral-social cause and inner-emotive response.

Num 23:8 shows the constraint on za.am: Balaam cannot denounce those whom God has not denounced — the term is here a speech act with inner moral-evaluative grounding.

Isa 66:14 and Mal 1:4 show za.am as God's sustained orientation of moral displeasure toward enemies and covenant-breakers respectively.

Pro 22:14 shows za.am expressed as divine judgement falling on those with whom God is angry.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Dan 11:30 uses za.am in a geopolitical-military register — rage driving political action — which is an extended use of the term's inner-emotive dimension applied to a foreign king's strategic response.

Zec 1:12 shows za.am applied to God's sustained displeasure over a period of seventy years with cities — temporal-corporate register that extends the inner-being meaning.

Mic 6:10's connection to za.am is unclear from the English translation alone, which speaks of accursed measures without apparent emotional-evaluative language; this may be a STEP anchor-occurrence artifact.

### she.qets — detestation — H8263 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

she.qets is a masculine noun (11 occurrences) meaning a detestable thing or idol, unclean thing, abomination, or detestation. It belongs to the same root family as shiq.quts and shiq.qets. Its verse coverage is almost entirely confined to the Levitical purity regulations concerning unclean animals and foods (Lev 11), with one verse in Isaiah (Isa 66:17) extending the cultic-food category to a specific group's ritual practice. The term's inner-being relevance is minimal from this data set.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular masculine absolute. As with shiq.quts, this is a categorising noun applied to external objects or creatures. The term labels things as belonging to the class of detestable things rather than naming an inner state.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'detestation' is an adequate rendering but slightly stronger in its inner-being connotation than the Hebrew, which in this term's actual usage is almost exclusively an external classification label. The English 'detestation' implies the feeling of the detester; the Hebrew she.qets primarily designates the category of the thing detested.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

All eight verses under she.qets in this source file are classified PERIPHERAL — they describe dietary and ritual categories of unclean creatures with no inner-being referent. The inner-being dimension of the root (the experiential act of detesting) is carried by the verbal form shiq.qets rather than this noun.

### miph.le.tset — horror — H4656 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

miph.le.tset is a feminine noun (4 occurrences) meaning a horrid thing or horrible thing. The meaning section provides only this bare phrase without sub-definitions. Its related words are pa.lats (to shudder), pal.la.tsut (shuddering), and tiph.le.tset (terror) — a root cluster built around the physical experience of shuddering or recoiling in horror. Only 2 of its 4 occurrences are provided in the source file, both referring to a physical idol image (the Asherah image of Maacah). The data is thin.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is noun common singular feminine absolute. The term categorises an object as belonging to the class of horrid things. The root's connection to pa.lats (to shudder) suggests that miph.le.tset originally designated an object that causes a physical shudder of horror — an olfactory-somatic response embedded in the etymology even though the term is used nominally of objects.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'horror' partially captures the intended sense but the somatic-shudder dimension of the pa.lats root is lost. The English noun 'horror' is more cerebral; the Hebrew root suggests a visceral, physical recoil.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Both provided verses (1Ki 15:13 and 2Ch 15:16) are parallel accounts of the same event and are classified PERIPHERAL — they describe a physical idol object. No inner-being referent is present in either verse. The inner-being dimension of this term's root cluster (shuddering, terror as inner states) is not represented in the provided verse data.

### bdelussomai — to abhor — G0948 — Greek

**What this term means**

bdelussomai is a verb (2 occurrences) with voice-dependent meanings: in the middle voice, to abhor, detest, or loathe; in the passive voice, to be vile, abhorrent, abominable, or detestable. The LSJ notes an earlier meaning of feeling physical loathing for food, and an ancient use meaning 'to be sick' — preserving the somatic-disgust root most clearly of all the bdel- family members. The term thus describes an inner act (the experience of abhorring or detesting) as well as a state of being (being abhorrent).

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Verb Present Middle or Passive Deponent Participle Vocative Singular Masculine. The deponent nature of the verb is significant: bdelussomai is a deponent verb, meaning it has middle or passive form but is used in active senses. The present tense of the participle indicates an ongoing characterisation — the subject is one who is currently in the state of abhorring, or is being characterised as habitually abhorrent.

**What the English translation conceals**

The English 'to abhor' adequately renders the middle-voice sense but conceals the passive-voice meaning ('to be abhorrent/vile') which is equally present in the term's range. The LSJ's note on the somatic-disgust root (physical loathing for food, becoming sick) is entirely absent from the English rendering, which has no visceral connotation.

**Core verse observations**

Rom 2:22 applies bdelussomai to a person who professes to abhor idols while robbing temples — the inner profession of abhorrence and the outer act of sacrilege are in direct contradiction. This verse shows bdelussomai as an inner-being state (the experience of abhorring) that is expected to govern conduct but has been severed from it.

Rev 21:8 uses bdelussomai in the passive — 'the detestable' as a class of persons alongside the cowardly, faithless, murderers, and liars, all of whom face eschatological judgement. The passive characterises their total personhood as abhorrent/vile.

### ta.av — to abhor — H8581 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

ta.av is a verb (23 occurrences) meaning to abhor, be abominable, or do abominably. Its stem range covers three stems: Niphal — to be abhorred or detested (in both ritual and ethical senses); Piel — to loathe, abhor, regard as abomination, or cause to be an abomination (both senses); Hiphil — to make abominable, do abominably (both senses). The Piel and Hiphil together cover both the internal experience of abhorring and the external act of making something or someone abhorrent. The source file notes a possible connection to H8374 (ta.av, 'to loathe') as an alternate form; this is recorded as a data quality flag and cannot be resolved from the source file alone.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb hiphil consecutive imperfective third singular masculine. The Hiphil stem is causative — meaning the anchor occurrence describes an act of causing something to be abominable, doing abominably. This is significant: the anchor is the causative-external register, but the full stem range (Niphal, Piel, Hiphil) shows that the term spans passive reception of abhorrence (Niphal), active inner experience of loathing (Piel), and causative production of abominableness (Hiphil). The Piel — to loathe — is the inner-being stem.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'to abhor' renders primarily the Piel active-inner sense. It does not distinguish between the three stems — the reader cannot determine whether the text is describing a person being abhorred (Niphal), a person actively loathing (Piel), or a person doing abominably to cause abhorrence (Hiphil). This is a significant loss of precision in translation.

**Core verse observations**

Job 19:19 shows all intimate friends abhorring the sufferer — the Piel as an inner-being experience of one's relational circle turning to active revulsion.

Job 30:10 shows the verbal abhorrence accompanied by physical distancing and spitting — the inner experience of abhorrence expressed in bodily acts.

Job 9:31 shows Job's own clothes abhorring him — a vivid extension of the inner-being experience of being loathed to the point where even one's own garments are metaphorically aligned against one.

Psa 5:6 shows God as the subject of ta.av — the Lord abhors the bloodthirsty and deceitful man. This is a divine inner-being state of abhorrence directed toward specific inner dispositions (bloodthirst, deceit).

Psa 119:163 shows the psalmist using ta.av and the parallel 'I hate' in sequence: 'I hate and abhor falsehood.' This double inner-being statement locates ta.av firmly in the category of volitional-emotive inner states.

Psa 107:18 shows those whose soul loathed any kind of food — a somatic-inner experience of revulsion aligned with physical deterioration and proximity to death.

Amo 5:10 shows those who abhor the truth-teller in the gate — an inner orientation of moral revulsion directed toward honest speech, revealing how ta.av can characterise a corrupt inner disposition.

Mic 3:9 applies ta.av to rulers who detest justice — an inner moral orientation of hostility toward right ordering.

Deu 23:7 shows the prohibition not to abhor an Edomite or an Egyptian — the command addresses an inner disposition (abhorrence) directed toward persons.

1Ch 21:6 shows Joab finding the king's command abhorrent — the inner experience of moral-ethical revulsion toward an instruction.

Psa 14:1 and Psa 53:1 both describe the fool's corrupt inner state and his abominable deeds as inseparable — the inner disposition and the external acts are paired.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Isa 14:19 uses ta.av of a king cast out as a loathed branch — extended register applied to a dead person's post-mortem treatment; no inner-being subject.

Isa 49:7 uses ta.av of the servant figure abhorred by the nation — corporate-relational register; the abhorrence is collective and the servant is its object rather than subject.

Eze 16:25 uses ta.av of an external sexual act; Eze 16:52 uses related language in a social-shame register. Both are classified PERIPHERAL.

### shiq.qets — to detest — H8262 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

shiq.qets is a verb (7 occurrences) occurring only in the Piel stem, meaning to detest, make abominable, count filthy, or make detestable. The Piel sub-definitions cover: to detest (active inner act of regarding something as abominable) and to make detestable (causative-Piel). The term is used primarily in Levitical purity contexts with two significant exceptions. No other stems are attested in the meaning section.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb piel imperfective second plural masculine. The Piel is the intensive or declarative-causative stem in Hebrew. Here it functions as a declarative-intensive — to actively regard or treat as detestable. The imperfective aspect in the anchor suggests an ongoing or characteristic action.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'to detest' renders the inner-experiential sense of the Piel adequately. It does not, however, capture the reflexive nuance present in Lev 11:43 and Lev 20:25 ('make yourselves detestable'), where the Piel's causative dimension produces a state in the subject through contact with prohibited categories. The self-directed consequence — becoming detestable through the act of not detesting — is a subtle inner-being dynamic English does not readily surface.

**Core verse observations**

Deu 7:26 combines shiq.qets with ta.av in direct sequence: 'you shall utterly detest and abhor it.' This pairing of the two detesting verbs intensifies the inner-being charge — not merely to avoid but to actively maintain a disposition of inner revulsion toward the prohibited object.

Psa 22:24 uses the Piel negatively and with God as subject: God has not despised or abhorred the affliction of the afflicted. This is a significant inner-being verse — it states that God's inner orientation toward the suffering person is the opposite of shiq.qets. The verse's force depends on the reader understanding shiq.qets as naming a real inner-being state of revulsion that God does not direct toward the afflicted.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Lev 11:43 and Lev 20:25 are classified ambiguous: the reflexive instruction 'you shall not make yourselves detestable' implies an inner consequence of ritual violation — the boundary between the external act of contact with an unclean thing and the inner state of becoming detestable is unclear from the verse text alone.

### ba.ash — to stink — H0887 — Hebrew

**What this term means**

ba.ash is a verb (19 occurrences) whose primary meaning is to have a bad smell, stink, or smell bad. Its stem range is the widest in this word cluster: Qal — literal stink; Niphal — to become or make oneself odious; Hiphil — to stink, emit stench, cause to stink, and figuratively 'of wickedness'; Hithpael — to make oneself odious. The TWOT additionally records the extended meaning 'to abhor', showing that the sensory-physical root of stench has been extended into the ethical-relational domain. ba.ash is included in this word cluster because of this semantic extension, not because of its primary literal meaning.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is verb hiphil infinitive construct. The Hiphil causative infinitive indicates the anchor occurrence involves causing something to stink or causing stench — a causative external act. However, the full stem range shows that the root's inner-being relevance lies primarily in the Niphal (becoming odious in others' perception) and the Hithpael (making oneself odious through action) — both of which have a relational-social inner-being dimension.

**What the English translation conceals**

The English 'to stink' captures only the Qal-literal register. The Niphal and Hithpael senses of becoming/making-oneself odious, and the TWOT extended meaning 'to abhor', are entirely absent from the English gloss. The figurative Hiphil 'of wickedness' — where ba.ash describes the stench-quality of moral evil — is a semantic domain English 'to stink' does not access at all.

**Core verse observations**

Psa 38:5 shows ba.ash applied to the psalmist's own wounds, which stink and fester because of his foolishness. This is an inner-being verse in the sense that the physical stench is explicitly linked to a moral-inner cause (foolishness), making the bodily experience an expression of an interior state.

Pro 13:5 in its English rendering ('the wicked brings shame and disgrace') is translated without the explicit ba.ash vocabulary, but the source file includes it under this term. The verse links the wicked person's inner disposition to a social consequence of shame and stench — the causal inner-being dimension is present.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

Gen 34:30, Exo 5:21, 1Sa 13:4, 1Sa 27:12, 2Sa 10:6, 2Sa 16:21, and 1Ch 19:6 all use ba.ash in a social-political register — persons or groups made odious in the eyes of others through specific acts. These are extended uses where the stench metaphor describes relational rupture, social exclusion, or political alienation; they amplify the ethical dimension by showing how inner moral failure becomes socially perceivable as revulsion in others.

Ecc 10:1 uses ba.ash in a wisdom metaphor: dead flies corrupt a perfumer's ointment and give off a stench, just as a little folly outweighs wisdom and honour. The extended register is wisdom-metaphorical; the inner-being connection is the folly that produces the corrupting effect.

### athemitos — unlawful — G0111 — Greek

**What this term means**

athemitos is an adjective (2 occurrences) meaning unlawful, criminal, wicked, or detestable. It derives from themis (divinely established custom or law), making it a term about violation of a divinely ordered structure rather than about repugnance or disgust. This distinguishes it from all the other terms in this cluster: athemitos does not carry a sensory-disgust root but a law-and-order root. Its semantic field intersects 'abomination' only at the point of divine prohibition.

**What the grammatical form reveals**

The anchor form is Adjective Nominative Singular Neuter, used predicatively. The neuter form is standard for adjectives used abstractly or of non-specified things. The adjective characterises an act or category of thing as violating divine order.

**What the English translation conceals**

English 'unlawful' captures the legal-structural sense but loses the divine-relational dimension of themis: this is not merely human law but the law of right ordering established by God. The rendering 'detestable' (also given in the meaning section) captures the relational-evaluative dimension but loses the structural-legal root. Neither English gloss alone captures both.

**Core verse observations**

Act 10:28 shows athemitos in a significant inner-being context: Peter states that it is unlawful for a Jew to associate with a Gentile — and then immediately reports that God has shown him not to call any person common or unclean. The verse documents an inner-being transformation: a previously held disposition of separation, grounded in a category of unlawfulness, being revised by divine revelation. The abomination-category here is the subject of an inner moral revision.

**Extended and Ambiguous verse notes**

1Pe 4:3 uses athemitos in a list of past-lifestyle practices ('lawless idolatry') that characterised the Gentile life before conversion. The term here describes external acts rather than an inner state, though the context of moral transformation implies that inner orientation has changed.

## Root and Stem Patterns Across the Term Set

The fourteen terms divide into three recognisable root families. The Greek bdel- cluster (bdeluktos, bdelugma, bdelussomai) shares a common disgust-root with attested somatic origins (physical loathing for food, nausea), extending into moral-evaluative territory. The Hebrew sheq/shiq cluster (shiq.quts, she.qets, shiq.qets) forms a tight root family around cultic uncleanness and the active-declarative experience of detesting, with the verb shiq.qets connecting the nominal categories to an inner-evaluative act. The Hebrew ta- cluster (to.e.vah, ta.av, and the referenced H8374) represents the broadest semantic range in the set, covering the nominal category of abomination (to.e.vah), the verbal act of abhorring (ta.av), and the verbal act of loathing (H8374), with the largest occurrence count by far (to.e.vah at 117). Across these clusters, causative stems are present in ta.av (Hiphil), ba.ash (Hiphil), shiq.qets (Piel causative sub-definition), and bdelussomai (LSJ notes a later causative form). This pattern shows that abomination-language in the biblical corpus is not merely a static category but a dynamic one involving causal relationships — persons causing things to be abominable, making themselves odious, producing the condition of repugnance in relational contexts. ba.ash stands apart as the only term whose primary semantic register is olfactory-literal, with its inner-being relevance carried by figurative extension and the TWOT's extended meaning. athemitos stands apart as the only term rooted in divine-law violation rather than disgust.
