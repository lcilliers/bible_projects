## Part A — Observations That Came From the Data

These are findings anchored to a specific text or vocabulary item. They are not interpretive frameworks — they are things the data showed.

---

### A1. The three-state pattern in the shame vocabulary
**Source:** Shame card (146), Section 10, Flag 2  
**Text anchors:** Ezr 9:6 (shame functioning correctly); Jer 6:15/8:12 (shame-capacity absent); Phil 3:19 (glorying in shame — faculty reversed)  
**Observation:** Three distinct states of the shame-faculty are present in the data: (1) functioning — shame directed upward toward God, proportional, corrective; (2) disabled — the capacity to blush is specifically named as absent; (3) inverted — what should produce shame produces boasting. These are not a spectrum — they are qualitatively distinct conditions.  
**Status:** Observed in shame vocabulary. Whether this pattern appears in other words is a question for subsequent cards — not an established finding.

---

### A2. Shame-faculty failure and abomination-faculty failure co-occur in the same text
**Source:** Shame card (146) and Abomination card (1), both flagging the same text independently  
**Text anchor:** Jer 6:15 / 8:12 — "they committed abomination... they did not know how to blush"  
**Observation:** The two cards, analysed separately, both arrived at this text as significant. The co-occurrence of abomination-insensitivity and shame-incapacity in a single verse — named physiologically (the blush) — is a data point, not a framework. What it means for the relationship between these two words requires more analysis.  
**Status:** Single confirmed inter-card textual observation.

---

### A3. The gaon root applies to both divine majesty and human arrogance
**Source:** Pride card (123)  
**Text anchors:** Exo 15:7; Psa 93:1 (gaon = God's majesty); Pro 16:18; Eze 7:10 (gaon = human arrogance)  
**Observation:** The same Hebrew root describes what is legitimately God's and what is sinfully claimed by the human. The word does not change — the referent does. This is a vocabulary finding. What it implies for anthropology (whether this means the capacity itself is neutral and the misdirection is the sin) is an unresolved question.  
**Status:** Confirmed lexical finding. Theological implications not yet resolved.

---

### A4. Anger and grief appear together in a single verse describing Jesus's moral response
**Source:** Anger card (4) and Grief card (72)  
**Text anchor:** Mark 3:5 — "he looked at them with anger, grieved at their hardness of heart"  
**Observation:** Orge (anger) and sullupeo (deeply grieved) are co-present in one moment. This is a textual observation. Whether it constitutes a "functional pair" as a structural claim about the soul is not established — that would require more data. What can be said is that the text does not treat these as contradictory or sequential but simultaneous.  
**Status:** Single textual observation. Worth carrying forward for testing.

---

### A5. The guilt-offering (asham) system has a built-in recognition stage
**Source:** Guilt card (73)  
**Text anchor:** Lev 5:2–5 — "when he realizes his guilt" repeated four times in consecutive verses  
**Observation:** The Hebrew guilt-offering system explicitly names a recognition moment as the trigger for the resolution sequence. The phrase "comes to know it" / "realizes his guilt" is the structural hinge before confession and offering become possible. The guilt-vocabulary has a built-in cognitive stage that precedes the cultic/relational resolution.  
**Status:** Direct textual observation from the Leviticus passage. Pastoral/theological implications not yet developed.

---

### A6. The avon root carries three dimensions in a single word
**Source:** Guilt card (73)  
**Text anchors:** Multiple — the root is translated variously as crime/iniquity (the act), guilt (the state), and punishment (the consequence)  
**Observation:** A single Hebrew root covers act, resultant state, and consequence without distinguishing them lexically. This is a vocabulary finding. English translations necessarily flatten this. The significance for a Framework B account of guilt — whether these three dimensions must be addressed separately in restoration — is an open question.  
**Status:** Confirmed lexical observation.

---

### A7. The Spirit's grief is named across three texts spanning both Testaments
**Source:** Grief card (72)  
**Text anchors:** Gen 6:6 (God grieved to his heart); Isa 63:10 (they grieved his Holy Spirit); Eph 4:30 (do not grieve the Holy Spirit)  
**Observation:** The capacity for grief is attributed to God in the OT and explicitly to the Holy Spirit in both Isaiah and Paul. The same root (atsav/lupeo) is used. This is a textual trajectory, not an inference. What it means theologically for the relationship between divine and human grief is an open question.  
**Status:** Textual observation. Theological development required.

---

### A8. The birth-pangs metaphor for anguish is used consistently and across multiple authors
**Source:** Anguish card (182)  
**Text anchors:** Isa 13:8; 21:3; Jer 4:31; 6:24; Joh 16:20–22; Gal 4:19; 1Th 5:3  
**Observation:** The same metaphor (chil/chul in Hebrew; odin in Greek) — birth-pangs as the image for extreme suffering — appears in Isaiah, Jeremiah, Jesus, Paul, and eschatological contexts. It frames anguish as purposive and directional (carrying something toward birth) rather than terminal. Whether this constitutes a structural theological category or is literary convention requires more analysis.  
**Status:** Consistent metaphor across multiple texts and authors. Significance not yet fully assessed.

---

### A9. The Lewis/Tangney guilt-shame distinction maps onto distinct Hebrew vocabulary
**Source:** Guilt card (73), Shame card (146)  
**Text anchors:** asham/avon roots (liability-of-act) vs. bosh/kelimmah roots (identity-signal)  
**Observation:** The psychological distinction between guilt ("I did something bad" — act-focused) and shame ("I am bad" — identity-focused), developed in modern psychology in the 1970s–90s, has a parallel in the Hebrew vocabulary. The two root clusters operate differently in the texts. This is a vocabulary observation — it does not prove the ancients held the psychological theory, but the lexical distinction is real.  
**Status:** Confirmed vocabulary observation. Requires careful handling in writing to avoid anachronism.

---

### A10. Suneidesis (conscience) has its own three-state description in the NT
**Source:** Guilt card (73)  
**Text anchors:** 1Ti 1:5 (good conscience); 1Ti 4:2 (seared conscience); Tit 1:15 (defiled conscience)  
**Observation:** The NT uses three distinct qualifiers for conscience independently of the shame vocabulary: good/clear, seared/cauterised, and defiled. These map onto functioning, disabled, and inverted states — but this observation came from the NT's own vocabulary, not from importing the typology developed in the shame card. Whether this confirms a broader pattern or is coincidental is a question for subsequent analysis.  
**Status:** Independent textual observation from the conscience vocabulary.

## Part B — Open Questions

These are questions the data raised but did not answer. They are not hypotheses to be tested against subsequent cards — they are genuinely unresolved.

---

**B1.** Is the three-state pattern (functioning/disabled/inverted) a feature of all evaluative words in the 181, or is it specific to this group of words? The question cannot be answered from eight cards. It requires substantially more data before any structural claim can be made.

**B2.** What is the relationship between the abomination-faculty and the shame-faculty? The co-occurrence in Jer 6:15 is a single text. Are they one system with two faces, two independent faculties that happen to fail together, or something else? The data does not yet resolve this.

**B3.** The gaon dual-referent raises the question of whether there is a created capacity in the image-bearing soul that pride corrupts rather than simply a vice added by the fall. This has implications for how Framework B treats the relationship between original creation and sin. Unresolved.

**B4.** The anger-grief co-presence in Mark 3:5 raises the question of whether these always belong together in a morally functioning soul, or whether they can operate independently in legitimate ways. The data so far is a single text. More cases needed.

**B5.** The avon three-dimensionality raises the question of whether biblical restoration addresses act, guilt-state, and consequence as distinct stages — and whether the guilt-offering system was designed to address all three or only some. This requires more exegetical work on the Levitical system.

**B6.** The Spirit's grief trajectory (Gen 6:6 → Isa 63:10 → Eph 4:30) raises the question of whether divine grief is presented as preceding divine forgiveness in the biblical narrative — and if so, what this implies for a theology of atonement and for the forgiveness project specifically. Unresolved.

**B7.** The birth-pangs metaphor raises the question of whether there is a biblical category of purposive suffering that is structurally distinct from suffering-as-consequence. If so, what defines the boundary between them? The texts use the same imagery for Servant-suffering (Isa 53), apostolic suffering (Gal 4:19), disciples' grief at the passion (Joh 16), and eschatological tribulation (1Th 5:3). Whether these are the same category is not established.

**B8.** The guilt-shame vocabulary distinction raises the question of whether the biblical restoration process addresses these separately — and whether pastoral and therapeutic approaches that collapse them into one category are missing something the vocabulary preserves. This is a practical question for the forgiveness project but not yet resolved exegetically.

**B9.** Eze 43:10–11 presents shame as the precondition for receiving the temple vision. This is a single text but a striking one — shame as epistemologically significant, opening the soul to revelation. Whether this is an isolated use or reflects a broader pattern of shame-as-gateway needs more data.

**B10.** The humility card raised the question of whether the anav root's overlap between virtue (humility) and social condition (poor/afflicted) means the humility-faculty can be opened by circumstance (suffering that strips self-sufficiency) as well as by spiritual formation. Luk 4:18 (Jesus quoting Isa 61:1 anav as his mission statement) keeps this question alive. Unresolved.

## Part C — What Was Retired

The synthesis document (WA-Synthesis-MoralConscience.docx) proposed:

- An "eight-component moral-conscience faculty" as a named structural unit
- A staged "generative sequence" model
- A claim that these eight words constitute a complete structural description of the soul's moral-conscience
- An "imago Dei dimension" argument built from the eight cards

These are premature. Eight words from a 181-word analysis is insufficient basis for structural claims about the soul's faculty architecture. The observations above are what the data actually showed. The structural conclusions were mine, not the data's.

The synthesis document is retired. These notes replace it as the working record of Phase 1 cross-card observations.
