## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** Abomination
**Registry ID:** 001
**Source List:** Missing Inner Being Words
**Testament Coverage:** Both (OT and NT)

The word set provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word on the basis that its primary terms — particularly to.e.vah (H8441), ta.av (H8581), bdelugma (G0946), and bdelussomai (G0948) — express a relational evaluative state: the state of a person, object, or action being experienced as deeply repugnant, revolting, or morally intolerable, either by God, by persons, or by communities. Several terms in the set are directed outward (the speaker abhors something), while others function as labels applied to the object (the thing is an abomination). Both directions are represented. The qualification is made with moderate-to-high confidence. The primary Hebrew and Greek terms carry consistent inner-being evaluative force across their verse ranges. A number of peripheral terms in the set — pig.gul (H6292), ba.ash (H0887), miph.le.tset (H4656), and athemitos (G0111) — are weakly or indirectly connected to the inner-being dimension and may function more as markers of uncleanness, social dishonour, or legal prohibition than as inner-state terms proper.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: TAAV — to.e.vah (H8441) and ta.av (H8581)

The TAAV root family is the dominant cluster in this word set. It contains the most frequently occurring term in the entire set — to.e.vah (H8441), which appears approximately 117 times — together with the verb ta.av (H8581, approximately 23 occurrences) and a note-entry for an alternate form H8374, also transliterated ta.av, meaning "to loathe."

The core image of the TAAV root is relational aversion: a state in which one party regards another with deep moral revulsion. The noun to.e.vah designates the thing or action that provokes this revulsion; the verb ta.av designates the inner act of abhorring or the state of being abhorred.

To.e.vah functions across two distinguishable domains within its verse set. In the ritual domain it names specific categories of prohibited food, prohibited practices, and prohibited objects — sexual violations (Lev 18:22), defective sacrifices (Deu 17:1), pagan religious practices (Deu 18:12), mixed weights (Pro 20:10), cross-dressing (Deu 22:5), and cult prostitution (Deu 23:18). In the ethical domain it names dispositions and character qualities that God regards as repugnant: the devious person (Pro 3:32), arrogance in heart (Pro 16:5), lying lips (Pro 12:22), the thoughts of the wicked (Pro 15:26), the sacrifice offered with evil intent (Pro 21:27). Proverbs is the heaviest concentration of the ethical usage. The Ezekiel material is where the two domains are most densely interwoven — ritual defilement and moral corruption are treated as a single complex in texts such as Eze 8 and Eze 16.

The god_as_subject flag is set true for to.e.vah. God is consistently the subject who experiences or expresses the abhorrence in the majority of the to.e.vah verse set. The Proverbs material is particularly explicit: "an abomination to the Lord" is a formulaic phrase in at least sixteen Proverbs verses. The Lord's to.e.vah is not incidental but structural — it is the stated basis for prohibitions, judgments, and ethical expectations. Jer 44:4 ("this abomination that I hate") and Isa 1:13 ("incense is an abomination to me") show the same pattern in prophetic speech.

Ta.av (H8581) adds the verbal dimension. The Piel stem is present in the meaning block: "to loathe, abhor, regard as an abomination." The Hiphil is also present: "to make abominable, do abominably." The Piel sub-sense 1b2 explicitly states "to cause to be an abomination," making causative_form_present true. This means the term does not only describe the state but can describe the act of rendering something abominable. The verse set for ta.av includes God as subject: Psa 5:6 ("the Lord abhors the bloodthirsty and deceitful man") and Psa 106:40 ("he abhorred his heritage"). It also includes human subjects abhorring other humans — Job 19:19 ("All my intimate friends abhor me") and Job 30:10 ("They abhor me; they keep aloof from me; they do not hesitate to spit at the sight of me"). The Job material is notable: abhorrence here is not a theological verdict but a social experience of being rendered revolting to one's community.

The TAAV family as a whole is doing real and differentiated work. To.e.vah is the nominative category — the label applied to the object or action. Ta.av is the experiential and active form — the inner act of abhorring and the causative act of rendering abhorrent. These are not near-synonyms; they represent the noun and verb poles of the same semantic field, and together they cover both the evaluative object (what is abominable) and the evaluating subject (who abhors, and the act of abhorring). English "abomination" captures to.e.vah reasonably but provides no single equivalent for ta.av's full range, particularly the causative dimension and the social-relational usage in the Job verses.

**Verdict:** The two terms in the TAAV family are doing genuinely different work. To.e.vah is primarily a categorising noun; ta.av is primarily an experiential and action verb. They are not candidates for consolidation.

### Root Family: SHQTS — shiq.quts (H8251), she.qets (H8263), shiq.qets (H8262)

The SHQTS root family contains three terms: a noun meaning primarily "detestable idol/thing" (shiq.quts, H8251), a second noun meaning "detestable/unclean thing" (she.qets, H8263), and a Piel verb meaning "to detest / to make detestable" (shiq.qets, H8262).

The core image of this root is contamination and disgust, with an initial concrete focus on the ritual uncleanness of specific categories of creatures, objects, and foods. She.qets concentrates almost entirely in Leviticus 11 — the dietary law chapter — applied to sea creatures without fins and scales (Lev 11:10, 11:12), insects (Lev 11:20, 11:23), and swarming ground creatures (Lev 11:41, 11:42). One outlier verse is Isa 66:17 where she.qets appears in a prophetic indictment alongside pig's flesh and mice. The term in this root family is the most narrowly ritual.

Shiq.quts extends into idol worship specifically. Its verse set concentrates on named foreign gods called "the abomination" of specific nations: Ashtoreth as "abomination of the Sidonians" (1Ki 11:5), Chemosh as "abomination of Moab" (1Ki 11:7), Milcom as "abomination of the Ammonites" (2Ki 23:13). The Daniel material uses shiq.quts in the eschatological phrase "abomination that makes desolate" (Dan 9:27, 11:31, 12:11). This is the term's most theologically loaded usage in the verse set, and it is the term that NT writers draw on for bdelugma (see Section 4).

Shiq.qets (H8262) is the verb of the family, operating in the Piel. Its meaning block gives "to detest" and "to make detestable," making causative_form_present true. Its verse set is confined to Leviticus 11 and one Deuteronomy verse (Deu 7:26), plus Psa 22:24. The Psalms verse is distinctive: "For he has not despised or abhorred the affliction of the afflicted" — here shiq.qets appears with God as the hypothetical (denied) subject, applied to a person in distress rather than to a prohibited food or idol. This is the only verse in the SHQTS family where the term operates with a person as its object in a non-ritual context.

The three SHQTS terms together cover: the categorical label for unclean creatures (she.qets), the label for foreign idols (shiq.quts), and the act of detesting (shiq.qets). The data quality section notes that she.qets and shiq.quts are consolidation candidates, since both are nouns with heavily overlapping semantic ranges. However, the verse sets do reveal a usage distinction: she.qets concentrates in clean/unclean animal categories, while shiq.quts concentrates in idol/foreign god categories. This is a genuine if partial distinction.

English translation consistently renders all three as "detestable/abomination" without distinguishing the creature-disgust usage from the idol-naming usage, hiding what the Hebrew preserves as a terminological distinction.

**Verdict:** The three terms in the SHQTS family are partially doing different work. She.qets and shiq.quts are near-synonyms in dictionary definition but used in reliably different domains in the verse set (creature uncleanness vs idol naming). Shiq.qets is the verbal form and should be retained separately. The noun pair are consolidation candidates, but the verse-set difference is worth noting before a consolidation decision is made.

### Root Family: ZAM — za.am (H2194)

Za.am stands alone as the only member of the ZAM root in this word set. Its meaning block covers: indignation, abhorrence, denouncing, and cursing. The verse set shows za.am used almost exclusively with divine subjects. Psa 7:11 states that God "feels indignation every day." Isa 66:14 refers to God showing "his indignation against his enemies." Zec 1:12 has the angel asking God how long he will remain angry with Jerusalem. Mal 1:4 states the people will be called "the people with whom the Lord is angry forever." Num 23:7-8 is distinctive: Balaam is summoned to curse Israel, but he observes that God has not cursed them — the za.am of God cannot be overridden by human cursing.

God_as_subject is set true for za.am. The term's place in this word set is peripheral: its primary semantic content is closer to wrath/indignation than to abomination as a moral category. It enters the set because the STEP search returned it as a related term, and its meaning block includes "to be abhorrent" as one sub-sense. However, the verse set does not primarily show za.am functioning as an abomination term; it functions as an anger/indignation term directed by God against his enemies. The cross-registry link to the anger word set is relevant here.

**Verdict:** Za.am is not doing abomination-specific work in its verse set. It is present in the STEP related word cluster and includes "to be abhorrent" in its meaning range, but its verse range is functionally anger/indignation vocabulary. It may be more at home in the anger or wrath word set than in abomination.

### Root Family: PLTS — miph.le.tset (H4656)

Miph.le.tset is the only member of the PLTS root in this word set. Its meaning block is brief: "horrid thing, horrible thing." Its related words are pa.lats (to shudder), pal.la.tsut (shuddering), and tiph.le.tset (terror). The somatic_link flag is set true because the root cluster is anchored in the physical experience of shuddering/trembling, and miph.le.tset is derived from a verb meaning to physically shudder with horror.

The verse set for miph.le.tset is thin: only two verses are present (1Ki 15:13 and 2Ch 15:16), both describing the same event — a queen mother's idolatrous image for Asherah, which is cut down and destroyed. The STEP occurrence count states approximately 4x, so two verses are missing from the source file. Both present verses use miph.le.tset as a label for an idolatrous object, parallel to shiq.quts usage. The somatic dimension — the "horror" that produces physical shuddering — is carried by the root cluster but not directly expressed in these two verses.

**Verdict:** Miph.le.tset is a peripheral term in this word set with thin data. Its root cluster connects it to the physical experience of horror/shuddering, which is a genuine somatic-inner link, but the verse sample is insufficient to establish how it functions beyond naming an idolatrous object. It cannot be consolidated with other terms given its distinct root.

### Individual Term: pig.gul (H6292)

Pig.gul is an isolated term with no root family in the data. Its meaning is highly specific: "foul thing, refuse" with the gloss "unclean sacrificial flesh (only use)." All four verses in its set (Lev 7:18, Lev 19:7, Isa 65:4, Eze 4:14) concern ritually disqualified sacrificial meat — flesh that has been retained beyond the prescribed time and is therefore rendered unacceptable/tainted. ESV renders the term consistently as "tainted."

Pig.gul is the most narrowly ritual term in the set and the most concrete. It does not carry inner-being evaluative content in its verse set; it designates a specific category of disqualified cultic material. Its presence in the STEP abomination cluster is by virtue of the general category of ritual uncleanness, not because it names an inner state.

**Verdict:** Pig.gul is a peripheral term. It belongs to the cultic/purity domain, not the inner-being domain. It is not a consolidation candidate with any other term given its specificity.

### Root Family: BASH — ba.ash (H0887)

Ba.ash has the concrete meaning "to stink, smell bad." Its stem forms extend from literal stench (Exo 7:18, 7:21, 8:14 — the Nile and piles of frogs literally stinking) through social figurative usage (making oneself "a stench" to another party — Gen 34:30, Exo 5:21, 1Sa 13:4, 2Sa 10:6, 2Sa 16:21) to a moral figurative usage (Pro 13:5, Ecc 10:1). The Hiphil is listed as causative ("to cause to stink"), making causative_form_present true.

The somatic_link flag is set true because the root is grounded in a literal sensory experience — olfactory disgust — and the transition from literal stench to moral abhorrence is traceable within the verse set itself. Psa 38:5 is the strongest inner-being verse: "My wounds stink and fester because of my foolishness" — here physical festering and inner moral failure are placed in direct parallel.

The social usage of ba.ash is notable: in multiple narrative verses, becoming "a stench" to someone describes a relational rupture — being rendered utterly unacceptable and repugnant in another's eyes. This is the same evaluative dynamic as to.e.vah and ta.av, but expressed through the sensory metaphor of smell rather than a primary moral vocabulary.

**Verdict:** Ba.ash is a peripheral inner-being term whose primary register is sensory-literal or social-figurative. Its verse set does include moral figurative usage, and its somatic grounding in olfactory disgust is relevant to the inner-being picture of abomination. It is not a consolidation candidate with other terms.

### Root Family: BDEL — bdelugma (G0946), bdeluktos (G0947), bdelussomai (G0948)

The BDEL root family is the primary NT cluster for abomination. The LSJ material confirms the root connects to the sensation of loathing or feeling sick at something, with early usage referring to food aversion. By NT usage the term has moved firmly into moral and eschatological register.

Bdelugma (G0946) is the noun form and the most frequently occurring Greek term (approximately 6x). Its verse set includes the eschatological "abomination of desolation" in Matt 24:15 and Mk 13:14, which directly references Daniel's shiq.quts usage (see Section 4 below). The Luk 16:15 usage is the most theologically significant inner-being verse in the NT portion of this set: "what is exalted among men is an abomination in the sight of God." Here bdelugma is used to describe how God evaluates a human inner disposition — pride or self-exaltation — in direct contrast to how humans evaluate it. God_as_subject is set true for bdelugma. The Revelation material (Rev 17:4, 17:5, 21:27) uses bdelugma in apocalyptic imagery of corrupt Babylon, applying it to a complex of idolatry, sexual immorality, and moral pollution.

Bdeluktos (G0947) is the adjective form, occurring only once (Tit 1:16). It describes people who "profess to know God but deny him by their works" as "detestable." The term functions here as a moral verdict on a category of person rather than on an action or object.

Bdelussomai (G0948) is the verb form (approximately 2x). Rom 2:22 uses it for human abhorrence of idols — "you who abhor idols, do you rob temples?" — creating a rhetorical contrast between professed repugnance and actual behaviour. Rev 21:8 uses the passive ("the detestable") as a character category placed alongside murderers, sorcerers, and idolaters. The LSJ entry confirms that bdelussomai carries a causative sense in later Greek ("cause to stink, make loathsome"), making causative_form_present true for the verb form. This parallels the TAAV and SHQTS causative forms in the Hebrew.

The BDEL family covers the noun (evaluative label), adjective (moral character verdict), and verb (active abhorring / being rendered abhorrent) dimensions, mirroring the noun-verb structure of the TAAV family in Hebrew.

**Verdict:** The three BDEL terms are doing genuinely different grammatical and functional work. They are not near-synonyms; they represent complementary grammatical forms of a single root family. Consolidation would be inappropriate.

### Individual Term: athemitos (G0111)

Athemitos belongs to the THEMI root family, which is the root for "law/ordinance/what is divinely established." The term means "unlawful, against divine law, detestable." Its two verses (Act 10:28 and 1Pe 4:3) both concern the crossing of lawful boundaries — Jewish-Gentile association laws (Act 10:28) and Gentile patterns of living (1Pe 4:3). ESV renders Act 10:28 as "unlawful" and 1Pe 4:3 does not translate athemitos directly as detestable but renders the surrounding vices.

Athemitos enters the STEP abomination cluster because detestability and unlawfulness overlap in the purity/law framework. However, the term's primary semantic content is legal-transgressive (against divine ordinance) rather than inner-experiential (evoking moral revulsion). Its related words — tithemi, diatithemi, and their compounds — are all covenantal/legislative vocabulary. Athemitos sits at the intersection of law and disgust but does not primarily function as an inner-being abhorrence term.

**Verdict:** Athemitos is a peripheral term. Its connection to abomination is real but secondary to its legal register. It is not a consolidation candidate with any other term.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

The verse sets reveal meaningful differences between sub-terms in the two largest root families.

**TAAV: to.e.vah versus ta.av**

To.e.vah concentrates heavily on God as the subject who regards something as an abomination — the Proverbs formulaic pattern "an abomination to the Lord" is structurally foundational. The term functions as a theological evaluative category applied by God to actions, objects, and dispositions. Ta.av, by contrast, includes God as subject but also occurs frequently with human subjects: Job 19:19 ("All my intimate friends abhor me"), Job 30:10 ("They abhor me; they keep aloof from me"), Deu 23:7 ("You shall not abhor an Edomite"), and Amo 5:10 ("they abhor him who speaks the truth"). In these verses, ta.av functions as the experienced state of being rendered abhorrent in relational and social contexts. The difference is genuine: to.e.vah is primarily a category label in God's evaluative framework; ta.av is the experiential and relational act of abhorring, applicable to human subjects and social contexts.

**SHQTS: she.qets versus shiq.quts**

She.qets concentrates in Leviticus 11's dietary law material — applied to sea creatures without fins and scales, insects, and swarming ground creatures. Shiq.quts concentrates in texts naming specific foreign gods as "the abomination" of their peoples, and in the eschatological "abomination of desolation" material in Daniel. The difference is genuine: she.qets designates unclean creatures in the purity system, while shiq.quts designates idols and cultic objects in the theological polemic against foreign gods. These are distinguishable domains within what the dictionary defines similarly.

**BDEL: bdelugma versus bdeluktos versus bdelussomai**

The BDEL family differences are primarily grammatical (noun/adjective/verb) rather than domain-based. However, bdelugma functions in both eschatological-apocalyptic contexts (Matt 24:15, Rev 17:4–5) and in direct moral theology (Luk 16:15), while bdelussomai functions primarily in a rhetorical context of hypocrisy (Rom 2:22) and a character-verdict context (Rev 21:8). The difference between bdelugma and bdelussomai is not merely grammatical; bdelugma labels the abominable thing or state, while bdelussomai focuses on the act of abhorring or the state of being abhorrent as a character quality.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

Testament coverage is "both," so this section is required.

The NT vocabulary for abomination is drawn almost entirely from the BDEL root family. There is no direct NT equivalent for the OT TAAV or SHQTS noun families as distinct terms; the Greek bdelugma functions as the translation equivalent of both to.e.vah and shiq.quts across the LXX and NT.

**What the OT vocabulary does that the NT does not:**

The OT term set covers a far wider domain range: ritual food laws (she.qets, shiq.qets, pig.gul), social-relational rejection (ta.av in Job), national boundary violations (to.e.vah in Genesis regarding Egyptians and Hebrews), pagan cultic practices across the covenant history of Israel (to.e.vah, shiq.quts throughout Kings, Chronicles, and Ezekiel), and wisdom-ethical vocabulary (to.e.vah across Proverbs). The OT terms function simultaneously as purity law categories, covenant violation markers, and wisdom-ethical evaluations.

The OT also carries a sensory-olfactory register (ba.ash, miph.le.tset) that has no direct NT counterpart in this word set.

**What the NT vocabulary introduces:**

The most significant NT contribution is the internalisation of the abomination category. Luk 16:15 ("what is exalted among men is an abomination in the sight of God") relocates the abomination verdict from external practices and objects to an inner disposition — self-exaltation or pride — specifically contrasting divine and human evaluation. This is a different use of bdelugma than the OT's primary pattern, where the abominable thing is typically an external action, object, or practice.

The NT also carries the eschatological development: Matt 24:15 and Mk 13:14 use bdelugma with explicit reference to Daniel's shiq.quts-based "abomination of desolation," projecting the OT cultic abomination category into an eschatological event. Rev 17:4–5 and Rev 21:27 extend this into Babylon imagery.

**The single most significant OT/NT difference observed in the verse sets:**

In the OT verses, the abomination verdict is primarily applied by God to categories of action, practice, or object, and functions as the basis for law, prohibition, or judgment. In the NT verses, the most concentrated inner-being use (Luk 16:15) applies the abomination verdict to an inner state (self-exaltation) and frames it as a discrepancy between divine and human evaluation — a matter of what God sees in the heart, not what human community or law codifies as prohibited. The OT pattern is categorical-external; the NT deployment in Luk 16:15 is evaluative-internal.
