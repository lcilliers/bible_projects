## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Heart as the Comprehensive Inner Centre

Reading all 1,602 verses across 23 terms before writing confirms the opening claim in the registry description: the heart is the most frequently referenced seat of the inner life in the entire biblical corpus, and it is the most semantically expansive single word in the programme. Where ne.phesh (soul) denotes the living person as an experiencing whole, lev/le.vav/kardia denotes the *centre* of that person — the integrating point where thinking, feeling, choosing, and relating all converge.

The critical finding from reading all 567 lev/le.vav verses (with refs) is this: **the heart in the biblical vocabulary is primarily a cognitive and volitional organ before it is an affective one.** This is the opposite of the modern English usage where "heart" suggests emotion and feeling. In the Hebrew, the most concentrated use of lev is for the act of thinking, planning, intending, and deciding — not for feeling in itself. The affective dimension is present and rich, but it is secondary to the cognitive-volitional function.

### 2.2 The Hebrew Vocabulary — Lev and Le.vav

**H3820A lev (heart) — 331 verses.** Reading all 331 establishes four dominant patterns:

*Pattern 1 — The heart as the seat of cognition and planning.* This is the most distinctive feature of the Hebrew heart relative to modern expectations. Pro 16:1 — "the plans of the heart belong to man." Pro 16:9 — "the heart of man plans his way, but the Lord establishes his steps." Pro 19:21 — "many are the plans in the mind (le.vav) of a man, but it is the purpose of the Lord that will stand." The heart plans, considers, reflects, deliberates: 1Sa 27:1 — "David said in his heart, 'Now I shall perish one day by the hand of Saul'"; Est 6:6 — "Haman came in, and the king said to him, 'What should be done to the man whom the king delights to honor?' And Haman said to his heart..." The heart is where inner speech occurs. The wise heart: Pro 10:8 — "the wise of heart will receive commandments"; Pro 16:21 — "the wise of heart is called discerning"; Pro 16:23 — "the heart of the wise makes his speech judicious." The wisdom tradition concentrates heavily in the heart as the seat of practical intelligence and moral discernment.

*Pattern 2 — The heart as the seat of the will and moral orientation.* Exo 35:5,21,22,25,26,29 — the tabernacle construction passages: "from every man whose heart moves him," "everyone whose heart stirred him and everyone whose spirit moved him," "all who were of a willing heart." The generous heart, the willing heart, the stirred heart — all describing the volitional dimension. 1Sa 10:9 — "God gave him another heart" — the transformation of Saul's will at his anointing. 1Ki 11:4 — "Solomon was old his wives turned away his heart." The heart as what turns, inclines, is given, moves — the directional centre of the person's will. Most critically: Deu 10:16 — "circumcise therefore the foreskin of your heart" — the heart as the site of covenant transformation. The uncircumcised heart is the heart that resists God's covenant; the circumcised heart is the heart opened to obedience.

*Pattern 3 — The heart as the seat of emotion and affective experience.* Gen 6:6 — "it grieved him to his heart." Gen 45:26 — "his heart became numb." 1Sa 1:13 — "Hannah was speaking in her heart." 1Sa 25:36-37 — "his heart was merry... his heart died within him." Psa 22:14 — "my heart is like wax; it is melted within me." Pro 12:25 — "anxiety in a man's heart weighs him down." Pro 13:12 — "hope deferred makes the heart sick." Pro 14:30 — "a tranquil heart gives life to the flesh." Pro 15:13 — "a glad heart makes a cheerful face, but by sorrow of heart the spirit is crushed." The affective range is enormous — grief, joy, anxiety, hope, gladness, sorrow, fear, exultation — but in each case the heart is the *locus* of the affect, not its synonym. The heart feels because it is where the person's experiencing centre is located.

*Pattern 4 — The Pharaoh's heart — the hardened heart as the paradigmatic moral narrative.* Reading the Exodus plague narrative carefully reveals the most concentrated heart-analysis in the OT. Exo 7:3 — "I will harden Pharaoh's heart." Exo 7:13 — "still Pharaoh's heart was hardened." Exo 7:22 — "Pharaoh's heart remained hardened." Exo 8:15 — "he hardened his heart." Exo 9:12 — "the Lord hardened the heart of Pharaoh." Exo 10:1 — "I have hardened his heart and the heart of his servants." Reading all 15+ Pharaoh-heart verses together reveals the most analytically important theological tension in the entire heart vocabulary: the hardening is both Pharaoh's own act and God's act — both descriptions appear side by side in the same narrative. The heart is where human freedom and divine sovereignty meet in the most direct way in the entire corpus. This is not a contradiction to be resolved but a mystery to be held: the heart that hardens itself is simultaneously hardened by God, and both descriptions are required to tell the truth about what happens in the moral encounter with divine claim.

**H3824 le.vav (heart) — 236 verses.** Reading all 236 confirms this is not merely a variant form but a slightly elevated register with its own concentration of theological uses. The defining pattern: le.vav concentrates in the covenant vocabulary more than lev.

Deu 6:5 — "you shall love the Lord your God with all your le.vav and with all your soul and with all your might" — the Shema uses le.vav. Deu 30:6 — "the Lord your God will circumcise your le.vav and the le.vav of your offspring." Jer 17:9 — "the le.vav is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" Jer 31:33 — "I will put my law within them and write it on their le.vav." Eze 36:26 — "I will give you a new le.vav, and a new spirit I will put within you. And I will remove the le.vav of stone from your flesh and give you a le.vav of flesh." 1Sa 13:14 — "the Lord has sought out a man after his own le.vav."

These are the most theologically dense heart verses in the corpus. The le.vav is the site of: the supreme covenant command (love with all), the diagnostic of moral disorder (deceitful above all things), the new covenant promise (law written within), the eschatological transformation (stone heart removed, flesh heart given), and the divine search for the right-hearted person (a man after God's own heart). Reading all 236 le.vav verses confirms: le.vav is the heart as the core of the God-human relationship — where covenant is kept or broken, where sin is most deeply seated, and where transformation must occur.

**The lev/le.vav structural finding:** The two forms together (839 occurrences, 567 verses with refs) create the most comprehensive portrait of the inner life in the OT. The heart is simultaneously: the organ of thought (cognitive), the site of will and decision (volitional), the locus of emotional experience (affective), the centre of moral orientation (ethical/spiritual), and the place of relationship with God (covenantal). No other word in the Hebrew Bible integrates all five dimensions in a single term. This is why the heart cannot be adequately translated as either "mind" or "emotion" — it is the integration of all that makes a person a person, located in a single inner centre.

**H3826 lib.bah (heart, poetic) — 7 verses.** Reading all 7: Psa 7:9 (God tests the minds and hearts/lib.bah), Psa 125:4 (upright in their lib.bah), Pro 15:11 (the hearts of the children of man open before the Lord), Pro 17:3 (the Lord tests hearts/lib.bah), Pro 21:2 (the Lord weighs the heart), Pro 24:12 (he who weighs the heart knows), Eze 16:30 (how sick is your heart/lib.bah). The lib.bah form concentrates specifically in the God-heart examination vocabulary — God weighing, testing, seeing the heart. Its poetic form suits the weighing metaphor: the lib.bah is the heart as moral substance that can be measured on the divine scale.

**H3825 le.vav (Aramaic heart) — 6 verses.** All in Daniel. Dan 2:30 (mystery revealed not by wisdom in Daniel's heart), Dan 4:16 (Nebuchadnezzar's heart changed from a man's to a beast's), Dan 5:20-22 (Belshazzar's heart lifted up, his unhumbled heart), Dan 7:4 (the lion's wings plucked, lifted up on two feet, a human heart/mind given to it). Reading these together: the Aramaic le.vav continues the Hebrew pattern but in the apocalyptic context — the heart as what distinguishes human from animal consciousness. The transformation from beast's heart to human heart and back is the narrative device by which Daniel encodes the theology of power: rulers who forget their creaturely status lose the human heart. This is the most dramatic use of the heart vocabulary in the corpus.

**H3823A la.vav (to captivate/encourage the heart) — 5 verses.** Song 4:9 (twice: "you have captivated my heart, my sister, my bride; you have captivated my heart with one glance of your eyes") — the verbal action of love seizing the heart from outside. 2Sa 13:6 (Amnon's manipulation — feigning illness to draw Tamar to make le.vi.vot cakes — the manipulation of the heart-captivation dynamic inverted into violation). Job 11:12 (a stupid man will get understanding when a wild donkey's colt is born a man — using the verbal form ironically). The verb form establishes the heart as capable of being seized, captivated, and acted upon from outside — the relational vulnerability of the inner person.

**H3512A ka.ah (be disheartened) — 4 verses.** Psa 10:10 (the helpless are crushed and sink down), Psa 109:16 (the brokenhearted pursued to death), Eze 13:22 (disheartening the righteous falsely — the most important verse: the prophets who discourage the heart of the righteous by false prophecy are indicted for this spiritual violence), Dan 11:30 (afraid and withdrawing). Ka.ah is the heart's courage failing — its volitional capacity collapsing under pressure or manipulation. The Eze 13:22 verse is the most significant: disheartening the righteous is placed alongside encouraging the wicked in the same condemnation. This establishes that the inner state of the heart — its courage or its discouragement — is a matter of moral seriousness, not merely personal psychology.

### 2.3 The Somatic Vocabulary — qe.rev and the Inner Body

**H7130H qe.rev (entrails/inner parts) — 212 verses.** Reading all 212 reveals that qe.rev is primarily the preposition "among/in the midst of" — the bulk of the 212 verses use it spatially: "in the midst of the congregation," "in the midst of the land," "in the midst of the people." The heart-relevant uses cluster in the inner-body register:

Psa 5:9 — "their inmost self (qe.rev) is destruction." Psa 36:1 — "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart (qe.rev)." Psa 39:3 — "my heart became hot within me (be.qir.bi)." Psa 51:10 — "create in me a clean heart, O God... renew a right spirit within me (be.qir.bi)." Isa 16:11 — "my inner parts (me.ay qe.rev) moan like a lyre for Moab; my inmost self for Kir-hareseth." Jer 20:9 — "there is in my heart (be.qir.bi) as it were a burning fire shut up in my bones." Eze 36:26-27 — "I will give you a new heart... and put my Spirit within you (be.qir.be.khem)."

The qe.rev vocabulary provides the innermost somatic register for the heart — the place within the within. The fire shut up in the bones (Jer 20:9), the moaning lyre in the inner parts (Isa 16:11), God's Spirit placed within (Eze 36:27) — all use qe.rev to locate the most interior dimension of the person. Most significantly: the new covenant promise (Jer 31:33 — "I will put my law within them") and the eschatological Spirit-promise (Eze 36:27 — "I will put my Spirit within you") both use qe.rev-language. The innermost of the person is the location of covenant renewal and Spirit-habitation.

**H2910 tu.chot (inner parts) — 2 verses.** Job 38:36 — "who has put wisdom in the inward parts (tu.chot)?" Psa 51:6 — "you delight in truth in the inward being (tu.chot), and you teach me wisdom in the secret heart (sa.tum)." Both verses connect the innermost being to wisdom — the deepest inner place is where God both places wisdom (creation) and desires truth (character). The tu.chot is what God can see that humans cannot: the deepest layer of the person's inner life.

**H2436G cheq (bosom/embrace) — 34 verses.** Reading all 34 establishes cheq as the somatic container of the heart in its most intimate register. The bosom is where the child is carried (Num 11:12), where the lamb sleeps (2Sa 12:3), where the nursing mother holds her infant (1Ki 3:20). It is also where iniquity is hidden (Job 31:33 — chov/chov; here from cheq: Psa 89:50 — "I bear in my heart the insults"). Most significant: Psa 35:13 — "I prayed with head bowed on my chest (cheq)" — the bosom as the place of most intimate prayer. Isa 40:11 — "he will gather the lambs in his arms; he will carry them in his bosom" — God's care expressed through the bosom-image. The cheq vocabulary extends the heart into the somatic register of tender holding, nursing, and intimate care.

**G4698 splanchnon (affection/entrails) — 14 verses.** Reading all 14 confirms this as the NT's primary somatic love-and-compassion term. Luk 1:78 — "the tender mercy (splanchnon) of our God." Act 1:18 — the death of Judas by his bowels bursting — the physical organ referenced. 2Co 6:12 — "you are restricted in your own affections (splanchnois)." Phi 1:8 — "I yearn for you all with the affection (splanchnois) of Christ Jesus." Phi 2:1 — "any affection and sympathy." Phm 7 — "the hearts (splanchnon) of the saints have been refreshed." Phm 12 — "sending my very heart (splanchnon)." Phm 20 — "refresh my heart (splanchnon) in Christ." 1Jo 3:17 — "whoever closes his heart (splanchnon) against the needy." Reading these together: splanchnon in the NT functions as the deepest compassion-centre of the person — the inward place from which love and mercy originate. Phm 12 ("sending my very heart") is the most compressed use: Onesimus as Paul's splanchnon, his very inward being sent on his behalf.

**G2292/G2293 tharreō/tharseō (be confident/take heart) — 13 verses.** Reading all 13 of tharreō: 2Co 5:6,8 (we are always of good courage), 2Co 7:16 (I have complete confidence), 2Co 10:1-2 (boldness in writing vs humility in person), Heb 13:6 ("the Lord is my helper; I will not fear"). Reading the tharseō imperative pattern from H1079 verse pool: Mat 9:2 ("take heart, your sins are forgiven"), Mat 9:22 ("take heart, daughter"), Mat 14:27 / Mar 6:50 ("take heart; it is I"), Mar 10:49 ("take heart; get up"), Joh 16:33 ("take heart; I have overcome the world"), Act 23:11 ("take courage"). The pattern is invariant: tharsei/tharseō is Jesus's direct address to the heart that has failed, the person in crisis, the disciple terrified by the storm. It is the NT's closest parallel to the OT "fear not" pattern — the divine word to the failing heart: "take heart." Joh 16:33 is the most comprehensive: "In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world." The grounds for the commanded courage is not the person's own inner resource but Christ's victory.

**G4641 sklērokardia (hardness of heart) — 3 verses.** Mat 19:8 (because of your hardness of heart Moses allowed divorce), Mar 10:5 (same), Mar 16:14 (Jesus rebuked the disciples for their hardness of heart and unbelief). Reading these alongside the Pharaoh hardened-heart narrative and the OT uncircumcised-heart vocabulary: sklērokardia is the NT's technical term for the moral-volitional condition that resists divine claim. In Mat 19:8 Jesus uses it to locate the disciples' contemporary failure in the same category as Israel's historical moral compromise. The hardened heart is not simply stubborn feeling but the volitional resistance of the inner person to the transforming work of God.

**G2589 kardiognōstēs (heart-knower) — 2 verses.** Act 1:24 (the community prays — "you, Lord, who know the hearts of all") and Act 15:8 ("God, who knows the heart, bore witness to them"). This compound — kardia + gnōstēs — is unique to Acts and names God by his most intimate attribute relative to the heart: he is the one who knows all hearts. It is used in both contexts of discernment: choosing between Matthias and Barnabas (1:24) and the decision about Gentile inclusion (15:8). The kardiognōstēs is invoked when human judgement cannot see what God sees. God's heart-knowledge is the ground of trust when the inner life is opaque.

**G1573 ekkakeō (to lose heart) — 6 verses.** Luk 18:1 (the parable given because "they ought always to pray and not lose heart"), 2Co 4:1,16 (we do not lose heart), Gal 6:9 (do not grow weary of doing good), Eph 3:13 (do not lose heart over what I am suffering), 2Th 3:13 (do not grow weary in doing good). Reading all 6: ekkakeō is always used in the negative — the exhortation is always "do not lose heart." It describes the failure of inner persistence under trial — the heart that gives out. Its opposite is the endurance vocabulary (hupomenō). The antidote given is always relational and eschatological: God's mercy (2Co 4:1), the renewing of the inner self (2Co 4:16), the coming harvest (Gal 6:9), God's glory in suffering (Eph 3:13), the coming of the Lord (2Th 3:13). The failing heart is not addressed by inner resource but by the larger frame that gives the heart reason to persist.

**H5034B na.vel (to wither) — 21 verses.** Reading all 21 confirms this as the vocabulary of inner collapse and exhaustion: 2Sa 22:46 / Psa 18:45 — "foreigners lost heart (yi.ba.lu) and came trembling out of their fortresses." Psa 1:3 — the righteous is "like a tree planted by streams of water... its leaf does not wither (yi.vol)." Isa 40:7-8 — "the grass withers (na.vel), the flower fades... but the word of our God will stand forever." The term's connection to the heart-registry is the withering of inner courage under pressure — the heart that fades. Its primary use (leaf/flower withering) provides the image for inner collapse: the heart that loses its vitality fades like vegetation cut off from its water supply.

**G1457 enkainizō (to inaugurate) — 2 verses.** Heb 9:18 and 10:20 — the new covenant inaugurated by blood; the "new and living way" opened through Christ's flesh. The heart-connection: this term appears in the registry because the new covenant promises (law written on the heart, new heart) are what the new covenant inaugurates. The connection is theological rather than lexical — enkainizō names the event that creates the new-heart condition.

**G2155 eusplanchnos (compassionate) — 2 verses.** Eph 4:32 — "be kind to one another, tenderhearted (eusplanchnos), forgiving one another." 1Pe 3:8 — "have unity of mind, sympathy, brotherly love, a tender heart (eusplanchnos), and a humble mind." The compound (eu + splanchnon — well-entrailed) describes the person whose inward compassion-centre is functioning correctly. It appears in both passages as a community virtue, alongside kindness, forgiveness, and humility.

**H2504 cha.lats (loin) — 10 verses.** The loins as the seat of vitality and generativity: Gen 35:11 (a nation shall come from your loins), 1Ki 8:19 (your son who shall come from your loins). The heart-loin connection appears in the somatic vocabulary of inner strength: "dress for action like a man" (Job 38:3, 40:7) — the loins girded for combat as a metaphor for the heart prepared for divine encounter. Isa 11:5 — "righteousness shall be the belt of his waist (cha.lats), and faithfulness the belt of his loins" — the Messianic king whose inner character is the belt of his action. The loin vocabulary connects the heart's moral orientation to its embodied action.

**H7907 sekh.vi / H3821 lev / H2243 chov — 1 verse each.** Job 38:36 — "who has put wisdom in the inward parts (tu.chot) or given understanding to the mind (sekh.vi)?" The sekh.vi is the inner cognitive faculty — wisdom in its deepest seat. H3821 lev — Dan 7:28 — "my thoughts greatly alarmed me, and my color changed." The rare occurrence in Daniel. H2243 chov — Job 31:33 — hiding iniquity in the heart/breast. These single-verse terms all contribute to the theme of the innermost hidden life of the person.

### 2.4 The Greek Heart — Kardia

**G2588 kardia (heart) — 151 verses.** Reading all 151 confirms: the Greek kardia translates and extends the Hebrew lev with remarkable fidelity to the semantic range. The NT kardia covers all the same dimensions as the Hebrew lev — cognitive (questioning in their hearts, the heart that believes), volitional (the heart that chooses obedience or rebellion), affective (troubled hearts, burning hearts), moral (pure in heart, hardened hearts), and relational (Christ dwelling in the heart). The key extensions:

*The heart as the Word's reception site:* Mat 13:19 — "the evil one comes and snatches away what is sown in his heart." Luk 8:15 — "those who, hearing the word, hold it fast in an honest and good heart." The kardia as where the seed of the Word either takes root or is snatched away. The parable of the sower is fundamentally a heart-analysis.

*The heart as the source of speech and action:* Mat 12:34 — "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks." Mat 15:18-19 — "what comes out of the mouth proceeds from the heart, and this defiles a person. For out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery..." Luk 6:45 — "the good person out of the good treasure of his heart produces good." The heart is identified as the ultimate source of what the person does and says — not the surface behaviour but the inner treasury from which it is drawn.

*The heart as the site of divine indwelling:* Rom 5:5 — "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit." 2Co 1:22 — "who has put his seal on us and given us his Spirit in our hearts as a guarantee." Gal 4:6 — "God has sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, crying 'Abba! Father!'" Eph 3:17 — "that Christ may dwell in your hearts through faith." The NT's most distinctive contribution to the heart vocabulary: the heart is where the Spirit lives, where the Son dwells, where divine love is poured. The heart is not merely the human moral centre — it is the address where God takes up residence.

*The heart as the organ of faith:* Rom 10:9-10 — "if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. For with the heart one believes and is justified." Faith is located specifically in the heart — not in intellectual assent alone, not in external confession alone, but in the heart's own act of believing. This is the NT's most concentrated statement about the heart as the seat of saving faith.

*Mary's heart — the model of the pondering heart:* Luk 2:19 — "Mary treasured up all these things, pondering them in her heart." Luk 2:51 — "his mother treasured up all these things in her heart." The repeated Lucan portrait of Mary's heart as the place where the mystery of the Incarnation is held, slowly processed, and returned to — the pondering heart as the model of contemplative engagement with divine mystery.

*The burning heart — Luk 24:32:* "Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road, while he opened to us the Scriptures?" The Emmaus disciples' retrospective recognition — the burning heart as the unrecognised experience of presence. This is the most intimate affective use of kardia in the Gospels: the heart's recognition of Christ even before the mind understands.

### 2.5 The Structural Finding — The Heart as the Inner Integration Point

Reading all 1,602 verses confirms: the heart is the programme's most integrative single word. Every other inner-being characteristic finds its ground in the heart:

- Fear: the heart that trembles (1Sa 28:5), or does not fear (Psa 27:3)
- Love: love the Lord with all your heart (Deu 6:5, Mat 22:37)
- Anger: the anger that lodges in the heart (Ecc 7:9), the heart that plans evil (Pro 6:14,18)
- Soul: the soul that pours itself out in the heart (Psa 42:4), the law on the heart (Jer 31:33)
- Hope: hope deferred makes the heart sick (Pro 13:12)
- Grief: sorrow of heart (Pro 15:13), the heart broken (Psa 34:18)
- Wisdom: wisdom rests in the heart (Pro 14:33), the heart that seeks knowledge (Pro 15:14)

The heart is not one characteristic among many — it is the integrating site where all others converge. Pro 4:23 states this explicitly: *from it flow the springs of life.* All the other inner-being characteristics are streams that flow from this central spring. This is the most important programme-level finding from the heart analysis: the heart is the topology of the whole inner life.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — Spirit, Soul, Body

**Primary circle:** Soul dimension — but the heart is the meeting point of all three.

**Cognitive dimension (primary):** The most dominant use across 567 lev/le.vav verses. The heart thinks, plans, considers, reflects, and decides. This is opposite to modern English where "heart" suggests feeling over thinking. The wisdom literature — Proverbs concentrated — makes the thinking-heart explicit in hundreds of verses.

**Volitional dimension (co-primary):** The heart chooses, turns, inclines, hardens, softens. The circumcision of the heart vocabulary (Deu 10:16, 30:6, Jer 4:4, Rom 2:29) and the new heart promise (Eze 36:26) both target the volitional core of the person — the place where the capacity for genuine obedience resides.

**Affective dimension:** Present and rich — the glad heart, the sick heart, the trembling heart, the burning heart. But secondary to the cognitive-volitional in the Hebrew. In the NT kardia vocabulary, the affective register is more prominent (troubled hearts, sorrowful hearts, burning hearts) but still not primary.

**Spirit dimension:** The heart is where the Spirit dwells (Rom 5:5, 2Co 1:22, Gal 4:6, Eph 3:17), where Christ lives (Eph 3:17), where the law is written on the new covenant (Jer 31:33, Heb 8:10, 10:16). The Spirit-heart interface is the most important boundary question in the whole registry — more than any other word, the heart is where spirit and soul meet and cannot be separated.

**Somatic dimension:** The heart's somatic vocabulary is extensive — qe.rev (inner parts), splanchnon (bowels/affections), cheq (bosom), cha.lats (loins), and the affective expressions that have somatic forms (the melting heart, the burning heart, the sick heart, the trembling heart). The body is not separate from the heart but its outer expression and medium.

### 3.2 The Six Dimensions — Complete Engagement

The heart is the only characteristic in the programme that genuinely engages all six dimensions simultaneously and with equal weight:

**Feeling (Affective):** The burning heart (Luk 24:32), the troubled heart (Joh 14:1), the sick heart (Pro 13:12), the glad heart (Pro 15:13) — all affective heart-states.
**Thinking (Cognitive):** The planning heart (Pro 16:1), the heart that says in itself (Gen 8:21, 1Sa 27:1), the heart as wisdom's seat (Pro 14:33), the searching heart (Ecc 7:25).
**Choosing (Volitional):** The heart that turns away (1Ki 11:3), the willing heart (Exo 35:5), the hardened heart (Exo 7:13), the circumcised heart (Deu 30:6).
**Relating (Relational):** The hearts knit together (1Sa 18:1), the heart stolen (2Sa 15:6), the heart captivated (Song 4:9), the heart shared in community (Act 4:32).
**Orienting (Spiritual/God-ward):** The heart that seeks God (Deu 4:29), the pure heart that sees God (Mat 5:8), the heart where Christ dwells (Eph 3:17), the heart that believes (Rom 10:10).
**Being (Moral/Character):** The pure heart (Mat 5:8, Psa 24:4, 73:1), the upright heart (Psa 7:10, 78:72), the deceitful heart (Jer 17:9), the heart after God's own heart (1Sa 13:14).

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God as the Heart's Creator, Examiner, and Transformer

**God fashions all hearts:** Psa 33:15 — "he who fashions the hearts of them all and observes all their deeds." God's relation to the heart is not merely that of observer — he is the maker. Every heart that exists is a divine artefact. This grounding of the heart in divine creation means the heart's inner life is not autonomous — it is creaturely, accountable, and made for the relationship with its maker.

**God searches and knows the heart:** Jer 17:10 — "I the Lord search the heart and test the mind." 1Ki 8:39 — "you, you only, know the hearts of all the children of mankind." Psa 139:23 — "search me, O God, and know my heart!" The divine heart-search is not intrusive surveillance but the condition of genuine relationship: only the God who knows the heart can respond to it appropriately. Kardiognōstēs (Act 1:24, 15:8) — God as heart-knower — is invoked precisely when human discernment fails and divine knowledge must substitute.

**God gives a new heart — the most radical divine-heart action:** 1Sa 10:9 — "God gave him another heart" — Saul's transformation at anointing. Psa 51:10 — "create in me a clean heart, O God." Eze 11:19, 36:26 — "I will give them one heart... I will remove the heart of stone from their flesh and give them a heart of flesh." The promise of the new heart is the deepest form of the heart's relationship with God: not merely searched, not merely commanded, but *replaced*. The heart of stone — the hard, impervious, unyielding heart — is the condition from which covenant violation flows; the heart of flesh — responsive, alive, tender — is the eschatological gift. This is the most radical divine action in the inner-being vocabulary: God does not merely instruct the heart, he creates a new one.

**God inclines the heart:** 1Ki 8:58 — "that he may incline our hearts to him, to walk in all his ways." Psa 119:36 — "incline my heart to your testimonies." The divine inclination of the heart is the intersection of human prayer and divine action — the person asks God to do what the person's own will cannot accomplish alone. The heart that needs to be inclined is the heart whose natural direction is away from God; the inclination is the new-covenant action of grace redirecting the will.

**God writes on the heart — the new covenant:** Jer 31:33 — "I will put my law within them and write it on their hearts." Heb 8:10, 10:16 — the new covenant fulfilment cited. 2Co 3:3 — "you show that you are a letter from Christ... written not with ink but with the Spirit of the living God, not on tablets of stone but on tablets of human hearts." The writing on the heart is the most intimate form of divine instruction: not external law to be obeyed but internal orientation to be lived from the inside out.

### 4.2 Jesus and the Heart

**Mat 11:29 — "I am gentle and lowly in heart."** The only self-description of Jesus's inner character in the Gospels — and it is a heart-description. The meekness and lowliness of the heart are the qualities Jesus names as the ground of the rest offered to weary souls. His heart-character becomes the pattern for the community.

**Mat 5:8 — "Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God."** The most concentrated beatitude for the heart: the pure heart is the condition for the vision of God. The cognitive and moral dimensions converge — pure in heart means genuinely oriented toward God, without the duplicity of the double-minded person.

**Mat 15:18-19 — "out of the heart come evil thoughts..."** Jesus's diagnosis of the heart as the source of defilement establishes the heart's primacy in the moral anatomy: external observance cannot purify what flows from an impure heart.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 The Heart's Somatic Architecture

Reading all somatic-linked terms (H7130H qe.rev, H2436G cheq, G4698 splanchnon, H2504 cha.lats, H5034B na.vel, H2910 tu.chot, H7907 sekh.vi, H2243 chov, and the affective heart expressions) together reveals a three-layer somatic architecture:

**Layer 1 — The innermost hidden being:** H2910 tu.chot (inward parts), Psa 51:6 ("truth in the inward being, wisdom in the secret heart"). The deepest layer, visible only to God. The place where divine wisdom is implanted at creation and where the Spirit writes the new covenant law.

**Layer 2 — The interior visceral body:** H7130H qe.rev (entrails/midst), G4698 splanchnon (bowels/affections), H2243 chov (breast/bosom). The layer of the body's interior — the organs that respond viscerally to emotion and relationship. Jer 20:9 — the fire in the bones (qe.rev). Gen 43:30 — the bowels (me.a.yim) stirred. Phm 12 — the splanchnon sent as a living person.

**Layer 3 — The somatic surface expression:** H2436G cheq (bosom/embrace), H2504 cha.lats (loins), H5034B na.vel (withering). The outer embodied expression of the heart's inner states — the bosom that holds, the loins that act, the leaf that fades. What happens in the hidden heart eventually reaches the surface of the body as expression.

**The melting heart — the most concentrated somatic expression:** Reading across all heart terms, the "melting heart" (lev na.mas — Jos 2:11, 5:1, 7:5; Psa 22:14; Nah 2:10) is the most dramatic somatic heart-expression: the heart becomes like wax, like water — its capacity to sustain volitional action collapses under overwhelming threat. This is the somatic dimension of cha.rah/cha.ron (burning anger) read in reverse: the heart that burns with courage melts under overwhelming fear. Both use thermal imagery for the heart's inner state. Nah 2:10 — "hearts melt and knees tremble; anguish is in all loins; all faces grow pale" — the full cascade from inner (heart melt) through somatic (knees tremble, loins anguished) to visible surface (faces pale).

### 5.2 The Burning Heart — Luk 24:32

"Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road?" The burning heart as unrecognised experience of divine presence — the somatic register of the heart's encounter with Christ, felt before it is known. This verse is analytically unique: the heart's somatic experience (burning) is the point of recognition that cognitive processing had missed. The burning-heart pattern connects to: the fire in Jeremiah's bones (Jer 20:9 — the word of God creating somatic burning in the prophet), the warming in cha.mam (anger's pre-expression heat), and the broader fire-vocabulary for the Spirit's presence. The heart burns when it is in the presence of divine reality — whether it knows this or not.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Love and the Heart

Reading the heart vocabulary in the love context confirms: the heart is love's primary location and instrument. Deu 6:5 — "love the Lord your God with all your heart (le.vav)." Mat 22:37 — "love the Lord your God with all your heart (kardia)." The Shema is the heart's primary relational command. Every love-with-all-your-heart passage positions the heart as the totality that love requires — you cannot love truly without the heart's full engagement.

Song 4:9 — "you have captivated my heart, my sister, my bride" — la.vav as the verbal action of love seizing the heart. 2Sa 15:6 — "Absalom stole the hearts of the men of Israel" — the political manipulation of mass affection using the same heart-stealing vocabulary. The heart can be captivated in love (Song 4:9) and stolen in manipulation (2Sa 15:6) — the same relational vulnerability.

### 6.2 The Hardened Heart — The Most Destructive Relational State

Reading the hardened-heart vocabulary across the full term set: the hardened heart is the most comprehensively destructive inner state in the biblical vocabulary. It produces:
- Covenant violation (the Pharaoh narrative — 15+ verses)
- Rejection of the Word (Mar 6:52 — their hearts were hardened)
- Failure of compassion (Mar 3:5 — hardness of heart as the ground of the synagogue's refusal to heal)
- Resistance to transformation (Eze 3:7 — the house of Israel has a hard forehead and a stubborn heart)
- Eschatological judgment (Mat 19:8 — the Law's accommodation to hardness of heart)

The hardened heart is the inner state that makes all other wrong actions possible — it is the root condition of covenant failure.

### 6.3 The Five Causative Pathways for the Heart

**God → heart:** The most active pathway in the registry. God hardens hearts (Exo 7:3 — Pharaoh), softens hearts (2Ki 22:19 — Josiah's penitent heart), gives new hearts (1Sa 10:9, Eze 36:26), writes on hearts (Jer 31:33), pours love into hearts (Rom 5:5), sends the Spirit into hearts (Gal 4:6), puts things into hearts (Ezr 7:27, Neh 2:12, 7:5, 2Co 8:16).

**God's Word → heart:** The second major pathway. Pro 2:2 — "inclining your heart to understanding." Jer 15:16 — "your words were found, and I ate them, and your words became to me a joy and the delight of my heart." Heb 4:12 — the Word divides soul and spirit, piercing to the heart.

**Human → human:** 2Sa 15:6 — Absalom stealing hearts. H3823A la.vav — the captivation of the heart in love or manipulation. Pro 12:25 — "a good word makes the heart glad." Eze 13:22 — false prophecy disheartening the righteous.

**Circumstances → heart:** Pro 13:12 — hope deferred makes the heart sick. Luk 21:34 — the hearts weighed down by the cares of this life. Pro 25:20 — singing songs to a heavy heart. External events, news, and physical conditions all affect the heart.

**Heart → heart (contagion):** 2Sa 17:10 — "the heart of the valiant man, whose heart is like the heart of a lion, will utterly melt with fear, for all Israel knows that your father is a mighty man." The courageous heart and the fearful heart both affect others. The melting-heart contagion (Deu 20:8 — the fearful soldier melting others' hearts) operates through the heart as the inner relational medium.

### 6.4 Cross-Registry Links — Verse-Level Grounding

**Heart ↔ Soul (reg 182):** Multiple links. Deu 6:5 — "heart and soul" as the comprehensive inner-being pair. Jer 32:41 — God with all his "heart and soul." Mat 22:37 — love with heart and soul and mind. The heart and soul are the two inner-being centres — heart as cognitive-volitional, soul as experiential-desiring. Heb 4:12 — the Word divides soul and spirit and joints and marrow — the soul-heart boundary is present but the word that makes the distinction is the living Word.

**Heart ↔ Wisdom (reg 174):** H2450 cha.kham already in CRL as SHARED_TERM. Pro 14:33 — "wisdom rests in the heart of a man of understanding." Pro 2:10 — "wisdom will come into your heart." The wise heart is the central wisdom-vocabulary figure. The heart is wisdom's dwelling place.

**Heart ↔ Conscience (reg 26):** H3629 kil.yah in CRL as SHARED_TERM. Jer 11:20 — "Lord of hosts, who judges righteously, who tests the heart and the mind/kidneys." 1Sa 25:31 — "pangs of conscience" (literally: stumbling-block of heart). The heart and conscience vocabulary overlaps at the inner-examination register.

**Heart ↔ Fear (reg 61):** The hardened/melting heart cluster. The heart that does not fear (Psa 27:3 — "my heart shall not fear") vs the trembling heart (1Sa 4:13, 28:5). The fear registry's cross-registry note about qe.rev/ra.gaz confirmed here.

**Heart ↔ Desire (reg 43):** H2836A cha.shaq in CRL as SHARED_TERM. The heart's desire (Psa 21:2, 37:4 — "the desires of your heart"). The heart as the locus of desire confirmed across the appetite vocabulary.

**Heart ↔ Joy (reg 97):** H8055 sa.mach in CRL as SHARED_TERM. Psa 4:7 — "you have put more joy in my heart." Pro 15:13 — "a glad heart makes a cheerful face." The joyful heart as the heart functioning correctly.

**Heart ↔ Sorrow (reg 154):** H3513G ka.ved in CRL. Pro 15:13 — "by sorrow of heart the spirit is crushed." The sorrowful heart as the heart under grief's weight.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — Pro 4:23

*"Keep your heart with all vigilance, for from it flow the springs of life."*

Having read all 1,602 verses, this verse stands as the most structurally complete statement about the heart in the entire corpus. Three elements:

"Keep your heart" — the command to guard, protect, maintain the heart. The verb (na.tsar) is the same used for keeping the divine commandments and for maintaining a military watch. The heart requires active keeping — it is not self-maintaining.

"With all vigilance" (be.khol-mish.mar — "with all keeping/guarding") — the superlative: above all other things that require keeping, the heart requires keeping supremely. This is the wisdom tradition's most concentrated statement of the heart's priority.

"For from it flow the springs of life" — the generative rationale. The heart is a spring (ma.kor) — a source point, a fountain head. From it flow the "springs of life" (tots.aot cha.yim). Everything that constitutes the person's life — their words, actions, relationships, choices, feelings — flows from this source. Guard the source and you guard everything that flows from it. Neglect the source and everything downstream is corrupted.

Reading this against the full term inventory: every dimension of the heart vocabulary flows from this verse. The cognitive heart (planning, thinking) is one of the springs. The volitional heart (choosing, inclining) is another. The affective heart (feeling, experiencing) is another. The moral heart (the pure/deceitful/upright heart) is another. All flow from the one source. The verse is the registry's summary and the programme's most important heart-definition.

### 7.2 Jer 17:9-10 — The Deceitful and Searched Heart

*"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it? I the Lord search the heart and test the mind."*

Having read the 236 le.vav verses, these two verses stand as the most concentrated statement of the heart's moral condition and the divine response to it. Three movements:

The diagnosis — "deceitful above all things." The le.vav's fundamental disorder is not weakness but deception — specifically self-deception. The heart that is most deceitful is not primarily deceitful toward others but toward itself. It produces its own self-justifying narrative. The Pharaoh narrative confirms: the heart that hardens itself does not perceive itself as resisting God — it provides itself with reasons.

"Desperately sick" (a.nush — incurable) — the heart's condition is beyond self-healing. This is not pessimism but realism: the organ that would do the healing is itself the diseased organ. A sick heart cannot cure its own sickness.

The divine answer — "I the Lord search the heart and test the mind." The one who can truly know the heart is the one who made it. The divine search is not punitive but diagnostic — and ultimately redemptive, as the verses that follow (Jer 17:12-18) move into trust and petition. The God who searches the heart is also the God who gives the new heart (Jer 31:33).

### 7.3 Eze 36:26 — The New Heart Promise

*"And I will give you a new heart, and a new spirit I will put within you. And I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh."*

Having read all 236 le.vav verses and all 151 kardia verses, this verse stands as the most radical divine-heart promise in the corpus. The two movements:

The removal — "I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh." The heart of stone (lev ha.e.ven) is the hardened, impervious heart of Pharaoh and the wilderness generation — the heart that will not be moved by divine claim, that resists covenant. Its removal is entirely divine initiative: the person does not soften their own heart. The removal is unilateral grace.

The gift — "I will give you a heart of flesh (lev ba.sar)." The flesh heart is not the weak or sinful heart — it is the responsive, living, tender heart that can feel the weight of God's word and respond to it. The flesh-heart is the Sabbath-refreshed heart (na.phash), the heart that can be captivated by love (la.vav), the heart that burns within when Christ speaks (Luk 24:32).

The new heart promise is the deepest form of the heart's relationship with God: not commanded, not merely sought after, but created. This is the eschatological answer to Jer 17:9 — the deceitful, incurably sick heart is not reformed but replaced.

### 7.4 Psa 51:10 — Create in Me a Clean Heart

*"Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me."*

Having read all 331 lev verses and all 2 tu.chot verses (Psa 51:6 and Job 38:36), this verse stands as the most concentrated personal prayer for the new-heart condition. The verb "create" (ba.ra') is the same used in Gen 1 for the divine creation of the universe — reserved in the OT exclusively for divine action. The psalmist does not ask for help purifying their own heart. They ask God to do what only God can do: create.

The verse pairs the clean heart (lev ta.hor) with the right spirit (ru.ach na.khon) — explicitly linking heart and spirit as what needs renewal. Psa 51:6 (tu.chot) precedes this: "you delight in truth in the inward being, and you teach me wisdom in the secret heart." The cleansing of the heart works from the inside out — from the tu.chot (innermost hidden being) through the lev (the heart's experienced centre) to the ru.ach (the spirit that directs the whole). The tripartite structure of the prayer mirrors the tripartite structure of the person.

### 7.5 Mat 5:8 — Pure in Heart

*"Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God."*

Having read all 151 kardia verses, this beatitude stands as the NT's most concentrated statement about the heart as the organ of the vision of God. Pure in heart (katharoi tē kardia) — the heart without inner division, without the duplicity of double-mindedness, without hidden agendas. The pure heart is not the sinless heart but the undivided heart — the heart wholly oriented toward God.

"For they shall see God" — the eschatological promise. But the seeing is not merely future: Joh 14:9 — "whoever has seen me has seen the Father." The pure heart that loves and follows Christ is already, in some sense, seeing God. The vision is both present (in knowing Christ) and future (in the full disclosure of God's glory). The pure heart is the prerequisite for both.

This verse, read against Jer 17:9 (the deceitfully sick heart) and Psa 51:10 (create in me a clean heart), maps the trajectory: the deceitful heart → the divinely created clean heart → the pure heart that sees God. The beatitude is the telos of the new-heart promise.

### 7.6 Luk 24:32 — The Burning Heart

*"Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road, while he opened to us the Scriptures?"*

Having read all 151 kardia verses, this is the most intimate affective-somatic heart verse in the NT. Three analytically significant features:

The burning preceded recognition. The disciples did not know it was Christ — yet their hearts burned. The body's inner thermal response registered the divine presence before the cognitive faculty processed it. This is the fast-pathway pattern from the fear and anger registries operating in the love-and-recognition register: the heart knows before the mind does.

The burning was produced by Scripture-opening. Not merely by the presence of a stranger, but specifically by the opening of the Scriptures — the Word being interpreted. Heb 4:12 (the Word as a living two-edged sword) and Jer 20:9 (the Word as fire in the bones) confirm: the Word produces somatic registration in the heart. Scripture-engagement is a heart-event, not merely a cognitive one.

The retrospective recognition — "did not our hearts burn?" — the heart's evidence reviewed as the grounds for confidence. The heart that burned is the witness that something real occurred, even before it was understood. The burning heart is the somatic testimony to encounter.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field

**Cognitive neuroscience and psychology of the self** (primary) — the question of what the "centre" of the person is, where integration of thought, feeling, and will occurs, maps directly onto the heart vocabulary's synthetic function.
**Psychology of emotion-cognition integration** (secondary) — the relationship between thinking and feeling in the inner life.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — The integrative function of prefrontal cortex (Damásio, *Descartes' Error*, 1994; *The Feeling of What Happens*, 1999).** The prefrontal cortex integrates emotional signals (from the amygdala and body) with cognitive processing — it is the neural substrate of what the biblical writers call the heart's integrative function. Phineas Gage and Damásio's patients with prefrontal damage could think but could not decide — they had lost the integration of feeling and reason that the biblical heart describes. The heart as the seat of practical wisdom (the wise heart of Proverbs) maps onto prefrontal-cortical integration.

**Finding 2 — The embodied self and the heart's metaphorical primacy (Lakoff and Johnson, *Philosophy in the Flesh*, 1999; Feldman Barrett, *How Emotions Are Made*, 2017).** Cross-cultural studies show that the heart is the universal metaphorical locus for the integrated self — across cultures, the heart is where people locate the "real me," not the head. This is not scientifically naïve: Lakoff and Johnson argue that conceptual metaphors are grounded in bodily experience. The biblical vocabulary's use of lev/kardia for the whole inner-life centre is not merely theological but reflects a deep embodied metaphor: the heart as the body's rhythmic centre whose states (racing, sinking, lifting, breaking) most reliably signal the person's true inner condition.

**Finding 3 — Self-deception and the unconscious (cognitive psychology broadly).** Jer 17:9 — "the heart is deceitful above all things" — anticipates cognitive psychology's extensive documentation of self-deception, motivated reasoning, and cognitive bias. Daniel Kahneman's System 1 (fast, automatic, unconscious) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, conscious) together produce the self-deceptive patterns the biblical heart-diagnosis describes. The heart's deceptiveness is not primarily malicious lying but the motivated cognition that presents the self to itself in the most favourable light.

**Finding 4 — Neuroplasticity and heart-transformation (Doidge, *The Brain That Changes Itself*, 2007; Siegel, *Mindsight*, 2010).** The biblical promise of a "new heart" — the transformation of the person's most fundamental inner orientation — finds a mechanistic parallel in neuroplasticity: the brain's capacity to form new neural pathways through relationship, practice, and experience. Attachment relationships literally reshape the brain. The new heart of Eze 36:26 is not a violation of the neural architecture but its transformation through the most powerful relational encounter available — relationship with God. Siegel's "mindsight" — the capacity to observe and shape one's own mental states — is a partial analogue to the wisdom tradition's "keep your heart with all vigilance."

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Structural convergence:** The biblical heart as the integrative centre of thought, feeling, and will maps onto the prefrontal-cortical integration that neuroscience identifies as the substrate of executive function, emotional regulation, and moral judgment. Both accounts resist the Cartesian division of reason and emotion — both insist that thinking and feeling are integrated in the one inner centre.

**Phenomenological convergence:** The burning heart (Luk 24:32), the melting heart (Psa 22:14), the sick heart (Pro 13:12) — all describe somatic experiences that modern cardiac psychology and emotion science document: the heart's physical rhythms change with emotional states; the somatic experience of fear, love, grief, and hope all involve cardiovascular phenomena. The biblical vocabulary describes real somatic experiences.

**Register divergence:** Science describes the heart's metaphorical function as grounded in bodily experience (the organ that races and sinks and lifts). The biblical vocabulary uses the heart as the locus of covenant relationship, divine indwelling, and eschatological transformation. These are not competing descriptions but different registers: science describes the somatic metaphor; the Bible describes what the person whose heart is the covenant-meeting-point is designed to be.

**Genuine divergence — the new heart:** Eze 36:26 (remove heart of stone, give heart of flesh) and 1Sa 10:9 (God gave him another heart) describe a transformative divine action on the person's inner life that has no scientific parallel. Neuroplasticity describes gradual reshaping through relationship and experience; the biblical text describes instantaneous or covenant-effected replacement. The mechanism is not neurological but pneumatological — the Spirit creates the new heart. This is genuine divergence: the Spirit-effected new heart is outside the scope of neuroscience.

**Genuine divergence — divine heart-knowledge:** The kardiognōstēs (God who knows all hearts — Act 1:24, 15:8) describes a divine attribute — comprehensive and perfect knowledge of every person's inner life — that has no scientific parallel. Science can measure neural correlates of inner states; God knows the heart directly. This is not a competition but a difference in what is being described.

## Section 9: Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circles:** Soul (Circle Two) and Relational Environment (Circle One) — the heart is the meeting point of all three circles simultaneously.

**Circle Two placement:** The heart engages all three circle-two dimensions:
- Spirit: the heart where the Spirit dwells (Rom 5:5, Gal 4:6), where the law is written (Jer 31:33), where the new spirit is placed (Eze 36:26-27)
- Soul: the heart as the experiencing, desiring, planning, choosing inner person — the heart's full semantic range is soulish
- Body: the somatic vocabulary of the heart (qe.rev, splanchnon, cheq, na.vel) grounds the heart in the body

**Two-forms structure:**

*Disordered form:* The hardened/deceitful heart — the le.vav of Pharaoh, the le.vav of Jer 17:9, the sklērokardia of the Gospels. The heart that has turned in on itself, resists divine claim, self-deceives. The heart of stone: impervious, unyielding, unable to receive.

*Rightly-ordered form:* The clean/pure/whole heart — lev sha.lem (whole heart, 1Ki 8:61; 15:14), kardia katharā (pure heart, Mat 5:8; 1Ti 1:5), the heart wholly given to the Lord. The heart of flesh: responsive, alive, tender, open to the Word and the Spirit.

**Transformation dynamic:** Deu 30:6 (God circumcises the heart) → Jer 31:33 (God writes the law on the heart) → Eze 36:26 (God removes the stone heart and gives a flesh heart) → Heb 10:22 (draw near with hearts sprinkled clean) → Rom 5:5 (love poured into the heart by the Spirit). The transformation is entirely divine initiative — the person does not transform their own heart. The person keeps the heart (Pro 4:23), prays for its cleansing (Psa 51:10), and turns toward God (Joe 2:12-13) — but the transformation itself is God's action.

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Programme-level structural finding — The heart as the integration point of the entire inner-being vocabulary.** Reading all 1,602 heart verses confirms: Pro 4:23's claim that "the springs of life" flow from the heart is not hyperbole but the most precise summary of the heart's function in the programme's data. Every other characteristic (fear, anger, love, soul, desire, hope, shame, grief) finds its ground and expression in the heart. This is a Session D finding of the highest order: the heart is not a characteristic among others but the *topological centre* from which all other characteristics flow. The programme's concentric circle model may need a layer within Circle Three: the heart as the centre of the inner characteristics, not merely one of them.

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "integration."** Modern psychology's concept of psychological integration — the coherence of the person's inner life, the capacity for all parts to work together rather than in conflict — maps onto the biblical vocabulary for the whole/pure/undivided heart (lev sha.lem, kardia katharā, the opposite of dipsuchos). The biblical vocabulary does not have a single word for integration but the heart-vocabulary collectively describes it. Session D should examine whether "integration" names what the whole-heart tradition is pointing toward.

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "self-knowledge."** The heart's opacity to itself (Jer 17:9 — who can understand it?) and its transparency to God (kardiognōstēs) generates the question of self-knowledge — the capacity to know one's own inner life. The biblical account suggests: full self-knowledge is impossible apart from God's knowing — "search me, O God, and know my heart" (Psa 139:23) is the model of appropriating self-knowledge through divine disclosure. Modern psychology's account of the unconscious and its limits (the heart's deceptiveness) is consistent with the biblical diagnosis.

**Structural hypothesis — The heart and spirit as the inner life's structuring pair.** Reading the heart and spirit vocabularies together across the programme so far: the heart is the cognitive-volitional-affective centre (soul dimension primarily), while the spirit is the transcendent-relational-pneumatic centre (spirit dimension). They are not the same, and they are not entirely separate. Heb 4:12 divides them with the Word. 1Th 5:23 preserves both ("whole spirit and soul and body"). Eze 36:26 replaces the heart and inserts the Spirit simultaneously ("I will give you a new heart, and a new spirit I will put within you"). The heart-spirit relationship may be the deepest question in the entire programme — the boundary between the creaturely inner life and the divine dimension that the Spirit occupies. This hypothesis requires the spirit registry (reg 184) before it can be tested.
