## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** Ambition
**Registry ID:** 003
**Source List:** Low Confidence / Inferred
**Testament Coverage:** NT_only

The word set provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word with low-to-moderate confidence, and only for a portion of the term set. The qualification rests on two NT terms. Eritheia (G2052) names an inner disposition — self-seeking factional drive — that the NT consistently treats as a soulish state located in the person and producing outward behaviours. Jam 3:14 explicitly places eritheia "in your hearts," confirming inner location. Filotimeomai (G5389) names an inner drive toward a goal — the aspiring state — used positively in all three of its NT occurrences. Together the two terms represent the negative and positive poles of what the modern word "ambition" covers, but neither term maps directly to the neutral modern sense of the word.

The qualification is made with low confidence for the word "ambition" as a category, because the source file itself assigns this word to the Low Confidence / Inferred list and notes that the neutral modern sense is absent from Scripture. What the STEP data confirms is that two real inner-being states exist — one negative (eritheia) and one positive (filotimeomai) — which together occupy the semantic space of ambition without either term meaning "ambition" in the modern neutral sense. The absence of any Hebrew data means the OT dimension of these states is unrepresented in this word set.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: ERITH — eritheia (G2052)

The ERITH root has one member in this word set. The LSJ confirms that the term originates in the idea of hired labour or working for wages, moving through the sense of canvassing for public office and political intrigue, and arriving by NT usage at the sense of self-seeking factional ambition. The single related word in STEP — erethizo (to provoke/irritate) — suggests a root connection between self-seeking drive and the irritation or provocation it generates in others, though these are distinct terms.

Eritheia occurs approximately 7 times in the NT and all occurrences are negative without exception. The verse set reveals three distinct contexts of use. First, eritheia appears in vice lists: Gal 5:20 lists it among the works of the flesh alongside idolatry, sorcery, enmity, and strife; 2Cor 12:20 lists it among community disorders alongside quarreling, jealousy, anger, and slander. In this context eritheia functions as one item in a catalogue of soulish states that damage community. Second, eritheia appears as a motive for ministry: Phili 1:17 describes people who proclaim Christ "out of selfish ambition, not sincerely" — here eritheia is the inner motivating state behind an outwardly correct action. The action (preaching Christ) is not the problem; the inner driving state is. Third, eritheia appears in James as an explicitly located inner state: Jam 3:14 reads "if you have bitter jealousy and selfish ambition in your hearts," placing eritheia alongside bitter jealousy as a heart-state that produces boasting and falseness to truth. Jam 3:16 then identifies eritheia as a generative state: "where jealousy and selfish ambition exist, there will be disorder and every vile practice."

The inner-being significance of eritheia is clearest in the Philippians and James material. Phili 2:3 places eritheia in direct contrast to humility — "Do nothing from selfish ambition or conceit, but in humility count others more significant than yourselves" — making eritheia the named inner state that humility displaces. This contrastive pairing is structurally important: eritheia is not simply bad behaviour but an inner orientation that shapes the self's relation to others.

### Root Family: FILOTIM — filotimeomai (G5389)

The FILOTIM root family is represented by a single verb in this word set. The compound structure of the word is transparent: filo- (to love) + time (honour). The LSJ confirms the classical sense: to love or seek honour, to be ambitious or emulous, to vie with others. In classical Greek the term could carry either positive or negative connotations depending on what the ambition was directed toward. In the NT it appears only in positive or neutral-positive contexts.

The three NT verses show filotimeomai used with a stated object or direction in each case. Rom 15:20 — "I make it my ambition to preach the gospel, not where Christ has already been named" — uses the verb with a ministry goal as its object. 2Cor 5:9 — "we make it our aim to please him" — uses the verb with pleasing God as its object. 1Th 4:11 — "aspire to live quietly, and to mind your own affairs" — uses the verb with a pattern of life as its object. In all three cases the aspiration is directed toward something outside and beyond the self: the advance of the gospel, the pleasure of God, or a quietly ordered life. The self-directed or self-aggrandising element present in the classical usage is absent from these NT occurrences.

The related word cluster for filotimeomai is extensive — the STEP entry lists over twenty filo- compound words — but most are proper nouns or place names and none are direct synonyms. The substantively related words are: time (honour), filos (friendly/friend), filia (friendship), filautos (selfish), filarguria (love of money), filoneikia (love of dispute). The last three are all negative filo- compounds, showing that the filo- root can generate both positive aspirational terms (filotimeomai) and negative self-directed terms (filautos, filarguria).

Its three NT occurrences consistently direct the aspiring state outward — toward God, gospel, and ordered life — rather than toward self-advancement. The contrast with the classical usage (which could be either positive or negative) is a genuine NT development worth noting.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

The two active terms in this word set are from different root families and carry opposite valences. The comparison is straightforward.

Eritheia and filotimeomai both name an inner driving state directed toward a goal, but they differ in every significant respect. Eritheia is always negative in its NT verse set: it names self-seeking drive, factionalism, and the use of others as instruments of personal advantage. Filotimeomai is always positive or neutral-positive in its NT verse set: it names earnest aspiring toward a worthy goal. The object of eritheia is implicitly the self — its own advancement, position, or recognition. The object of filotimeomai in all three NT occurrences is explicitly external to and greater than the self.

Jam 3:14 places eritheia in the heart as a located inner state producing cognitive and relational consequences (boasting, falseness to truth, disorder). The filotimeomai verses do not locate the state in a named inner faculty; the aspiration is described behaviourally — "I make it my aim," "we make it our aim," "aspire to live quietly."

The difference is genuine and is not a labelling distinction. These are two distinct inner states that occupy opposite moral poles of the same semantic field.
