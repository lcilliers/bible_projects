## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

Anguish provisionally qualifies as a genuine inner-being word cluster with moderate-to-high confidence across its core terms, but the word set is unusually large and contains a significant number of peripheral terms that qualify only weakly or not at all on present data. The word set spans both Testaments, with 26 Hebrew terms covering OT vocabulary and 7 Greek terms covering NT vocabulary exclusively.

The strongest qualification is carried by the CHUL root family and its noun derivatives (chil, chal.cha.lah), by the TSR noun family (tsa.rah, tsar in its distress sense), and by the NT terms agonia, odunaō, and thlipsis. These terms have clear inner-being use, sustained verse sets, and in several cases display somatic links or god_as_subject features that mark them as analytically significant. Several other terms in the set — tsu.qah, me.tsu.qah, stenochōria (absent from the body of the file), and the CHUL sub-entries dealing exclusively with birth and dance — are present through root proximity and will require Phase 2 review for genuine inner-being qualification. Four terms in the set — tsar in its enemy sense (H6862C), ra.ah in its harm sense (H7451C), be firm/chil (H2342B), and ir (H5892A) — carry anomaly flags and are not treated as established anguish terms in this analysis; they are expected to clarify when those roots are analysed as standalone registry entries.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### CHUL Family (H2427A chil, H2479 chal.cha.lah, H2342B chil/be firm, H2342A chul/tremble, H2342H chul/give birth, H2342I chul/writhe in pain, H2342J chul/dance, H2342K chul/anticipate)

The CHUL root is the largest single family in this word set, covering eight entries across eight STEP sub-identifiers. The root image is physical twisting or whirling — a body in motion under strain or intensity. From this core image the family generates an unusually wide semantic range: birth labour, combat writhing, trembling before God, dancing, anxious waiting, and the named inner states of anguish and agony. The breadth of this range is itself the most important observation about the family: the same bodily image underlies states that in English would be placed in entirely separate categories.

The main inner-being noun forms are chil (H2427A) and chal.cha.lah (H2479). Chil (7 occurrences) names the inner state of anguish most directly — the three verses in this file show it applied to nations on hearing threatening reports: the inhabitants of Philistia seized with pangs (Exo 15:14), anguish as of a woman in labour seizing a hostile army (Psa 48:6), and pain seizing Zion at the departure of leadership (Mic 4:9). The state is almost always triggered by threat or catastrophe rather than personal grief; the subject is typically collective.

Chal.cha.lah (H2479, 4 occurrences) intensifies this picture. Its three verses in this file are eschatological and catastrophic in register — anguish in Cush at the fall of Egypt (Eze 30:4, 30:9), and the anatomically specific description at Nah 2:10: hearts melt, knees tremble, and anguish is in all loins. The term locates the inner state in the body's core — the loins — rather than describing it as a named feeling. Its somatic_link is marked true and its body_inner_expression feature is raised in the JSON. It appears reserved for the most extreme collective dread.

The verb entries (H2342A/H/I/J/K) give the root's full functional range. H2342I (writhe in pain, 16 verses) is the primary anguish use of the verb — nations writhe at threat, individuals writhe in pain, Babylon writhes as the Lord's purposes fall on it (Jer 51:29), and a single verse of individual inner experience stands out: Psa 55:4, where the psalmist's heart is in anguish within him and the terrors of death have fallen upon him. H2342A (tremble, 10 verses) sits at the border of fear and anguish — the earth trembles before the Lord (Psa 96:9; 97:4; 114:7), and Esther is deeply distressed (Est 4:4). H2342H (give birth, 9 verses) uses the same root for actual parturition, including the theologically significant Deu 32:18 where God is the one who gave birth — this is the one CHUL verse in this file where god_as_subject is true, and it operates through the birth-travail metaphor. H2342J (dance, 8 verses) and H2342K (anticipate/wait anxiously, 5 verses) represent the outer edges of the root's reach: dance as physical whirling, and waiting with anxious longing as a form of internal tension. H2342B (be firm, 2 verses) carries the firmness/strength sense of the root — neither Job 20:21 nor Psa 10:5 contains anguish language — and this sub-entry is the CHUL anomaly noted in the data quality flags.

**Verdict on CHUL family:** The noun forms chil (H2427A) and chal.cha.lah (H2479) are doing genuinely different work: chil names the inner state as felt from inside, while chal.cha.lah locates it anatomically and registers the body's response. The verb form H2342I is the primary anguish verb and warrants treatment as a distinct inner-being term. H2342A (tremble) and H2342K (wait anxiously) name related but not identical states — fear-anguish and anticipatory tension respectively. H2342H (give birth) and H2342J (dance) are not anguish terms but demonstrate how the same root image covers the full range of intense physical activity, which is relevant context for why the anguish meaning developed as it did. H2342B (be firm) is not an inner-being term in this word set.

### TSR Family (H6869B tsa.rah, H6862B tsar, H6862C tsar/enemy, H6735C tsir, H4712 me.tsar)

The TSR root image is physical narrowness — being hemmed in, constrained, pressed from all sides. The family generates terms covering the inner experience of distress as well as the external conditions that produce it (straits, enemy, hard circumstances). The two primary inner-being distress terms are tsa.rah (H6869B, 70 occurrences) and tsar in its distress sense (H6862B, 31 occurrences).

Tsa.rah is the dominant OT word for distress across the entire verse set. Its 61 listed verses span every type of distress situation: personal crisis (Gen 42:21 — Joseph's brothers recognise the distress of his soul), divine discipline (Deu 31:17 — God warns of many troubles and evils), the classic cry-to-God formula repeated at Psa 107:6, 13, 19, and 28 — each time they cried to the Lord in their trouble and were delivered — and eschatological language (Jer 30:7 — Jacob's time of distress, Dan 12:1 — unprecedented trouble). The word straddles circumstances and inner experience: it can name the external situation of being in straits and the inner state of the person who is there. The Gen 42:21 use is particularly marked: the brothers saw the distress of his soul — tsa.rah is applied to an inner state perceived by others.

Tsar (H6862B, distress sense) is a closely related adjective-noun functioning as an inner state marker. Its 30 verses follow a similar pattern to tsa.rah, with the crucial difference that tsar tends to appear in the cry-to-God register more than in narrative description — 2Sa 22:7 (In my distress I called upon the Lord), Psa 18:6 (same), Psa 4:1, and repeated at Psa 107:6, 13, 19, 28. Its god_as_subject flag is true because the Lord is the one who delivers from distress in this literature, though God is not said to experience tsar himself in these verses.

Tsar in its enemy sense (H6862C, 69 occurrences) is distinct. Its verse set overwhelmingly addresses military and relational adversaries, not inner states, and it is treated as an anomaly note in this analysis.

Tsir (H6735C, 5 occurrences, 3 verses in this file) is a pang or sharp distress, appearing in three distinctly different contexts: actual birth pangs (1Sa 4:19), the prophet's somatic crisis before a vision at Isa 21:3 (loins filled with anguish, pangs seizing him), and visionary distress at Dan 10:16 (pains came upon me by reason of the vision). The Isa 21:3 verse is notable: the prophet names the inner state through its bodily location (loins filled with anguish), connecting tsir to the body_inner_expression pattern found in chal.cha.lah.

Me.tsar (H4712, 3 occurrences, 2 verses) is a concentrated form of the narrow/pressed image. Psa 118:5 (out of my distress I called on the Lord) shows it in the cry-to-God formula; Lam 1:3 uses it of Judah's exile as a state of being overtaken in the midst of distress.

**Verdict on TSR family:** Tsa.rah and tsar (H6862B) are doing similar but not identical work — tsa.rah is the wider narrative distress term covering both situation and inner state, while tsar appears more concentrated in the prayer cry-to-God register. They are near-synonyms within this word set rather than terms doing clearly separable inner-being work. Tsir adds the pang-as-somatic-inner-state concept not present in the others. Me.tsar is sparse but genuine. Tsar as enemy (H6862C) does not belong in the inner-being analysis for this word.

### TSUQ Family (H4164 mu.tsaq, H4689 ma.tsoq)

The TSUQ root image is pressing or pouring under constraint — the sense of being pressed into a narrow place with no escape. Both terms mean straitness or constraint-distress and share the same root verb and related-word cluster.

Ma.tsoq (H4689, 6 verses) appears most prominently in the siege covenant curses of Deuteronomy 28, where it is paired three times with the state of being besieged by enemies (Deu 28:53, 55, 57). This is distress as a condition of maximum external pressure — famine conditions within a siege. Psa 119:143 provides the clearest inner-being use: trouble and anguish have found me out, but your commandments are my delight — here ma.tsoq is named as an inner state that does not cancel delight in obedience. Jer 19:9 returns to the siege register.

Mu.tsaq (H4164, 3 occurrences) appears in the narrow/cramped sense. Job 36:16 contrasts distress with a broad place. Job 37:10 uses the root for frozen water — the physical image of constrained/solidified. Isa 9:1 uses a related form for the former anguish that will give way to glory.

**Verdict on TSUQ family:** Mu.tsaq and ma.tsoq are near-synonyms within this word set. Ma.tsoq has the richer inner-being verse set; mu.tsaq leans toward the physical constraint image. Neither has a strong independent inner-being profile distinguishable from the broader TSR distress vocabulary. Phase 2 will clarify whether these terms deserve their own registry entries or consolidate under a broader distress cluster.

### MAR Family (H4751 mar)

Mar is the single member of the MAR root family in this word set. The root image is bitterness — a taste sensation transferred to inner experience. Mar (39 occurrences) covers both the physical sense (bitter water, Exo 15:23) and the transferred inner sense: bitter in soul (Job 3:20), bitterness of soul (Job 10:1; 21:25), bitter cry (Gen 27:34; Est 4:1), and bitter distress in Lam 1:4 and Eze 27:31. The inner-being register of mar is consistently intense personal grief and anguish rather than situational distress — Job 3:20 (life given to the bitter in soul), Isa 38:15 (I walk slowly all my years because of the bitterness of my soul) and Isa 38:17 (it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness) place mar in the language of soul-depth suffering. The causative sense is present: Num 5:24 states the water will cause bitter pain, and Num 5:27 repeats this — the water acts on the woman to produce the inner and physical state. This makes mar a causative term for inner bitterness as well as a descriptive one.

Mar's somatic connection is strong — bitterness in soul is named as a state that drives speech (Job 10:1), shapes posture (Isa 38:15), and expresses in crying (Gen 27:34; Est 4:1; Eze 27:30–31). The word straddles anguish and grief, and will likely appear in the grief registry as well.

### CHAVEL Family (H2256B che.vel)

Che.vel (9 occurrences) is the single member of the CHAVEL family in this word set. The root image is a cord or rope under tension, from which the family generates meanings of pledge, destruction, and pain. In its inner-being use, che.vel names the sharp pang — most precisely the birth pang applied metaphorically to catastrophe. Job 21:17 says God distributes these pangs in his anger, making god_as_subject true through the causative — God is the distributor of pangs. Isa 13:8 places che.vel in a catalogue of collective catastrophic response (pangs and agony will seize them, anguish like a woman in labour). Isa 26:17 uses the writhing woman in pangs as a metaphor for Israel's failed effort. Jer 13:21, 22:23, and 49:24 apply the same metaphor to cities and nations. The pattern is consistent: che.vel names the acute seizing pang of crisis rather than sustained anguish, and it operates almost always through the birth-travail metaphor applied to collective entities.

### ODUN Family (G3601 odunē, G3600 odunaō)

The ODUN root names pain, grief, and anguish in Greek — classical usage covers both bodily and mental pain (LSJ), and the NT usage focuses the inner sense. Odunē (G3601, 2 occurrences) is a noun used at Rom 9:2 — Paul names great sorrow and unceasing anguish in his heart — and at 1Ti 6:10 — those who crave money have pierced themselves with many pangs. Both uses locate the inner state explicitly: Rom 9:2 names the heart as the location, 1Ti 6:10 uses the striking image of self-inflicted wounding through inner craving. This is the clearest NT example in the word set of nt_faculty_naming — the inner state is located in a named inner faculty (heart) in a way the OT distress vocabulary generally does not.

Odunaō (G3600, 4 occurrences) is the verb form, carrying causative capacity. Its verse set divides into two distinct registers. Luk 2:48 (in great distress) and Act 20:38 (sorrowful at Paul's departure) represent the grief-distress register — inner states generated by relational separation. Luk 16:24–25 represents the post-mortem torment register — the rich man in Hades cries out that he is in anguish in the flame. This is the same term covering personal grief at separation and conscious torment after death. The verb does not resolve whether these are meaningfully different inner states or one state expressed in different contexts.

**Verdict on ODUN family:** Odunē and odunaō are doing different work by their grammar — noun naming the state, verb enacting it — but share the same semantic space. Together they give anguish both a stationary location (in the heart) and a dynamic quality (the person is placed into the state, or the state is caused by external agency or craving). The post-mortem use of odunaō raises a question about whether the inner-being model applies to disembodied existence.

### AGON Family (G0074 agonia)

Agonia (1 occurrence) is a single-verse term: Luk 22:44 — Jesus in Gethsemane, in agony, praying more earnestly, with sweat like great drops of blood. The LSJ entry traces the word from athletic contest through gymnastic struggle to mental anguish, and this classical range is relevant because the Gethsemane use holds all these senses simultaneously: a genuine contest, an extreme inner state, and a physical somatic response (the sweat).

Three features make this term analytically significant beyond its single occurrence. First, god_as_subject is true — the person experiencing agonia here is Jesus. Second, somatic_link is true — the sweat response is described in physical detail. Third, the verse combines an inner state (agonia) with a bodily expression and a heightened physical action (earnest prayer) in a single event — a compressed body_inner_expression and somatic_inner_link instance. The full implications of god_as_subject on this term — whether the inner-being model requires a separate category for the incarnate Son experiencing an inner state — remain genuinely open.

### BASAN Family (G0928H basanizō)

Basanizō (12 occurrences, 4 verses in this file) is the torment/torture verb whose semantic history runs from testing gold on a touchstone through judicial torture to disease suffering and physical agitation. In this file's verse set: Mat 8:6 — a servant lying paralysed, suffering terribly (disease context); Mat 14:24 — a boat beaten by waves (physical agitation, metaphor); Mar 6:48 — disciples making headway painfully against the wind (same register); Rev 12:2 — a woman crying out in birth pains and the agony of giving birth (birth-travail metaphor applied eschatologically). The causative sense is present throughout — in each case an external force (disease, waves, wind, birth process) is the agent and the subject is the one being tormented.

This is notable as a term where the inner and somatic are inseparably bound: the torment being described in each verse is both an inner experience and a bodily condition simultaneously. The term does not appear in the post-mortem sense in this file's verse set (unlike in registry 002 agony, where the Hades uses appear under basanizō) — the post-mortem uses that appeared in registry 002 are not in this file's four verses for the term.

### THLIBO Family (G2347 thlipsis)

Thlipsis (45 occurrences, 34 verses in this file) is the dominant NT distress and affliction term in this word set. Its root image is compression or pressure, and the NT range covers: persecution-tribulation (Mat 13:21; 24:9 — tribulation arising over the word; tribulation unto death), eschatological tribulation of unprecedented scale (Mat 24:21; Mar 13:19), general distress (Joh 16:21 — a woman forgets the anguish of childbirth in the joy of birth), individual affliction and suffering (Rom 5:3 — we rejoice in our sufferings; 2Cor 1:4–8; 2Cor 7:4), and cosmic tribulation (Rev 1:9; 7:14 — those coming out of the great tribulation). God's agency is present in 2Th 1:6 — God will repay with affliction those who afflict you — making god_as_subject true in the retributive causative sense.

The range of thlipsis in its verse set is striking: it covers the same situational register as the OT tsa.rah (distress in general circumstances) but adds a specific NT theological meaning around tribulation-as-the-path-of-faith that is without direct OT parallel in this word set. 2Cor 2:4 (out of much affliction and anguish of heart) is also notable: thlipsis and sunochē appear in the same verse, where thlipsis covers general affliction and sunochē names the anguish of heart specifically — a distinction within one verse that may be significant for Phase 2.

### PONOS Family (G4192 ponos)

Ponos (3 occurrences, 3 verses in this file) covers pain, agony, hard labour and travail. ## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

**TSR family — tsa.rah vs tsar (H6869B vs H6862B):** Tsa.rah operates in a wider narrative register than tsar. Tsa.rah can name the distress of an individual perceived by others (Gen 42:21 — the distress of his soul), the distress of a nation (Jer 30:7), and the distress that God sees (Psa 106:44). Tsar in the distress sense (H6862B) consistently appears in the first-person prayer formula — in my distress I called (2Sa 22:7; Psa 4:1; 18:6; 118:5) — suggesting it is the preferred term for named inner distress spoken before God. Whether this is a reliable distinction or a function of the verse sample requires Phase 2 verification.

**CHUL family — chil (H2427A) vs chal.cha.lah (H2479):** Chil names the inner state as a named condition (pangs seized them, anguish as of a woman in labour) in situations of collective threat. Chal.cha.lah uses the same root in anatomically specific form — the loins, the knees, the face turning pale (Nah 2:10) — making the body the named register of the state rather than the inner faculty. These two terms are doing genuinely different work: chil is an inner-state name, chal.cha.lah is a body-expression-of-inner-state description.

**CHUL family — H2342I (writhe in pain) vs H2342A (tremble) vs H2342K (anticipate):** H2342I's verse set is dominated by peoples writhing at threat and individuals writhing in pain (Deu 2:25; Job 15:20; Isa 23:5; Jer 5:3; Zec 9:5). H2342A's verse set is dominated by cosmic and communal trembling before God (Psa 96:9; 97:4; 114:7). H2342K's verse set covers anticipatory waiting — Gen 8:10 (Noah waiting), Psa 37:7 (wait patiently for the Lord), Mic 1:12 (waiting anxiously for good). These three sub-entries cluster around different situational types: active anguish-writhing, fearful trembling before the divine, and tense waiting. They may warrant Phase 2 treatment as genuinely distinct inner-being states rather than as variants of one state.

**ODUN family — odunē (G3601) vs odunaō (G3600):** Odunē names the inner state as located in a faculty: unceasing anguish in my heart (Rom 9:2), and pangs from self-wounding craving (1Ti 6:10). Odunaō as a verb carries causative force: the state is placed upon a person by an external situation (Luk 2:48 — parental distress), by post-mortem torment (Luk 16:24–25), or by relational loss (Act 20:38). Odunē names; odunaō enacts. The post-mortem context (Luk 16:24–25) raises a question not present in the OT vocabulary: whether this is the same type of inner state occurring in a disembodied existence.

**TSUQ family — mu.tsaq (H4164) vs ma.tsoq (H4689):** Ma.tsoq has a more developed inner-being verse set, particularly Psa 119:143 (trouble and anguish have found me out, but your commandments are my delight). Mu.tsaq's verse set leans toward the physical constraint image (Job 36:16; 37:10; Isa 9:1). No significant inner-being distinction between the two terms is established on present data. They appear as near-synonyms.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The anguish word set covers both Testaments. The OT vocabulary is entirely Hebrew (26 terms); the NT vocabulary is entirely Greek (7 terms). The two sets do not overlap in language, but they overlap significantly in concept, with the NT terms representing both continuations and extensions of OT patterns.

**What the OT vocabulary does that the NT does not.** The OT set is dominated by the TSR and CHUL families, which between them cover the full range of distress, writhing-anguish, pang, and trembling. The OT vocabulary applies anguish language extensively to collective entities — nations, cities, Zion, Babylon — and the terms regularly appear in contexts of military threat, divine judgment, and covenant-curse fulfillment. The birth-travail metaphor is the governing image for acute collective anguish across multiple terms (chil, chal.cha.lah, che.vel, tsir, and the chul verb forms). The OT vocabulary also contains terms for sustained personal suffering of a less acute kind: ya.gon, mar, tsa.rah, a.mal. Within the OT set, the terms for anguish as pressed-constraint (TSR and TSUQ families) are more numerous and more developed than anything in the NT set.

**What the NT vocabulary does that the OT does not.** The NT introduces four developments not clearly present in the OT portion of this word set. First, anguish is explicitly located in a named inner faculty: Rom 9:2 names the heart as the location of unceasing anguish (odunē), and 2Cor 2:4 places thlipsis alongside anguish of heart (sunochē) in a single verse. The OT terms name the state without consistently locating it in a specific faculty in this word set (Psa 55:4 and Jer 8:18 are partial exceptions). Second, the NT introduces anguish in post-mortem consciousness: odunaō at Luk 16:24–25 describes conscious inner torment in Hades, which has no direct parallel in the OT anguish vocabulary of this file. Third, agonia at Luk 22:44 applies an anguish term to the incarnate Son — a theologically unique instance without OT parallel. Fourth, thlipsis develops a specific meaning around tribulation-as-the-path-of-faith-and-endurance (Rom 5:3–4; 2Cor 4:17) that has no direct OT equivalent in this word set, though it is conceptually related to the OT distress-and-deliverance pattern.

**What is present in the OT but absent from the NT.** The birth-travail metaphor applied to collective national anguish is extensive in the OT (multiple CHUL and TSR verses) and absent in the NT portion of this word set, except Rev 12:2 (basanizō applied to the woman in birth pains — eschatological use). The OT bitter-soul vocabulary (mar) is entirely absent from the NT word set here. The OT cry-to-God formula — in my distress I called and was delivered — appears repeatedly in the OT (tsa.rah, tsar, me.tsar) and has no direct NT equivalent in this word set, though it has theological continuity.

**Most significant single difference:** The OT anguish vocabulary applies predominantly to collective events — nations, peoples, cities in crisis — while the NT vocabulary consistently locates anguish in the inner experience of individuals, and in two cases (odunē and sunochē) explicitly names the inner faculty in which the state resides. This is an observed shift in subject and location across the two Testaments, not a conclusion about what it means.
