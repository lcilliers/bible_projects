## 2. Meaning Findings

### Primary semantic picture

The mercy vocabulary of Registry 111 is not a cluster of synonyms — it is a complete system with four semantically distinct families, each naming a different aspect of how mercy operates:

1. **Mercy-compassion family** (eleos, eleeō, eleēmōn, oiktirmos, oiktirmōn, ra.cha.min): The core mercy vocabulary — the felt inner movement of compassion (oiktirmos, ra.cha.min) and its expression as character disposition (eleēmōn) and active showing (eleeō). The foundational noun eleos names mercy as the moral quality of compassion moved to action. *Eleos* is the LXX translation of both *chesed* (covenant loyalty) and *rachamim* (womb-love), making the NT mercy vocabulary heir to both covenantal and visceral registers of the OT.

2. **Propitiation-atonement family** (hilaskomai, hilasmos, hilastērios, hileōs, kip.per, ka.phar, kap.po.ret, kip.pu.rim, ko.pher): The structural mechanism of mercy — the vocabulary describing how mercy operates in the presence of moral guilt. The covering metaphor (kap.har root) unifies the family: guilt is addressed by interposing a covering between the guilty person and divine judgment. The Greek hilaskomai underwent a critical directional reversal from classical usage: in classical Greek, the human appeases the god; in the NT, God provides the propitiation (1Jo 4:10). This reversal is analytically central.

3. **Grace-favour-supplication family** (cha.nan, te.chin.nah, a.tar, H2604 cha.nan): The relational dimension — favour freely extended to one with no claim, and the human posture of seeking it. The cha.nan root is structurally remarkable: the same verb names both the granting of grace (Qal — God shows favour) and the seeking of it (Hithpael — the person seeks favour). The giver and seeker share a root. *Te.chin.nah* (supplication for favour) arises from *yada et nega levavo* — knowing the affliction of one's own heart (1Ki 8:38). Authentic mercy-seeking presupposes self-knowledge.

4. **Negation and persistence family** (aneleēmōn, anileōs, anosios, lo-ruhamah, od): The boundary terms defining mercy by its absence (mercilessness, withheld compassion) and its temporal character (continuance, sustained disposition). Notably, the od particle names sustained divine inner dispositions in both directions — mercy persisting (Hab 2:3) and wrath persisting (Isa 5:25). Both mercy and wrath are characterised by duration using the same vocabulary.

### Significant tensions

**Tension 1 — Mercy as disposition vs. mercy as mechanism:** The registry contains two analytical layers that must be distinguished: mercy as inner disposition/character quality (eleos, oiktirmos, eleēmōn), and atonement as the structural mechanism that makes mercy operative in the presence of guilt (kip.per, kap.po.ret, hilaskomai). Session B finding DIM-111-001 correctly identified this. The two layers are not separate concepts — the mechanism is the expression of the disposition in the specific condition of human guilt — but they must be clearly distinguished in the word study.

**Tension 2 — Classical vs. NT direction of hilaskomai:** In classical Greek, hilaskomai always named the human act of appeasing the deity. The NT inverts this: the propitiation is provided by God, not offered to him. This inversion is the theological centre of the propitiation vocabulary and must be preserved in any description of these terms. *meaning_numbered* for G2433 has been updated (D1-003) to capture the middle/passive voice distinction encoding this.

**Tension 3 — Universal provision vs. particular reception:** 1Jo 2:2 ("not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world") names the mercy provision as universal in scope. The reception remains personal and particular (by faith, by the mercy-cry). The tension between universal provision and particular reception is not resolved in this registry's data but requires Session D treatment across the atonement and salvation vocabulary.

## 3. Divine Dimension Summary

### Dominant pattern of divine involvement

God is the primary actor in 15 of 36 owner dimension groups. The pattern is comprehensive:

- **God as originator**: Mercy is God's defining inner attribute before it is a human quality. He is "rich in mercy" (Eph 2:4), "Father of mercies" (2Cor 1:3), "compassionate and merciful" (Jam 5:11). Human mercy is derivative.
- **God as sovereign**: Mercy is not distributed by merit or formula — "I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy" (Rom 9:15). Mercy cannot be claimed; it can only be received.
- **God as provider of mechanism**: The propitiation is provided by God, not offered to him (1Jo 4:10 — "he sent his Son to be the propitiation"). God provides what the human needs to receive mercy.
- **God as withholder**: Lo-Ruhamah (Hos 1:6) and Isa 5:25 — mercy's withdrawal is as theologically intentional as its extension. Mercy is not automatic.
- **God as sustained disposer**: The od vocabulary confirms that divine mercy is not momentary but characterised by duration. God's inner relational stance is sustained.

### Divine-human relationship type

**Derivative and responsive.** Human mercy is not an independent virtue operating in parallel with divine mercy — it derives from divine mercy (Luk 6:36 "as your Father is merciful") and responds to it. The receiving of mercy generates the capacity for extending mercy (Mat 18:33, 1Pe 2:10). The human characteristic participates in and reflects the divine one.

### Eschatological dimension

No ESCHATOLOGICAL_USAGE phase2 flags are present, but eschatological content is substantial in the anchor verses: the reversal of Lo-Ruhamah in 1Pe 2:10; the new covenant mercy of Heb 8:12 (sins no longer remembered); the universal propitiation of 1Jo 2:2. The eschatological dimension of mercy is the comprehensive fulfilment of what was provisionally and annually enacted in the atonement system.

### Somatic pattern description

Three somatic registers operate simultaneously:
- **Instrument register** (blood, hand, fire): the atonement sub-vocabulary uses the body as the instrument through which the moral standing before God is addressed
- **Expression register** (face-direction, eyes, breast-beating, fasting, weeping, stretched hands): the supplication vocabulary uses the body to manifest the inner condition of need and dependence
- **Recipient register** (soul/nephesh, heart): the inner person is the *target* of mercy — the one for whom the covering is provided

The mercy vocabulary is the most somatically dense in the programme to date: blood alone appears in 20+ owner term verses; the supplication posture in Luk 18:13 encodes three simultaneous somatic elements. The body is integral to mercy at every level.

## 5. Spirit-Soul-Body Provisional Classification

**Classification: Spirit-soul interface**

**Reasoning:** Mercy is received at spirit level — it originates in God and is mediated through the propitiation mechanism (a divine provision, not human achievement). It is expressed at soul level as compassion toward the other, directed in supplication toward God, and enacted in concrete acts of favour toward those in need. The body mediates both directions: atoning blood provides the spirit-level covering (Lev 17:11); supplication posture expresses the soul-level appeal (Luk 18:13, 1Ki 8:38).

**Confidence: Medium.** The trichotomy-spanning alternative is plausible given the depth of somatic involvement in the atonement sub-vocabulary. The question is whether the atonement somatic elements operate instrumentally (body as tool of spirit-level transaction) or as an independent body-level dimension. If the former, spirit-soul interface holds. If the latter, trichotomy-spanning is more accurate. Session D corpus evidence needed.
