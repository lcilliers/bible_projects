## Section 2 — Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 Primary Meaning and Semantic Range

The repentance vocabulary is structured around two root families with completely different etymological characters: the **SHUV** family (directional turning — repentance as movement) and the **NACHAM** family (affective change — repentance as a shift in inner disposition). These are not synonymous but complementary: shuv names the act of turning, nacham names the inner state that precedes or accompanies it. The Greek **METANOIA** vocabulary adds a third dimension — repentance as cognitive reconstitution, a fundamental change of mind.

**The SHUV root — repentance as turning:**

The structural ground of the Hebrew repentance vocabulary is directional. Shuv means to turn, return, come back. Repentance in Hebrew is not primarily an inner emotional state but a spatial act: a change of direction. The person who repents is the person who was going one way and has turned to go another.

H7725O (shuv: repent, 77 verses) is the most concentrated single term in the registry and the most analytically decisive. The verse set is almost entirely prophetic, and the pattern is nearly invariant: "turn from your evil way" (Jer 25:5, 35:15, 36:3, 36:7; Eze 3:19; 14:6; 18:21-30; 33:9-19). The structural elements of the prophetic call to repentance are: an addressee (Israel/Judah/specific king), a direction (from evil/wickedness/idols), a destination (to the Lord), and a consequence (life/forgiveness/healing). These elements are present in nearly every H7725O verse.

Ezekiel 18 is the most concentrated shuv-repentance text in the OT. The chapter presents repentance as the governing principle of divine justice: the wicked who turns from all his sins shall live (18:21-23); the righteous who turns from righteousness shall die (18:24-26); the wicked who turns away from wickedness saves his life (18:27-28). The structural law is symmetrical: in either direction the turn determines the outcome. Eze 18:32 — "I have no pleasure in the death of anyone, declares the Lord God; so turn, and live" — is the sharpest expression of the divine motivation for issuing the repentance call. The Eze 33 parallel (vv. 9-19) confirms and extends this.

The Amos 1-2 oracle sequence introduces the most important negative use of H7725O in the corpus: "I will not revoke the punishment" (lo ashuv — I will not turn back from it). Repeated eight times across the nations (Amos 1:3, 6, 9, 11, 13; 2:1, 4, 6), this formula is the irrevocable-judgment declaration — divine shuv withheld. Repentance is precisely the reversal of this formula: where God does not turn back his judgment, the wicked has forfeited their last possibility. The Amos sequence reveals that shuv operates symmetrically: human turning produces the possibility of divine turning; human refusal to turn closes that possibility. This is the structural logic of the OT repentance theology.

2 Chr 7:14 — "if my people who are called by my name humble themselves, and pray and seek my face and turn from their wicked ways, then I will hear from heaven and will forgive their sin and heal their land" — is the programmatic covenantal repentance formula. Four acts (humble, pray, seek, turn) produce three divine responses (hear, forgive, heal). The turning is the fourth and decisive human act; the three divine acts are its consequence.

Jon 3:8-10 is the most concentrated single narrative of the repentance cycle: Nineveh turns from its evil way (H7725O) → God sees their turning → God relents (H5162H). The human turning and the divine relenting are structurally paired. This is the registry's clearest demonstration that shuv and nacham operate as a matched pair: human shuv produces divine nacham.

H7725I (shuv: turn back, 91 verses) covers the turning-back sense across contexts: turning back from sin, turning of divine anger, turning away from following God (apostasy direction), and commands to turn. The Isa 5:25 refrain — "his anger has not turned away (lo shuv)" — repeated five times across Isa 5-10 — establishes the divine anger as a second turn that depends on the first human turn. When Israel does not turn (H7725O absent), God's anger does not turn away (H7725I withheld). The structural interdependence of the two turns is the theological spine of the shuv theology.

Exo 32:12-14 is the paradigmatic intercession-repentance text: Moses commands God to "turn from your burning anger" (H7725I) and relent (H5162H) — and God does. The sequence (Moses' shuv-command to God → divine nacham) inverts the usual direction (divine shuv-call to humans → human turning → divine nacham), showing that the structures work in both directions. Intercession is the human calling-back of divine anger using the same shuv vocabulary.

H7725J (shuv: again/restoration, 73 verses) covers the repetition and restoration senses — God acting again in mercy (Deu 30:3, 9; Psa 85:4-6; Mic 7:19 "he will again have compassion"), Israel returning repeatedly to sin (Judg 2:19; Neh 9:28), and the cyclical pattern of Judges and Kings. The "again" sense reveals the temporal structure of shuv: repentance is not a once-for-all event but a repeated orientation. The covenant history of Israel is narrated in shuv rhythm — turn away, judge, cry out, deliver, rest, turn away again.

H7725N (shuv: recall, 7 verses) is the cognitive dimension of repentance: "call to mind" (Deu 30:1 — "you call them to mind among all the nations"; Lam 3:21 — "but this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope"). Shuv reaches into the cognitive register: repentance begins with remembering — turning the mind back to what was true before the departure.

H7725H (shuv: rescue/restore, 102 verses) is structurally the most important of the newly extracted terms. This is the divine-action counterpart to human repentance. When Israel turns back (H7725O: repent), God turns back Israel's fortunes (H7725H: restore). The "restore the fortunes" formula (shuv shevut) appears across Jeremiah (29:14, 30:3, 18; 32:44; 33:7, 11, 26), Ezekiel (16:53; 29:14; 39:25), Amos (9:14), Zephaniah (2:7; 3:20), and Psalms (14:7; 53:6; 85:1; 126:1, 4). The formula is the covenantal promise: what exile took, return will restore. Psa 23:3 — "He restores my soul" — is the most intimate use: the divine shuv applied to the individual soul. Lam 5:21 — "Restore us to yourself, O Lord, that we may be restored" — is the prayer that makes the human-divine turn explicit: "bring us back to yourself" (a prayer of human turning) "so that we may be restored" (the desired divine turning-to-us).

H7729 (shu.vah: returning, 1 verse) — Isa 30:15 — "In returning and rest you shall be saved; in quietness and trust shall be your strength." The repentance noun in the most compressed formulation of the OT: returning (shu.vah) paired with rest (nu.ach), quietness and trust. Repentance is here linked directly to peace (shalom registry) through the rest vocabulary.

**The NACHAM root — relenting and comfort:**

Na.cham covers two distinct but etymologically unified senses: relenting/repenting (H5162H) and comforting (H5162G). The underlying root sense is a deep inner change — the visceral shift of one's disposition toward another. This makes na.cham the affective interior of what shuv enacts externally.

H5162H (na.cham: relent, 34 verses) is almost entirely divine. God relenting is the most extensively documented na.cham use in the OT. The structure is nearly invariant: human turning (shuv) → divine relenting (na.cham). Exo 32:14 — "the Lord relented from the disaster." Jon 3:10 — "God relented of the disaster that he had said he would do to them." Jer 18:8 — "if that nation turns from its evil, I will relent of the disaster." The conditionality is explicit: divine relenting is not arbitrary but responsive. God's na.cham is the divine counterpart to human shuv.

The most theologically dense na.cham texts are the 1 Sam 15 cluster (vv. 11, 29, 35): "I regret that I have made Saul king" (v.11 — God na.cham); "the Glory of Israel will not lie or have regret, for he is not a man that he should have regret" (v.29 — God does not na.cham); "the Lord regretted that he had made Saul king over Israel" (v.35 — God na.cham). The text contains the tension between divine na.cham (God regrets) and the statement that God does not na.cham (he is not a man), in the same chapter, separated by 20 verses. This is not a contradiction to be resolved but a theological tension the text holds open: God is genuinely affected by human choices (na.cham) while remaining unchangeable in his essential character (Num 23:19; 1 Sa 15:29). The registry documents this tension explicitly and does not resolve it.

Jer 15:6 — "I am weary of relenting" — is the darkest na.cham statement in the corpus. Divine relenting has limits: sustained refusal to turn exhausts even God's capacity to relent. This is the most extreme statement of the relenting vocabulary — not the withdrawal of mercy but the exhaustion of it after repeated cycles of repentance refused.

H5162G (na.cham: comfort, 66 verses) is the comfort sense of the same root — the disposition-shift directed toward another in their grief. The most concentrated divine comfort texts are in Deutero-Isaiah: Isa 40:1 — "Comfort, comfort my people, says your God"; Isa 51:3, 12; 52:9; 54:11; 66:13 — the sustained comfort promise to Zion. The Lamentations cluster (1:2, 9, 16, 17, 21) is the comfort-absence contra-pattern: "she has no comforter" appears five times across Lam 1. The absence of comfort is the condition of desolation. Comfort is what God's relenting produces in the inner life of the person who has been restored.

H5164 (no.cham: repentance, 1 verse) — Hos 13:14 — "Compassion is hidden from my eyes." The single repentance noun in Hebrew, used in a divine-judgment context: God withholds his na.cham/compassion. The scarcity of this form (1 verse) confirms that Hebrew expresses repentance primarily through the verb shuv, not through a dedicated repentance noun.

The comfort noun family (H5150 ni.chum, 3v; H5165 ne.cha.mah, 2v; H8575 tan.chum, 5v) completes the na.cham family. Hos 11:8 — "my compassion (ni.chum) grows warm and tender" — is the most intimate divine comfort expression: God's inner warmth for his people overcoming his anger. Psa 94:19 — "your consolations (tan.chum) cheer my soul" — is the somatic consequence of divine comfort: consolation as a direct experience of inner brightening.

**The apostasy contra-terms — what repentance turns from:**

H4878 (me.shu.vah: faithlessness/apostasy, 12 verses) is the noun of turning-away, derived from the same shuv root as repentance. Where repentance (shuv) is turning toward God, apostasy (me.shu.vah) is turning away. The terms share a root and are structural opposites within the same semantic field. Jer 3:22 — "Return, O faithless sons; I will heal your faithlessness (me.shu.vah)" — juxtaposes the repentance command (return/shuv) and the apostasy condition (faithlessness/me.shu.vah) in a single verse: the turn heals the turning-away. Hos 14:4 — "I will heal their apostasy (me.shu.vah)" — is the divine promise to heal the condition that repentance turns from.

H7726 (sho.vav: backslider, 4 verses) and H7728 (sho.vev: apostate, 3 verses) name the person in the condition of having turned away. Jer 3:14 — "Return, O faithless (sho.vav) children" — is the repentance command addressed to those whose identity has become their turning-away. The command to shuv is addressed to the sho.vav: repentance is what the apostate does to cease being an apostate.

**The METANOIA family — Greek repentance as mind-change:**

G3341 (metanoia: repentance, 22 verses) is etymologically "after-mindedness" — a change of mind that comes after reflection. The meta- prefix (change/after) applied to nous (mind) gives a cognitive and temporal structure: repentance is what happens when the mind, having gone in one direction, turns to see things differently.

The NT metanoia vocabulary adds several dimensions not present in the OT shuv vocabulary:

*John's baptism of repentance* — the first NT use (Mat 3:8, 11; Mar 1:4; Luk 3:3, 8) frames metanoia as the precondition of reception into the kingdom. "Bear fruit in keeping with repentance" — repentance is not the terminus but the beginning; its authenticity is demonstrated in what follows.

*Jesus' repentance call* — Mat 4:17; Mar 1:15 — "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand." The announcement of the kingdom is the ground of the repentance call. Repentance in Jesus' preaching is the appropriate response to the arrival of the reign of God.

*Repentance as divine gift* — Act 5:31 — "God exalted him... to give repentance to Israel"; Act 11:18 — "God has granted repentance that leads to life"; 2 Ti 2:25 — "God may perhaps grant them repentance." The NT introduces what is absent from the OT vocabulary: repentance as something God gives, not merely calls for. This is the sharpest structural difference between OT shuv (commanded) and NT metanoia (granted). The demand is still present; but the capacity to meet it is itself a divine gift.

*2 Cor 7:9-10* — anchor verse: "godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret (ametamelēton), whereas worldly grief produces death." Two gripping structural distinctions: (1) Godly grief vs. worldly grief — the same outward emotional state (grief) produces opposite inner outcomes depending on its orientation. Godly grief is grief in the presence of God, oriented toward him; worldly grief is grief oriented around self-preservation and loss of status. (2) Repentance without regret vs. death — metanoia is characterised by ametamelēton (without regret, without being-pained-afterwards). True repentance is its own resolution; it does not leave a residue of regret. This is the anchor verse's most analytically significant contribution: the quality of the grief that produces repentance determines whether the repentance itself is alive or deadly.

*Heb 6:6* — "it is impossible... to restore them again to repentance." The one text in which repentance appears as something that can be permanently foreclosed. This is theologically the most difficult verse in the registry. The verse does not explain the mechanism but asserts the possibility of a state beyond repentance — the limit of metanoia's availability.

*Rev 2-3 and Rev 9, 16* — the Revelation pattern is the most repeated use of metanoeō in the NT: seven "repent" commands to the seven churches (Rev 2:5, 16, 21, 22; 3:3, 19), followed by the contra-pattern — those who did not repent despite the plagues (9:20-21; 16:9, 11). The Revelation corpus establishes that repentance commands can be refused and that the refusal to repent in the face of judgment is itself a final stance.

*2 Pe 3:9* — "not wishing that any should perish but that all should reach repentance" — is the most explicit statement of divine desire regarding repentance. The patience of God (patience registry, CRL) is the mechanism of this desire: God delays not because he is slow but because he is giving space for repentance. This is the patience-repentance structural link.

G3338 (metamellomai: to regret/repent, 5 verses) — the regret-form of meta- repentance. Mat 21:29 — the son who said "I will not" but afterward changed his mind (metamelētheis) and went — is the paradigmatic metamellomai: after-the-event remorse that produces action. Mat 27:3 — Judas "changed his mind" (metamelētheis) when he saw Jesus condemned — is the counter-example: the same after-event change of mind that does not produce salvific repentance but only horror and self-destruction. The two uses of metamellomai in Matthew establish the distinction the anchor verse makes: regret-without-godly-grief produces despair; regret-with-God-directedness produces life.

**The meta- Greek family — transformation and the fixed God:**

G3339 (metamorfoō: transform, 4 verses) is the transformation vocabulary. Rom 12:2 — "be transformed by the renewal of your mind" — is the repentance-transformation link: the renewal of the nous (mind) is the mechanism of transformation, directly parallel to metanoia (change of nous). The Transfiguration (Mat 17:2; Mar 9:2) is metamorfoō applied to the body of Jesus — the visible transformation that is the telos of what renewal of mind begins in believers (2 Cor 3:18 — "being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another").

G0278 (ametamelētos: irrevocable/without regret, 2 verses) — Rom 11:29 — "the gifts and calling of God are irrevocable." God's repentance vocabulary (na.cham) belongs to his responsiveness to human choices; but his covenant calling is ametamelētos — beyond the reach of his own relenting. This is the guarantee that undergirds the repentance call: the God who calls to repentance is the same God whose gifts cannot be revoked. 2 Cor 7:10 — repentance "without regret" — applies the same term to authentic repentance: it does not leave a residue of backward-looking pain.

G0276 (ametathetos: unchangeable, 2 verses) — Heb 6:17-18 — "the unchangeable character of his purpose." The prefix a- negates the meta- (change) root: God is a-meta-thetos. While God responds and relents (na.cham), his covenant purpose is ametathetos — immune to the meta- (change/after) that characterises both repentance and his own responsive relenting. The two terms together establish the theological frame: God changes in response (na.cham) but does not change in character (ametathetos). Repentance is issued and received by a God who is both responsive and stable.

G3346 (metatithēmi: desert/transfer, 5 verses) — Gal 1:6 — "so quickly deserting him who called you in the grace of Christ." The meta- prefix here describes the movement away from God — the opposite of repentance. Desertion is the negative of the return; Jude 4 — "pervert the grace of our God into sensuality" — is the active corruption of what repentance restores.

### 2.2 The Two Root Structures

The shuv and nacham families address repentance from complementary perspectives:

**Shuv** — repentance as directional act. It can be commanded ("turn from your evil way"), observed ("when they turned from their evil way"), refused ("they refused to turn"), and completed ("they turned"). It is primarily external and volitional — the turning of the whole person in a new direction.

**Nacham** — repentance as inner disposition-shift. It names the affective interior of the turning: the divine grief over what must be judged (Gen 6:6-7), the divine relenting when judgment is averted (Jon 3:10), and the human grief that reaches toward God (Job 42:6 — "I repent in dust and ashes"). Na.cham covers both the sorrowful side of repentance (grief, regret) and its resolution (comfort, consolation) — the inner arc from grief to peace.

**Metanoia** — repentance as cognitive reconstitution. The Greek vocabulary adds the cognitive structure that the Hebrew vocabulary leaves implicit: repentance involves a specific change in how one thinks (meta-nous), a re-ordering of the mind's orientation. Rom 12:2 makes this explicit: the transformation is by the renewal of the nous — the same faculty that metanoia names.

The three root structures together give a complete picture: the outward act of turning (shuv), the inward shift of disposition (nacham), and the cognitive reconstitution that underlies both (metanoia).

### 2.3 Structural Polarity

**Form 1 — Genuine repentance:** Shuv from evil toward God; nacham of sin in the presence of God (godly grief — 2 Cor 7:10); metanoia as renewed mind oriented toward God's reality. Produces life (Eze 18:21), divine relenting (Jon 3:10), forgiveness (2 Chr 7:14), comfort (Isa 66:13), restoration of fortunes (Jer 29:14).

**Form 2 — Apostasy (contra-term):** Me.shu.vah — the sustained turning away from God that is the structural negative of repentance within the same root family. "Perpetual backsliding" (Jer 8:5). Named as a healed condition: "I will heal their apostasy" (Hos 14:4) — what repentance undoes is precisely what apostasy is.

**Form 3 — False/incomplete repentance:** Worldly grief (2 Cor 7:10) — grief oriented around self, producing death. Metamellomai without God-directedness (Judas, Mat 27:3) — regret without salvific turning. The Judges cycle — repentance repeated without transformation.

**Transformation dynamic:** The movement from apostasy/sin to restored relationship follows a documented sequence: hearing the repentance call (prophetic shuv-command) → godly grief (nacham of sin) → turning (shuv act) → divine relenting (divine nacham) → restoration of fortunes (shuv shevut). This is the full repentance cycle, most clearly displayed in Jon 3 (Nineveh: hearing → turning → divine relenting) and most fully articulated in Jer 15:19 — "if you return, I will restore you."

## Section 4 — Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God as Subject

The registry's most distinctive and theologically complex feature: God appears as the subject of repentance vocabulary more extensively than in any other registry except peace.

**God regrets/relents (na.cham — 25+ verses):**
Gen 6:6-7 — "the Lord regretted that he had made man on the earth, and it grieved him to his heart." The most primordial divine na.cham: the Creator's grief over his own creation before the flood. 1 Sa 15:11 — "I regret that I have made Saul king." Jon 3:10 — "God relented of the disaster." The divine na.cham is the most extensive GOD_AS_SUBJECT corpus in the registry.

**The 1 Samuel 15 tension — explicit and unresolved:**
The text places in close proximity: v.11 ("I regret/na.cham"), v.29 ("the Glory of Israel will not lie or have regret/na.cham, for he is not a man"), v.35 ("the Lord regretted/na.cham that he had made Saul king"). This is not careless contradiction but deliberate theological tension: God is genuinely responsive to human choices (he na.cham over Saul), and yet he is not a man who na.chams in the sense of instability or unreliability (v.29). The distinction the text preserves but does not resolve is between God's relational responsiveness and his essential immutability. Num 23:19, Heb 6:17-18 (ametathetos), and Mal 3:6 all affirm divine immutability; the na.cham texts affirm divine responsiveness. The registry holds both without resolution, as the text itself does.

**Jer 15:6 — divine exhaustion:**
"I am weary of relenting." The furthest extreme of divine na.cham in the negative: after sustained cycles of relenting that produced no lasting repentance in Israel, God speaks of being weary of the process. This is not a denial of divine compassion but a statement about the limit of what sustained grace can do in the face of persistent refusal.

**God issues the repentance call (shuv H7725O — GOD_AS_SUBJECT):**
The prophetic "turn from your evil way" formula is spoken by God through the prophets. The shuv-call is a divine speech act — the command to turn is itself an act of divine grace: God who could simply judge instead issues a call to return.

**God grants repentance (NT):**
Act 5:31; 11:18; 2 Ti 2:25 — "God gives/grants repentance." The NT introduces what the OT expresses less explicitly: the capacity to repent is itself a divine gift. This is the deepest level of the divine dimension — God does not merely call for repentance but enables it.

**2 Pe 3:9 — God desires universal repentance:**
"Not wishing that any should perish but that all should reach repentance." The divine will regarding repentance is stated explicitly and universally. The patience of God is the expression of this desire — he withholds judgment to give space for repentance. The CRL with patience registry (116) is formally grounded here.

## Section 5 — Somatic Dimension

**Somatic engagement: PRESENT through conventional and physiological markers.**

**Sackcloth and ashes — the somatic inscription of repentance:**
Jon 3:5-8 — Nineveh covering themselves with sackcloth, sitting in ashes. Mat 11:21 — "they would have repented long ago in sackcloth and ashes." Sackcloth is the physical material worn as the outer expression of inner turning. The body is clothed in the texture of mourning; repentance has a somatic costume. Ashes on the head are the visual sign of solidarity with what has been burned away. Repentance is inscribed on the body before it is expressed in words.

**"I struck my thigh" — Jer 31:19:**
"After I was instructed, I struck my thigh; I was ashamed and I was confounded, because I bore the disgrace of my youth." The striking of the thigh (or flank — the ke.sel/loin region referenced in the hope registry) is a gesture of self-reproach, the body expressing what the inner person feels. It is the somatic accompaniment to shame and repentance. The body participates in the repentance before the confession is complete.

**"Repent in dust and ashes" — Job 42:6:**
Job's repentance is expressed in spatial-somatic terms: the posture of dust and ashes, the position of complete humiliation before God. The somatic lowering (dust) corresponds to the inner recognition of the distance between creature and Creator. Repentance has a posture.

**The somatic cascade of repentance refused:**
Jer 8:6 — "no man relents of his evil, saying 'What have I done?' Everyone turns to his own course, like a horse plunging headlong into battle." The absence of repentance is described in somatic-kinetic terms: the body that does not stop, does not turn, does not interrupt its momentum. The horse plunging into battle has no reversal capacity. Repentance is the interruption of momentum — the body stopping, turning, reversing.

**Psa 94:19 — consolation's somatic effect:**
"When the cares of my heart are many, your consolations (tan.chum) cheer my soul." The comfort that follows repentance and divine relenting has an inner-somatic consequence: the soul is cheered, brightened. The tan.chum is experienced as a felt lightening of what was heavy.

## Section 6 — Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Five Causative Pathways

**Pathway 1: Divine call → human turning → divine relenting:**
The primary OT pattern. God calls through prophets → Israel turns (H7725O) → God relents (H5162H). Most completely displayed in Jon 3. The call initiates; the turn activates; the divine relenting follows.

**Pathway 2: Human intercession → divine turning:**
Exo 32:12-14 — Moses commands God to "turn from your burning anger" → God relents. The intercessor's shuv-call to God activates divine na.cham. This reverses the primary direction: the human calls the divine to turn, and God responds.

**Pathway 3: Godly grief → metanoia → salvation:**
2 Cor 7:10 — the anchor verse mechanism. Grief (lupe) oriented toward God produces metanoia → salvation. The affective state (grief) produces the cognitive-volitional act (metanoia) which produces the outcome (salvation). The quality of the grief (godly vs. worldly) determines the direction of the output.

**Pathway 4: Divine patience → space for repentance:**
2 Pe 3:9 — God's patience is the delay that creates the temporal space within which repentance remains possible. The patience-repentance causal link: patience is the mechanism; repentance is what it enables.

**Pathway 5: Repentance → restoration of fortunes (shuv shevut):**

## Section 7 — Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 2 Cor 7:10 — Anchor: Godly grief and repentance
*"For godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."*

**Contribution:** This verse is the NT's most precise analytical statement about repentance. Three structural things emerge: (1) The quality of grief determines the outcome — the same affective state (grief) produces opposite results depending on its orientation. Godly grief is grief in the presence of and directed toward God; worldly grief is grief oriented around self-loss. (2) True metanoia is ametamelēton — "without regret." The word uses the meta- root negated: true repentance does not produce after-pain; it is self-resolving. This distinguishes it from metamellomai (Judas) which produced regret but not resolution. (3) Worldly grief produces death — not merely failure but the opposite of what godly grief produces. The stakes of the grief-orientation are absolute. The verse establishes that the affective register is not the endpoint (grief is not the goal) but the mechanism (grief is what produces the turn).

### 7.2 Eze 18:23 and 32 — The divine desire as repentance ground
*"Have I any pleasure in the death of the wicked, declares the Lord God, and not rather that he should turn from his way and live?" (v.23) "So turn, and live." (v.32)*

**Contribution:** These two verses frame the entire Ezekiel 18 corpus. The rhetorical question (v.23) reveals the divine motivation for the repentance call: it is not coercive but desiderative — God wants the wicked to turn. The compressed command (v.32) is the shortest and most absolute repentance command in the corpus: "turn, and live." The directional act and its consequence in two words. The entire chapter's intricate case-law (the righteous man who sins, the wicked man who turns, the turning from righteousness) resolves to this: the turn determines life or death, and God desires life.

### 7.3 Jon 3:10 — The complete repentance cycle
*"When God saw what they did, how they turned from their evil way, God relented of the disaster that he had said he would do to them, and he did not do it."*

**Contribution:** This is the most compressed and complete single expression of the OT repentance cycle in the corpus. Human turning (shuv) → divine observation of the turn → divine relenting (na.cham) → consequence averted. Five elements in one verse: the human act, the divine attention, the divine inner-change, the divine outer-change, and the counterfactual (what would have happened). It establishes structurally that repentance in the OT is inherently relational: the human turn is not a private inner act but a visible change that God sees and responds to.

### 7.4 1 Sa 15:11, 29, 35 — The divine tension explicit
*"I regret that I have made Saul king" (v.11); "the Glory of Israel will not lie or have regret, for he is not a man that he should have regret" (v.29); "the Lord regretted that he had made Saul king" (v.35).*

**Contribution:** The registry's most theologically challenging canonical passage. The same na.cham vocabulary applied to God in contradictory directions within the same chapter — God does na.cham and God does not na.cham — is not a textual error but a deliberate holding of the tension between divine responsiveness and divine stability. The text insists on both without resolving their relation. This is programme-significant: the repentance vocabulary when applied to God reveals a genuine tension in the divine character that the text refuses to domesticate.

### 7.5 Jer 3:22 — The repentance promise
*"Return, O faithless sons; I will heal your faithlessness."*

**Contribution (intersection of two forms):** This verse contains in miniature both forms of the repentance vocabulary: the command (shuv — return) and the condition it addresses (me.shu.vah — faithlessness). The divine offer is that the turning heals the turning-away. Three things: (1) The command is addressed to those already in the apostasy condition — "faithless sons." Repentance is available to those who have already broken faith. (2) The result of returning is healing of the me.shu.vah itself — not merely forgiveness of acts but restoration of the relational orientation. (3) The answer comes immediately (v.22b): "Behold, we come to you, for you are the Lord our God." The return is met by God as the object toward whom the return is directed. The repentance call and its reception are placed side by side.

### 7.6 Isa 30:15 — The shu.vah-rest connection
*"In returning and rest you shall be saved; in quietness and in trust shall be your strength."*

**Contribution (intersection of two registries):** This verse links the repentance vocabulary (shu.vah: returning) directly to the peace registry vocabulary (nu.ach: rest; sha.qat: quietness; ba.tach: trust). The single-verse concentration of shuv-vocabulary, peace-vocabulary, and hope-vocabulary establishes that repentance, peace, and trust are not separate experiences but aspects of a single relational orientation toward God. The verse's final clause — "but you were unwilling" — is the refusal contra-pattern: Israel declined to enter the condition that returning would produce. Repentance refused forfeits all that returning would give.

### 7.7 Lam 3:21 — Recall as the mechanism of hope
*"But this I call to mind (H7725N), and therefore I have hope."*

**Contribution (unexpected register — hope-repentance interface):** This is the most structurally surprising verse in the registry. The shuv vocabulary (H7725N: recall, call to mind) is used as the mechanism that produces hope from despair. In the context of Lam 3:18 ("my endurance has perished; so has my hope from the Lord"), the cognitive shuv — turning the mind back to the steadfast loves of the Lord — is what reverses the despair. This is the shuv vocabulary operating in the cognitive register to produce the same result as the hope vocabulary. Repentance-as-recall is the cognitive equivalent of repentance-as-turning: the mind returns to what was true before the departure from hope. The CRL with hope registry (078) is formally grounded here.

### 7.8 Rev 9:20-21 — Repentance refused as final stance
*"The rest of mankind, who were not killed by these plagues, did not repent of the works of their hands... nor did they repent of their murders or their sorceries or their sexual immorality or their thefts."*

**Contribution (contra-pattern — eschatological refusal):** This is the sharpest contra-pattern in the registry: judgment operating without producing repentance. The plagues of Revelation are explicitly designed to produce metanoia (they are the divine call amplified to maximum intensity), and they produce its refusal instead. The structural claim is that repentance, though always available, can be permanently refused — and that the persistent refusal is itself a form of final stance. This is the eschatological limit of the shuv theology: "they refused to return to me" (Hos 11:5) extended to its ultimate conclusion.

## Section 8 — Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field
**Primary:** Clinical Psychology — the psychology of change, remorse, and guilt processing.
**Secondary:** Cognitive neuroscience — the neural correlates of moral judgement, remorse, and behavioural reorientation.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1: The transtheoretical model of change (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983)**
The stages-of-change model (pre-contemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance → relapse) is the most widely applied framework for understanding behavioural change. It maps onto the shuv repentance cycle in several ways: pre-contemplation is the state before the prophetic call; contemplation is the consideration of whether to turn; action is the shuv act itself; maintenance is the sustained new orientation; relapse is the Judges cycle. The model describes change as a process with predictable stages and setbacks, not a single moment.

**Finding 2: Guilt vs. shame in moral repair (Tangney & Dearing, 2002)**
Research distinguishes guilt (negative evaluation of a specific behaviour, producing motivation to repair) from shame (negative evaluation of the self, producing withdrawal and self-concealment). Guilt-proneness predicts constructive moral repair; shame-proneness predicts defensiveness, anger, and avoidance. The 2 Cor 7:10 distinction between "godly grief" (productive) and "worldly grief" (deadly) maps onto this research pattern: godly grief is guilt-oriented (focused on the act and its relational consequences); worldly grief is shame-oriented (focused on self-protection and loss of status).

**Finding 3: The neural correlates of remorse and reappraisal (Koster-Hale & Saxe, 2013; Gyurak et al., 2011)**
Neuroimaging research on moral remorse identifies activity in the anterior cingulate cortex (conflict monitoring, error detection) and medial prefrontal cortex (self-referential processing and mentalising). Cognitive reappraisal — the deliberate reframing of an experience — engages lateral prefrontal cortical circuits and reduces limbic reactivity. Rom 12:2 ("renewal of the mind") and the metanoia vocabulary align with the cognitive reappraisal literature: repentance involves a deliberate re-framing of one's prior orientation in light of a different evaluative standard.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Phenomenological convergence:** The stages-of-change model describes the process structure of repentance accurately: the OT corpus shows pre-contemplation (the period before the prophetic call penetrates), contemplation (the weighing of whether to turn), action (the shuv act), and relapse (the Judges cycle). The model's empirical grounding confirms that behavioural change follows a staged process with predictable features.

**Mechanistic convergence:** The guilt-vs.-shame research maps well onto 2 Cor 7:10: godly grief as productive guilt-orientation vs. worldly grief as destructive shame-orientation. The biblical distinction is earlier and theologically grounded, but the psychological distinction confirms the phenomenological reality.

**Register divergence — the relational object:**
The stages-of-change model is behaviourally and person-centred: change is evaluated in terms of the person's relation to their own behaviour. The biblical repentance vocabulary is relationally and God-centred: repentance is evaluated in terms of the person's relation to God. The same behaviours (ceasing an addiction, repairing a relationship) can be described by both frameworks, but the ground of evaluation is completely different. "I need to change for myself" (stages of change) vs. "I need to turn to God" (shuv) are not the same act even when they produce similar surface behaviours.

**Genuine divergence — divine relenting:**
The na.cham vocabulary introduces a dimension that psychology cannot address: God's inner response to human repentance. Psychology can describe the social consequences of repentance (restored relationships, changed social dynamics); it cannot address the divine response. The Jon 3:10 structure (human turning → divine relenting → consequences averted) is not a psychological claim but a theological one about the responsiveness of God to human choices. This is the irreducible theological centre of the repentance vocabulary.

**Genuine divergence — repentance as gift:**
Act 5:31; 11:18; 2 Ti 2:25 — repentance as something God grants. Psychology assumes that the capacity for change is inherent in the person; the NT claim is that the capacity to repent is itself a divine gift. This is not merely a theological assertion about origins but a structural claim about the limits of unaided human will — that the cognitive-volitional reconstitution (metanoia) that repentance requires is not self-generated but enabled from outside the person.

## Section 9 — Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circles:** Circle Three (Inner Operations — repentance as a volitional-affective act) and Circle One (Relational Environment — repentance is inherently relational, directed toward God and expressed in restored community).

**Centre of gravity:** The soul's volitional register, with strong relational and affective dimensions. Repentance is the most decisively volitional characteristic in the programme — it is consistently commanded as an act of will (shuv: turn) while also engaging the deepest affective layers (na.cham: grief, regret, compassion).

**Two-forms structure:**
Form 1 (True repentance): Shuv from evil toward God; godly grief (2 Cor 7:10); metanoia as renewed mind. Produces life (Eze 18:21), divine relenting (Jon 3:10), comfort (Isa 66:13), restoration of fortunes (Jer 29:14).
Form 2 (Apostasy): Me.shu.vah — sustained turning-away. The negative of shuv within the same root family. "Perpetual backsliding" (Jer 8:5).
Form 3 (False repentance): Worldly grief (2 Cor 7:10); metamellomai without God-directedness; the Judges cycle of repentance without lasting transformation.
Transformation dynamic: The full repentance cycle — divine call → godly grief → turning → divine relenting → restoration. Most completely displayed in Jon 3; most theologically articulated in Jer 15:19.

**Relational network position:**
Repentance presupposes → guilt (the turn is from sin/transgression)
Repentance co-occurs with → shame (Jer 31:19 — struck thigh, ashamed)
Repentance produces → restoration/peace (Isa 30:15 — returning and rest)
Repentance produces → hope (Lam 3:21 — recall produces hope)
Repentance is enabled by → God's patience (2 Pe 3:9)
Repentance produces → divine relenting (Jon 3:10)
Repentance is followed by → comfort/consolation (na.cham family)

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Conceptual Word Register candidates:**

1. *Turning* — The most structurally accurate English equivalent to shuv. But "turning" is too generic (it lacks the covenantal and relational specificity). "Return" is more accurate (shuv often means going back to a prior relationship). Neither fully captures the directional, relational, and volitional complexity of shuv. Anchor registry: Repentance (135).

2. *Remorse* — Captures the na.cham affective dimension but misses the shuv directional act. Remorse can be entirely self-referential (Judas's metamellomai); biblical repentance is God-directed. Remorse without turning is precisely 2 Cor 7:10's "worldly grief that produces death." Anchor registry: Repentance (135), cross-reference to guilt (073), shame (146).

3. *Metanoia* — The most precise single word in any language for the biblical repentance concept because it was coined to translate the combined shuv/na.cham theology into Greek. It carries both the cognitive (mind-change) and directional (after/toward) dimensions. The English transliteration "metanoia" is increasingly used in theological and therapeutic contexts. Anchor registry: Repentance (135).

**Emerging cross-word patterns:**

1. **The shuv-nacham structural pair:** Human shuv → divine na.cham. This is the registry's governing structure. Every time repentance produces a change in the divine response, the structure is: human turning first, divine relenting second. The pair operates as a matched mechanism. Session D should examine whether this paired structure (human act producing divine response) is unique to repentance or whether other registries show similar structures.

2. **The me.shu.vah-shuv polarity within a single root:** Repentance (shuv) and apostasy (me.shu.vah) are derived from the same Hebrew root. The same word-root names both the cure and the disease. This is structurally distinctive — no other registry has its contra-term derived from the same root as its primary term. Session D should examine whether this root-polarity is a structural feature of the repentance vocabulary specifically, or whether it appears elsewhere.

3. **The cognitive shuv (H7725N: recall) as bridge to hope:** Lam 3:21 — "but this I call to mind (shuv/recall), and therefore I have hope." The act of cognitive return (calling the Lord's character to mind) is the mechanism that produces hope from the ash-heap of despair. This is the most direct single-verse link between repentance and hope in the corpus, and it operates through the cognitive register of shuv. Session D should examine whether the cognitive dimensions of shuv (recall, remember, take to heart) constitute a sub-register of the repentance vocabulary that bridges multiple other registries.

**Programme-level structural hypotheses:**

1. **Repentance as the turning-point characteristic:** In the covenantal narrative of the OT, shuv is the decisive event — the moment at which the trajectory of destruction is reversed. Every restoration-of-fortunes text (shuv shevut) is preceded by or connected to a turning. Repentance may function as the hinge characteristic in the programme — the one that most directly changes the direction of the other characteristics. Session D should examine whether repentance functions as a structural pivot — the characteristic whose presence or absence most directly determines which cluster of other characteristics becomes active (the guilt-shame-despair cluster or the hope-peace-joy cluster).

2. **The bidirectionality of shuv as a structural principle:** The shuv vocabulary operates in both directions: humans turn to God (repentance), God turns to humans (relenting/restoration), divine anger turns away (forgiveness), fortunes are turned back (restoration). This bidirectionality is without parallel in the programme: shuv is the vocabulary of every directional change in the covenant relationship. Session D should examine whether this bidirectionality is unique to repentance or whether other registries show similar structural expansiveness across human and divine domains.
