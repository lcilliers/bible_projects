## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Primary Hebrew Vocabulary — ne.phesh

Reading all 757 verses confirms ne.phesh as the most semantically complex single word in the entire corpus. It cannot be reduced to one meaning. The sub-glosses in the registry are not artificial divisions but genuine semantic registers of a single term operating in distinct modes — and the most important analytical finding from the full verse reading is that **these modes are not separate: they collapse into one another constantly**. The life that can be lost (H5315H) is experienced by the same self (H5315I) who eats and desires (H5315L) and who, when dead, becomes a corpse that defiles (H5315M). Reading the full 757 verses makes the unity underneath the diversity unmistakeable.

**Origin and constitution — ne.phesh as throat and breath.** The etymology is located at the somatic level: ne.phesh originally denoted the throat — the passage through which breath and food move, the place where desire is felt as hunger and life is felt as breathing. This somatic root is visible in H5315N (neck/throat, Psa 69:1 — "the waters have come up to my neck"; Psa 105:18 — "his neck was put in a collar of iron") and in the appetite sub-gloss H5315L, where the *craving* ne.phesh is not metaphor but the throat-desire of the hungry person, felt physically before it is known cognitively. The word moves from physical throat to the full person who breathes and desires — not by metaphorical extension but by organic development: the throat that breathes is the life; the life is the person.

**Ne.phesh as the living creature.** Gen 1:20, 1:21, 1:24 — every living creature (ne.phesh cha.yah) that moves in the waters and on the earth. Gen 2:7 — man himself becomes a living soul (ne.phesh cha.yah) when God breathes into his nostrils. The same phrase applies to animals and to human beings. This is analytically important and must not be harmonised away: the biblical text does not reserve ne.phesh for humans. The human soul is a *kind* of ne.phesh, not a unique category. What distinguishes the human ne.phesh from the animal ne.phesh is not the word but the act — the divine inbreathing of Gen 2:7.

**Ne.phesh as the complete person.** H5315J — the person-sub-gloss — confirms that ne.phesh can mean simply "a person" counted in census (Gen 46:15-27 — repeated use of "persons" in the family census). H5315I — the reflexive self — uses ne.phesh to mean "myself", "yourself", "himself" (Gen 27:4 — "that my soul may bless you" = that *I* may bless you). The soul is not a component of the person; it *is* the person — the whole living, breathing, desiring, deciding human being considered from the inside.

**Ne.phesh as life that can be lost.** H5315H — 180 verses — is the most extensive sub-gloss and the one most concerned with death and its threat. The person who "takes a life" (Lev 24:17), the one who holds their life in their hand (1Sa 19:5), the enemies who seek David's life (Psa 35:4, 38:12, 40:14, 54:3, 59:3, 63:9, 70:2) — all use ne.phesh as that which can be taken. The life-soul is under threat throughout the Psalter. The phrase "seek my life" is a formula: ne.phesh as the target of violence, the vulnerable animate existence that can be extinguished. Crucially, Lev 17:11 — "the life (ne.phesh) of the flesh is in the blood" — identifies the soul not with an immaterial substance separate from the body but with the blood that flows through the body. The life-soul *is* the animated flesh; blood is its physical medium.

**Ne.phesh as the seat of inner experience.** H5315G — 179 verses — shows ne.phesh as the experiencing inner centre: the soul that is drawn to someone (Gen 34:3), the soul that is bitter (Job 3:20, 10:1), the soul that pants (Psa 42:1), the soul that waits (Psa 62:1), the soul that is cast down (Psa 42:5,6,11; 43:5). Reading all 179 verses reveals that the inner-experience register of ne.phesh is not confined to exceptional spiritual moments but covers the full emotional range — desire, distress, love, anguish, joy, bitterness, longing, grief. The soul *is* the experiential centre of the person: to have a soul is to feel.

**Ne.phesh as appetite and desire.** H5315L — 45 verses — concentrates in the desire/satisfaction register. Exo 15:9 — "my desire (ne.phesh) shall have its fill." Num 11:6 — "our appetite (ne.phesh) is dried up." Deu 12:20 — "I will eat meat, as much as you desire (te.av.veh naphsh.cha)." Ecc 6:7 — "all the toil of man is for his mouth, yet his appetite (ne.phesh) is not satisfied." Isa 5:14 — "Sheol has enlarged its appetite (ne.phesh)." The appetite-soul does not describe something lesser than the "real" soul — it describes the same capacity. The person who hungers *for God* (Psa 42:1-2) and the person who hungers *for food* (Deu 12:20) are both using their ne.phesh. The soul's fundamental orientation is *toward* — it is constitutively appetitive, and the question the Bible asks is not whether it will desire but what it will desire.

**Ne.phesh as the dead body.** H5315M — 13 verses — all in the purity laws (Lev 19:28, 21:1, 21:11, 22:4; Num 5:2, 6:6, 6:11, 9:6-10; Num 19:11, 19:13; Hag 2:13). When a person dies, their corpse is a "dead soul" (ne.phesh met). The same word that denotes the living, experiencing, desiring person also denotes their corpse. This is not contradiction — it is the Bible's refusal to separate what we call "body" from what we call "soul." The corpse is the soul that no longer lives, not a container the soul has vacated. The purity-law framing treats the dead soul as a source of defilement — contact with it requires ritual cleansing — which places the boundary of the sacred at precisely the point where the ne.phesh ceases to animate its flesh.

**The structural finding from the ne.phesh verse set:** reading all 637 ne.phesh verses across the six sub-glosses confirms that ne.phesh denotes **the living human person considered as an animated whole** — breathing, desiring, experiencing, counted, threatened, lost. It does not denote an immortal substance lodged within the body. It denotes the body insofar as it is alive. This is the most important single analytical finding for the soul registry, and it only becomes visible by reading the full verse set: any single sub-gloss can be misread as a component of the person, but the full set shows they are all the same integrated reality viewed from different angles.

### 2.2 The Greek Vocabulary — psuchē and Compounds

**G5590G psuchē — 46 verses.** Reading all 46 reveals the Greek term operating in immediate continuity with ne.phesh but also extending it in two NT-specific directions.

The ne.phesh continuity is explicit: Mat 22:37 / Mar 12:30 / Luk 10:27 — "love the Lord your God with all your heart and all your soul and all your mind and all your strength" — psuchē in the Shema's expansion quotes directly from Deu 6:5 where ne.phesh appears. The soul-as-the-whole-person appears in Act 2:41 (three thousand souls added), Act 4:32 (the community of one heart and soul), Rom 13:1 (every soul subject to governing authorities). The soul's vulnerability appears in 1Pe 2:25 (Christ as Shepherd and Overseer of souls), 2Pe 2:8 (a righteous soul tormented daily), Rev 6:9 (souls of the martyred under the altar).

The first NT extension is **the soul as the object of salvation**: 1Pe 1:9 — "obtaining the outcome of your faith, the salvation of your souls." Jam 1:21 — "receive the implanted word which is able to save your souls." Heb 10:39 — "those who have faith and preserve their souls." This salvation-language for the soul is not absent from the OT (Psa 34:22 — "the Lord redeems the life of his servants") but it concentrates and intensifies in the NT. The soul that needs saving is the whole person, but specifically the person in their vulnerability before death and judgment.

The second NT extension is **the soul's relationship to Jesus**: Mat 26:38 / Mar 14:34 — "my soul is very sorrowful, even to death" — the Gethsemane soul of Christ. Joh 12:27 — "now is my soul troubled." Heb 10:38 — "my soul has no pleasure in him who shrinks back." These are among the most analytically important verses in the whole soul registry: Jesus has a soul, experiences soul-sorrow, soul-distress, soul-pleasure. The incarnate Son is not soul-exempt. His psuchē operates exactly as any human soul operates — which confirms that the soul is not merely the divine image in abstraction but the full person as a living, feeling, experiencing creature.

**G5590H psuchē: life — 33 verses.** The "lay down one's life" cluster (Joh 10:11,15,17; 15:13; 1Jo 3:16) uses psuchē-as-life for the most theologically concentrated set of soul verses in the NT. "The good shepherd lays down his life (psuchē) for the sheep" — the soul/life given in sacrifice. The "save your life/lose it" paradox (Mat 10:39, 16:25; Mar 8:35; Luk 9:24, 17:33; Joh 12:25) structures the soul as both the thing most desperately sought (the life one wants to preserve) and the thing that must be *released* to be truly found. This is the NT's most radical statement about the soul: the natural instinct of the soul is self-preservation; the way of the kingdom inverts this instinct. The soul that clings to itself loses itself; the soul that gives itself away finds itself. This is the transformation dynamic at the heart of the NT soul vocabulary.

**G5590I psuchē: myself — 6 verses.** Luk 1:46 — "My soul magnifies the Lord" — the Magnificat opens with the reflexive psuchē, Mary's whole self engaged in praise. Joh 12:27 — "Now is my soul troubled" — the same reflexive form for Christ's inward distress. Mar 14:34 — Gethsemane soul-sorrow. These three verses (plus 3Jo 2 — "as it goes well with your soul") show the reflexive soul in worship, distress, and wellbeing — covering the experiential range from its most elevated to its most anguished.

**G5591 psuchikos — 5 verses.** This is the most analytically important term in the Greek soul inventory for the soul-spirit boundary question. The five verses fall into two groups: 1Co 2:14 and 15:44,46 (the natural vs spiritual body and person) and Jam 3:15 and Jude 19 (earthly, unspiritual wisdom vs heavenly wisdom). In all five verses, psuchikos describes a mode of existence that is *limited to the soul-level*, without the Spirit's dimension. The natural (psuchikos) person cannot receive the things of the Spirit of God because they are spiritually discerned (1Co 2:14). The natural body (sōma psuchikon) is the present form; the spiritual body (sōma pneumatikon) is the resurrection form (1Co 15:44). Jude 19 — "worldly people, devoid of the Spirit (pneuma mē echontes)." In every case, psuchikos marks the *lower* register — not evil, but incomplete, limited to the creaturely dimension without the Spirit's animating presence.

This is the most important cross-registry data point for the spirit-soul boundary from within the soul registry itself: the soul (psuchē) and the spirit (pneuma) are genuinely distinct, and the soul without the spirit is a real possibility — the person operating on their natural creaturely capacities alone. 1Co 15:45 adds the christological dimension: "the first man Adam became a living being (psuchēn zōsan — the LXX of Gen 2:7); the last Adam became a life-giving spirit (pneuma zōopoioun)." The soul describes Adam's creaturely mode; the life-giving spirit describes Christ's resurrected mode. The soul is the beginning; the Spirit is the eschatological completion.

**G3642 oligopsuchos — 1 verse.** 1Th 5:14 — "encourage the fainthearted (oligopsuchous)." Small-souled: the soul that has contracted under pressure, lost its capacity to sustain itself, to hope, to act. The single verse places the fainthearted person in a triad with the idle and the weak — the church's pastoral responsibility toward those whose inner life has diminished under trial.

**G1374 dipsuchos — 2 verses.** Jam 1:8 — "a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways." Jam 4:8 — "purify your hearts, you double-minded (dipsuchoi)." The double-souled person is one whose inner life is divided between two orientations — God and the world — and this interior division produces external instability in all their ways. The soul's singleness (wholeness of orientation) is both a moral and a spiritual reality. The double-minded person does not suffer from intellectual confusion but from a split allegiance at the soul's centre.

**G1634 ekpsuchō — 3 verses.** The soul going out: Act 5:5 (Ananias), Act 5:10 (Sapphira), Act 12:23 (Herod struck down by the angel). Three deaths, three persons whose souls depart at divine judgment. The verb marks the moment of transition — the soul's exit. Its grammatical subject is always the person dying; its implied agent, in all three cases, is divine action.

**G0674 apopsuchō — 1 verse.** Luk 21:26 — "people fainting (apopsuchounton) with fear and with foreboding of what is coming on the world." The soul fainting under overwhelming eschatological dread — the soul's capacity exhausted and giving way. Where ekpsuchō describes the soul departing finally, apopsuchō describes the soul giving out under extreme pressure while the person still lives.

**G0895 apsuchos — 1 verse.** 1Co 14:7 — "lifeless instruments, such as the flute or the harp." The apsuchos instrument produces sound without a soul behind it — this is not spiritual commentary on musical instruments but a rhetorical contrast: a lifeless instrument gives unintelligible sound; a living person speaking in tongues without interpretation is equally unintelligible. The verse defines the soul negatively — by what its absence produces.

**G2174 eupsucheō — 1 verse.** Phi 2:19 — "that I too may be cheered (eupsucheō) by news of you." Soul-gladness: the good condition of the soul when news is favourable. A minor term that confirms soul-wellbeing as a category the NT takes seriously.

**G2473 isopsuchos — 1 verse.** Phi 2:20 — "I have no one like him, who will be genuinely concerned (isopsuchos) for your welfare." Like-souled — sharing the same soul-orientation, the same inner direction. The term describes Timothy's unique quality among Paul's co-workers: his soul is oriented toward the same things Paul's soul is. Community is possible when souls share orientation.

**G4861 sumpsuchos — 1 verse.** Phi 2:2 — "being in full accord (sumpsuchos) and of one mind." Same-souled: the Philippians are urged toward a communal sharing of inner orientation. The prefix sum- (together) makes explicit what isopsuchos implies — multiple souls joined in common direction. This is the relational and community dimension of the soul: the soul is not only private but capable of genuine union with other souls through shared orientation.

**H5314 na.phash — 3 verses.** Exo 23:12 — the Sabbath for the ox and donkey "that they may be refreshed (ye.na.phash)." Exo 31:17 — "in six days the Lord made heaven and earth, and on the seventh day he rested and was refreshed (wa.yi.na.phash)." 2Sa 16:14 — David and his people arrived weary at the Jordan "and there he refreshed himself (wa.yi.na.pesh)." The most analytically significant verse is Exo 31:17: God himself rests and *is refreshed*. The verb na.phash, used here of the Creator, attributes to God the capacity for soul-refreshment — the experience of restoration after activity. This is the only verse in the corpus that attributes an inner soul-state to God in terms drawn from the soul's physical/experiential register (exhaustion and refreshment). It is not casual language — it is embedded in the Sabbath theology as the ground for human Sabbath rest.

### 2.3 The Structural Finding — Hebrew vs Greek

Reading the full verse sets of both ne.phesh and psuchē confirms: the Greek term translates the Hebrew with substantial continuity in the LXX tradition and in the NT's OT quotations. Both terms describe the whole living person, the experienced inner life, the life that can be lost, the self as agent and patient of inner states.

The divergence is real but specific: the Greek compounds (psuchikos, dipsuchos, oligopsuchos, etc.) introduce a vocabulary of soul-analysis that has no Hebrew equivalent. The NT can talk *about* the soul as an object of description — its natural vs spiritual mode (psuchikos), its wholeness vs division (dipsuchos), its fullness vs diminishment (oligopsuchos). The Hebrew vocabulary is almost entirely first-person: the soul speaks, desires, laments, waits, praises — it does not analyse itself from outside. The Greek compounds represent a genuinely new analytical register, not available in the OT vocabulary.

The most significant structural difference: the OT vocabulary consistently grounds the soul in the body — the throat, the blood, the breathing flesh — while the NT vocabulary, especially the psuchikos terms, begins to describe the soul as a level of existence that can be superseded (by the pneumatikon). The direction of travel is toward a greater distinction between soul and spirit, without ever making them separate substances.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — Spirit, Soul, Body

**Primary circle:** Soul — the soul registry is the definition of Circle Two's centre. Every term in the inventory occupies the soul dimension by definition.

**Spirit-soul boundary:** G5591 psuchikos (5 verses) establishes the boundary from within. The psuchikos person is fully human but Spirit-absent. 1Th 5:23 — "your whole spirit (pneuma) and soul (psuchē) and body (sōma)" — the only explicit tripartite statement in the NT, placing soul between spirit and body. Heb 4:12 — "the word of God pierces to the division of soul and spirit, of joints and marrow" — soul and spirit are adjacent but distinguishable, like joints and marrow in a bone. They can be distinguished analytically, but the act of distinguishing them requires divine speech.

**Soul-body boundary:** H5315H (blood as life-soul), H5315N (throat/neck), H5315L (appetite), H5314 (refreshment) — all locate the soul at the body's surface, not above it. The soul experiences through the body; the body expresses the soul's states. The boundary is a gradient, not a wall.

**Key finding from full verse reading:** The soul in the biblical vocabulary is not the "upper" or "better" part of the person. It is the person as alive — the whole creature animated by divine breath, oriented by desire, threatened by death, capable of joy and anguish, and sustained by God. Its "above" is not the body — it is the spirit.

### 3.2 The Six Dimensions — Soul's Engagement

**Feeling (Affective):** Primary. The soul is the experiential centre of the person's affective life — cast down (Psa 42:5), bitter (Job 3:20), joyful (Psa 35:9), sorrowful (Mat 26:38), troubled (Joh 12:27), magnifying God (Luk 1:46). The full ne.phesh verse set shows feeling is not a secondary function of the soul but its primary mode of operation.

**Desiring/Orienting (Appetitive):** Primary. H5315L establishes that the soul's fundamental structure is appetitive — it reaches toward what it craves. The question is always the object of the craving. Psa 42:1-2 — "as a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God" — orients the appetitive soul toward God. Ecc 6:7 — "the appetite (ne.phesh) is not satisfied" — describes the soul's appetitive restlessness when wrongly directed.

**Orienting (Spiritual/God-ward):** Very high. The most concentrated use of ne.phesh across the 637 OT verses is in the Psalter's God-ward address: "my soul waits for God" (Psa 62:1), "my soul thirsts for you" (Psa 63:1), "my soul magnifies the Lord" (Luk 1:46 — LXX psuchē language). The soul is the locus of the person's orientation toward God — not the spirit alone.

**Choosing (Volitional):** Present. H5315I (myself) carries volitional weight — the soul as the agent of commitment (Num 30:2-13 — the vows a person "binds upon their soul"). G1374 dipsuchos — the divided soul that cannot commit. The soul's wholeness is what makes genuine choice possible; its division (dipsuchos) makes the person unstable.

**Being (Moral/Identity):** Present. The ne.phesh carries moral accountability: Eze 18:4,20 — "the soul who sins shall die." The soul is the seat of moral identity, not merely the locus of emotional states. 1Pe 1:9 — "the salvation of your souls" — the soul as what is ultimately at stake in salvation.

**Thinking (Cognitive):** Secondary but present. Psa 13:2 — "how long must I take counsel in my soul?" The soul deliberates. Pro 2:10 — "wisdom will come into your heart, and knowledge will be pleasant to your soul." The soul is the site of wisdom's reception, not merely of desire's expression.

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God as the Soul's Creator and Sustainer

**Gen 2:7 — the constitutive act.** God forms dust and breathes the breath of life (nishmat cha.yim) into it; the man becomes a living soul (ne.phesh cha.yah). The soul is not pre-existent, not self-generated, not a fragment of divine substance — it is the result of divine breath meeting dust. The soul is the creature-in-animation: it exists only because God breathes into it, and it exists only as a whole (dust + breath = living soul, not dust + soul = living person + soul).

**Gen 2:7 read against the whole verse set:** Reading all 180 verses of H5315H (life-soul) confirms: God is the one who redeems the life (Psa 34:22, 49:15, 72:14), who preserves the life (Psa 121:7, 97:10), who requires the life (Luk 12:20 — "this night your soul is required of you"), who can save the soul from death (Jam 5:20). Every life-soul in the corpus stands in a relationship of dependence on God — not merely at origin but continuously.

**Isa 42:1 — God's soul delights.** "Behold my servant, whom I uphold, my chosen, in whom my soul delights; I have put my Spirit upon him." God has a ne.phesh — or at minimum employs the vocabulary of soul-delight to describe his own inner response. This parallels Exo 31:17 (God refreshed). Both verses apply soul-level vocabulary to God — not asserting that God has a soul in the creature's sense, but using the soul vocabulary as the available language for describing divine inner states that are analogous to human inner states.

**Jer 32:41 — God's soul in the new covenant.** "I will rejoice in doing them good, and I will plant them in this land in faithfulness, with all my heart and all my soul." God's whole-soul commitment to Israel's restoration. The soul vocabulary used for divine wholeness of intention mirrors its use for human wholeness — God's commitment is not partial but total, reaching his soul's depth.

**Eze 18:4 — all souls belong to God.** "Behold, all souls are mine; the soul of the father as well as the soul of the son is mine." This is the most direct assertion of divine ownership of the soul in the corpus. Every ne.phesh belongs to God — not as property but as creature. The soul's moral accountability is grounded in this ownership: the soul who sins shall die because the soul is accountable to its owner.

**1Co 15:45 — the christological soul-Spirit axis.** "The first man Adam became a living being (psuchēn zōsan — Gen 2:7); the last Adam became a life-giving spirit (pneuma zōopoioun)." The soul belongs to the Adamic order; the life-giving Spirit belongs to the Christic order. This is the deepest NT statement about the soul's eschatological trajectory: the soul-mode of existence is not its final mode. The resurrection transforms the psuchikon into the pneumatikon.

### 4.2 The Shepherd and Overseer of Souls

1Pe 2:25 — "you have now returned to the Shepherd and Overseer (episkopos) of your souls." Christ is explicitly titled as the one who exercises authority over souls — not in an abstract theological sense but as the returning-point for straying souls. The soul's natural orientation (toward God, toward the good) can be lost; Christ is the overseer who returns and reorients it.

Heb 13:17 — "they are keeping watch over your souls, as those who will have to give an account." The community's leaders are accountable to God for the souls entrusted to them. The soul is the unit of pastoral responsibility.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 The Soul's Somatic Groundedness

The soul vocabulary in this registry is among the most somatically grounded in the entire corpus. Reading all somatic-linked terms (H5315G through N — all carry som=1 flag) in full confirms the pattern established at Section 2.1: the soul is not housed in the body, it *is* the animated body.

**H5315N — neck/throat (2 verses).** Psa 69:1 — "waters have come up to my neck (ne.phesh)" — the soul at the body's most vulnerable point, the throat where life enters and can be cut off. Psa 105:18 — "his neck (ne.phesh) was put in a collar of iron" — Joseph's neck/soul in captivity. The throat is the point of the soul's somatic vulnerability.

**H5315L — appetite (45 verses).** The appetite ne.phesh is felt physically before it is known cognitively. Num 11:6 — "our strength is dried up" — the soul's appetitive failure produces physical weakness. Pro 16:26 — "a worker's appetite (ne.phesh) works for him; his mouth urges him on" — the soul drives the body to labour in order to satisfy its hunger. The motivational structure here runs from soul (appetite) through body (mouth, labour) back to soul (satisfaction).

**H5314 na.phash — refreshment (3 verses).** Exo 23:12 — the ox and donkey "may be refreshed" on the Sabbath. Exo 31:17 — God himself refreshed after creation. 2Sa 16:14 — David refreshed at the Jordan. The refreshment of the soul is a somatic event — rest after exertion, restoration of the body's animation. The Sabbath is the weekly event of soul-refreshment for creatures and, by implication, a reflection of the Creator's own pattern.

**Gen 35:18 — the soul departing at death.** "As her soul was departing (for she was dying)" — Rachel's ne.phesh leaving in childbirth. The soul's departure is the moment of death; its presence is the condition of life. But the soul departs from where? Not from a distinct location within the body, but from the body as animated whole. The soul's departure is the body's death, and they are the same event viewed from two angles.

### 5.2 The Somatic Cascade of the Soul Under Threat

Reading the full H5315H verse set reveals a consistent pattern: the soul under mortal threat produces a cascade of bodily responses — the life-soul is felt in the body as urgency, as paralysis, as desperate action. 1Sa 19:11 — Michal warns David, "tomorrow you will be killed" — the soul's threat produces immediate physical action (escape by night). 2Sa 23:17 — "shall I drink the blood of the men who went at the risk of their lives?" — the life-soul as the highest possible stake in action.

The most significant somatic-soul verse in the NT: Heb 4:12 — "the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and spirit, of joints and marrow." The parallel of soul/spirit with joints/marrow places the distinction in the body-register: as joints and marrow are both within the bone but distinguishable, soul and spirit are both within the person but distinguishable. The instrument that makes the distinction is not philosophy but the living Word. This is the most important somatic-dimensional verse for the soul registry.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 The Soul in Relationship

The soul is constitutively relational. It does not exist in isolation. Reading the full verse set confirms five primary relational modes:

**Soul-to-God:** The dominant relational mode across the Psalter. "My soul waits for God" (Psa 62:1), "my soul thirsts for you" (Psa 63:1), "to you, O Lord, I lift up my soul" (Psa 25:1). The soul's orientation toward God is not one option among many — it is the soul's designed relational direction, and the Psalter records what happens when the soul is both fulfilling and struggling to fulfil this direction.

**Soul-to-soul:** 1Sa 18:1 — "the soul of Jonathan was knit to the soul of David." The most explicit soul-to-soul bonding in the corpus: two ne.pheshim joined in covenant friendship. Act 4:32 — "the full number of those who believed were of one heart and soul" — corporate soul-unity as the mark of the early community. G4861 sumpsuchos (Phi 2:2) and G2473 isopsuchos (Phi 2:20) extend this relational soul-vocabulary into the NT community.

**Soul-to-self:** G1374 dipsuchos — the interior relationship of the soul with itself, which can be whole or divided. The divided soul's instability is not merely ethical but relational — the soul that cannot be at peace with its own orientation cannot be at peace in any other relationship.

**Soul-to-threat:** H5315H's 180 verses are predominantly about the soul under threat from human enemies. "Those who seek my life" is a formula running through David's Psalms. The life-soul exists in a hostile relational environment and requires divine protection. 1Pe 4:19 — "entrust their souls to a faithful Creator while doing good" — the relational act of trust that commits the soul to God's custody under threat.

**Soul under divine authority:** Eze 18:4 — "all souls are mine." Luk 12:20 — "this night your soul is required of you." The soul stands under divine authority that can summon it at any moment. This is the ultimate relational context within which all other soul-relationships exist.

### 6.2 Cross-Registry Links — Verse-Level Grounding

**Soul ↔ Fear (reg 61):** Mat 10:28 — "do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul (psuchē). Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell." The soul is introduced as the dimension that survives bodily death — and the fear appropriate to its ultimate vulnerability before God is the reverential fear the fear registry identifies as rightly ordered. The verse establishes the soul as the primary object of ultimate threat and ultimate protection simultaneously.

**Soul ↔ Desire (reg 43):** H5315L — the appetite sub-gloss of ne.phesh — is the primary SHARED_TERM link. The ne.phesh that desires, craves, is satisfied or unsatisfied, is the same ne.phesh analysed under the desire registry. Deu 14:26 — "spend the money for whatever you desire (te.av.veh naphsh.cha) — oxen or sheep or wine or strong drink, whatever your appetite (naphsh.cha) desires" — ne.phesh and ta.a.vah (desire) are used in synonymous parallelism. The soul *is* the desiring capacity, not merely its location.

**Soul ↔ Grief (reg 71):** Job 30:25 — "was not my soul (ne.phesh) grieved for the needy?" H5315G connects to grief throughout the lament Psalter. The soul's capacity for grief is not a disorder but its appropriate response to loss and injustice.

**Soul ↔ Hope (reg 78):** Psa 42:5,11; 43:5 — "why are you cast down, O my soul...hope in God." The soul's capacity for despair is met by the command to hope — the soul itself is addressed as the locus of both the downward pull and the upward command.

**Soul ↔ Peace (reg 117):** Lam 3:17 — "my soul is bereft of peace." Heb 6:19 — "we have this as a sure and steadfast anchor of the soul, a hope." The soul's need for peace and its finding of an anchor — the soul as the site where peace is experienced or lost.

**Soul ↔ Anguish (reg 5):** Job 7:11 — "I will speak in the anguish of my spirit; I will complain in the bitterness of my soul." Both anguish and soul appear together, the soul as the locus where anguish is felt.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — Gen 2:7

*"Then the Lord God formed the man of dust from the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living soul (ne.phesh cha.yah)."*

Having read all 757 verses, this verse stands as the only one in the corpus where the soul comes into existence. Three elements: dust (material ground), divine breath (animating agency), and the result (living soul). The soul is not the breath; it is not the dust; it is what happens when the breath meets the dust. This means: the soul is *relational* at its origin — it exists only in the relation between divine agency and creaturely matter. Remove either element and there is no soul. The soul is neither pure spirit (breath without dust) nor mere material (dust without breath).

Reading this against Gen 1:20,21,24 — the animals are also ne.phesh cha.yah — confirms that the human soul shares its category with the animals. What distinguishes the human soul is not the noun but the act: the direct divine inbreathing. The animals receive life; the human receives the breath of the Life-giver directly into his face.

The verse names no soul before the breath. The soul does not pre-exist its constitution. It is not imprisoned in the body. It is not a fragment of divinity. It is the creature as alive — the body animated, the dust breathed-upon.

### 7.2 1Co 15:45 — The Soul's Eschatological Trajectory

*"Thus it is written, 'The first man Adam became a living being (psuchēn zōsan)'; the last Adam became a life-giving spirit (pneuma zōopoioun)."*

Paul quotes Gen 2:7 (LXX) and names Adam's condition as the psuchē-condition — the creaturely, animated, mortal mode of existence. The "last Adam" — Christ in resurrection — becomes pneuma zōopoioun: the Spirit that gives life to others. The trajectory implied: the soul is not the final form. It is the beginning. The resurrection body is pneumatikon, not psuchikon (1Co 15:44). The soul is the creature's present reality; the Spirit is the creature's eschatological destiny.

This verse, read against the full G5591 psuchikos set (1Co 2:14, 15:44,46; Jam 3:15; Jude 19), establishes the soul not as a problem to be escaped but as a legitimate creaturely mode that the Spirit does not abolish but transforms. The psuchikon is raised as the pneumatikon — not discarded but glorified.

### 7.3 Psa 42:1-5 — The Soul's Self-Address

*"As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God...Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God."*

Reading all 179 ne.phesh (H5315G) verses confirms: Psalm 42-43 contains the highest concentration of soul vocabulary in any single passage of Scripture (10 uses of ne.phesh across the two psalms). The soul pants (craving), thirsts (appetite), is poured out (lament), is cast down (despair), is addressed (the psalmist speaks to his own soul), is commanded (hope in God). Five distinct modes of soul-operation in consecutive verses.

The self-address of the soul is the most psychologically sophisticated move in the OT: the psalmist observes his own soul from the outside ("why are you cast down?"), diagnoses it, and gives it a counter-command ("hope in God"). This is not dissociation but integration — the person who can address their own soul is more whole, not less, because they can see their inner state and not be passively carried by it.

### 7.4 Mat 26:38 / Mar 14:34 — The Soul of Christ in Gethsemane

*"My soul is very sorrowful, even to death; remain here, and watch with me."*

Having read all 46 G5590G verses, this verse carries the full weight of the soul vocabulary's history. The soul that pants for God (Psa 42:1), that is sorrowful unto death in Lamentations, that Rachel's soul departs from in Gen 35:18 — this is the same vocabulary now applied to the Son of God in the moment before his passion. The Incarnation is not soul-exempt. The eternal Son, taking human nature, takes a human soul — which means he takes the capacity for sorrow, for anguish, for the desperate desire that this cup might pass. Gethsemane is the fullest expression of the soul's God-ward relationship under mortal threat: the soul desires to be delivered and simultaneously submits to the Father's will.

### 7.5 Heb 4:12 — The Sword that Divides Soul and Spirit

*"For the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and spirit, of joints and marrow."*

The soul-spirit distinction is not made by philosophy but by the living Word. The parallel with "joints and marrow" is crucial: both are within the bone, both are bone-realities, but one can distinguish them — not from outside but from within, by cutting. The Word of God performs this surgery. This implies: the distinction between soul and spirit is not immediately self-evident to the person. The soul cannot reliably know whether it is operating in the Spirit's dimension or merely in its own creaturely dimension. Only divine speech makes the distinction visible.

### 7.6 1Th 5:23 — The Tripartite Preservation

*"Now may the God of peace himself sanctify you completely, and may your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ."*

The only explicit tripartite statement in the NT. Paul does not present the three as separate substances but as aspects of the whole person that God preserves entirely. The prayer is for *holoteles* (complete) sanctification — the whole person, not a preferred component. Reading against G5591 psuchikos (soul without Spirit), this verse presents the opposite: the person in whom spirit, soul, and body are all held by God, all sanctified, all kept. The soul's sanctification is neither absorbed into the spirit nor left behind — it is preserved alongside the spirit as part of the whole.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Field

**Neuroscience and cognitive science** (primary) — the questions of what consciousness is, where it is, and how it relates to the body map most directly onto the soul vocabulary.
**Psychology** (secondary) — the questions of the self, personal identity, and psychological integration.

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — The embodied self (Damásio, *The Feeling of What Happens*, 1999).** Damásio's central claim: consciousness and the sense of self emerge from the body's ongoing representation of its own states. The "proto-self" is a moment-to-moment neural map of the body. The felt sense of "I" is not separable from the body's ongoing self-representation. This is phenomenological convergence with the ne.phesh vocabulary: the soul is constitutively embodied, not a substance that uses the body as its instrument.

**Finding 2 — The brain-body integration (Damásio, *Descartes' Error*, 1994).** The somatic marker hypothesis: emotions and feelings are not separate from cognition — they are implemented in the body and feed back into decision-making. Feelings are body-signals that the brain reads as inputs, not merely outputs. This maps precisely onto the appetitive ne.phesh: the soul's desires are felt first in the body (throat, stomach, blood) before they are known consciously.

**Finding 3 — Psychological integration vs fragmentation (clinical psychology broadly).** The concept of psychological integration — the person whose parts cohere — maps onto the biblical soul vocabulary's contrast between the whole soul (Psa 86:11 — "unite my heart to fear your name") and the divided soul (dipsuchos). The double-minded person of Jam 1:8 is exactly what attachment theory describes as the person with disorganised attachment: unstable, unable to commit, pulled in multiple directions.

**Finding 4 — The "hard problem" of consciousness (Chalmers, 1995).** Why physical processes give rise to subjective experience remains scientifically unexplained. Science can describe the neural correlates of experience; it cannot explain why there is "something it is like" to be a conscious being. The biblical ne.phesh is precisely the word for the living, experiencing person — and the hard problem is exactly the gap where the soul vocabulary does its work.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Phenomenological convergence:** The biblical soul and the neuroscientific account of embodied consciousness converge on the same core claim: the experiencing self is not a substance separate from the body but emerges from the body's animation. The ne.phesh is not Cartesian — it does not need to be.

**Mechanistic convergence:** Damásio's somatic marker hypothesis describes the mechanism by which the body's states feed into the person's felt experience and decision-making. This is exactly what the appetite ne.phesh describes from the inside: the body's hunger *is* the soul's craving.

**Register divergence:** Science describes the self as an emergent property of neural processes. The Bible describes the soul as the creature in relationship with God — its meaning is not generated from within but received from outside (divine breath, divine ownership, divine redemption). The soul is not self-explanatory; its nature is given by the God who breathes into it and redeems it.

**Genuine divergence — resurrection and the psuchikon/pneumatikon:** 1Co 15:44-46 describes a transformation of the mode of existence that has no scientific parallel. The resurrection body is not a resuscitated biological body; it is a pneumatikon — a Spirit-mode body. Science has no category for this. It is not merely outside science's current knowledge but outside its domain. Named as genuine divergence and held open.

**Genuine divergence — the soul of God:** Exo 31:17 and Jer 32:41 use soul-language for God (refreshed, whole-soul commitment). Science's account of consciousness is entirely creaturely. There is no scientific category for divine inner states.

## Section 9: Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circle:** Soul — by definition. Every term in the registry contributes to the Soul dimension of Circle Two.

**Spirit-soul boundary:** G5591 psuchikos establishes the boundary with precision: the soul without the Spirit is a real condition (the natural person of 1Co 2:14), not merely a theoretical distinction. The soul is not the spirit; the spirit is the soul's higher dimension. Heb 4:12 confirms the distinction is real but requires the Word to make it visible. 1Th 5:23 confirms that both are preserved — the soul is not absorbed into the spirit at salvation or at death, but kept alongside it.

**Soul-body boundary:** The ne.phesh vocabulary collapses this boundary rather than drawing it. The soul *is* the animated body. The body's death *is* the soul's departure. The soul's appetites *are* the body's hungers. The boundary is a gradient, not a division.

**Two-forms structure:** The soul vocabulary does not present a disordered form and a rightly-ordered form in the same way as fear or anger. Instead it presents two modes: the psuchikos (creaturely, soul-level, Spirit-absent) and the pneumatikos (Spirit-animated, eschatologically transformed). This is not moral disorder vs order — it is creaturely incompleteness vs eschatological completion. The soul is not corrupted by sin so much as it is *insufficient without the Spirit*. The transformation dynamic is: divine breath at creation (Gen 2:7) → Spirit's presence during life → pneumatic body at resurrection (1Co 15:44). The soul is the beginning of the journey, not its end.

**Relational orientation:** The soul is constitutively oriented toward God (Psa 42:1-2, 62:1, 63:1), toward other souls (1Sa 18:1, Act 4:32), and toward its own objects of desire (H5315L). The soul's distress occurs when these orientations are disrupted; its wellbeing occurs when they are fulfilled.

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "consciousness."** The biblical soul vocabulary addresses the territory of consciousness — the experiencing, self-aware, desiring person — without using a word for "consciousness." The hard problem of consciousness is precisely the soul problem restated in modern vocabulary. Session D should examine whether the soul vocabulary provides a richer account of consciousness than the modern concept (which tends toward the purely cognitive) because it includes desire, appetite, moral accountability, and relational orientation as constitutive of the experiencing self.

**Conceptual Word Register Candidate — "identity."** The soul functions as the seat of personal identity across the corpus — the self that persists through time, that is threatened, that can be lost and found (Mat 10:39), that is known by God (Psa 139). But the biblical soul is *given* identity (by the divine breath, by covenant, by redemption) rather than *constructing* identity. This contrasts sharply with modern identity theory. Session D should note the distinction.

**Emerging pattern — The soul as the site of encounter.** Across 757 verses, the soul is the consistent location where divine-human encounter occurs. God addresses the soul (Eze 18:4 — "all souls are mine"), the soul thirsts for God (Psa 42:1), God's word divides soul and spirit (Heb 4:12), God's peace anchors the soul (Heb 6:19). The soul is not merely the human side of this encounter — it is the *place* of encounter. This may be the most important contribution of the soul registry to Session D: the soul as the locus of God-human relation.

**Emerging pattern — The soul's self-address.** Psa 42:5,11; 43:5 — the psalmist speaks to his own soul, commands it, challenges it. This reflexive capacity — the soul's ability to observe itself and give itself a counter-command — appears across multiple soul-languages in the corpus (Luk 12:19 — the rich fool speaks to his soul; Psa 35:3 — "say to my soul, I am your salvation"). This is a distinctive feature of the soul vocabulary that has no equivalent in the spirit vocabulary. The soul can be self-reflective; it can be its own observer. Session D should examine whether this reflexivity is integral to what the biblical writers understood the soul to be.

**Structural hypothesis — The soul as created appetite.** The full ne.phesh verse set suggests a hypothesis for Session D: the soul is fundamentally the creature as *desiring* — the animated being whose orientation is constitutively *toward*. Every soul-use in the corpus (apart from the dead-soul purity law material) involves the soul reaching toward something: toward God, toward food, toward other souls, toward life, toward peace. If this is correct, the soul is not first a knowing faculty (that is the heart/mind) or a willing faculty (that is the spirit/will) but a *desiring faculty* — and the question of the soul's health is always the question of its objects: what is it reaching toward, and is that the right thing? This would connect directly to the desire registry and to the question of how the soul's orientation is formed and reformed.
