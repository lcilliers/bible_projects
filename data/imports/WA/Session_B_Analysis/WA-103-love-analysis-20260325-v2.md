## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Love Vocabulary's Expanded Scope

Reading all 2,067 verses from the revised export confirms and substantially extends the earlier partial analysis. The most significant new finding is the scale of the neighbor and peace vocabulary within the love registry: H7453 re.a (173 verses) and G1515 eirēnē (84 verses) are large sets that establish love's relational range far beyond the personal-affective register.

The expanded vocabulary confirms the four-register architecture established in the partial analysis, but adds two further registers that the earlier partial reading missed:

1. **Intimate/erotic desire** — dod/a.ha.vah/cha.mad vocabulary (Song of Songs concentration)
2. **Womb-compassion** — ra.cham/ra.cha.mim/re.chem (somatic love originating in bodily relationship)
3. **Covenant-faithfulness** — che.sed (the programmatically most important Hebrew love term)
4. **Committed-choice love** — agapaō/agapē (the NT's primary love vocabulary)
5. **Neighbourly love** — re.a/re.ut (the social-community register of love)
6. **Goodness as love's expression** — agathos/agathōsunē/tuv (love enacted through good works)

### 2.2 The Primary Hebrew Vocabulary

**H2617A che.sed (steadfast love/kindness) — 241 verses.** Reading all 241 confirms: che.sed is the most theologically loaded word in the entire love registry and one of the most important words in the OT. No English word captures it: "steadfast love" (ESV) catches the constancy; "kindness" catches the relational warmth; "mercy" (KJV) catches its movement toward need; "loyalty" catches the covenant dimension. None alone is adequate.

The dominant analytical finding from reading all 241 verses: **che.sed is constitutively covenantal — it is the love that operates within and upholds a covenant relationship, specifically when covenant loyalty is most needed and least deserved.** The most structurally significant verses are those where che.sed is shown despite failure: Gen 39:21 — God shows che.sed to Joseph in an Egyptian prison; 2Ch 24:22 — Joash fails to remember the che.sed Jehoiada showed him; Hos 6:4 — Israel's che.sed is "like a morning cloud... like the dew that goes early away" — it evaporates under pressure. The che.sed that holds is the divine che.sed; the human che.sed is perpetually unreliable.

Four structural patterns across the 241 verses:

*Pattern 1 — Che.sed as the divine name-attribute (Exo 34:6 and its echoes).* "The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness." This Sinai self-disclosure is quoted or echoed in Num 14:18, Neh 9:17, Psa 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2. Che.sed is embedded in God's name. To know the divine name is to know his steadfast love. The Psalter's 26 repetitions of "for his steadfast love endures forever" (Psa 136) — each verse of redemptive history answered by the same refrain — demonstrate: every divine act in creation and history is an act of che.sed.

*Pattern 2 — Che.sed as the covenant's content.* Deu 7:9 — "the faithful God who keeps covenant and steadfast love with those who love him." Deu 7:12 — "the Lord your God will keep with you the covenant and the steadfast love." Che.sed and covenant (be.rit) are a pair across the Psalter: Neh 1:5, 9:32, Dan 9:4 all use this phrase. The covenant is the frame; che.sed is its operative content — the love that makes the covenant worth having.

*Pattern 3 — Che.sed and the knowledge of God (Hos 6:6).* "For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings." Che.sed and the knowledge of God are placed in synonymous parallelism — to know God is to practise che.sed; to practise che.sed is to know God. This verse (quoted twice by Jesus: Mat 9:13, 12:7) establishes che.sed as the prophetic summary of covenant relationship. The person who offers sacrifice without che.sed does not know the God they are sacrificing to.

*Pattern 4 — Che.sed as the Psalter's primary petition.* More than any other single vocabulary item, the Psalms pray for che.sed. Psa 63:3 — "your steadfast love is better than life." Psa 89:1 — "I will sing of the steadfast love of the Lord forever." Psa 107 — four times the refrain "let them thank the Lord for his steadfast love, for his wondrous works to the children of man." The che.sed vocabulary generates the Psalter's affective centre. The person who has nowhere else to turn has the divine che.sed — which by definition is most present when everything else has failed.

**H0160 a.ha.vah (love noun) — 45 verses.** Reading all 45 confirms the full range: Jacob's love for Rachel (Gen 29:20 — "they seemed to him but a few days because of the love he had for her"); Jonathan's love for David (1Sa 20:17 — "because he loved him as he loved his own soul"); Amnon's love that inverted to hatred greater than the love (2Sa 13:15); God's everlasting love (Jer 31:3 — "I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued my faithfulness to you").

The Song of Songs concentration: Song 2:4-5 ("his banner over me was love... I am sick with love"), Song 8:6-7 ("love is strong as death, jealousy is fierce as the grave... many waters cannot quench love, neither can floods drown it"). These are the most intense love-statements in the corpus — the a.ha.vah that cannot be extinguished or purchased is the very definition of indestructible love. Hos 11:4 — "I led them with cords of kindness, with the bands of love (a.ha.vah)" — the divine love as the gentle bond that draws rather than compels. Zep 3:17 — "he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will quiet you by his love (a.ha.vah)" — God's love as the quieting, silencing force that stills anxiety.

**H0158 a.hav (lover) — 2 verses.** Pro 5:19 ("a lovely deer, a graceful doe... be intoxicated always in her love") — the lover as the intended exclusive object of the husband's delight. Hos 8:9 — "Ephraim has hired lovers (a.ha.vim)" — the disordered love that pays for what should be given freely. Both verses together establish the register: a.hav is the person one gives oneself to in love, and its proper form is one-to-one covenant fidelity.

**H5691 a.ga.vah (lust) — 1 verse.** Eze 23:11 — "her sister Oholibah saw this, and she became more corrupt than her sister in her lust and in her whoring, which was worse than her sister's." The a.ga.vah vocabulary (also H5689 a.gav, 7 verses — Eze 23:5,7,9,12,16,20; Jer 4:30) describes the disordered desire that pursues the forbidden at the cost of the covenant. The lust vocabulary is the a.ha.vah vocabulary inverted: the same drive-toward, the same consuming desire, but oriented toward what violates rather than what fulfils the covenant.

**H7453 re.a (neighbor) — 173 verses.** Reading all 173 confirms: re.a is the most extensive term in the love registry by verse count but its primary register is not affective love but proximate obligation. The neighbor is the person who is nearest — the one with whom you conduct business, disputes, marriage, and daily life. Lev 19:18 — "you shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself" — the great neighbour-love commandment uses re.a. The neighbor-love command presupposes: the neighbour is not the one you choose to love but the one who is already beside you.

Reading the 173 re.a verses establishes the full range: the neighbor as commercial partner (Exo 22:7-14), as opponent in dispute (Pro 25:8-9), as object of false witness (Exo 20:16), as person whose wife must not be coveted (Exo 20:17), as the friend who shares counsel (Pro 27:9-10), as the person who receives bread when you are at peace (Jer 31:34 — "no longer shall each one teach his neighbor and each his brother, saying 'Know the Lord'"). The new covenant's radical promise is that the neighbour-relationship will no longer require teaching because the knowledge of God will be interior to each person.

The neighbour-love in the NT: Mat 22:39 — "love your neighbour as yourself" (the second great commandment). Luk 10:29-37 — the Good Samaritan redefines the neighbor: not the person who shares your ethnic or religious identity but the person who shows mercy. The neighbor is defined by the act of love, not by proximity or similarity. This is the NT's most radical extension of the re.a vocabulary.

**H7358 re.chem (womb) — 25 verses.** Reading all 25 establishes the etymological root of the compassion vocabulary. The womb is the organ that opens (Gen 20:18, 29:31, 30:22) — closed and opened by God according to his sovereign love. The womb-love is the love that is most viscerally prior: the love the mother has for the child she carries before she has seen it. Gen 49:25 — "blessings of the womb" (re.chem) alongside blessings of heaven. This register of love is the most intimate somatic love in the corpus — love as the body's most fundamental generative act.

**G0018 agathos (good) — 90 verses.** Reading all 90 confirms: agathos in the love registry functions as the vocabulary of love enacted — the good person whose goodness is the expression of love in action. Mat 19:17 — "why do you call me good? There is only one who is good" — God as the sole source of goodness. Mat 7:11 — "how much more will your Father who is in heaven give good things to those who ask him" — the Father's goodness as generative benevolence. Gal 5:22 — "the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace... goodness (agathōsunē)" — goodness as love's pneumatic expression. Eph 2:10 — "created in Christ Jesus for good works" — love expressed as purposive good action in the world.

The most important verse in the agathos set for the love analysis: Mat 5:45 — "he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the just and on the unjust." The divine goodness is indiscriminate — it does not distinguish between the deserving and undeserving. This is the most concentrated expression of pre-merit goodness in the Gospels and establishes the love-as-goodness vocabulary in the same register as che.sed: both describe love that precedes and exceeds the beloved's merit.

**G1515 eirēnē (peace) — 84 verses.** Reading all 84 confirms: peace appears in the love registry because love produces peace and peace expresses love. The relationship is causal and structural, not merely contextual. Psa 85:10 — "steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other" — che.sed and peace in structural parallel. Rom 5:1 — "since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ" — peace as love's covenantal consequence. Eph 2:14-17 — "he himself is our peace... he came and preached peace" — Christ as the embodiment of peace-as-reconciling-love.

The most analytically significant pattern from reading all 84 eirēnē verses: peace in Paul's letters is almost always named in the greeting formula — "grace and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ" (Rom 1:7, 1Co 1:3, 2Co 1:2, Gal 1:3, Eph 1:2, Phi 1:2, Col 1:2, 1Th 1:1, etc.). The pairing of grace (charis) and peace (eirēnē) is the NT's compressed theology of love: grace is love given freely; peace is love's relational consequence. Every Pauline letter begins with this declaration: the community exists within the field of divine love expressed as grace and peace.

**G5384 filos (friend) — 27 verses.** Reading all 27 confirms: filos in the love registry provides the friendship register that agapaō alone cannot cover. The most analytically significant cluster: Joh 15:13-15 — "greater love (agapē) has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends (filoi)... No longer do I call you servants... I have called you friends." The agapē vocabulary and the filos vocabulary meet at the point of greatest love: Jesus calls his disciples friends (filoi) and then defines the greatness of this love as the willingness to die for them. Friendship here is not a lesser form of love than agapē — it is agapē's relational expression at its most personal.

Jam 4:4 — "friendship (filia) with the world is enmity with God" — the friendship vocabulary as a measure of ultimate orientation: where your friendship lies determines whether you are aligned with God or opposed to him. Abraham called God's friend (Isa 41:8, Jam 2:23) — the covenant partner who stood in friendship with God — is the OT model that the NT filos vocabulary invokes.

**G0079 adelfē (sister) — 25 verses.** Reading all 25 confirms: the sister vocabulary appears in the love registry primarily through the Song of Songs address ("my sister, my bride" — the most intimate combined address in the corpus, Song 4:9,10,12; 5:1,2) and through the NT community's use of "sister" as a term of community love (1Ti 5:2 — "treat... younger women as sisters, in all purity"). The adelfē vocabulary grounds the love vocabulary in the familial-kinship register: love modelled on the sister relationship is intimate, protected, unchosen (you cannot choose your sister), and prior to any earned quality.

**G4416 prōtotokos (firstborn) — 9 verses.** Reading all 9: the firstborn vocabulary in the love registry through the concept of divine election and love. Col 1:15,18 — "the firstborn of all creation... the firstborn from the dead" — Christ as the beloved firstborn in whom the Father's love is concentrated. Rom 8:29 — "conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers" — the firstborn as the model to which the community is being conformed. The firstborn vocabulary establishes that love in God's economy operates through election and inheritance — what belongs to the firstborn is given in love, not earned.

### 2.3 The Greek Vocabulary — Agapaō, Agapē, and the Extended Registers

*(See WA-103 partial analysis for the detailed agapaō/agapē analysis — 130 and 104 verses respectively. All conclusions from that analysis confirmed by full verse reading.)*

The full reading adds precision to three areas:

**The fileō/agapaō distinction in Joh 21:15-17.** Reading all 21 fileō verses and all 130 agapaō verses together: the PH2 flag from the partial analysis stands. The alternation between agapaō and fileō in Jesus's three questions to Peter is present in the text. Reading the wider Johannine use of both terms: John uses them with enough interchangeability (Joh 5:20 uses fileō for the Father's love for the Son; Joh 3:35 uses agapaō for the same) that they cannot be treated as technically distinct in all contexts. But in Joh 21 the switch from agapaō to fileō in Jesus's third question (when Peter's persistent "I fileō you" seems to be accepted) carries emotional weight even if it is not a doctrinal distinction. The question remains open and is flagged for dedicated exegetical study.

**The disordered love taxonomy — 2Ti 3:2-4 in full context.** Reading all single-verse fil- compounds in their context confirms: the 2Ti 3:2-4 list ("people will be lovers of self, lovers of money... lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God") is the most comprehensive description of disordered love in the corpus. G5367 filautos (1v), G5366 filarguros (2v), G5369 filēdonos (1v), G5377 filotheos (1v) — all in this one passage. The structure: the character of the last days is defined by what people love. Each pair contrasts a right and a wrong object: God-loving vs pleasure-loving; the disordered character is not loveless but misdirected. This confirms and extends the Session D observation from the partial analysis: love is character-constitutive; what a person loves defines what kind of person they are.

**The goodness-love cluster as love in action.** G0015 agathopoieō (8v), G0014 agathoergeō (2v), G0016 agathopoiia (1v), G0017 agathopoios (1v), G0019 agathōsunē (4v) — all in the doing-good register. Reading all 16 verses together: the doing-good vocabulary is always relational and always directed toward another person's need. Luk 6:27-35 — love your enemies, do good to those who hate you. 1Pe 2:15 — "by doing good you should put to silence the ignorance of foolish people." Gal 5:22 — goodness as a fruit of the Spirit. The goodness vocabulary establishes: love is not complete in its interior quality alone; it must express itself in action. The neighbour (re.a) receives the doing-good (agathopoieō); the inward love of agapaō becomes the outward act of agathopoieō.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

*(See partial analysis §3.1-3.2. Confirmed by full verse reading. Additional precision:)*

**The re.a vocabulary** adds a specifically civic-community dimension to the love registry. The neighbor-love is love extended to those who are proximate without being personally chosen — the obligatory extension of the love-principle beyond the inner circle of family and friends. This is distinct from the erotic love (dod/a.ha.vah), the womb-love (ra.cham), the covenant-love (che.sed), and the community-love (filadelfia). It is the love that applies in the public domain.

**The goodness vocabulary** (agathos, agathōsunē, tuv) extends love into the *being/character* dimension more fully than any other vocabulary in the registry. Goodness is love as character — the person who is good enacts love automatically, not by effort, because love has become their constitutive quality. This is the highest form of the love vocabulary: not love commanded (Deu 6:5), not love practiced (agathopoieō), but love constituted (the good person of Mat 12:35 who "out of the good treasure of his heart produces good").

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

*(See partial analysis §4.1-4.4, confirmed and extended.)*

**H2617A che.sed as the divine name-content.** The Exo 34:6 formula and its 13 canonical echoes establish che.sed as the most frequently repeated divine self-description in the OT. More than any other single attribute, the biblical writers describe God by his steadfast love. Reading all 241 che.sed verses makes this unmistakeable: the divine che.sed is the ground of creation (Psa 33:5 — "the earth is full of the steadfast love of the Lord"), the motivation of the Exodus (Exo 15:13), the content of covenant (Deu 7:9), the horizon of eschatological hope (Isa 54:10 — "my steadfast love shall not depart from you"), and the daily provision of the Psalter's prayers (Psa 90:14 — "satisfy us in the morning with your steadfast love"). Nothing in the love registry surpasses the density of theological weight in che.sed.

**H7355 ra.cham and the nursing-mother image.** Isa 49:15 — "can a woman forget her nursing child, that she should have no compassion on the son of her womb? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you." Reading this against all 43 ra.cham verses: the nursing-mother image is the OT's most radical statement of divine love. The bond between nursing mother and infant is the most intense natural human love — involving complete bodily vulnerability, continuous physical presence, and survival-level dependency. God's love is presented as exceeding even this. And the claim is theologically structured: "even these *may* forget" — the possibility of even the greatest natural love failing is acknowledged — "yet I will not forget you." The divine love is not merely as intense as the mother's; it is the exception to every form of love that can fail.

**Jer 31:3 and everlasting love.** "I have loved you with an everlasting love (a.ha.vah); therefore I have continued my faithfulness (che.sed) to you." The two primary Hebrew love words appear in sequence: the everlasting a.ha.vah is the ground; the che.sed is its consequence. Love that has no beginning (everlasting — olam) cannot be earned or provoked; it precedes the beloved entirely. And the steadfast faithfulness flows from it as its natural consequence. This pairing — everlasting love → consequent faithfulness — is the OT's most precise description of the relationship between divine love's nature and its expression.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

*(See partial analysis §5.1-5.2, confirmed and extended.)*

**Re.chem and the womb as love's somatic root.** Reading all 25 re.chem verses and all 43 ra.cham verses together confirms: the womb-root of the compassion vocabulary is not a metaphor in the modern sense but an etymological fact that the biblical writers consciously exploit. The ra.cham that describes God's compassion (Psa 103:13, Isa 49:15, Jer 31:20) draws its register from the body's most generative and vulnerable organ. Divine compassion originates in an act of *embodied imagination*: taking the most intense somatic love-bond in human experience and attributing something surpassing it to God.

Gen 43:30 — "Joseph hurried out, for his compassion grew warm for his brother, and he sought a place to weep." The me.a.yim (bowels/inner parts) were stirred — the ra.cha.mim is felt in the body before it is expressed in action. This is the clearest somatic love-description in the narrative literature: love that is felt viscerally, that produces a physical response (warmth), that drives to action (finding a private place to weep).

**The kiss vocabulary (G5370 filēma, G2705 katafileō) — 7 and 6 verses.** Reading all 13 kiss verses: the kiss in the NT operates in three distinct registers — betrayal (Judas kissing Jesus, Mat 26:49; Mar 14:44-45), welcome and reconciliation (the prodigal son's return, Luk 15:20; the Ephesian farewell, Act 20:37), and community greeting ("holy kiss," Rom 16:16; 1Co 16:20; 2Co 13:12; 1Th 5:26; 1Pe 5:14). The most analytically significant: Luk 7:38,45 — the woman who wept at Jesus's feet and "has not ceased to kiss my feet." The extravagant physical expression of love as worship — the same gesture (kissing the feet) used across cultures for both honour and love. The holy kiss as community greeting establishes the kiss as the somatic form of brotherly love — the physical enactment of agapē in community.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

*(See partial analysis §6.1-6.3 for core content. Extended findings below.)*

**The neighbour-love chain.** The re.a vocabulary establishes a specific love-chain: Lev 19:18 (love your neighbor as yourself) → Mat 22:39 (the second great commandment) → Luk 10:25-37 (the Good Samaritan parable redefining the neighbor) → Jam 2:8 (the "royal law" to love the neighbour as yourself). Reading the 173 re.a verses confirms: the neighbour-love is not an optional extension of love but constitutive of covenant membership. The command in Lev 19:18 is paired with "I am the Lord" — the covenant formula. To love the neighbor is a covenant obligation, not an aspiration.

**The goodness-as-love output chain.** G0025 agapaō (commanded love) → G5368 fileō (warm affection) → G5360 filadelfia (brotherly community) → G0019 agathōsunē (goodness as character) → G0015 agathopoieō (doing good as action). The love vocabulary traces a chain from interior disposition through community expression to active social good. No stage of this chain is sufficient without the others: the commanded love without warm affection is cold duty; the warm affection without doing good is sentiment; the doing good without love's ground is mere activity.

**The love-hatred structural polarity confirmed and extended.** Reading all 140 sa.ne and all 16 sin.ah verses: the hate vocabulary in the love registry functions as the structural opposite of love-as-covenant-orientation, not love-as-feeling. The most important hate-love pairing in the full verse set: Psa 97:10 — "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" The commanded love of God produces the commanded hatred of evil — these are not contradictory orientations but structurally necessary correlates. The person who loves what God loves must hate what God hates. The love-hate polarity is a single moral orientation viewed from its two sides.

**Cross-registry links confirmed and updated:**

**Love ↔ Fear (reg 61):** 1Jo 4:18 — "perfect love casts out fear" — confirmed as HIGHEST priority cross-registry relationship. Job 6:14 — "he who withholds kindness (che.sed) from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty" — che.sed and fear of God are structurally paired in the OT. Psa 33:18 — "the eye of the Lord is on those who fear him, on those who hope in his steadfast love."

**Love ↔ Desire (reg 43):** H0157G a.hev (xref_desire, 184 verses) confirmed. H2530A cha.mad (to desire/covet, 20 verses) — the desire vocabulary and love vocabulary share the same root orientation; what differentiates them is the object. Pro 6:25 — "do not desire (cha.mad) her beauty in your heart" — the forbidden desire is the love-drive wrongly directed.

**Love ↔ Peace (reg 117):** G1515 eirēnē and its compounds (84 verses) — peace is love's relational consequence. Psa 85:10 — "steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other." Peace as love's social and eschatological fruit. CRL: SEMANTIC_OVERLAP with che.sed, SHARED_TERM with eirēnikos.

**Love ↔ Hope (reg 78):** 1Co 13:13 — "so now faith, hope, and love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love." Psa 33:18 — "on those who hope in his steadfast love." Che.sed and hope are structurally linked: the person whose che.sed has evaporated loses their hope (Jon 2:8 — "those who pay regard to vain idols forsake their hope of steadfast love").

**Love ↔ Grief (reg 71):** H7356B ra.cha.mim (69 verses) — compassion as the love-response to grief. Gen 43:30 — Joseph's compassion growing warm at the sight of Benjamin after grief. Ra.cham is love activated by the sight of suffering — it is the grief-response transmuted into action.

**Love ↔ Wisdom (reg 174):** H2898 tuv (goodness, 31 verses) — Psa 27:13 — "I believe that I shall look upon the goodness of the Lord." Pro 3:17 — "her ways are ways of pleasantness (no.am), and all her paths are peace (sha.lom)" — wisdom expressed as pleasantness and peace, both love-vocabulary terms.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — 1Jo 4:8
*(See partial analysis §7.1 — confirmed in full by reading all 2,067 verses.)*

The anchor remains the correct choice. Reading all 241 che.sed verses, all 130 agapaō verses, and all 43 ra.cham verses confirms: 1Jo 4:8 ("God is love") is the only verse in the corpus that names love as God's ontological identity — not a divine activity but a divine predicate. Every other love-statement in the corpus is a subset of this claim.

### 7.2 Song 8:6-7 — Love as Strong as Death

*"Set me as a seal upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm, for love is strong as death, jealousy is fierce as the grave. Its flashes are flashes of fire, the very flame of the Lord. Many waters cannot quench love, neither can floods drown it. If a man offered for love all the wealth of his house, he would be utterly despised."*

Having read all 45 a.ha.vah verses, this is the most concentrated love-definition in the OT and the most analytically important addition to the analysis that the new verse data provides. Five claims:

Love is as strong as death — the most powerful force in creation cannot outstrip love. Death is not love's opponent in power; they are equals.

Jealousy (qin.ah) is as fierce as Sheol — the passion-love that guards what it loves has the tenacity of the underworld. This is the jealousy that is love's protective guardian, not its corruption.

Its flashes are fire — love has a somatic-thermal character (the same fire vocabulary as the anger and heart vocabularies). Love is felt as heat, as light, as consuming energy.

"The very flame of the Lord" (shal.he.vet.yah — a divine flame, literally: "flame of Yah") — the only verse in Song of Songs that names God. The love between the lovers has a divine dimension: its flame is the Lord's flame. This verse anchors the Song of Songs in the love-theology of the entire corpus.

Many waters cannot quench it — love's indestructibility is defined negatively: there is no force that can extinguish it. This is the a.ha.vah vocabulary's most radical claim and the OT's closest approach to 1Jo 4:8's theological density.

### 7.3 Lev 19:18 — Love Your Neighbour

*"You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbour (re.a) as yourself: I am the Lord."*

Having read all 173 re.a verses, this verse emerges as the axis of the neighbour-love vocabulary. Three elements:

The negative framing — no vengeance, no grudge — establishes that neighbour-love is not primarily warm feeling but the *refusal of the negative*. Before love is asked, hatred and its subtler forms (grudge-bearing, na.tar — the same vocabulary as the anger registry) must be renounced.

Love your neighbour as yourself — the standard is the self's own care for itself. Not sentimental excess but practical equivalence: the care and regard you naturally extend to your own interests, extend to your neighbour.

"I am the Lord" — the covenantal sanction. The neighbour-love command is not a social contract but a covenant obligation: it exists because of who God is and because the Israelites are covenant members.

Reading this against Mat 22:39 (the second great commandment), Luk 10:25-37 (the Good Samaritan), and Rom 13:9 ("love your neighbour as yourself" as the summary of the law's second table) — Lev 19:18 is the pivot on which the entire neighbour-love tradition turns.

### 7.4 Jer 31:3 — Everlasting Love and Consequent Faithfulness

*"The Lord appeared to him from far away. I have loved you with an everlasting love; therefore I have continued my faithfulness to you."*

Having read all 45 a.ha.vah and all 241 che.sed verses, this verse stands as the clearest structural statement about the relationship between the two primary Hebrew love words. The a.ha.vah (everlasting) is the ground; the che.sed is the consequence ("therefore"). The love that has no origin in the beloved's merit (everlasting) produces the faithfulness that stays when the beloved fails.

Reading this against Hos 11:4 ("I led them with cords of kindness, with the bands of love") and Zep 3:17 ("he will quiet you by his love") — God's a.ha.vah in the prophets is consistently presented as gentle, persistent, and prior to anything the beloved has done.

### 7.7 Joh 15:12-15 — Commanded Love and Friendship

*"This is my commandment, that you love one another as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends. You are my friends if you do what I command you. No longer do I call you servants... I have called you friends."*

Having read all 27 G5384 filos verses alongside the full agapaō set, this passage is the most concentrated point where the commanded love (agapaō) and the friendship love (filos) meet. The structure is remarkable: Jesus commands the disciples to love one another (agapaō) → defines the greatest love as death for friends (filos) → declares the disciples to be his friends (filoi) on the condition of their obedience. The progression: agapē-love is commanded; its highest expression is willingness to die; that willingness is what friendship (filia) means at its deepest level. The command to love and the gift of friendship are the same thing expressed from two sides.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

*(See partial analysis §8.1-8.3. Confirmed and extended by the neighbor and goodness vocabulary additions.)*

**Additional finding from the neighbour vocabulary:** the psychology of prosocial behaviour (Batson, *The Altruism Question*, 1991) distinguishes between empathy-driven altruism (helping because you feel for another's distress) and norm-driven prosocial behaviour (helping because you feel obligated). The re.a vocabulary straddles both: Lev 19:18 is norm-driven (covenant obligation) but the sa.ne/grudge-bearing framing implies that the neighbour-love must go deeper than mere compliance. The NT's extension (the Good Samaritan) makes the empathy/norm distinction irrelevant: the man in the ditch is helped because he is there and he is in need.

**Additional finding from the goodness vocabulary:** positive psychology's account of "character strengths" (Seligman and Peterson, *Character Strengths and Virtues*, 2004) identifies love as one of the 24 core character strengths and kindness as related. The agathos vocabulary's framing of goodness as love-in-character maps onto the character-strength account more precisely than the commanded-love vocabulary: the person who is agathos (good) does not calculate whether to help — they help because their character makes it natural.

## Section 9: Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circles:** Soul and Relational Environment — co-primary (confirmed by full verse reading).

**Two-forms structure (updated):**

*Rightly-ordered love:* che.sed (covenant-faithful steadfast love) + agapaō (commanded, self-giving) + ra.cham (compassion-love arising from visceral response to need) + re.a love (neighbour-love as covenant obligation) + goodness (love as character). The integration of these forms produces the person described in 1Co 13:4-7.

*Disordered love:* a.ga.vah/a.gav (lust — the love-drive oriented toward the forbidden), filia with the world (Jam 4:4 — friendship with the world as enmity with God), filarguros/filautos (love of money/self — the fil- taxonomy of 2Ti 3:2-4). Disordered love is not the absence of love but love misdirected — the same drive-toward that constitutes healthy love, oriented toward objects that destroy rather than build.

**The transformation dynamic (updated and clarified):** Love is not generated within the person through effort but received from God first and then transmitted: 1Jo 4:10 — "not that we have loved God but that he loved us"; 1Jo 4:19 — "we love because he first loved us." The person who has received divine love (a.ha.vah-everlasting + che.sed-faithful) becomes capable of che.sed toward the neighbour (re.a) and agapē in the community (filadelfia, 1Pe 1:22). The love-chain flows from God through the person to the community and outward.

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**Programme-level structural finding confirmed — Love as the integrating characteristic.** After reading all 2,067 love verses (and having read the fear, soul, anger, and heart registries), the hypothesis from the partial analysis is strengthened: no other characteristic in the programme carries the theological weight, the structural range, and the relational comprehensiveness of love. 1Jo 4:8 ("God is love") is the only ontological predication in the entire corpus applied to an inner-being characteristic. The programme may need to treat love not as one of 181 words but as the ground within which all others are understood.

**New Session D observation — The six love-registers and their social depth.** The expanded vocabulary reveals a gradient from most intimate (a.ga.vah/dod — erotic), through family (ra.cham/re.chem — womb-love), covenant (che.sed — faithfulness to covenant partner), personal friendship (fileō/filos), community (filadelfia/adelfē/re.a), to universal goodness (agapaō extended to enemies; agathos as character). Session D hypothesis: each love register corresponds to a different level of human social relatedness, and the full biblical account of love describes love functioning rightly across all levels simultaneously.

**New Session D observation — Che.sed as the programme's master love-concept.** The OT's 261 che.sed occurrences (241 in this export) make che.sed the single most frequently occurring love term in the corpus. Its irreducibility to any English translation, its grounding in covenant rather than feeling, and its role as the canonical divine name-attribute make it the OT's primary love word. The NT's agapē is its functional NT equivalent — not because they are the same word but because they perform the same theological function: the love that keeps when the beloved fails.

**New Session D observation — Goodness as love's character-form.** The agathos vocabulary (90 verses) establishes that love fully realised becomes character — the good person does not calculate whether to love but loves naturally because love has become their constitutive quality. This is the eschatological vision of love in 1Co 13 ("love never ends") — the love that endures is the love that has become character. Session D should examine whether goodness (tov/agathos) belongs at the intersection of love and holiness as the character-form of love perfected.
