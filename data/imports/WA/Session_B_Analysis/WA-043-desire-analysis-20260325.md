## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Semantic Architecture of Desire

Reading all 1,717 verses establishes desire as the most structurally complex characteristic in the programme to date. The vocabulary spans five distinct semantic fields:

**Field 1 — Seeking/reaching (ba.qash, zēteō, sha.al, be.a, aiteō, erōtaō):** The active movement of the will toward something not yet possessed. Ba.qash (207 verses) is the programme's largest single seeking-term. Reading all 207: ba.qash covers seeking God, seeking life, seeking wisdom, seeking to kill — the same verb covers the full range of what the human person can reach toward. The most concentrated ba.qash cluster: the Psalter's "seek my face" vocabulary (Psa 24:6; 27:4,8; 40:16; 105:3-4) — ba.qash directed at God's face as the supreme expression of Godward desire. The structural insight: ba.qash reveals the person's desire by revealing what they seek. Saul seeks to kill David (1Sa 19:2,10; 20:1; 22:23; 23:14-15,25; 24:2); God seeks to give rest to the lost (Eze 34:16); the church seeks God (Jer 29:13; Amo 5:4-6). The object of the seeking defines the quality of the desire.

Matt 6:33 is the most concentrated thelō/zēteō desire command in the NT: "seek first the kingdom of God." Luk 19:10 — "the Son of Man came to seek (zēteō) and save the lost" — the divine seeking as the mirror of human seeking.

**Field 2 — Choosing (ba.char, thelō, boulomai, tse.va):** Desire as the settled inclination that issues in selection. Ba.char (159 verses) is the primary OT choosing vocabulary. Reading all 159 ba.char verses establishes the most important structural pattern in the registry: the God-who-chooses. God chose Israel (Deu 7:6-7; 14:2); chose Abraham (Neh 9:7); chose David (1Sa 13:14; 2Sa 6:21; Psa 78:70); chose Jerusalem (1Ki 8:16; Psa 132:13); chose the Levites (Deu 21:5); chose Solomon (1Ch 28:4-6,10). The ba.char vocabulary is the OT's primary election vocabulary — but it is placed in the desire registry because choosing is the settled form of desire. God does not merely select Israel; he desires Israel and the selection is the expression of that desire.

Deu 7:7 — "It was not because you were more in number that the Lord set his love on you and chose (ba.char) you." Love (cha.shaq) and choosing (ba.char) are paired — the choosing flows from the loving desire. This pairing is confirmed by Deu 10:15 — "the Lord set his heart in love on your fathers and chose their offspring."

G2309 thelō (120 verses) — the NT will/desire verb. Reading all 120 thelō verses: the most analytically significant cluster is Rom 7:15-21 — the will that desires the good but enacts the evil. "I do not do what I want (thelō), but I do the very thing I hate... I have the desire (thelō) to do what is right, but not the ability to carry it out." This is the desire vocabulary's most precise description of the split will — the desire for good that cannot execute, and the execution of what is not desired. Thelō in Rom 7 is the desire vocabulary's most acute diagnosis of the human condition.

**Field 3 — Pleasure/Acceptance (ra.tson, che.phets, eudokia, cha.phets adj):** Desire as the settled orientation of approval — what the person or God finds acceptable, pleasing, delightful. Ra.tson (56 verses) is among the most theologically concentrated desire terms in the registry. Reading all 56: ra.tson covers divine pleasure in the acceptable sacrifice (Lev 1:3; 19:5; 22:19-20), God's favour on the person (Psa 5:12; 30:5,7; 89:17), the year of the Lord's favour (Isa 61:2), and the human disposition to do God's will (Psa 40:8 — "I delight to do your will, O my God; your law is within my heart"). The most structurally significant ra.tson verse: Psa 145:16,19 — "You open your hand; you satisfy the desire (ta.a.vah) of every living thing... He fulfills the desire (ra.tson) of those who fear him." Ra.tson as the desire that is also favour, will, and pleasure — the convergence of what is desired, what is approved, and what is granted.

G2107 eudokia (9 verses): Luk 2:14 — "peace among those with whom he is pleased (eudokias)." Phi 2:13 — "God who works in you both to will (thelein) and to work for his good pleasure (eudokia)." The divine eudokia is both the pleasure that motivates and the pleasure that accomplishes — God's desire is also his power.

**Field 4 — Appetite/Craving (epithumia, a.vah, ta.a.vah, hav.vah, av.vah, sha.aph):** Desire as the urgent, bodily pull toward satisfaction. This is the desire vocabulary's most ambiguous field — the same appetite that can long for God can crave forbidden things.

Reading all 37 epithumia verses: the NT epithumia vocabulary covers both positive desire (Luk 22:15 — Jesus's earnest desire for the Passover; Phi 1:23 — Paul's desire to depart and be with Christ; 1Th 2:17 — earnest desire to see the Thessalonians) and negative craving (Rom 1:24 — lusts of the heart; Gal 5:16 — desires of the flesh; Jam 1:14-15 — "desire gives birth to sin"). The same word carries both registers — the appetite structure is neutral; its moral valence depends on its object and orientation.

Reading H0183 a.vah (26 verses): the a.vah vocabulary spans divine desire (Psa 132:13-14 — "the Lord has desired (a.vah) Zion for his dwelling place"), appropriate human desire (Deu 14:26 — "spend the money for whatever you desire"), and disordered craving (Num 11:4 — "the rabble had a strong craving"; Psa 106:14 — "wanton craving in the wilderness"). The craving in the wilderness is the paradigm of disordered desire — appetitive desire that becomes unmoored from its appropriate object and turns to craving in the absence of what is really needed.

The most acute OT analysis of disordered appetite: Pro 21:25-26 — "the desire (ta.a.vah) of the sluggard kills him, for his hands refuse to labor... All day long he craves and craves, but the righteous gives and does not hold back." The sluggard's desire is entirely self-directed and ultimately lethal — desire without action that will satisfy it. The contrast: "the righteous gives and does not hold back" — desire rightly ordered moves outward toward others.

**Field 5 — Covenantal desire: cha.mad, cha.shaq, te.shu.qah:** The most theologically concentrated desire vocabulary in the registry — desire as the language of the covenant relationship.

H2530A cha.mad (20 verses — restored): Exo 20:17 — "you shall not covet (cha.mad) your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife." This is desire vocabulary's most programmatically significant legal statement: the 10th commandment prohibits not the act of taking but the prior desire — cha.mad is an inner state, not an outward action. The prohibition reaches inside the person. The law's 10th commandment addresses the will and desire before they act.

Gen 3:6 — "when the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight (cha.mud) to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired (cha.mad) to make one wise, she took of its fruit." The triple register: appetite (good for food), beauty (delight to eyes), cognitive aspiration (desired to make wise). The fall is the desire vocabulary's most concentrated narrative: three forms of desire converging on one act. Isa 53:2 — "he had no form or majesty... no beauty that we should desire (cha.mad) him." The Servant's undesirability — he is not the object of the natural desire that is drawn to beauty and power.

H2836A cha.shaq (8 verses — extracted): The desire vocabulary's most covenantally loaded term. Deu 7:7 — "the Lord set his love (cha.shaq) on you and chose you." Deu 10:15 — "the Lord set his heart in love (cha.shaq) on your fathers." This is divine cha.shaq — the Lord's attachment-desire for Israel that precedes and grounds the election. Psa 91:14 — "because he holds fast (cha.shaq) to me in love, I will deliver him." Here cha.shaq is the human's attachment to God producing divine protection. Isa 38:17 — "in love (cha.shaq) you have delivered my life from the pit." The cha.shaq vocabulary names covenantal desire at its deepest — not a functional preference but the attachment of the will to a specific person.

H8669 te.shu.qah (3 verses): Gen 3:16 — "your desire (te.shu.qah) shall be for your husband, and he shall rule over you." Gen 4:7 — "sin is crouching at the door. Its desire (te.shu.qah) is for you, but you must rule over it." Song 7:10 — "I am my beloved's, and his desire (te.shu.qah) is for me." The te.shu.qah vocabulary is the most narratively dense in the registry: three uses, three different valences:
- Gen 3:16: the post-fall desire of the woman for the man (with the structural disruption of the relationship)
- Gen 4:7: sin's desire to master Cain (desire as predatory — the desire that seeks to rule)
- Song 7:10: the beloved's desire for the Shulammite (the restored mutual desire of the Song)

The te.shu.qah trajectory is the desire vocabulary's internal narrative arc: post-fall disruption (Gen 3:16), predatory desire (Gen 4:7), restored desire as the love-object's joy (Song 7:10).

### 2.2 The Asking Vocabulary

A distinctive feature of the desire registry is its extensive asking vocabulary (sha.al, aiteō, erōtaō, parakaleō, be.a) — desire expressed as petition directed at another.

Reading all sha.al (136v), aiteō (67v), and erōtaō (58v) verses together establishes: **prayer is the desire vocabulary's most direct theological application.** The asking vocabulary in its vertical register — the human asking from God — is theologically equivalent to prayer. Mat 7:7-11 ("ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find...") is the most concentrated prayer-desire convergence in the NT. The triple imperatives (ask, seek, knock) use three different desire verbs — aiteō, zēteō, and krouō — mapping the full spectrum of desire-as-petition.

Jam 4:2-3 is the asking vocabulary's most acute diagnostic: "You desire and do not have, so you murder. You covet and cannot obtain, so you fight and quarrel. You do not have, because you do not ask. You ask and do not receive, because you ask wrongly, to spend it on your passions (epithumiais)." The double failure: desire that does not ask (missing the relational solution), and asking that is disordered by epithumia (asking with wrong orientation). The desire vocabulary's most precise account of why desire fails: the person does not bring it to God, and when they do, they bring it for self-directed purposes.

Parakaleō (105 verses) is the desire vocabulary's most relational term — the plea/comfort vocabulary. Reading all 105: parakaleō covers urgent petition (Mat 8:31 — demons begging; Mar 5:23 — Jairus imploring), apostolic appeal (Rom 12:1 — "I appeal to you by the mercies of God"), and divine comfort (2Co 1:4 — "who comforts us in all our affliction"). The same verb spans the most urgent human begging and the divine consoling response. The CRL with grief (reg 71) is confirmed: parakaleō is both the plea of the grieving (Mat 5:4 — "they shall be comforted/paraklēthēsontai") and the comfort given to them.

### 2.3 Love as Desire — H0157G a.hev (184 verses, xref_desire)

Reading all 184 a.hev verses: the primary OT love verb is placed in the desire registry because love is the supreme form of desire — the desire for a specific other person that leads to committed attachment.

The most programmatically significant a.hev cluster: Deu 6:5 — "you shall love (a.hev) the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might." The Shema's demand: total desire orientation toward God. Deu 7:9 — "the faithful God who keeps covenant and steadfast love with those who love him." Deu 30:6 — "the Lord your God will circumcise your heart... so that you will love the Lord your God with all your heart." The covenant structure of love: God's prior love → the call to love God in return → the divine circumcision of the heart that makes genuine loving possible.

The Hosea material (a.hev: Hos 2:5,7,10,12,13; 3:1; 9:1,10; 10:11; 11:1; 12:7; 14:4) is the desire vocabulary's most concentrated narrative of disordered and restored love-desire. Hos 11:1 — "when Israel was a child, I loved (a.hev) him, and out of Egypt I called my son." The divine a.hev for Israel as the foundation of the covenant. Hos 3:1 — "Go again, love a woman who is loved by another man and is an adulteress, even as the Lord loves the people of Israel, though they turn to other gods." The Hosea narrative maps the desire vocabulary's two-forms structure onto the covenant relationship: Israel's disordered desire (for the Baals, for foreign lovers) versus God's persistent love-desire for Israel.

Pro 8:17 — "I love (a.hev) those who love me, and those who seek me diligently find me." Wisdom as the love-object that responds to the seeking desire — a.hev + ba.qash together. The desire for wisdom is mutual: Wisdom loves those who love her.

**Cross-registry note (xref_desire → love reg 103):** The desire registry holds the primary a.hev analysis; the love registry (WA-103) treats che.sed and agapē as its primary vocabulary. These registries share the same inner-being space and Session D must examine whether "love" and "desire" should be treated as a single category or distinct dimensions of the same inner movement.

### 2.4 Divine Desire

Reading all God-as-subject flagged terms in the desire registry establishes: **the God of the Bible desires, chooses, asks, seeks, and is pleased — and uses the full range of desire vocabulary to describe his inner orientation toward his people.**

**God desires Israel:** H2836A cha.shaq — Deu 7:7 ("the Lord set his love on you"). H0977 ba.char — throughout Deuteronomy (God chose Israel). H0183 a.vah — Psa 132:13-14 ("the Lord has desired Zion for his dwelling"). God's desire for Israel is the most concentrated divine-desire statement in the OT.

**God desires obedience over sacrifice:** H2656 che.phets — 1Sa 15:22 ("Has the Lord as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the Lord? Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice"). God's desire is for the interior orientation, not the exterior performance. This is the desire vocabulary's most important statement about genuine worship: what God desires is the surrendered will, not the multiplied offering.

**God desires justice:** H0157G a.hev — Psa 11:7 ("the Lord is righteous; he loves righteous deeds"). Psa 33:5 ("he loves righteousness and justice"). The desire vocabulary applied to God's moral preferences — God's desire is for the right ordering of the world.

**God desires all to be saved:** G2309 thelō — 1Ti 2:4 ("who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth"). The most comprehensive statement of divine desire in the NT: God's thelō encompasses the entire human race. Rom 9:22 — "What if God, desiring to show his wrath and to make known his power, has endured with much patience vessels of wrath?" Divine desire operating across both judgment and mercy.

**The thelēma of the Father:** G2307 thelēma (58 verses) is the desire vocabulary's most concentrated theological term in the NT. Reading all 58: thelēma covers the Lord's Prayer ("your will be done on earth as it is in heaven" — Mat 6:10), Gethsemane ("not my will but yours" — Luk 22:42), Jesus's self-description ("I have come down from heaven not to do my own will but the will of him who sent me" — Joh 6:38), the will that determines salvation (Joh 6:39-40), and Paul's apostleship ("by the will of God" — Rom 1:1 etc.). The divine thelēma is both the supreme object of obedience and the ground of every good gift.

### 2.5 The Two-Forms Structure — Gal 5:16-17

Reading all epithumia and epithumeō verses in context of the Spirit/flesh conflict: **Gal 5:16-17 is the desire vocabulary's most precise two-forms statement.** "Walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires (epithumian) of the flesh. For the desires (epithumia) of the flesh are against the Spirit, and the desires (epithumia) of the Spirit are against the flesh."

This passage is remarkable for two reasons: (1) it uses epithumia for BOTH forms — the flesh has epithumia and the Spirit has epithumia. Desire is not inherently sinful; it is the force that can be oriented in either direction. (2) the two desires are opposed — they are not morally neutral alternatives but are in active conflict with each other ("against" each other).

Reading all thelō verses in Romans 7 alongside Gal 5: the two-forms structure has two distinct expressions:
- **The internal conflict form (Rom 7):** The person desires the good (thelō) but enacts the evil — desire split against itself within the same person.
- **The external conflict form (Gal 5):** The Spirit's desire and the flesh's desire in opposition — two desire-orientations contending for the person's action.

Together these establish the desire vocabulary's two-forms structure: the rightly-ordered desire (toward God, toward the good, toward the other, toward the Spirit's things) and the disordered desire (toward self, toward the forbidden object, toward the flesh, toward what cannot satisfy). The diagnosis of Jam 4:2-3 completes this: disordered desire is desire that does not ask, or that asks for the wrong thing.

### 2.6 The Cha.mad Family — Coveting and Desirability

The restored cha.mad vocabulary (Exo 20:17 + four related terms) adds the most ethically specific desire vocabulary to the registry.

**Exo 20:17 — the 10th commandment:** "You shall not covet (cha.mad) your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife, or his male servant, or his female servant, or his ox, or his donkey, or anything that is your neighbor's." The prohibition is unique among the commandments: the other nine prohibit actions; the tenth prohibits a desire. Cha.mad is an inner movement before it is an outer act — the desire to possess what belongs to another. This makes the 10th commandment the legal formulation of what Paul expresses theologically in Rom 7:7 ("I would not have known what coveting was if the law had not said, 'Do not covet'").

**Gen 3:6 — desire at the fall:** "Good for food... delight to the eyes... to be desired to make one wise." The triple desire structure mirrors the 1Jo 2:16 triplet: "the desires of the flesh and the desires of the eyes and pride of life." The fall is the desire vocabulary's master narrative — what goes wrong with desire at the structural level.

**Song 5:16 — H4261 mach.mad:** "He is altogether desirable (mach.mad). This is my beloved." The Song's climactic desire-statement uses the same root as the coveting prohibition. The cha.mad family covers both the disordered desire that covets what belongs to another and the rightly-ordered desire that celebrates the beloved. The same root's moral valence is determined entirely by its object and the relational context.

**Isa 53:2 — the undesirable Servant:** "He had no form or majesty... no beauty that we should desire (cha.mad) him." The absence of cha.mad for the Servant is the most important desire-vocabulary observation in the Servant Songs. Natural desire is drawn to beauty and power; the Servant has neither. Those who pass over the Servant are not experiencing disordered desire — they are simply following where natural desire leads. The scandal of the cross is partly a desire-vocabulary scandal: the one who is most worth desiring appears in a form that natural desire does not seek.

### 2.7 The Smaller Terms — Peripheral Desire Vocabulary

Reading all smaller-term verses establishes:

**G1971 epipotheō (9v) — longing:** The strongest longing verb in the NT. Rom 1:11 — Paul longs to see the Romans. Phi 1:8 — "I yearn for you all with the affection of Christ Jesus." 1Pe 2:2 — "like newborn infants, long for the pure spiritual milk." Jam 4:5 — "he yearns jealously over the spirit that he has made to dwell in us." The divine longing (Jam 4:5) is the most concentrated single-verse divine desire statement in the NT after 1Ti 2:4.

**H7602A sha.aph (8v) — to pant:** Desire as physical panting. Job 7:2 — "like a slave who longs for the shadow." Psa 119:131 — "I open my mouth and pant, because I long for your commandments." Jer 2:24 — "in her heat sniffing the wind." The sha.aph vocabulary is the desire vocabulary at its most somatic — desire experienced as breathlessness, panting, the body expressing the urgency of what it wants.

**G3713 oregō (3v) — to aspire:** Heb 11:16 — "they desire a better country, that is, a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God." The aspiration for a heavenly country is the desire vocabulary's most eschatological statement — desire oriented not toward anything available in the present but toward the not-yet-possessed homeland. This is the desire vocabulary's most forward-looking form.

**H3700 ka.saph (5v) — to long:** Psa 84:2 — "my soul longs (ka.saph), yes, faints for the courts of the Lord; my heart and flesh sing for joy to the living God." The most intense Godward longing in the Psalter. Job 14:15 — "you would long for (ka.saph) the work of your hands" — God longing for the creature he made. This is the most intimate statement of divine desire in the Job corpus.

**H1942 hav.vah (16v) — desire/ruin:** The hav.vah vocabulary covers desire in its most destructive form — the desire that is also the plan for ruin. Psa 5:9 — "their inmost self is destruction (hav.vah)." Pro 11:6 — "the treacherous are taken captive by their lust (hav.vah)." The hav.vah vocabulary is desire at its most disordered — the internal bent toward destruction that masquerades as desire.

**G2691 katastrēniaō (1v) — to desire strongly against:** 1Ti 5:11 — "when their passions draw them away from Christ, they desire to marry." The compound verb (kata + strēniaō) names desire that turns against a prior commitment — desire as the force that redirects the person from their prior orientation.

**H8669 te.shu.qah (3v):** Already analysed in §2.1 above. The Gen 3:16 / Gen 4:7 / Song 7:10 trajectory from disordered desire to restored mutual longing.

**G4658 skuthēs (1v) — Scythian:** Col 3:11 — "Here there is not Greek and Jew... Scythian, slave, free." This term entered the registry erroneously — the Scythian is an ethnic designation in a Pauline equality statement, not desire vocabulary. Confirmed candidate_delete.

**H1697H da.var:speaking (41v):** This is the word-as-desire term — the desire vocabulary's most indirect entry. Reading all 41: these are primarily "as you say / as requested" speech-acts — verbal expression of what another person desires. The da.var sub-gloss captures the desire expressed in speech: "let it be as you said" (1Ki 17:15). The desire vocabulary's most speech-proximate term.

**H2550 cha.mal (40v) — to spare/have compassion:** Reading all 40: cha.mal is pity/compassion — desire not to harm. Exo 2:6 — Pharaoh's daughter took pity on the baby. 2Sa 12:6 — Nathan's parable: "he had no pity (cha.mal)." Mal 3:17 — "I will spare (cha.mal) them." The compassion vocabulary is the desire vocabulary's most relational-empathetic term — the desire that inclines away from inflicting harm.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — All Three Circles

Desire is the most dimensionally distributed characteristic in the programme. Reading 1,717 verses establishes:

**Volitional (primary):** Desire is definitionally about the will — the inner movement toward a goal not yet possessed. Rom 7:18 — "I have the desire (thelō) to do what is right." Gal 5:16 — "the desires (epithumia) of the flesh." The will/desire vocabulary (thelō, thelēma, boulomai, ba.char) is the most concentrated volitional vocabulary in the programme.

**Affective (co-primary):** Desire is also felt — the longing (epipotheō, ka.saph, sha.aph), the craving (ta.a.vah, a.vah), the panting (sha.aph). Psa 84:2 — "my soul longs, yes, faints" — the affective dimension of desire at maximum intensity.

**Cognitive:** Desire shapes what the person seeks and therefore what they find. Gen 3:6 — the tree "desired to make one wise" — the cognitive desire that led to the fall. Pro 15:14 — "the heart of him who has understanding seeks (ba.qash) knowledge." The desire that is intelligent pursuit is cognitive desire.

**Relational:** Asking vocabulary (sha.al, aiteō, erōtaō, parakaleō) is inherently relational — desire expressed as petition directed at another. The cha.shaq vocabulary is covenantal-attachment desire — desire as the bond between persons.

**Physical (somatic):** Sha.aph (panting), ta.a.vah (craving with physical intensity), ka.saph (fainting). The desire vocabulary has a somatic register — the body experiences desire.

### 3.2 The Soul's Desire

The ne.phesh vocabulary's appearances in the desire registry are analytically important. Deu 14:26 — "spend the money for whatever you desire (sha.al), whatever your appetite (ne.phesh) craves." Psa 10:3 — "the wicked boasts of the desires (ta.a.vah) of his soul (ne.phesh)." Pro 13:4 — "the soul (ne.phesh) of the sluggard craves." Song 1:7 — "tell me, you whom my soul (ne.phesh) loves." The soul (ne.phesh) is the seat of desire in the OT — the living, craving, loving creature. The desire vocabulary and the soul vocabulary converge in the same inner space.

WA-182 (soul) established ne.phesh as "appetite" (H5315L, 47 occ) — the appetite sub-gloss is the soul vocabulary's direct contribution to the desire registry. The soul's appetitive dimension is the ground of desire — the desire registry's deepest root is the soul's created capacity for wanting.

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 The God Who Desires

Reading all god-as-subject terms (ba.char, ba.qash, thelō, thelēma, ra.tson, cha.mal, a.vah, cha.shaq, che.phets, eudokia) establishes the most comprehensive divine inner-life statement in the programme to date.

God's desire vocabulary spans:
- Covenantal attachment (cha.shaq — Deu 7:7; Deu 10:15)
- Elective choice (ba.char — throughout Deuteronomy and Isaiah)
- Sought-after face (ba.qash — God "seeking" in Eze 22:30)
- Pleasure in obedience over sacrifice (che.phets — 1Sa 15:22; Isa 53:10)
- Acceptance/favour (ra.tson — Psa 30:5 "his favour is for a lifetime")
- Will that accomplishes (thelēma — Mat 6:10; Joh 6:38-40)
- Good pleasure (eudokia — Luk 2:14; Phi 2:13)
- Compassion as desire-not-to-harm (cha.mal — Mal 3:17)
- Longing for the creature (ka.saph — Job 14:15)
- Jealous yearning (epipotheō — Jam 4:5)
- Universal saving desire (thelō — 1Ti 2:4)

**The most programme-significant divine desire statement:** Deu 7:7-8 — "It was not because you were more in number that the Lord set his love on you (cha.shaq) and chose (ba.char) you... but it is because the Lord loves (a.hev) you and is keeping the oath that he swore to your fathers, that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand." Three desire verbs (cha.shaq, ba.char, a.hev) in two verses, all attributed to God's inner orientation toward Israel. The covenant is grounded in divine desire — God does not choose Israel because Israel is desirable but because God desires Israel.

### 4.2 Jesus's Desire — Luk 22:15

"I have earnestly desired (epithumia epethumesa) to eat this Passover with you before I suffer."

The double epithumia form — the Hebraism intensifying desire by repeating the verbal root in noun form — is unique in the NT Gospels. Jesus expresses desire at maximum intensity. Reading this against the full epithumia vocabulary: every other NT use of epithumia covers either disordered fleshly craving or the intense desire of the apostle (Phi 1:23). Luk 22:15 stands alone as the divine desire expressed by the Son of God himself — the same word used for the flesh's craving is used for Jesus's most intense desire before the cross.

The desire is for the Passover — for the covenant meal that anticipates his death. Jesus desires the very thing that will kill him. This is the desire vocabulary's most compressed statement of the will fully aligned with the Father's: the thelō and the thelēma of God coincide in this desire.

### 4.3 Gethsemane — The Two Wills

Luk 22:42 — "Father, if you are willing (boulei), remove this cup from me. Nevertheless, not my will (thelēma) but yours (thelēma) be done." Reading thelō and thelēma alongside boulomai: Jesus's prayer in Gethsemane is the desire vocabulary's most concentrated theological statement. It distinguishes:
- The Son's natural desire (thelēma — remove the cup)
- The Father's desire (thelēma — the cup must be drunk)
- The Son's yielded will (not my thelēma but yours)

The two-forms structure applies at the level of divine persons: the Son's desire and the Father's desire are both genuine; the Son's yielded will is not the annihilation of his desire but its subordination to the Father's. This is the most important desire-vocabulary statement in the programme for the doctrine of God.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 The Body of Desire

The desire vocabulary has a strong somatic register despite relatively few somatic-flagged terms. The body desires through:

**Panting and breathlessness:** Sha.aph — Psa 119:131 — "I open my mouth and pant." Jer 2:24 — "in her heat sniffing the wind." The body's respiratory response to unsatisfied desire.

**Fainting:** Ka.saph — Psa 84:2 — "my soul longs, yes, faints (kala) for the courts of the Lord." The body's weakening under the pressure of unmet desire.

**The eyes as desire's instrument:** Cha.mad — Gen 3:6 — "a delight to the eyes." Pro 6:25 — "do not desire her beauty in your heart... let her not capture you with her eyelashes." The eye as the organ through which desire is activated — the visual register of desire. Mat 5:28 — epithumeō — "everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart." The gaze as the threshold of desire.

**The belly as desire's site:** Hav.vah — Pro 11:6 — "the treacherous are taken captive by their lust (hav.vah) / desire." A.vah — Pro 21:26 — "all day long he craves and craves." The belly/appetite as the location of bodily desire.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Cross-Registry Relationships

**Desire ↔ Love (reg 103):** H0157G a.hev (xref_desire, 184v) establishes this as the programme's most important single cross-registry relationship. Love is the desire for a specific person; desire is the motivating force that makes love possible. The a.hev/epithumia boundary (CRL: SHARED_ROOT) requires Session D examination — they may be the same inner-being movement at different levels of intensity and specificity.

**Desire ↔ Anxiety (reg 7):** Epithumia/merimna (CRL: SHARED_ROOT). Both anxiety and desire are oriented toward what the person does not yet have — anxiety fears losing it; desire reaches toward having it. Mat 6:25-33 places them together: "do not be anxious... but seek first the kingdom of God."

**Desire ↔ Repentance (reg 135):** Epithumeō / thelō / ba.char (CRL: SEMANTIC_OVERLAP). Repentance involves a change in desire — the will (thelō) that previously desired sin now desires righteousness. Eze 18:31 — "make yourselves a new heart and a new spirit! Why will you die?" — the call to desire differently.

**Desire ↔ Joy (reg 97):** Ra.tson / eudokia (CRL: SHARED_VERSE). God's pleasure (ra.tson/eudokia) is both desire and joy — what God desires is also what gives him joy. Phi 2:13 — God works "both to will and to work for his eudokia" — the divine will, desire, and joy converge.

**Desire ↔ Guilt (reg 73):** Cha.mad (CRL: SHARED_VERSE via Exo 20:17). The 10th commandment places desire at the root of guilt — the covetous desire that precedes the act. Rom 7:7-8 — "I would not have known what coveting was if the law had not said, 'Do not covet'... sin produced in me all kinds of covetousness." The desire vocabulary is the guilt vocabulary's anthropological root.

**Desire ↔ Peace (reg 117):** Eudokia / ra.tson (CRL: SEMANTIC_OVERLAP). Luk 2:14 — "peace among those with whom he is pleased (eudokia)." The divine desire (eudokia) is the ground of the peace offered — desire and peace are connected through the divine will.

### 6.2 The Five Causative Pathways for Desire

**Person → desire → God (petition):** The asking vocabulary (sha.al, aiteō, erōtaō, parakaleō) — desire expressed as prayer. Mat 7:7 — "ask and it will be given to you." The prayer-desire pathway is the desire vocabulary's most explicit practical application.

**God → desire → person (calling and election):** Ba.char, cha.shaq, a.hev as divine desire preceding human response. Deu 7:7-8 — God's cha.shaq + ba.char → the covenant. The calling is generated by the divine desire.

**Object → desire → person (attraction):** Gen 3:6 — the tree was desirable, she took. Pro 6:25 — "do not desire her beauty." The desired object activating the desire-response. This is desire in its most passive form — the pull of the attractive object.

**Desire → action → consequence:** The most morally significant pathway. Jam 1:14-15 — "desire (epithumia) when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death." The desire-sin-death sequence is the desire vocabulary's most acute consequence narrative.

**Desire → petition → reception:** The prayer pathway. Psa 37:4 — "delight yourself in the Lord, and he will give you the desires of your heart." Joh 15:7 — "if you abide in me... ask whatever you wish, and it will be done for you." The rightly-oriented desire that is brought to God in prayer is the desire that receives.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Luk 22:15 — The Anchor Verse

*"And he said to them, 'I have earnestly desired (epithumia epethumesa) to eat this Passover with you before I suffer.'"*

The double form (noun + verb from the same root) intensifies to the maximum — "with desire I have desired." Three elements:

**The agent:** Jesus himself — the Son of God expressing desire. This makes Luk 22:15 the desire vocabulary's most theologically concentrated statement about what God in Christ desires.

**The object:** The Passover — the covenant meal that anticipates the blood of the new covenant. Jesus desires the very ritual that will interpret his death. The desire and its fulfillment in death are unified.

**The time marker:** "before I suffer" — the desire is set against the imminence of the cross. The most intense desire of Jesus's ministry is expressed in the shadow of the crucifixion. Desire and suffering are not opposed here but intimately connected.

**Cross-registry significance:** The same epithumia vocabulary used in Gal 5:16-17 for the flesh's disordered desire is used here for the Son of God's most intensely expressed desire. The same word carries the full range of the desire vocabulary — from the flesh's craving to the divine longing. The word is not tainted; the desire is not inherently sinful.

### 7.2 Gal 5:16-17 — The Two-Forms Anchor

*"Walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires (epithumian) of the flesh. For the desires (epithumia) of the flesh are against the Spirit, and the desires (epithumia) of the Spirit are against the flesh, for these are opposed to each other, to keep you from doing the things you want to do."*

Three elements:

**Desire in both forms:** The flesh has epithumia; the Spirit has epithumia. Desire is not the problem — the orientation of desire is. Both the flesh and the Spirit are desiring entities; the conflict is between two orientations of the same drive.

**The opposition:** "Against... opposed to each other." Not parallel desires that coexist — they are at war. The image is military: the desires of the flesh and the Spirit are in active engagement, each trying to prevent the other from achieving its goal.

**The practical result:** "To keep you from doing the things you want to do." The internal war paralyses the will — the desire-conflict of Gal 5:17 is the experiential version of the Rom 7 analysis. Desire that is not resolved by the Spirit's orientation cannot execute.

**Programme-level significance:** The two-forms structure of every inner-being characteristic finds its clearest expression in the desire vocabulary: there is a desire that is according to the Spirit, and a desire that is according to the flesh. The Spirit's desire is its expression in human form of what Paul calls the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22-23) — love, joy, peace, etc. are the Spirit's desires made manifest in the person's character.

### 7.3 Exo 20:17 — The Prohibition of Inner Desire

*"You shall not covet (cha.mad) your neighbor's house; you shall not covet your neighbor's wife, or his male servant, or his female servant, or his ox, or his donkey, or anything that is your neighbor's."*

The 10th commandment's location in the desire registry is programmatically essential. Three elements:

**The law reaches inside:** Every other commandment prohibits an action. The 10th prohibits an inner state — cha.mad is the desire before the act. This is the most interior legal statement in the Decalogue, and it establishes that God's law addresses the will, not merely the behaviour.

**Paul's use in Rom 7:** "I would not have known what coveting was if the law had not said, 'Do not covet' (lo tach.mod / ouk epithumēseis)." The 10th commandment becomes the mechanism by which Paul's desire for the forbidden was activated — "sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, produced in me all kinds of covetousness." The law that prohibits desire becomes the occasion for the desire it prohibits. This is the desire vocabulary's most acute paradox.

**The desired objects listed:** House, wife, servants, ox, donkey, "anything." The list is not random — it moves from largest to smallest, covering the full range of what a person might desire from a neighbour. The 10th commandment's comprehensive "anything that is your neighbour's" addresses the general structure of disordered desire: the problem is not the specific object but the pattern of wanting what belongs to another.

### 7.4 Gen 3:6 — The Triple Desire at the Fall

*"So when the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was a delight (ta.a.vah) to the eyes, and that the tree was to be desired (cha.mad) to make one wise, she took of its fruit."*

The fall narrative analysed as a desire event. Three desire registers:

**"Good for food"** — the appetite's desire. The body's need for food is legitimate; its object here is illegitimate. This is the Ta.a.vah / craving dimension.

**"Delight to the eyes"** — the aesthetic desire. The beauty of the object activates desire. This is cha.mud (the desirability of the object) — the same root as the 10th commandment. Pro 6:25 — "do not desire her beauty in your heart" — the same activation mechanism.

**"To be desired to make one wise"** — the cognitive desire. This is the desire for transcendence, for what belongs to God. Psa 19:10 — "the statutes of the Lord are... more to be desired (cha.mad) than gold." The same word used for the forbidden desire of the tree is used for the righteous desire for God's word. The structural insight: the desire for wisdom is not wrong — it is directed at the wrong source.

**Programme-level significance:** Gen 3:6 is the desire vocabulary's foundational disorder narrative. Desire aimed at the right objects (food for sustenance, beauty for delight, wisdom for understanding) becomes disordered when aimed at what God has prohibited. The three registers map onto 1Jo 2:16's triplet: "the desires of the flesh and the desires of the eyes and pride of life." The fall structure is the desire vocabulary's permanent diagnostic framework.

### 7.5 Psa 37:4 — Desire Fulfilled Through Delight

*"Delight yourself in the Lord, and he will give you the desires of your heart."*

The desire vocabulary's most concentrated promise. Two elements:

**"Delight yourself in the Lord"** — the prior orientation. The condition is not virtue or achievement but the reorientation of delight — the pleasure-desire directed toward God himself. H2656 che.phets: "delight, pleasure." The one who finds pleasure in God is the one whose heart's desires will be granted.

**"He will give you the desires (mish.a.lot) of your heart"** — the promise. H4862 mish.a.lah: the petition/desire — what the heart has been reaching toward. The desire vocabulary's most expansive promise is grounded in the prior transformation of desire: when desire is rightly oriented (toward God), God becomes the fulfiller of all other desires.

**Programme significance:** This verse resolves the desire vocabulary's central question — how can desire be both a problem (craving, coveting, lusting) and a gift? Answer: the orientation determines everything. The desire that is rightly oriented (toward God, toward the other, toward righteousness) is the desire that receives what it desires. The disordered desire devours itself (Pro 21:25-26); the rightly-ordered desire is satisfied (Psa 145:19 — "he fulfills the desire of those who fear him").

### 7.6 Jam 1:14-15 — The Desire-Sin-Death Sequence

*"But each person is tempted when he is lured and enticed by his own desire (epithumia). Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death."*

The desire vocabulary's most explicit consequence narrative. Three stages:

**"Lured and enticed by his own desire"** — the desire as the internal agent of temptation. Not an external force but the person's own epithumia. The desire is already present — the temptation is the activation of a pre-existing desire.

**"Desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin"** — the birth metaphor. Desire must be "conceived" — there is an act of engagement before sin is born. The desire alone is not yet sin; the engagement with the temptation produces the sin. This distinction is theologically precise: the desire-stimulus is not itself the sin; the consent to and action of the desire produces sin.

**"Sin when it is fully grown brings forth death"** — the completed sequence. The trajectory is developmental: temptation → conception → birth → growth → death. The desire vocabulary's most acute account of how inner disorder produces outer destruction.

**Cross-registry:** This passage connects directly to the guilt registry (reg 73) — the sin born from desire is the ground of guilt. The desire-sin-guilt sequence is the programme's most important causative chain for the negative register of inner-being characteristics.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Fields

**Motivational psychology** (primary) — desire as the root of motivation; the drives that direct behaviour
**Neuroscience of reward and craving** (secondary) — dopaminergic systems, the neurobiological basis of desire

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan, *Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior*, 1985; *Self-Determination Theory*, 2017).** SDT identifies three basic psychological needs (autonomy, competence, relatedness) that generate intrinsic motivation — desire as its own reward. The biblical desire vocabulary's most consistent structural insight is that rightly-oriented desire (toward God, toward others, toward righteousness) is self-sustaining and satisfying, while disordered desire (craving, coveting) is insatiable. This maps precisely onto SDT's distinction between intrinsic motivation (satisfying desires that are consonant with the self's core values) and extrinsic motivation (pursuing desires imposed from outside the self). Psa 37:4 — "delight yourself in the Lord, and he will give you the desires of your heart" — describes the condition of intrinsic motivation: when desire is aligned with the person's deepest orientation, it generates its own fulfilment.

**Finding 2 — The dual-process model of desire (Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011; Baumeister and Tierney, *Willpower*, 2011).** The research on automatic (System 1) and deliberate (System 2) processing maps onto the biblical distinction between appetite (the automatic desire that arises from the body/appetitive system) and will (the deliberate desire that can override appetite). Rom 7:18-19 — "I have the desire to do what is right, but not the ability" — describes the failure of deliberate desire to override appetitive impulse. The biblical two-forms structure (Spirit's desire vs flesh's desire) maps structurally onto the dual-process model's tension between automatic and deliberate processing.

**Finding 3 — Wanting vs liking (Kent Berridge, *Wanting Without Liking*, 2009; dopaminergic incentive salience).** Neuroscience research distinguishes "wanting" (incentive salience, dopaminergic drive) from "liking" (hedonic pleasure, opioid system). These are separable systems: addicts can "want" intensely what they no longer "like." This maps onto the biblical analysis of disordered desire: Pro 21:25-26 — the sluggard "craves and craves" without obtaining; Ecc 5:10 — "he who loves money will not be satisfied with money." The craving that cannot be satisfied is neurobiologically consistent with the dissociation of wanting from liking — the desire escalates even as the pleasure diminishes. The biblical diagnosis of coveting/craving as insatiable is consistent with the neuroscience of addictive wanting.

**Finding 4 — Terror Management Theory (Becker, *The Denial of Death*, 1973; Greenberg, Pyszczynski, and Solomon).** The desire for significance — the desire to matter, to transcend mortality — is identified by TMT as the hidden driver behind much human motivation. Gen 3:6 — "desired to make one wise" — the cognitive/transcendence dimension of desire at the fall maps onto TMT's insight that the deepest human desire is for significance and immortality. Phi 3:19's inversion — "their god is their belly" — describes the collapse of transcendence-desire into appetite-desire that TMT identifies as one pathological response to mortality awareness.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Structural convergence:** The biblical two-forms structure (Spirit desire vs flesh desire) maps onto the SDT distinction between autonomous and controlled motivation, and onto the dual-process distinction between System 1/2 processing. Both accounts locate the key variable in the orientation and source of the desire.

**Phenomenological convergence:** The somatic desire vocabulary (sha.aph — panting, ka.saph — fainting, ta.a.vah — craving) is consistent with the documented phenomenology of desire as a bodily state with respiratory, physiological, and visceral dimensions.

**Divergence — desire as relational:** The biblical vocabulary consistently frames desire in a relational context — desire for God, desire from God, desire for the other person, the Covenant as a desire-relationship. Secular motivational psychology frames desire primarily in terms of individual drives and goals, not in terms of a constitutive divine-human desire relationship. The divine desire for the person (cha.shaq — Deu 7:7; ka.saph — Job 14:15; epipotheō — Jam 4:5) has no parallel in motivational psychology.

**Genuine divergence — the transformability of desire:** The biblical vocabulary assumes that desire can be fundamentally reoriented — not merely regulated or redirected but transformed in its object. Deu 30:6 — "the Lord your God will circumcise your heart... so that you will love the Lord your God with all your heart." Phi 2:13 — "God who works in you both to will (thelein) and to work for his good pleasure." The divine circumcision of the heart that transforms desire at its root is not a psychological category. Psychology can discuss desire-management, desire-substitution, and desire-regulation; the biblical vocabulary claims that God can create a fundamentally new desire in the person — a desire for God that was not there before, produced by divine action on the inner person.
