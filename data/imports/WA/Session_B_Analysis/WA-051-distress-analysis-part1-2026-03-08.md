## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

The word *distress* (registry 051, Part 1) draws on nineteen terms across Hebrew and Old Testament Greek, with testament coverage across both Testaments. The Part 1 term set covers the root families TSR, TSUQ, RA, MAR, SUNECHO, ODUNE, and THAMB, together with five terms carrying no root family assignment. The source list is High Confidence.

On the basis of the Part 1 JSON data, *distress* provisionally qualifies as an inner-being term. The evidence is as follows. The TSR family's central term tsa.rah (H6869B) appears with direct inner-being reference at Gen 42:21 ("the distress of his soul") and Psa 25:17 ("the troubles of my heart are enlarged"), marking the state as located within the person rather than as a purely external event. The MAR family's term mar (H4751) is used consistently for bitterness of soul (Job 3:20, 7:11, 10:1), identifying an inward emotional texture. The Greek term ademoneo (G0085) describes a condition that is explicitly troubled and sorrowful in the inner person (Mat 26:37, Mar 14:33). The qualification, however, is partial at this stage: several terms in the Part 1 set function as much as descriptions of external threatening circumstances as of inner states — tsar (H6862B), the TSUQ family terms, and ra (H7451A) all operate frequently at the level of situation or event rather than inner experience. Whether the inner-being use is primary or secondary for these terms cannot be determined from Part 1 data alone. Confidence in the qualification is *moderate*, sufficient to proceed to further analysis, with the boundary between external distress-event and inner distress-state as the key issue requiring resolution across all three parts.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### TSR Family: ya.tsar (H3334), bats.tsa.rah (H6869A), tsa.rah (H6869B), tsar (H6862B)

The TSR root carries the image of constriction — a narrowing, a pressing in, a place where movement and breath are restricted. It generates both noun and verb forms across the Old Testament, all sharing this spatial core: the person caught in a tight place with no way out. Part 1 contains four TSR terms, and together they map the root across very different registers of use.

Ya.tsar (H3334, "be distressed," 65 occurrences, 10 verses in source) is the verb form, meaning to be cramped or confined. Its verse set shows it at work in acute personal crisis situations: Jacob facing Esau (Gen 32:7), David facing his own men's threat of stoning (1Sa 30:6), Amnon's frustrated obsession with Tamar (2Sa 13:2). What is notable is that the cramping is not always purely emotional. In Job 20:22 the term appears in the image of distress coming upon a man in his fullness, and in Pro 4:12 it describes the contrast between a free, unimpeded gait and a hampered one — the physical and the inner are held together in the root without being distinguished. Isa 49:19 uses the same root for literal spatial narrowness ("too narrow for your inhabitants"), which confirms that the transition between physical and inner-being senses is not a metaphorical extension in the usual English sense but is built into the term itself.

Bats.tsa.rah (H6869A, "dearth," 2 occurrences, 2 verses in source) is a derived noun used only in Psalms. Both occurrences appear in consecutive Psalms in the phrase "stronghold in times of trouble" (Psa 9:9, Psa 10:1), functioning as a temporal marker — "in the time of pressing" — rather than as a description of the inner state of the sufferer. The term contributes minimally to inner-being analysis on its own; its significance lies in showing that the TSR root also generates compound expressions describing the circumstance of distress as a period or season, not merely as a felt inner state.

Tsa.rah (H6869B, "distress," 70 occurrences, 58 verses in source) is the primary TSR noun and by far the most analytically rich term in Part 1. Its verse set is the largest and spans the entire canon from Genesis to Zechariah. Three functional registers can be identified within its verse set. The first is distress as a circumstance that prompts turning to God — this is the dominant register, appearing across Psalms (Psa 20:1, 46:1, 50:15, 77:2, 86:7, 91:15, 120:1) and the prophets (Isa 33:2, Jer 14:8, Jon 2:2), where the pattern is consistently: distress arises, the sufferer cries out, God answers. The second register is distress as an explicitly inner-located state. Gen 42:21 is the clearest instance: "we saw the distress of his soul," where tsa.rah is directly qualified by the word for soul. Psa 25:17 uses it in the plural ("the troubles of my heart"), placing it within the inner person. Psa 31:7 pairs it with "affliction" in a context of God knowing the inner experience. The third register is distress as physical-somatic event: Jer 4:31, 6:24, 49:24, and 50:43 all use birth-pain imagery — anguish and sorrows seizing a woman in labour — and Hab 3:16 describes tsa.rah as producing bodily trembling, quivering lips, and rottenness entering the bones. This third register is significant: tsa.rah is not confined to an inner psychological state but is shown producing physical expressions in the body.

One verse stands out for the god_as_subject flag: 2Ch 15:6, "God troubled them with every sort of distress." Here tsa.rah appears as something God administers — the distress is not merely permitted or foreseen but is attributed directly to divine action against a people. This is a qualitatively different use from the psalmic pattern where God is the refuge in distress: here God is the agent of distress.

Tsar (H6862B, "distress," 31 occurrences, 29 verses in source) is adjectival in form and carries the sense of a tight, hemmed-in condition. Its verse set overlaps considerably with tsa.rah in context type — Psalms of lament, narrative accounts of military and personal crisis — but the distribution is notably different. Tsar appears much more frequently as a self-description in direct speech: "I am in great distress" (2Sa 24:14, 1Ch 21:13), "In my distress I called upon the Lord" (2Sa 22:7, Psa 18:6, Psa 120:1). The term functions as a first-person declaration of the felt experience rather than as a third-person description of circumstances. This is a functionally distinct use from tsa.rah, even though the two terms are closely related. The god_as_subject signal is present at Isa 63:9, where "in all their affliction he was afflicted" uses tsar to describe God's participation in the distress of his people — a strikingly different direction from 2Ch 15:6, where tsa.rah is something God imposes.

**TSR verdict:** The four TSR terms in Part 1 are not near-synonyms. They do genuinely different work. Ya.tsar is the verb of being constricted; bats.tsa.rah marks the period or season of trouble; tsa.rah is the noun for the distress-event and the inner distress-state (both registers present, often simultaneously); tsar is the first-person felt condition, frequently voiced in direct speech. The translation "distress" in English obscures these distinctions almost entirely. Tsa.rah and tsar are candidates for close comparison in Section 3.

### TSUQ Family: tsu.qah (H6695B), mu.tsaq (H4164), ma.tsoq (H4689), tsoq (H6695A)

The TSUQ root carries the image of pressing or squeezing — constriction applied to something from outside. It is closely related to the TSR root in its spatial imagery but appears more consistently at the level of severe, acute distress rather than the generalised state of trouble. All four TSUQ terms in Part 1 are Old Testament, all appear in small verse sets, and none carries the god_as_subject flag.

Tsu.qah (H6695B, "anguish," 3 occurrences, 3 verses) appears in three high-intensity contexts. Pro 1:27 pairs it with distress in a scene of overwhelming calamity. Isa 8:22 uses it in an apocalyptic context of thick darkness and anguish. Isa 30:6 places it in a dangerous wilderness. In all three cases tsu.qah is co-occurring with other distress terms, functioning as an intensifier within a cluster of disaster language. Its meaning ranges toward "anguish" — a more acute and severe pressing than the general distress of tsa.rah.

Mu.tsaq (H4164, "constraint," 3 occurrences, 3 verses) appears in contexts where constriction or compression is the primary image: Job 36:16 uses it as the state from which God draws one out into a broad place, and Job 37:10 uses the related form for the freezing of water — broad waters being compressed into ice. The Isa 9:1 verse is the most significant: "no gloom for her who was in anguish," where the constraint or pressing is the pre-deliverance condition that God reverses. Mu.tsaq contributes the sharpest spatial image in the family: the person compressed, with no room.

Ma.tsoq (H4689, "distress," 6 occurrences, 1 verse in source) appears once at Psa 119:143 alongside "trouble," which is where its verse data ends in this part. Its meaning summary indicates it is a nominal form meaning distress or anguish of a particularly pressing kind. With only one verse, its analytical weight in Part 1 is limited.

Tsoq (H6695A, "distress," 1 occurrence, 1 verse) appears only at Dan 9:25, where it describes the troubled time during which Jerusalem was rebuilt. The single occurrence makes independent analysis impossible. Its presence in the family is noted but its contribution to inner-being analysis in Part 1 is nil.

**TSUQ verdict:** The four TSUQ terms appear to function as near-synonyms within a single semantic range of severe pressing and constraint. The distinctions among them are largely those of formation (noun type, verbal root) and register (poetic vs. narrative) rather than of meaning. Tsu.qah leans toward anguish-severity; mu.tsaq leans toward spatial compression; the other two add little that is distinct. These terms are candidates for consolidation in the final word analysis unless Part 2 or Part 3 data reveals functional separation.

### RA Family: ra (H7451A), ra.a (H7489A)

The RA root denotes badness in the broadest sense — what is morally or experientially adverse, harmful, or evil. It is the most semantically wide of all the Part 1 roots, and its presence in the distress word set requires careful qualification: not all uses of ra are inner-being uses, and the 684 occurrences of H7451A and 96 of H7489A span a range from ethical categories to experiential suffering to instrumental divine action.

Ra (H7451A, "bad: harmful," 684 occurrences, 64 verses in source) is an adjective covering bad news, bad report, physical defect, moral evil, harmful circumstance, and inner emotional states simultaneously. Within the Part 1 verse set, several clusters are relevant to inner-being analysis. The "harmful spirit from the Lord/God" sequence (1Sa 16:14, 15, 16, 23; 18:10; 19:9) is the most striking: in each verse a ra spirit from God comes upon Saul, producing a distress state that manifests as raving and is relieved by music. The ra here is not an abstract moral quality but an active agent producing an inner state in a specific person. This is the god_as_subject signal at its sharpest in this term: God is not responding to distress but is administering a ra spirit that produces it. Neh 2:2 ("sadness of the heart") shows the term applied directly to inner emotional condition. Psa 78:49 co-occurs ra with wrath, indignation, and distress as a cluster of divine action. The verse set also contains many uses that are clearly not inner-being: blemishes on sacrificial animals, bad figs, assessments of good and bad. The inner-being uses are real but minority within the full data.

Ra.a (H7489A, "be evil," 96 occurrences, 65 verses in source) is the Hiphil and Qal verb of the same root. The causative_form_present flag is set for this term, and the Hiphil form "to do harm, to hurt, to make worse" is central to its meaning. In this form, the term explicitly encodes one agent causing distress to another: Gen 31:7 (God did not permit harm), 1Sa 25:34 (restrained from hurting), 2Sa 20:6 (do us more harm). The plain Qal appears in contexts of displeasure: God is displeased (Gen 21:12, 1Ch 21:7), Samuel is displeased (1Sa 8:6), the thing is evil in the sight of the Lord (Gen 38:10). The term also frames the experience of the oppressed: Num 20:15 (Egyptians dealt harshly), Deu 26:6 (treated us harshly). The causative dimension is the main analytical contribution of ra.a to inner-being analysis: it is the mechanism by which distress is inflicted rather than merely experienced.

**RA verdict:** Ra and ra.a are doing different work. Ra names the quality or character of harmful experience from the perspective of the one affected or evaluating. Ra.a adds the dynamic of agency — action producing harm in another. The two terms together constitute both the experiential and the relational-causal register of RA-type distress. Neither is primarily an inner-being term, but both have genuine inner-being applications within their verse sets.

### MAR Family: mar (H4751)

The MAR root generates terms for bitterness — a sensory metaphor applied to inner emotional experience. Mar (H4751, "bitter," 39 occurrences, 33 verses in source) is the most consistent inner-being term in Part 1 in the sense that it functions across its verse set almost entirely as a descriptor of inner experience rather than external circumstance.

The verse set shows three distinct registers. The first is bitterness as a quality of crying out or weeping: Gen 27:34 (bitter cry), Isa 33:7 (weep bitterly), Eze 27:30 (cry out bitterly), Zep 1:14 (the mighty man cries aloud). In each of these the bitterness qualifies the outward expression of an inner state — the cry is the somatic expression of the inner condition. The second register is bitterness as a quality of the inner person itself: Job 3:20 ("the bitter in soul"), Job 7:11 ("the bitterness of my soul"), Job 10:1 ("the bitterness of my soul"), 1Sa 22:2 ("everyone who was bitter in soul"). Here mar is applied directly to the soul (nefesh) as an inner-being location. This is the somatic_link signal at work: the bitterness is felt as a physical-sensory quality experienced in the inner person. The third register covers bitterness as a quality of taste or substance (Exo 15:23, Num 5:18-27, Pro 5:4, 27:7) — the physical bitter that underlies the metaphorical. The transition between physical bitterness and inner bitterness is built into the term.

1Sa 1:10 (Hannah "deeply distressed and prayed to the Lord and wept bitterly") combines all three registers in a single verse: inner distress, prayer as the inner person's direction, and bitter weeping as the bodily expression.

**MAR verdict:** Mar is a genuine inner-being term with consistent application to the soul and to somatic expression. It is not a near-synonym of the TSR or TSUQ terms. Those terms carry the image of being pressed and confined; mar carries the image of an adverse taste that pervades the inner person. The two root images are complementary, not overlapping.

### ODUNE Family: odunao (G3600)

Odunao (G3600, "be anguished," 4 occurrences, 4 verses) is a Greek verb meaning to cause pain or to be in pain, with the causative_form_present flag set. Its verse set is small but analytically significant. Luk 2:48 (Mary's anguish at losing the child Jesus) is an individual inner state arising from relational loss. Luk 16:24-25 (the rich man in Hades in anguish/torment) shows the term applied to post-mortem existence as a condition of ongoing suffering. Act 20:38 shows the Ephesian elders experiencing anguish at the prospect of not seeing Paul again — grief over anticipated permanent separation. The causative dimension appears not as a Hiphil but as the active form of the Greek verb, which can function transitively (to cause anguish) or intransitively (to be in anguish). All four NT verses show the state arising from relational circumstances: loss, separation, and judgment. This is a different trigger pattern from the TSR-TSUQ range, where distress typically arises from external threat or danger.

**ODUNE verdict:** Odunao is a genuine inner-being term in its NT usage, consistently inner-located and relationally triggered, with a causative form present. Its analytical significance is greater than its verse count suggests.

### THAMB Family: ekthambeo (G1568)

Ekthambeo (G1568, "be awe-struck," 4 occurrences, 3 verses in source — with a data quality gap noted in Section 5) is a compound Greek verb meaning to be greatly alarmed, amazed, or overwhelmed with awe. Its three present verses show the crowd's astonishment at Jesus (Mar 9:15), and the women's alarm at the angel in the tomb (Mar 16:5-6). All three uses involve an overwhelming reaction to an unexpected encounter with the transcendent or the miraculous. The question of whether this constitutes inner-being distress in the same register as the Hebrew terms is genuinely open: the state is acute and involves a full-person reaction, but it is closer to overwhelming awe than to the experience of being pressed, confined, or in anguish. The term's place in the distress word set warrants scrutiny.

**THAMB verdict:** Ekthambeo's classification within the distress registry rests on its overlap between alarm and being overwhelmed. It may represent a distinct category — distress arising from transcendent encounter — rather than distress as adverse circumstance or inner anguish. This is an open question.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

Two comparisons within Part 1 reveal genuine functional differences between sub-terms within families.

**Tsa.rah (H6869B) and tsar (H6862B) within TSR**

Both terms are translated "distress" in the source data and share the TSR root. The verse sets, however, show them operating differently. Tsa.rah functions across a wide range: it describes the distress-event (the day of trouble that occasions prayer), the inner state explicitly located in the soul (Gen 42:21: "the distress of his soul"), the condition that produces somatic effects (Jer 4:31; Hab 3:16), and the state that God administers as judgment (2Ch 15:6). Tsar, by contrast, appears predominantly in first-person self-declaration: "I am in great distress" (2Sa 24:14; 1Ch 21:13), "in my distress I called" (2Sa 22:7; Psa 18:6; Psa 4:1; Psa 18:6; Psa 120:1). Tsar is the felt condition as the sufferer names it; tsa.rah is the distress-event or state as observed or described from any vantage point. The difference is one of perspective and use rather than semantic field. Tsa.rah is the broader, more versatile term. Tsar is the first-person experiential declaration. Additionally, at Isa 63:9, tsar appears in the striking statement that God was "afflicted in all their affliction" — not as agent causing distress (as with tsa.rah at 2Ch 15:6) but as the one who participates in the distress of his people. This directional difference — God as source of distress versus God as co-sufferer in distress — is a genuine functional distinction within the TSR family, even though it falls across two sub-terms rather than within one.

**Ra (H7451A) and ra.a (H7489A) within RA**

Ra names the quality of harmfulness or badness as it is experienced or evaluated. Ra.a names the action of causing harm or the act of doing what is evil. The causative dimension of ra.a is the decisive functional difference. Where ra describes what is experienced as bad or harmful (the harmful spirit that comes upon Saul, the bad news, the distressing circumstances), ra.a describes what an agent does to produce harm in another (1Sa 25:34: God "restrained me from hurting you"; 2Sa 20:6: "do us more harm"). The two terms together cover both the experiential and the causal-relational register of RA-type distress, but they do so from different positions in the action. This is a genuine functional difference, not merely a labelling distinction.

**No significant differences between sub-terms were found in the TSUQ family** based on Part 1 data. All four terms operate within the same range of severe pressing and constraint, differentiated by nominal formation and verse context rather than by meaningful functional distinctions.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

Testament coverage in Part 1 is "both." The Hebrew terms cover Old Testament data exclusively; the Greek terms (sunoche, odunao, ekthambeo, ademoneo) are all New Testament.

The most significant difference between the OT and NT verse sets in Part 1 is one of *inner-being explicitness*. The OT terms — particularly tsa.rah and tsar — operate across a wide range from external circumstance to inner state, with the inner location sometimes explicit (Gen 42:21: "distress of his soul") but more often implied. The NT terms, by contrast, present distress as an inner state from the outset and with greater consistency. Ademoneo at Mat 26:37 and Mar 14:33 describes Jesus himself as sorrowful and troubled, with no ambiguity about whether the state is inner or circumstantial. Odunao at Luk 2:48 and Act 20:38 is consistently an inner experience arising from relational causes. The NT appears to use distress terms in a narrower, more consistently inner-directed register than the OT term set.

A second difference is in the type of trigger. The OT verse set shows distress arising predominantly from military threat, national crisis, personal danger, physical affliction, and divine judgment. The NT verse set shows distress arising from relational causes: loss of a person (Act 20:38), separation (Phili 2:26), anticipated suffering (Mat 26:37), and transcendent encounter (Mar 9:15; 16:5-6 for ekthambeo). This shift in trigger type is observable in the verse data, though it may partly reflect the narrower scope of the NT verse set rather than a categorical difference in the register.

A third observable difference is that the OT terms show explicit divine agency on both sides of distress: God as the one who sends distress (ra from God in 1Sa 16:14; tsa.rah from God in 2Ch 15:6) and God as the one who delivers from it (tsar at Psa 4:1, 18:6, etc.). The NT terms in Part 1 do not show God or the Holy Spirit as the agent causing distress. The distress in the NT verse set arises from human circumstances. Whether this represents a genuine theological difference or simply the narrower scope of the NT vocabulary in Part 1 cannot be determined here.

The single most significant observable difference is that the OT terms in Part 1 show the same root and sometimes the same term functioning in both directions — producing distress and delivering from it — whereas the NT terms in Part 1 function in one direction only: naming the state of being in distress. The generation-resolution ambidexterity seen in the TSR family (tsa.rah as the distress God sends and tsa.rah as the distress from which God saves) is not visible in the NT data within Part 1.

Whether me.tsu.qah belongs in Part 2 or Part 3, or is missing entirely from the source data, is not determinable from Part 1. This is not a gap that can be estimated around.

**OCCURRENCE_COUNT_MISMATCH — ekthambeo (G1568):** The source meaning block for ekthambeo states four occurrences and references Mar 14:33 as one of them, but only three verse texts are present in the source (Mar 9:15, Mar 16:5, Mar 16:6). The verse text for Mar 14:33 is absent from the JSON. This means the THAMB family section above is based on three of four verses. The missing verse (Mar 14:33) describes Jesus being "greatly distressed and troubled" in Gethsemane — the same scene as ademoneo in Mar 14:33, where ademoneo appears in the parallel account. If ekthambeo also appears in Mar 14:33, the THAMB section analysis would need to add that Gethsemane use, which would strengthen the case for ekthambeo as an inner-being term rather than merely an awe-response term. Analysis of the THAMB family should be treated as provisionally incomplete.

Additionally, this analysis covers Part 1 of a three-part word set. Several TSR-family terms present in Parts 2 and 3 (tsar as "enemy," "hard," and "narrow"; tsa.rar; tsa.rah as "vexer") are not available in Part 1. The TSR verdict in Section 2 and the OT comparison in Section 4 are therefore based on an incomplete picture of the family. All TSR findings should be read as provisional until Part 2 and Part 3 analyses are complete.
