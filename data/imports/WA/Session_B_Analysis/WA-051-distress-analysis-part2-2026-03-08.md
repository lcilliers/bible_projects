## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

The Part 2 term set for distress includes twelve terms across nine root families: ra.ah (H7451I and H7451C, root family RA); tsar in four senses — narrow (H6862A), enemy (H6862C), hard (H6862D), and me.tsar (H4712) — grouped under root family TSR; a.ven (H0205H, root family AVN); ze.a.qah (H2201, root family ZAQ); tse.va.chah (H6682, root family TSAVACH); ke.ev (H3511, root family KAV); thlipsis (G2347, root family THLIBO); stenochoria (G4730, root family STENO); anankē (G0318, root family ANANKE); and riv (H7379, root family RIV). The set spans both Testaments.

Provisional qualification under the inner-being definition is mixed across the set. Several terms qualify with reasonable confidence: ra.ah in both its H7451I and H7451C senses appears consistently in verse contexts where an experienced state of suffering, harm, or distress is an inner-being event; ke.ev carries explicit inner and bodily pain language and qualifies clearly; thlipsis (G2347) has both external circumstantial and inward experiential uses and qualifies provisionally; stenochoria (G4730) qualifies as a felt state of constriction across its four verses; and anankē (G0318) partially qualifies across its distress-bearing verses while other uses in the verse set are non-experiential. The TSR family terms qualify at different levels — me.tsar (H4712) qualifies directly as a term for experienced distress, tsar-narrow (H6862A) is a physical spatial term that serves as the underlying image for other TSR distress terms rather than itself naming an inner state, and tsar-enemy (H6862C) and tsar-hard (H6862D) are relational and material terms whose distress contribution is situational rather than experiential. Ze.a.qah (H2201) and tse.va.chah (H6682) qualify as outer expressions of inner distress — vocalisations generated by distress — rather than distress states themselves. A.ven (H0205H) qualifies partially, with some verse uses pointing toward experienced trouble and others carrying a wickedness or emptiness sense that is not inner-being distress. Riv (H7379) does not clearly qualify as an inner-being distress term; its verse set is dominated by legal, forensic, and social conflict uses, with no consistent evidence that riv names an inner-experienced state.

The term lupēros listed in the Part 2 source header has no word analysis block in the JSON and is not represented in the verse_records. It cannot be assessed here and is noted as a data gap.

Confidence level: moderate overall, with higher confidence on ra.ah (both senses), ke.ev, thlipsis, me.tsar, and stenochoria; lower confidence on a.ven, anankē, ze.a.qah, tse.va.chah, tsar-narrow, tsar-enemy, tsar-hard, and riv pending Phase 2 assessment.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family RA — ra.ah (H7451I and H7451C)

The root RA carries the core image of that which is bad, harmful, or broken — the opposite of shalom. It generates a semantic field that spans moral evil, harmful outcome, experienced distress, and relational injury. Within Part 2, it is represented by two STEP sub-entries under the same Strong's base number H7451: H7451I (glossed "distress: evil") and H7451C (glossed "distress: harm"). Both share an identical meaning_summary, identical occurrence count of 684, and identical related_words list. They are distinguished solely by their STEP gloss labels.

H7451I in its verse set functions predominantly as a moral and ethical term. The verses orient around what is done in God's sight, what is planned against another, and what issues from corrupt character — from "every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (Gen 6:5) to Proverbs' repeated attention to the one who plans evil against a neighbour (Pro 3:29; 16:27). In Ecclesiastes, H7451I extends into a philosophical register, naming what is grievous or absurd about human experience under the sun (Ecc 2:21; 5:13; 6:1). The prophets deploy it in covenantal and judgement contexts, often as a description of Israel's conduct that has provoked divine response. In all of these, the experiential-distress dimension is secondary or implicit: the term names the quality or category of what is evil, not primarily the inner state of the one who endures it.

H7451C in its verse set clusters differently. Across the Samuel, Kings, and Jeremiah texts, it consistently appears as the expected harm or disaster directed against a person or people — harm that Saul plots against David (1Sa 23:9), harm that is "determined" against someone (1Sa 20:9, 13), disaster that the Lord declares he will bring upon Jerusalem and Judah. In Jeremiah especially, H7451C is the operative term for the divinely sent disaster that either is coming or may be averted if Israel repents (Jer 26:3, 13; 32:42; 44:2). Moses prays that God will "relent from this disaster" (Exo 32:14) and God does so in Jonah (Jon 3:10). This relent-from-disaster pattern is a distinctive feature of H7451C's verse set and is absent from H7451I. The god_as_subject flag is true for both terms, reflecting the fact that in multiple verses, God is the agent who brings, purposes, or directs the ra.ah upon others.

The distinction between the two sub-entries, as represented in the verse sets, appears to reflect a functional difference in usage: H7451I leans toward ra.ah as moral quality — evil characterised by wickedness of intent or character — while H7451C leans toward ra.ah as experienced event — disaster, harm, calamity that lands on someone. This is a real difference in how the word is deployed across the verse sets, though it should be noted that this is a STEP-imposed sub-classification and both entries share the same underlying lexeme. The meaning_summary makes no distinction between the two.

The English translation practice for this root routinely splits across "evil," "wickedness," "disaster," "harm," and "calamity" depending on context. This distributes what is in Hebrew a single root across a wide range of English moral and situational vocabulary, concealing from the English reader that the same root is generating both the description of a wicked person's heart and the description of the calamity that falls on a city.

**Verdict:** H7451I and H7451C are not genuinely separate lexemes. They are sub-entries derived from the same base lemma, distinguished only by STEP gloss label. Their verse sets do, however, reveal a genuine functional difference in usage — moral characterisation versus experienced calamity — that the STEP classification has tracked imperfectly. The two entries together cover the full semantic range of the root. Whether they should be retained as separate entries or merged with an internal disambiguation note is flagged as a data quality and methodology question. See Section 5.

### Root Family TSR — tsar (H6862A, H6862C, H6862D) and me.tsar (H4712)

The root TSR carries the primary image of narrowness, tightness, or constriction. This physical image is foundational: being in a tight space, unable to manoeuvre, unable to escape. From this root image, the family generates several distinct derivative senses: spatial narrowness, the experience of being hemmed in by circumstances (distress), the one who does the hemming in (enemy, adversary), and the hardness of a small sharp object. Me.tsar is a derived noun from the same root, carrying the sense of straits or constriction as experienced circumstance.

H6862A (tsar-narrow) holds the spatial-literal end of the family. Its verses range from the physical narrowing of a path where the angel of the Lord blocks Balaam's donkey (Num 22:26) to the overcrowded place of Elisha's prophets (2Ki 6:1), a narrow well (Pro 23:27), and the too-narrow land envisioned by returning exiles (Isa 49:20). The distress-bearing uses of the root — what in Part 1 would be tsa.rah and in this part me.tsar — depend on this spatial image as their metaphorical base. H6862A itself, in the verse set here, is not a distress-experience term; it is the word for literal narrowness from which the distress metaphor grows. One verse, Pro 24:10, makes the transition explicit: "if you faint in the day of adversity, your strength is small" — but here "adversity" is how the English renders a context reading, not the word itself.

H6862C (tsar-enemy) takes the root in a relational direction. The one who constitutes the tight place, the one pressing against you, becomes the adversary or foe. Its verse set is dominated by military and political contexts — the enemies who besiege or oppress God's people in Psalms and Lamentations, and God's own adversaries (Isa 1:24; Nah 1:2). In Lamentations, the term reaches particular density, naming the foes who have triumphed over Jerusalem and before whom her children were taken captive (Lam 1:5, 7, 10, 17; 2:4, 17). The experiential dimension of distress is entirely external and situational in this sub-entry: tsar-enemy names the agent of the constriction, not the inner state.

H6862D (tsar-hard) is represented by a single verse (Isa 5:28), describing the horses' hoofs as being like flint. This is a marginal entry — a rare physical use of the root's core hardness sense — and contributes nothing to the distress picture independently.

Me.tsar (H4712) is the most directly distress-bearing term in this family within Part 2. With only three verses, all in poetic books, it functions consistently as the name of an inner experience of constriction: "I suffered distress and anguish" (Psa 116:3); "Out of my distress I called on the Lord" (Psa 118:5); "her pursuers have all overtaken her in the midst of her distress" (Lam 1:3). In all three, me.tsar names the experiential state rather than the external circumstance or agent. The cross_registry_links in the JSON note that me.tsar shares the TSR root with tsa.rah, tsar, and ya.tsar in Part 1, which means the full distress contribution of the TSR root spans across both parts.

The English translations handle this family with considerable variety: "narrow," "enemy," "foe," "adversary," "distress," "straits." This variety correctly reflects the different functions the root takes on, but the underlying unity — that all of these are variants of the experience of being tight-pressed — is not visible to the English reader.

**Verdict:** The four TSR terms in Part 2 represent four distinct functions of the same root and are genuinely doing different work. Tsar-narrow (H6862A) is the root's foundational spatial image; tsar-enemy (H6862C) is the root applied to persons who constitute the pressing; tsar-hard (H6862D) is a marginal material use; and me.tsar (H4712) is the root applied to the experienced inner state of constriction. Only me.tsar is a clear inner-being term. Tsar-enemy and tsar-narrow are candidates for consolidation-with-note rather than full analysis entries, depending on whether the root's origin image is treated as analytically significant for Part 1's distress terms.

### Root Family AVN — a.ven (H0205H)

The root AVN carries a complex semantic field: the primary senses include trouble, toil, sorrow, and wickedness, with a secondary idolatry sense. The related_words include strength/vitality (on) and toil (te.u.nim), suggesting the root may be connected to the idea of effort or striving that comes to nothing. With 11 verses in the source and 77 total occurrences, a.ven is a minor term in the distress set.

In the verse set, a.ven appears in contexts of hardship or affliction that is either unavoidable (Job 5:6 — "affliction does not come from the dust") or the natural lot of human life (Psa 90:10 — "their span is but toil and trouble"). In Proverbs, it appears as the outcome of injustice (Pro 12:21; 22:8). In Habakkuk, the tents of Cushan are in affliction before the approach of the Lord (Hab 3:7). In the Hosea and Amos material, a.ven slides toward its idolatry or wickedness sense — "Bethel shall come to nothing" (Amo 5:5) and the mourning bread of an idolatrous feast (Hos 9:4).

The verse set does not clearly establish a.ven as an inner-being distress term. It more consistently names trouble as circumstance or condition than as an inner-experienced state. No somatic link or god-as-subject flag is present. The term's range of meaning — from trouble-as-affliction to wickedness-as-trouble to sheer toil — means that its distress function is only one strand of a wider semantic weave.

**Verdict:** A.ven is a partial candidate for the distress inner-being word set. Its verse set here does not establish consistent use as a named inner state. Pending Phase 2 assessment of the full occurrence range, it should be held as a borderline qualifier with a note on its semantic multiplicity.

### Root Family ZAQ — ze.a.qah (H2201)

Ze.a.qah derives from za.aq, the verb "to cry out," and denotes the outcry or cry itself — the audible vocalisation that distress produces. It is not a distress-state term; it is a term for the behavioural output that distress generates. Its somatic_link flag is true, correctly flagging that this term connects an inner state (the distress that occasions the cry) to a bodily act (the vocal cry itself).

The verse set shows ze.a.qah functioning in three related ways. First, as the morally weighty cry of the oppressed heard by God — the outcry against Sodom that has reached God (Gen 18:20), and the cry of the fathers in Egypt that God heard (Neh 9:9). Second, as the cry of lamentation accompanying grief — Mordecai's loud and bitter cry on hearing of the decree against the Jews (Est 4:1), Job's cry that he wishes would find no resting place (Job 16:18), and the lamenting in the Purim observance (Est 9:31). Third, as the wail of nations in judgement — Moab's cry going around the land (Isa 15:5, 8), Babylon's cry heard among the nations (Jer 50:46; 51:54), and the city-cry as a sign of destruction coming or fallen (Jer 18:22; 20:16; 48:4; Eze 27:28). Isaiah 65:19 marks the eschatological end of ze.a.qah as one sign of future restoration: "no more shall be heard in it the sound of weeping and the cry of distress."

The god-as-subject flag is false. Ze.a.qah is heard by God (Gen 18:20; Neh 9:9) but is not attributed to God.

**Verdict:** Ze.a.qah qualifies as a somatic-expressive term for distress rather than a distress-state term. It names the bodily-vocal output of inner distress, not the state itself. Its analytical value for the inner-being set is in what it reveals about the relationship between inner state and outward expression.

### Root Family TSAVACH — tse.va.chah (H6682)

Tse.va.chah derives from the verb tsa.vach (to cry aloud) and, like ze.a.qah, names an outcry or shout. It is a small term — 4 verses, 4 occurrences — and its verse set is correspondingly thin. Its somatic_link flag is true.

The four verses show tse.va.chah in distress-cry, lament, and public-wailing contexts: no cry of distress in the streets as an expression of national peace (Psa 144:14), an outcry in the streets for want of wine amid judgement (Isa 24:11), Jerusalem's lament going up (Jer 14:2), and the earth full of the cry of Egypt in its defeat (Jer 46:12). The term appears to function as a synonym of ze.a.qah in its public-wailing and city-cry uses, though its smaller occurrence count limits confident differentiation.

**Verdict:** Tse.va.chah is, like ze.a.qah, a somatic-expressive distress term rather than an inner-state term. With only four verses, its independent analytical contribution is thin. Whether it is meaningfully distinct from ze.a.qah in inner-being terms cannot be established from this verse set alone.

### Root Family KAV — ke.ev (H3511)

Ke.ev derives from ka.av (to pain) and names pain — mental and physical. The meaning_summary explicitly bridges the two: "pain (mental and physical), sorrow." Its somatic_link flag is true, and this is the most significant somatic link in the Part 2 set: the term is defined as covering both the physical and the psychological simultaneously, making it a primary witness for the inner-body connection in the distress vocabulary.

The six verses place ke.ev consistently in contexts of sustained, intensely experienced suffering. Job's suffering is so evident that his friends sat silent seven days before it (Job 2:13). The pain of speaking and the pain of silence are both named in Job 16:6, locating ke.ev as an inner state that neither expression nor restraint can resolve. In Psalm 39:2, silence increases the distress rather than relieving it — "my distress grew worse." In Isaiah 17:11, the harvest flees in "a day of grief and incurable pain." Isaiah 65:14 places ke.ev in direct contrast to joy: "you shall cry out for pain of heart and shall wail for breaking of spirit" — a verse that explicitly connects ke.ev to the heart and spirit as its location. Jeremiah 15:18 names the pain as "unceasing" and "incurable," asking why it refuses to be healed.

The Isa 65:14 verse is particularly significant: "pain of heart" locates ke.ev in a named inner faculty. This is the kind of NT-faculty-naming pattern that the instructions specifically flag for attention — though here it is OT rather than NT. The verse does not merely say someone is in pain; it says the pain is located in the heart.

**Verdict:** Ke.ev qualifies clearly as an inner-being distress term with a strong somatic link. Its verse set establishes it as a term for pain that is simultaneously inward and bodily, of the kind that neither speech nor silence can fully process, and that in at least one verse is explicitly located in the heart.

### Root Family THLIBO — thlipsis (G2347)

Thlipsis derives from thlibō (to press, to crowd) and carries the same metaphor of pressure or constriction that the Hebrew TSR family carries — the Greek term is, in effect, a structural parallel to the TSR root family in Part 1 and Part 2. Its meaning_summary explicitly notes: "primarily pressure, compression; metaphorically affliction, distress of mind." The god_as_subject flag is true, reflecting that in at least one verse God is associated with its occurrence.

With 41 verses in the source and 45 occurrences overall, thlipsis is the largest NT term in Part 2 and one of the most significant in the whole distress set. Its verse set covers a wide range. The eschatological-cosmic register appears in the Synoptic Gospels, where thlipsis names the unparalleled tribulation of the end times (Mat 24:21; Mar 13:19; Rev 7:14). The experiential-personal register appears in Paul — the affliction he endured in Asia beyond his strength, so that he despaired of life (2Cor 1:8); affliction as the context in which God comforts (2Cor 1:4); affliction as the circumstance in which the Macedonians overflowed in generosity (2Cor 8:2). The judicial register appears in Romans 2:9, where thlipsis and stenochoria are paired as the consequence facing those who do evil. The apostolic-vocational register appears in Acts and the Pauline epistles, where tribulation is named as the expected path into the kingdom (Act 14:22) and as something believers are "destined for" (1Th 3:3).

Several verses locate thlipsis in relation to a transformation dynamic: affliction produces endurance (Rom 5:3), the light and momentary affliction prepares an eternal weight of glory (2Cor 4:17), and the affliction that believers receive the word in is accompanied by the joy of the Holy Spirit (1Th 1:6). These uses are not mere description of distress as suffered; they place thlipsis within a framework where the state is productive of something beyond itself.

Jesus in John 16:21 uses thlipsis for the birth-pain analogy — the anguish forgotten when the child arrives — a brief but structurally important use that locates thlipsis as intense momentary suffering subsumed by the outcome. John 16:33 states that believers will have thlipsis in the world but can have peace in Christ — a direct inner-outer contrast.

The Revelation material (Rev 1:9; 2:9, 10, 22; 7:14) uses thlipsis for both present suffering under persecution and eschatological tribulation, with the seven churches addressed as those already enduring it.

The god_as_subject flag for thlipsis is true. The specific verse that warrants this needs to be identified carefully from the verse set: Acts 20:23 — "the Holy Spirit testifies to me in every city that imprisonment and afflictions await me" — positions the Holy Spirit as the one giving foreknowledge of thlipsis, not necessarily experiencing or causing it directly. Revelation 2:22 — "I will throw her onto a sickbed, and those who commit adultery with her I will throw into great tribulation" — has Christ as the one sending thlipsis as judgement, which is the strongest god-as-subject instance in the set.

**Verdict:** Thlipsis is doing substantial, differentiated work across the NT verse set. It functions as a term for sustained pressure-bearing distress that is simultaneously external (circumstances of persecution and hardship) and internal (the experienced state of being pressed). It qualifies clearly as an inner-being distress term with significant theological and developmental dimensions.

### Root Family STENO — stenochoria (G4730)

Stenochoria is a compound derived from stenos (narrow) and chōra (space, country), meaning narrowness of place — a direct Greek parallel to the TSR root's spatial image. Its four verses all use it as a term for the experienced state of being hemmed in or under pressure, in two occurrences paired with thlipsis in the same lists.

Romans 2:9 pairs thlipsis and stenochoria as the twofold distress consequence for those who do evil. Romans 8:35 lists stenochoria alongside tribulation, persecution, famine, nakedness, danger, and sword as things that cannot separate believers from Christ's love. Second Corinthians 6:4 and 12:10 place it in Paul's catalogue of apostolic hardships — things endured with patience or contentment. In none of these verses does stenochoria appear to be doing work independent of thlipsis; it functions as a near-synonym in catalogue contexts, contributing spatial-constriction nuance to lists that thlipsis alone could carry.

**Verdict:** Stenochoria qualifies as an inner-being distress term but is, across its verse set, functionally near-synonymous with thlipsis in the contexts where it appears. It adds the explicit spatial-constriction image to the meaning but does not appear to name a distinct type of inner state distinguishable from thlipsis at the level of use.

### Root Family ANANKE — anankē (G0318)

Anankē carries a primary sense of necessity or compulsion — the state of being under constraint, unable to avoid a course of action. Its related_words suggest a root connected to binding or pressing. In the NT, it covers a wide range: logical necessity (Heb 7:12, 27; 9:16, 23), compulsion in giving (2Cor 9:7), moral obligation (Rom 13:5), and, in a minority of verses, experienced distress or hardship.

The verses that carry distress meaning are: Luke 21:23 ("great distress upon the earth"), 1 Corinthians 7:26 ("the present distress"), 2 Corinthians 12:10 (Paul's catalogue of hardships), and 1 Thessalonians 3:7 (Paul comforted in "distress and affliction"). The non-distress uses of the verse set are comparably represented: Matthew 18:7 (necessary temptations), Luke 14:18 (compulsion to go see a field), Romans 13:5 (moral necessity), 1 Corinthians 7:37 (freedom from compulsion), 1 Corinthians 9:16 (necessity of preaching), and the Hebrews priestly-law necessity passages.

The distress function of anankē appears to be one pole of a wider word that fundamentally names what cannot be avoided — whether that is a logical entailment, a moral obligation, or an external pressure that presses on someone. When it names distress, it does so by extension of this core sense: the distress that cannot be escaped, the situation of inescapable pressure.

**Verdict:** Anankē partially qualifies as a distress term. Across its verse set in this JSON, the distress uses are in the minority. Its contribution to the inner-being picture is as a term for the inescapability dimension of distress — the quality of pressure from which there is no apparent exit — rather than as a primary distress-state label.

### Root Family RIV — riv (H7379)

Riv derives from a verb meaning to contend or strive and names a dispute, quarrel, controversy, or legal case. Its related_words include ya.riv (opponent), me.ri.vah (provocation), and the verb riv (to contend). With 58 verses in the source, this is a substantial entry.

The verse set is dominated by forensic and social-conflict uses: disputes brought before judges (Deu 17:8; 19:17; 25:1), legal cases in the courts of Israel (Exo 23:3, 6; 2Ch 19:8, 10), strife among herdsmen (Gen 13:7), quarrelling at Massah and Meribah (Exo 17:7), and the contention of Moses with the people (Deu 1:12). In the Psalms and prophets, riv extends to the individual's appeal for God to "plead my cause" — a legal metaphor that personalises the forensic context (Psa 31:20; 35:23; 43:1; 74:22; 119:154; 1Sa 24:15; Lam 3:58). In Hosea, Micah, and Jeremiah, God holds a riv — an indictment or covenant lawsuit — against Israel and the nations (Hos 4:1; 12:2; Mic 6:2; Jer 25:31; Eze 44:24).

There is no verse in this set where riv functions as a named inner-experienced state of distress. The term generates situations that produce distress — being embroiled in strife, being under accusation, having one's cause undefended — but the distress produced is circumstantial. The Proverbs material is consistent in treating riv as the social-relational phenomenon of contention and strife (Pro 15:18; 17:1, 14; 18:6; 20:3; 26:17, 21; 30:33).

**Verdict:** Riv does not qualify as an inner-being distress term. It names a relational-social event — contention, dispute, legal controversy — that may generate distress but does not name the distress state itself. Riv's inclusion in the word set appears to reflect its association with distressing circumstances rather than an inner-being function. Whether riv belongs in the distress word set at all is a question for Phase 2.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

The most analytically significant within-family distinction in Part 2 is between H7451I and H7451C in the RA family.

H7451I verse clusters around the description of what is evil in moral-character terms: the intention of the heart that is evil (Gen 6:5), the evil planned by one person against another (Gen 50:20; Pro 3:29; Zec 7:10, 8:17), the wickedness that Ecclesiastes observes under the sun (Ecc 5:13; 6:1; 10:5), and the evil deeds that the prophets identify as the cause of covenant breach (Hos 7:1-3; Jer 44:3-9). The subject of evil here is typically a person or a people whose character or act is being described as ra.ah. When the verse has God as subject in H7451I, it is typically in the context of God's moral judgement on evil, not God as the bearer of ra.ah.

H7451C verse clusters differently: harm plotted against David and likely to befall him (1Sa 20:7-13; 23:9; 24:9); the disaster the Lord declares and may relent from (Exo 32:14; Jon 3:10; Jer 26:3, 13); the calamity that will come "upon them" as consequence (Deu 29:21; Jer 1:14; 4:6; 11:11; 19:3). In these verses, ra.ah is an event that approaches, falls, or is averted rather than a quality characterising someone's will. The prayer of Psa 91:10 — "no evil shall be allowed to befall you" — illustrates this directional sense: ra.ah is something incoming, not something resident in character.

The difference is genuine in terms of syntactic slot and contextual cluster: H7451I tends to appear predicated of a person's heart, will, or deeds; H7451C tends to appear as the subject or object of events of falling, coming, and averting. The distinction is not absolute — both verse sets contain mixed uses — but the clustering is observable.

Within the TSR family, me.tsar (H4712) does clearly different work from tsar-enemy (H6862C) and tsar-narrow (H6862A). Me.tsar names the inner-experiential state of constriction (Psa 116:3; 118:5; Lam 1:3), while H6862C names the external agent of constriction and H6862A names the physical spatial image. These are not near-synonyms but genuinely distinct functions of the same root at different points in its semantic extension.

Within the NT families, stenochoria (G4730) and thlipsis (G2347) show co-occurrence in the same lists (Rom 2:9; Rom 8:35; 2Cor 6:4; 2Cor 12:10) without clear semantic differentiation in those contexts. The verse sets do not reveal a consistent distinction in what type of distress each term names when they appear together.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The Part 2 set has terms in both Testaments and testament_coverage is "both."

The OT term set is dominated by three semantic clusters. First, ra.ah (both H7451I and H7451C) represents distress as moral-causal event: distress as the outcome of evil character or as the consequence arriving through divine judgement. Second, the TSR family (through tsar-enemy and me.tsar) represents distress as the experience of being pressed and hemmed in — me.tsar in the individual's cry from constriction, tsar-enemy as the external agent. Third, ke.ev, ze.a.qah, and tse.va.chah represent distress at the level of bodily pain and vocalised cry — the somatic expression of inner suffering.

The NT term set does not contain a direct equivalent for ra.ah's moral-cause framing. Thlipsis and stenochoria frame distress as pressure and tribulation, but neither carries ra.ah's axis of wickedness-as-distress or disaster-as-judgement in the same lexical unit. The NT distress vocabulary in this set is more consistently experiential-presentational: what is endured, what presses in, what cannot separate believers from God's love, what is to be entered through on the way to the kingdom.

The most significant observed difference is that thlipsis in the NT develops an explicit transformative-purposive framing that has no direct equivalent in the OT verse set here. Romans 5:3 — suffering produces endurance; 2 Corinthians 4:17 — momentary affliction preparing eternal glory; 1 Thessalonians 1:6 — receiving the word in affliction with the joy of the Spirit. None of the OT verses in this Part 2 set frame distress as productive or preparatory in this way. The OT verses present distress as something to be cried out from (ze.a.qah, ke.ev), as evidence of divine judgement (ra.ah H7451C), or as the experience of being hemmed in by enemies (me.tsar, tsar-enemy). The purposive transformation framing is absent.

A second observed difference is in eschatological scope. The OT uses of ra.ah, ze.a.qah, and tsar in this set are predominantly situated in historical events — specific nations, specific crises, specific covenantal moments. The NT uses of thlipsis include a sustained eschatological register (the great tribulation of the end times, the tribulation of the seven churches, the tribulation believers are destined to pass through on the way into the kingdom) that operates at a different temporal and cosmic scale.

A third observed difference is in the who-bears-distress. In the OT verse set, distress is most often borne by individuals in acute situations (Job, the Psalmists, Jeremiah) or by nations under judgement. In the NT verse set, thlipsis and anankē are regularly framed as the expected condition of those who follow Christ — distress as the normative apostolic and communal experience rather than as the exceptional crisis.

The presence of God as subject for both ra.ah and thlipsis is notable across Testaments, but in different registers: God brings ra.ah as judgement in the OT; in the NT, thlipsis occurs in contexts where Christ overcomes it (Joh 16:33) or the Spirit accompanies those in it (1Th 1:6).
