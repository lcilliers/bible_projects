## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** pride | **Registry ID:** 123 | **Source list:** High Confidence | **Date:** 2026-03-09

Pride qualifies as an inner-being word with high confidence. The Hebrew and Greek term data together establish a condition that is located in the interior of the person — the heart, the spirit, the will — and that generates observable external behaviour without being reducible to that behaviour. The clearest evidence for this comes from verses where pride is explicitly attributed to the inner disposition before its consequences appear (Pro 16:18; Pro 18:12; 2Ch 26:16; 2Ch 32:25), and from the use of the phrase "pride of the heart" as a distinct unit across multiple terms. The word set also carries a secondary, non-inner-being range — several terms apply to God's majesty, to the physical height of trees or buildings, or to legitimate national honour — and this dual valence must be accounted for throughout the analysis. The large size of the term set (26 terms across 8 root families, 285 verse records) gives this analysis a broad evidentiary base, though several high-frequency terms have verse samples somewhat below their stated occurrence counts.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### 2.1 — GA family (ge.ut, ge.vah, ga.on, ga.a.vah, ge.ah, ge.eh, ge)

The GA root generates the largest and lexically richest family in the set, with seven terms spanning nouns, adjectives, and one verb. The core image of the root is vertical — a rising, a lifting up, a swelling that exceeds its proper level. The family applies both to God's majesty (where rising up is appropriate) and to human arrogance (where it is not), and many of the most theologically loaded verses in the word set come from this family.

**ga.on (H1347)** is the most frequent term in the entire word set, with 44 verse records from Exodus to Zechariah. Its meaning range spans "exaltation, majesty, pride" and the STEP data explicitly marks a good-sense and a bad-sense sub-definition. In the verse set the good-sense use clusters around God (Exo 15:7; Job 40:10; Isa 2:10–21; Mic 5:4; Nah 2:2) and the natural or national order (Psa 47:4; Isa 4:2; Amo 8:7 — where the LORD swears by "the pride of Jacob" in an oath). The bad-sense use is the dominant one numerically: it is the pride of Moab (Isa 16:6; Jer 48:29), of Egypt (Eze 30:6, 30:18, 32:12, 33:28), of Philistia (Zec 9:6), of Babylon (Isa 13:11, 13:19), and of Israel itself when unfaithful (Hos 5:5; 7:10; Eze 16:49; Amo 6:8). The term functions most prominently at a collective or national scale — it is the pride of a people, a power, a king — more often than the private pride of an individual. It appears as God's target in prophetic judgement with notable regularity.

**ga.a.vah (H1346)** shares the GA root and a meaning range almost identical to ga.on — "pride, majesty, a rising up" — with 19 verse records. The two terms co-occur in the same verse at Isa 13:11, Isa 16:6, and Jer 48:29, where they appear in near-synonymous parallel. However, ga.a.vah clusters more heavily in Psalms than ga.on does, appearing there in contexts of personal interior pride — the foot of arrogance that threatens the psalmist (Psa 36:11), the necklace of pride worn by the wicked (Psa 73:6), the loud mouth of pride (Psa 31:18). The majestic or national sense (Deu 33:26, 33:29; Psa 68:34) is present but less prominent. This is a difference of register weight rather than of meaning class: both terms span the good-sense/bad-sense range, but ga.a.vah's verse sample is more concentrated in individual and devotional contexts while ga.on's verse sample is more concentrated in prophetic and national ones.

**ge.ut (H1348)** covers the same semantic ground — "majesty, pride" — in 8 verse records. Its most distinctive feature is that three of those records apply the term positively to God (Psa 93:1; Isa 12:5; Isa 26:10), and one describes a natural event (the rising column of smoke at Isa 9:18). Among the GA terms it carries the highest concentration of non-pejorative uses in proportion to its verse set. It appears once in an explicitly negative human pride context (Isa 28:1, 28:3: the proud crown of the drunkards of Ephraim). The term is a minor one by frequency but contributes to the observation that the GA root's vertical image is theologically neutral at the root level — the term for God's rising and the term for human arrogance share the same family.

**ge.vah (H1467)** is the Aramaic equivalent of the Hebrew ge.vah (H1466, not separately represented in this word set). With 4 verse records, all from Job and Jeremiah plus Daniel 4:37, it is a minor term. Its verse set shows an interesting range: Dan 4:37 closes Nebuchadnezzar's account of his humiliation with the statement that God is able to humble those who walk in pride, while Job 22:29 and Job 33:17 connect the term to God's humbling work. The term does not appear as a positive quality anywhere in its verse set.

**ge.ah (H1344)** occurs once in the word set (Pro 8:13), where the divine speaker lists "pride and arrogance" as objects of hatred. With a single verse record, analytical comment is limited to noting the term's occurrence within a synonymous pairing alongside ga.on and the path of evil.

**ge.eh (H1343)** yields 9 verse records, all in the negative sense — proud persons whom God addresses in Job 40:11–12, proud persons whom the psalmist calls God to punish (Psa 94:2), the contempt of the proud in Psa 123:4, and the haughty whose houses God tears down (Pro 15:25; 16:19). Notably, Job 40:11–12 attributes the action of humbling the proud to God as subject, which is the only signal feature for this term. The term functions as an adjective for persons characterised by inner-state pride. No positive or majestic sense appears in the verse set.

**ge (H1341)** is a minimal entry — 1 verse record (Isa 16:6) — and appears only in synonymous parallel with ga.on. As a stand-alone analytical subject it adds nothing beyond confirming the root.

**Verdict on the GA family:** The seven terms represent a single root image (vertical elevation) applied across a theologically dual range: God's majesty on one hand, human arrogance on the other. Among the negative-sense terms, differentiation by register is present but subtle: ga.on predominates in national and prophetic contexts; ga.a.vah in personal and devotional ones; ge.ut, ge.vah, ge.ah, ge.eh, and ge are lower-frequency and do not consistently mark a distinct sub-type. ga.on and ga.a.vah are the primary carriers for Phase 2 analysis; the smaller terms are likely near-synonyms or stylistic variants that will not sustain independent analytical weight.

### 2.2 — GAVAH family (gav.hut, go.vah, ga.vah)

The GAVAH root generates three terms that span the full range from physical height to interior pride to the resulting behaviour. The family shares a core image with the GA family (vertical elevation) but differs in that its primary referent is physical stature before it is spiritual condition, and it includes one of the two causative forms in the word set.

**ga.vah (H1361)** is the verb form and with 33 verse records is the most evidentially rich term in its family. Its STEP meaning range is carefully articulated: Qal uses include being physically tall (1Sa 10:23; 2Ch 33:14; Job 39:27), being exalted in a positive sense applied to God or God's servant (Psa 103:11; Psa 113:5; Isa 5:16; Isa 52:13; Isa 55:9), and being haughty or arrogant in the negative sense (2Ch 26:16; 2Ch 32:25; Pro 18:12; Eze 28:2, 28:5, 28:17). The Hiphil (causative) use is attested — "to make high, exalt" — adding an important dimension: the term can describe the action of one agent causing elevation in another. This is the first causative form in the set and it produces a required Phase 2 flag. The god_as_subject signal is also active: Isa 55:9 attributes the elevated quality to God's ways and thoughts; Isa 52:13 speaks of the servant being high and lifted up; and several Psalm verses attribute exaltation to God (Psa 113:5). Ezekiel 28:2, 28:5, and 28:17 provide the most elaborated interior account in the entire GAVAH family: the pride of Tyre's prince is located in his heart ("your heart is proud," "your heart has become proud in your wealth," "your heart was proud because of your beauty") — three distinct verses anchoring the inner state to the heart, with specific triggers (wisdom, wealth, beauty) identified.

**go.vah (H1363)** is the noun form of the same root, meaning "height, exaltation, haughtiness," with 9 verse records. The verse set spans physical height (1Sa 17:4; 2Ch 3:4; Eze 1:18; Eze 40:42; Eze 41:8) and inner-being pride (2Ch 32:26; Job 11:8; Job 22:12; Amo 2:9). Of these, 2Ch 32:26 is particularly precise: Hezekiah humbled himself specifically "for the pride of his heart" (le-go.vah libbo), where the noun is combined with the heart as its locus. Job 11:8 and 22:12 use the term for God's elevation, adding a god_as_subject dimension implicit in the data though not flagged in the JSON.

**gav.hut (H1365)** is the most restricted term in the family — 2 verse records (Isa 2:11; 2:17) — where it occurs in identical parallel: "the haughty looks of man" shall be brought low, and "the haughtiness of man" shall be humbled. Both verses identify God as the agent of humbling and name the Lord alone as the one exalted in that day. The term functions as an abstract noun for the state or quality of haughtiness; it does not appear in positive or majestic uses.

**Verdict on the GAVAH family:** The three terms represent a coherent family where the verb (ga.vah) is the analytical centre, carrying both the good-sense/bad-sense dual range and the causative form. The Ezekiel 28 material makes ga.vah the most developed inner-being account in the entire Hebrew word set, with explicit heart-locus language and named triggering conditions. go.vah and gav.hut function as derived nouns that lean more heavily toward the negative register.

### 2.3 — ZUD family (za.don, zud)

The ZUD root generates a verb and a derivative noun. Its core image is boiling — a heat that overflows its vessel. Both terms occupy the arrogance–presumption end of the semantic range; neither carries a positive or majestic sense.

**zud (H2103)** is the verb, with 11 verse records. Its meaning range covers "to act proudly, be presumptuous," with the Aramaic Aphel form attested for Daniel 5:20. The verse set is distinctive in that it concentrates heavily in narrative contexts of defiance against divine authority: the Exodus material uses the term for Pharaoh's arrogance (Exo 18:11), the Deuteronomy material for rebellious Israel (Deu 1:43; 17:13; 18:20), the Nehemiah material for Israel and the ancestors (Neh 9:10, 9:16, 9:29), and Jeremiah 50:29 for Babylon's defiance of the Holy One. Daniel 5:20 specifies that the hardened and proud spirit led to the removal of Nebuchadnezzar from his throne. This verb form consistently describes pride as an act against a superior, and specifically against God. It is the only term in the set whose verse set is almost entirely framed as an offence against God rather than a social or relational failing.

**za.don (H2087)** is the noun form, with 11 verse records. It shares the boiling/arrogance meaning range and adds "insolence, presumptuousness." Its verse set is wider in context type than zud's: it appears in Proverbs wisdom contexts (Pro 11:2; 13:10; 21:24), in narrative (1Sa 17:28, where David's brother charges him with za.don in coming to the battle), in prophetic judgement (Jer 49:16; 50:31–32; Eze 7:10; Obd 3), and twice with the Deuteronomy law material (Deu 17:12; 18:22). The Proverbs contexts introduce something the verb zud lacks: a sapiential framing where pride is contrasted with wisdom and humility and identified as a source of strife and disgrace. Obadiah 3 names the pride of Edom's heart explicitly as the deceiver — "the pride of your heart has deceived you" — which is the sharpest interior-state formulation in this family.

**Verdict on the ZUD family:** zud and za.don are closely related but not interchangeable. The verb clusters in covenantal and narrative contexts of presumptuous rebellion against God; the noun spans a wider register including Wisdom literature where pride is a relational and social failing. The Obadiah 3 formulation ("the pride of your heart has deceived you") stands as a notable inner-being anchor for za.don. Neither term carries a positive sense.

### 2.4 — RAHAB family (ra.hav H7294, ra.hav H7292, ra.hav H7295, ro.hav H7296)

The RAHAB family is complicated by the fact that three of its four terms share the same transliteration (ra.hav) despite having distinct Strong's numbers and distinct grammatical and semantic functions. The family must be handled carefully on transliteration alone. The shared phonological form makes English translation a particularly unreliable guide to what is happening in this family.

**ra.hav (H7294)** — Rahab the sea monster — is a proper noun or mythological name applied to Egypt as a poetic epithet. Its 4 verse records (Job 9:13; 26:12; Psa 87:4; Isa 30:7) are all poetic and all apply the term to Egypt as a defeated or passive power. The lexical note that the root carries the meaning "storm, arrogance" but functions only as a proper name in these texts means that the inner-being dimension here is allusive at most — the name evokes arrogance at the root level but does not deploy it as an interior state. The term's contribution to the word set is symbolic and literary rather than lexical.

**ra.hav (H7292)** is the verb "to be assertive / to act stormily or arrogantly," with 4 verse records. Its meaning range includes both the Qal (to act insolently, storm against, beset) and the Hiphil causative (to disturb, alarm, awe, confuse, make bold or proud). The causative form is the signal feature here — the Hiphil means that this root can describe one agent causing the proud or emboldened state in another. The verb's verse set is mixed: Psa 138:3 uses the Hiphil in a positive sense ("my strength of soul you increased"), Pro 6:3 uses it for importuning a neighbour, Song 6:5 for being overwhelmed by beauty, and Isa 3:5 for youth acting insolently toward elders. The range is notably wider than the strictly negative terms in the set, and the positive Psalm use makes the inner-being contribution ambiguous. The stray formatting artefact in the source block (the "me ni" line) does not affect the content.

**ra.hav (H7295)** is the adjective "proud, defiant," with a single verse record (Psa 40:4). The verse counsels against turning to "the proud" — a brief negative reference. No analytical weight can be placed on a single verse.

**ro.hav (H7296)** is the noun "arrogance, pride, object of pride," also with a single verse record (Psa 90:10). The verse uses it in the phrase describing human life as "toil and trouble." The connection is tangential and the verse record does not establish a clear inner-being locus for this term.

**Verdict on the RAHAB family:** This family is lexically marginal to the inner-being analysis. The mythological proper noun (H7294) is symbolic rather than lexical; the adjective (H7295) and the noun (H7296) each have one verse record; and the verb (H7292) has the most developed content but a verse set that includes positive and ambiguous uses. The causative Hiphil of ra.hav (H7292) is the most analytically significant element. Phase 2 will need to assess whether this family warrants full inner-being treatment or whether its contribution is mainly structural (demonstrating that the pride concept was etymologically linked to stormy, forceful, assertive behaviour at the root level).

### 2.5 — MAROM family (ma.rom H4791)

The MAROM family contains a single term. Its root is RUM (to exalt, to be high) rather than GA or GAVAH, making it etymologically distinct from the other height-related families.

**ma.rom (H4791)** has 50 verse records — the second largest verse sample in the set — covering "height, elevation, elevated place" and carrying both physical and figurative senses, including the adverbial "proudly." The verse set is dominated by references to God dwelling or acting from on high (Psa 92:8; 93:4; 102:19; Isa 33:5; 57:15; Jer 17:12; 25:30), to Zion's mountain height, and to the height of the heavens as a measure of God's superiority. The god_as_subject flag is active and well-evidenced: God is described as dwelling on high, sending from on high, looking down from his holy height, and roaring from on high. Among the negative-pride uses, Psa 56:2 uses ma.rom adverbially ("many attack me proudly"), Psa 73:8 notes that the wicked "speak loftily" (using ma.rom), and 2Ki 19:22–23 has Sennacherib lifting his eyes "to the heights" in arrogance against the Holy One — a textbook example of ma.rom used for human presumption against God. The most distinctive contribution of this term to the word set is that it establishes height itself as the contested territory: God occupies the true height; human pride is the illegitimate claim to the same position.

**Verdict on the MAROM family:** ma.rom is primarily a spatial and theological term whose inner-being contribution lies in the adverbial and figurative uses. It is not a direct inner-being word in the way ga.on or ga.vah are, but it supplies the spatial metaphor that underlies the entire Hebrew word set — the idea that pride is a vertical claim. The god_as_subject signal is its most analytically productive feature.

### 2.6 — TUPHOS family (tufoō G5187, fusioō G5448)

The Greek TUPHOS family is built on the image of smoke, vapour, or inflation — something puffed up rather than structurally elevated. Both terms are passive in their primary NT usage: they describe the state of being puffed up rather than the act of inflating oneself.

**tufoō (G5187)** occurs 3 times, all in the Pastoral and second epistle material (1Ti 3:6; 6:4; 2Ti 3:4). Each occurrence is a warning about the condition of being puffed up with conceit. The most developed is 1Ti 6:4, where the puffed-up person is characterised by absence of understanding and by disordered appetite for controversy. 1Ti 3:6 specifies that the state leads into the condemnation of the devil — the only place in the NT word set where pride is explicitly linked to a trajectory of judgement parallel to a named agent's fall. The LSJ entry for the term locates the root image in smoke, fumes, and mental derangement. The term's verse set is entirely confined to lists of character flaws and leadership warnings; it does not appear in narrative or theological argumentation the way kauchaomai does.

**fusioō (G5448)** occurs 7 times, all in the Corinthian correspondence and Colossians 2:18. The causative form is the key signal: the Hiphil-parallel in Greek — the active-voice use of the verb meaning "to puff up" someone — is attested at 1Cor 8:1 ("knowledge puffs up, but love builds up"), where knowledge is the agent inflating the person. This makes fusioō the NT term that most clearly identifies an external trigger for the inner state, and it introduces a unique element: the trigger is cognitive (knowledge), not material or social. The passive uses throughout 1 Corinthians (4:6, 18, 19; 5:2; 13:4; Col 2:18) describe persons already in the puffed-up condition. Col 2:18 specifies that the inflation is "without reason" and is connected to a sensuous mind — a phrase that may imply a faculty-specific locus in Paul's anthropology, though the JSON does not provide enough data to confirm this from the verse text alone.

**Verdict on the TUPHOS family:** tufoō and fusioō are closely related and their verse sets are almost entirely non-overlapping by book (Pastorals vs. Corinthian correspondence), suggesting functional-contextual specialisation rather than strict synonymy. fusioō's causative use with knowledge as the inflating agent is the most significant distinctive element in the family.

### 2.7 — KAUCHOMAI family (alazoneia G0212, kauchēsis G2746, kauchēma G2745, kauchaomai G2744)

The KAUCHOMAI family is the largest Greek family and introduces a range not present in the Hebrew word set: the notion that boasting or pride can be legitimate, directed at appropriate objects, and even commanded. This positive-or-neutral use of pride-language is unique to this family in the word set.

**kauchaomai (G2744)** is the verb with 32 verse records — the largest NT verse sample in the set — drawn almost entirely from Paul's letters, concentrated in Romans and 2 Corinthians. The meaning range distinguishes boasting about self (negative: 1Cor 4:7; Gal 6:13), boasting in God and the cross (positive: Rom 5:2–3, 11; 1Cor 1:31; Gal 6:14), and boasting about persons or communities one cares for (mixed: 2Cor 7:14; 9:2; 10:8; 2Th 1:4). The concentration of verses in 2 Corinthians 10–12 is notable: Paul argues at length about the conditions under which boasting is and is not legitimate, treating the inner state as something that must be directed rather than simply eliminated. This is the most theologically developed treatment of pride-language in the entire word set.

**kauchēsis (G2746)** and **kauchēma (G2745)** are noun forms of the same root with largely overlapping verse sets (both appear in Romans and the Corinthian correspondence). The STEP data distinguishes them: kauchēsis is the act or process of boasting; kauchēma is the ground or object of boasting — the thing one boasts about. In practice this distinction maps onto verse usage with moderate reliability: kauchēma is used where a reason or source of boasting is identified (Rom 4:2; 1Cor 5:6; 9:15–16; Gal 6:4; Phili 1:26), while kauchēsis appears where the activity or tone of boasting is described (2Cor 1:12; 7:4; 11:10; 11:17; 1Th 2:19). Both terms include positive uses (Paul's pride in the communities) and negative uses (Rom 3:27; 1Cor 5:6).

**alazoneia (G0212)** is etymologically distinct — from the root of the wandering braggart — and occupies only the negative end of the range. Its 2 verse records (Jam 4:16; 1Jo 2:16) both carry strong negative valence. James 4:16 identifies boasting in one's own arrogance as evil. 1 John 2:16 places alazoneia alongside desires of the flesh and eyes as a worldly triad opposed to the Father. The term functions as a pejorative label for the vain display or pretension aspect of pride, without the nuanced inner-directed quality of kauchaomai.

**Verdict on the KAUCHOMAI family:** This family's defining feature is its internal differentiation: kauchaomai and its noun derivatives span a legitimacy spectrum from condemned self-boasting to commanded boasting in the Lord. This dual range is formally analogous to the GA family's good-sense/bad-sense range in Hebrew, but operates by different logic — in the GA family the differentiation is primarily between God and humans as subject; in the KAUCHOMAI family it is primarily about the object of the boasting. alazoneia is confined to the pejorative end and functions as the closest NT equivalent to za.don's social-display register.

### 2.8 — HUPEREFANOS family (huperēfania G5243, huperēfanos G5244)

The HUPEREFANOS family is built on the compound image of being visibly above ordinary men — showing oneself above, appearing conspicuously elevated. It is the most consistently negative family in the Greek word set.

**huperēfania (G5243)** occurs once (Mar 7:22), in Jesus' list of things that come from within the heart and defile the person. The verse is the single clearest NT statement of pride as an inner-being defiling agent, and its location within a vice list that explicitly originates from inside (ek tes kardias — from the heart) gives this single-verse term a structural importance disproportionate to its frequency.

**huperēfanos (G5244)** has 5 verse records, all negative: Luk 1:51 (God scatters the proud); Rom 1:30 (a vice list); 2Ti 3:2 (a vice list); and the pivotal double citation Jam 4:6 and 1Pe 5:5 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." This last phrase, cited in both James and 1 Peter, is the most formulaic statement of the divine response to pride in the NT, and it specifies that huperēfanos is the quality God actively resists.

The unattested verb form huperēfaneō (no verse data) means the family lacks a verb in this word set — only noun and adjective are present.

**Verdict on the HUPEREFANOS family:** Small verse set but high analytical value. The Mar 7:22 heart-location of huperēfania and the dual citation of the "God opposes the proud" formula in James and 1 Peter make this family the NT locus for both the inner location of pride and its divine consequence. It is closely linked to the KAUCHOMAI family in function but differs in emphasis: where KAUCHOMAI frames pride as a directional problem (wrong object of boasting), HUPEREFANOS frames it as a positional problem (standing above what is appropriate).

### 2.9 — Unclassified term: sha.chats (H7830)

**sha.chats (H7830)** stands alone with no root family connection to the other terms. Its meaning covers "dignity, pride" with a sub-definition of "majestic wild beasts." Both verse records come from Job: Job 28:8 describes the proud beasts not having trodden the hidden mine path; Job 41:34 names God's creature (Leviathan) as king over "all the sons of pride." The term appears to function as a poetic marker for a pride-quality that belongs to powerful creatures in their natural state — a pride of bearing or magnificence rather than an interior moral failing. Its inner-being contribution is minimal.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

Several pairs and clusters within this word set reveal meaningful differentiation when their verse sets are examined together.

**ga.on versus ga.a.vah (GA family):** These two terms co-occur in the same verse at Isa 13:11, Isa 16:6, and Jer 48:29, confirming near-synonymy at the lexical level. However, the distribution of verse records reveals a register difference. ga.on dominates in prophetic judgement oracles against nations (Egypt, Moab, Babylon, Philistia, Assyria), and appears most frequently in books of prophecy (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, the Minor Prophets). ga.a.vah's verse distribution is more heavily weighted toward Psalms (Psa 10:2; 31:18; 31:23; 36:11; 73:6) and Proverbs (Pro 14:3; 29:23), where the interior and personal dimensions of pride are more prominent. The difference is not absolute — ga.on appears in Proverbs too (Pro 8:13; 16:18) and ga.a.vah in Isaiah — but the cluster pattern suggests these terms were preferred in different compositional environments. Whether this reflects a genuine semantic distinction or a matter of stylistic and generic convention is a question for Phase 2.

**zud versus za.don (ZUD family):** The verb zud is concentrated in texts where pride is an act of rebellion against God or divine authority — Exodus, Deuteronomy, Nehemiah, Daniel. The noun za.don appears in a wider range of contexts including Proverbs, where it functions within wisdom rhetoric comparing pride with humility and identifying it as a source of strife (Pro 11:2; 13:10). The verb describes the dynamic event; the noun can describe the stable character trait. Obadiah 3 ("the pride of your heart has deceived you") is the clearest inner-being formulation in this family and belongs to the noun.

**kauchaomai versus alazoneia (KAUCHOMAI family):** kauchaomai's verse set is divided between legitimate and illegitimate boasting — the distinction is carried by the object, not the act itself. alazoneia has no positive use in the word set; its 2 verses both condemn the state as evil (Jam 4:16) or worldly (1Jo 2:16). The act of boasting and the vice of arrogant display are different enough that these terms are not interchangeable even though they occupy related semantic space.

**kauchēsis versus kauchēma (KAUCHOMAI family):** The STEP distinction (kauchēsis = the act; kauchēma = the ground or object) is supported by the verse sets, though not perfectly. Where kauchēma appears with explicit object-of-boasting language (Rom 4:2; 1Cor 9:15; Gal 6:4), the distinction holds clearly. Where the two terms both describe Paul's pride in a community (2Cor 1:14 for kauchēma; 1Th 2:19 for kauchēsis), the functional difference is negligible.

**tufoō versus fusioō (TUPHOS family):** tufoō occurs only in the Pastoral epistles in warning lists about character; fusioō occurs only in 1 Corinthians and Colossians in active community correction. The books of attestation do not overlap. fusioō is the only term in the NT set to introduce an explicit external trigger for the inner state (knowledge puffing up in 1Cor 8:1), which tufoō does not do.

**huperēfania versus huperēfanos (HUPEREFANOS family):** The noun and the adjective divide their function predictably: the adjective (huperēfanos) is used in vice lists and in the "God opposes the proud" formula, identifying the person characterised by pride; the noun (huperēfania) appears once in the heart-sourced defiling vice list of Mark 7:22. The heart-locus statement belongs to the noun; the divine-opposition formula belongs to the adjective.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The word set has terms in both Testaments (`testament_coverage: "both"`), and the differences between the two registers are substantial.

**Scope of vocabulary:** The Hebrew word set contains 18 terms from 6 root families plus one unclassified term. The Greek word set contains 8 terms from 3 root families. The Hebrew set is therefore more than twice the size in terms of distinct lexical items, with a much wider range of root-level images (vertical elevation, boiling, storm-force, height, majesty). The Greek set is more compact and more conceptually focused.

**Positive-sense range:** The Hebrew GA, GAVAH, and MAROM families all carry a positive or neutral sense — God's majesty, legitimate national pride, the proper height of creation. In the Greek set, only the KAUCHOMAI family carries a positive range (boasting in God, in the cross, in community). The HUPEREFANOS and TUPHOS families have no positive uses in the verse set whatsoever.

**Interior location:** The NT is more explicit than the OT about locating pride in a specific inner faculty. Mark 7:22 places huperēfania in a list that originates "from within, out of the heart of man." 1Ti 3:6 links the puffed-up state to falling into the condemnation of the devil. The OT formula "pride of the heart" (le-go.vah libbo at 2Ch 32:26; pride of your heart in Eze 28:2; Obd 3; Jer 13:17) appears several times, but the most systematic interior account in the Hebrew set — Ezekiel 28's anatomy of the prince of Tyre's pride (heart, wealth, beauty as triggers) — is a narrative-theological account rather than a categorical statement.

**Divine response:** The OT verse set records God breaking, humbling, and judging pride in multiple prophetic contexts, but this is woven into individual oracles and narratives. The NT crystallises the divine response into a formula: "God opposes the proud, but gives grace to the humble" (Jam 4:6; 1Pe 5:5). This formula does not appear in the OT verse records in the JSON — it is the NT's own theological compression of what the OT shows through narrative and oracle.

**Social versus cognitive triggers:** The OT pride terms cluster around social and political triggers — wealth (Eze 28:5), beauty (Eze 28:17), military strength (2Ki 19:23), national power (Isa 16:6). In the NT, fusioō at 1Cor 8:1 introduces knowledge as a trigger — a cognitive trigger with no clear parallel in the Hebrew set. This is the single most significant difference in trigger-type between the two Testaments in this word set.

**Boasting as a distinct category:** The KAUCHOMAI family introduces a mode of pride — public verbal boasting — that is more elaborated in the NT than in the OT. The OT has za.don in this space (presumptuous speech, insolent boasting) but without the sustained theological analysis of legitimate versus illegitimate boasting that characterises Paul's treatment in 2 Corinthians 10–12.

**The single most significant observed difference:** The OT verse set treats pride primarily as a national, political, and prophetic concern — it is the pride of nations, kingdoms, and their rulers that fills the prophetic oracles. The NT verse set treats pride primarily as a personal character and community concern — it appears in vice lists, leadership qualifications, and community correction. The unit of analysis shifts from the collective to the individual between the Testaments in this word set.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

The following gaps and limitations in the JSON affect the completeness of the analysis.

**huperēfaneō (no Strong's number in this set):** The STEP list contains the verb form "be prideful (huperēfaneō)" but no verses were found in STEP for this verb form. It is recorded in the JSON as a NO_VERSES term. The absence means the HUPEREFANOS family has no verb in the word set, limiting the analysis of verbal/active expressions of pride in that family.

**Source file formatting artefacts (H2103, H1467, H7292):** Three terms carry FORMAT_ERROR_IN_SOURCE flags for carry-over lines and duplicate headings in the source STEP data. The data content for all three terms was confirmed correct and the artefacts do not affect the analysis.

**SMALL_VERSE_SAMPLE terms:** Four terms have verse records below their stated occurrence counts: ma.rom (H4791, 50 of ~54), ga.vah (H1361, 33 of ~34), kauchaomai (G2744, 32 of ~37), and ga.on (H1347, 44 of ~49). All four samples are above the 20% threshold and are analytically workable, but the gap is noted for each. For kauchaomai particularly — the term with the widest range (37 stated occurrences, 32 in the sample) — unsampled verses may include additional occurrences that bear on the legitimacy-spectrum analysis.

**CONSOLIDATION_CANDIDATE:** ga.on (H1347) and ga.a.vah (H1346) are flagged as consolidation candidates on grounds of shared root, meaning range, and shared verse citations (Isa 13:11; 16:6; Jer 48:29). The analysis in Section 3 identifies a register-distribution difference as the main observable distinction. Whether this difference warrants maintaining them as analytically separate entries will need to be decided in Phase 2.

**ma.rom (H4791) inner-being contribution:** ma.rom is primarily a spatial term. The majority of its 50 verse records describe physical height, divine elevation, or geographical location. The inner-being uses (adverbial "proudly" and the Sennacherib passage at 2Ki 19:22–23) are a minority of the verse set. This limits confidence in any inner-being characterisation of this term based on the current verse sample.
