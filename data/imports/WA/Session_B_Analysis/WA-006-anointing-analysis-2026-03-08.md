## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** anointing | **Registry ID:** 006 | **Source List:** Missing Inner Being Words

The question of whether anointing provisionally qualifies as an inner-being word cannot be answered with confidence from the JSON data alone. The source file itself records a researcher comment raising exactly this concern: anointing may function primarily as a status or role conferred on a person rather than as an inner state residing within the person. The data supports both readings. On one hand, the verb ma.shach (H4886) and the noun ma.shi.ach (H4899) name an outward act performed upon a person by another agent — oil applied to the head, a person designated for office. On the other hand, chrisma (G5545) in 1 John 2:20 and 2:27 describes something received that abides within the believer and has a teaching function, which is closer to an inner-being description. Similarly, chriō (G5548) in Act 10:38 names the conferral of the Holy Spirit and power upon Jesus, and in 2Cor 1:21 it is applied to believers as an act of God. The NT data therefore contains at least some vocabulary where anointing names a received inner state or indwelling quality rather than merely an external status.

**Provisional assessment:** Anointing qualifies as a borderline case with low confidence as an inner-being state in its OT form, and with moderate confidence in the NT terms chrisma and chriō. The OT data is predominantly ritual, civic, and positional rather than inner-being. The decision on full qualification requires review of the separate analysis file referenced in the source (WS-Word Analysis-Anointing_Analysis 2026-03-01), which was not available for this session.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: MSHCH (ma.shach, mish.chah, ma.shi.ach, ma.she.chah, mim.shach, me.shach)

The MSHCH root is the core Hebrew anointing cluster. Its basic physical image is of smearing or spreading a substance — oil — onto a surface or person. From this concrete action the root generates a range of derived forms covering the act itself (ma.shach), the substance used (mish.chah, me.shach), the status resulting from the act (ma.shi.ach), a portioned allocation of the substance (ma.she.chah), and a nominal form of uncertain meaning connected to the root's semantic range (mim.shach).

The verb ma.shach (H4886, about 70 occurrences) is the most active term in the family. Its verse set covers three types of anointing: the anointing of objects (altar, tabernacle, vessels — Exo 30:26, Num 7:1), the anointing of persons for priestly office (Exo 28:41, Lev 8:12), and the anointing of persons for royal office (1Sa 16:13, 2Sa 2:4, 1Ki 1:39). Across all three categories God is consistently the authorising agent, even when a human performs the physical act. The most theologically significant verse in the set is 1Sa 16:13, where the act of anointing is immediately followed by the Spirit of the Lord rushing upon David — the closest the OT data comes to linking the ritual act to an inner-being change. Isa 61:1 presents a first-person speaker who declares that the Spirit's presence upon them is the basis of their anointing, reversing the sequence and making the Spirit's presence, not the oil, the primary reality.

The noun ma.shi.ach (H4899, about 39 occurrences) names the person who has received the anointing — always a person of designated standing: the Messianic prince, the king of Israel, the high priest, Cyrus, the patriarchs. The verse set is almost entirely Psalms and historical narrative. God speaks of ma.shi.ach as "my anointed" in possessive terms (Psa 2:2, Psa 18:50, 1Sa 2:10), which means God is the subject in the sense of the one who confers and protects the status, not the one who experiences it. The term names a relational and covenantal designation rather than an inner quality. Dan 9:25–26 uses it in a prophetic and eschatological frame that anticipates an anointed one who will be cut off — the most forward-pointing use in the OT set.

The noun mish.chah (H4888A, about 25 occurrences) refers consistently to the anointing oil itself and to the anointing as a consecrating act applied to tabernacle furniture and priests. Its verse set is almost entirely Exodus–Numbers, dense with cultic instruction. The term does not appear in contexts where an inner state is named. It is a procedural and material term.

The noun ma.she.chah (H4888B, 1 occurrence, Num 18:8) is a near-homophone of mish.chah sharing the same Strong's base number. Its single use means "consecrated portion" or "allotted portion" and has no direct inner-being dimension. It is a consolidation candidate with mish.chah (see data quality flags).

The noun mim.shach (H4473, 1 occurrence, Eze 28:14) is the most puzzling term in the family. Its meaning is explicitly uncertain in the STEP data. Its one verse — the anointed guardian cherub on God's holy mountain — is cosmologically extraordinary: it applies MSHCH-root vocabulary to a heavenly being. Whether this indicates that anointing vocabulary extends to celestial designation and function, or whether it is simply a hapax with an unclear lexical relationship to the root, cannot be determined from the source data.

The Aramaic oil-noun me.shach (H4887, 2 occurrences) is a cognate form appearing only in Ezra in post-exilic administrative contexts (Ezr 6:9, 7:22). It names oil as a supply item, not as an anointing substance. Its connection to the MSHCH family is etymological only.

**Verdict on MSHCH family:** The terms are doing genuinely different work. Ma.shach names the act; mish.chah names the substance and the cultic procedure; ma.shi.ach names the status and identity of the designated person; ma.she.chah names a material allocation; mim.shach is of uncertain meaning; me.shach is a cognate oil-noun with no anointing function. None of these terms, in their OT verse sets, reliably names an inner state of the person anointed. The closest approach is 1Sa 16:13 (ma.shach, where anointing and Spirit-conferral are juxtaposed) and Isa 61:1 (ma.shach, where the anointed speaker describes inner empowerment). These two verses are the strongest OT candidates for inner-being relevance in the entire family.

### Root Family: CHRIO (chriō, chrisma, christos, enchriō, epichriō, chrisis)

The CHRIO root is the NT counterpart of MSHCH and shares its core meaning of rubbing or smearing. The LSJ entry for chriō notes its range from physical anointing of skin and dead bodies to the LXX use in consecration, to the NT use in conferral of spiritual function. The family in the NT is semantically richer for inner-being purposes than the MSHCH family is in the OT.

The verb chriō (G5548, 5 occurrences) carries the most theologically concentrated meaning in the entire word set. Its STEP meaning block explicitly names both physical and spiritual anointing and describes the act as assigning a person to a special task with a giving of power by God. Its five verse references distribute across three types of subject: Jesus anointed by God with the Holy Spirit and power (Luk 4:18, Act 4:27, Act 10:38), believers anointed by God (2Cor 1:21), and the messianic figure anointed with oil of gladness (Heb 1:9, quoting Psa 45:7). The 2Cor 1:21 use is singular: God is said to have anointed "us" — the apostolic community — in the same sentence that speaks of establishing them in Christ. This is the only NT verse where chriō is applied to ordinary believers in a non-messianic frame, and it is the verse most directly relevant to an inner-being interpretation.

The noun chrisma (G5545, 3 occurrences, all in 1 John 2:20 and 2:27) is the most significant term in the entire word set for inner-being purposes. Its STEP meaning describes it as "an anointing, in the reception of spiritual privileges." The LSJ entry records the NT use as "spiritual grace." The two verses describe an anointing that (a) comes from "the Holy One," (b) was "received," (c) "abides in you," and (d) has a cognitive-spiritual function — it "teaches you about everything." This is vocabulary of an inner state: received, resident, and active within the person. If any term in this word set qualifies for inner-being classification, chrisma is it.

The title christos (G5547, about 1,453 occurrences) is the direct Greek translation of ma.shi.ach. In its NT use it functions overwhelmingly as a proper name or messianic title for Jesus. Its CHRIO root etymology is preserved in the LSJ entry, but in actual NT usage christos does not name a state experienced by ordinary persons. Verse records have been omitted by user decision and the term is retained in the inventory for root-family completeness only.

The compound verbs enchriō (G1472, 1 occurrence, Rev 3:18) and epichriō (G2025, 2 occurrences, Joh 9:6 and 9:11) are physical application terms. Enchriō appears in the Laodicean letter where the risen Christ counsels the church to anoint their eyes with salve so that they may see — a metaphorical use where physical application images spiritual perception. Epichriō appears twice in the healing of the blind man: Jesus makes mud and anoints the man's eyes. Both terms involve application to the eyes and both, in their specific verses, carry a connection between physical anointing and the restoration or granting of sight. The somatic link is explicit.

The nominal chrisis is listed in the header term list with no word analysis block, and is not further analysable from the source data.

**Verdict on CHRIO family:** The terms are doing different work across a spectrum from physical application (enchriō, epichriō) to received inner-spiritual quality (chrisma), with chriō occupying a mediating position as the verb of divine conferral. Christos is a title, not a state. The inner-being case for this word rests primarily on chrisma and secondarily on chriō in 2Cor 1:21.

### Root Family: ALEIF (aleifō, aleimma)

The ALEIF root covers general anointing with oil or ointment, distinct from the cultic-consecration sense of MSHCH or the Spirit-conferral sense of CHRIO. The LSJ entry for aleifō describes anointing the skin after bathing, anointing for gymnastic exercise, and anointing the sick. Its NT verse set (9 occurrences) is entirely physical and social in character: fasting practice (Mat 6:17), healing of the sick (Mar 6:13), preparation of Jesus' body (Mar 16:1), and the anointing of Jesus' feet by the weeping woman (Luk 7:38, 7:46, Joh 11:2, Joh 12:3). The James 5:14 use is the most institutionally significant: the elders are to anoint the sick person with oil while praying, linking the physical act to healing and to prayer in the Lord's name.

There is no inner-being state named in the aleifō verse set. The anointing in these passages is an act done to or for a person — it is not a state resident within them. The somatic dimension is prominent (application to feet, head, body, the dead), but this is external somatic action, not a somatic expression of an inner state.

Aleimma appears in the header with no word analysis block and is not further analysable.

**Verdict on ALEIF family:** The terms in this family name a physical action with no direct inner-being function. They are linguistically and functionally distinct from the CHRIO family, though the two families overlap in English translation. The distinction matters for Phase 2.

### Individual terms with no root family assignment

**sukh (H5480, about 9 occurrences)** belongs to the SKH root family, etymologically connected to ya.sakh but treated separately in the source. Its verse set covers personal anointing as a hygienic or mourning-related practice: Ruth 3:3 (self-preparation before meeting Boaz), 2Sa 12:20 (David washing and anointing himself after his child's death — ending mourning), 2Ch 28:15 (the compassionate anointing of captives), and Dan 10:3 (Daniel's fast including abstention from anointing). The 2Sa 12:20 and Dan 10:3 verses are notable: anointing or its absence is a physical marker of an inner state (grief, mourning, fasting) but is not itself the inner state. The SKH family marks the transition point or sign of an inner state rather than the state itself.

**ya.tsaq (H3332, about 50 occurrences)** is a general pouring verb with a broad application range in the source verse set. Most of its verses involve pouring oil on grain offerings or in cultic contexts unrelated to anointing. The two most relevant verses are Gen 28:18 and 35:14 (Jacob pouring oil on stone pillars), 1Sa 10:1 (Samuel pouring oil on Saul's head), and Isa 44:3 (God pouring his Spirit upon offspring). The Isa 44:3 verse is the most significant for inner-being purposes, drawing the action of physical pouring as an image for the Spirit's conferral — a cross-over verse linking the ya.tsaq root to the Spirit theme that dominates the CHRIO family in the NT. The term itself, however, does not name an inner state.

**da.shen (H1878, about 11 occurrences)** is the most peripheral term in the inventory relative to anointing. Its primary meaning is fatness, prosperity, enrichment. The Piel forms include an anointing sense (to make fat, anoint — 1b1) and a cultic sense (to take away ashes from the altar — 1b3). Its verse set is dominated by prosperity language (Deu 31:20, Pro 11:25, 13:4, 15:30, 28:25) with two graphic uses in Isaiah describing the Lord's sword gorged with fat (Isa 34:6–7). The anointing connection is marginal and the term does not operate in inner-being space in any of its listed verses. Pro 13:4 ("the soul of the diligent is richly supplied") uses nephesh vocabulary but the da.shen form here describes abundance rather than an inner state of being anointed.

**ya.sakh (H5258)** and **ba.lal (H1101)** have no word analysis blocks and no verse records in the source data beyond the single Exo 30:32 verse assigned to ya.sakh. They are peripheral mixing and pouring terms and do not contribute to inner-being analysis.

**murizō (G3462, 1 occurrence)** stands alone from the main Greek families. Its one verse (Mar 14:8) is the woman anointing Jesus' body for burial. The LSJ entry describes it as rubbing with ointment. It is a physical application term with no inner-being dimension in its single verse.

**lipainō (G3040)** has no word analysis block and no verse records.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

Several meaningful distinctions emerge between sub-terms that share root families.

Within the MSHCH family, ma.shach and ma.shi.ach do genuinely different work despite deriving from the same root. Ma.shach names the act of anointing as it is performed — it is a verb of agency. Ma.shi.ach names the resulting identity of the one anointed. The verse sets are distinguishable: ma.shach verses tend to be narrative accounts of the act being performed (Samuel anointing Saul, David anointing Solomon), while ma.shi.ach verses tend to be theological and covenantal references to the anointed person's protected and honoured status before God ("touch not my anointed ones," "the Lord saves his anointed"). The difference is functionally real and not merely a labelling distinction. The noun mish.chah occupies a third distinct position as the ritual substance and procedure; it is a cultic-material term with no narrative agency.

Within the CHRIO family, chriō and chrisma are distinguishable in a theologically important direction. Chriō is a verb of divine conferral — it names what God does in appointing Jesus and believers to their tasks. Chrisma is a noun of received possession — it names what the believer now has as a result. The verse sets confirm this: chriō appears in declarative and historical statements (God anointed Jesus, God has anointed us), while chrisma appears in present-tense possession statements (you have an anointing, the anointing abides in you). This is not a near-synonym relationship. The two terms are capturing different moments of the same event: the act of conferral versus the ongoing possession and function of what was conferred.

The distinction between aleifō and chriō in the Greek set is the most significant cross-family distinction. Both are translated "to anoint" but they do different semantic work. Aleifō's verse set is entirely physical and relational: anointing a body, anointing feet, anointing the sick. Chriō's verse set is entirely divine-appointment: God is the agent and the conferral of the Spirit or of task-authority is the content. A reader who encounters "anoint" in English cannot determine from the translation whether the original names a physical act, a social practice, a cultic procedure, or a Spirit-conferral. All four are present in this word set and all four are rendered by the same English word.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The testament_coverage for this word is "both." The differences between the OT and NT portions of the verse set are substantial and go beyond register differences to functional differences in what anointing names.

In the OT verse set, anointing is overwhelmingly a public, outward, and mediated act. Oil is applied by a human agent to another human (or to an object) at God's authorisation. The result is a change of status — the person becomes "the Lord's anointed" — but the locus of the resulting state is social and covenantal rather than inward. Isa 61:1 and 1Sa 16:13 are the two verses that move closest to an inner-being dimension in the OT data, both by juxtaposing the anointing act with the presence or activity of the Spirit. In Isa 61:1 the Spirit's presence is given as the reason for the anointing, suggesting the Spirit's indwelling is the primary reality and the anointing vocabulary describes its effect. In 1Sa 16:13 the sequence is reversed: Samuel anoints David, and then the Spirit rushes upon him from that day. Both verses link anointing to Spirit-conferral but the link is expressed as sequence or rationale, not as identity.

In the NT verse set, the dominant movement is toward internalisation. Chrisma in 1 John 2:20 and 2:27 describes an anointing that was received at a point in time, that now "abides in you," and that has an active teaching function. This is language that places the anointing inside the person as a resident quality with ongoing cognitive and spiritual effects. There is no OT parallel to this use in the source data. Chriō in 2Cor 1:21 applies the same divine-anointing vocabulary to all believers, not only to messianic or priestly figures. The NT data therefore extends the category of those who can be anointed from a select few (king, priest, prophet, the Messiah) to all who belong to Christ, and simultaneously internalises what the anointing is — from a status marker to a resident spiritual quality.

The most significant single difference between the OT and NT verse sets: in the OT, anointing primarily names what is done to a person by another agent and what it makes them in relation to others (their status and role). In the NT, anointing additionally names what a person receives and has within them — a quality resident in the inner person that teaches, abides, and connects the believer to the Holy One. This difference in the direction and location of the state is the key OT/NT contrast in this word set.

A secondary difference is the absence of cultic oil in the NT data. The mish.chah-type vocabulary — anointing as a ritual procedure involving a specified holy oil — has no NT counterpart in this word set. The NT does retain physical anointing (aleifō) but the cultic-material dimension drops away entirely.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

Several gaps in the source data limit the completeness of this analysis.

Seven terms in the header list have no word analysis block in the source file: she.men (H8081), ya.sakh (H5258), ba.lal (H1101), lipainō (G3040), aleimma (G0220), chrisis (G5545B), and effectively christos (G5547) in terms of inner-being analysis. Of these, she.men is the most significant omission. With an estimated occurrence count in the hundreds, she.men is the most common Hebrew word for oil and appears in many anointing contexts. Its absence from the word analysis data means the she.men verse set — which would cover a wide range of oil uses, some with anointing significance and some without — cannot be assessed for inner-being relevance. This gap affects any analysis of how the OT conceptualises oil as a symbol or vehicle of divine action.

Christos (G5547) has been excluded from verse record collection at the researcher's instruction due to its scope as a proper title rather than an inner-being term. This means approximately 1,453 verse references are not assessed. Depending on the outcome of the qualification review, it may be necessary to revisit a subset of christos verses for inner-being relevance (particularly the "in Christ" and "Christ in you" formulations), though these may belong to a separate word or concept rather than this word card.

Mim.shach (H4473) is explicitly marked as having an uncertain meaning in the STEP data. Its one verse (Eze 28:14 — the anointed guardian cherub) is potentially significant for understanding how anointing vocabulary was extended beyond human figures in the OT, but the uncertainty of the term's meaning prevents any firm linguistic conclusions.

The researcher's own comment in the source file notes that anointing may not qualify as an inner-being word and references a deeper analysis file (WS-Word Analysis-Anointing_Analysis 2026-03-01 in Zotero) that was not available for this session. Until that file is reviewed, the qualification assessment in Section 1 must remain provisional. This is the most consequential data gap in the record.
