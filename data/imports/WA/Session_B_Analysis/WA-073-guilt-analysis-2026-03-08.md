## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

This file covers the word *guilt* (registry 073) as a single-part analysis. The term inventory contains sixteen terms — ten Hebrew and six Greek — drawn from the High Confidence source list. The JSON meta records testament coverage as "both."

Provisional qualification: the word *guilt* qualifies as an inner-being word. The qualification is supported at a high confidence level on the following bases. The Hebrew ASHAM family (a.sham verb, a.sham noun, ash.mah, a.shem) names a state of being guilty that includes an internal dimension of realized or incurred guilt, not merely an external legal verdict. The CHATA family (cha.ta, chet, cha.ta.ah) in its Qal forms describes the inward act or state of missing or going wrong, and in its confessional uses — "I have sinned against you" — the subject reports an inner condition, not only an outcome. The Greek suneidesis (conscience) names an explicitly inward faculty whose quality — clear, defiled, seared, good — registers the inner condition of the person in relation to guilt. The Greek hamartia, while often used as a theological category, is also applied to inner propensity and dwelling tendency (Rom 7:17, 7:20). The Greek enochos and katakrima lean primarily toward legal verdicts, but their occurrence alongside other terms in this set supports the set's overall qualification. The confidence level for this word is high but not uniform across all terms: a.von, enochos, and katakrima carry the weakest direct inner-being signals among the sixteen and will require closer examination in Phase 2.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### ASHAM family — a.sham (H0816 verb), a.sham (H0817 noun), ash.mah (H0819), a.shem (H0818)

The ASHAM root carries the basic image of incurring a debt or becoming entangled in an offense, with a strong consequential sense: guilt is not only a state but something that must be borne or discharged. The root generates four distinct terms in this word set, covering the verb (to be guilty, to incur guilt), a noun that doubles as the name of the guilt offering itself (a.sham), an abstract noun for the condition of guiltiness (ash.mah), and an adjective for the guilty person (a.shem).

The verb a.sham (H0816) is used predominantly in contexts where guilt is recognized or realized. The Levitical passages (Lev 4:13, 5:2, 5:3, 5:4) cluster around the moment of recognition — when something hidden becomes known — and the Niphal form introduces the consequence of suffering punishment (Psa 34:21–22). The Hiphil, where present, points toward a declarative or causative use: making someone guilty or declaring guilt. The Hosea passages are striking in volume (five verses) and use the verb in a prophetic register: Ephraim "incurred guilt" through Baal and died (Hos 13:1); Samaria "shall bear her guilt" (Hos 13:16); the heart is false and "they must bear their guilt" (Hos 10:2). In this prophetic use, a.sham describes a guilt that accumulates and must eventually be carried to its consequences. The verb in these contexts does not appear to be merely legal; it names a condition of the subject that connects to what that subject has become through its choices.

The noun a.sham (H0817) is used overwhelmingly for the guilt offering — the cultic mechanism for addressing guilt — and its concentration in Leviticus (seventeen of thirty-four verses) marks it as primarily a liturgical-legal term. Its guilt-condition sense is present in Gen 26:10 (bringing guilt upon a community) and Jer 51:5 (the land full of guilt). Isa 53:10 is particularly significant: the servant's soul makes itself "an offering for guilt" (a.sham), using the noun in both its cultic and personal senses at once. The noun's dominant register, however, is cultic and institutional rather than subjective.

Ash.mah (H0819) is the most community-oriented member of the family. Its verse set concentrates heavily in Chronicles and Ezra, where it names the accumulated guilt of a community or nation (2Ch 24:18, 2Ch 28:13, Ezr 9:6–7, Ezr 10:10). The term in these contexts frames guilt as something that can grow, accumulate, and mount up before God — Ezr 9:6 states "our iniquities have risen higher than our heads, and our guilt has mounted up to the heavens." Ash.mah thus appears to function less as an account of individual inner experience and more as a collective theological description of a people's standing.

A.shem (H0818) is represented by only two verse records in the JSON. Gen 42:21 — "In truth we are guilty concerning our brother" — is notable because the brothers speak this out of their own recognition of inner culpability. 2Sa 14:13 uses the adjective in a judicial argument. With only two verses in the sample, no strong register assessment can be made for this term.

The four ASHAM terms are doing genuinely different work. The verb a.sham moves between realized guilt, borne guilt, and declared guilt. The noun a.sham is primarily cultic. Ash.mah names community-level accumulated guilt. A.shem is the adjectival form with the smallest footprint. All four contribute to an interlocking picture of guilt as a condition that is incurred, recognized, accumulated, ritually addressed, and ultimately carried to consequence. None of the four is a candidate for consolidation with the others; each occupies a distinct register.

### AVON family — a.von (H5771G)

A.von stands alone in its root family in this word set, though it also encompasses the sub-labels "iniquity: guilt" and "iniquity: punishment" which appear in the STEP source header but have no separate sections. The meaning field records all three semantic strands — iniquity as crime, as guilt, and as the consequent punishment — as resident in the same term.

The verse set for a.von (approximately 30 records, noted as a sample from 233 occurrences) reveals a term of exceptional range. At one end, the Genesis passage (Gen 15:16) uses it for an act not yet complete — "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete" — treating a.von as a quantity that accumulates. The Exodus passages use it in the divine self-description: God visits "the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation" (Exo 20:5), and also forgives iniquity (Exo 34:7). Lev 16:21–22 places it at the centre of the Day of Atonement: all the iniquities of Israel are confessed over the scapegoat, which then bears them away — a ritual that treats a.von as something weighty and transferable. The Psalms verses are especially dense with inner-experience language: a.von "gone over my head" like a heavy burden (Psa 38:4), bones wasting away because of iniquity (Psa 31:10), "I was brought forth in iniquity" (Psa 51:5). The Isaiah 53 passages apply a.von to the servant's bearing of corporate iniquity. Lam 5:7 uses it for inherited guilt. Eze 18:20 uses it in the individualisation principle — "the soul who sins shall die."

A.von is singular among the terms in this word set in that its verse set shows all three semantic strands — act, guilt, and punishment — operating in the same surface form, with context alone determining which strand is dominant. This makes a.von the most semantically dense term in the set. It is also the only OT term in this analysis whose verse set explicitly images the inner state with spatial and physical language: guilt rising above one's head, the body weakening, weight that cannot be sustained.

### NASA family — na.sa (H5375J)

Na.sa is a high-frequency verb (approximately 659 occurrences) whose meaning range in the JSON covers lifting, bearing, carrying, and — in the guilt-specific use — forgiving. Its entry into this word set is as the verb for bearing guilt (Lev 16:22's scapegoat "bears all iniquities"), and the STEP source has assigned it the sub-label "to lift: guilt." Only two verse records are present in the JSON (Pro 19:19 and Eze 16:52), both of which concern bearing consequences or disgrace rather than directly naming the lifting of guilt.

Given the SMALL_VERSE_SAMPLE flag, no reliable register assessment is possible for na.sa from the current data. What can be observed is that the term functions as the verb of guilt-transference — the mechanism by which guilt is moved from one party to another — and this places it in the same semantic field as the Day of Atonement language in the a.von section. Whether na.sa consistently carries this inner-transfer sense or whether the guilt-bearing uses are a small subset of a much wider lifting vocabulary cannot be determined from two verses. This term requires a fuller verse set review in Phase 2.

### CHEN family — chin.nam (H2600)

Chin.nam belongs to the root family CHEN, the grace-and-favour group, and its inclusion in this word set requires clarification. The data quality flag notes that chin.nam's guilt-relevance is indirect: it denotes absence of legitimate cause, and thus the absence of guilt in those who act against the speaker. In the verse set, chin.nam covers a wide range: working without wages (Gen 29:15, Jer 22:13), receiving something at no cost (Exo 21:2), being persecuted without cause (Psa 35:7, 35:19, 69:4), and God acting with stated reasons (Eze 6:10, 14:23).

The guilt-register of chin.nam lies entirely in the "without cause" cluster. When someone hates without cause (Psa 69:4), lies in wait without reason (Pro 1:11), or sheds blood without cause (1Ki 2:31, 1Sa 19:5), chin.nam frames the victim's innocence and the perpetrator's groundless action. The term does not describe an inner state of guilt in the person using it; it locates guilt's absence. The god_as_subject flag is true for this term because in Eze 6:10 and Eze 14:23, God speaks of having "not acted without cause" — affirming that divine judgment is not groundless. This use is theologically significant: it places God as the subject who acts with cause, implicitly asserting that the accused bear real guilt.

The most notable guilt-adjacent verse in the chin.nam set may be 1Sa 25:31, where Abigail uses the term in the context of conscience: David should not have cause of grief or "pangs of conscience" for shedding blood without cause. This verse links the absence of chin.nam (groundless action) directly to the inner experience of relief from a guilt burden, suggesting that chin.nam, while not an inner-being term itself, interacts closely with terms that are.

Verdict: chin.nam is a peripheral member of this word set. Its guilt-relevance is structural (it names the absence of legitimate guilt) rather than experiential. It is not a candidate for inner-being classification in its own right, but it provides a conceptual boundary marker for what constitutes genuine guilt.

### CHATA family — cha.ta.ah (H2401), chet (H2399), cha.ta (H2398)

The CHATA root carries the primary image of missing a mark — failing to reach the intended target — and this physical image underlies the entire semantic range of sin, guilt, and guilt-bearing that the three terms express. The family generates both a verb (cha.ta, H2398) and two nouns (cha.ta.ah H2401 and chet H2399), with the verb being by far the largest in occurrence count.

Cha.ta (H2398) is the most action-oriented and the most consistently interpersonal of the three. Its verse set of forty records spans the entire Hebrew canon from Genesis to the minor prophets. The confessional uses are particularly numerous: "I have sinned" (Exo 9:27 — Pharaoh; 1Sa 15:24 — Saul; 2Sa 12:13 — David; Num 21:7 — Israel; Dan 9:5 — Daniel's corporate confession). In each case, the statement is voiced by a person or community recognizing their own condition. Cha.ta is therefore the primary verb of moral self-recognition in this word set. The Hiphil forms are also significant: cha.ta in the causative means to induce another to sin — "he made Israel to sin" (1Ki 14:16, 2Ki 17:21, and repeatedly in the Kings material). This causative use, combined with the god_as_subject flag (Gen 20:6, where God keeps Abimelech from sinning), marks cha.ta as a term whose inner-state is both experienced by a subject and potentially produced in another subject by an external agent.

Psa 51:4 — "Against you, you only, have I sinned" — is particularly notable: cha.ta is used here to describe the relational orientation of sin, naming God as the ultimate object against whom every act of sin is directed. This verse also names the inner experience as something that must be acknowledged before God can be justified. Psa 4:4 uses cha.ta in a restraint context: "Be angry, and do not sin" — treating cha.ta as an outcome to be avoided even when the underlying emotion (anger) is present.

Chet (H2399) functions predominantly as a noun of incurred guilt-state rather than of action. The Deuteronomy cluster (Deu 15:9, 23:21–22, 24:15, 24:16) uses chet in conditional warnings: if you do this, you will incur chet; if you refrain, you will not incur chet. This conditional framing treats chet as a guilt-status that attaches to a person and must be avoided. Isa 1:18 uses chet in a colour metaphor — "though your sins are like scarlet" — suggesting accumulated visible stain. Isa 53:12 uses chet for the servant's bearing of the sin of many. Lam 3:39 uses it in the context of bearing punishment for sin. Chet thus sits between the action-sense of cha.ta and the more abstract guilt-as-accumulated-debt sense of a.von.

Cha.ta.ah (H2401) is the smallest member of the CHATA family in this set (eight verse records). Its concentration in the Exodus golden calf narrative (Exo 32:21, 30, 31) as "great sin" — a massive collective failure — suggests it tends to name sin of unusual magnitude or communal weight. Psa 32:1 — "whose sin is covered" — uses cha.ta.ah in the context of forgiveness and relief, pairing it with the covered-over state. Psa 109:7 uses it for a prayer counted as sin. The noun appears to double as "sin offering" in cultic contexts (Psa 40:6), adding the same cultic ambiguity present in a.sham.

The three CHATA terms are doing related but distinct work. Cha.ta is the verb of missed-mark action and voiced self-recognition, with both causative and God-as-subject forms. Chet is the noun of incurred guilt-status with strong conditionality. Cha.ta.ah names sin of particular magnitude or collective weight, and doubles in cultic language for the sin offering. They are not candidates for consolidation; each occupies a distinct usage pattern, though their overlap in some verses (Exo 32:30 uses both cha.ta verb and cha.ta.ah noun in adjacent phrases) shows that the root family is deployed as an integrated field.

### AITIA family — aitia (G0156), aitios (G0159)

The Greek AITIA root carries the classical sense of responsibility, causation, and fault. In the NT verse set, aitia spans a wide range: cause or reason in a neutral sense (Act 10:21, 2Ti 1:6, Heb 2:11), formal legal charge (Mat 27:37, Mar 15:26, Act 23:28, 25:18, 25:27), and absence of guilt (Joh 18:38, 19:4, 19:6 — Pilate's three declarations "I find no guilt in him"). The triple Johannine use of aitia in the trial narrative is the most guilt-significant cluster in this term's verse set: Pilate's repeated use of aitia as the standard for acquittal frames guilt as something that must be established through charge and evidence, not merely assumed.

Aitios (G0159) follows the same register in five verses. Three are from Luke's Passion narrative (23:4, 14, 22) — Pilate again finding "no guilt" in Jesus, using aitios as the formal verdict term. Heb 5:9 uses aitios for Christ as the "source" (causer) of eternal salvation, a non-guilt use of the root. Act 19:40 uses it for an inability to give cause for a civic disturbance.

The AITIA family is primarily a judicial and causal vocabulary. Its guilt content lies in the negative: these terms are deployed most densely in the NT to establish that a specific person (Jesus) bears no guilt charge. The root does not describe the inner experience of guilt; it names the forensic category. In this respect the AITIA family resembles enochos and katakrima more than it resembles suneidesis or hamartia.

Verdict: aitia and aitios are doing the same essential work in different grammatical forms — noun and adjective respectively. Both are judicial-forensic terms describing guilt as charge or attribution. They are near-synonyms in their actual NT deployment and could be treated as a single entry in Phase 2 analysis, with their differentiation being grammatical rather than semantic.

### SUNEIDESIS family — suneidesis (G4893)

Suneidesis is the only term in the entire word set — OT or NT — that names an inner faculty by which guilt is registered, rather than naming the guilt itself or the act that produces it. Its root in suneidesis includes the sense of shared knowledge, awareness, and moral consciousness, and the LSJ entry notes its meaning range from shared information through to inner consciousness, conscience, and complicity.

The verse set of twenty-nine records is dominated by Pauline and Hebrews usage. Paul's use falls into two patterns. The first is personal testimony: "I have lived my life before God in all good conscience" (Act 23:1), "my conscience bears me witness" (Rom 9:1), the conscience as witness to integrity (2Cor 1:12). The second is pastoral-communal: the conscience of a weaker believer that can be wounded (1Cor 8:7, 10, 12), the conscience that regulates food practices (1Cor 10:25–29), the conscience as the inner basis for civic obedience (Rom 13:5). The Pastoral Epistles add a further pattern: the conscience as something that can be maintained clearly (1Ti 1:5, 1:19, 3:9, 2Ti 1:3) or become seared and defiled (1Ti 4:2, Tit 1:15).

The Hebrews material is the most directly relevant to guilt: "gifts and sacrifices are offered that cannot perfect the conscience of the worshiper" (Heb 9:9); "purify our conscience from dead works" (Heb 9:14); worshipers "would no longer have any consciousness of sins" once truly cleansed (Heb 10:2); "hearts sprinkled clean from an evil conscience" (Heb 10:22). These Hebrews verses treat the conscience as the locus where guilt residue persists and where genuine atonement must make its mark.

1Pe 3:21 adds the forensic-inner connection: baptism is "an appeal to God for a good conscience" — not external cleansing but an inner clearing of the faculty that registers guilt.

Suneidesis uniquely names guilt as an ongoing inner registration, capable of being more or less clear, capable of defilement, capable of being purified, and capable of bearing witness. This distinguishes it sharply from every other term in this word set, all of which name guilt as act, state, charge, or condition rather than as the faculty that detects it.

### HAMARTIA family — hamartia (G0266)

Hamartia is the central NT sin-and-guilt term by occurrence count (approximately 172 times) and by theological weight. Its root image — missing the mark — parallels the Hebrew CHATA root, and some scholars treat hamartia as the NT functional equivalent of cha.ta, though the verse sets reveal meaningful differences in register.

The twenty-four verse records cover several distinct usages. Hamartia is used for specific acts (Mat 1:21 — "save his people from their sins"), for an enslaving power (Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin"), for a convicting function that the Spirit performs (Joh 16:8–9), for a universal condition of all people (Rom 3:9 — "all are under sin"), for an indwelling agent (Rom 7:17 — "sin that dwells within me"), for a law or principle in conflict with the law of the mind (Rom 8:2), for an act of commission (1Cor 15:3), and for an atoning bearing by the sinless Christ (2Cor 5:21, Heb 9:26). 1Joh uses hamartia for regular practice (3:4, 3:8), for all wrongdoing (5:17), and for the state that Christ's blood cleanses (1:7).

The most distinctive feature of hamartia in comparison to the OT terms is its personification as an indwelling power or agent in Romans (especially 7:17, 7:20, 8:2–3). The OT terms do not use this personified-power register. Hamartia "dwells," "has dominion," and is something to be "condemned in the flesh" — language that treats the inner condition of guilt-generating tendency as a quasi-personal force rather than a specific act or recognized state.

Hamartia also has a unique NT use in Joh 9:41 where guilt is tied to knowledge: "if you were blind, you would have no guilt; but now that you say 'We see,' your guilt remains." Here hamartia functions as something that attaches specifically where knowledge is present and denied — an epistemically-conditioned guilt, which has no direct parallel among the OT terms surveyed.

### ENOCHOS family — enochos (G1777)

Enochos is a legal term meaning "held in" or "bound by," used in the NT for liability and deserving punishment. Its eight verse records include the Sermon on the Mount's graduated liability clauses (Mat 5:21–22 — liable to judgment, to the council, to hell of fire), the trial condemnation passages (Mat 26:66, Mar 14:64 — "deserving death"), the unique Markan usage "guilty of an eternal sin" (Mar 3:29), the Eucharistic guilt passage (1Cor 11:27 — "guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord"), the lifelong-slavery passage (Heb 2:15 — "subject to lifelong slavery"), and the whole-law passage (Jas 2:10 — "guilty of all of it").

Enochos describes guilt as legal entailment: to be enochos is to be bound to a consequence. The inner-being dimension is present but thin. Mar 3:29's "guilty of an eternal sin" uses enochos to name an ongoing state of guilt that cannot be discharged, which is the closest this term comes to an inner-state characterisation. 1Cor 11:27 is notable because it locates guilt in the manner of participation — an unworthy approach to the Lord's Supper creates enochos — which implies an inner condition of the participant rather than merely an external offence. Jas 2:10's "guilty of all" uses enochos for the totality of law-obligation: failing at one point entangles the person in all of it.

Enochos is a boundary case for inner-being qualification. Its primary register is judicial entailment, but several uses suggest that enochos names a condition the person is in rather than only a verdict issued over them.

### KATAKRIMA family — katakrima (G2631)

Katakrima is the most forensic term in the entire word set. Its three verse records (all in Romans 5–8) place it in the context of universal condemnation under sin (Rom 5:16, 5:18) and its reversal in Christ (Rom 8:1 — "no condemnation"). The term names a condemning sentence or judgment, not the inner experience of guilt or the act that produced it. Its significance in this word set is structural: katakrima appears as the formal verdict that guilt generates and that atonement removes.

Rom 8:1 is the most inner-being adjacent use: "no katakrima for those in Christ Jesus" implies a change of the person's standing before God that has an inner counterpart — but katakrima itself names the standing, not the inner experience. Of the sixteen terms in this set, katakrima is the most clearly judicial and the least directly experiential. Whether it belongs in the inner-being word set or functions primarily as a theological frame-term for other words in the set is a question for Phase 2.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

Three root families in this word set contain multiple terms with meaningful divergences in their verse sets.

**ASHAM family.** The most significant divergence is between the verb a.sham (H0816) and the noun a.sham (H0817). The verb's verse set is primarily cultic-Levitical and prophetic, focused on the process of recognizing, incurring, or bearing guilt as a condition. The noun's verse set is primarily cultic-institutional, focused on the guilt offering as a ritual mechanism. The overlap in the golden calf narrative and Isa 53:10 shows the two can co-occur in the same conceptual space, but the verb consistently does more work in the register of inner recognition, while the noun does more work in the register of cultic resolution. Ash.mah and a.shem are both less well-attested in the verse set, but ash.mah clearly concentrates in the register of community-level accumulated guilt (Chronicles, Ezra, Lamentations) while a.sham the verb operates across individual and collective registers. A.shem's two verse records are insufficient for a reliable comparison, but Gen 42:21 — the brothers' recognition "we are guilty" — places a.shem at the moment of dawning personal recognition.

**CHATA family.** Cha.ta (H2398) and chet (H2399) are the most divergent pair. Cha.ta in its verb form consistently appears in confessional, narrative, and relational contexts — it is what persons say they have done, often in first-person declaration. Chet in its noun form appears more frequently in conditional legal warnings (Deuteronomy) and prophetic declarations of burden: "though your sins are like scarlet" (Isa 1:18), "bear their sin" (Isa 53:12). Chet is less the verb of action and more the noun of accumulated stain or incurred liability. The two share the same root but function at different stages: cha.ta at the point of action and recognition, chet at the point of accumulated burden and consequence. Cha.ta.ah (H2401) occupies a middle position, appearing in the context of massive or collective sin events (the golden calf) and in cultic language for the sin offering. The difference between cha.ta.ah and chet is subtle from the verse set alone and may primarily reflect scope: cha.ta.ah tends to appear with emphatic or communal force ("a great sin"), while chet appears more neutrally for individual guilt-incursion.

**AITIA family.** Aitia and aitios operate in the same semantic space with minimal divergence — the former as a noun, the latter as an adjective. Both appear most prominently in the Passion trial narrative asserting Jesus's innocence. The one distinction visible in the verse set is that aitios in Heb 5:9 uses the root in the neutral sense of "cause/source" (Christ as source of salvation) rather than in the guilt register. Aitia does not show a comparable non-guilt use in this verse set. This difference is too thin to support a strong verdict but suggests that aitios retains slightly more semantic breadth than aitia in NT usage.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The word *guilt* has testament_coverage of "both," and the differences between the OT and NT verse sets are substantial.

**What the OT vocabulary does that the NT does not.** The OT verse set contains the full range of guilt as a cultic-ritual category. The guilt offering (a.sham H0817), the scapegoat bearing iniquity (a.von via the Lev 16 passage, na.sa in the transference sense), the sin offering (cha.ta.ah in its cultic use), and the priestly atonement procedures — all of these occupy the OT side of the set and have no direct NT equivalent among the terms surveyed. The OT also contains the most explicit imagery of guilt as a spatial and physical burden: iniquity "gone over my head" (Psa 38:4), guilt "mounting up to the heavens" (Ezr 9:6), bones wasting because of iniquity (Psa 31:10). These spatial and somatic images are largely absent from the NT terms in this set, though Hebrews engages with them conceptually when discussing the conscience being perfected.

The OT also contains the generational and communal dimensions of guilt most fully. A.von is "visited upon the children to the third and fourth generation" (Exo 20:5, Num 14:18); iniquities are confessed as belonging to "us and our fathers" (Ezr 9:7, Lev 26:40); Lam 5:7 states bluntly "Our fathers sinned, and are no more; and we bear their iniquities." The NT verse set does not use guilt language in this transgenerational direction (Rom 5:12's "all sinned" is individual-universal rather than generational-familial).

**What the NT vocabulary does that the OT does not.** The NT introduces suneidesis — a term with no direct OT parallel in this word set — naming guilt as a registered inner condition in an identifiable inner faculty. The conscience can be clear, defiled, good, evil, seared, or purified. This faculty-language is absent from the OT terms, which name guilt as an act, a state, a burden, or a category but not as something registered in a named inner instrument.

The NT also introduces the personification of sin as an indwelling power (hamartia in Romans 7) — sin that dwells, dominates, and must be condemned in the flesh. The OT terms do not personify sin in this way; they treat it as act, guilt, and consequence. The NT hamartia verse set further introduces guilt as tied to knowledge and self-claim (Joh 9:41), and guilt as a state whose reversal is named as forensic verdict (katakrima in Rom 8:1).

The AITIA family (aitia and aitios) appears only in the NT and primarily in the Passion trial narrative. The OT has no equivalent to this juridical exculpatory register within this word set — there is no OT term in this set that functions primarily to establish the absence of charge against a named individual.

**The single most significant difference.** The OT verse set treats guilt primarily as a condition that is incurred, borne, accumulated, and resolved through cultic mechanisms. The NT verse set shifts the primary register: guilt is treated as a condition that is recognized through an inner faculty (conscience), declared removed through forensic verdict (katakrima), and whose root power is an indwelling agent to be condemned and displaced (hamartia). The movement is from ritual mechanism to inner faculty and forensic resolution. The OT mechanisms and the NT register do not contradict each other — several NT verses (notably in Hebrews) bridge them explicitly — but the primary explanatory frameworks differ between the two Testaments' portions of this word set.

## Section 6 — Open Questions

1. 2. [TERM: a.sham] In Isa 53:10, the servant's soul makes an offering for guilt (a.sham H0817) — does this verse use the noun in a cultic sense, a personal sense, or both simultaneously, and what does this dual resonance indicate about where guilt-resolution was understood to take place?

3. [ROOT: a.von] Does the spatial and physical imaging of a.von — guilt rising above one's head, bones wasting — constitute a somatic connection in the inner-being sense (the inner state of guilt producing or being expressed through the body), and if so, how does this compare to the OT imaging of other inner-being words in the Programme?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to decide whether spatially-imaged guilt language in the Psalms represents genuine somatic expression of the inner guilt state or conventional hyperbole, and how this affects classification of a.von.*

4. [TERM: na.sa] What is the full verse distribution of na.sa (H5375J) in its guilt-bearing and guilt-transference uses, and does that distribution support treating na.sa as a distinct inner-being term or as a general mechanical verb for guilt-movement between parties?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to determine whether guilt-transference via na.sa involves any inner-state dimension in the bearer, or whether na.sa in guilt contexts functions purely as a legal and ritual mechanism with no inner-being component.*

5. [TERM: chin.nam] The verse at 1Sa 25:31 links chin.nam (acting without cause) to "pangs of conscience" being avoided — does this verse indicate that chin.nam was understood in the OT as having a direct relationship to the inner experience of guilt, and are there other chin.nam verses that show a similar connection?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to decide whether chin.nam's role in defining groundless guilt attribution gives it a secondary inner-being function, or whether its classification should remain peripheral to this word set.*

6. [ROOT: a.sham] The ASHAM verb (H0816) has causative_form_present marked true — what is the full distribution of the Hiphil causative uses of a.sham, and does causing guilt in another person imply that guilt is treated as an inner state that can be produced externally?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to address whether causative guilt-induction implies that guilt is understood as an inner state susceptible to external causation, which would affect the model's account of guilt's inner-being status.*

7. [ROOT: cha.ta] The CHATA verb (H2398) has both causative_form_present and god_as_subject marked true — in Gen 20:6, God prevents Abimelech from sinning; in the Kings passages, kings cause Israel to sin. What does the causative use of cha.ta reveal about the understanding of sin and guilt as inner states that can be guarded against or induced by an external agent?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to decide how to account for guilt-states that are caused or prevented by agents external to the person who bears them, including divine agency, without reducing guilt to a purely externally-determined category.*

8. [TERM: chin.nam] The god_as_subject flag is true for chin.nam (H2600): in Eze 6:10 and Eze 14:23, God declares that the divine judgment was not without cause. Does the use of chin.nam in God's own speech have a different inner-being valence than its use in human complaint, and what does it indicate about the OT understanding of divine action and guilt attribution?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to address whether chin.nam in divine speech functions as a guilt-attribution term in a meaningful inner-being sense, or whether it operates purely in the register of divine justification.*

9. [OT/NT] The OT verse set contains extensive cultic-ritual mechanisms for guilt-resolution (guilt offering, scapegoat, priestly atonement) while the NT verse set does not contain these mechanisms but does contain their reinterpretation (notably in Hebrews). Does the shift from OT ritual mechanism to NT forensic verdict (katakrima) and inner-faculty registration (suneidesis) represent a change in where guilt-resolution was understood to occur — in the external ritual or in the inner person?

10. [OT/NT] The OT verse set contains spatially-imaged guilt (guilt mounting above one's head, bones wasting because of iniquity) while the NT verse set contains the personification of sin as an indwelling agent (hamartia in Romans 7). Are these two registers describing the same inner dynamic — guilt as something that presses on or inhabits the person — in different conceptual idioms, or are they genuinely different accounts of what guilt does to the person?

11. [OT/NT] Suneidesis (conscience) has no OT parallel in this word set. Is there an OT term or combination of terms that functions as a rough equivalent to the inner-faculty register of suneidesis, or does the OT treat the registration of guilt entirely through different conceptual instruments?

12. [OT/NT] The generational transmission of guilt (a.von visited on children, iniquities of the fathers confessed by later generations) is prominent in the OT verse set and absent from the NT verse set. Does this absence represent a genuine theological difference in how guilt's scope was understood in the two Testaments, or is the NT silence on this point a feature of the particular terms selected for this word set?

13. [TERM: hamartia] Rom 7:17 — "sin that dwells within me" — treats hamartia as a personal agent inhabiting the person rather than an act the person commits. Does this personification of hamartia shift it from an inner-being term (naming a state the person is in) to a separate-agent term (naming a power that acts in the person), and how should the inner-being model categorize an inner state that behaves as an agent?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to decide how to classify a term that names both an inner state (guilt-generating tendency) and a personified agent (sin that dwells), since the two uses may require different classification rules.*

14. [TERM: hamartia] Joh 9:41 makes guilt (hamartia) conditional on knowledge and self-claim: "now that you say 'We see,' your guilt remains." Does this epistemically-conditioned guilt use of hamartia have any OT parallel in this word set, or is it a distinctively NT register?

15. [TERM: suneidesis] The Hebrews material treats the conscience as something that cultic sacrifice cannot perfect (Heb 9:9) but that Christ's blood can purify (Heb 9:14). Does suneidesis in these verses function as a site where guilt residue persists until genuine atonement acts on it, and what does this indicate about the relationship between the inner faculty and the inner state of guilt?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to account for whether suneidesis is the site where guilt is registered, the state that guilt produces, or the faculty that monitors and reports on guilt — and whether these are the same thing or three distinct functions.*

16. [TERM: enochos] Whether enochos (G1777) qualifies as an inner-being term depends on whether being "bound to a consequence" includes an inner dimension. Mar 3:29 ("guilty of an eternal sin") and 1Cor 11:27 (guilty in the manner of one's approach to the Lord's Supper) suggest an inner condition; Mat 5:22 (liable to judgment for anger) connects enochos to an inner state (anger) as its basis. Is enochos describing the inner condition that produces legal liability, the legal liability itself, or are these inseparable in its NT use?

17. [TERM: katakrima] Rom 8:1 — "no condemnation for those in Christ Jesus" — uses katakrima as a forensic verdict whose removal has apparent inner-experience correlates. Does the removal of katakrima involve a change in the inner state of the person, and if so, does this give katakrima inner-being relevance beyond its primary judicial register?
*Phase 2 flag: The inner-being model will need to decide whether a forensic verdict term qualifies for inner-being classification when its removal is described as experientially significant, or whether katakrima should be classified as a frame-term that conditions but does not itself name an inner state.*

18. Does Phase 2 analysis need to treat these as one inner-being word with multiple registers, or as a cluster of related but distinct inner states that require separate tracking?
