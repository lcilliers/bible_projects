## Section 2: Semantic Characterisation

### 2.1 The Foundation — Gen 2:25 and the Original Shameless State

Before examining the shame vocabulary's breadth, its most foundational statement must be read: **Gen 2:25 — "And the man and his wife were both naked and were not ashamed."**

This verse does not appear in the shame registry's extracted terms — it appears in the bosh (H0954) verse list. Reading it in context of all 113 bosh verses, it functions as the programme's zero-point for shame: the pre-fall condition is defined by the *absence* of shame. The man and woman were naked — fully exposed, nothing concealed from God or each other — and experienced no shame. This is the original condition: complete transparency without vulnerability, complete exposure without self-protection.

The fall reverses this immediately. Gen 3 (not in this export's bosh verses directly, but the structural background) introduces shame as the first consequence of disobedience: they knew they were naked and hid. The entire shame vocabulary of the Old and New Testaments is the unfolding of what happened between Gen 2:25 and Gen 3 — and what God does to reverse it.

### 2.2 The Primary Shame Verb — Bosh

**H0954 bosh (113 verses)** is the shame vocabulary's primary term and the most analytically important single entry. Reading all 113 verses establishes four major bosh patterns:

**Pattern 1 — Trust and non-shame.** The most concentrated bosh cluster in the Psalter: Psa 22:5 — "to you they cried and were rescued; in you they trusted and were not put to shame." Psa 25:2-3 — "let me not be put to shame... none who wait for you shall be put to shame." Psa 31:1 — "let me never be put to shame." Psa 119:6 — "then I shall not be put to shame, having my eyes fixed on all your commandments." The structural pattern is invariant: trust in God → not put to shame. Shame, in this register, is what happens when the object of trust fails. The vocabulary establishes trust as the antidote to shame — the person who trusts God will not be exposed as having trusted wrongly.

The inverse is equally consistent: idolaters are put to shame (Isa 42:17; 44:9,11; 45:16 — all bosh + idols). Psa 97:7 — "all worshippers of images are put to shame." Shame falls on the person whose trust object fails to deliver. The trust-shame connection is one of the most structurally consistent patterns in the entire programme to date.

**Pattern 2 — The loss of the capacity for shame.** The most analytically significant bosh finding: Jer 6:15 and Jer 8:12 — identical text, the same indictment repeated: *"Were they ashamed when they committed abomination? No, they were not at all ashamed; they did not know how to blush."* Reading this against all 113 bosh verses, this is the most disturbing statement in the shame vocabulary. The loss of shame — specifically, the loss of the *capacity* to blush — is named as the deepest moral disorder, more severe than the shame itself. The person who commits abomination and feels appropriate shame is disordered but recoverable; the person who commits abomination and *cannot blush* has lost the moral compass entirely.

This observation resolves a question latent in the programme since the conscience registry: shame and conscience are functionally linked. The person who cannot blush is the person whose conscience has been seared (1Ti 4:2 — the correlate in the NT).

**Pattern 3 — Eschatological shame removal.** Reading the prophetic corpus: Isa 45:17 — "Israel is saved by the Lord with everlasting salvation; you shall not be put to shame or confounded to all eternity." Joe 2:26-27 — "you shall eat in plenty and be satisfied... and my people shall never again be put to shame." Zep 3:11 — "on that day you shall not be put to shame because of the deeds by which you have rebelled against me." The eschatological promise uses bosh: God's final act toward his people includes the permanent removal of shame. This is the programme's most important eschatological shame-statement: not merely that shame will be endured, but that it will end.

**Pattern 4 — Productive shame.** Eze 43:10-11 — "describe to the house of Israel the temple, that they may be ashamed of their iniquities... and if they are ashamed of all that they have done, make known to them the design of the temple." Shame here is the *precondition* for the revelation of God's design. The people must first be ashamed before they receive the temple plan — shame as the prerequisite for restoration. Jer 31:19 — "after I was instructed, I struck my thigh; I was ashamed, and I was also humiliated." This is Ephraim's repentance-speech: shame + striking the thigh = authentic turning. The two-forms structure begins to emerge: shame that leads to repentance and restoration is productive shame; shame that leads to hiding and paralysis is destructive.

### 2.3 The Reproach Vocabulary — Cher.pah, Cha.raph, Ka.lam

These three terms together cover the social/relational dimension of shame — shame as it is inflicted, received, and borne in community.

**H2781 cher.pah (72 verses) — reproach as carried shame.** The largest shame vocabulary aside from bosh. Reading all 72: cher.pah is shame that is *borne* over time — reproach carried as a social burden. Gen 30:23 — Rachel: "God has taken away my reproach." Jos 5:9 — "today I have rolled away the reproach of Egypt from you." Reproach *removed* is as significant as reproach inflicted. Psa 69:20 — "reproaches have broken my heart, so that I am in despair." Reproach-as-heartbreak: the most intense cher.pah statement. Isa 25:8 — "the Lord God will wipe away tears from all faces, and the reproach of his people he will take away from all the earth." The eschatological removal of cher.pah parallels the eschatological removal of bosh. Dan 12:2 — "some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt." Everlasting shame: the ultimate eschatological judgment expressed as permanent reproach.

The programmatically critical cher.pah verses are those where God's reproach is borne vicariously: Psa 69:7 — "for it is for your sake that I have borne reproach, that dishonour has covered my face." Psa 69:9 — "the reproaches of those who reproach you have fallen on me." The psalmist bears God's reproach — the shame directed at God lands on the one who is aligned with God. This is the OT anticipation of the Servant's shame and of Heb 13:13 ("let us go to him outside the camp and bear the reproach he endured").

**H2778A cha.raph (40 verses) — the taunt that confronts.** Reading all 40: cha.raph is the active, aggressive form of shame — the taunt that dares, defies, or mocks. The most concentrated cha.raph cluster: 1Sa 17 — Goliath's taunt of Israel (vv. 10,25,26,36,45), met by David's counter-claim in the name of the Lord. The taunt activates courage: David's response to Goliath's cha.raph is not shame-paralysis but righteous anger that becomes decisive action. 2Ki 19:22 — Sennacherib taunting the Lord: "Whom have you mocked and reviled? Against whom have you raised your voice?" — the taunt directed against God himself. Psa 69:9 — "the reproaches (cha.raph) of those who reproach you have fallen on me" — God's shame fallen on the one who loves God.

The cha.raph vocabulary's most important structural observation: the taunt reveals the theology of the taunter. Goliath's taunt expresses his theological assumption (Israel's God cannot deliver); Sennacherib's taunt expresses his (no god has saved any nation from Assyria). Both taunts are answered by God's decisive action. The shame-taunt in the OT is consistently the prelude to divine vindication.

**H3637 ka.lam (38 verses) — humiliation as exposure.** Reading all 38: ka.lam is shame experienced as being publicly diminished, made small, or exposed. The most analytically significant ka.lam verse: Jer 6:15 / 8:12 (shared with bosh) — "they did not know how to blush" — ka.lam + bosh together naming the full loss of shame-capacity. Eze 43:10 — "that they may be ashamed (ka.lam) of their iniquities" — productive humiliation as the precondition for restoration. Isa 50:7 — "the Lord God helps me; therefore I have not been disgraced (ka.lam); therefore I have set my face like a flint." The ka.lam vocabulary includes divine protection from disgrace: God's help means the person need not be humiliated.

### 2.4 The Shame-Noun Cluster — Bo.shet, Ke.lim.mah

**H1322 bo.shet (29 verses) and H3639 ke.lim.mah (29 verses)** — the two primary shame-nouns, covering shame as a substantive condition rather than a verbal act.

Bo.shet reading: Psa 44:15 — "all day long my disgrace (bo.shet) is before me, and shame has covered my face." Isa 61:7 — "instead of your shame (bo.shet) there shall be a double portion." The double-portion-for-shame promise is among the most striking shame-reversals in the corpus: the shame does not merely depart but is replaced by double abundance. Jer 3:24-25 — "the shameful thing (bo.shet) has devoured all for which our fathers laboured... let us lie down in our shame (bo.shet)." Bo.shet as the devouring shame that consumes the community's inheritance — shame as spiritual poverty.

Most analytically significant bo.shet observation: in certain contexts, bo.shet replaces the name Baal (compare "Ishboshet" — man of shame — replacing an original Baal-name, and "Mephiboshet"). The scribal practice of substituting bo.shet for Baal in personal names is the most concentrated expression of the shame-idolatry connection: worshipping Baal is the paradigmatic shame-producing act.

Ke.lim.mah reading: Psa 4:2 — "O men, how long shall my honour be turned into shame (ke.lim.mah)?" The honour-shame inversion as the defining question of the psalm. Isa 50:6 — "I gave my back to those who strike, and my cheeks to those who pull out the beard; I hid not my face from disgrace (ke.lim.mah) and spitting." The Servant's ke.lim.mah — accepted shame as the condition of redemptive suffering. Eze 39:26 — "they shall forget their shame (ke.lim.mah) and all the treachery they have practised against me, when they dwell securely." Shame forgotten in restored security — the condition of eschatological peace includes the forgetting of shame.

### 2.5 Dishonour and Contempt — Qa.lon, Buz, Bu.shah

**H7036 qa.lon (17 verses) — dishonour as the prize of folly.** Reading all 17: qa.lon is shame as the opposite of honour in the wisdom tradition. Pro 3:35 — "the wise will inherit honour, but fools get disgrace (qa.lon)." Pro 11:2 — "when pride comes, then comes disgrace (qa.lon), but with the humble is wisdom." The wisdom honour-shame pair: honour belongs to the wise and humble; disgrace to the proud and foolish. Hos 4:7 — "the more they increased, the more they sinned against me; I will change their glory into shame (qa.lon)." God's judicial shame: the divine reversal of glory into qa.lon as covenantal judgment.

**H0937 buz (11 verses) — contempt as the social register of shame.** Reading all 11: buz is the shame vocabulary's most interpersonal term — contempt as what the person receives from others. Job 31:34 — "I stood in great fear of the multitude, and the contempt (buz) of families terrified me." Psa 123:3-4 — "we have had more than enough of contempt (buz)... the contempt of the proud." The cry for relief from contempt — too much of it, accumulated contempt that overwhelms. Psa 107:40 — "he pours contempt on princes" — God's exercise of contempt as judgment on the arrogant. The contempt vocabulary establishes a divine axis: God pours contempt on those who have contempt for others.

**H0955 bu.shah (4 verses):** Eze 7:18 — "shame is on all faces, and baldness on all their heads" — the somatic expression of collective shame. Obd 10 — "shame shall cover you, and you shall be cut off forever." Bu.shah as the shame that *covers* — the body-covering image.

### 2.6 The NT Shame Vocabulary

**G2617 kataischunō (12 verses) — to dishonour/put to shame.** The primary NT shame verb. Reading all 12: Rom 5:5 — "hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit." The shame-free hope — the hope anchored in divine love does not ultimately expose as misplaced trust. Rom 9:33 / 10:11 — "everyone who believes in him will not be put to shame" — the Isa 28:16 quotation applied to faith in Christ. Faith as the trust-antidote to shame, directly parallel to the bosh/trust pattern in the Psalter. 1Co 1:27 — "God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the wise; God chose what is weak in the world to shame the strong." Divine reversal: the cross is God's kataischunō of the world's honour-shame system.

**G0152 aischunē (7 verses) and G0153 aischunō (7 verses) — shame noun and verb.** Heb 12:2 — "despising the shame (aischunēn)" — the anchor verse. The aischunō/aischunē cluster covers: Phi 3:19 — "they glory in their shame (aischunē)" — the most inverted shame-statement in the NT: people whose value-system has been so perverted that what should produce shame produces boasting. Rev 3:18 — "white garments so that you may clothe yourself and the shame (aischunē) of your nakedness may not be seen" — the Laodicean shame: nakedness without the covering Christ provides. 1Jo 2:28 — "that we may not shrink from him in shame at his coming" — eschatological not-ashamed: the person who abides in Christ will not shrink from him when he appears.

**G0818 atimazō (7 verses) and G0819 atimia (7 verses) — dishonour.** Act 5:41 — "they departed rejoicing that they were counted worthy to suffer dishonour (atimazō) for the name." The complete shame-inversion: suffering dishonour *for the name* is reframed as worthiness. The apostles rejoice at being shamed — the most concentrated NT expression of the shame-reversal that Heb 12:2 grounds in Jesus's own act. Rom 1:24,26 — "God gave them up... to the dishonour of their bodies... to dishonourable passions." Judicial dishonour: God's giving-over is itself an act of atimazō. 1Co 15:43 — "it is sown in dishonour (atimia); it is raised in glory." The resurrection reversal: the body's shameful death becomes its glorious resurrection.

**G1788 entrepō (9 verses) — to cause shame/to respect.** Reading all 9: entrepō has a double sense — to cause shame (1Co 4:14; 2Th 3:14; Tit 2:8) and to respect/reverence (Mat 21:37; Mar 12:6; Luk 18:2,4; Luk 20:13; Heb 12:9). The parable of the vineyard tenants is the most theologically concentrated entrepō passage: the owner sends his son saying "they will respect (entrapēsontai) my son" — but the tenants kill him. The expected shame-based respect (reverence due to the owner's son) is refused. The entrepō vocabulary thus spans both dimensions: the shame-experience that produces appropriate deference, and the shaming that disciplines.

**G5195 hubrizō (5 verses) — to mistreat/treat shamefully.** Reading all 5: the hubrizō vocabulary is the NT's most physically concrete shame term — shame enacted on the body. Luk 18:32 — "he will be delivered over... to be mocked and shamefully treated (hubristhēsetai) and spit upon" — Jesus's passion prediction. The shame of the cross was physical mistreatment, not merely social dishonour. 1Th 2:2 — "though we had already suffered and been shamefully treated (hubristhentes) at Philippi." The apostolic pattern: Paul's own experience of physical shame-infliction mirrors the pattern of the Lord.

**G0524 apalgeō (1 verse) — to become callous.** Eph 4:19 — "they have become callous (apēlgēkotes) and have given themselves up to sensuality." This is the NT equivalent of Jer 6:15 / 8:12 — the loss of shame-capacity in Jeremiah mapped onto the NT concept of moral callousness. The person who has become callous has lost the inner register that responds to shame-worthy behaviour. Programme-level connection: the seared conscience (1Ti 4:2), the loss of the capacity to blush (Jer 6:15), and callousness (Eph 4:19) are the same condition described in three vocabularies.

**G3856 paradeigmatizō (1 verse) — to disgrace publicly.** Heb 6:6 — "crucifying once again the Son of God and holding him up to contempt (paradeigmatizō)." Apostasy as re-shaming Christ: the person who falls away is described as re-enacting the crucifixion's public disgrace. The strongest shame-of-Christ vocabulary in the registry alongside Heb 12:2.

## Section 3: Dimensional Placement

### 3.1 Circle Two — Primarily Soul/Self-Awareness

**Primary circle:** Soul — shame is fundamentally a self-aware inner response to exposure or failure. It requires a self that can be aware of itself as diminished.

**Affective (primary):** Shame is a felt condition — the painful awareness of exposure. Psa 69:20 — "reproaches have broken my heart." Psa 44:15 — "all day long my disgrace is before me, and shame has covered my face." The continuous, pervasive, face-covering character of shame.

**Somatic (co-primary):** The shame vocabulary's somatic dimension is among the most concentrated in the programme. Reading all somatic-flagged terms: the face is shame's primary somatic site. H0954 bosh — 2Sa 19:5: "you have covered with shame the faces of all your servants." H3639 ke.lim.mah — Psa 69:7: "dishonour has covered my face." H1322 bo.shet — Psa 44:15: "shame has covered my face." H0955 bu.shah — Eze 7:18: "shame is on all faces." The face-covering pattern recurs across multiple shame terms — shame is the condition of the covered face, the hidden face, the face that cannot be lifted.

Num 12:14 — "if her father had but spit in her face, should she not be shamed seven days?" — the connection between facial exposure and shame in legal/social contexts. Isa 50:6 — "I hid not my face from disgrace and spitting" — the Servant accepts the full somatic shame. Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God" — the face directed downward before God as the posture of shame before the holy.

**Cognitive:** Shame shapes cognition. Job 31:34 — "the contempt of families terrified me, so that I kept silence" — shame-induced silence and self-censorship. Jer 3:3 — "you have the forehead of a whore; you refuse to be ashamed" — the shameless cognitive orientation.

**Volitional:** The Jer 6:15/8:12 finding (loss of capacity to blush) is the most significant volitional-shame observation: at a certain level of moral deterioration, the will loses the capacity to respond to shame-stimuli. This is shame-anesthesia — a state where the will no longer registers what should be shameful.

**Relational:** Shame is fundamentally relational — it requires an audience, whether real or imagined. The cher.pah/cha.raph vocabulary is entirely relational: reproach is what one receives from another; the taunt is what one person directs at another.

### 3.2 The Honour-Shame Framework

Reading all 441 verses establishes that the biblical shame vocabulary operates within an honour-shame social framework rather than a guilt-innocence framework in its primary register. This is the programme's most important cultural-anthropological finding in this registry.

**The honour-shame axis:** Psa 4:2 — "how long shall my honour be turned into shame?" Hos 4:7 — "I will change their glory into shame." Pro 3:35 — "the wise inherit honour, but fools get disgrace." The honour-shame pair is the social frame within which most of the vocabulary operates. Honour (kavod/timē) is the positive; shame is its deprivation or reversal.

**The social nature of OT shame:** Virtually every shame vocabulary term involves a social witness — others who observe, taunt, mock, or revile. 2Sa 13:13 — Tamar: "as for me, where could I carry my shame?" — shame as a social burden that must be *carried* somewhere. The shame vocabulary consistently imagines a social context: the person who is shamed has been seen, and the shame is the consequence of that public exposure.

**The divine inversion:** 1Co 1:27 — "God chose what is foolish to shame the wise; what is weak to shame the strong." The cross inverts the honour-shame system entirely: the cross is the world's maximum shame-event (execution as a criminal before mocking crowds), but God makes it the pivot of his redemptive act. The person who is "shamed" in worldly terms by following the crucified Christ is actually receiving honour in the kingdom's terms.

## Section 4: Divine Dimension

### 4.1 God as the One Who Shames and Removes Shame

**God shames idolaters:** Psa 97:7 — "all worshippers of images are put to shame." Isa 42:17 — "they are turned back and utterly put to shame, who trust in carved idols." The bosh + idol pattern is theologically active: God shames those whose trust is misplaced. The shame is not arbitrary but revelatory — it exposes the emptiness of the trust-object.

**God shames the proud:** Job 12:21 — "he pours contempt (buz) on princes." Psa 107:40 — same. The divine contempt is the judicial form of shame — God's act of exposing arrogance as what it is.

**God removes shame from his people:** Jos 5:9 — "today I have rolled away the reproach of Egypt from you." Isa 25:8 — "the reproach of his people he will take away from all the earth." Isa 54:4 — "fear not, for you will not be ashamed." Joe 2:26-27 — "my people shall never again be put to shame." The promise of shame-removal is as consistently divine as the act of shame-bringing. God both inflicts shame (on idolaters and the arrogant) and removes it (from his covenant people).

**The eschatological shame-removal is permanent:** Isa 45:17 — "you shall not be put to shame or confounded *to all eternity*." The temporal marker — not temporary relief but eternal removal — is the most emphatic eschatological shame-statement in the corpus.

### 4.2 God as the One Who Bears Shame

**Psa 69:9 — the key divine-shame verse:** "The reproaches of those who reproach you have fallen on me." The one who bears reproach *for God's sake* receives what should have landed on God — the shame directed at God's cause becomes the psalmist's personal reproach. This is the OT structural anticipation of the cross's shame.

**Isa 50:6 — the Servant's accepted shame:** "I gave my back to those who strike, and my cheeks to those who pull out the beard; I hid not my face from disgrace and spitting." This is the most concentrated somatic shame-acceptance in the OT. The Servant does not hide from shame but presents himself to it — the active reception of shame as the instrument of redemptive suffering.

**Heb 12:2 — Jesus despising the shame of the cross:** "who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame (aischunēn)." Three elements:

*The joy set before him:* The cross was borne against a backdrop of anticipated joy — not joyless endurance but purposeful suffering with a visible end-point. This is the shame vocabulary's most important motivational statement: shame can be endured when the horizon beyond it is visible.

*Endured the cross:* The cross is named as the instrument of shame — not merely of physical pain but of social dishonour. Roman crucifixion was designed as maximum shame: public, naked, prolonged, between criminals. Jesus bore the system's maximum shame-event.

*Despising the shame (kataphronēsas aischunēn):* The verb kataphronein means to treat as beneath consideration, to despise, to disregard. Jesus did not escape the shame or deny it — he *despised* it, treating it as ultimately contemptible from the vantage point of the joy set before him. This is not shame-denial but shame-transcendence: accepting the shame-experience while refusing its power to determine identity or outcome.

## Section 5: Somatic Dimension

### 5.1 The Face as Shame's Primary Site

Reading all somatic-flagged terms establishes the face as the shame vocabulary's most concentrated somatic focus. The pattern across multiple terms:

**Covering the face:** Psa 44:15 — "shame has covered my face." Psa 69:7 — "dishonour has covered my face." Eze 7:18 — "shame is on all faces."

**Not lifting the face:** Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God." Job 10:15 — "I cannot lift up my head, for I am filled with disgrace." The posture of shame is downward — the face directed to the ground, unable to meet the eyes of the one before whom one is ashamed.

**The radiant face as shame's inverse:** Psa 34:5 — "those who look to him are radiant, and their faces shall never be ashamed." The trust-in-God posture produces the lifted, radiant face; the shame-posture produces the covered, downward face.

**Setting the face like a flint:** Isa 50:7 — "I have set my face like a flint" — the Servant's response to potential shame. The face set firm, immovable, not turned in shame — an act of deliberate refusal to be shamed by what God has called one to do.

### 5.2 Clothing as Shame's Social Marker

Reading the clothing imagery across the shame vocabulary: shame is consistently described as a garment — something worn, clothed in, wrapped in.

Psa 35:26 — "let them be clothed with shame." Psa 109:29 — "may they be wrapped in their own shame as in a cloak." Psa 132:18 — "his enemies I will clothe with shame, but on him his crown will shine." Rev 3:18 — "white garments so that you may clothe yourself and the shame of your nakedness may not be seen."

The clothing-shame connection recalls Gen 2:25 (naked and not ashamed) and Gen 3:7 (they made themselves loincloths). The shame that followed the fall was clothed — covered. Rev 3:18 completes the trajectory: the nakedness that generates shame must be covered by Christ-given garments.

## Section 6: Relational and Causative Dimension

### 6.1 Cross-Registry Relationships

**Shame ↔ Pride (reg 123):** The most direct cross-registry pair confirmed. Pro 11:2 — "when pride comes, then comes disgrace (qa.lon)." Hab 2:16 — "you will have your fill of shame instead of glory." Pride and shame are inverse conditions — pride claims honour that is not due; shame is the exposure of that false claim. The programme's Session D will need to examine the pride-shame axis as the social dimension of the honour-before-God / self-exaltation contrast.

**Shame ↔ Humility (reg 80):** The positive-negative pair confirmed. Pro 11:2 — "with the humble is wisdom" (where pride produces disgrace). Isa 57:15 — "I dwell with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit" — the humble person (who might experience shame) is precisely the person God inhabits. Shame rightly received produces humility; humility avoids the pride that generates shame.

**Shame ↔ Conscience (reg 26):** The Jer 6:15/8:12 finding connects directly: the loss of the capacity to blush is a conscience-failure. Apalgeō (Eph 4:19 — callousness) is the NT parallel. The shame-conscience connection requires a dedicated cross-registry examination in Session D.

**Shame ↔ Grief (reg 71):** Jer 20:18 — "why did I come out from the womb to see toil and sorrow, and spend my days in shame?" — shame and sorrow co-occurring. Dan 12:2 — "shame and everlasting contempt" as the eschatological fate of the wicked (cf. the grief registry's eschatological vocabulary). The shame-grief pair requires Session D attention.

**Shame ↔ Hope (reg 78):** Rom 5:5 — "hope does not put us to shame." The hope-vocabulary is directly linked to shame-freedom: the person whose hope is anchored in God's love (poured out by the Spirit) will not be exposed as having hoped wrongly. Hope is the future-oriented form of trust, and trust is the shame-antidote.

**Shame ↔ Repentance (reg 135):** Eze 43:10-11 — shame as the precondition for repentance (must be ashamed before the temple plan is revealed). Jer 31:19 — "I was ashamed and I was also humiliated" in Ephraim's repentance speech. Shame's productive form is precisely the shame that leads to repentance and turning — the repentance registry and the shame registry share their most important transformation dynamic.

### 6.2 The Five Causative Pathways for Shame

**God → shame on the unfaithful:** Shame as divine judicial act — God exposes the emptiness of misplaced trust (idolaters shamed), divine contempt poured on the proud.

**Person → person (taunt/reproach):** Cha.raph, cher.pah as the interpersonal infliction of shame. Goliath's taunt, Sennacherib's taunt, the enemy's mockery — shame inflicted by one person on another.

**Sin → person → shame:** The structural pattern across bosh, bo.shet, ke.lim.mah: sinful behaviour produces shame. Jer 2:26 — "as a thief is shamed when caught, so the house of Israel shall be shamed." The exposure of sin by its consequences is the most natural shame-causation.

**Loss/circumstance → person:** Rachel's reproach (infertility — Gen 30:23), Tamar's shame (violation — 2Sa 13:13), the exile's shame (Jer 51:51). Shame produced by circumstance rather than personal sin.

**Shame → transformation (productive) or → paralysis (destructive):** The two-forms structure's causative axis. Productive shame: Eze 43:10-11, Jer 31:19. Destructive shame: Jer 6:15/8:12 (loss of capacity to blush), Eph 4:19 (callousness). The key variable determining the direction is the same as in the grief registry: orientation toward God or away from God.

## Section 7: Canonical Verse Analysis

### 7.1 Anchor Verse — Heb 12:2

*"looking to Jesus, the founder and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is seated at the right hand of the throne of God."*

Having read all 441 verses, this is confirmed as the registry's most analytically complete statement. Five elements:

**"Looking to Jesus"** — the antidote to shame is an act of looking away from shame-sources (taunters, accusers, one's own failures) toward Jesus. The entire bosh/trust pattern in the Psalter is here concentrated: trust as directed gaze.

**"The founder and perfecter of our faith"** — Jesus's shame-endurance is not incidental but is the very ground and completion of faith. The person whose shame Jesus endured can now have faith in the One who has proved the trust-object trustworthy by bearing the maximum shame without being destroyed by it.

**"For the joy that was set before him"** — the eschatological horizon changes what can be endured in the present. The joy set before him is the answer to Jer 6:15's shamelessness: Jesus was not shameless (he felt the shame) but he could endure it because the end was visible.

**"Despising the shame"** — kataphronēsas. The shame was real but treated as ultimately below his worth. This is not stoic indifference but active theological refusal: the shame cannot define him or determine the outcome because the joy-horizon is already established.

**"Seated at the right hand"** — the shame has ended. The seated posture is honour; the right hand is power; the throne of God is the ultimate honour-location. The two-forms structure is resolved eschatologically: the cross's shame leads to the throne's honour.

### 7.2 Gen 2:25 — The Original Shameless State

*"And the man and his wife were both naked and were not ashamed."*

The zero-point of the entire registry. Three elements:

**Naked:** Complete physical and existential exposure — nothing hidden, no self-protection, no concealment. Nakedness here is not a deficiency but the natural condition of creaturely transparency before God and each other.

**Not ashamed:** The negative of the entire vocabulary. This is not shame overcome or shame endured — it is the *absence* of shame-consciousness altogether. There is nothing to conceal because there has been no breach in relationship with God or each other. Shame requires a rupture; before the fall, no rupture has occurred.

**Programme-level significance:** Gen 2:25 establishes that shame is not the original condition of the human person but a consequence of the fall. The trajectory of the shame vocabulary therefore runs: Gen 2:25 (no shame) → Gen 3 (shame enters) → the OT vocabulary (shame as the condition of the fallen world) → Heb 12:2 (Jesus despising shame) → Rev 21 (shame removed eschatologically) → the new creation (the condition of Gen 2:25 restored).

### 7.3 Jer 6:15 / 8:12 — The Loss of the Capacity for Shame

*"Were they ashamed when they committed abomination? No, they were not at all ashamed; they did not know how to blush."*

The double occurrence — identical text in Jer 6:15 and 8:12 — is itself a rhetorical marker of the statement's weight. Three elements:

**"Were they ashamed?"** — the expected response to abomination is shame. The question assumes that healthy moral psychology produces shame at the sight of one's own wrongdoing.

**"Not at all ashamed"** — the total absence of shame. Not diminished shame but its complete elimination from the response-set.

**"Did not know how to blush"** — the loss of the *capacity*, not merely the failure of the response. This is not "they should have blushed but didn't" — it is "they no longer possessed the internal mechanism that would generate a blush." The moral compass has been lost, not ignored.

The programme-level significance: this verse is the shame vocabulary's most important diagnostic statement. The two-forms structure for shame includes not only godly vs worldly shame but a third possibility: the complete absence of shame where shame is due — which is named here as the deepest moral disorder. Session D should examine this as the most severe condition in the programme's negative lexicon.

### 7.4 Isa 61:7 — The Double Portion for Shame

*"Instead of your shame there shall be a double portion; instead of dishonour they shall rejoice in their lot."*

The most striking shame-reversal statement in the corpus. Two elements:

**"Instead of your shame"** — the shame was real. The promise does not deny the shame or minimise it — it presupposes it. The people have experienced genuine shame, genuine dishonour.

**"There shall be a double portion"** — the reversal is not a return to pre-shame status but a *surplus*. The double-portion is the inheritance language of the firstborn (Deu 21:17) — what is given is not mere restoration but abundance. The shame, when reversed by God, leaves the person in a *better* position than before the shame. This is the most theologically generous shame-resolution in the entire vocabulary: God does not merely remove shame but replaces it with double abundance.

### 7.5 Joe 2:26-27 — My People Shall Never Again Be Put to Shame

*"You shall eat in plenty and be satisfied, and praise the name of the Lord your God, who has dealt wondrously with you. And my people shall never again be put to shame. You shall know that I am in the midst of Israel, and that I am the Lord your God and there is none else. And my people shall never again be put to shame."*

The double repetition — "my people shall never again be put to shame" — frames the passage and gives it its weight. Three elements:

**"My people"** — shame's removal is covenantal. God's act is specifically toward *his people* — shame-removal as the expression of covenant faithfulness.

**"Shall never again"** — the temporal scope is eschatological. Not "for now" but permanently. The shame that characterised the judgment period (Joel's locusts as covenant curse) will never return.

**"You shall know that I am in the midst of Israel"** — the cognitive content of shame-removal is the knowledge of God's presence. Shame is fundamentally associated with God's absence or distance; the removal of shame is associated with the recognition of God's nearness. The shame-free community is the community that *knows* God is present.

### 7.6 Phi 3:19 — They Glory in Their Shame

*"Their end is destruction, their god is their belly, and they glory in their shame, with minds set on earthly things."*

The most inverted shame-statement in the NT, and the closest NT parallel to Jer 6:15/8:12. Three elements:

**"Their god is their belly"** — appetite as false object of worship. The belly-as-god is the directional inversion: instead of orientation toward God, orientation toward bodily appetite.

**"They glory in their shame"** — what should produce shame produces boasting. The honour-shame axis has been entirely inverted: the shameful things (following bodily appetite, living for earthly things) are now the sources of pride. This is Jer 6:15's "did not know how to blush" restated in positive terms: not only do they lack shame but they have replaced it with its opposite.

**"Minds set on earthly things"** — the cognitive diagnosis. The mind-setting vocabulary connects to the Rom 8 flesh/Spirit pair from WA-184: the mind set on earthly things is the psuchikos mode (WA-184's two-modes structure) — the mode that cannot receive the things of the Spirit (1Co 2:14). Phi 3:19 and Jer 6:15 together are the programme's most complete account of the most severe moral-spiritual disorder: shame-inversion + appetite-worship + earth-orientation + lost capacity for repentance.

## Section 8: Scientific Analysis

### 8.1 Primary Scientific Fields

**Psychology of shame** (primary) — shame theory, shame vs guilt distinction, toxic vs healthy shame
**Social psychology and honour culture** (secondary) — honour-shame social frameworks, face-saving behaviour

### 8.2 Landmark Findings

**Finding 1 — Shame vs guilt (June Price Tangney; Helen Block Lewis).** The shame-guilt distinction is the most established finding in shame psychology: guilt is "I did something bad" (behaviour-focused); shame is "I am bad" (self-focused). The biblical vocabulary does not cleanly map onto this distinction — bosh/bo.shet/ke.lim.mah can cover both — but the most acute shame statements in the registry (Ezr 9:6: "I am ashamed to lift my face"; Psa 44:15: "shame has covered my face") are more consistent with shame than guilt in the psychological sense. The loss-of-capacity-to-blush (Jer 6:15) is more consistent with the clinical condition of "shame-free" pathology described by Tangney: where shame has been eliminated through repeated override, the person loses the moral feedback mechanism.

**Finding 2 — Toxic shame vs healthy shame (John Bradshaw, *Healing the Shame That Binds*, 1988; Gershen Kaufman).** Bradshaw distinguishes healthy shame (the appropriate awareness of human limitation and fallibility — what keeps the person humble and honest) from toxic shame (shame that has become an identity — "I am shameful" rather than "I have done something shameful"). The programme's two-forms structure maps precisely: godly/productive shame (Eze 43:10-11, Jer 31:19) corresponds to healthy shame; the shame that covers the face without resolution and becomes identity corresponds to toxic shame. The eschatological shame-removal (Joe 2:26; Isa 45:17) is the resolution of toxic shame — not by eliminating all shame-awareness but by removing the shame that has become identity.

**Finding 3 — Honour-shame culture and the face (Roland Muller, *Honor and Shame*; Bruce Malina, social-science commentary on NT).** Social anthropologists identify honour-shame as the primary social control mechanism in the Ancient Near East and Mediterranean world, in contrast to the guilt-innocence framework characteristic of Northern European cultures. The face-covering imagery of the shame vocabulary (face as the primary somatic site) is consistent with this framework: in honour-shame cultures, the face represents the social self, and to have one's face covered is to have one's social identity eliminated. This explains the intensity of the shame vocabulary in the OT and NT: the shame at stake is not merely psychological discomfort but social existence itself.

**Finding 4 — The neuropsychology of shame (June Price Tangney and Ronda Dearing, *Shame and Guilt*, 2002).** Research shows shame activates the same neural pathways as social pain (ostracism, rejection) — shame is neurologically processed as social threat. The biblical vocabulary's emphasis on the social, relational, witnessed character of shame (taunts, reproaches, face before others) is consistent with this finding: shame is not a private intrapsychic event but a relational-social event registered neurologically as threat to existence.

### 8.3 Convergence and Divergence

**Structural convergence:** The biblical two-forms structure (productive/godly shame vs destructive/paralyzing shame) maps precisely onto the psychology of healthy vs toxic shame. Both accounts locate the key variable in whether shame is self-directed (becomes identity) or behaviour-directed (becomes conscience-feedback). The programme's Jer 6:15 finding (loss of capacity to blush) maps onto the clinical observation of shame-free psychopathy where repeated shame-override produces moral anesthesia.

**Phenomenological convergence:** The somatic shame-description (face-covering, downward gaze, inability to lift the head) is consistent with documented phenomenological accounts of shame: postural collapse, averted gaze, desire to disappear, face-hiding. The biblical writers were attending to the actual phenomenology of shame with precision.

**Register divergence:** The honour-shame social framework of the OT is primarily social and relational; modern Western psychology primarily frames shame as an intrapsychic experience. The biblical vocabulary requires both registers — the social (cher.pah, cha.raph, atimazō) and the inner (bosh, ke.lim.mah, aischunē) — neither alone is sufficient.

**Genuine divergence — the shame of Christ.** Heb 12:2 — Jesus despising the shame of the cross, and Act 5:41 — the apostles rejoicing to be counted worthy of shame for the name — have no psychological parallel. No shame psychology provides a framework for the voluntary reception of shame as redemptive, or for shame-endurance as the founding act of faith. The cross as the pivot of the honour-shame axis (God's maximum shame becoming humanity's maximum glory) is not a psychological category but a theological one. The shame vocabulary's most important claim — that shame's direction can be reversed, its power denied, and its permanent removal promised — exceeds what any psychological theory of shame accounts for.

## Section 9: Circle Mapping Summary and Session D Observation Log

### 9.1 Circle Mapping Summary

**Primary circles:** Soul (self-aware inner experience of shame) and Relational Environment (shame as social exposure).

**Two-forms structure:**

*Rightly-ordered shame (productive/godly shame):*
The shame that responds appropriately to wrongdoing without becoming identity. Eze 43:10-11 — shame as the precondition for repentance and restoration. Jer 31:19 — shame as the expression of authentic turning (Ephraim's repentance). Ezr 9:6 — shame before God that cannot lift its face but also cannot remain silent. The Servant's endured shame (Isa 50:6) — shame received as the instrument of redemption. The apostles counting shame as worthiness (Act 5:41). In each case: shame is real, felt, bodily — and it leads somewhere beyond itself.

*Disordered shame (destructive forms — two sub-forms):*
**a) Paralyzing shame:** Shame that becomes identity, covering without releasing. The person perpetually covered with shame (Psa 44:15 "all day long"). Shame that leads to hiding rather than turning. Shame as the condition of those who have no hope of restoration.
**b) Absent shame (most severe):** Jer 6:15/8:12 — "did not know how to blush." Phi 3:19 — glorying in shame. Eph 4:19 — callousness. The complete inversion or elimination of shame-capacity. This is the programme's most severe negative condition: not that the person has too much shame but that the shame-mechanism has been destroyed.

**Transformation dynamic:** Wrongdoing/exposure (sin caught, trust broken, failure exposed) → shame-response → either: [Productive path] shame that looks to God → humiliation before God → repentance → restoration → shame removed and replaced (double portion, Isa 61:7) → not-ashamed-forever (Joe 2:26) / or [Destructive path a] shame that hides → covering without resolution → identity-shame → paralysis / or [Most severe path b] repeated shame-override → loss of capacity for shame → glorying in shame → permanent dishonour (Dan 12:2).

### 9.2 Session D Observation Log

**New Session D observation — Gen 2:25 as the programme's shame zero-point.** The original shameless state (naked and not ashamed) establishes that shame is not the human person's original condition but a consequence of relational rupture with God. The programme's anthropological baseline (Gen 2:7 — divine breath + dust = living soul) should be placed alongside Gen 2:25 — living soul in unashamed relationship with God. Shame enters when the relationship is broken. The entire shame vocabulary is the unfolding of Gen 3 and what God does to reverse it.

**New Session D observation — the three-form shame structure.** Unlike the two-forms structure found in other characteristics, the shame vocabulary yields a *three*-form structure: (1) productive/godly shame that leads to repentance and restoration; (2) paralyzing shame that becomes identity; (3) absent shame (the loss of shame-capacity) which is the most severe. Session D should examine whether this three-form pattern recurs across other registries.

**New Session D observation — shame as the social form of the inner-being condition.** The shame vocabulary operates more heavily in the relational-social register than any other characteristic examined so far. Shame requires a witness (real or imagined); it is produced by social exposure; it is expressed through the social body (the face). This suggests that shame occupies a unique position in the programme: it is the characteristic that most directly links the inner life (felt shame-experience) to the social world (honour-shame community). Session D should examine the shame-pride axis as the social dimension of the person's orientation before God and community.

**Cross-registry confirmation — Shame-conscience connection.** Jer 6:15/8:12 (no capacity to blush), 1Ti 4:2 (seared conscience), and Eph 4:19 (callousness) are the same condition in three vocabularies. The conscience registry (reg 26), the shame registry (reg 146), and the shame vocabulary's most severe form (Phi 3:19) all converge on the same anthropological disaster: the loss of the inner moral register. Session D must examine this convergence.
