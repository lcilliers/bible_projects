## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### 2.1 CHARAH root — cha.rah (H2734)

**Root profile:** The root conveys burning heat as a physical image for the inner state of anger. The related words confirm a tight semantic field: cha.ron (burning anger), cho.ri (burning), cha.rar (to scorch). All three senses converge on sustained high-temperature intensity rather than a brief flash.

**Verse distribution:** 81 verses sampled from 90 occurrences, spanning Genesis to Zechariah. The dominant pattern is covenantal: God's anger burns against Israel for idolatry and covenant breaking (Exo 4:14; 22:24; 32:10–11; Num 11:1,10,33; 25:3; Judg 2:14,20; 3:8; 10:7; Deu 6:15; 7:4; 11:17; 29:27; Jos 7:1; 23:16; Isa 5:25). The human anger uses are structurally parallel — Cain (Gen 4:5–6), Jacob (Gen 30:2; 31:36), Moses (Exo 32:19), Balaam (Num 22:27), Jonathan (1Sa 20:34 via a.tsav cross-term).

**Inner-being qualification:** cha.rah is the burning-anger experience at its most internal. The somatic_link flag is confirmed: Gen 4:5 shows the face falling as the embodied register of cha.rah; Isa 21:3 links it to loins filling with anguish and inability to hear or see. The Jon 4:4,9 exchange is analytically critical: Jonah declares himself angry enough to die (ya.tav la.mut), establishing cha.rah as an inner state of sufficient intensity to threaten life. This is not a transient mood; it is a consuming interior burning.

**God-as-subject:** Confirmed true. The covenantal anger of God is the dominant use of cha.rah by volume. The burning is not arbitrary; it is morally motivated and covenantally framed. The Hab 3:8 interrogative — was God's wrath against rivers, against sea? — sets God's cha.rah within cosmic agency, not mere reaction.

**Verdict: Qualifies as inner-being term.** The burning-anger somatic-inner complex is well-evidenced. However, cha.rah is primarily an anger term appearing in the distress word set because anger is frequently distress-producing or distress-accompanied. The distinction between cha.rah as an anger experience and as a distress experience requires Phase 2 assessment.

### 2.2 TSUR root — tsur (H6696A and H6696B)

**H6696A — to confine (besiege):** 65 occurrences, 32 verses sampled. The overwhelmingly dominant use is military siege: besiege cities, lay siege, surround walls (Deu 20:12,19; 2Sa 11:1; 2Ki 6:24–25; 17:5; Jer 32:2; 39:1; Eze 4:3; Dan 1:1). Two non-siege uses appear: Deu 14:25 (binding up money in hand) and 2Ki 5:23 (tying up bags of silver). The one verse that could suggest inner-being relevance is Psa 139:5 — "You hem me in, behind and before, and lay your hand upon me" — but the context is awe and wonder at divine omniscience, not distress. The hemming-in image is used of God's encompassing presence, not of inner constriction experienced by the subject.

**H6696B — to provoke (show hostility):** 4 occurrences, 3 verses sampled. Deu 2:9,19 carry divine prohibitions against harassing Moab and Ammon. Est 8:11 lists enemies who attack. All are relational-hostile uses.

**Verdict: H6696A and H6696B do not qualify as inner-being terms.** H6696A is a military-operational term. Psa 139:5 uses the root image metaphorically for divine encompassing, but this is an awed self-description of creatureliness, not a distress inner-being state. H6696B is purely relational-hostile.

### 2.3 TSR root — tsa.rar sub-IDs (H6887B, H6887C, H6887D, H6887E) and tsa.rah (H6869C)

This root continues from Part 2 (Part 1 contained tsa.rah H6869B and tsar H6862 sub-IDs). Part 3 adds five further sub-IDs.

**H6887B — to constrain:** 65 occurrences, 22 verses. The verses divide into two semantic registers. Physical-binding uses: Exo 12:34 (dough bound in cloaks), 1Sa 25:29 (life bound in bundle of the living), Job 26:8 (waters bound in clouds), Pro 26:8; 30:4; Isa 8:16 (various binding images). Distress-inner uses: 1Sa 28:15 ("I am in great distress, for the Philistines are warring against me, and God has turned away from me"), 2Sa 1:26 (David distressed for Jonathan), Psa 31:9 ("I am in distress; my eye is wasted from grief; my soul and my body also"), Psa 69:17 ("I am in distress; make haste to answer me"), 2Ch 28:22 (Ahaz in distress became more faithless), 2Ch 33:12 (Manasseh in distress humbled himself). Military-siege uses also present: Deu 28:52; 1Ki 8:37; Jer 10:18; Zep 1:17.

The inner-being distress uses are the most analytically significant. Psa 31:9 explicitly locates the distress across the triad: eye, soul, and body — confirming psycho-somatic-physical spread. 2Ch 33:12 demonstrates the transformative-responsive pattern: distress producing humility before God. 2Sa 1:26 is the most relational use: grief-distress at the loss of a beloved person.

**H6887C — to suffer distress (stative):** 4 occurrences, 4 verses. All four are pure inner-being stative expressions. Psa 59:16 and Psa 102:2 use "day of my distress" as the occasion of prayer. Jer 48:41 and 49:22 deploy the birth-pangs image for the heart of warriors in battle crisis. This sub-ID isolates the experiential-stative pole of the TSR root.

**H6887D — to vex (show hostility):** 26 occurrences, 25 verses. All are relational-hostile — the term designates adversaries, enemies, foes, those who harass and afflict others. Psa 6:7; 42:10; 69:19 appear in lament psalms, but in every case tsa.rar-D designates the external agent (foes, adversaries) causing distress, not the inner state of the subject experiencing it. Psa 129:1–2 is typical: "Greatly have they afflicted me from my youth — yet they have not prevailed."

**H6887E — to rival:** 1 occurrence (Lev 18:18). The single verse prohibits taking a rival wife alongside her sister. This is a social-relational legal use with no inner-being distress content.

**H6869C — vexer/rival wife:** 1 occurrence (1Sa 1:6). Peninnah provokes Hannah because the Lord had closed her womb. The term designates the agent of provocation, not the inner state.

### 2.4 BAHAL root — ba.hal (H0926)

**Root profile:** The meaning_summary covers two related but distinct functions: (1) to be dismayed, terrified, alarmed — an inner state; (2) to be hasty, hurried — a behavioural consequence. Related words: be.ha.lah (dismay) and ba.lah (to trouble). The two-function split is confirmed in the verse data.

**Verse distribution:** 38 verses sampled of 51. The inner-state (terror/dismay) uses are the most analytically significant: Gen 45:3 (brothers cannot answer Joseph, dismayed at his presence), 1Sa 28:21 (Saul terrified after the séance), Job 4:5; 21:6; 23:15–16 (Job dismayed/terrified in the presence of suffering and of God), Psa 6:2–3 ("my bones are troubled"; "my soul also is greatly troubled"), Psa 30:7 (dismay at God hiding his face), Psa 90:7 (dismayed by God's wrath), Psa 104:29 (creatures dismayed when God hides his face), Isa 13:8 and 21:3 (dismay with somatic description: loins filled with anguish, bowed down, cannot see), Eze 7:27 (hands of people paralysed by terror).

The haste/urgency uses: Est 2:9; 6:14; 8:14; 2Ch 26:20; 35:21; Ecc 5:2; 7:9; 8:3; Pro 20:21; 28:22. In these uses ba.hal captures urgency or rashness with no inner-distress content.

**Key analytical observation:** Psa 6:2–3 is an important convergence verse — ba.hal appears across both the physical register (bones troubled) and the inner register (soul greatly troubled), with the lament "how long?" establishing this as an enduring inner-distress state directed toward God. Isa 21:3 is the most vivid somatic description: the prophet's loins fill with anguish, he is bowed down, he cannot hear or see.

**Verdict: Qualifies — with semantic-register caveat.** The dismay/terror function carries a clear inner-being distress state. The haste/urgency function does not. The inner-distress use is the dominant register by verse volume and theological weight.

### 2.5 LUPE root — lupeō (G3076)

**Root profile:** The verb operates in two directions: active (to cause grief/distress) and passive (to be grieved, distressed, sorrowful). Related word: lupē (grief noun). This term links to the grief registry via cross-registry note, and its appearance in the distress word set reflects the overlap between grief and distress as inner states.

**Verse distribution:** 20 verses sampled of 26. The uses span the Synoptic tradition, Johannine, and Pauline. The semantically richest cluster is 2 Corinthians 2 and 7: lupeō is used for the pain caused by a corrective letter (2Cor 2:2,4–5; 7:8), and most significantly for godly grief that produces repentance (2Cor 7:9,11). The Eph 4:30 use — "do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God" — applies lupeō to the Holy Spirit as subject of the inner state, marking a distinctive NT theological move. The 1Pe 1:6 use embeds lupeō within a trial-and-joy framework consistent with the transformative-purposive register observed in Part 2's thlipsis analysis. The Joh 16:20 use establishes a temporal-redemptive contrast: "you will be sorrowful, but your sorrow will turn into joy."

**God/Spirit-as-subject:** Eph 4:30 directly. Gen 6:6 — "it grieved him to his heart" — uses a.tsav (OT), not lupeō; but the cross-Testament anthropopathic pattern is present.

**Verdict: Qualifies as inner-being term.** lupeō names a sorrow-grief-distress inner state. The godly grief → repentance pathway (2Cor 7) and the Spirit-grieving reference (Eph 4:30) are analytically significant for the soul-transformation framework.

### 2.6 QASHAH root — qa.shah (H7185)

**Root profile:** The primary semantic domain is hardness/severity — what is hard, difficult, fierce. Related words: qa.sheh (severe/harsh), qe.shi (stubbornness), miq.sheh (hairstyle — the shaped/hard sense in a different domain). The causative form present flag is confirmed; God hardens (Exo 7:3; Deu 2:30) as well as humans stiffening their own necks.

**Verse distribution:** 28 verses, full set. The dominant use is neck-stiffening (covenantal rebellion against God): Deu 10:16; 2Ki 17:14; 2Ch 30:8; 36:13; Neh 9:16–17,29; Jer 7:26; 17:23; 19:15; Pro 29:1. The hard-labour/hardship uses: Gen 35:16–17 (Rachel's hard labour), 1Ki 12:4 and 2Ch 10:4 (heavy/hard service), 2Ki 2:10 (a hard thing to ask). The experiential-distress use is present in Isa 8:21: "They will pass through the land, greatly distressed and hungry."

**Inner-being qualification:** qa.shah does not name an inner-being distress state; it names the quality of hardness that characterises a situation, a person's will, or an action. The neck-stiffening uses are about volitional resistance, not experienced suffering. The hard-labour uses describe external conditions. Isa 8:21 is the closest to inner distress, but even there qa.shah describes how distressed/hungry they are as a circumstantial descriptor, not the name of an inner state.

**Verdict: Does not qualify as an inner-being distress term.** qa.shah names hardness/severity as a quality; it does not name the inner experience of suffering or distress. Its appearance in the distress word set likely reflects semantic overlap in English translation ("hard, severe") rather than Hebrew inner-being content.

### 2.7 SUNECHO root — sunechō (G4912)

**Root profile:** sun (with/together) + echō (to have/hold) = to hold together, press together, constrain. Related word: sunochē (anguish) — a direct cognate that appears in other registries. The meaning_summary confirms a wide range: to cover ears, crowd against, guard/hold in custody, compel/urge on.

**Verse distribution:** 12 verses, full set. The uses divide sharply by function. Physical holding/pressing: Mar 3:9 (crowd pressing/crushing Jesus), Luk 8:45 (crowd pressing in), Act 7:57 (stopping ears), Luk 19:43 (enemies hemming in Jerusalem), Luk 22:63 (holding Jesus in custody). Physical illness: Mat 4:24 (those oppressed by demons/afflicted with diseases), Luk 4:38 (Peter's mother-in-law held fast by fever), Act 28:8 (father of Publius sick with fever and dysentery). Inner-compulsion/distress: Luk 12:50 ("how great is my distress until it is accomplished!" — Jesus speaking of his baptism/death), 2Cor 5:14 ("the love of Christ controls/constrains us"), Phi 1:23 ("I am hard pressed between the two").

**Key verse:** Luk 12:50 is analytically decisive. sunechō here names Christ's own inner state — a pressing distress/urgency about his approaching passion — applying to the divine subject the same constraining image used elsewhere for crowds and illness. This is the NT equivalent of the OT inner-constriction pattern.

**Verdict: Qualifies — with multi-register caveat.** The constraining/pressing distress function is clearly present in Luk 12:50, Phi 1:23, and implicitly 2Cor 5:14. The majority of uses are physical or social holding/pressing, not inner states. The analytically significant minority carries unambiguous inner-being content.

### 2.8 ATSAV root — a.tsav (H6087A)

**Root profile:** The root carries hurt, pain, grieving, vexing. The related-words field is extensive: e.tsev (toil/vessel — the shaped/laboured thing), o.tsev (pain/idol), a.tsav (to shape), ma.a.tse.vah (torment), a.tsev (worker). This root family spans both inner-being pain and the idea of something formed under strain — a convergence of the labour-of-shaping and the anguish-of-suffering in a single root.

**Verse distribution:** 15 verses sampled of 16. The inner-being uses are strong throughout. Gen 6:6 is the most theologically weighty: "it grieved him to his heart" — God's a.tsav at human wickedness is explicitly heart-located. Isa 63:10 — "they rebelled and grieved his Holy Spirit" — applies a.tsav to the Holy Spirit, an exact OT antecedent to the NT Eph 4:30 (lupeō). Gen 45:5 instructs the brothers not to be distressed or angry at having sold Joseph. 1Sa 20:34 describes Jonathan as grieved for David because his father had disgraced him. Neh 8:10–11 instructs the returned exiles not to be grieved on a holy day. Psa 78:40 — "how often they grieved him in the desert" — applies a.tsav to God's inner response to wilderness rebellion. Isa 54:6 describes a wife grieved in spirit.

**God/Spirit-as-subject:** Gen 6:6 (God grieved to his heart); Psa 78:40 (God grieved in the desert); Isa 63:10 (Holy Spirit grieved). Three separate loci of the divine a.tsav.

**Verdict: Qualifies as inner-being term.** The heart-location (Gen 6:6), the Spirit-grieving (Isa 63:10), and the consistent emotional-relational content confirm a.tsav as an inner-being distress/pain term. It sits at the intersection of hurt, grief, and vexation — closer to the grief registry than some distress terms, but with sufficient distress content to qualify here.

### 2.9 QUTS root — quts (H6973)

**Root profile:** The meaning_summary defines two related states: (1) to feel a loathing or abhorrence; (2) to feel a sickening dread. The related word qut (to loathe) confirms a tight semantic field.

**Verse distribution:** 8 verses sampled of 9. Gen 27:46 (Rebekah loathes her life because of the Hittite women — an existential weight-of-life statement). Exo 1:12 (Egyptians were in dread of Israel's multiplication). Lev 20:23 (God detested the nations' practices). Num 21:5 (Israel loathes the manna — "this worthless food"). Num 22:3 (Moab overcome with fear/dread of Israel). Pro 3:11 (do not loathe God's discipline). Isa 7:6,16 (the dread/loathing of enemies).

**Inner-being qualification:** quts names a state of combined revulsion and dread — the visceral inner response to something experienced as intolerable. Gen 27:46 is the most direct inner-being distress use: existential loathing of life itself. Exo 1:12 and Num 22:3 show the dread function. Num 21:5 shows the revulsion function applied to sustained endurance.

**Verdict: Qualifies — marginal.** quts names a genuine inner state (loathing-dread), but it occupies a specific narrow register: the inner response of revulsion and sickening dread at something intolerable. It is not a general distress term. Its inner-being content is present but the verse set is small (8 verses) and the range is limited.

### 2.10 SIACH root — si.ach (H7878)

**Root profile:** The meaning_summary spans: to complain, to muse, meditate, ponder, sing. Related words: si.ach (complaint/thought), si.chah (meditation). This is a cognitive-verbal activity term.

**Verse distribution:** 19 verses sampled of 20. The uses are overwhelmingly meditative/cognitive: Psa 77:6,12 (meditate in heart, ponder mighty deeds), Psa 105:2; 1Ch 16:9 (tell of wondrous works), Psa 119:15,23,27,48,78,148 (meditate on precepts, statutes, commandments), Psa 143:5 (meditate on all God has done), Psa 145:5 (meditate on wondrous works), Pro 6:22 (wisdom talks with you), Isa 53:8 (considered/pondered his generation).

The two distress-adjacent uses: Psa 55:17 ("evening and morning and at noon I utter my complaint and moan, and he hears my voice") and Psa 77:3 ("when I remember God, I moan; when I meditate, my spirit faints").

**Inner-being qualification:** The distress-adjacent uses (Psa 55:17; 77:3) show si.ach in a lament-complaint function. But even here, si.ach names the cognitive-verbal act (uttering, moaning in meditation) rather than the inner distress state itself. The act can be distress-expressing, but the term names the activity (musing/complaining) not the experience.

**Verdict: Does not qualify as an inner-being distress term.** si.ach is a cognitive-meditative-verbal activity term. Distress-complaint is one context in which it appears, but the term designates the activity rather than the state. Comparable to ze.a.qah / tse.va.chah in Part 2 (somatic-expressive terms) except that si.ach is cognitive-expressive rather than vocal-expressive.

### 2.11 KATAPONE root — kataponeō (G2669)

**Root profile:** kata (down/against) + ponos (labour/travail) = to exhaust by labour or suffering, to wear out, to oppress. Related words: kata (preposition) and ponos (travail/pain). Only 2 occurrences.

**Verse distribution:** Both verses present. Act 7:24 — someone being wronged and oppressed; Moses defends him. 2Pe 2:7 — Lot "greatly distressed by the sensual conduct of the wicked." The 2Pe 2:7 use is the inner-being use: Lot's righteous soul tormented/distressed by what surrounded him.

**Verdict: Qualifies — minimal.** 2Pe 2:7 is the only inner-being distress use. The term's meaning (oppressed, worn out by suffering) carries inner-being content, but with only 2 total occurrences the evidential base is very small. kataponeō names the distress of a righteous person under sustained exposure to moral wickedness — a specific sub-type of inner-being distress.

### 2.12 NAGAS root — na.ga.s (H5065)

**Root profile:** To press, drive, oppress, exact, exert demanding pressure. No related words listed.

**Verse distribution:** 22 verses sampled of 23. All are relational-structural: Exo 3:7; 5:6,10,13–14 (Pharaoh's taskmasters pressing Israel), Deu 15:2–3 (exacting debts from a neighbour), 1Sa 14:24 (people hard-pressed by Saul's oath), 2Ki 23:35 (Jehoiakim exacting tribute), Isa 3:5; 3:12; 9:4; 14:2,4; 53:7; 58:3; 60:17 (oppressor, taskmasters, exacter of tribute), Dan 11:20 (exactor of tribute), Zec 9:8; 10:4 (oppressor, ruler). Isa 53:7 — "he was oppressed and afflicted" — applies na.ga.s to the Servant of the Lord.

**Inner-being qualification:** na.ga.s consistently designates the external agent or the act of oppressive pressure. The subject experiencing na.ga.s is acted upon; the term names what is done to them, not the inner state produced in them. Even Isa 53:7 names the external treatment of the Servant, not the Servant's inner distress.

**Verdict: Does not qualify as an inner-being term.** na.ga.s is a transitive pressure-exaction term naming the act of oppression, not the inner state of the one oppressed.

### 2.13 KA.AV — ka.av (H3735)

**Root profile:** To be in pain, be sore, have pain, be sorrowful — covering both physical and mental pain. Root family field is null. Related words: ke.ev (pain noun) and makh.ov (pain noun) — connecting ka.av to the ke.ev terms analysed in Part 2.

**Verse distribution:** 8 verses, full set. Gen 34:25 (men sore after circumcision — physical). 2Ki 3:19 (ruin good land — unrelated use). Job 5:18 (God wounds but binds up), Job 14:22 ("he feels only the pain of his own body, and he mourns only for himself"). Psa 69:29 ("I am afflicted and in pain"). Pro 14:13 ("even in laughter the heart may ache, and the end of joy may be grief"). Eze 13:22 (disheartening the righteous falsely — God "has not grieved him"). Eze 28:24 (no more thorn to hurt among neighbours).

**Key verse:** Pro 14:13 — "even in laughter the heart may ache" — explicitly locates the pain of ka.av in the heart, establishing ka.av as an inner-being term with cardiac location. Job 14:22 isolates body-pain alongside personal mourning in a first-person frame.

**Note on null root_family:** ka.av (H3735) has no root_family assigned in the JSON. The related_words field connects it to ke.ev (H3511 — analysed in Part 2) and makh.ov. This suggests ka.av is the verbal form of the ke.ev / makh.ov pain cluster. The null field should be flagged for Phase 2 root-family assignment.

**Verdict: Qualifies as inner-being term.** The heart-ache reference in Pro 14:13, the self-mourning of Job 14:22, and the affliction-pain compound of Psa 69:29 confirm ka.av as an inner-being pain-sorrow term. It is the verbal form of the ke.ev pain cluster identified in Part 2.

### 2.14 TSUQ root — tsuq (H6693)

**Root profile:** Hiphil: to constrain, press, bring into straits, oppress. Related words confirm a rich derivative family: mu.tsaq (constraint), ma.tsoq (distress), me.tsu.qah (distress), tsoq (anguish), tsu.qah (anguish). The derivatives are all inner-being distress nouns; the verb itself is causative.

**Verse distribution:** 10 verses sampled of 11. Deu 28:53,55,57 — three parallel curses using the siege-and-distress formula, where tsuq names the distress inflicted by enemies during siege. Judg 14:17 (pressing Samson's wife) and Judg 16:16 (Delilah pressing Samson hard "until his soul was vexed to death") — these are relational-pressure uses. Job 32:18 ("the spirit within me constrains me"). Isa 29:2 (God will distress Ariel), Isa 29:7 (nations fight against and distress Jerusalem), Isa 51:13 (fear of the oppressor). Jer 19:9 (enemies distress in siege).

**Key verse:** Judg 16:16 is analytically significant: Delilah's pressing produces a distress of Samson's soul so severe it is described as "vexed to death" — the inner state under relentless relational pressure reaching a life-threatening intensity.

**God-as-subject:** Isa 29:2 (God distresses Ariel). The causative use applies to God as the one who brings distress.

**Verdict: Qualifies — primarily as distress-causing verb.** tsuq itself names the act of causing distress; the inner-being state is what it produces. The derivative nouns (ma.tsoq, me.tsu.qah, tsoq, tsu.qah) are the pure inner-being forms. tsuq in the Judg 16:16 and Isa 29:2 contexts reaches into the inner-being state it produces.

### 2.15 THLIBO root — thlibō (G2346)

**Root profile:** The verb form of thlipsis (analysed extensively in Part 2). Active: to press upon, crowd, cause trouble. Passive: to be pressed, troubled, persecuted. Related words: apothlibō (crowd up to), thlipsis (pressure/affliction noun), sunthlibō (push against).

**Verse distribution:** 10 verses, full set. Mat 7:14 (narrow way that leads to life — a metaphorical use of the root image). Mar 3:9 (crowd crushing Jesus physically). 2Cor 1:6 (affliction for your comfort and salvation). 2Cor 4:8 ("afflicted in every way, but not crushed"). 2Cor 7:5 ("afflicted at every turn — fighting without and fear within"). 1Th 3:4 (predicting affliction as normal experience). 2Th 1:6 (God repaying with affliction those who afflict you). 2Th 1:7 (relief granted to those who are afflicted). 1Ti 5:10 (caring for the afflicted as a mark of godly widowhood). Heb 11:37 (heroes of faith who were afflicted, destitute, mistreated).

**Relationship to thlipsis:** thlibō is the verbal form from which thlipsis derives. The Part 2 analysis established thlipsis as one of the two major NT distress terms. thlibō confirms the same register: vocational-apostolic suffering, normative expectation of affliction for disciples, and the reciprocity framing of 2Th 1:6–7. 2Cor 4:8's "afflicted but not crushed" — using both thlibō and not-stenochōreō — links this root directly to stenochōreō (below).

**Verdict: Qualifies.** thlibō is the verb form of the thlipsis complex and shares its registers fully. No new analytical content beyond thlipsis (Part 2), but confirms the verb-noun relationship within the THLIBO root family.

### 2.16 STENO root — stenochōreō (G4729)

### 2.18 CHUL root — chul (H2342A)

**Root profile:** To twist, whirl, dance, writhe, fear, tremble, travail, be in anguish, be pained. This is one of the most somatic roots in the biblical vocabulary: the physical twisting/writhing motion is the basis for both birth-travail and inner anguish. The related-words field confirms: chil (agony), chi.lah (agony), chal.cha.lah (anguish), ma.chol (dance). The same root generates both ecstatic movement and extreme suffering.

**Verse distribution:** 10 verses sampled of 56. The somatic-terror uses dominate: 1Ch 16:30 and Psa 96:9 (tremble before God), Psa 97:4 and Psa 114:7 (earth trembles), Job 26:5 (the dead tremble), Jer 5:22 (tremble before the Lord). The inner-anguish uses: Est 4:4 ("the queen was deeply distressed" — chul applied to Esther at hearing of Mordecai's mourning), Psa 55:4 ("my heart is in anguish within me; the terrors of death have fallen upon me"), Lam 4:6 (chastisement of Jerusalem greater than Sodom's overthrow — the wrung-hands image: "no hands were wrung for her" — a negative chul reference).

**Key verse:** Psa 55:4 — "my heart is in anguish within me" — explicitly locates chul in the heart (lev), confirming inner-being status. The pairing with "terrors of death" shows the intensity of the state. Est 4:4 shows chul as the response to bad news about a loved one.

**Verse-count note:** Only 10 of 56 occurrences are in the source. The 10 sampled verses are heavily weighted toward cosmological/theophanic trembling. The full 56-verse set would likely reveal a larger proportion of inner-anguish uses. This limits confidence in Register distribution assessments.

**Verdict: Qualifies — with caveat.** The heart-anguish function is clearly present (Psa 55:4; Est 4:4). The cosmological-trembling uses are the majority of the sample but not necessarily of the full term. The small sample relative to total occurrences is a genuine analytical limitation.

## Section 4 — OT / NT Register Analysis

### 4.1 Burning and pressing: shared root images across Testaments

Part 3 reinforces the observation from Part 2 that the distress word set clusters around two primary physical images: burning (cha.rah / CHARAH) and pressing/constraining (TSR root family, TSUQ, THLIBO/thlibō, STENO/stenochōreō, SUNECHO/sunechō). These images appear in both Testaments but with different theological framings.

In the OT, the burning-anger complex (cha.rah) is most prominently associated with God's covenantal anger. The pressing-constraining complex (TSR, TSUQ) is most prominently associated with siege and military crisis, with inner-being uses as a secondary register. In the NT, the pressing-constraining complex dominates and is thoroughly interiorised: thlibō/thlipsis, stenochōreō, sunechō all name inner-being states or inner-compulsion using spatial-constraining images.

### 4.2 The anger-distress intersection: cha.rah and lupeō

Part 3 introduces cha.rah as the primary anger-as-inner-state term. Its appearance in the distress word set raises a taxonomic question about the relationship between anger and distress. In the OT, cha.rah and distress-language frequently co-occur because anger is a common response to perceived injustice, loss, or covenantal violation. In the NT, lupeō names a grief-sorrow state that bridges distress and grief registries. The two terms together suggest an OT anger-distress profile and an NT grief-distress profile as the dominant emotional registers of their respective Testaments — though both states are present in both Testaments.

### 4.3 The dismay cluster: ba.hal and its NT absence

ba.hal (BAHAL) is an OT term with no direct NT equivalent in this Part 3 set. The dismay-terror inner state (Gen 45:3; Job 23:15–16; Psa 6:2–3; 30:7; Isa 13:8; 21:3) is a distinctive OT register: the creature overwhelmed by God's presence or by catastrophic events. The NT does not lack terror language, but the specific complex of dismay-at-God's-face-being-hidden (Psa 30:7; 104:29) has no direct NT parallel in this set. This may reflect a genuine register difference: the OT divine-hiddenness pattern and the NT indwelling-Spirit pattern create different emotional frameworks for the believer's distress experience.

### 4.4 The Spirit-grieving parallel: a.tsav and lupeō

One of the most significant cross-Testament findings in Part 3 is the structural parallel between a.tsav (H6087A) in the OT and lupeō (G3076) in the NT as applied to the divine spirit. Isa 63:10 — "they grieved his Holy Spirit" — and Eph 4:30 — "do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God" — use the same theological claim (human conduct grieves the Holy Spirit) expressed in two different languages. The OT term carries the additional resonance of God's own grief-to-heart at the creation of humanity (Gen 6:6). This cross-Testament structural parallel is analytically significant for the soul-transformation framework: if the Holy Spirit can experience and be caused to experience a.tsav/lupeō, the term names something that participates in the inner life of God as well as in the inner life of humans.

### 4.5 Godly grief as transformative: lupeō's unique NT function

The 2 Corinthians 7 cluster (lupeō 7:8–11) introduces a transformation pathway not visible in the OT: grief that is "according to God" (kata Theon) produces repentance leading to salvation. This is a specific NT theological reframing of inner-distress: the inner state of grief/pain is not merely something to be resolved but something that can be directed toward God and become transformative. This parallels the transformative-purposive framing of thlipsis in Part 2 (Rom 5:3; 2Cor 4:17), suggesting that the NT consistently reframes inner distress states as spiritually purposive when oriented toward God.
