## Section 1 — Header and Qualification Assessment

**Word:** Anger
**Registry ID:** 004
**Source List:** High Confidence
**Testament Coverage:** Both

The word set qualifies as an inner-being word with high confidence. The qualification is straightforward for the primary terms. Aph (H0639), che.mah (H2534), orgē (G3709), and thumos (G2372) all name inner states of strong displeasure or hostile arousal. The verb families qa.tsaph (H7107), cha.rah (H2734), orgizō (G3710), and ka.as (H3707) name the process of entering or provoking that state. This is among the most extensively attested inner-being fields in Scripture, with a verse set running into the hundreds across both testaments.

The qualification extends with moderate confidence to the peripheral terms retained in this word set. These terms — including a.phah (to bake), it (to scream), na.phal (to fall: angry), ru.ach (spirit: temper) — are not direct anger words but appear to represent the somatic, metaphorical, and related-state dimensions of the anger semantic field. They are retained precisely because premature discarding would collapse the multi-layered nature of how Scripture represents anger, which appears to operate simultaneously at the physiological, inner-state, and relational levels.

One observation governs the whole word set: the anger semantic field in Hebrew has a pronounced somatic-physiological character that has no direct equivalent in the Greek terms. The Hebrew vocabulary for anger is saturated with heat, fire, burning, nostrils, face, breath, and overflowing — physical body-states or body-images are embedded in the root structure of the primary terms themselves. The Greek terms (orgē, thumos) carry this less overtly, though thumos retains a classical association with the breath/life principle. Phase 2 analysis of this OT/NT contrast will be essential for the inner-being model.

## Section 2 — Root Family Analysis

### Root Family: APH — aph (H0639G/H/I)

The APH root family is the most structurally significant in this word set because it demonstrates directly the physiological-inner being layering that the data preserves across three STEP sub-entries. The same Hebrew word — aph — means nostril, nose, face, and anger simultaneously. The meaning is not ambiguous; it is genuinely multi-layered. All three sub-sense glosses are in active use in the verse set.

The nose/anger connection is not a metaphor applied to an otherwise unrelated word. It appears to be the original somatic ground of the anger concept in Hebrew: the flaring nostril as the visible, physiological sign of arousal. Exo 15:8 depicts the blast of God's nostrils piling up the waters of the sea — a cosmological action rooted in the same anatomical image. Exo 34:6 uses the aph root in the phrase "slow to anger" — literally, long of nostril — indicating that the patience/anger spectrum is expressed in terms of the rate of nasal arousal. The face-falling idiom (Gen 4:5-6, Cain's face fell) connects na.phal and aph in a single narrative moment, with the face as the somatic display surface of the inner anger state.

The related word a.naph (to be angry) and the peripheral term a.phah (to bake) both share the APH root family. Whether the baking term shares the root meaningfully or coincidentally requires Phase 2 investigation, but Hos 7:4-6's use of the heated oven as an explicit metaphor for inner smoldering anger — using language drawn from this same sound-space — is worth noting as a potential root-metaphor cluster.

The God-as-subject data for the anger sub-entry (H0639G) is true. God's anger is expressed in aph language extensively throughout the OT, and the slow-anger formula (Exo 34:6, Num 14:18, Psa 86:15, Psa 103:8, Psa 145:8, Nah 1:3) is consistently applied to God. The formula's frequency suggests it functions as a theological anchor for the anger concept in the OT — the primary anger word (aph) is deployed as the defining characteristic of divine patience, which is defined as its opposite.

**Verdict:** APH is doing core inner-being work. It is the primary anger word of the OT and the only term in this word set where the somatic (physical body) and inner-being dimensions are fully fused at the lexical level rather than being related by metaphor. It will be a central term in Phase 2.

### Root Family: CHAR — cha.ron (H2740), cha.rah (H2734), ta.cha.rah (H8474)

The CHAR root family represents the burning/heat dimension of anger as a distinct cluster. Cha.rah (to be incensed, to burn, to kindle) is the verbal root, occurring approximately 90 times. Cha.ron (burning anger) is the nominal derivative, occurring approximately 41 times with the significant data quality note that the source meaning block states it is always used of God's anger. This is an exceptionally strong god_as_subject marker — not simply that God is frequently the subject, but that the entire word function is divine-anger-specific. Ta.cha.rah (to heat oneself in vexation) is a rare Tiphel form occurring approximately 2 times.

The CHAR root family produces verbs in multiple stems: Qal (to burn), Niphal (to be angry with, be incensed), Hiphil (to burn, kindle), and Hithpael (to heat oneself in vexation). The causative (Hiphil) form confirms that provoking-to-anger is a core grammatical function of the root — anger is not only an inner state but something that can be deliberately induced in another. The Hithpael reflexive form (to heat oneself) is particularly significant: it names a self-directed process of anger-cultivation, which has implications for the inner-being model's account of how anger states are entered and sustained.

The burning metaphor is not merely figurative in this root family. The related word cha.mam (to warm, H2552) in the CHAM family operates in the same heat-register, and Hos 7:6 deploys the baking oven and smoldering imagery in a passage that moves seamlessly between literal baking language and the description of inner anger. Psa 39:3 — "my heart became hot within me; as I mused, the fire burned; then I spoke with my tongue" — captures the CHAR-register sequence: internal heat, cognitive activity (musing), then verbal expression. This is a three-stage anger sequence recorded in a single verse that is significant for the inner-being model.

**Verdict:** The CHAR family captures the heat-arousal dimension of anger as an inner state that kindles, burns, and can be deliberately fanned. The cha.ron sub-term's god-only usage makes it a theologically important anchor for understanding divine anger as distinct in its character from human anger.

### Root Family: CHEM — che.mah (H2534), che.ma (H2528)

Che.mah is the primary term for burning rage or hot displeasure, occurring approximately 127 times. Its semantic range is broader than a modern reader might expect: the meaning block includes heat, fever, venom/poison, and burning anger. The venom/poison sub-sense is attested in the verse set (Deu 32:24, 32:33; Job 6:4; Psa 58:4; Psa 140:3) and is not a marginal usage. This creates an unusual semantic field in which the same word covers both the hot-anger state and a lethal biological substance — possibly because both represent destructive internal intensity that overflows outward. Che.ma (H2528) is the Aramaic equivalent, appearing in Daniel 3 in the description of Nebuchadnezzar's rage at Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego. The choice of Aramaic form in those verses may reflect the court-language context of Daniel and deserves attention in Phase 2.

The verse set for che.mah is extensive and covers both human anger (Est 1:12, Est 3:5, Est 7:7 — the court of Ahasuerus is a particularly concentrated context for che.mah usage) and divine anger (multiple prophetic books). Amo 1:11's use — "his anger tore perpetually, and he kept his wrath forever" — places che.mah alongside na.tar (to keep/harbour) as a description of sustained, unrelenting anger that connects to the registry's na.tar entry.

**Verdict:** Che.mah is doing primary inner-being work in the rage/burning-displeasure register. Its poison sub-sense is an unexpected semantic extension that Phase 2 will need to assess — it may indicate that the Hebrew conceptual framework treats intense anger as a form of internal toxic contamination.

### Root Family: AVAR — a.var (H5674B), ev.rah (H5678)

The AVAR root family is structurally interesting because the anger sub-sense (H5674B) sits within a root whose primary meanings are to pass, cross over, go beyond, and — crucially — to overflow. Ev.rah (fury, H5678) carries the explicit meaning of outpouring, overflow, excess, outburst. The root metaphor appears to be one of crossing a boundary or overflowing a container: the anger is the state of crossing over, the fury is the overflow itself. Related words in the cluster include ford (ma.a.var), side/beyond (e.ver), and be arrogant (a.var) — the last of these pairing arrogance and anger as co-expressions of the same crossing/exceeding orientation.

This is a distinct inner-being model from the APH (nostril/breath) and CHAR (burning/heat) families: anger as transgression of a boundary, as exceeding what is contained or permitted. The arrogance/anger pairing in the same root is notable — Psa 10:4 uses the aph root in the phrase "in the pride of his face the wicked does not seek him," suggesting that face, anger, and arrogance converge in the same semantic space. Phase 2 may find that these root families together build a composite Hebrew picture of anger as simultaneously physiological (nostril-flaring), thermal (burning/heat), and social-transgressional (overflowing/arrogance).

**Verdict:** The AVAR family adds the overflow/transgression dimension to the anger field. The arrogance pairing within the same root is significant for the inner-being model's account of how anger and pride are related.

### Root Family: QTSPH — qa.tsaph (H7107), qe.tseph (H7110A), qe.tseph/splinter (H7110B)

The QTSPH family covers the wrathful/displeased dimension with approximately 35 verbal occurrences and 28 nominal occurrences. The verbal form carries all three major stems: Qal (to be wroth), Hiphil (to provoke to wrath), and Hithpael (to put oneself in a rage, anger oneself). The Hithpael reflexive — to anger oneself, to put oneself into a state of rage — is one of the clearest examples in the word set of anger as a self-directed process. Combined with the CHAR Hithpael (to heat oneself in vexation), this confirms that the OT recognises the deliberate self-cultivation of anger as a distinct category of inner activity.

The splinter sub-sense (H7110B, ~1x, meaning uncertain) is retained as a data point. Whether the broken-twig image and the wrath/rage meaning share a common root metaphor — anger as something that snaps or fractures — cannot be determined without Phase 2 analysis.

**Verdict:** The QTSPH family provides the most explicit reflexive anger vocabulary in the set: the Hithpael forms of both qa.tsaph and cha.rah model anger as something that can be cultivated in the self, which is significant for any inner-being account of anger's relationship to the will.

### Root Family: ZAM — za.am (H2195)

Za.am appears in both the Abomination word set (registry 001, verbal form H2194) and here as a noun (H2195). In the verse set for this registry, za.am functions primarily as divine indignation, with Psa 38:3's connection of God's indignation to the psalmist's loss of physical soundness and bone-health being particularly significant — indignation producing somatic consequences in the one who receives it. Jer 15:17 uses za.am for the prophet's own inner state of being filled with indignation, suggesting the term can apply to human inner states as well as divine ones. The Phase 2 decision on whether za.am belongs primarily in the abomination or anger registry has not yet been made.

### Root Family: KAS — ka.as (H3707), ka.a.s (H3708B)

The KAS family covers the vexation/provocation register, with the verb (~53x) carrying Qal (to be vexed, be angry), Piel (to provoke to anger), and Hiphil (to vex, provoke to anger) forms. The verb is causative-heavy: much of its verse set involves provoking God to anger through idolatry, which is the dominant KAS context in the OT (Deuteronomy through Kings, with heavy concentration in Jeremiah). This makes ka.as theologically significant: it is the primary word for human actions that trigger divine anger. The noun ka.a.s covers the experiential state of vexation/grief that results from provocation — it appears in both an anger register (God's vexation with Israel) and a distress register (Hannah's vexation, Job's vexation, Ecclesiastes' repeated use of vexation as a description of the futility of wisdom).

The na.ats (to spurn) entry was grouped with ka.as in the source file and is retained in the KAS context. Its connection to anger is indirect — spurning as the attitude that underlies provocation — but Num 14:11 (how long will this people despise me?) places despising/spurning and God's anger in direct proximity.

### Root Family: RGAZ — ro.gez (H7267), re.gaz (H7265)

The RGAZ family covers agitation, turmoil, and trembling/trepidation. Ro.gez includes both the raging/agitation sense and the trembling/trepidation sense within a single word, which is notable: the one experiencing rage and the one experiencing fear of rage may use the same vocabulary. Re.gaz (Aramaic, Ezr 5:12) is the causative enrage form, used to describe Israel's provoking of the God of heaven. The connection between RGAZ and anger is looser than the primary families but is retained for Phase 2 investigation of the trembling-rage axis.

### Root Family: ORG — orgē (G3709), orgizō (G3710), parorgizō, parorgismos

The ORG family is the primary NT anger root, covering both the settled dispositional anger (orgē) and the verbal entering-of-anger-state (orgizō). The LSJ confirms that orgē originally meant natural impulse, temperament, or disposition before narrowing to anger specifically — the classical Greek background shows anger as a specialisation of a broader inner propensity concept. This is significant: the NT's use of orgē draws on a term that historically named the whole dispositional orientation of a person, not merely a reactive emotional state.

Orgē is the term most consistently used for God's eschatological wrath in the NT — the wrath to come (Mat 3:7; Luk 3:7), the day of wrath (Rom 2:5), the wrath that is being revealed (Rom 1:18), the wrath from which Christ delivers (1Th 1:10). This eschatological concentration of orgē in the NT represents a distinct development from the OT anger vocabulary, where divine anger is primarily responsive and historical rather than eschatological and judicial.

Eph 4:26 — "be angry and do not sin; do not let the sun go down on your anger" — is the most theologically complex verse in the NT anger set. It is a command permitting anger while prohibiting its unchecked continuation. The verse distinguishes between the anger state and the sinful expression or prolongation of anger, implying that anger itself is not sinful but that its duration and direction can become sinful. This distinction will be significant for the inner-being model.

Mar 3:5 records Jesus looking around with orgē, grieved at the hardness of heart — pairing anger and grief as co-occurring inner states in the same moment. This is the only NT verse in the set where righteous anger is attributed to Jesus as a named inner state, and it is worth noting that it co-occurs with grief rather than appearing alone.

Jam 1:19-20 states that human anger (orgē) does not produce the righteousness of God, placing a categorical limit on the inner-being function of human anger that has no direct equivalent in the OT. This is a theological judgment on the anger state itself as generative capacity.

**Verdict:** The ORG family carries the primary NT anger vocabulary. The eschatological development of orgē, the Eph 4:26 permission/limit distinction, and the Mar 3:5 anger-grief pairing are three data points that will require sustained Phase 2 analysis.

### Root Family: THUM — thumos (G2372), thumoō

Thumos is the NT's secondary anger term, carrying the connotation of sudden passionate outburst as distinct from orgē's settled disposition. The LSJ entry for thumos is one of the most revealing in the entire word set. In classical Greek, thumos named the soul or spirit as the principle of life, feeling, and thought — it was a comprehensive inner faculty word covering breath/life, desire/appetite, mind/will, courage, and the seat of emotions including anger, joy, and grief. By NT usage, thumos has narrowed significantly toward the angry/passionate register while retaining the intensely passionate character.

The Revelation usage of thumos is concentrated in the wrath-of-God contexts, often paired with orgē (Rev 16:19 "the cup of the wine of the fury of his wrath"; Rev 19:15 "the fury of the wrath of God the Almighty"). The pairing of both NT anger words in these eschatological contexts — thumos providing the passionate/burning intensity and orgē the settled judicial character — suggests the two terms are used together precisely to indicate the fullness of divine anger rather than as interchangeable synonyms.

The classical LSJ background for thumos as a comprehensive soul-faculty word — covering life, desire, emotion, thought, and anger — is a significant Phase 2 flag for the NT faculty-naming investigation. The word has a history of naming something very close to what the inner-being model calls the soul, before narrowing in NT usage to its passionate/anger register.

**Verdict:** Thumos adds the passionate-outburst register to the NT anger field and is distinguished from orgē's settled-disposition character. Its classical history as a comprehensive soul-faculty word is a significant Phase 2 flag.

### Root Family: PAROX — paroxunō (G3947)

The PAROX family covers the provoking/sharpening dimension. Paroxunō (to provoke, sharpen) occurs twice in the NT. Act 17:16 reports Paul's spirit being provoked within him at the sight of idols in Athens — a significant verse because it locates the provoked state in the spirit (pneuma) as the inner faculty and connects it to idolatry, which is the same trigger as the dominant OT ka.as context. 1Cor 13:5 places paroxunō in the love chapter — love is not irritable or provoked — setting the provoked state as the direct opposite of charitable love. The related word paroxusmos (stirring up) also appears in the word family.

### Peripheral Term Analysis: NTAR, NPHL, APH/baking, IT, CHAM

**Na.tar (H5201) — to keep/harbour anger:** The idiom of keeping anger (na.tar aph) frames anger as something that can be stored, maintained, or released — treating the anger state as a retentive capacity of the inner life. Lev 19:18 commands against bearing a grudge alongside the command to love neighbour, making harboured anger a direct ethical category. Psa 103:9 applies the idiom to God — he will not keep his anger forever — making the release of stored anger a theological statement about divine mercy.

**Na.phal (H5307K) — face falling:** The falling-face idiom appears in Gen 4:5-6 as the somatic expression of Cain's anger — God addresses both the anger state and the fallen face as part of the same inner-outer dynamic. This connects na.phal directly to the APH (face/nose/anger) cluster, suggesting that the face-falling is the visible somatic correlate of the anger state named by aph.

**A.phah (H0644) — to bake:** The Hosea 7:4-6 oven/baker passages use baking language as an explicit inner-state metaphor. The sequence in Hos 7:6 — hearts like an oven, anger smoldering through the night, blazing in the morning — describes an inner state with temporal progression: ignition, sustained heat, and final eruption. The baking process provides the metaphorical structure for understanding how anger develops and intensifies over time as an inner activity.

**It (H5860A) — to scream:** The single verse (1Sa 25:14, Nabal railed at David's messengers) records the verbal/somatic output of an inner anger state. The screaming is the expressed outer form; the connection to the anger field is through the scream as anger's acoustic expression.

**Cha.mam (H2552) — to warm:** Psa 39:3 — "my heart became hot within me; as I mused, the fire burned; then I spoke with my tongue" — is the most significant verse in the peripheral set. It gives a three-stage inner sequence: warming (somatic/inner), musing (cognitive), then speaking (external). This is a rare explicit description of the inner process of anger moving from bodily state through thought to expression, and it uses the CHAM root.

## Section 3 — Where Sub-terms Do Different Work

The anger word set in Hebrew does not present a set of near-synonyms. Each primary root family captures a distinct dimension of the anger semantic field:

APH captures anger as physiological arousal — the nostril as both the anatomical site of breath and the somatic expression of aroused displeasure. It is the most embodied anger term.

CHAR captures anger as burning/heat process — the kindling, sustaining, and self-kindling of an inner thermal state. It is the most processual anger term.

CHEM captures anger as hot displeasure that overflows into rage — including the poison/venom extension. It is the most intensive anger term.

AVAR captures anger as overflow and transgression of boundary — with arrogance as a co-expression. It is the most transgressive anger term.

QTSPH captures the state of being wroth/displeased, with strong reflexive forms for self-induced rage. It is the most volitional anger term.

KAS captures the provocation-vexation axis — anger as triggered inner distress and as the state produced in God by human covenant betrayal. It is the most relational-provocational anger term.

ZAM captures divine indignation — morally grounded displeasure — as an inner state with somatic consequences for the recipient.

In the NT, orgē and thumos do different work:

Orgē names settled dispositional anger, judicial wrath, and the eschatological wrath that is building and will be revealed. It is the more cognitive/dispositional term.

Thumos names the passionate-outburst, the sudden burning intensity of anger. It is the more emotional/eruptive term. The Revelation pairing of both terms (thumos tēs orgēs — the fury of the wrath) suggests they are complementary rather than synonymous.

## Section 4 — OT/NT Register Analysis

The most significant OT/NT difference in this word set is the eschatological concentration of orgē in the NT. In the OT, divine anger (aph, cha.ron, qe.tseph, che.mah, ev.rah) is characteristically historical and responsive — it is provoked by covenant violation, expressed in historical judgment, and capable of being turned away by repentance and intercession (Moses at Sinai, Hos 11:9 God restraining his burning anger). The slow-anger formula (Exo 34:6 and parallels) defines God's anger as something that exists within a larger framework of mercy and steadfast love.

In the NT, orgē carries a forward-looking judicial dimension that the OT vocabulary does not. The wrath that is coming (Mat 3:7), the wrath being stored up (Rom 2:5), the wrath from which Christ delivers (1Th 1:10), and the wrath poured out in Revelation — these present divine anger as an eschatological verdict that is approaching rather than a historical event that has already occurred and can be turned away. This represents a genuine development in how the inner-state of divine anger is understood theologically.

The human anger vocabulary shows a different development. The OT human anger texts in this set are largely descriptive narratives — Moses's anger at the army officers, Saul's anger at David's acclaim, Naaman's anger at Elisha. The NT human anger texts are more prescriptive and ethical — be angry and do not sin, the anger of man does not produce God's righteousness, love is not provoked. The NT shifts the anger vocabulary in human contexts from description to ethical regulation.

Eph 4:26-31 is the clearest instance of this shift: it permits anger (verse 26), limits its duration (verse 26), warns against giving the devil a foothold through anger (verse 27), and then commands the putting away of all bitterness, wrath, and anger as a category (verse 31). This is not a simple contradiction; it reflects a distinction between the immediate anger state (permitted, limited) and the settled disposition of wrath (to be put away). The orgē/thumos distinction maps partially onto this, with thumos as the immediate outburst and orgē as the more settled state, though the verse 31 command uses both terms together.

## Section 5 — Data Quality Notes

This word set is the largest in the programme to date by term count. The data quality is high for the primary terms (aph, che.mah, cha.ron, cha.rah, qa.tsaph, orgē, thumos, orgizō) where full word analysis blocks, occurrence counts, and verse sets are all present.

Several structural complexities require Phase 2 attention. The aph triple entry (H0639G/H/I) preserves the physiological-inner being layering but produces an artificially large occurrence count figure (~278x appears three times in the term inventory). The actual occurrence count for aph as a single word is ~278x; the three sub-entries are different semantic registers of the same term, not three separate words with independent counts. Phase 2 should address how to count and present aph's occurrences.

The Aramaic terms (che.ma H2528; re.gaz H7265) carry their data but cannot be contextually assessed until the Daniel and Ezra passages are analysed. The choice of Aramaic forms in those contexts may or may not be significant.

Three NT stub terms (mēnis, enkotēma, cholos) have no word analysis data. They appear in the source header and require Phase 2 STEP investigation.

The parorgismos entry has a source block that resolves to orgizō rather than parorgismos itself; the Strong's number is marked as uncertain. This should be resolved in Phase 2.

The peripheral terms (a.phah, it, na.phal, na.tar, ta.cha.rah, ro.gez) are retained with their flags. Their Phase 2 value will depend on context analysis of the verse sets.
