This document records two passes over the forgiveness word study:

**Pass 1 — Catalogue application:** Each question from the Grace catalogue (v1.1) is applied to the forgiveness study. For each question the result is recorded as:
- **ANSWER** — the forgiveness study yields a clear response
- **PARTIAL** — the study touches the question but does not fully answer it
- **SILENT** — the study says nothing on this question
- **N/A** — the question structurally does not apply to this word (with reason)

Where the study is SILENT on a question that should have an answer, this is flagged as **[SHOULD ANSWER]** with a brief note on why.

**Pass 2 — Catalogue extension:** Observations in the forgiveness study not reached by any existing catalogue question are identified. New questions are formulated and added to the catalogue.

# PASS 1 — CATALOGUE APPLICATION

---

## Section 1 Questions — Word Characteristic Summary

---

**Q001: What is the structural disposition of the word — where does it originate?**

ANSWER. Forgiveness is a releasing act — the deliberate cancellation of a legitimate claim. It originates in God's own character and disposition; human forgiveness is downstream of divine forgiveness received.

---

**Q002: What determines whether the word is extended or withheld?**

ANSWER. Whether the word is extended depends on the inner posture of the one who holds the claim. Prior receipt of forgiveness and the inner compassion it produces determine extension. Hardness of heart, pride, and self-justification determine withholding.

---

**Q003: What are the distinct modes of operation of the word in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Three modes are identifiable: (1) as something received — a settled possession, a changed inner standing; (2) as a sustained inner orientation of release rather than retention; (3) as something extended outward from within the person.

---

**Q004: What does the word produce in the inner being of the recipient?**

ANSWER. Receiving forgiveness produces love as a downstream inner state (Luke 7:47), and reverential awe — not relief but the awe of standing before the one who held your debt and chose to release it.

---

**Q005: What does the word produce in the relational position of the recipient toward another?**

ANSWER. It produces restored standing before the one who forgave. It also opens the inner capacity to extend the same releasing act toward others — the recipient's relational posture toward others is transformed.

---

**Q006: What does the word reveal about the disposition of God toward the human being?**

ANSWER. God is the primary source and defining model. *Sallāch* is used exclusively of God as subject. The disposition is named as permanent, intrinsic character — not occasional act but essential quality (Psalm 86:5). God holds forgiveness as a possession and makes it accessible through relationship (Psalm 130:4).

---

**Q007: What does the word reveal about the basis on which God's disposition toward a person is established?**

ANSWER. Not on the basis of merit. The forgiveness formula in Leviticus is objective and covenantal — not dependent on the worshipper's emotional state. Daniel 9:19 appeals to God's own name and character, not Israel's merit. The new covenant promise (Jeremiah 31:34) is unilateral.

---

**Q008: Where, somatically or relationally, does the word locate the reality of being in favour?**

PARTIAL. The study does not use explicit somatic location language for forgiveness received in the way the grace study uses gaze-language. The closest is Matthew 18:35 — "from your heart" — which locates forgiveness in the heart as the inner locus where the claim is either held or released.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question should be asked of this word. The heart is named as the location. This observation deserves an explicit answer in the catalogue record.

---

**Q009: Does the word describe a one-time act or an ongoing condition — and how are these distinguished?**

ANSWER. Both are present. Forgiveness is received as a settled possession — "we have… the forgiveness" (Ephesians 1:7). It is also an ongoing orientation: the inner posture of release rather than retention is described as sustained. The act is completed; the orientation continues.

---

**Q010: What spatial or directional language is used to describe the ongoing condition the word produces?**

PARTIAL. No explicit spatial metaphor equivalent to "standing in grace" appears for forgiveness. The nearest is the locational language of Psalm 130:4 — forgiveness is *with* God, located with him. The word moves from God toward the person; the ongoing condition is described as a settled possession, not a space inhabited.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies and the study has a partial answer. The absence of spatial metaphor in the forgiveness study may itself be a significant observation — forgiveness does not appear to generate the same spatial condition-language that grace does.

---

**Q011: Does the word produce enabling capacity in the inner being — and if so, what kind?**

ANSWER. Yes — forgiveness received opens the inner capacity for forgiveness extended. Worship and atonement are described as upstream of the human capacity to forgive. The enabling is relational and moral, not functional in the sense of gifting for a task.

---

**Q012: What character quality does the word produce in the inner being?**

ANSWER. A settled inner orientation of release rather than retention. Compassion as a visceral, body-involving movement of the interior that precedes the act of forgiving. Love as the first downstream fruit.

---

**Q013: How does the word express itself in relation to others?**

ANSWER. As the release of a legitimate claim held against them. As the cancellation of debt. As the opening of relational space from which another had been closed. As the enablement of continuation where continuation would otherwise be impossible.

---

**Q014: What inner condition or orientation is identified as the ground of genuine expression of the word?**

ANSWER. Prior receipt of divine forgiveness is the ground. The inner compassion that precedes the act is upstream of the decision. Prayer — which keeps the forgiver in touch with their own need — feeds the capacity.

---

**Q015: Is there a discernible sequence or movement in the way the word operates through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Yes. The study names a sequence: divine forgiveness received → inner compassion and love produced → capacity to extend forgiveness to others opened → forgiveness extended. The sequence has both a vertical axis (received from God) and a horizontal axis (extended to others).

---

**Q016: What direction of movement does the word follow within and through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Inward from God first (received); then outward toward others (extended). The vertical precedes and enables the horizontal.

---

**Q017: What does the word identify as its originating source?**

ANSWER. God's own character — his essential disposition, not a response to the situation. The *sālach* family is exclusively divine in usage. *Sallāch* names forgiveness as a divine character quality before it names any act.

---

**Q018: What does the word reveal about the origin of the human capacity to seek it?**

PARTIAL. The study names the act of seeking forgiveness (Daniel 9:19 — escalating urgent imperatives) as an act of confidence in God's character. It does not explicitly state whether the capacity to seek it is itself divinely given, as the grace study states for supplication. This is a structural gap relative to the grace study's finding in Zechariah 12:10.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. The study is silent on whether seeking forgiveness is itself a given capacity. This should be examined in the cross-study analysis of Zechariah 12:10 (which anchors both grace and forgiveness-related vocabulary).

---

**Q019: Does the word share an etymological root with another inner-being characteristic — and if so, what is that characteristic?**

ANSWER. No — the study explicitly states that forgiveness shares no vocabulary with any other word study. Its terms are exclusively its own. There is no shared root with adjacent characteristics, in contrast to grace's root-sharing with joy and supplication.

---

**Q020: What does the word produce as a natural inner response in the one who receives it?**

ANSWER. Love (Luke 7:47 — forgiven much → loves much) and reverential awe (Psalm 130:4 — forgiveness produces fear rather than presumption). These are named as specific inner orientations, not vague feelings.

## Section 2 Questions — Word Impact Description

---

**Q021: What inner-being logic or orientation functions as the structural opposite of the word?**

ANSWER. Retention of the claim — the inner insistence that the debt remains, the account is still open. The image is the person who receives mercy and immediately refuses to extend it: the logic of the closed inner posture.

---

**Q022: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to receive the word?**

PARTIAL. The study does not explicitly develop the merit-logic as a barrier to receiving forgiveness in the way the grace study does. The logic of retained debt and account-keeping is developed as a barrier to extending forgiveness. The receiving side is less developed here.

---

**Q023: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. The cultivated sense of entitlement to the debt, pride, self-justification, and comparison of one's own debts to those of others all close the inner hand that forgiveness requires to open.

---

**Q024: What is the inner-being condition of the person who has received the word but failed to recognise it?**

PARTIAL. The study does not name a specific term equivalent to *acharistos* for forgiveness. The parable of the unforgiving servant (Matthew 18:35) names the failure: the servant received forgiveness verbally but it did not reach the heart. The condition is described behaviourally (he immediately demanded his own debt repaid) rather than as a named inner-being state.

---

**Q025: What inner-being experience cultivates the capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. The prior experience of being forgiven — and understanding the magnitude of what has been released. The more profoundly a person understands their own forgiveness, the more freely they can forgive.

---

**Q026: What inner-being posture does the act of earnest appeal express?**

ANSWER. Confidence in God's character, not transaction between equals. The posture of the creature before the Creator: knowing two things simultaneously — that the need is real, and that only the offended party can supply the remedy (Daniel 9:19).

---

**Q027: What somatic forms does the inner act of seeking the word take?**

PARTIAL. The study references Daniel 9 (sackcloth and ashes implied by the broader context) and the visceral compassion (*splagchnizomai*) that precedes forgiving. But explicit somatic vocabulary for seeking forgiveness is less developed in the forgiveness study than in the grace study.

---

**Q028: What inner-being orientation closes off the reception of the word?**

PARTIAL. The study focuses more on what closes off the extension of the word. For reception, Mark 3:29 (blasphemy against the Holy Spirit) is cited as the outer limit — the condition of permanently closed reception — but the inner-being logic of that closure is not developed in detail.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. The study names the outer limit of closed reception without fully characterising its inner-being mechanics. This is a gap that cross-study analysis should address.

---

**Q029: What relational orientation toward others diminishes the operation of the word?**

ANSWER. Hardness of heart. The cultivated comparison of one's own debts to those of others. The refusal to allow the record of another's offence to be cleared.

---

**Q030: What is the relationship between the word and conditions of weakness in the inner being?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop the word's relationship to weakness explicitly. The closest parallel is the state of incapacity in which forgiveness is received — but this is framed as guilt and deadness rather than weakness.

---

**Q031: What kind of inner-being transformation does the word produce — does it change the condition or the person's orientation to the condition?**

ANSWER. The study names genuine inner transformation: receiving forgiveness changes the person's inner orientation (Luke 7:47 — love produced). It also changes relational standing (restored before God). Both condition and orientation appear to change.

---

**Q032: What is the first inner-being response to receiving the word unexpectedly?**

PARTIAL. The study names reverential awe (Psalm 130:4) as a downstream response. It does not describe an immediate first response in the same way the grace study identifies fear as the first response to unexpected divine favour.

---

**Q033: What is the sequence of inner-being states as the word takes hold in the person?**

ANSWER. The study implies a sequence: encounter with the character of God who holds and releases → reverential awe → love produced → capacity to extend the word. But this sequence is not laid out as explicitly as in the grace study.

---

**Q034: In the eschatological context, what is the first inner-being response to the full outpouring of the word?**

SILENT. The forgiveness study does not develop an eschatological dimension in the same way as the grace study (Zechariah 12:10). Jeremiah 31:34 is the closest — the new covenant promise — but its inner-being sequence is not developed.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question should be asked. Jeremiah 31:34 is the forgiveness study's primary eschatological anchor. The inner-being response to the new covenant declaration of forgiveness deserves examination.

---

**Q035: What is the relationship between mourning and the full reception of the word in the inner being?**

PARTIAL. The study does not directly develop mourning as a first fruit of forgiveness. The connections section notes repentance as a consistent companion, and repentance carries mourning vocabulary — but this is deferred to cross-study analysis.

---

**Q036: What quality of inner-being stability does the word produce?**

ANSWER. A settled possession — "we have… the forgiveness" (Ephesians 1:7). Restored standing before God that holds regardless of changing circumstances. The account is genuinely closed, not deferred.

---

**Q037: What orientation does the stability produced by the word carry?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop a forward orientation for forgiveness equivalent to grace's hope-orientation. Forgiveness resolves the past (the account is closed); its forward dimension is not explicitly developed.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. Whether forgiveness carries a forward orientation is a genuine question the study leaves open.

---

**Q038: What capacity toward others does the word produce in the inner being of the one who has received it?**

ANSWER. The capacity to forgive others. The capacity to open relational space from which another had been closed. The enabling of continuation where continuation would otherwise be impossible.

---

**Q039: What is the inner-being source of the capacity to extend the word to others — is it discipline or transformation?**

ANSWER. Transformation — the inner compassion that precedes the act is itself upstream of the decision. The encounter with divine forgiveness softens the inner person. Prayer feeds it by keeping the forgiver in contact with their own need.

---

**Q040: How does the word relate to genuine human effort — does it displace it, supplement it, or reconstitute it?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop the effort-agency paradox as explicitly as the grace study. Forgiving from the heart (Matthew 18:35) implies a genuine inner act of release — it is not passive. But the relationship between divine enabling and human effort in the act of forgiving is not explicitly examined.

---

**Q041: What does the word reveal about the source of its human expression — is it generated from within the human being or received from elsewhere?**

ANSWER. Received before given. The study states this explicitly: "it is a characteristic received before it is given." Human forgiveness mirrors and flows from divine forgiveness — not an independent moral achievement.

ANSWER (Ephesians 1:7). The study names a chain: redemption → blood → forgiveness → grace. Grace is the upstream disposition; forgiveness is the specific act it generates.

ANSWER (Leviticus 4:20; Daniel 9:19). Merit ruled out. The appeal in Daniel is to God's character, not Israel's. The Leviticus formula is objective — not dependent on emotional state.

ANSWER (Ephesians 1:7). The study names the category: a settled possession — "we have the forgiveness." It is held, not merely hoped for.

---

**Q045 type: What prior condition does the access-vocabulary in the verse imply?**

ANSWER (multiple verses). The condition is guilt — real debt genuinely owed. Forgiveness addresses a real wrong, not a perceived or nominal one. The release is real because the debt is real.

---

**Q046 type: Does the verse describe the outcome as a completed event or as a continuing state?**

ANSWER (Ephesians 1:7; Jeremiah 31:34). Both. The act is completed (the account is closed); the result is a continuing possession and a lasting change in standing.

---

**Q047 type: What is the context in which the word is declared sufficient, and what alternative was sought?**

ANSWER (Daniel 9:19). The context is urgent petition for national restoration. The alternative sought is immediate divine action. The study reads this as confidence in God's character.

---

**Q048 type: In what mode is the word conveyed — relational address or impersonal provision?**

ANSWER (multiple). Relational — the study consistently frames forgiveness as a relational act: the offended party chooses to release the debt. Leviticus formula is more covenantal-objective, but still mediated and relational.

---

**Q049 type: What condition does the verse identify as the location where the word is most fully operative?**

ANSWER. Guilt — the moral condition of genuine debt. Also: the condition of being beyond one's own capacity to remedy (Daniel 9:19 — creature before Creator).

---

**Q050 type: What is the relationship between the word and the condition in which it operates?**

ANSWER. Forgiveness addresses the condition directly — it cancels the debt and closes the account. Unlike grace in weakness (which inhabits rather than resolves), forgiveness appears to resolve the condition of guilt, not merely inhabit it.

---

**Q051 type: What reason does the verse give for the extension of the word — in the recipient's actions or elsewhere?**

ANSWER (Psalm 86:5; Psalm 130:4). God's own character and possession — not the recipient's merit or appeal quality. The readiness to pardon is already present in the one who is addressed.

---

**Q052 type: Where does the verse locate the reality of being in favour — in a specific faculty of the giver?**

PARTIAL. No faculty-of-the-giver language equivalent to the eye-gaze of grace. God's forgiveness is located as a divine possession (Psalm 130:4 — "with you").

---

**Q053 type: What practical outcome does being in favour produce for the recipient?**

ANSWER. Restored standing before God. The account closed. The claim released. Access to worship made genuine (Matthew 5:24).

**Q054 type: What inner state does the verse identify as the first response to the announcement of favour?**

ANSWER (Psalm 130:4). Reverential awe — not presumption, not mere relief. The encounter with one who held your debt and chose to release it.

---

**Q055 type: What comes first — the establishment of standing, or the commission that follows?**

ANSWER. Standing first. Forgiveness received precedes and enables everything that follows — including the capacity to extend forgiveness, to worship authentically, to love.

---

**Q056 type: What does the verse identify as the concrete content of the favour given?**

ANSWER (Jeremiah 31:34). Two distinct acts named: the releasing of the penalty, and the ceasing to remember. Both are required for genuine forgiveness — not forbearance but actual cancellation.

---

**Q057 type: What spatial language does the verse use?**

PARTIAL. "With you there is forgiveness" (Psalm 130:4) — forgiveness is located with God. No standing-in language equivalent to grace's Romans 5:2 spatial metaphor.

---

**Q058 type: Does the verse distinguish between the means of entry and the condition itself?**

PARTIAL. The study names faith as the instrument of receiving (Acts 10:43 in the connections section) but does not develop the entry/condition distinction as explicitly as the grace study does.

PARTIAL. Not developed. Jeremiah 31:34 is the new covenant promise and is forward-facing as promise, but the forgiveness study does not develop hope as forgiveness's forward dimension.

## Section 4 Questions — Original Language Vocabulary

---

**Q086: What is the range of translation equivalents for the term — and does the range suggest narrow or broad semantic scope?**

ANSWER. *Aphesis*: forgiveness, pardon, release, cancellation of a debt, liberty. Broad scope — spanning legal, personal, financial, and relational registers. *Sālach*: narrower — to forgive, to pardon (divine subject only). The Hebrew is theologically concentrated; the Greek is structurally broad.

---

**Q087: Does the translation range cluster around one aspect of meaning or span multiple aspects?**

ANSWER. *Aphiēmi* spans three registers: leaving/abandoning, forgiving, permitting/granting access. All share the underlying act of non-retention. The moral character depends entirely on what is released and why.

---

**Q088: What is the root etymology of the term — and does it connect it to another inner-being characteristic?**

ANSWER. No shared root with any other word study — stated explicitly. The vocabulary is exclusively its own.

---

**Q089: What does the root etymology reveal about the inner state associated with the act the term names?**

PARTIAL. The Greek root (*aphiēmi* — to release, to let go) encodes the act-structure (releasing what is held) but does not reveal the inner state of the actor in the way *charis* etymology reveals gladness. The focus is on the act, not the actor's inner state at the time of acting.

---

**Q090: How many distinct semantic domains does the term operate in — and what are they?**

ANSWER. Four named in the synthesis: legal (cancellation of real debt), relational (restoration of broken standing), characterological (divine disposition), transformative (produces downstream inner orientations).

---

**Q091: What was the dominant sense of the term in its pre-biblical usage?**

ANSWER. *Aphiēmi* family: releasing prisoners, cancelling financial obligations, dismissing persons from service. The act-structure (releasing something held) is pre-biblical; the theological application is the New Testament contribution.

---

**Q092: What relational economy did the classical usage assume?**

PARTIAL. Not developed for forgiveness in the same way as for grace. The pre-biblical usage was legal and financial rather than relational-reciprocal.

---

**Q093: What change in relational economy does the term undergo between classical and New Testament usage?**

ANSWER. The New Testament takes a word with legal-financial and social releasing senses and applies it to the releasing of sin's hold on a person — theologically concentrated but structurally continuous with the pre-biblical meaning.

---

**Q094: Which bodily organs or locations does the verse evidence associate with the term — and what role does each play?**

ANSWER. The heart (Matthew 18:35 — the inner locus of genuine forgiveness). The inner parts — *splagchnizomai* (compassion preceding the act). No mouth or flesh associations developed in the same way as for grace.

---

**Q095: What are the distinct senses of the verbal form — and do they all operate in the same relational direction?**

ANSWER. *Aphiēmi*: leaving/abandoning, forgiving, permitting access. They do not all operate in the same moral direction — the same act-structure can name faithful forgiving or faithless abandonment (Revelation 2:4).

---

**Q096: Does the verbal form distinguish the disposition of the giver — or is it structurally neutral?**

ANSWER. Structurally neutral in form. The moral character depends entirely on what is released and why. Same conclusion as for *charizō* in the grace study.

---

**Q097: Where is the moral distinction between the uses of the verbal form located — in the act or in the giver?**

ANSWER. In what is released and the reason for releasing — not in the form of the act itself. The study makes this explicit.

---

**Q098: What does the negative form of the term name — and how is it defined relative to the positive form?**

PARTIAL. The study does not develop a named negative form equivalent to *acharistos* for grace. The nearest is the hardened inner posture of the unforgiving servant — but this is behavioural/narrative rather than a specific lexical term.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The absence of a named failure-mode term in the forgiveness vocabulary is itself significant and should be noted explicitly. Is there a Hebrew or Greek term for the person who withholds forgiveness? This is a vocabulary gap the study does not address directly.

---

**Q099: Does the negative form describe a moral failing, an inner-being condition, or both?**

PARTIAL (see Q098). Not developed because no named negative form is identified.

---

**Q100: How does the particularised form relate to the primary form — what is the difference in scope?**

PARTIAL. No particularised form equivalent to *charisma* is identified in the forgiveness vocabulary.

## Observations in the Forgiveness Study Not Reached by Existing Questions

The following observations appear in the forgiveness study but were not surfaced by any existing catalogue question. New questions are formulated for each and added to the catalogue.

### New Pair F-001

**[OBS]** The word has a defined outer limit — a condition under which it is stated to be permanently unavailable (Mark 3:29: blasphemy against the Holy Spirit).

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks about limits or boundary conditions of the word.

**New Q:** Does the word have a stated outer limit — a condition under which it cannot operate or does not reach?

### New Pair F-002

**[OBS]** The same word-family that names the right use of the releasing act (forgiveness) also names its wrong use (abandonment of first love, Revelation 2:4). The faculty can be directed well or badly.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks whether the word's core faculty can be misused or inverted.

**New Q:** Can the core act-structure of the word be directed toward a wrong object — and if so, what does that misuse look like?

---

### New Pair F-003

**[OBS]** The word has both a vertical axis (received from God) and a horizontal axis (extended to others). These two axes are structurally linked — a broken horizontal relationship creates an obstacle to vertical offering (Matthew 5:24).

**Existing gap:** The three-axis template (inner being, God, others) is applied, but the structural interdependence between the vertical and horizontal axes is not explicitly probed.

**New Q:** What is the structural relationship between the word's vertical axis (toward God) and its horizontal axis (toward others) — do they operate independently or does one affect the other?

---

### New Pair F-004

**[OBS]** The primary Hebrew verb for divine forgiveness (*sālach*) is used exclusively of God as subject — no human being is ever the subject of this verb. This is a grammatical restriction with theological significance.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks whether the word's usage is grammatically or lexically restricted to a specific subject.

**New Q:** Is the primary term for the word grammatically or lexically restricted to a specific actor as subject — and what does that restriction reveal?

---

### New Pair F-005

**[OBS]** In the old covenant, forgiveness is administered through sacrifice and priestly mediation — the mechanism is outward; the inner reality conferred is restored standing. Forgiveness is objective and covenantally administered, not dependent on the worshipper's emotional state.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks about the mechanism of administration or the relationship between outer mechanism and inner reality.

**New Q:** What is the mechanism through which the word is administered or conveyed — and what is the relationship between the outward mechanism and the inner reality it produces?

---

### New Pair F-006

**[OBS]** The word is genuinely conditional — God grants it in response to repentance and intercession; he can withhold it where the covenant has been finally broken. It is not automatically available.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks about conditionality — whether the word operates unconditionally or has stated conditions for extension.

**New Q:** Does the word operate unconditionally — or does it have stated conditions under which it is granted or withheld?

---

### New Pair F-007

**[OBS]** Jeremiah 31:34 joins two distinct acts: releasing the penalty of sin, and ceasing to remember it. Genuine forgiveness requires both — not forbearance (tolerating a debt while retaining a record) but actual cancellation (releasing the claim and expunging the account).

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks whether the word is a single act or a compound of distinct acts.

**New Q:** Is the word a single act or a compound of distinct component acts — and if compound, what are the components and are they always simultaneous?

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks what the word enables in relationship that would otherwise be impossible.

**New Q:** What does the word make possible in a relationship that would otherwise be closed or broken — and what relational cycle does it break?

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks about the word's relationship to worship or to other spiritual practices it conditions or enables.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks whether the downstream effects of the word are proportional to the degree of the word received.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks about the degree of vocabulary isolation or overlap as a structural signal about the word's relational position.
