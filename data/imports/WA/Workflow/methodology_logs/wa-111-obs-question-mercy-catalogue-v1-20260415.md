# PASS 1 — CATALOGUE APPLICATION

---

## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary
*(Q001–Q020)*

---

**Q001: What is the structural disposition of the word — where does it originate?**

ANSWER. Mercy is a disposition that moves toward another in their need, guilt, or helplessness — and acts. It originates in God's own compassionate character. Human mercy is described as a response to, reflection of, and participation in divine mercy — not an independent human virtue.

---

**Q002: What determines whether the word is extended or withheld?**

ANSWER. Extension is determined by the inner posture of the one who holds power: whether they lay aside the claim they could press. What feeds extension is the prior experience of having received mercy. What blocks extension is self-sufficiency, self-righteousness, and the inner posture that does not believe it needs mercy itself. The unmerciful servant's failure is diagnostic: mercy received but not integrated at depth leaves the inner character unchanged.

---

**Q003: What are the distinct modes of operation of the word in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Four modes named simultaneously: (1) a felt inner movement — visceral, unchosen, womb-love quality; (2) a settled disposition of character — the habitual openness of the person for whom mercy is natural rather than forced; (3) a relational commitment — covenantal faithfulness that holds to another because a bond exists; (4) a concrete act — the specific extension of relief, covering, forgiveness, sparing. These are not four different things but four dimensions of the same thing fully expressed.

---

**Q004: What does the word produce in the inner being of the recipient?**

ANSWER. A changed inner standing — identity reconstituted by mercy received (1 Peter 2:10). Openness — a heart not armoured against the weakness of others. The capacity for mercy toward others. Comfort (2 Corinthians 1:3). Reverent silence in the case of comprehensive mercy received (Ezekiel 16:63 — mercy at its deepest produces the quiet of the fully forgiven, not self-congratulation).

---

**Q005: What does the word produce in the relational position of the recipient toward another?**

ANSWER. The capacity to extend mercy toward others — opened by having received it. The experiential knowledge of need from the inside, which becomes the seedbed of mercy toward others. Restored standing before the one who showed mercy. The relational possibility of return, starting again, continuation on the basis of grace rather than performance.

---

**Q006: What does the word reveal about the disposition of God toward the human being?**

ANSWER. God is described as compassionate and merciful, rich in mercy, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love. The disposition is intrinsic character — not occasional response but essential quality. God holds mercy as a permanent stance: the new covenant is characterised by mercy that does not retract (Hebrews 8:12). Luke 6:36 names the divine disposition as the original of which human mercy is the echo.

---

**Q007: What does the word reveal about the basis on which God's disposition toward a person is established?**

ANSWER. Not on the basis of merit. Daniel 9:19 appeals to God's own name and character, not Israel's merit. The new covenant promise (Hebrews 8:12) is unilateral. Psalm 51:1 — the appeal is structured entirely by what David knows about who God is, not by any claim David can make.

---

**Q008: Where, somatically or relationally, does the word locate the reality of being in favour?**

ANSWER. Multiple somatic locations named. The inner organs (*splagchna*) as the site from which compassion arises (Colossians 3:12). The mercy seat (*kappōret*) as spatial-architectural location — mercy has an address, the innermost room. The face: the shining face (Numbers 6:25) and the face-covering of appeasement (*kippēr*) share the same spatial logic. The tax collector's three somatic acts encode the inner experience: standing far off, downcast eyes, beating the breast (Luke 18:13).

---

**Q009: Does the word describe a one-time act or an ongoing condition — and how are these distinguished?**

ANSWER. Both. The atoning act is completed — the sevenfold sprinkling is total and unreserved. The divine disposition is permanent and ongoing — a sustained relational posture, not a repeated transaction (Hebrews 8:12). The vocabulary distinguishes precisely: *hilaskomai* names the act; *hileōs* names the prior settled disposition from which the act flows.

---

**Q010: What spatial or directional language is used to describe the ongoing condition the word produces?**

ANSWER. The mercy seat (*kappōret*) is the most concentrated spatial language: mercy has a specific location — the innermost room, accessible once yearly. The shining face (Numbers 6:25) names the direction of divine favour. The tax collector's far-off standing names the spatial condition before mercy is received. *Hilasthēti moi* (Luke 18:13) — "let propitiation be turned toward me" — names directional movement of the mercy-seat work toward the person.

---

**Q011: Does the word produce enabling capacity in the inner being — and if so, what kind?**

ANSWER. Yes. Mercy received opens the inner capacity to extend mercy to others — relational and experiential enabling. The person who has known mercy understands need from the inside; this is the seedbed of capacity. James 3:17 — wisdom from above is full of mercy: mercy is constitutive of the wise person's inner capacity.

---

**Q012: What character quality does the word produce in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Openness — a heart not armoured against the weakness of others. Willingness to bear cost, to forgo what is owed, to be inconvenienced by another's need. The merciful character (*eleēmōn* — Matthew 5:7) is named as a settled habitual quality, not a momentary state.

---

**Q013: How does the word express itself in relation to others?**

ANSWER. As relief — moving toward another's need and acting. As covering — forgiving, sparing, releasing the claim that could be pressed. As clemency. As comfort — the pastoral output of mercy extended (2 Corinthians 1:3-4). As restoration — opening the door the other person cannot open for themselves. As one of the weightier matters of the law alongside justice and faithfulness (Matthew 23:23).

---

**Q014: What inner condition or orientation is identified as the ground of genuine expression of the word?**

ANSWER. Prior receipt of divine mercy — the consistent biblical logic. Self-knowledge is named as a structural component of authentic mercy-seeking (1 Kings 8:38 — knowing the affliction of one's own heart). Mercy that passes through without being truly received leaves the inner character unchanged (Matthew 18 — unmerciful servant).

---

**Q015: Is there a discernible sequence or movement in the way the word operates through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Yes. Divine mercy as prior character → atoning act provides the structural ground → mercy received at spirit level → inner transformation producing openness → capacity for mercy toward others opened → mercy extended. Psalm 78:38 states the causal sequence explicitly: inner compassion (*rachum*) → act of atonement → restraint of wrath.

---

**Q016: What direction of movement does the word follow within and through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Always from the one who has something toward the one who has nothing. Inward first (received at spirit level); then outward (expressed at soul level as compassion toward others, directed in supplication toward God, enacted in acts of grace). The directionality is structural to the word.

---

**Q017: What does the word identify as its originating source?**

ANSWER. God's compassionate character — his intrinsic disposition, not a response to the situation. Luke 6:36 names the divine mercy as original, human mercy as echo. 1 John 4:10 states the causal chain for the mercy-atonement structure: divine love → sending of Son → propitiation provided.

---

**Q018: What does the word reveal about the origin of the human capacity to seek it?**

ANSWER. The supplication vocabulary (*cha.nan*, *te.chin.nah*) reveals that giver and seeker share a root — the human posture of mercy-seeking is shaped by and oriented toward the divine posture of mercy-giving. Authentic mercy-seeking requires self-knowledge (1 Kings 8:38) — the capacity to reckon honestly with the inner condition before making the appeal.

---

**Q019: Does the word share an etymological root with another inner-being characteristic?**

ANSWER. Yes — multiple. *Cha.nan* (gracious favour) shares the CHANAN root with grace — root code collision between Registries 111 and 68 noted, requires programme-level reconciliation. *Chinnam* (for nothing, freely) shares the same root — gratuitousness shared between grace given freely and suffering that comes without cause (Job 2:3). *Ra.cha.mim* shares the root *rechem* (womb). *Kip.per* shares face-direction logic with *cha.nan*.

---

**Q020: What does the word produce as a natural inner response in the one who receives it?**

ANSWER. Reverent silence — the quiet of the person found by grace they did not deserve (Ezekiel 16:63). Identity transformation — being named as a people who have received mercy (1 Peter 2:10). Comfort (2 Corinthians 1:3). The capacity to seek mercy on behalf of others. In the eschatological register, the Beatitude promise (Matthew 5:7) names the merciful as those who shall receive mercy.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description
*(Q021–Q041)*

---

**Q021: What inner-being logic or orientation functions as the structural opposite of the word?**

ANSWER. Inner closure — the hardening of the inner orientation against the claims of another's need. Self-sufficiency and self-righteousness. Romans 1:31 — mercilessness (*aneleēmōn*) as the terminal item of inner moral collapse: the relational face of the whole dissolution. Mercilessness is not a neutral choice but a symptom of an inner life that has lost its capacity for connection.

---

**Q022: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to receive the word?**

ANSWER. Self-sufficiency and self-righteousness close the capacity to receive mercy. The Pharisee in Luke 18 — the person who does not know the affliction of their own heart cannot make authentic appeal. The merit-logic of the unmerciful servant reveals how debt-accounting prevents mercy received from being integrated.

---

**Q023: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. The cultivated belief that one's own standing is earned produces the demand that others' debts be paid. The unmerciful servant — having received mercy, he immediately reverted to merit-logic and demanded repayment. Merit-logic and mercy-extension are structurally incompatible inner orientations.

---

**Q024: What is the inner-being condition of the person who has received the word but failed to recognise it?**

ANSWER. The inner character is unchanged — mercy passed through without being truly received. The unmerciful servant is the defining case: mercy received verbally and formally, but it did not reach and transform the heart. The failure is a failure of genuine encounter, not merely moral lapse.

---

**Q025: What inner-being experience cultivates the capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. The experience of having received mercy and understanding it from the inside. Self-knowledge (*yada et nega levavo*) — honest reckoning with the inner condition. The practice of ongoing supplication keeps the person in contact with their own need, which shapes the capacity to extend mercy.

---

**Q026: What inner-being posture does the act of earnest appeal express?**

ANSWER. Total dependence before one who holds power over the supplicant's condition. Luke 18:13 — three simultaneous somatic acts encoding it precisely: spatial distance (moral distance acknowledged), downcast eyes (shame before God), beating the breast (the body identifying with guilt). The supplication vocabulary consistently presupposes two things known simultaneously: the need is real, and only the offended party can supply the remedy.

---

**Q027: What somatic forms does the inner act of seeking the word take?**

ANSWER. Richly developed. Luke 18:13 — three somatic gestures simultaneously. 1 Kings 8:38 — stretched hands directed toward the temple (toward the mercy seat within). Daniel 9 — continuous confession as active speech ("while I was speaking"). Psalm 51:1 — triple mercy-term invocation as the full ground of appeal. The somatic and theological are inseparable: the body enacts what the inner person knows about itself.

---

**Q028: What inner-being orientation closes off the reception of the word?**

PARTIAL. Self-sufficiency and self-righteousness named. Lo-Ruhamah (Hosea 1:6) — mercy withdrawn as covenant consequence — names the outer limit. The inner-being mechanics of permanently closed reception are not fully developed.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The study names the outer limit (Lo-Ruhamah) without fully characterising the inner-being logic of the closure that precedes it.

---

**Q029: What relational orientation toward others diminishes the operation of the word?**

ANSWER. The cultivated belief that others' debts must be paid. Hardness of heart. The substitution of religious precision for relational openness (Matthew 23:23 — meticulous tithing with neglect of mercy).

---

**Q030: What is the relationship between the word and conditions of weakness in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Mercy is directed specifically toward the weak. The strength/power connection is formally confirmed: mercy and the power-related cluster share all four confirmed dimensions. Psalm 78:38 — God restrains his wrath through inner compassion: mercy as restraint of power, directed toward the vulnerable.

---

**Q031: What kind of inner-being transformation does the word produce?**

ANSWER. Both condition and orientation. 1 Peter 2:10 is ontological: having received mercy is constitutive of who the person now is — changed standing and name. Ezekiel 16:63 — comprehensive mercy produces reverent silence, a new inner posture before God.

---

**Q032: What is the first inner-being response to receiving the word unexpectedly?**

PARTIAL. The study develops the inner experience of receiving mercy (reverent silence, identity transformation, comfort) but does not name a specific first response equivalent to grace's fear-as-first-response. Ezekiel 16:63 names the settled result rather than the first moment.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. Rich material on posture before and settled condition after — the first inner moment of mercy received is a gap.

---

**Q033: What is the sequence of inner-being states as the word takes hold in the person?**

ANSWER. Approach in self-knowledge and somatic humility → appeal to divine character → mercy-seat work done → inner transformation (identity changed, standing restored) → reverent silence or comfort → capacity for mercy toward others opened. The Psalm 51:1 sequence is the most concentrated statement.

---

**Q034: In the eschatological context, what is the first inner-being response to the full outpouring of the word?**

PARTIAL. Matthew 5:7 has an eschatological register — the Beatitude promise names merciful character as the condition of those who stand rightly before God. 1 Peter 2:10 has a new-covenant framing but this is not developed as an eschatological first-response sequence.

---

**Q035: What is the relationship between mourning and the full reception of the word in the inner being?**

PARTIAL. Not directly developed. Psalm 51:1 carries mourning vocabulary in its broader context but the mercy study does not name mourning as a distinct first fruit of mercy received. Reverent silence (Ezekiel 16:63) is adjacent to mourning but distinguished from it.

---

**Q036: What quality of inner-being stability does the word produce?**

ANSWER. Settled character — mercy as habitual inner quality (*eleēmōn*). The new covenant mercy (Hebrews 8:12) is permanent and non-retracting. The stability is grounded in the character of the source: God rich in mercy, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love — stable, intrinsic qualities.

---

**Q037: What orientation does the stability produced by the word carry?**

PARTIAL. Matthew 5:7 carries a future eschatological promise: the merciful shall receive mercy. Forward-facing. But the study does not develop a hope-orientation for mercy as a present condition equivalent to grace's standing-in-hope.

---

**Q038: What capacity toward others does the word produce in the inner being of the one who has received it?**

ANSWER. The capacity to extend mercy. Openness of a heart not armoured against others' weakness. Willingness to bear cost, forgo what is owed, be inconvenienced by another's need. Mercy alongside justice and faithfulness as co-constitutive of covenantal character (Matthew 23:23, Micah 6:8).

---

**Q039: What is the inner-being source of the capacity to extend the word to others — is it discipline or transformation?**

ANSWER. Both. Transformation — the inner person changed by receiving mercy. And formation — James 3:17 implies mercy is developed as a quality of the wisely-formed person. The capacity is both transformational (from the mercy-encounter) and formational (from the shaping of inner character over time).

---

**Q040: How does the word relate to genuine human effort?**

PARTIAL. The command "be merciful" (Luke 6:36) implies genuine human effort. The formation language implies cultivated character. But the relationship between divine enabling and human effort in mercy's extension is not explicitly examined.

---

**Q041: What does the word reveal about the inner-being condition of the person who does not receive it?**

ANSWER. Romans 1:31 — mercilessness as the terminal item of inner moral collapse. Its pairing with heartlessness (*astorgos*) names mercy and natural family-love as companion capacities, both extinguished together. The person without mercy has become, in some fundamental sense, less human. Lo-Ruhamah names the condition of those from whom mercy has been withdrawn as covenant consequence.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence
*(Q042–Q085)*

---

**Q042: What does the word's primary verse reveal about its essential character?**

ANSWER. Luke 6:36 — "Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful." Mercy as settled disposition of character (*oiktirmōn* — quality of being compassionate as a person); human disposition in explicit correspondence with the divine; human mercy not invented but modelled; the call as character-formation, not only ethical directive.

---

**Q043: What does the primary verse identify as the relationship between the human and divine versions?**

ANSWER. Human mercy is the echo of the divine original — placed in explicit correspondence with it ("as your Father is merciful"). Not merely modelled on it but structurally dependent on it. The same pattern as the call to reflect God's holiness.

---

**Q044: What does the primary verse identify as the primary subject of the word?**

ANSWER. God is the primary subject — the source, the defining model, the one who exercises mercy as essential character. Human beings are secondary subjects — called to exercise mercy as reflection of and participation in the divine exercise.

---

**Q045: What double function does the primary verse name?**

PARTIAL. Luke 6:36 names one function and its ground rather than a double function. The double function is clearest in *hilaskomai* — both the active provision of propitiation and the experienced reception, encoded in the same verb by voice alternation.

---

**Q046: What does the primary verse reveal about the breadth of those toward whom the word operates?**

ANSWER. Luke 6:36 in the context of love-of-enemies — mercy explicitly extends beyond those with a natural claim. 1 John 2:2 extends propitiation to "the sins of the whole world." The covenantal form (Psalm 86:5) addresses "all who call on you."

---

**Q047: What does the verse evidence reveal about the direction of the word's operation?**

ANSWER. Always from the one who has something toward the one who has nothing. The mercy-seat architecture encodes the direction: God speaks from above the mercy seat downward toward the people.

---

**Q048: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to suffering or affliction?**

ANSWER. James 5:11 — God does not remove suffering quickly but is *polysplanchnos* (abundantly compassionate) in the midst of it. Endurance through suffering is possible because the God before whom one suffers is tenderly moved by the sufferer's condition. Leviticus 23:27 — the Day of Atonement requires self-affliction of the *nephesh*: suffering as the bodily participation in annual mercy-seeking.

---

**Q049: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to moral failure or guilt?**

ANSWER. Mercy is the primary divine response to moral failure. Psalm 51:1 — the convergence verse for three mercy registers in a single supplication from moral failure. Psalm 78:38 — inner compassion → atonement → restraint of wrath. The atonement mechanism (Leviticus 16:14, 17:11) addresses moral accountability specifically.

---

**Q050: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to covenantal relationship?**

ANSWER. Deep and structural. *Chesed* as one of the two great mercy vocabularies. The Aaronic blessing as priestly speech-act directing divine favour covenantally. The new covenant (Hebrews 8:12) names mercy as the defining character of the new covenantal relationship. Mercy operates within covenant and — in its NT extension — across covenant boundaries (1 John 2:2).

---

**Q051: What reason does a key verse give for the extension of the word?**

ANSWER. God's own character — not the recipient's merit. Psalm 86:5 — "you are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love to all who call on you." Daniel 9:19 — appeal to God's own name. The Aaronic blessing — God's gracious turning not contingent on the recipient.

---

**Q052: Where does a key verse locate the reality of being in favour — in a specific faculty of the giver?**

ANSWER. The face — the shining face of God in the Aaronic blessing (Numbers 6:25). The mercy seat (*kappōret*) as architectural location. Exodus 25:22 — God speaks from above the mercy seat: the face and the mercy seat share the same logic of located divine favour.

---

**Q053: What practical outcome does a key verse show being in favour producing for the recipient?**

ANSWER. Atonement — the specific covering of moral accountability (Leviticus 16:14, 17:11). Access to God's speech (Exodus 25:22). Restored standing before God. The capacity for continuation in relationship.

---

**Q054: What inner state does a key verse identify as the first response to the announcement of favour?**

PARTIAL. Ezekiel 16:63 names the settled result: reverent silence. Luke 18:13-14 narrates approach and outcome (the tax collector went home justified) but not the first inner moment of mercy received.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The first inner moment of mercy received is a gap in the study.

---

**Q055: What comes first in a key verse — the establishment of standing, or the commission that follows?**

ANSWER. Standing first — consistently. Romans 3:25 — propitiation put forward; reception by faith follows. Exodus 25:22 — atonement effected first; divine speech from the mercy seat follows. Mercy provides the ground; everything built on it comes after.

---

**Q056: What does a key verse identify as the concrete content of the favour given?**

ANSWER. Hebrews 8:12 — two distinct promises: merciful toward iniquities (covering), and sins remembered no more (relational commitment). Leviticus 17:11 — life addressing life: the specific mechanism. 1 John 2:2 — propitiation as the substance of what Christ is for the person.

---

**Q057: What spatial language does a key verse use for the reality of the word?**

ANSWER. The mercy seat (*kappōret*) as the programme's most concentrated architectural mercy-language: mercy as a place. The tax collector far off (Luke 18:13) — spatial distance as the pre-mercy condition. *Hilasthēti moi* — directional movement of the mercy-seat work toward the person.

---

**Q058: Does any verse distinguish between the means of entry and the condition itself?**

ANSWER. Romans 3:25 — faith as the means of access to the propitiation God has provided. Faith is the NT mechanism of mercy-seat approach. The propitiation is the condition; faith is the means of entry.

---

**Q059: Does any verse describe the present condition as carrying a forward orientation?**

PARTIAL. Matthew 5:7 — the merciful shall receive mercy — forward eschatological promise. But the study does not develop a hope-orientation for mercy as a present posture equivalent to grace's standing-in-hope.

---

**Q060: What does a key verse identify as the ground of the recipient's identity?**

ANSWER. 1 Peter 2:10 — having received mercy is constitutive of who the person now is: changed standing and name. The Lo-Ruhamah reversal — those defined by mercy's absence are now defined by mercy received. Identity is grounded in reception, not achievement.

---

**Q061: How does a key verse handle the co-presence of genuine human effort and prior divine initiative?**

PARTIAL. "Be merciful" (Luke 6:36) implies genuine human effort; "as your Father is merciful" names the prior divine initiative. Present but not as explicitly paradoxical as in grace.

---

**Q062: In what form does a key verse present the word — and what does that form enable?**

ANSWER. The mercy seat as architecture — a physical structure encoding the theological reality. Propitiation as a person (1 John 2:2 — Christ as the *hilasmos*). The Aaronic blessing as priestly speech-act. Each form enables a specific mode of access.

---

**Q063: What effect does the word in communicative form produce in those who receive it?**

ANSWER. The priestly blessing — directed toward the people, producing the condition of divine favour. Luke 18:14 — the pronouncement of justification. Daniel 9:23 — receiving the divine response while still speaking.

---

**Q064: Does a key verse describe the word's work as directed toward the present only, or toward a future state?**

ANSWER. Both. The atonement work is present and complete. The new covenant formula (Hebrews 8:12) is forward-pointing. Matthew 5:7 is eschatological. Present completeness and future assurance held simultaneously.

---

**Q065: Does any verse distinguish between a performed version and a genuine version of the word?**

ANSWER. James 3:17 — wisdom from above full of mercy distinguished from counterfeit earthly wisdom. Matthew 18:35 — "from your heart" as the distinction between genuine and performed (the forgiveness-mercy parallel applies directly here).

---

**Q066: What inner orientation does a key verse identify as the source of the genuine quality?**

ANSWER. Self-knowledge — knowing the affliction of one's own heart (1 Kings 8:38). Prior receipt of divine mercy. The inner compassion (*splagchnizomai*, *rachum*) felt before the act is decided.

---

**Q067: What is the sequence of somatic and verbal response in a key verse?**

ANSWER. Luke 18:13 — somatic comes first: posture (standing far off, downcast eyes, breast-beating) precedes the verbal cry. 1 Kings 8:38 — somatic (stretched hands) accompanies the verbal simultaneously. The body enacts what the inner person knows before or alongside the verbal expression.

---

**Q068: What does a recipient's response in a key verse reveal about their inner experience of receiving?**

ANSWER. Ezekiel 16:63 — reverent silence: the person who has received comprehensive mercy for comprehensive faithlessness has nothing left to say for themselves. The silence is the inner experience of having been found by grace they did not deserve.

---

**Q069: What structural condition in a key verse establishes the recipient as having no basis for the favour received?**

ANSWER. Guilt — real debt genuinely owed. The tax collector's self-description ("a sinner" — Luke 18:13). Daniel 9:20 — confession of personal and communal sin as the ground from which the appeal is made.

---

**Q070: In a key verse, does the word operate within or across existing boundaries?**

ANSWER. Within covenant primarily — the mercy-seat architecture is bounded by the covenant community. But 1 John 2:2 explicitly crosses boundaries ("not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world"). 1 Peter 2:10 applies covenant mercy-language to Gentiles.

---

**Q071: What is the logical structure of a key argument?**

ANSWER. James 2:13 — two-clause argument: (1) judgment without mercy to the one who showed no mercy; (2) mercy triumphs over judgment. Premise: inner disposition of mercy or mercilessness. Conclusion: the judicial outcome corresponds. Second clause names mercy as the stronger judicial category.

---

**Q072: What inner-being state does the argument structure in a key verse produce in the recipient?**

PARTIAL. James 2:13 clarifies the judicial stakes but does not name a specific inner-being state in the one who holds merciful character. No equivalent to grace's Romans 8:32 confidence-argument.

---

**Q073: What is the relationship between the divine act and the human act in a key verse — illustrative or causal?**

ANSWER. Causal throughout. Luke 6:36 — divine as causal model. Matthew 18:33 — divine act as cause of expected human response. 1 John 4:10 — causal chain stated explicitly. The grammar is consistently causal, not illustrative.

---

**Q074: Where does a key verse locate the inner quality from which the act flows?**

ANSWER. The inner organs — *splagchna* (Colossians 3:12). *Rachum* (womb-love — Psalm 78:38). The heart as the centre of the inner person. The body's interior is named repeatedly as the site from which compassion arises before it is acted on.

---

**Q075: What does a verse about the withdrawal or absence of the word reveal?**

ANSWER. Hosea 1:6 — Lo-Ruhamah: mercy withdrawn as covenant consequence. The negation of womb-love measures the extremity. The withdrawal reveals that mercy is not automatic — it is a genuine gift that can be genuinely withheld. The mercy-seat and Lo-Ruhamah form the two poles of the mercy structure.

---

**Q076: What does a verse about the word in extremity reveal about its depth of operation?**

ANSWER. Psalm 51:1 — three mercy registers simultaneously invoked from the depths of moral failure. Daniel 9 — ongoing confession and supplication in extremity. The depth of operation corresponds to the depth of the need. The fullest appeal draws on the complete mercy vocabulary simultaneously.

---

**Q077: What does a verse about the word in the judicial or accountability context reveal?**

ANSWER. James 2:13 — mercy as the stronger judicial category, able to triumph over judgment. Romans 3:25 — mercy does not bypass justice but satisfies it through the atoning mechanism. Psalm 78:38 — mercy restrains wrath without eliminating it.

---

**Q078: What does a verse about the word in the supplication context reveal about what the inner person knows about itself?**

ANSWER. 1 Kings 8:38 — authentic supplication requires self-knowledge: knowing the affliction of one's own heart. The inner person must reckon honestly with its condition: two things known simultaneously — the need is real, and only the offended party can supply the remedy. Daniel 9:20 — ongoing moral honesty as the ground of sustained supplication.

---

**Q079: What does a verse about the word in the worship or liturgical context reveal about its function?**

ANSWER. Exodus 25:22 — the mercy seat as the place of divine speech: the innermost location of worship, where propitiation is effected, is also where God's word is given. Mercy and revelation share a location. Leviticus 16 and 23 — the Day of Atonement as annual mercy-seeking institution encoding that access to God requires the mercy-seat ground.

---

**Q080: What does a verse about the word at the level of covenantal promise reveal about its scope and duration?**

ANSWER. Hebrews 8:12 — God's merciful disposition toward iniquities is permanent and non-retracting. The forgetting of sin is a relational commitment. 1 John 2:2 — universal scope. The covenantal promise reveals that mercy at the divine level is not conditional on the ongoing worthiness of the recipient.

---

**Q081: What does a verse about the word in the context of moral collapse reveal?**

ANSWER. Romans 1:31 — mercilessness as the terminal item of moral collapse. Its pairing with heartlessness names mercy and natural affection as companion capacities — the innermost relational architecture of the person. Their simultaneous absence names the completion of inner relational death.

Without the crossing of distance and the act, the inner disposition has not yet become mercy fully expressed.

**L-009: Is the word named as constitutive of the divine essence — what God *is*?**

PARTIAL. Not as explicitly as love (1 John 4:8 — "God is love"). But "Father of mercies" (2 Corinthians 1:3) names mercy as so intrinsic it defines God relationally. "Rich in mercy" (Ephesians 2:4) names it as constitutive abundance. The direction is consistent with L-009 but the explicit statement stops short.

---

**L-010: Does the word's structural opposite operate under the same moral logic as the word itself?**

ANSWER. With qualification. Mercilessness (*aneleēmōn*) is uniformly condemned — there is no licit form of mercilessness, unlike love's opposite (hatred) which has licit and illicit forms. However, mercy must operate in structural partnership with justice — the mercy-judgment tension in James 2:13 shows judgment as the legitimate backdrop against which mercy triumphs, not as uniformly wrong.

---

**L-011: What is the relationship between the word as an inner disposition and the word as an outward act?**

ANSWER. Co-constitutive — inner movement and outward act are inseparable. Section 1: mercy "crosses the distance. It acts." The inner disposition that stops short of action has not yet fully expressed mercy. *Splagchnizomai* precedes the act but the act is the necessary completion of the movement. Disposition without act is incomplete; act without inner movement is performance.

---

**L-012: Does the word carry an epistemic dimension — is knowing and being known structural?**

ANSWER. Yes — self-knowledge is structural to authentic mercy-seeking. 1 Kings 8:38 — knowing the affliction of one's own heart as prerequisite of genuine supplication. Being known by God is present: Daniel's appeal addresses God as the one who knows the condition of his people. The epistemic dimension is primarily self-knowledge-as-prerequisite.

---

**L-013: Does the word function as a publicly legible signal?**

ANSWER. Yes. Matthew 23:23 — the neglect of mercy among the Pharisees is publicly legible as religious failure; practice of mercy is the publicly visible mark of covenantal character. Micah 6:8 — loving mercy named as the external marker of right covenantal living. The merciful community is publicly identifiable by its mercy.

---

**L-014: Does the word produce a reorganisation of social dynamics?**

ANSWER. Yes. The mercy-justice-faithfulness triad (Matthew 23:23, Micah 6:8) names mercy as a structural element of social-covenantal order. The Beatitude (Matthew 5:7) — merciful character as the marker of those who stand rightly before God — reorganises the status hierarchy. Mercy reorganises social dynamics by inverting the logic of debt, obligation, and accumulated grievance.

### New Pair M-003

**[OBS]** *Hilaskomai* (G2433) undergoes a directional reversal from classical Greek to NT use: in classical usage the human propitiates the deity; in NT usage God provides the propitiation for the human. **Existing gap:** Q109 captures the fact of the reversal. ### New Pair M-004

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks at which level of the inner being (spirit, soul, body) the word is received, and at which level it is expressed — or whether these are distinct.

### New Pair M-005

**[OBS]** *Cha.nan* (H2603A) is structurally remarkable: the same verbal root names both the granting of favour (God, Qal) and the seeking of it (human, Hithpael). This is the only root in the programme so far where the act of giving and the act of seeking share an identical root — giver and seeker are structurally united at the linguistic level.

**Existing gap:** Q121 asks about a term holding human and divine simultaneously — answered ANSWER. ### New Pair M-006

**[OBS]** *Chinnam* (H2600 — for nothing, without cause, freely) shares the *cha.nan* root with mercy and grace. The same root holds unmerited favour (grace given freely) and unmerited affliction (suffering in Job 2:3 that comes without cause). The gratuitousness is shared: the root names both the gift that is given freely and the suffering that comes freely — without earning either.

**Existing gap:** No catalogue question asks whether the word's root vocabulary includes terms naming both unmerited gift and unmerited affliction — whether the gratuitousness of the word's extension has a structural parallel in unmerited suffering.

**New Q:** Does the word's root vocabulary include terms that name both unmerited gift and unmerited affliction — and if so, what does the shared root reveal about the relationship between grace and suffering?
