# PASS 1 — CATALOGUE APPLICATION

---

## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary
*(Q001–Q020)*

---

**Q001: What is the structural disposition of the word — where does it originate?**

ANSWER. Compassion is an inner movement toward another person's pain — a stirring, a being-moved from within by what is suffered by another. It is not primarily a decision or act of will. It originates in God's own character (Exodus 34:6 — the foundational self-description) and is derived in human beings from the experience of God's compassion. The womb-etymology names the quality's origin as primal and somatic, not cognitive.

---

**Q002: What determines whether the word is extended or withheld?**

ANSWER. In God: free sovereign determination (Romans 9:15 — "I will have compassion on whom I have compassion"). In human beings: the experience of having received it from God, shared vulnerability and experience of suffering, and the formation of wise character (James 3:17). Compassion is withheld deliberately in judicial contexts — not as failure but as structural complement: justice requires its suspension. The *chus* vocabulary establishes that its withdrawal is the exception to a default orientation, not the norm.

---

**Q003: What are the distinct modes of operation of the word in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Five recognisable modes: (1) visceral inner movement — gut-level, somatic, pre-volitional (*splanchnizō*, *rachamim*); (2) settled covenantal character — loyal, persisting disposition (*chesed*, *chan.nun*); (3) comfort reaching its goal in restoration (*ni.chum*, *na.cham*); (4) fellow-suffering — participation in another's experience (*sym-* compounds); (5) gracious favour as compassion's expression (*chan.nun*/*chen*). These are not alternatives but aspects that operate simultaneously or sequentially depending on context.

---

**Q004: What does the word produce in the inner being of the recipient?**

ANSWER. Relief and restoration — Isaiah 57:18 (God sees straying, moves toward restoration, comfort given). Identity reconstitution — 1 Peter 2:10 applied through the womb-root reversal. In the case of compassion-despite-inadequacy (Genesis 19:16), action on behalf of those who cannot act well on their own behalf — mercy overriding the recipient's unreadiness. In Revelation 3:17, the pitiable condition reveals what the compassion vocabulary requires of the recipient: recognition of need.

---

**Q005: What does the word produce in the relational position of the recipient toward another?**

ANSWER. The experience of divine compassion generates the capacity and demand for human compassion toward others (Micah 6:8 — love kindness as a character orientation). The Good Samaritan sequence (Luke 10:33-37) names the human compassion capacity as what correctly answers "who is my neighbour?" — compassion reconstitutes relational boundaries. 2 Samuel 22:26 names the mirror dynamic: the one who brings compassionate character encounters it returned from God.

---

**Q006: What does the word reveal about the disposition of God toward the human being?**

ANSWER. God's compassion is described in terms of its freedom (Romans 9:15), its depth (*polysplanchnos* — James 5:11), and its grounding in God's own settled character (Exodus 34:6 — the foundational self-description). It is deliberative (Hosea 11:8 — inner contest between judgment and compassion), everlasting as against judgment's momentary quality (Isaiah 54:8), and unfailing even in catastrophe (Lamentations 3:22 — steadfast love never ceases). The divine compassion precedes Israel's capacity to know or respond (Isaiah 46:3 — carried from the womb).

---

**Q007: What does the word reveal about the basis on which God's disposition toward a person is established?**

ANSWER. Not on the basis of merit. Romans 9:15 — God's compassion flows from his own free determination. Jonah 4:11 — divine pity grounded in the simple fact of creaturely need (their number and ignorance, including the cattle) — not in moral renewal. Isaiah 46:3 — carried from the womb before Israel's capacity to respond. The basis is creaturely need combined with divine character, not human qualification.

---

**Q008: Where, somatically or relationally, does the word locate the reality of being in favour?**

ANSWER. In the body's most interior places — the womb (*rechem*, *racham*) and the bowels/inner organs (*splanchnizō*, *splanchnon*). The heart as the seat of compassion's warmth (Hosea 11:8 — "my heart recoils within me"). The womb-etymology names the innermost body-site as the word's somatic origin. God as the sovereign of the womb (Psalm 22:10, Jeremiah 1:5) — locating divine compassion at the threshold of the human person's existence.

---

**Q009: Does the word describe a one-time act or an ongoing condition — and how are these distinguished?**

ANSWER. Both. *Splanchnizō* names a triggered moment — compassion stirred by a specific encounter with suffering. *Chesed* names the ongoing, non-triggered settled disposition. *Chan.nun* names the characterological quality persisting through time. Lamentations 3:22 — the steadfast love never ceases, mercies never end — names the ongoing condition as the ground on which the momentary stirrings rest. The vocabulary distinguishes act, triggered movement, and stable disposition.

---

**Q010: What spatial or directional language is used to describe the ongoing condition the word produces?**

ANSWER. Compassion always faces outward — consistently described as directed toward the one who suffers, not inward. Isaiah 46:3 — God carrying Israel from the womb: a sustained holding that names spatial intimacy. Isaiah 57:18 — God moves toward restoration: directional language of approach. The *sym-* compounds name a spatial-relational entering-into: genuine entry into shared experience rather than observation from a distance.

---

**Q011: Does the word produce enabling capacity in the inner being — and if so, what kind?**

ANSWER. Yes. The *sym-* compounds name the capacity produced by shared suffering: Hebrews 4:15 — Christ can sympathise because he has himself lived in the conditions of human weakness. The capacity for compassion arises from shared experience of vulnerability. Hebrews 5:2 — *metriopatheō*: the capacity for calibrated, wise engagement with the weak arises from shared creaturely weakness, not philosophical self-mastery. Compassion produces in the inner person the capacity to deal gently — a wisdom-shaped capacity, not merely an emotional one.

---

**Q012: What character quality does the word produce in the inner being?**

ANSWER. The settled disposition of loyal, steadfast love (*chesed*) — persisting through time, underlying all relationship. The character of the person who loves kindness (Micah 6:8) — compassion as an object of love, not merely a behaviour. The sympathy disposition (*sumpathēs* — 1 Peter 3:8) as a community virtue alongside love, tenderness, and humility. The gracious character (*chan.nun*) — inner inclination toward favour, specifically addressing human failure.

---

**Q013: How does the word express itself in relation to others?**

ANSWER. As action following inner movement — Luke 7:13 (widow of Nain: inner stirring → word to the grieving → raising of the son). As teaching and calling (Matthew 9:36 — compassion for the crowds → teaching and calling of the twelve). As enacted care (Luke 10:33-37 — Good Samaritan: compassion → mercy enacted). As charitable giving (*eleēmosunē* — compassion institutionalised as its normative social form). As gentle, calibrated engagement with the weak (Hebrews 5:2). As mutual fellow-suffering in community (1 Corinthians 12:26).

---

**Q014: What inner condition or orientation is identified as the ground of genuine expression of the word?**

ANSWER. Shared vulnerability and experience of suffering — Hebrews 4:15; 5:2. Prior receipt of divine compassion — the prophetic demand pattern: Israel is required to show compassion because they have received it from God. The inner disposition of the heart as the location (Hosea 11:8 — heart recoils, compassion grows warm). Humility — 1 Peter 3:8 places sympathy alongside humble mind as co-constitutive virtues. Inner quality as the source of the valid outward act (Luke 11:41 — inner things given as alms cleanse the whole person).

---

**Q015: Is there a discernible sequence or movement in the way the word operates through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Yes — three phases named explicitly: inner stirring → turn toward the sufferer → action that meets the need. Luke 7:13 is the most concentrated statement: seeing the widow, being moved in the gut, speaking to her, raising the son. Luke 10:33-37 makes the sequence explicit: compassion (inner gut-movement) → mercy enacted in care. The inner movement is the spring of the action. The sequence spans visceral compassion through deliberative compassion (Hosea 11:8) to eschatological compassion (Romans 8:17).

---

**Q016: What direction of movement does the word follow within and through the inner person?**

ANSWER. Always outward — compassion faces the one who suffers, not the self. The direction is from the body's interior (womb, bowels) outward to action. The *sym-* compounds add a further direction: into the shared space of another's experience — not movement toward but movement alongside. Romans 8:17 extends the direction eschatologically: through shared suffering toward shared glory.

---

**Q017: What does the word identify as its originating source?**

ANSWER. God's own character — the foundational self-description of Exodus 34:6 names compassion (*rachum*) as one of God's settled character attributes. The womb-etymology names the quality as arising from God's most intimate interior, preceding the creature's capacity to know or respond. Romans 9:15 — compassion flows from God's own free determination, not from the creature's condition.

---

**Q018: What does the word reveal about the origin of the human capacity to seek it?**

ANSWER. The shared creaturely condition — Job 31:15 ("Did not he who made me in the womb make him?") names the womb as the shared ground of equal creatureliness before God. This shared origin is itself the ground of appeal: all creaturely sufferers have equal standing before the one who formed them. Psalm 25:7 — the petitioner grounds appeal in God's steadfast love. The womb-sovereign (Psalm 22:10; Jeremiah 1:5) — the same God who forms the person in the womb is the one to whom they appeal in need.

---

**Q019: Does the word share an etymological root with another inner-being characteristic?**

ANSWER. Yes — multiple. The RACHAM root is shared with mercy (Registry 111) — 38 shared terms, the largest term-sharing in the programme. The same root connects to love through the womb-tenderness field. The CHESED family spans compassion, kindness (Registry 99), loyalty (Registry 104), and devotion (Registry 46). The PATHĒ root connects compassion to suffering and passion. The CHANAN root overlaps with grace (Registry 68). Compassion has the broadest root-sharing of any word studied so far.

---

**Q020: What does the word produce as a natural inner response in the one who receives it?**

ANSWER. Restoration and comfort — Isaiah 57:18 (healing, leading, restoring comfort to mourners). The capacity to return — Hosea 11:8's compassion produces the possibility of restoration rather than destruction. In Lot's case (Genesis 19:16), rescue despite inadequacy — compassion acts on behalf of those whose own response is insufficient. Meditation on divine compassion as a sustaining inner practice (Psalm 48:9 — thinking on divine steadfast love in worship). In the eschatological frame, shared glory as the outcome of shared suffering (Romans 8:17).

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description
*(Q021–Q041)*

---

**Q021: What inner-being logic or orientation functions as the structural opposite of the word?**

ANSWER. The logic of deserving — measuring response by the other's merit rather than their need. The judicial withdrawal of pity (*chus*) establishes the default: pity is the natural inner orientation; its deliberate suspension for justice is the exception, not the norm. Jonah's resentment of divine pity toward Nineveh (Jonah 4) names the inner orientation that cannot extend compassion beyond those it deems deserving. The *chesed* root's negative pole (shame/disgrace) names what happens when covenantal compassion is violated: the opposite of compassion is not neutrality but its active inversion.

---

**Q022: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to receive the word?**

ANSWER. Revelation 3:17 is the primary text: the Laodicean church cannot receive the compassion it needs because it cannot perceive its own need — material prosperity masks the pitiable condition. The logic of self-sufficiency functions as the barrier to reception: "I need nothing" closes the recognition of pitiable condition. The need-recognising capacity fails in the one who most requires help.

---

**Q023: What effect does the logic of merit have on the inner being's capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. Jonah 4 — Jonah's resentment of divine pity toward Nineveh names merit-thinking as the block on compassion's extension. The assumption that compassion should be calibrated to the other's moral standing produces the refusal to extend it beyond what has been earned. Jonah 4:11 — God's pity toward Nineveh is grounded in creaturely need alone, explicitly contrasting with merit-based calculus.

---

**Q024: What is the inner-being condition of the person who has received the word but failed to recognise it?**

PARTIAL. Not named as a specific inner-being condition in the way *acharistos* names it for grace. Revelation 3:17 names the condition of the one who needs compassion but cannot perceive the need. The study does not name a specific term for the person who has received compassion but has not integrated it. The unmerciful servant parallel (from mercy) would apply but is not developed in this study.

---

**Q025: What inner-being experience cultivates the capacity to extend the word to others?**

ANSWER. Shared experience of suffering — Hebrews 4:15; 5:2. The prophetic pattern: Israel's experience of divine compassion grounds the prophetic demand for human compassion. Formation in wisdom (James 3:17 — wisdom from above is full of mercy). Meditation on divine steadfast love (Psalm 48:9) as a sustaining inner practice that shapes the person toward compassion.

---

**Q026: What inner-being posture does the act of earnest appeal express?**

ANSWER. The posture of creaturely dependence — the womb-origin as the shared ground of appeal: all who share the creaturely condition have equal standing before the one who formed them. Psalm 25:7 — grounding the appeal in God's steadfast love, not the petitioner's merit. The cry that turns toward a compassionate God — named as one of the four dominant patterns across the compassion evidence.

---

**Q027: What somatic forms does the inner act of seeking the word take?**

ANSWER. Shared creaturely posture — Job 31:15 names the womb as the origin of equal creatureliness, implying the posture of the creature before its maker. Psalm 25:7 — the petitioner lifting the appeal toward God's steadfast love. The supplication vocabulary (*techinah*, *tahanun*, *chanan*) shared with prayer names the somatic structure of mercy-seeking (developed more explicitly in the mercy study). The womb-sovereign connection (Psalm 22:10; Jeremiah 1:5) implies the posture of the creature formed within — entirely dependent, preceded by divine knowing.

---

**Q028: What inner-being orientation closes off the reception of the word?**

ANSWER. Self-sufficiency — Revelation 3:17 names this precisely: "I am rich, I have prospered, I need nothing." The failure of the need-recognising capacity closes the reception of compassion. Jonah's self-pity and resentment (Jonah 4) — an orientation that can feel pity for a plant but not for 120,000 human beings — names a contracted, self-referential inner orientation that blocks compassion's extension.

---

**Q029: What relational orientation toward others diminishes the operation of the word?**

ANSWER. The assumption that need must be earned or deserved before compassion is warranted. The judicial contexts where pity is prohibited reveal the complementary danger: the failure to distinguish when justice requires compassion to be suspended. Both the indiscriminate extension and the self-righteous withholding of compassion diminish its genuine operation. Matthew 23:23 parallel (from mercy) — the substitution of religious precision for genuine inner orientation.

---

**Q030: What is the relationship between the word and conditions of weakness in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Compassion is constitutively shaped by weakness — not only responding to it but acquiring its deepest form through shared experience of it. Hebrews 4:15 — Christ's capacity to sympathise with weakness is grounded in having himself lived in the conditions of weakness. *Metriopatheō* (Hebrews 5:2) — calibrated compassion arises from shared creaturely weakness. The womb vocabulary names the origin of the human person in the condition of utter creaturely dependence — the ground of maximum weakness from which the compassion vocabulary begins.

---

**Q031: What kind of inner-being transformation does the word produce?**

ANSWER. Restoration — Isaiah 57:18 (straying seen, healing and comfort given). Identity constituted by divine care preceding the person's capacity to know or respond (Isaiah 46:3 — carried from the womb). In the community of faith: mutual fellow-suffering that constitutes the body of Christ as a suffering-together entity (1 Corinthians 12:26). The transformation is both individual (restored, comforted, carried) and corporate (the body constituted by shared suffering).

---

**Q032: What is the first inner-being response to receiving the word unexpectedly?**

PARTIAL. The study does not isolate a specific first-response moment. Isaiah 57:18 names the result (restoration, comfort) without the first inner moment. Genesis 19:16 — Lot's rescue despite lingering — names the result (brought out and set outside the city) without the first inner response to having been compassionately rescued. The study is richer on the inner movement of the giver than on the first inner response of the recipient.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The question applies. What is the first inner response of the person who discovers they have been carried from the womb — preceded by divine care they could not have known about?

---

**Q033: What is the sequence of inner-being states as the word takes hold in the person?**

ANSWER. The three-phase sequence is named explicitly: inner stirring → turn toward the sufferer → action. Luke 7:13 — seeing, being moved, speaking, acting. For the recipient: pitiable condition → being seen by the one who has compassion → compassionate act received → restoration. The Samaritan sequence makes the full arc explicit: encounter → gut-movement → mercy enacted in care → practical outcome (the innkeeper instructed, return promised).

---

**Q034: In the eschatological context, what is the first inner-being response to the full outpouring of the word?**

ANSWER. Romans 8:17 — the eschatological register of the compassion vocabulary: fellow-suffering with Christ leads to glorification with Christ. The eschatological outpouring of compassion is not isolated as a first-response moment but as a completed movement: shared suffering → shared glory. James 5:11 — the deep compassion of God is the eschatological perspective on suffering and endurance, the final word that makes perseverance possible.

---

**Q035: What is the relationship between mourning and the full reception of the word in the inner being?**

ANSWER. Comfort is named as the telic form of compassion — what compassion aims at when it reaches its goal. Isaiah 57:18 — restoring comfort to mourners is the specific form compassion takes when it reaches completion. The *ni.chum*/*na.cham* root family spans comfort and compassion — they are not two separate characteristics but compassion's inward stirring and its completed outward arrival. Mourning is the condition that calls compassion's comfort-form into being.

---

**Q036: What quality of inner-being stability does the word produce?**

ANSWER. Lamentations 3:22 — the steadfast love never ceases, mercies never end: stability grounded in the non-cessation of divine compassion. *Chesed* as settled covenantal disposition — the most stable form of compassion, underlying all of God's dealings. The inner practice of meditation on divine steadfast love (Psalm 48:9) as a sustaining orientation that produces stability in the worshipper.

---

**Q037: What orientation does the stability produced by the word carry?**

ANSWER. Forward — Romans 8:17 names the eschatological orientation explicitly: shared suffering with Christ carries forward toward shared glory. Isaiah 54:8 — everlasting love and compassion as the forward-facing ground on which restoration is promised. James 5:11 — divine deep compassion as the eschatological perspective that makes perseverance possible. The stability is not static but forward-moving.

---

**Q038: What capacity toward others does the word produce in the inner being of the one who has received it?**

ANSWER. The capacity for human compassion toward others — the prophetic demand pattern. Micah 6:8 — loving kindness as a character orientation produced in the person rightly related to God. The community of mutual fellow-suffering (1 Corinthians 12:26) — the capacity to suffer-with, constituted by union with the body rather than achieved by individual virtue. The capacity for gentle, calibrated engagement (Hebrews 5:2 — *metriopatheō*).

---

**Q039: What is the inner-being source of the capacity to extend the word to others — is it discipline or transformation?**

ANSWER. Transformation — shared vulnerability produces the capacity (Hebrews 4:15; 5:2). But also formation: wisdom from above is full of mercy (James 3:17) implies cultivated character. The *sym-* compounds name a participation that is constituted by union (1 Corinthians 12:26 — the body's suffering-together is not an individual achievement but a corporate reality). The capacity is transformational (from shared experience), formational (from cultivated wisdom), and constitutive (from union with the body of Christ).

---

**Q040: How does the word relate to genuine human effort — does it displace it, supplement it, or reconstitute it?**

ANSWER. Reconstitutes it. The *metriopatheō* vocabulary names a human effort that is shaped by shared vulnerability rather than philosophical self-mastery — the effort is real but its ground is changed. Micah 6:8 — love kindness as a commanded orientation: genuine human effort is required. Luke 11:41 — giving the inner things as alms: the effort of outward giving is valid only when its ground is genuine inner compassion. Effort is present throughout but reconstituted by its inner source.

---

**Q041: What does the word reveal about the inner-being condition of the person who does not receive it?**

ANSWER. Revelation 3:17 — pitiable, wretched, poor, blind, naked: the person who does not receive compassion because they cannot perceive their need is described in terms of its accumulated absence. The *chesed* root's negative pole — shame/disgrace — names the inner-being condition when covenantal compassion is violated. Lamentations 4:10 names the most extreme form: compassion under catastrophic pressure can be violated in its own subject — the compassionate woman who becomes the agent of her child's destruction.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence
*(Q042–Q085)*

---

**Q042: What does the word's primary verse reveal about its essential character?**

ANSWER. Exodus 34:6 — the foundational self-description of God: "The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful (*rachum*) and gracious (*chan.nun*), slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love (*chesed*) and faithfulness." This is the most cited, echoed, and alluded-to statement about God's inner character in the Old Testament. It names compassion as one of God's settled character attributes, held in integrated combination with graciousness, slowness to anger, and faithfulness. The formula establishes compassion not as a response to circumstances but as a constitutive quality of divine character.

---

**Q043: What does the primary verse identify as the relationship between the human and divine versions?**

ANSWER. The divine version is primary and foundational. Luke 6:36 parallel (from mercy) — "be merciful as your Father is merciful" — applies directly to compassion since *oiktirmōn* is the compassion-term used. Human compassion is derived from, modelled on, and demands as its ground the divine compassion named in Exodus 34:6. The formula becomes a pastoral resource precisely because it establishes the divine character as the stable original to which appeal can be made.

---

**Q044: What does the primary verse identify as the primary subject of the word?**

ANSWER. God — the womb-root vocabulary and gut-movement vocabulary are used predominantly of God and of Jesus in his earthly ministry. Romans 9:15 — God's compassion belongs to his sovereign freedom. The formula of Exodus 34:6 establishes God as the original; human compassion is the derived form.

---

**Q045: What double function does the primary verse name?**

ANSWER. Exodus 34:6 names a compound character: compassion (*rachum*) and graciousness (*chan.nun*) appear together as the opening pair. These are not redundant but complementary: compassion names the visceral, womb-rooted inner movement; graciousness names the settled disposition of favour toward the one in need. The pair names both the felt dimension and the characterological dimension of the same divine inner orientation.

---

**Q046: What does the primary verse reveal about the breadth of those toward whom the word operates?**

ANSWER. Jonah 4:11 — compassion extended toward Nineveh, grounded in creaturely need alone: their number, their ignorance, and even the cattle. The breadth is defined by creatureliness, not covenant membership. Isaiah 46:3 — carried from the womb: compassion preceding the creature's capacity to know or respond. The breadth is as wide as creaturely need and as old as the creature's existence.

---

**Q047: What does the verse evidence reveal about the direction of the word's operation?**

ANSWER. Always outward — toward the one who suffers. Luke 7:13 — Jesus's gut-movement directed immediately toward the widow, producing speech and action. The *sym-* compounds add a lateral direction: into the shared space of another's experience. Romans 8:17 — the direction extends forward eschatologically through shared suffering toward shared glory.

---

**Q048: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to suffering or affliction?**

ANSWER. Deep and structural. James 5:11 — God's deep compassion is revealed precisely through suffering he permits. *Sumpatheō*, *sumpaschō* — compassion shaped by participation in suffering. Hebrews 4:15 — Christ's compassion capacity grounded in shared conditions of weakness. Romans 8:17 — fellow-suffering as the path to glorification. Lamentations 3:22 — steadfast love declared in the context of the canon's most sustained grief. Compassion does not resolve suffering by explaining it; it accompanies it.

---

**Q049: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to moral failure or guilt?**

ANSWER. *Chan.nun* — the gracious character quality specifically named as the response to the condition of guilt. Exodus 34:6 in its canonical context (the golden calf — the covenant's first major violation) establishes that the foundational self-description of God is spoken precisely into moral failure. Psalm 25:7 — "remember not the sins of my youth": appeal to divine compassion from the specific condition of past moral failure. Joel 2:13 — "return to the Lord, for he is gracious and compassionate": divine compassion as both the call to repentance and its ground.

---

**Q050: What does the verse evidence reveal about the word's relationship to covenantal relationship?**

ANSWER. Central. *Chesed* — steadfast love as the covenantal-loyalty form of compassion. Lamentations 3:22 — the never-ceasing steadfast love that holds the covenantal community alive in catastrophe. Hosea 6:6 — God's preference for steadfast love over ritual performance as the inner-being requirement of covenantal life. The divine mirror (2 Samuel 22:26) — covenantal compassion reflected back to those who bring it. The compassion vocabulary operates within covenant as its primary relational frame.

---

**Q051: What reason does a key verse give for the extension of the word?**

ANSWER. God's own character — not the recipient's merit. Romans 9:15 — God's compassion from his own sovereign determination. Jonah 4:11 — creaturely need and ignorance alone. Isaiah 46:3 — womb-love preceding the creature's capacity to respond. The ground is consistently in the giver's character and the creature's need, never in the creature's merit.

---

**Q052: Where does a key verse locate the reality of being in favour — in a specific faculty of the giver?**

ANSWER. The womb (*rechem*, *racham*) as the most intimate inner location of divine compassion. The bowels/inner organs (*splanchnizō*) as the site of gut-level compassion in Jesus. The heart (Hosea 11:8 — "my heart recoils within me") as the seat of compassion's warmth. Multiple inner locations named — no single external faculty of gaze as in grace, but a series of interior body-sites.

---

**Q053: What practical outcome does a key verse show being in favour producing for the recipient?**

ANSWER. Healing and restoration (Isaiah 57:18). Raising of the dead (Luke 7:13 — widow of Nain). Teaching and calling (Matthew 9:36 — harassed crowds). Rescue despite inadequacy (Genesis 19:16 — Lot). Practical care (Luke 10:33-37 — Good Samaritan). The practical outcomes span from the eschatological (raising of the dead) to the immediate (care, feeding, teaching) — consistently concrete and action-oriented.

---

**Q054: What inner state does a key verse identify as the first response to the announcement of favour?**

PARTIAL. Luke 7:13 — Jesus speaks to the widow ("do not weep") before performing the miracle: the first response the compassion produces in the recipient is addressed before the power is displayed. But the widow's inner state at the moment of receiving compassion is not named. Isaiah 57:18 names the result (comfort to mourners) without naming the first inner moment. Genesis 19:16 — Lot's inner state upon rescue is not named.

---

**Q055: What comes first in a key verse — the establishment of standing, or the commission that follows?**

ANSWER. The inner movement comes first and generates the outward act — consistently. Luke 7:13 — compassion stirred before speech or miracle. Luke 10:33 — being moved before mercy enacted. Hosea 11:8 — compassion winning the inner contest before restoration is promised. The movement precedes and generates the act in every instance.

---

**Q056: What does a key verse identify as the concrete content of the favour given?**

ANSWER. Isaiah 57:18 — healing, leading, restoring comfort to mourners. Luke 7:13 — the son raised, the widow's grief addressed. Matthew 9:36 — teaching, the calling of workers for the harvest. Luke 10:33-37 — oil, wine, bandages, lodging, the promise of return. Jonah 4:11 — preservation, sparing of the city. The content is consistently specific and need-matched: the compassion produces what the specific condition requires.

---

**Q057: What spatial language does a key verse use for the reality of the word?**

ANSWER. Isaiah 46:3 — "carried from the womb": sustained intimate holding, spatial language of containment and bearing. Isaiah 57:18 — "I will lead him": directional spatial language of guidance and accompanying. The *sym-* compounds — entering into shared space of suffering: spatial language of alongside-ness. Romans 8:17 — the eschatological path: spatial-directional language through suffering toward glory.

---

**Q058: Does any verse distinguish between the means of entry and the condition itself?**

PARTIAL. The study names faith as the means of access to divine compassion (Psalm 25:7 — grounding appeal in divine steadfast love; the *chesed*-faith co-presence in the Psalter). But the entry/condition distinction is not as sharply drawn as in grace or mercy.

---

**Q059: Does any verse describe the present condition as carrying a forward orientation?**

ANSWER. Yes — explicitly. Romans 8:17 — fellow-suffering carrying forward toward shared glory. Isaiah 54:8 — everlasting love with compassion as the forward-facing ground of restoration. James 5:11 — God's deep compassion as the eschatological perspective that makes present endurance possible. The forward orientation is the strongest of any word studied so far.

---

**Q060: What does a key verse identify as the ground of the recipient's identity?**

**Q066: What inner orientation does a key verse identify as the source of the genuine quality?**

ANSWER. Shared vulnerability and experience of suffering (Hebrews 4:15; 5:2). The inner things as the source of valid outward giving (Luke 11:41). **Q067: What is the sequence of somatic and verbal response in a key verse?**

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary
*(Q086–Q121)*

---

**Q086: What is the total number of terms and their distribution?**

ANSWER. The study does not give a single total count in the way the mercy study does. Five root families are named: RACHAM (Hebrew), SPLANCHN (Greek), CHESED (Hebrew), PATHĒ (Greek), ELEEIN/CHUS (Greek and Hebrew). The shared terms with mercy (38) and love (13) indicate a high-sharing registry. The overall structure is four distinct root families plus a fifth solidarity/fellow-feeling cluster — broader root-family coverage than any other word studied.

---

**Q087: What is the sharing ratio?**

ANSWER. Described as "more than half of all terms shared with other word-studies" — one of the most highly connected words in the study. The Section 5 connections span 17 named adjacent characteristics. The sharing confirms compassion is not self-contained but deeply embedded in the vocabulary of the whole inner-being landscape.

---

**Q088: What is the primary Hebrew term, and what does its root meaning reveal?**

ANSWER. Two primary Hebrew families: *ra.cham*/*rechem* — the womb root; and *chesed* — steadfast covenantal love. The womb-etymology is constitutive: the abstract word (compassion, *rachamim*) is named after the concrete (womb, *rechem*). The somatic grounding is not a metaphor added later but built into the word's formation. The unusual etymological direction — abstract named after concrete — is unique in the programme and signals that the somatic reality is primary.

---

**Q089: What is the primary Greek term, and what does its range reveal?**

ANSWER. *Splanchnizō* (to be moved in the gut) — used almost exclusively of Jesus in the Gospels, appearing twelve times. Its range spans four domains: physical suffering and healing, spiritual destitution, hunger, and relational need — unified not by the type of need but by the visceral, inward, movement-producing quality of the response. *Polusplanchnos* — the NT intensive compound: coined to express divine compassion beyond ordinary vocabulary.

---

**Q090: Does the vocabulary distinguish between the word as an inner disposition and the word as an outward act?**

ANSWER. Yes — structurally. *Splanchnizō* names the inner triggered movement. *Eleēmosunē* names compassion institutionalised as outward practice — charitable giving as the normative social form of compassion. *Chesed* names the settled inner disposition. The *sym-* compounds name participation. *Metriopatheō* names the calibrated outward engagement shaped by inner wisdom. The vocabulary holds inner disposition and outward expression as distinct but inseparable.

---

**Q091: Does the vocabulary include a term for the word as a settled character quality?**

ANSWER. *Chan.nun* (gracious, 13 occurrences) — the settled character disposition of graciousness. *Sumpathēs* (sympathetic, 1 Peter 3:8) — the adjective naming a settled character disposition of responsiveness. *Chesed* as character quality — the disposition of covenantal loyal love persisting through time.

---

**Q092: Does the vocabulary include a term for the one who habitually exercises the word?**

ANSWER. *Ra.cha.ma.ni* (compassionate, 1 occurrence) — person-type term for the compassionate one. *Sumpathēs* — the sympathetic person as character type. The Good Samaritan (Luke 10:33-37) — functionally named as the person who correctly answers "who is my neighbour?" by the act of compassion — the compassionate person as the defining human type in the parable.

---

**Q093: Does the vocabulary include a term for the word's absence or structural opposite?**

ANSWER. *Che.sed* (shame/disgrace, 3 occurrences) — the negative pole of the *chesed* root: the same root that yields steadfast love also yields shame. *Chus* in prohibition contexts — the judicial withdrawal of pity, naming the deliberate suspension rather than the structural opposite. Ezekiel 7:4 — "my eye will not spare nor will I have pity": the judicial formula names the compassion's deliberate absence. The study notes that Lamentations 4:10 names the extreme inversion: the compassionate woman's defining quality violated by extreme circumstance.

---

**Q094: What does the relationship between root meaning and primary sense reveal?**

ANSWER. The unusual etymological direction: abstract (compassion) named after concrete (womb), not vice versa. This is the only word in the programme where the abstract characteristic is etymologically derived from a somatic reality rather than the somatic being a later metaphor. The somatic is constitutive of the meaning from the word's formation. This means the inner experience of compassion carries womb-memory at the linguistic level — it is not merely like womb-love; it is linguistically formed from it.

---

**Q095: Does the vocabulary include terms that name the word as received passively and actively exercised?**

ANSWER. Yes. Received: *splanchnizō* passive — being stirred by what is suffered by another. Exercised: *eleēmosunē* — charitable giving as the active outward form. *Metriopatheō* — the active calibrated engagement. *Sumpaschō* — the active suffering-alongside. The vocabulary spans the full range from passive reception of stirring to active outward expression.

---

**Q096: Does any term carry both the human and divine expression simultaneously?**

ANSWER. *Rachum*/*chan.nun* — used of God in Exodus 34:6 and demanded of humans in the prophetic pattern (Hosea 6:6, Micah 6:8). *Chesed* — God's steadfast love and the human character quality required of covenantal faithfulness, in the same term. *Splanchnizō* — used of Jesus in the Gospels and the Good Samaritan parable: both divine and human compassion in the same verb.

---

**Q097: What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal?**

ANSWER. The LXX translation of *rachum* and *chesed* into Greek carries both the womb-compassion and the covenantal-steadfast-love dimensions into the NT vocabulary. The *sym-* compound family reappropriates classical Greek philosophical terms (Stoic *sumpatheō* — soul-body sympathy) for the NT context of Christ and community: a structural reappropriation that changes the ground from philosophical self-mastery to shared creaturely vulnerability and union with Christ.

---

**Q098: Is there a named failure-mode term for the word received but withheld?**

PARTIAL. *Che.sed* (shame) — the negative pole of the *chesed* root names the violation of covenantal compassion as producing shame. But no specific term names the person who has received compassion but withholds it. Jonah functions narratively as this type — the person who has received divine compassion but resents its extension to others — but no technical term names the type.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** Whether a specific term exists for compassion-received-but-withheld deserves examination.

---

**Q099: Does the vocabulary include a term newly coined in the NT period?**

ANSWER. *Polusplanchnos* (James 5:11) — coined to name God's deep, overwhelming compassion: "ordinary vocabulary was insufficient — a new intensification was needed." This is the same term noted in the mercy study, shared between the two registries. The coinage reveals that the NT found existing vocabulary insufficient for the claim it needed to make about divine compassion in the context of suffering.

---

**Q100: What does the verbal form of the primary term reveal?**

ANSWER. *Splanchnizō* — gut-movement verb: the verbal form names a process of being-moved, not a state held still. The word is constitutively dynamic — it names the movement, not the position. The passive form names the being-stirred by external reality; the gut-movement is not self-generated but called forth by what is seen.

---

**Q101: Does the vocabulary include a spatial or architectural term?**

SILENT. No equivalent to the mercy seat (*kappōret*) appears in the compassion vocabulary. The somatic location terms (womb, bowels) are bodily rather than architectural. Isaiah 46:3 (carried from the womb) uses spatial language of containment but it is somatic, not architectural. Compassion does not appear to have an architectural instantiation.

---

**Q102: What does the semantic range of the primary term reveal?**

ANSWER. *Splanchnizō*'s range across four domains (physical suffering, spiritual destitution, hunger, relational need) unified by the quality of inner response rather than the type of need reveals that compassion is not need-specific but response-constituted. The same inner gut-movement produces compassion across all types of human need. The breadth is defined by the quality of the giver's inner response, not the category of the recipient's condition.

---

**Q103: Does any term carry a somatic or physiological dimension?**

ANSWER. Most extensively in the programme. The womb (*rechem*, *racham*). The bowels/intestines (*splanchnizō*, *splanchnon*). The heart (Hosea 11:8). The *sym-* compounds carry the body's shared-suffering logic (1 Corinthians 12:26 — the body suffers together). The compassion vocabulary has the most extensive and most specifically located somatic grounding in the programme — three distinct named interior locations (womb, bowels, heart) all functioning as sites of the word's operation.

---

**Q104: What does the grammatical range of the primary term reveal?**

ANSWER. Verb (*splanchnizō* — the dynamic movement), noun (*splanchnon* — the physical seat), adjective (*sumpathēs* — the settled character), compound adjective (*polusplanchnos* — the intensive quality). *Chesed* as noun (the quality held), verb (*cha.sad* — to be kind), and noun-of-violation (*che.sed* — shame). The grammatical range is extensive — compassion is conceptualised as movement, location, character quality, and violation-consequence simultaneously.

---

**Q105: Does the vocabulary include a supplication term?**

ANSWER. Yes — *techinah*, *tahanun*, *chanan* shared with the prayer vocabulary (11 shared terms). These sit at the intersection of compassion and petition: the inner act of pleading for mercy assumes that God is compassionate. The supplication vocabulary is shared with mercy and prayer, not exclusive to compassion.

---

**Q106: What does the supplication term reveal about the inner posture required?**

ANSWER. The posture of creaturely dependence — grounding appeal in God's steadfast love and compassionate character (Psalm 25:7; Joel 2:13). The shared womb-origin as the structural basis of appeal: creatureliness before the one who formed in the womb. The return-call of Joel 2:13 assumes the posture of the one who has strayed but can return because God's compassion is the ground.

---

**Q107: Does the vocabulary include a term for the mechanism through which the word is administered?**

PARTIAL. Less developed than in mercy. No equivalent atonement-mechanism vocabulary. *Eleēmosunē* names the normative social form (charitable giving) but this is the expression of compassion, not its mechanism. The compassion vocabulary does not have an equivalent to mercy's extensive propitiation family.

---

**Q108: What does the atonement or propitiation vocabulary reveal?**

SILENT. The compassion study does not develop an atonement or propitiation vocabulary in the same register as mercy. The *chesed*-based compassion operates covenantally but without the mechanism-vocabulary of *kip.per*, *kap.po.ret*, *hilasmos*. This is a genuine silence: compassion addresses need and guilt without naming the mechanism by which the moral condition is covered.

**[SHOULD ANSWER]** The absence of atonement mechanism vocabulary in compassion, compared with mercy's extensive development, may be a structurally significant observation — whether compassion presupposes mercy's mechanism or operates independently of it.

**Q109: Does any term reveal a classical-to-NT directional reversal?**

ANSWER. *Metriopatheō* — the Stoic ideal of moderating the passions through self-mastery is reappropriated in Hebrews 5:2 with a new ground: the moderation arises not from philosophical self-mastery but from shared creaturely weakness. The classical term carried one ground (reason over emotion); the NT term carries another (shared vulnerability). The direction is not reversed (as in *hilaskomai*) but the ground is fundamentally changed — a ground-reversal rather than direction-reversal.

---

**Q110: What does the vocabulary reveal about whether the word operates within or across relational boundaries?**

ANSWER. Across — more consistently than any other word studied. Jonah 4:11 — Nineveh (across covenant boundary). Luke 10:33-37 — the Samaritan (across ethnic-religious boundary). Romans 9:15 — sovereign freedom not bounded by covenant membership. *Splanchnizō* applied across all types of need (Matthew 9:36 — a multitude). The compassion vocabulary more consistently names boundary-crossing as intrinsic than any other word in the programme.

---

**Q111: What does the vocabulary reveal about the word's relationship to covenant?**

ANSWER. *Chesed* is covenantally grounded — the foundational form of compassion within covenant. But Jonah and the universal reach of divine compassion establish that the word consistently presses beyond covenant boundaries. The tension is held: covenant is the relational framework; the divine compassion is not exhausted by it. Hosea 6:6 establishes that the inner disposition of *chesed* is what God requires — the covenant's inner reality, not its external performance.

---

**Q112: Does any term name the word as a divine possession?**

ANSWER. Exodus 34:6 — compassion (*rachum*) and graciousness (*chan.nun*) named as settled character attributes of God: divine possessions in the sense of intrinsic qualities. Lamentations 3:22 — "his mercies" (*rachamim*): compassion named as what belongs to God. The formula consistently presents compassion as a possession of divine character before it is an act of divine will.

---

**Q113: What does the root architecture reveal about the word's relationship to adjacent characteristics?**

ANSWER. The broadest root-sharing in the programme. RACHAM root shared with mercy and love. CHESED root shared with kindness, loyalty, devotion, and shame. PATHĒ root shared with suffering and passion. CHANAN root shared with grace. The root architecture places compassion at the structural centre of the programme's inner-being vocabulary — connected by root to more adjacent characteristics than any other word studied.

---

**Q114: Does the vocabulary include compound terms?**

ANSWER. *Polusplanchnos* (James 5:11) — very compassionate: NT compound intensifying the *splanchn* root beyond ordinary range. *Sumpatheō*, *sumpaschō*, *sumpathēs* — the *syn-* (with/together) compounds naming fellow-suffering. *Metriopatheō* — the compound of moderation and passion. The compound vocabulary in compassion is more extensive than in any other word studied: multiple compound families, each naming a distinct dimension of the word.

**Q115: What does the vocabulary reveal about the word's temporal character?**

ANSWER. Both momentary and permanent. *Splanchnizō* — triggered, momentary. *Chesed* — persisting, non-triggered, never-ceasing (Lamentations 3:22). *Chan.nun* — settled character attribute. Isaiah 54:8 — the contrast: anger momentary, steadfast love everlasting. The compassion vocabulary makes the temporal distinction more explicitly than any other word in the programme: the momentary stirring and the permanent disposition are named as different but related expressions of the same characteristic.

---

**Q116: Does the vocabulary reveal whether the word operates as a judicial category, a relational one, or both?**

ANSWER. Primarily relational — the dominant dimension is relational disposition. But the *chus* judicial prohibition vocabulary names compassion as a judicial category by naming its deliberate suspension: the judicial is defined by the deliberate withholding of what is relational by nature. The judicial dimension appears as the exception that defines the rule: compassion is so fundamentally relational that it must be deliberately suspended for justice to function.

---

**Q117: What does the most intensive or plural form reveal?**

ANSWER. *Polusplanchnos* — the most intensive form in the programme, coined specifically for the claim that God's compassion exceeds ordinary vocabulary. The plural *rachamim* (tender mercies) — compassion as accumulated and many-fold. James 5:11's use of the coined compound in the context of suffering signals that the deepest expression of divine compassion requires new vocabulary.

---

**Q118: Does the vocabulary include terms that name the word's expression in speech?**

ANSWER. Exodus 34:6 — the spoken character formula: God's compassion expressed in self-declaration. Luke 7:13 — "do not weep": the first speech-act of compassion toward the widow, preceding the miracle. Joel 2:13 — the communicated character of God as the ground of the return-call. The compassion vocabulary includes both the speech of comfort (addressing the sufferer) and the speech of character-declaration (the foundational formula).

---

**Q119: Does the vocabulary include terms whose etymology connects to the body?**

ANSWER. Most extensively in the programme. The womb (*rechem*, *racham*) as the etymological foundation of the word itself. The bowels/inner organs (*splanchnizō*, *splanchnon*). The heart (Hosea 11:8). The body as the site of mutual fellow-suffering (1 Corinthians 12:26). Compassion is uniquely distinguished in the programme by having its somatic grounding constitutive of its meaning from etymological formation — not added as metaphor but built into the word's origin.

---

**Q120: What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about the word's complete semantic range?**

ANSWER. The vocabulary moves across a spectrum named explicitly in Section 4: from somatic (womb and bowel vocabulary — physical resonance with another's suffering) through characterological (chesed — settled disposition, requiring no triggering event) through participatory (sym- compounds — genuine entry into shared experience) through eschatological (Romans 8:17 — shared suffering as the path to shared glory). This arc is the most complete statement of compassion's semantic range: from the body's most interior movement to the eschatological horizon of glorification.

---

**Q121: Does the vocabulary include a term that holds both the human and divine simultaneously?**

ANSWER. *Chesed* — God's steadfast love and the required human character quality, in the same term. *Rachum*/*chan.nun* — applied to God in Exodus 34:6 and demanded of humans. *Splanchnizō* — used of both Jesus (divine-human) and the Good Samaritan (human): both the divine-human compassion of Jesus and the human compassion of the Samaritan named in the same verb.

**M-003: Where the word undergoes a directional reversal, what does that reveal about the biblical vs cultural understanding?**

ANSWER. *Metriopatheō* — the Stoic ground (philosophical self-mastery) replaced by the biblical ground (shared creaturely weakness). The reversal reveals that the biblical understanding of compassion is not the achievement of emotional distance but the fruit of entering into shared vulnerability. The cultural ideal was compassion through rising above suffering; the biblical ideal is compassion through entering into it. The *sym-* compounds make this explicit: fellow-suffering, not sympathy from a safe distance.

---

**M-004: At which level of the inner being is the word received — and at which level is it expressed?**

ANSWER. The womb and bowel vocabulary names the deepest interior of the body as the site of reception — the pre-volitional stirring that arises before the will engages. Expression operates at the soul level: the outward act of comfort, care, restoration, fellow-suffering. The study does not use spirit-soul-body terminology explicitly, but the movement from interior somatic stirring (womb, bowels) to outward soul-level expression (action, comfort, community) is structurally parallel to mercy's spirit-soul interface.

---

**M-005: Does any root in the word's vocabulary unite the act of giving and the act of seeking in the same form?**

ANSWER. The *chesed* root — covenantal steadfast love as both the divine character quality extended to humans and the human character quality required in return (Hosea 6:6; Micah 6:8; 2 Samuel 22:26). The same *chesed* names both the divine giving and the human orientation of return. The mirror principle of 2 Samuel 22:26 ("with the merciful you show yourself merciful") names the structural correspondence: giver and seeker share the same quality, meeting each other in kind.

---

**M-006: Does the word's root vocabulary include terms naming both unmerited gift and unmerited affliction?**

ANSWER. Yes — the *chesed* root's negative pole (shame/disgrace — *che.sed*) names the affliction that follows the violation of covenantal compassion. The same root holds both the gift (steadfast love) and the consequence of its violation (shame). This is parallel to the *chinnam* observation in mercy: the root holds both gratuitousness-of-gift and gratuitousness-of-suffering. The *chesed* root holds both the gift and the violation-consequence.

---

**M-007: Does the word identify a specific quality in which the human being is called to image or reflect God?**

ANSWER. Yes — most explicitly in the programme. Luke 6:36 — "be merciful (*oiktirmōn*) as your Father is merciful": the compassion-term names the specific quality in which human beings are called to reflect divine character. Micah 6:8 — "love kindness": the *chesed*-compassion quality as the required imago Dei character. The prophetic demand pattern — Israel required to show compassion because they have received it from God — is the most systematic imago Dei argument in the programme for any single characteristic.

**M-008: Does receiving the word produce a changed name or identity-designation?**

ANSWER. Yes — through the womb vocabulary. Isaiah 46:3 — "borne by me from before your birth, carried from the womb": identity constituted by divine carrying preceding the person's capacity to know or respond. Jeremiah 1:5 — "before I formed you in the womb I knew you": identity constituted by prior divine knowing. The *Lo-Ruhamah* reversal (Hosea 1:6 → 1 Peter 2:10), shared between the two registries, applies directly: the name-change from "No Compassion" to "people who have received mercy" is the compassion vocabulary's most concentrated identity-designation change.
