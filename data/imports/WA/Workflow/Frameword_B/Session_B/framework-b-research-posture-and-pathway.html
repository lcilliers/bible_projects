§ 01 — Motivation

Why This Programme Exists

Framework B was not conceived as a path to academic credentialling. The researcher holds postgraduate formation at Masters level and understands what institutional scholarship demands — not just intellectually, but in terms of the negotiations, compromises, and unwanted distractions that come with operating inside that world. That world was considered and set aside.

The programme exists because the questions are genuinely compelling. The driving motivation is the oldest and most honest form of scholarship: being stimulated by the thinking, and wanting to know. Not to be seen to know. Not to be rewarded for knowing. Simply to follow the questions wherever they lead, with rigour and without a predetermined destination.

The purest scholarship has always been driven not by the desire for recognition but by an inability to leave a question alone. Framework B sits in that tradition.

This matters methodologically as well as personally. Research motivated by curiosity rather than credentialling is less likely to foreclose questions prematurely, less likely to fit findings to expected conclusions, and more likely to tolerate the long seasons of unresolved uncertainty that serious inquiry requires. It is also less likely to be distorted by the pressure to publish before the work is ready.

§ 02 — The Companionship Problem

What the Work Actually Needs

Peer review in the formal academic sense — blind review, institutional gatekeeping, credentialled evaluation — is not the primary need. That kind of review serves a publication pipeline. It answers the question: is this work fit for this journal? That is a legitimate but secondary question.

The primary need is different, and harder to meet: genuine intellectual companionship. Someone who finds the same questions compelling, who brings independent depth to the conversation, who will push back not to protect a position but because they are genuinely thinking. Someone who is stimulated rather than threatened by the intersection of biblical languages, consciousness studies, neuroscience, and theological anthropology.

The Structural Problem

Framework B sits at an intersection that no single academic community owns. Theologians may not engage the neuroscience. Neuroscientists will not engage the Hebrew. Biblical scholars may be uncomfortable with the scope. Each community sees the work as slightly someone else's territory. This is not a failure of the research — it is a symptom of its originality. But it means the right conversation partners are scattered rather than clustered, and will not be found through ordinary community channels.

The communities currently available to the researcher have not yet yielded serious traction — not because the work is inaccessible, but because the combination of interests required to engage it fully is genuinely rare. This is a real constraint, not a temporary inconvenience, and it needs to be planned around rather than simply waited out.

§ 03 — Where Companionship Might Be Found

Targeted Approaches to a Scattered Community

The following are not exhaustive but represent the most promising directions for finding intellectually serious engagement without requiring full entry into institutional academic life:

Scholars Working the Same Intersection

The conversation between biblical anthropology and neuroscience is active, if not yet mainstream. Figures such as Joel Green (body-soul-spirit and neuroscience), Warren Brown (neuropsychological approaches to soul), and scholars engaging the Nancey Murphy school of non-reductive physicalism represent one pole. Those working in the tradition of John Cooper's defence of holistic dualism represent another. Both poles take the lexical questions seriously.

Joel Green Warren Brown Nancey Murphy John Cooper Moisés Silva John Walton David Clines

A short, precisely targeted methodology paper — two to four pages, framing the open questions honestly rather than asserting conclusions — sent directly and personally to one or two of these scholars represents the highest-probability route to serious engagement. The ask should be specific and humble: not endorsement, but critical response to the methodological questions.

Lower-Barrier Entry Points

Academia.edu and ResearchGate allow independent scholars to publish working papers and be found by people searching adjacent topics. Zotero research groups on biblical studies attract serious independent scholars outside institutional settings. Biblical theology podcasters with serious academic networks may broker introductions. SBL open membership provides access to special interest groups including psychology and biblical studies.

The document produced in this session — The Soul, the Brain, and the Algorithm (DOC-001) — is itself a potential instrument for finding conversation partners. Its questions are provocative enough to surface genuine interest and specific enough to filter for the right kind of mind.

§ 04 — The Publication Question

If the Work Arrives Somewhere Remarkable

Publication was not the starting point of Framework B. It should not become a distorting pressure on the work in progress. But it cannot be entirely set aside either, for a simple reason: if the programme produces genuinely original findings at the intersection of biblical lexicography, consciousness studies, and theological anthropology, the obligation to make those findings available becomes real.

The conditions under which publication would become not just possible but arguably necessary:

1

A robust and defensible methodology

The Barr critique is answered at the discourse level. The scope boundary is formally declared. The Strong's dependency is acknowledged and managed. The synthesis layer is anchored in verse-level evidence. This is already substantially in place.

2

An original taxonomic contribution

If the programme produces a data-driven taxonomy of inner-being characteristics — including the unexpected finding of which characteristics are computationally replicable and which are not — this would represent a genuinely original scholarly contribution with no clear precedent.

3

Engagement with at least one serious external critic

Not for endorsement, but for the credibility that comes from having subjected the work to genuine intellectual friction from outside. One rigorous critical exchange is worth more than a dozen affirmations.

4

A destination worth the journey

If the synthesis phase (Session D) produces findings that illuminate the biblical vocabulary of inner being in ways that speak meaningfully to contemporary neuroscience, consciousness studies, and theological anthropology — then publication in a form accessible to all three communities would be worth pursuing seriously, on the researcher's own terms and timeline.

The Key Principle

Publication, if it comes, should be the natural overflow of work that has arrived somewhere worth reporting — not a target that shapes the research toward it. The moment the programme begins optimising for publishability rather than truth, the original motivation is compromised. The sequence must remain: know first, then decide whether the knowing is worth sharing in that form.

§ 05 — On the Role of AI in This Programme

A Tool That Must Be Kept Honest

This document was produced in part through dialogue with an AI assistant. That fact is worth noting explicitly, because it bears on the integrity of everything captured here.

The affirmation bias discussed in DOC-001 applies here as much as anywhere. An AI system primed by months of engagement with Framework B will tend to reflect the programme's own assumptions back at it in a more polished form. The risk is not that the AI produces false information — it is that it produces plausible-sounding elaborations of what the researcher already believes, creating a false sense of external validation.

The corrective is not to abandon AI as a thinking partner — the quality of intellectual friction in this session demonstrates genuine value. The corrective is to treat AI engagement as a necessary but not sufficient condition for rigorous thinking: useful for pressure-testing arguments, surfacing blind spots, and drafting structured output, but incapable of replacing the kind of engagement that comes from a mind with genuine independence, its own research programme, and nothing invested in the survival of Framework B.

That kind of mind is what the programme still needs, and is still looking for.

Framework B began with a question about what the biblical text actually says about the inner life of human beings — not what tradition has assumed it says, and not what contemporary psychology projects onto it, but what emerges when the vocabulary is systematically extracted, catalogued, and allowed to generate its own categories. That remains the governing question.

Whether it ends in a private archive, a working paper, a scholarly monograph, or a contribution to a conversation that is only just beginning — the value of the knowing does not depend on what is done with it afterwards. But if the destination is remarkable enough, sharing it will not be optional. It will simply be the right thing to do.
