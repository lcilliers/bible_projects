## Section 1 — What [Word] Is

*Synopsis. 250–300 words. The entry point for the reader. A compressed statement of the word's essential character as a human inner quality. It answers: what is this, in plain terms? It names the word's defining nature, its most distinctive quality, and where it ultimately comes from in a human being. No verse references. No language terminology. No sub-headings. One or two flowing paragraphs. A reader who finishes this section should be able to state in one sentence what this word names.*

## Section 2 — The Nature of [Word]

*What the characteristic is. 400–600 words. This section opens up what Section 1 compressed. It describes what kind of inner quality this is — its character, its range, its different registers or dimensions, and the related concepts and words that help define it by proximity or contrast. It answers: what does this look like from the inside? What adjacent qualities does it resemble, and how is it distinct from them? What does it feel like, and what is its settled quality when it becomes part of a person's character?*

*This section draws on:*
- *The semantic range of the word's usage — the full territory the word covers across the biblical text*
- *The dimensions assigned to the word — what kind of inner-being territory it occupies (relational disposition, moral character, transformation, emotion, cognition, divine-human correspondence)*
- *God as subject — where God is the primary actor, this reveals what the characteristic is at its source level and defines its fullest expression*
- *Related vocabulary — words in the same family or adjacent semantic territory that sharpen the definition*

*Section 2 does not describe what the characteristic produces, what feeds it, or how it operates dynamically. Those belong in Section 3. The boundary: Section 2 defines; Section 3 moves.*

## Section 3 — How [Word] Works

*The dynamic of the characteristic. 400–600 words. This section describes the characteristic in motion — what it does, what it produces, what activates it, what opposes it, and how it expresses itself in a life. It answers: what happens when this characteristic is present? What does it generate in the person who has it, and in others? What does it require, and what does it make possible?*

*This section draws on:*
- *The movement and directionality of the word — whether it flows toward others, is received from outside, is self-generated, or moves in both directions*
- *Causative structures — where the word has a causative form, or where the verse evidence shows it producing or being produced by something else*
- *Somatic evidence — how the characteristic expresses itself through the body: gesture, posture, physiological response, body-part language in the verse text. The body is part of the story, not a footnote*
- *The opposite and the absence — what the characteristic's negation or withdrawal looks like, and what it reveals about the characteristic itself*
- *God as subject — where God acts, this reveals what the characteristic does at its fullest and what it produces in those who receive it*

*Section 3 does not re-describe what the characteristic is. It builds on Section 2's definition and shows it operating. The boundary: Section 2 defines; Section 3 moves.*

## Section 5 — The Language

*Original language vocabulary. 600–900 words. This section describes the words behind the English — what the Hebrew and Greek terms are, what they mean, how they relate to each other, and what the full word family reveals. The vocabulary analysis specifically builds out the concepts established in Sections 2 and 3: terms that carry the characteristic's defining nature belong to Section 2's territory; terms that carry its dynamic, causative, or somatic dimension belong to Section 3's territory. The vocabulary section shows how the original language embeds these distinctions.*

*Structure:*
- *Opening paragraph: the language families, term count, and what the sharing pattern with other word studies signals*
- *Each key term in turn: transliteration and Strong's number in parentheses, occurrence count, range of English translations, core meaning and sub-senses, what this term contributes that the others do not. For Greek terms, significant classical background noted briefly where it illuminates the meaning*
- *Closing synthesis paragraph: what the complete vocabulary reveals as a whole — the arc from the word's root to its fullest expression*
