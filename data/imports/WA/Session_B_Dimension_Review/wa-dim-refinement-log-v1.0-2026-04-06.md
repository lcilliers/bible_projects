### Vocabulary observations

**The Theological/Divine-Human dimension is markedly dominant in C01 (62 groups, 21%).** This is the highest count for any dimension in this cluster, tied with Moral/Conscience. This reflects a structural reality: the primary inner-being terms (mind, soul, heart, spirit, flesh) are inherently relational — they exist in the created-Creator relationship. The structural vocabulary of the inner being is constitutively shaped by its relationship to God. This is a significant finding for Session D.

**The absence of Sin & Vice from C01 is notable.** No group in any of the six structural registries was assigned Sin & Vice. The structural terms name the capacity for sin and moral failure, but not sin itself — they are the theatre in which sin operates, not sin as a category.

**Identity/Selfhood appears 9 times across C01** — all from the soul and spirit registries, and one from heart (lev Aramaic as 'the essential inner person'). These groups name what the person fundamentally *is* (living soul, natural person, spiritual person) rather than what they *do* or *experience*. The Identity/Selfhood dimension is concentrated in the structural vocabulary — as expected.

**The sha.mar family (H8104) spans four dimensions across four groups:** Volitional/Will (G, covenantal obedience), Theological/Divine-Human (H, God's guarding), Moral/Conscience (H subset, self-keeping; I, moral observation), and Character/Disposition (J, vigilance). This cross-dimensional range within a single root is a significant inner-being observation for Session B.

**The ye.tser root spans two dimensions:** Moral/Conscience (936-001, formed moral inclination) and Theological/Divine-Human (936-002, creaturely formation known by God). The OT's closest approach to a foundational moral disposition is both a moral and a creaturely-relational reality.

**Repentance vocabulary spans three registries (112, 182, 183)** through na.cham, metanoia, metanoeo, metamellomai, and the ka.na groups — mapping the inner-being act of turning across Hebrew and Greek, through cognitive, volitional, and spiritual dimensions.
