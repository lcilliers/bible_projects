## Section 2 — Root Family Synthesis

### TSR Root Family

The TSR root is the largest root family in the distress word set and the most analytically complex. It spans all three parts and accounts for 17 sub-IDs — more than any other root. The core image of the family is constriction: being pressed, hemmed in, unable to move or breathe. This physical image is the foundation from which the entire family extends outward into different domains.

**Part 1** established the foundational TSR distress terms: tsa.rah (H6869B), tsar (H6862B), and ya.tsar (H3334), together with bats.tsa.rah (H6869A) as a temporal marker. **Part 2** added the spatial-literal term tsar-narrow (H6862A, which is the root's foundational image stripped of inner-being application), tsar-enemy (H6862C, the one who does the constricting), tsar-hard (H6862D, a marginal material use), and me.tsar (H4712, a pure inner-being term for experienced constriction). **Part 3** added five further tsa.rar sub-IDs (H6887B, C, D, E) and tsa.rah-C (H6869C), completing the picture of the root's full semantic extension.

The cross-part picture reveals a coherent root structure with four functional zones:

**Zone 1 — Pure inner-being distress** (all qualify): tsa.rah (H6869B), tsar (H6862B), ya.tsar (H3334), me.tsar (H4712), tsa.rar-B (H6887B), tsa.rar-C (H6887C). These terms all name the experienced state of being in straits — from the most versatile and widely attested (tsa.rah, 70 occurrences, Gen to Zechariah) to the most concentrated stative form (tsa.rar-C, 4 occurrences, all pure inner-being). Within Zone 1, tsa.rah is the primary term: it spans all three registers (distress-as-circumstance, distress-as-inner-state, distress-producing-somatic-expression) and carries the widest theological range, including the generation-resolution pattern and the god-as-subject flag. Tsar is the first-person felt-condition term. Ya.tsar is the verb of being cramped. Me.tsar is the concentrated inner-state noun. Tsa.rar-B and tsa.rar-C isolate the stative-inner pole without the circumstantial range.

**Zone 2 — Foundational spatial image** (does not qualify as inner-being): tsar-narrow (H6862A), bats.tsa.rah (H6869A). These name literal narrowness and the period of pressing respectively. They are the root's own self-description — the image that generates Zone 1's inner-being language.

**Zone 3 — Relational-hostile** (does not qualify): tsar-enemy (H6862C), tsa.rar-D (H6887D), tsa.rah-C (H6869C), tsur-B (H6696B). These apply the root to persons who function as the constricting agent. The one who hemmed you in becomes the adversary.

**Zone 4 — Legal-social / marginal** (does not qualify): tsar-hard (H6862D), tsa.rar-E (H6887E). Single-occurrence or marginal material/legal uses with no inner-being content.

The god-as-subject pattern in the TSR root reveals a directional split that only becomes visible across all three parts. In Part 1, tsa.rah at 2Ch 15:6 shows God sending distress as agent. In Part 1, tsar at Isa 63:9 shows God participating in the people's distress as co-sufferer. In Part 3, tsa.rar-B at 2Ch 33:12 shows distress leading to humility before God. The TSR root thus carries three divine-relationship registers: God as source of distress (judgement), God as co-bearer of distress (solidarity), and distress as occasion of turning to God (response). No single sub-ID carries all three; the three-way pattern is only visible in the full cross-part synthesis.

**Provisional verdicts from Part 3 on TSR: resolved.** Part 3 identified tsa.rar-B and tsa.rar-C as qualifying, and tsa.rar-D, tsa.rar-E, and tsa.rah-C as non-qualifying. These verdicts are confirmed in the full-family synthesis. The question of whether the TSR sub-IDs should be consolidated into functional clusters (raised in Part 3 Q4) is confirmed as a Phase 2 task — the cross-part analysis supports consolidation into the four zones described above, but the final decision on how to represent the root in the Word Analysis Card is for Phase 2.

### RA / RA.A Root Family

The RA family spans Parts 1 and 2. It is the most semantically wide root in the entire distress set, with H7451A (ra) occurring 684 times. The family presents the deepest methodological challenge in the word set.

**Part 1** covered H7451A (ra, adjective) and H7489A (ra.a, verb). **Part 2** added two STEP sub-entries under the same base number — H7451I (ra.ah, moral-quality sense) and H7451C (ra.ah, experienced-calamity sense).

The cross-part picture reveals that the RA family is doing three distinct kinds of work, carried by functionally distinct sub-IDs:

Ra (H7451A) operates as a quality descriptor across its enormous occurrence range — bad/harmful/evil as an adjective applied to persons, deeds, circumstances, and inner states. Its inner-being application is real but minority: the harmful spirit sequence (1Sa 16:14 ff.), the face-sadness of Neh 2:2, and Psa 78:49's cluster with wrath and distress are the strongest inner-being markers in Part 1's ra verse set.

Ra.a (H7489A) is the verb carrying the causative dimension: to do harm, to cause evil, to make worse. This term belongs in the inner-being analysis primarily as the mechanism of inflicting distress on another rather than as a primary distress-state term. The causative_form_present flag is confirmed.

Ra.ah H7451I and H7451C present the methodological complication flagged in Part 2: both share the identical base number, meaning_summary, and occurrence count. The verse-set clustering analysis in Part 2 revealed a genuine functional difference — H7451I leans toward moral-character evil; H7451C leans toward experienced-disaster-event — but whether this is a lexical sub-distinction or a distributional tendency within one word remains unresolved. This consolidation-candidate question is confirmed as a pending Phase 2 decision.

The god-as-subject pattern in the RA family is present in both Parts 1 and 2: God sending a harmful spirit upon Saul (1Sa 16:14 ff., Part 1); God as the one who brings, declares, and may relent from ra.ah (Jer 26:3; Jon 3:10; Exo 32:14, Part 2). The relent-from-disaster pattern in H7451C is the most theologically distinctive feature of the RA family — the divine-distress mechanism has a repentance-responsive dimension built into the root's usage. This parallels the distress-to-response pattern in the TSR root but operates at a covenantal-national scale rather than the individual scale that dominates TSR.

**Consolidated verdict:** The RA family qualifies partially as an inner-being distress set, with H7451C and H7451A (harmful-spirit and emotional uses) carrying the strongest qualification. H7451I qualifies only partially — its verse set is more consistently moral-characterisation than experienced inner state. Ra.a qualifies as the causative mechanism of inflicting distress.

### TSUQ Root Family

The TSUQ root spans Parts 1 and 3. Part 1 covered tsu.qah (H6695B), mu.tsaq (H4164), ma.tsoq (H4689), and tsoq (H6695A). Part 3 added tsuq (H6693), the verbal form.

Together, the family presents a tight cluster of pressing-constraint vocabulary. The four Part 1 nouns all operate within a single range of severe constraint and anguish, differentiated primarily by nominal formation rather than by meaningful functional difference. The Part 3 addition of the causative verb tsuq (H6693) adds the infliction-mechanism: the Hiphil form that describes one agent causing the pressing in another (Isa 29:2: God distresses Ariel; Judg 16:16: Delilah pressing Samson's soul to the point of death). The derivative noun chain (tsuq → ma.tsoq, me.tsu.qah, tsoq, tsu.qah) is the most explicit vocabulary for the constriction-image in pure noun form, separate from the TSR family's broader inner-being range.

The notable absence within the family is me.tsu.qah, which is listed in the Part 1 source header but has no word analysis block in any of the three parts. This gap is unresolved.

**Consolidated verdict:** The TSUQ family qualifies as a set of inner-being distress terms, with the Part 1 nouns naming the state and the Part 3 verb naming the mechanism of infliction. The family is analytically secondary to the TSR family — it covers a narrower, more acute register — and consolidation of the near-synonymous nouns is a viable Phase 2 option.

### MAR Root Family (Part 1 only)

Mar (H4751) is the sole MAR-family term. The bitterness-as-inner-quality it names is functionally distinct from the pressing-constraint image of TSR and TSUQ. Mar consistently applies to the soul as an inner-being location (Job 3:20; 7:11; 10:1; 1Sa 22:2), describes the bodily expression of inner bitterness (bitter crying, bitter weeping), and in 1Sa 1:10 combines both simultaneously. The root image — adverse taste pervading the inner person — is unique among the distress word set's root images.

**Verdict confirmed.** Mar qualifies as a clear inner-being term. The Part 1 verdict stands. No cross-part modifications are needed.

### THLIBO / STENO Root Families (Parts 2 and 3)

Part 2 established thlipsis (G2347) and stenochoria (G4730) as the major NT distress terms. Part 3 added their verbal forms: thlibō (G2346) and stenochōreō (G4729).

The cross-part picture confirms the verb-noun relationships as expected. Thlibō adds no new analytical content beyond thlipsis — it confirms the same registers (apostolic-vocational suffering, normative affliction of disciples, reciprocity framing). Stenochōreō in its single verse (2Cor 6:12) is analytically the most direct inner-being use in the entire STENO family: the constriction is explicitly located in the affections (splanchna), not in external circumstances.

The co-occurrence of thlibō and stenochōreō in 2Cor 4:8 ("afflicted in every way but not crushed" — using thlibō followed by "not stenochōreō") is a cross-part finding that only becomes visible in synthesis: the two roots appear as an intentional pair in a single Pauline assertion, with thlibō naming the outer pressing and stenochōreō naming the inner restriction. This pair confirms that the two root images are not synonyms but complementary — the physical pressing-in and the inner cramping of feeling.

**Consolidated verdict:** The THLIBO and STENO families together constitute the NT structural equivalent of the TSR root family. They share the pressing-constriction image, operate predominantly in an inner-being register, and carry the transformative-purposive framing that is the NT's distinctive theological contribution to the distress word set.

### ATSAV Root Family (Part 3 only)

A.tsav (H6087A) qualifies clearly. The root is unique in carrying three separate god-as-subject references: Gen 6:6 (God's a.tsav at human wickedness, explicitly heart-located), Psa 78:40 (God grieved in the desert), and Isa 63:10 (Holy Spirit grieved). The ka.av note in Part 3 observed the connection between the a.tsav root and the concept of shaping-under-strain (the related words e.tsev = toil/vessel, a.tsav = to shape). This root-image — that the inner anguish of a.tsav is connected to the labour of forming — is the most theologically fertile observation in Part 3 and warrants explicit Phase 2 attention.

The cross-Testament parallel between a.tsav (Isa 63:10) and lupeō (Eph 4:30) in their application to the Holy Spirit is confirmed as the single most significant structural link between OT and NT in the entire distress word set. Both terms apply the same theological claim — human conduct grieves the Holy Spirit — in the language of their respective Testaments. This is not merely verbal overlap; it is a direct cross-Testament theological parallel on the inner-being capacity of the divine Spirit.

**Verdict confirmed.** A.tsav qualifies. The ATSAV root carries the Spirit-grieving parallel that no other root in the set carries.

### LUPE Root Family (Part 3 only)

Lupeō (G3076) crosses the grief and distress registries. The cross-registry link to grief is confirmed in the cross_registry_links of the Part 3 JSON. Within the distress word set, lupeō's most significant contribution is the godly-grief pathway of 2Cor 7:9–11 (grief that is "according to God" producing repentance) and the Spirit-grieving application of Eph 4:30.

Lupeō shares its transformative-purposive framing with thlipsis (Part 2), confirming that this is a consistent NT pattern rather than a feature of any single term: inner distress states, when oriented toward God, are reframed as productive of something beyond themselves. Thlipsis produces endurance and glory; lupeō (kata Theon) produces repentance; sunechō (Part 3, Phi 1:23) names the productive tension of Paul's life-or-death choice. The NT does not merely describe distress; it consistently reframes it as directional.

**Verdict confirmed.** Lupeō qualifies. Phase 2 will need to decide how to handle the grief-distress boundary in a word that deliberately occupies both registries.

## Section 3 — Full-Word OT/NT Register Analysis

The distress word set is predominantly an OT set by term count: 38 Hebrew terms versus 15 Greek NT terms (with one Aramaic term of marginal relevance). The OT set covers a broader semantic range and includes many terms that are situational rather than inner-being. The NT set is narrower, more consistently inner-directed, and carries a distinctive transformative-purposive framing absent from the OT.

**The OT distress vocabulary organises around three root images.** The pressing-constraint image (TSR, TSUQ) dominates by volume and by the range of inner-being content. The adverse-taste image (MAR) names a qualitatively different inner texture. The burning image (CHARAH, RA-family harmful-spirit uses) introduces the anger-distress intersection. Across these images, the OT distress vocabulary tends toward versatility: the same term often names both the external circumstance and the inner experienced state without distinguishing between them — tsa.rah is simultaneously the day of trouble, the distress of the soul, and the distress that produces somatic trembling. This semantic elasticity is characteristic of OT inner-being terms and is not a vagueness in the vocabulary; it reflects a different relationship between the inner person and the world they inhabit.

The OT carries two directional patterns for distress that are not present in the NT vocabulary of this set. First, the god-as-source pattern: God sends distress as discipline or judgement (tsa.rah at 2Ch 15:6; ra.ah at 1Sa 16:14; ra.a at Mic 4:6; tsuq at Isa 29:2), and may relent from it upon repentance (ra.ah H7451C, the relent-from-disaster pattern). Second, the generation-resolution bidirectionality: in the TSR root especially, the same root appears in both the distress-generating and the distress-resolving registers — tsar names the trouble one cries from (Psa 18:6) and the trouble that God ends (Psa 107:6, 13, 19, 28). God is both the one encountered in distress and, at other moments, the agent of it.

**The NT distress vocabulary organises around the pressing-constraint image exclusively** — thlipsis/thlibō (THLIBO), stenochoria/stenochōreō (STENO), sunechō (SUNECHO), anankē (ANANKE). The bitter-taste and burning-anger images are absent. The NT adds three elements not present in the OT vocabulary of this set.

The first is the transformative-purposive reframing. Thlipsis produces endurance and then character and then hope (Rom 5:3). Momentary affliction prepares eternal glory (2Cor 4:17). Receiving the word in affliction with the joy of the Spirit is the apostolic norm (1Th 1:6). Godly grief (lupeō kata Theon) produces repentance and salvation (2Cor 7:10). No OT term in this set carries this reframing as a primary feature, though OT distress-leading-to-prayer and distress-producing-repentance patterns (2Ch 33:12 via tsa.rar-B; the Psalms' generation-to-resolution pattern) constitute partial structural parallels.

The second NT addition is the normative expectation of distress as the expected condition of disciples and apostles — "you are destined for this" (1Th 3:3), "through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom" (Act 14:22), Paul naming thlipsis as the routinely expected apostolic experience (2Cor 6:4; 12:10; 1Th 3:4). The OT treats distress as exceptional, situational, and to be cried out from. The NT treats it as structural to the life of faith.

The third is the cross-Testament Spirit-grieving parallel, which is the single most significant OT-NT connection in the word set. A.tsav at Isa 63:10 and lupeō at Eph 4:30 apply identical theological content — human conduct grieving the Holy Spirit — in the vocabularies of their respective Testaments. This parallel establishes that the inner-being capacity of the divine Spirit spans both Testaments, and that at least two of the distress terms in this word set name something the Spirit of God experiences.

## Section 4 — Qualification Summary

Of 56 terms assessed across the three parts, approximately 26 qualify as inner-being distress terms (at varying confidence levels), 8 are partial or borderline, and approximately 22 do not qualify. The qualifying terms are predominantly OT Hebrew, which reflects both the overall composition of the set and the OT's wider range of inner-being vocabulary in the distress domain.

The dominant register of the qualifying terms is **constriction-pressure**: the TSR family (Zone 1), the TSUQ family, and the NT THLIBO/STENO/SUNECHO families all name the inner experience of being pressed, hemmed in, unable to breathe or move. This is the central image of the distress word set and the one with the most consistent inner-being evidence across both Testaments. It is not accidental that the most frequently occurring single term (tsa.rah, 70 occurrences) and the NT's major distress term (thlipsis, 45 occurrences) both operate within this image.

The inner-being event that the qualifying terms cluster around is: an experience of constriction — inward, sustained, and inescapable — that prompts vocalisation (ze.a.qah/tse.va.chah), generates somatic expression (tsa.rah at Hab 3:16; ba.hal at Isa 21:3; chul at Psa 55:4), produces behaviour (the crying-out to God in the Psalms; repentance in 2Ch 33:12; capitulation in Judg 16:16), and in the NT, creates conditions in which divine purposes are worked out. Distress in this word set is not a passive endured state; it is consistently shown producing directional responses — toward God, toward expression, toward transformation.

The single most analytically significant term in the full set is **tsa.rah (H6869B)**. It has the widest semantic range (all three OT registers present simultaneously), the highest occurrence count of any unambiguously qualifying term, the clearest direct inner-being markers (Gen 42:21: "the distress of his soul"), the most extensive somatic evidence (Jer 4:31; Hab 3:16), the god-as-source use (2Ch 15:6), and the generation-resolution bidirectionality (Psa 107 fourfold pattern). No other term carries this breadth. Thlipsis is its NT structural equivalent in scope and theological weight, and the two terms together span the word set's temporal range.

## Section 6 — Consolidated Open Questions

### Word-Level

**W1 [WORD-LEVEL]** Is the inner-being word set for distress intended to include somatic-expressive terms (ze.a.qah, tse.va.chah, si.ach) that name the bodily output of distress rather than the inner state itself, and if so, how should they be categorised differently from distress-state terms?

**W2 [WORD-LEVEL]** The distress word set contains three root images — pressing/constraint (TSR, TSUQ, THLIBO), adverse taste (MAR), and burning heat (CHARAH, RA-harmful-spirit) — alongside the OT dismay-at-divine-encounter cluster (BAHAL). Should the final Word Analysis Card represent these as sub-types of a single distress category, or as distinct inner-being states that the word "distress" gathers under one English label?

**W3 [WORD-LEVEL]** The distress word set includes terms whose primary referent is anger (cha.rah), grief (lupeō, a.tsav, mar, ke.ev), fear-dismay (ba.hal), loathing-dread (quts), and pain (ke.ev, ka.av, odunao) alongside terms whose primary referent is constriction-pressure (TSR, TSUQ, THLIBO). Is distress a category that contains these states or a state that accompanies them?

**R1 [ROOT: TSR]** Across all three parts the TSR root now has at least 15 sub-IDs covering inner-distress, spatial image, enemy/agent, relational-hostile, and legal-social functions. Should these be consolidated into the four functional zones identified in Section 2 for the Word Analysis Card, and if so, how should Zone 2 (spatial image) be noted — as background etymology or as an active analytical entry?

**R2 [ROOT: TSR]** The TSR root carries three divine-relationship registers: God as source of distress (judgement), God as co-bearer of distress (solidarity at Isa 63:9), and distress as the occasion of turning to God. Are these three registers of a single conceptual complex or three analytically distinct patterns?

**R3 [ROOT: RA]** Does the functional difference between H7451I (moral-quality sense) and H7451C (experienced-calamity sense) reflect a genuine lexical sub-distinction within the H7451 lemma, or is it a distributional tendency within one word that the STEP gloss labelling has partially captured? **R4 [ROOT: RA]** The relent-from-disaster pattern in H7451C (God declaring ra.ah and then relenting upon repentance) constitutes a distress that is anticipated and may be averted. Is anticipated-but-averted distress a distinct inner-being category from distress that is actually experienced?

**R7 [ROOT: BAHAL]** The dismay-at-God-hiding-his-face cluster in ba.hal (Psa 30:7; 104:29) has no direct NT equivalent in this word set. Is this a genuine theological-register difference between OT and NT (reflecting the different frameworks of divine hiddenness versus indwelling Spirit), or does it reflect the particular NT term selection?

### Term-Level

**T1 [TERM: tsa.rah]** Is the inner-being reference in tsa.rah — where the state is located in the soul (Gen 42:21) — the primary usage of the term, or is the inner location an application of what is fundamentally a situational term? This determines whether tsa.rah qualifies as a primary inner-being entry or a contextual marker.

**T2 [TERM: tsa.rah]** Does the somatic expression associated with tsa.rah (Hab 3:16; Jer 4:31; 6:24) represent the inner state producing physical expression, or physical experience generating inner distress?

**T3 [TERM: tsar]** Is the predominance of first-person declaration in the tsar verse set (Psa 4:1; 18:6; 2Sa 22:7; 24:14) a semantic property of the term — that it names felt inner distress as the subject names it — or a genre effect of the lament psalm and narrative prayer contexts in which it appears?

**T5 [TERM: ra.ah]** In the "harmful spirit from the Lord" sequence (1Sa 16:14–19:9), ra names both the character of the divine agent sent and the resulting inner state in Saul. Does the term operate as both a causal and an experiential descriptor simultaneously in these passages?

**T6 [TERM: mar]** Mar is applied directly to the soul in multiple verses (Job 3:20; 7:11; 10:1; 1Sa 22:2). Does mar name a quality of the soul's condition (bitterness as an inner texture) or the soul's expression outward (bitter crying, bitter speech)?

Does the psycho-somatic span of this root family require its own category in the inner-being taxonomy, or should ke.ev/ka.av be absorbed into existing pain/distress categories?

**T8 [TERM: a.tsav / lupeō]** Isa 63:10 (a.tsav) and Eph 4:30 (lupeō) constitute the single most significant cross-Testament parallel in the word set. Do these two terms function as functional equivalents for the Spirit-grieving concept, or do they name differently-nuanced states that the Spirit-grieving context is applying to the same theological claim?

**T9 [TERM: ademoneo]** Ademoneo appears at Mat 26:37 and Mar 14:33 as a descriptor of Jesus in Gethsemane alongside lupeō. Does ademoneo name a distinct inner state or an amplification of grief — and is it possible to distinguish it from lupeō in the Gethsemane context?

### OT/NT Register

**OT1 [OT/NT]** The OT shows God as both the agent sending distress and the deliverer from it, using the same root vocabulary in both directions. The NT does not carry this bidirectionality within the THLIBO/STENO family — Christ overcomes thlipsis (Joh 16:33) but is not named as its agent within those terms. Is this a theological shift or a vocabulary-selection effect?

**OT2 [OT/NT]** The NT consistently reframes distress as transformative when directed toward God (thlipsis → endurance; lupeō kata Theon → repentance; sunechō → productive tension). The OT contains structural parallels (tsa.rar-B leading to humility in 2Ch 33:12; the generation-resolution Psalms pattern). Is the NT transformative-purposive framing a theological development of OT patterns or a qualitatively new theological move?

**OT3 [OT/NT]** The OT presents distress as exceptional and situational (crisis, judgement, danger). The NT presents thlipsis and related terms as the normative expected condition of disciples and apostles. What does this shift in framing mean for the inner-being model's understanding of who the subject of distress is and what situational context generates it?

**OT4 [OT/NT]** The a.tsav / lupeō Spirit-grieving parallel is the most direct cross-Testament connection in the word set. Does its existence imply that the inner-being states named by a.tsav and lupeō are not merely human experiences but are participatory in the inner life of God — and if so, what are the implications for how the distress word set is framed in the Word Analysis Card?
