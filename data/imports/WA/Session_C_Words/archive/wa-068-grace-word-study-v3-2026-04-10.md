# Grace — Word Characteristic Study
## Framework B Soul Word Analysis Programme
### Registry 068 | Session C v3 | 2026-04-10

---

## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It does not arise from the quality of the recipient. It arises from within the giver, from a settled orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in four distinct but related forms. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and discovering, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you. This is grace as relational position. It creates something real: access, protection, standing, the sense of being known and held. In its deepest biblical expression this is what a human being discovers in relationship with God — not mere tolerance, but active, personal goodwill that precedes any action on the recipient's part. Moses finds that he is known by name; that prior knowledge is the entire basis of his standing. The gaze of the greater party is the location of the gift: to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention.

The second form is grace as a sustained inner condition — the ongoing state of being in favour, standing in grace as a sphere one inhabits rather than a transaction that concludes. This is not the same as receiving grace; it is the continuing inner condition of the person whose standing has been established by grace. Romans 5:2 names it spatially: grace is something one stands in, and from that standing one is oriented forward toward what grace promises.

The third form is grace as specific capacity — the concrete gift that equips the inner person for a particular function. The word of grace builds (Acts 20:32); grace given to Paul enables his apostolic effort (1Cor 15:10). Grace as capacity is grace as enabling endowment — it makes the person able to do and be what they could not from their own resources.

The fourth form is grace as inner character quality — the gracious disposition that belongs to the person formed by grace, expressed in speech, in attention to others, and in the way they hold power without using it to extract. Proverbs 31:30 names the genuine form against its imitation: the fear of the Lord is the inner ground from which lasting gracious character grows.

These four forms are not separate phenomena. They are the same grace working its way through the inner person: received at the spirit level as divine disposition, constituting a standing at the relational level, equipping for function at the capacity level, and expressing itself outward as gracious character. Grace has a direction — it flows inward and then back out — and the full inner-being picture requires all four faces.

The originating source of grace is divine character. Grace enters human experience from above and works its way outward from there. And in its most radical expression, even the capacity to seek grace — the posture of earnest appeal itself — is something God gives, not something the human being generates independently.

Grace and joy share a common root in Greek: *charis* (grace) derives from *chairo* (to rejoice). This is not incidental etymology. Grace is the disposition of one who gives with gladness; receiving grace produces joy as its natural inner response. The root connection signals that grace and joy are not merely theologically related but structurally bonded at the level of language.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The natural enemy of grace is transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on exchange, that what you receive is proportional to what you have earned. Where transaction governs, grace cannot operate. Its negation is not hostility but the closed logic of merit: you get what you deserve, no more. Wherever this logic dominates a person's inner life, it shuts down the capacity both to receive grace and to extend it. The lexical shadow of grace in the word family confirms this: *acharistos* — ungrateful, graceless — names the person who has received grace but has not registered it as grace. Ingratitude is the inner failure to recognise what has been given.

What feeds grace in a person is primarily the experience of having received it undeservedly and knowing it. Supplication — earnest, vulnerability-based appeal — is itself a form of grace-awareness: it acknowledges that what is needed cannot be demanded, only asked for. That posture already recognises that grace is freely given. The body knows this: the person seeking grace prostrates themselves, raises their hands, fasts, weeps — the whole body is brought into alignment with what the soul is doing, enacting the inner posture of non-leverage before the one who can give or withhold. What diminishes grace is the loss of that recognition: self-sufficiency closes the space through which grace moves, as does contempt — placing others below the level where they deserve consideration.

Three structural features of grace's inner-being work distinguish it from easier accounts of blessing or favour.

The first is that grace does not resolve weakness — it inhabits it. Paul's request for relief is denied; grace is the answer instead. The inner-being transformation grace produces is not from weakness to strength but from weakness-as-problem to weakness-as-the-place-where-grace-operates. The person formed by grace discovers that their conditions of insufficiency are not the obstacle to grace but its most active theatre.

The second is that grace produces fear before it produces peace. The initial inner response to unexpected divine favour is not comfort but the weight of recognition — the creaturely recoil before the magnitude of what is being given. The angel says "do not be afraid" because fear is precisely what has arisen. Grace settles into peace as the inner person adjusts to the new condition of standing-in-favour; but the first movement is fear, not relief.

The third is that at its fullest, grace produces mourning before joy. Zechariah 12:10 — the most layered verse in the registry — describes the eschatological outpouring of a spirit of grace producing mourning and weeping as the first inner response. This is not mourning in the absence of grace but mourning as grace's first fruit in the inner person who has seen clearly what grace has cost. The grief-laden recognition of the one who has been pierced is the inner-being response of the person to whom grace has been fully given and who understands it.

What grace produces in the person who has it is stability that is not earned — a standing in relationship that holds even when circumstances do not support it. This is not a passive or static condition; grace as a present standing is oriented toward a future, and the person who stands in it lives with a forward-directed hope. From that stability comes the capacity to extend grace outward: to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt. The one who has had their own debt cancelled becomes capable of cancelling another's — and this extension flows not from discipline but from a softened inner orientation, a tenderness of heart that is itself the work of grace. The grace-agency paradox is important here: the person formed by grace works, genuinely and effortfully — yet the work is simultaneously the operation of grace rather than the person's own achievement. Grace does not produce passivity; it reconstitutes agency.

What grace produces in relationships is access and loyalty. The person who discovers unexpectedly that another is disposed toward them — that they have been seen by someone with no obligation to see them — experiences something that contractual relationship cannot produce. Grace does not originate in human character. The data consistently places the divine disposition as the source. Human grace is downstream of what has been received.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It is not triggered by the quality of the recipient. It arises entirely from within the one who grants it, from a settled inner orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

> **Ephesians 2:8** — *For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God.*
>
> This verse establishes the grammar of grace with maximum compression: grace precedes faith, not the reverse — "by grace... through faith" places grace as the sphere and faith as the channel. The explicit denial "not your own doing" rules out any reading of grace as a reward for faith. The characterisation as gift closes the circuit: a gift is not owed, not earned, and cannot be returned on equal terms. The Greek word for access — *prosagōgē*, approach and admission to a presence — implies a prior state of no-access. Grace is not merely a gift given from a distance; it is an opening of the threshold. The person has moved from outside to inside, and the inside is grace as a sustained condition, not a concluded transaction. What the verse does that no prose summary can is settle three things in a single sentence: the agent (God), the means (faith), and the negation of the alternative (not your own doing).

> **2 Corinthians 12:9** — *But he said to me, "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me.*
>
> The context is refusal — Paul's request for relief is denied, and grace is offered instead as the answer. This is the most precise definition of grace as sufficiency within difficulty rather than escape from it. The pairing "my grace is sufficient / my power is made perfect in weakness" establishes the structural principle: grace does not resolve weakness; it operates within weakness as its fullest theatre. The inner-being transformation grace produces here is not from weakness to strength but from weakness-as-problem to weakness-as-the-place-where-grace-is-most-fully-operative. Crucially, grace here is spoken — God says "my grace is sufficient for you" — which makes it a relational address, not merely an impersonal provision. The experience of grace in this verse is the experience of being spoken to by the one who withholds relief: not abandonment, but a refusal that names what is actually being given. Grace *inhabits* the condition rather than *removing* it.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in four forms. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and finding, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you rather than against you.

> **Exodus 33:17** — *And the Lord said to Moses, "This very thing that you have spoken I will do, for you have found favor in my sight, and I know you by name."*
>
> The basis of Moses' favour is named precisely: not performance, not petition quality, but prior personal knowledge — "I know you by name." God's knowing of Moses precedes the favour and grounds it. The formula "found favour in my sight" is the Hebrew idiom of grace: the gaze of the greater party is the bodily location of the gift — to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention. This is why the eye is the primary somatic marker of grace in Hebrew: to find favour *in the eyes of* names the inner-being experience of being seen by the one who matters. The verse also shows that grace creates something specific and practical: access. The thing Moses asked for is granted. Favour is not merely a feeling in God; the divine inner disposition produces a concrete outcome for the one who has been seen and known.

> **Luke 1:30** — *And the angel said to her, "Do not be afraid, Mary, for you have found favor with God."*
>
> The opening phrase — "do not be afraid" — names the actual inner response that grace initially produces: fear. Favour does not arrive as something naturally expected; it comes as interruption, and its weight generates creaturely recoil before it generates peace. The sequence is deliberate: grace announced → fear → reassurance → receptivity ("let it be to me according to your word," Luke 1:38). Grace settles into peace as the inner person adjusts to the new condition of standing-in-favour — but the first movement is fear, not comfort. Notably, the announcement that Mary has found favour precedes any statement of what she is being asked to do: the characteristic is given before the vocation. Grace creates the standing before it commissions the person. The content of the grace is immediate: "the Lord is with you." Divine presence is the gift.

The second form is grace as a sustained inner condition — standing in grace as a sphere one inhabits rather than a transaction one receives and completes.

> **Romans 5:2** — *Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God.*
>
> The spatial metaphor is load-bearing: grace is described as a sphere in which one stands — a completed entry with continuing effect. *Prosagōgē* (access, approach, admission to a presence) implies a prior state of no-access, a threshold that has been opened. Faith is the means of entry, but what one enters is grace as a sustained condition. The connection to hope and the glory of God opens the forward dimension: what one stands in is not a static safety but an orientation toward a future. Grace as present standing and hope as its forward reach belong together as parts of a single inner posture — the characteristic holds the person now, and points them toward what is still to come.

> **1 Corinthians 15:10** — *But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me was not in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me.*
>
> This is the clearest statement in the corpus of grace as identity-constituting. "I am what I am" is not a neutral self-description; it is a claim about the ground of being — Paul's identity is grounded entirely in reception rather than achievement. The verse holds in tension two things that a merit-based framework cannot hold simultaneously: Paul's genuine effort and the priority of grace. Grace does not produce passivity — it reconstitutes agency. The work is real and Paul's; and the work is simultaneously not Paul's but grace. This is the grace-agency paradox as the normal inner-being condition of the person formed by grace, not a special spiritual experience. What has been given becomes the source from which the person acts, not the reward for acting well.

The third form is grace as specific capacity — the enabling endowment that equips the inner person for a particular function.

> **Acts 20:32** — *And now I commend you to God and to the word of his grace, which is able to build you up and to give you the inheritance among all those who are sanctified.*
>
> Grace has been given a communicative form: the word of his grace. It can be spoken, transmitted, and built upon. The capacity of this word to "build you up" (*oikodomeō* — to construct, to edify) names grace as a constructive and formative force — it creates substance in those who receive it rather than merely informing them. The inheritance language opens the eschatological dimension: what grace is building toward is not merely present wellbeing but a future possession among a community set apart. Grace commissions, constructs, and points forward — it is not static gift but living power.

The fourth form is grace as inner character quality — the gracious disposition that belongs to the person so formed.

> **Proverbs 31:30** — *Charm is deceitful, and beauty is vain, but a woman who fears the Lord is to be praised.*
>
> This verse names the distinction between genuine grace and its performed imitation. The Hebrew word for charm here is *chen* — the same word as grace. The verse uses the language of grace to describe what grace, when merely performed, becomes: deceptive attractiveness that achieves the social effect of favour without its inner ground. The fear of the Lord is named as the inner orientation from which lasting gracious character genuinely grows. Grace as character quality shares its source with grace as divine gift: both are rooted in the same place — the orientation of the inner person toward God. Gracious character cannot be cultivated as a social skill; it grows from the inner orientation of reverence.

> **Ruth 2:10** — *Then she fell on her face, bowing to the ground, and said to him, "Why have I found favor in your eyes, that you should take notice of me, since I am a foreigner?"*
>
> Ruth's posture — prostrate, face to the ground — is the somatic expression of the inner posture that grace-reception produces, enacted before a word is spoken. The body moves first; the question follows. This sequencing matters: the inner recognition of receiving what cannot be accounted for by anything in oneself is registered in the body before it is articulated in words. Her question names genuine incomprehension — the experience of receiving what cannot be explained by anything about the recipient. The word "foreigner" identifies the structural reason she has no claim: she stands outside every circle of natural obligation. Grace in this scene moves precisely across the boundary of exclusion — the one with no standing receives standing. The gaze of Boaz resting on Ruth in favour is the somatic form of the grace dynamic: to find favour in someone's eyes is to be seen by someone who had no obligation to see you.

The natural enemy of grace is transaction. Grace by definition gives what has not been earned.

> **Romans 8:32** — *He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?*
>
> The logic proceeds from the greater act to the lesser: the supreme giving (the Son) is the inferential guarantee of all subsequent giving. The argument structure encodes something specific: not merely "God is generous" but "because the supreme gift has been given, all other grace follows as a logical inference." The inner-being state this produces in the recipient is not merely hope but confidence — assurance derived from reasoning about what has already happened, not from feeling or present circumstances. The rhetorical question makes the giver's internal consistency the ground of the recipient's security: the one who gave in the greater circumstance will give in the lesser. Grace, once established as the mode of God's relationship with humanity, becomes the reliable orientation of all that follows.

> **Ephesians 4:32** — *Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you.*
>
> The verse structures grace as a circuit: "as God in Christ forgave you" is not illustrative but causal — the divine forgiveness is the enabling ground of the human act. Grace received is supposed to become grace extended. The inner disposition from which this flows is described as kindness and tender-heartedness — the second word (*eusplanchnos*, tender in the inner parts) names the specific inner-being organ of grace-transmission: the softened, compassionate interior that grace produces and through which grace then flows outward. Grace as humanly extended is not a willed performance; it is an inner-organ condition, a softening that has occurred at the core of the person. The somatic register of *eusplanchnos* confirms that forgiving another is not merely a decision but a condition of the inner life — the inner parts have been made tender by what they have received.

> **Luke 1:28** — *And he came to her and said, "Greetings, O favored one, the Lord is with you!"*
>
> The salutation "favored one" (*kecharitōmenē*) is a perfect passive participle — one who has been graced and who remains in that completed condition. Mary is addressed not by name first but by what has been done to her: her identity in the angel's address is constituted by the grace-act. She is defined by what has been received, not by lineage, status, or achievement. The grammatical form encodes both the completeness of the act and its continuing state. The content of the grace is named immediately: "the Lord is with you." Divine presence is the gift. The simplest and most concentrated form of the grace dynamic: the greater comes to the lesser, names them as favoured, and the content of the favour is presence itself. The salutation also opens with *chairē* — Greetings, Rejoice — the joy-root that grace shares. Grace arrives accompanied by the call to joy, even before the weight of what it carries is known.

> **Daniel 9:3** — *Then I turned my face to the Lord God, seeking him by prayer and pleas for mercy with fasting and sackcloth and ashes.*
>
> "I turned my face" — the first movement is the inner act of orientation, the whole person directed toward God. Everything else follows from this turn. Fasting, sackcloth, ashes, raised voice — four expressions of a single inner posture enacted simultaneously through the body. Each element removes something from the ordinary bodily state — food, normal clothing, cleanliness — and replaces it with reduction and directed orientation. The body is brought into complete alignment with what the soul is doing: acknowledging unworthiness and need, approaching the one who can give or withhold. This is the most complete somatic expression of grace-seeking in the corpus — the whole person, inner and outer, unified in the direction of appeal.

> **Zechariah 12:10** — *"And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy, so that, when they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child, and weep bitterly over him, as one weeps over a firstborn son."*
>
> This verse is the most layered and structurally significant in the registry. It formally links grace and supplication in the same outpouring — both are poured out together, by God — confirming that even the capacity to seek grace is a gift of grace. The result is not relief but mourning: grief-saturated recognition of what has been done, described in the most intense lamentation language of the OT. Grace here does not produce comfort as its first effect. It produces seeing — the recognition of the one who has been pierced — and that seeing produces grief before it produces restoration. The mourning is not the absence of grace but its first fruit in the inner person who has fully received it and now understands what it cost. The body is fully present: mourning, weeping bitterly. This verse is the formal link between grace, supplication, mourning, and repentance — all bound together in a single eschatological act of divine giving. Both the Hebrew word for grace (*chen*) and the word for earnest supplication (*ta.cha.nu.nim*) anchor here: the two faces of the grace dynamic — the divine disposition and the human response it produces — appear in the same verse, because the response is itself a gift of the disposition.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Grace in Scripture is carried by two root families: a Hebrew family centred on the root *chen* (favour, grace) and a Greek family built from *charis* (grace, favour, gift). Together they account for 13 terms in the research inventory, 5 of which are owner terms in this registry. The sharing ratio of 0.85 indicates that grace is highly connected — only 2 terms are unique to grace; 11 are shared with adjacent word studies. This degree of sharing signals that grace is not an isolated concept but one that operates in close relationship with several neighbouring characteristics, particularly those in the supplication, mercy, and covenant-love clusters.

**Greek: charis (G5485)**

The primary New Testament term is *charis* (khar-ees), the Greek word that gives English "charity" and anchors the NT's theology of grace. It appears 88 times in this registry's active verse set, with 241 total NT occurrences. Its range of English translations is exceptionally broad: grace, favour, gift, thanks, blessing, benefit, credit, kindness. This breadth reflects a structural feature of the term: *charis* names not just a disposition but the full arc from disposition through act to the response the act evokes.

The root etymology is significant: *charis* derives from *chairo* (to rejoice, be glad), the χαρ- root connecting grace and joy at the lexical level. This is not incidental. Grace is the disposition of one who gives with gladness; receiving grace produces joy as its natural inner response. The word for joy (*chara*, G5479) is a root-family relative of grace — they share a semantic source. The NT's pairing of grace and peace in epistolary greetings (*charis kai eirēnē*) should be read against this etymological background: grace is the glad-giving disposition that produces peace as the inner-being condition of its recipients.

The Mounce definition moves through at least four distinct domains: the state of favour (relational posture), the gift (the concrete expression), the gracious commission (a task given on the basis of grace), and the emotional response of gratitude (grace acknowledged and returned as thanks). These four domains correspond to the four-face semantic architecture identified in Stage 2: divine disposition (face 1), relational standing (face 2), specific capacity (face 3), and gracious character quality (face 4). The single word names both the origin and the completion of the grace dynamic.

*Charis* has a documented classical history (Homer, 8th century BC) carrying the sense of that which gives pleasure or charm — the quality in a person or thing that produces delight in an observer. This is the *chen* sense carried into Greek: gracious attractiveness, the quality that generates favour. What the NT adds is the theological weight of a God who holds this disposition toward humanity unilaterally and without obligation — transforming the classical reciprocal grace-economy into the unidirectional grace of the gospel.

Two phase 2 flags are raised on *charis*: RELATIONAL_DIRECTION, indicating that the term's meaning is inherently directed toward another — it cannot exist in isolation from a recipient — and SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH, noting that the term crosses four or more semantic domains.

The documented somatic connections of *charis*: the heart as the seat of its reception and transformation (Rom 6:17, 2Cor 8:16); the mouth as the organ of its expression (Luk 4:22: gracious words from his mouth); the flesh as the domain into which it enters most fully (Joh 1:14: the Word became flesh, full of grace and truth). `somatic_link = 1` confirmed from Stage 2.

**Greek: charizō (G5483)**

*Charizō* (khar-id-zo) is the verbal form of the grace family, occurring 24 times in the NT. Its primary senses are: to give freely as a favour or gift, to forgive or cancel a debt, and — in a juridical usage — to hand over a person to the demands of others. The third sense (Lk 23:25, Acts 27:24) sits alongside the first in the same term: the same word names both God graciously giving all things (Rom 8:32) and Pilate handing Jesus over to those demanding his death. Both uses involve giving from a position of authority. The inner-being distinction is entirely in the disposition of the giver: grace in the gift sense flows from free goodwill; grace in the custody sense flows from external pressure overriding the proper direction of authority. The term is structurally neutral in its form — the giving-from-authority structure can be inhabited by genuine grace or its corruption.

The related word *acharistos* (G0884, ungrateful, graceless) is *charizō*'s semantic shadow — the one who has received grace but has not registered it as grace. *Acharistos* names the inner failure mode of grace-reception: receiving the gift without recognising it as gift. Ingratitude is not merely a moral failing; it is the inner-being condition that closes off the grace dynamic by misidentifying what is happening. The word sits in the same root family as grace — gracelessness is defined by its relationship to grace, not independently. The related word *charisma* (G5486, gift) names the particularisation of grace into a defined endowment given to a specific person for the benefit of others: the distinction between *charis* (the source-disposition and its general expression) and *charisma* (a specific outflow) is the distinction between the river and a particular channel it cuts.

`somatic_link = 1` confirmed from Stage 2: Eph 4:32 (heart as the organ of tender forgiveness), Col 2:13 (flesh as the domain of the human condition into which grace intervenes).

**Greek: charitoō (G5487)**

*Charitoō* (khar-ee-to-o) appears only twice in the NT — Luk 1:28 and Eph 1:6 — both in the passive: to have grace shown one, to be made an object of grace. The term is a biblical coinage: it does not appear in classical Greek before the NT writers. The need to create a new word for "to grace someone" suggests that the concept of being *constituted* as an object of grace — not merely receiving a gift but being defined by the completed act of having been graced — exceeded what existing vocabulary could carry. The perfect passive participle (*kecharitōmenē*, Luk 1:28) encodes both the completeness of the act and its continuing state: she has been graced and remains so. *Charitoō* describes a transformation of identity, not just a transaction.

**Hebrew: chen (H2580)**

The primary Hebrew term is *chen* (khane), occurring 69 times in the OT. The relational sense governs this registry. The dominant syntactic form is the idiom "find favour in the eyes/sight of" — a phrase appearing across Genesis, Ruth, Samuel, Esther, Ezra, Nehemiah, and more. This formula makes the gaze of the greater party the systematic somatic locus of grace: to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention. Approximately 20 of the 67 active verses use this eye-vocabulary directly. This is not a metaphor layered onto an abstract concept — it is the primary mode in which the Hebrew world expressed the inner-being reality of relational favour. The eyes of the Lord, the king, the master are the literal bodily location of the relational standing grace creates.

The aesthetic sense of *chen* — grace as charm or attractiveness — appears in Proverbs 31:30 and Ecclesiastes 10:12 (gracious speech generates favour). This usage reveals that the Hebrew word family understood grace not only as relational posture but as a quality that can be performed and imitated — which is precisely why Proverbs 31:30 relativises it against the fear of the Lord as its genuine ground.

The root shared between *chen* and *tachanum* (supplication) is the same root: חנן (*chanan*). This is the semantic architecture of the whole Hebrew grace vocabulary — the word for the disposition and the word for the appeal to that disposition share a root. Seeking grace rightly is already to be oriented in a direction shaped by the reality of what is sought.

The related word family: *channun* (H2587, gracious) — applied overwhelmingly to God; *chinnam* (H2600, for nothing, freely, without cause or cost) — the adverbial form making the economic logic of grace explicit: grace is given for nothing, without merit in the recipient, without cost to the recipient. *Chinnam* is the structural logic of grace made transparent. Also: *chaninah* (H2594, favour) and the supplication vocabulary (H8469, H8467 — see below).

The related proper names (Hannah, Hanun, Hanan, Henadad) built from this root indicate that grace was a prized characteristic whose blessing parents wished on their children.

**Hebrew: tachanum (H8469)**

*Tachanum* (*takh-an-oom*) and its related form *techinnah* (H8467) name supplication — specifically, earnest, grace-seeking appeal. The plural form (*ta.cha.nu.nim*) is intensive: not multiple supplications but the single act of earnest petition at its most concentrated. The term always refers to the human act of appeal before God or authority — `god_as_subject = 0` throughout. Both sub-senses (supplication toward God and toward human authority) name the same inner posture: deep need, relational vulnerability, and the recognition that what is required cannot be demanded but only requested.

The somatic dimension of *tachanum* is among the richest in the entire registry: raised hands toward the sanctuary (Psa 28:2), fasting and sackcloth and ashes (Dan 9:3), weeping (Jer 3:21, 31:9), turning the face toward God (Dan 9:3,17), mourning (Zec 12:10). The body does not remain neutral when the soul is in the posture of supplication. The somatic evidence confirms the inner-being analysis: *tachanum* engages the whole person — not a speech act alone but a whole-person orientation of need toward the one who can give or withhold.

Two phase 2 flags on *tachanum*: ESCHATOLOGICAL_USAGE (Zechariah 12:10 is the primary instance) and RELATIONAL_DIRECTION (inherently other-directed). The eschatological flag points to the most structurally significant finding in the Hebrew material: Zechariah 12:10 shows supplication appearing at the end-time not as something humanity finally produces from within itself, but as something God pours out. The capacity for earnest appeal is itself a grace. This reversal — that the response is a gift as much as the grace being sought — is the deepest observation the vocabulary yields, and it is encoded in the shared root between *chen* and *tachanum*: to seek grace rightly is already to be given the seeking.

**Synthesis observation**

The full vocabulary of grace reveals a semantic architecture that moves from divine character through relational gift to human response and back. At one end stands the divine disposition — the inner orientation of goodwill that precedes all human action. At the other stands supplication — the human inner act that is both a recognition of need and a form of grace-awareness. Between them lies the rich middle territory: favour found in the eyes of the greater, standing conferred, identity constituted, gifts given freely, debts cancelled. The vocabulary also reveals what grace is not: *chinnam* (for nothing) makes explicit that grace operates outside the economy of merit; *acharistos* (ungrateful) names the failure mode of the recipient. Together the vocabulary defines a relational territory in which one party holds all the initiative and the other holds all the need — and yet genuine relationship is precisely what this asymmetry creates. And at the eschatological horizon, even the capacity to seek grace is given — the response is as graced as the gift.
