# Grace — Word Characteristic Study
## Framework B Soul Word Analysis Programme
### Registry 068 | Session C v2 | 2026-04-09

---

## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It does not arise from the quality of the recipient. It arises from within the giver, from a settled orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in two ways. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and discovering, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you. This is grace as relational position. It creates something real: access, protection, standing, the sense of being known and held. In its deepest biblical expression this is what a human being discovers in relationship with God — not mere tolerance, but active, personal goodwill that precedes any action on the recipient's part. Moses finds that he is known by name; that prior knowledge is the entire basis of his standing.

The second way grace appears is as a quality of character in the person formed by it. The one who has received grace and understood it begins to carry something of it — a gracious disposition in speech, attention to others, and the way they hold power without using it to extract. This is grace not as event but as settled character.

The human capacity to receive grace and to extend it is shaped by the experience of having received it. What a person has been shown shapes what they become able to show. But the originating source is not human character. It is divine character. Grace enters human experience from above and works its way outward from there. And in its most radical expression, even the desire to seek grace — the posture of earnest appeal itself — is something God gives, not something the human being generates independently.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The natural enemy of grace is transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on exchange, that what you receive is proportional to what you have earned. Where transaction governs, grace cannot operate. Its negation is not hostility but the closed logic of merit: you get what you deserve, no more. Wherever this logic dominates a person's inner life, it shuts down the capacity both to receive grace and to extend it.

What feeds grace in a person is primarily the experience of having received it undeservedly and knowing it. Supplication — earnest, vulnerability-based appeal — is itself a form of grace-awareness: it acknowledges that what is needed cannot be demanded, only asked for. That posture already recognises that grace is freely given. The body knows this: the person seeking grace prostrates themselves, raises their hands, fasts, weeps — the whole body is brought into alignment with what the soul is doing, enacting the inner posture of non-leverage before the one who can give or withhold. What diminishes grace is the loss of that recognition: self-sufficiency closes the space through which grace moves, as does contempt — placing others below the level where they deserve consideration.

What grace produces in the person who has it is a stability that is not earned — a standing in relationship that holds even when circumstances do not support it. This is not a passive or static condition; grace as a present standing is oriented toward a future, and the person who stands in it lives with a forward-directed hope. From that stability comes the capacity to extend grace outward: to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt. The one who has had their own debt cancelled becomes capable of cancelling another's — and this extension flows not from discipline but from a softened inner orientation, a tender-heartedness that is itself the work of grace in the person.

What grace produces in relationships is access and loyalty. The person who discovers unexpectedly that another is disposed toward them — that they have been seen by someone with no obligation to see them — experiences something that contractual relationship cannot produce. Grace does not originate in human character. The data consistently places the divine disposition as the source. Human grace is downstream of what has been received.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It is not triggered by the quality of the recipient. It arises entirely from within the one who grants it, from a settled inner orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

> **Ephesians 2:8** — *For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God.*
>
> This verse establishes the grammar of grace with maximum compression: grace precedes faith, not the reverse — "by grace... through faith" places grace as the sphere and faith as the channel. The explicit denial "not your own doing" rules out any reading of grace as a reward for faith. The characterisation as gift closes the circuit: a gift is not owed, not earned, and cannot be returned on equal terms. What the verse does that no prose summary can is settle three things in a single sentence: the agent (God), the means (faith), and the negation of the alternative (not your own doing). The grammar of grace — subject, means, and the ruled-out alternative — is encoded here definitively.

> **2 Corinthians 12:9** — *But he said to me, "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me.*
>
> The context is refusal — Paul's request for relief is denied, and grace is offered instead as the answer. This is the sharpest possible definition of grace as sufficiency within difficulty rather than escape from it. The pairing "my grace is sufficient / my power is made perfect in weakness" establishes a structural principle: grace is most fully operative where human resource fails. Crucially, grace here is spoken — God says "my grace is sufficient for you" — which makes it a relational address, not merely an impersonal provision. The experience of grace in this verse is the experience of being spoken to by the one who withholds relief: not abandonment, but a refusal that names what is actually being given.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in two distinct but related ways. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and finding, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you rather than against you.

> **Exodus 33:17** — *And the Lord said to Moses, "This very thing that you have spoken I will do, for you have found favor in my sight, and I know you by name."*
>
> The basis of Moses' favour is named precisely: not performance, not petition quality, but knowledge — "I know you by name." Prior personal knowledge is the entire ground of the relational standing. The formula "found favour in my sight" is the idiom of grace in Hebrew: the gaze of the greater party is the locus of the gift — to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention. The verse also shows that grace creates something specific and practical: access. The thing Moses asked for is granted. Favour is not merely a feeling in God; it produces a concrete outcome for the one who has been seen.

> **Luke 1:30** — *And the angel said to her, "Do not be afraid, Mary, for you have found favor with God."*
>
> The opening phrase — "do not be afraid" — names the instinctive human posture before unexpected divine attention: fear. Favour does not arrive as something naturally expected; it comes as interruption. The reorientation from fear to favour is the inner movement grace produces in its initial reception — the relational condition changes before any task is announced. Notably, the announcement that Mary has found favour precedes any statement of what she is being asked to do: the characteristic is given before the vocation. Grace creates before it commissions. This sequence — favour, then calling — is consistent across the data.

The second way grace appears is as a quality of character in the person who has been so formed. The one who has lived under grace begins to carry something of it — a gracious disposition in speech, in the quality of their attention to others, in the way they hold power lightly.

> **Proverbs 31:30** — *Charm is deceitful, and beauty is vain, but a woman who fears the Lord is to be praised.*
>
> This verse names the distinction between genuine grace and its performed imitation. Charm and beauty produce social goodwill — they achieve the effect of grace — but the verse calls them unstable and deceptive. The fear of the Lord is named as the ground of the character quality that genuinely generates lasting standing. This implies that gracious character is not a cultivated social skill; it grows from right orientation toward God. Grace as character quality shares its source with grace as divine gift: both are rooted in the same place. The Hebrew word for charm here is the same root as the word for favour — the verse uses the language of grace to describe what grace, when merely performed, becomes.

> **Ruth 2:10** — *Then she fell on her face, bowing to the ground, and said to him, "Why have I found favor in your eyes, that you should take notice of me, since I am a foreigner?"*
>
> Ruth's posture — prostrate, face to the ground — is the somatic expression of the inner posture that grace-reception requires: complete non-leverage, no basis for claim, full vulnerability. The body enacts what the soul is doing. Her question names genuine incomprehension — the experience of receiving what cannot be accounted for by anything in herself. The word "foreigner" identifies the structural reason she has no claim: she stands outside every circle of natural obligation. Grace in this scene moves precisely across the boundary of exclusion — the one with no standing receives standing. Prostration as the bodily form of grace-reception is consistent across the data: the body cannot remain upright when the soul has nothing to stand on.

The natural enemy of grace is transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on the basis of exchange. Grace by definition gives what has not been earned.

> **Romans 5:2** — *Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God.*
>
> The spatial language is load-bearing: grace is described as a sphere in which one stands — a completed entry with continuing effect. "Access" names the movement from outside to inside; faith is the means of entry, but what one enters is grace as a sustained condition, not a transaction that concludes. The connection to hope and the glory of God opens the forward dimension: what one stands in is not a static safety but an orientation toward a future. Grace as present standing and hope as its forward reach belong together as parts of a single inner posture — the characteristic holds the person now, and points them toward what is still to come.

> **1 Corinthians 15:10** — *But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me was not in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me.*
>
> This is the strongest statement in the corpus of grace as identity-constituting. The phrase "I am what I am" is not a neutral self-description; it is a claim about the ground of being — Paul's identity is grounded not in achievement but in reception. The verse holds in tension two things that a merit-based framework cannot hold simultaneously: Paul's genuine effort and the priority of grace. Grace does not produce passivity — it produces more effort, but reframed. The activity is named as the expression of grace rather than its cause. What has been given becomes the source from which the person acts, not the reward for acting well.

What grace produces in the person who has it is stability — a standing in relationship that holds. From that stability comes the capacity to extend grace outward — to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt.

> **Romans 8:32** — *He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?*
>
> The logic proceeds from the greater act to the lesser: the supreme giving (the Son) is the guarantee of all subsequent giving. The word translated "graciously give" is the verbal form of grace deployed as action — giving freely on the basis of grace alone, without condition or merit in the recipient. The rhetorical question makes the giver's internal consistency the ground of the recipient's security: the one who gave in the greater circumstance will give in the lesser. Security does not rest on the recipient's condition but on the coherence of the giver's character. Grace, once established as the mode of God's relationship with humanity, becomes the reliable orientation of all that follows.

> **Ephesians 4:32** — *Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you.*
>
> The verse structures grace explicitly as a circuit: "as God in Christ forgave you" is not illustrative but causal — the divine forgiveness is the ground of the human act. Grace received is supposed to become grace extended. The inner disposition from which this flows is described as kindness and tender-heartedness — this second word in the Greek names a quality located in the deep interior, the physical seat of compassion. Grace as humanly extended is not a willed performance; it is an inner-organ experience, a softening that has occurred at the core of the person. The somatic register of the word reminds us that forgiving another is not merely a decision but a condition of the inner life.

> **Acts 20:32** — *And now I commend you to God and to the word of his grace, which is able to build you up and to give you the inheritance among all those who are sanctified.*
>
> Grace here has been given a communicative form: the word of his grace. It can be spoken, transmitted, and built upon. The capacity of this word to "build you up" names grace as formative and constructive — it creates substance in those who receive it rather than merely informing them. The inheritance language opens the eschatological dimension: what grace is building toward is not merely present wellbeing but a future possession among a people who have been set apart. Grace commissions and grace constructs — it is not static gift but living power.

> **Luke 1:28** — *And he came to her and said, "Greetings, O favored one, the Lord is with you!"*
>
> The salutation "favored one" is a passive form of the grace verb — one who has been graced, and who remains in that condition. Mary is addressed not by name first but by this designation: her identity in the angel's address is constituted by the grace-act done to her. She is defined by what has been done to her, not by lineage, status, or achievement. The grammatical form encodes both the completeness of the act and its continuing state — she has been graced and remains so. The content of the grace is named immediately: "the Lord is with you." Divine presence is the gift. The simplest and most concentrated form of the grace dynamic: the greater comes to the lesser, names them as favoured, and the content of the favour is presence itself.

> **Daniel 9:3** — *Then I turned my face to the Lord God, seeking him by prayer and pleas for mercy with fasting and sackcloth and ashes.*
>
> The bodily enactment of supplication in this verse is total and deliberate: fasting, sackcloth, ashes, the face turned toward God. Each element removes something from the ordinary state of the body — food, normal clothing, cleanliness — and replaces it with reduction, coarseness, and directed orientation. The body is brought into complete alignment with what the soul is doing: acknowledging unworthiness and need. This is the most complete somatic expression of grace-seeking in the corpus — body and soul act together in the same direction. Turning the face toward God is itself a directional act: the body orienting itself toward the one who can give or withhold.

> **Zechariah 12:10** — *"And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy, so that, when they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child, and weep bitterly over him, as one weeps over a firstborn son."*
>
> This verse is eschatological in register and uniquely layered. It formally links grace and repentance in the same outpouring: both grace and the capacity to seek grace are poured out together — the response to grace is itself given as grace. This is the most radical form of the dynamic: even the capacity to seek and receive is a gift. The result is mourning — grief-saturated recognition of what has been done, described in some of the most intense lamentation language in the Old Testament. Grace here does not produce relief but a penetrating, grief-laden awareness. The body is fully involved: mourning, weeping bitterly, the sharpness of grief for a firstborn. This verse is the formal link in the corpus between grace, supplication, mourning, and repentance — all bound together in a single eschatological act of divine giving. Both the Hebrew word for grace and the word for earnest supplication anchor here — the two faces of the one grace dynamic appear in the same verse.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Grace in Scripture is carried by two root families: a Hebrew family centred on the root *chen* (favour, grace) and a Greek family built from *charis* (grace, favour, gift). Together they account for 13 terms in the research inventory, 5 of which are owner terms in this registry. The sharing ratio of 0.85 indicates that this word is highly connected — only 2 terms are unique to grace; 11 are shared with adjacent word studies. This degree of sharing signals that grace is not an isolated concept but one that operates in close relationship with several neighbouring characteristics, particularly those in the supplication, mercy, and covenant-love clusters.

**Greek: charis (G5485)**

The primary New Testament term is *charis* (khar-ees), the Greek word that gives English "charity" and anchors the NT's theology of grace. It appears 88 times in this registry's active verse set, with 241 total NT occurrences. Its range of English translations is exceptionally broad: grace, favour, gift, thanks, blessing, benefit, credit, kindness. This breadth is itself significant — *charis* names not just a disposition but the full arc from disposition through act to the response the act evokes. The Mounce definition moves through at least four distinct domains: the state of favour (relational posture), the gift (the concrete expression of that favour), the gracious commission (a task given on the basis of grace), and the emotional response of gratitude (grace acknowledged and returned as thanks). These are not separate senses so much as contextual inflections of a unified concept: every usage can be traced to the core idea of a relational orientation moving from a greater toward a lesser that produces a concrete benefit. The single word thus names both the origin and the completion of the grace dynamic.

Two phase 2 flags are raised on *charis*: RELATIONAL_DIRECTION, indicating that the term's meaning is inherently directed toward another — it cannot exist in isolation from a recipient — and SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH, noting that the term crosses four or more semantic domains and warrants careful attention in analysis. Both flags confirm what the data independently shows: *charis* is relational at its core and conceptually wide.

In classical Greek before the New Testament, *charis* carried the sense of that which gives pleasure or charm — the quality in a person or thing that produces delight in an observer (Homer onwards, 8th century BC). The Greek lexical tradition shows the word already carrying the dual sense of inner disposition and external effect before the NT writers appropriated it. What the NT adds is the theological weight of a God who holds this disposition toward humanity unilaterally and without obligation.

*Charis* has a documented somatic dimension: where grace is described as operating in the body, the connection is to the heart as the seat of its reception and the mouth as the organ of its expression — gracious words that carry the quality of grace itself.

**Greek: charizō (G5483)**

*Charizō* (khar-id-zo) is the verbal form of the grace family, occurring 24 times in the NT. It means to give grace, to bestow freely as a favour, to forgive or cancel a debt, and — in a darker usage — to hand over a person to the demands of others. The Mounce senses show the full range: giving sight freely, giving the Son for all, delivering a prisoner as a favour to accusers, and remitting debt. The same verb that names the supreme gift can also name the unjust surrender of an innocent person — grace as free giving is structurally neutral in its form; its moral character is determined entirely by the giver's inner disposition and the direction of the giving. This range matters: the structure of grace (free giving, without obligation) can be inhabited by both genuine goodwill and self-serving calculation.

The related word *acharistos* (G884, ungrateful) is *charizō*'s semantic shadow — the one who has received grace but returns no acknowledgment. Its presence in the word family names the characteristic's negation not as the absence of grace but as the failure to receive it rightly. The related word *charisma* (G5486, gift) names the particularisation of grace into a defined endowment or ability — grace expressed as a specific capacity given to a specific person for the benefit of others. The distinction between *charis* (the disposition and its general expression) and *charisma* (the specific gift form) is worth noting: one names the source, the other names a particular outflow.

**Greek: charitoō (G5487)**

*Charitoō* (khar-ee-to-o) appears only twice in the NT, both in Luke's infancy narrative. Its definition is: to give graciously, to show acts of kindness by freely giving; to make an object of favour. The passive form occurs both times — to be visited with free favour, to be graced — and this passive is a perfect tense in Luke 1:28, encoding not only the completed act but its continuing state: she has been graced and remains so. The word's rarity gives it weight: where it appears, it names the act of being constituted as an object of grace — not merely receiving a gift but being defined by having been graced. The word describes a transformation of identity, not just a transaction.

**Hebrew: chen (H2580)**

The primary Hebrew term is *chen* (khane), occurring 69 times in the OT. BDB distinguishes three senses: favour or grace (the most common), grace or elegance (an aesthetic quality), and favour in the sense of acceptance. The relational sense governs this registry. The dominant syntactic form is the idiom "find favour in the eyes/sight of" — a phrase that appears across Genesis, Ruth, Samuel, Esther, Ezra, and more. This formula makes the gaze of the greater party the locus of grace: to be in favour is to be within the field of another's favourable attention. The eyes of the Lord, the king, the master are the literal somatic location of the relational standing grace creates. This is why *chen* has a documented somatic dimension — the characteristic is constitutively tied to the act of being seen.

The aesthetic sense of *chen* — grace as beauty or elegance — appears in Proverbs 31:30 where it names charm that can be deceptive. This usage reveals that the Hebrew word family understood grace not only as a relational posture but as a quality that can be performed and imitated — which is precisely why the verse contrasts it with the fear of the Lord as its genuine, non-deceptive form.

The related word family around *chen* is extensive. Stripping out proper names (Hannah, Hanun, Hanan, Henadad — personal names built from this root, indicating that grace was a prized characteristic whose blessing parents wished on their children), the meaningful semantic vocabulary includes: *channun* (gracious, H2587), a character description applied overwhelmingly to God; *chinnam* (for nothing, freely, H2600), the adverbial form meaning without cause or without cost — making the economic logic of grace explicit; *chaninah* (favour, H2594); and *tahanun* / *techinnah* (supplication, H8469 / H8467), the words for earnest plea. The *chinnam* family makes the structure of grace transparent: it is given for nothing.

**Hebrew: tachanum (H8469)**

*Tachanum* (takh-an-oon) and its related form *techinnah* (H8467) name supplication — specifically, earnest, grace-seeking appeal. *Tachanum* occurs 18 times in the OT and carries two sub-senses: supplication directed toward God and supplication directed toward human authority. Both senses name the same inner posture: deep need, relational vulnerability, and the recognition that what is required cannot be demanded but only requested. The somatic dimension of these terms is marked: raised hands (Psalm 28:2), fasting, sackcloth, and ashes (Daniel 9:3), weeping (Jeremiah 3:21, 31:9), the face turned toward God (Daniel 9:17). The body does not remain neutral when the soul is in the posture of supplication.

Two phase 2 flags are raised on *tachanum*: ESCHATOLOGICAL_USAGE, noting that the word appears in eschatological and apocalyptic passages (Zechariah 12:10 is the primary instance), and RELATIONAL_DIRECTION, confirming the inherently directed, other-oriented nature of the term's meaning. The eschatological flag points to the most structurally significant finding in the Hebrew material: Zechariah 12:10 shows supplication appearing at the end-time not as something humanity finally produces but as something God pours out. The capacity for earnest appeal is itself a grace. This reversal — that the response is a gift as much as the grace being sought — is the deepest observation the vocabulary yields.

The root shared between *chen* (favour) and *tachanum* (supplication) is the same root. This is not coincidental. To seek grace rightly is already to be oriented in a direction that only makes sense if grace exists and if it is freely given. The vocabulary of grace and the vocabulary of grace-seeking share a root because the act of seeking is already shaped by the reality of what is sought.

**Synthesis observation**

The full vocabulary of grace reveals a semantic arch that moves from divine character through relational gift to human response and back. At one end stands the divine disposition — the inner orientation of goodwill that precedes all human action. At the other stands supplication — the human inner act that is both a recognition of need and a form of grace-awareness. Between them lies the rich middle territory: favour found, standing conferred, gifts given freely, debts cancelled, identity constituted. The vocabulary also reveals what grace is not: the adverbial form meaning "for nothing" makes explicit that grace operates outside the economy of merit; the word for ingratitude names the failure mode of the recipient. Together the vocabulary defines a relational territory in which one party holds all the initiative and the other holds all the need — and yet genuine relationship is precisely what this asymmetry creates. And at the eschatological horizon, even the capacity to seek grace is given — the response is as graced as the gift.

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Grace is among the most connected words in the programme. With a sharing ratio of 0.85 — 11 of its 13 terms shared with other word studies — it sits at the intersection of multiple relational and theological domains. Only 2 terms are unique to this registry. This degree of sharing does not indicate that grace lacks definition; it indicates that grace is a characteristic that cannot be understood in isolation. It is structurally embedded in the fabric of the biblical inner-life vocabulary.

---

**Mercy / Steadfast Love**

*Nature:* Formal and theological. The Hebrew root *chen* (favour) is closely associated with *chesed* (steadfast love, covenant loyalty), and the two words appear together frequently in the OT. While *chesed* is registered separately in this programme, the two words travel together and are often translated into the same English domain.

*Key question:* Does grace as *chen* name the disposition that *chesed* then expresses over time? Is *chen* the inner posture and *chesed* the sustained relational pattern that flows from it?

*Priority:* High

---

**Forgiveness**

*Nature:* Formal and linguistic. *Charizō* (G5483) carries "to forgive" and "to cancel a debt" among its primary senses. Ephesians 4:32 names forgiveness as the relational enactment of grace received. The connection is not inferential — forgiveness is one of the named expressions of the grace dynamic, formally embedded in the Greek term itself.

*Key question:* Is forgiveness a subset of grace or a distinct characteristic that requires grace as its precondition? Does the data support a hierarchical relationship?

*Priority:* High

---

**Repentance**

*Nature:* Theological. Zechariah 12:10 — an eschatological anchor verse — connects the pouring out of a spirit of grace and supplication directly to mourning and recognition of sin. Grace enables repentance; the capacity to turn appears as a gift, not a prior human achievement. Joel 2:13 reinforces the same sequence: the divine character of graciousness is invoked as the basis for the summons to return. Both verses move in the same direction — grace precedes and enables the turn rather than following it.

*Key question:* To what degree does the programme's repentance data support the sequence found in Zechariah — that grace precedes and enables the turn rather than following it?

*Priority:* High

---

**Hope**

*Nature:* Theological. Romans 5:2 places standing in grace and rejoicing in hope in the same sentence. Grace as the present condition and hope as its forward orientation appear as parts of a single inner posture. The eschatological dimension in Acts 20:32 (inheritance) reinforces this.

*Key question:* Is hope in the programme data consistently grounded in the grace-standing, or does hope appear independently of it?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Humility**

*Nature:* Inferential, with formal support. The posture of the grace-seeker in this registry — Ruth prostrate, Daniel in sackcloth, Moses as servant — is the posture of humility. The act of supplication structurally requires the inner disposition that humility names. The data implies a causal relationship but does not formally establish it.

*Key question:* Does the humility registry confirm that lowliness of self-assessment is a precondition for grace-reception, or does grace create humility as a consequence?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Peace**

*Nature:* Theological. NT epistolary greetings consistently pair "grace and peace" (*charis kai eirēnē*) as the characterisation of the believer's inner condition. The pairing is formulaic but not accidental — peace appears as what grace produces in the recipient. The programme's peace data may illuminate what the pairing implies about their relationship.

*Key question:* Does the peace registry's data support the reading that peace is grace received and settled?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Lament**

*Nature:* Formal and somatic. The weeping-supplication complex in the *tachanum* verse set — Jeremiah 3:21, 31:9; Zechariah 12:10; Esther 8:3; Daniel 9:3 — connects the grace-seeking posture directly to the lament tradition. Weeping, mourning, and earnest plea travel together consistently. The body's involvement in both grace-seeking and lament (tears, prostration, fasting) makes this a formally grounded connection.

*Key question:* When the programme processes lament vocabulary, does the somatic profile of lament align with the somatic profile of grace-seeking identified here?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Supplication / Prayer**

*Nature:* Formal. *Tachanum* (H8469) and *techinnah* (H8467) are classified in this registry as XREF terms whose ownership belongs to the supplication domain. Their presence here as cross-references confirms a formal documented connection — grace-seeking is the content of earnest prayer. The shared root between *chen* and *tachanum* makes this a linguistic as well as formal connection.

*Key question:* When the programme processes the supplication registry, what inner dispositions does it find structurally embedded in the act of prayer beyond the grace-seeking identified here?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Wisdom**

*Nature:* Linguistic. *Chen* (H2580) carries an aesthetic and conduct dimension — gracious speech, gracious manner — that overlaps with the wisdom vocabulary. Proverbs uses both *chen* and wisdom language to describe the person of good character whose conduct generates community standing.

*Key question:* Is the gracious person of Proverbs the same as the wise person, or does the data distinguish them?

*Priority:* Lower

---

**Identity Formation**

*Nature:* Theological. 1 Corinthians 15:10 — "by the grace of God I am what I am" — makes grace the constitutive ground of personal identity, not merely a gift received but the basis of being. This is the strongest statement in the corpus of grace as identity-shaping, and it connects to the programme's vocabulary of selfhood and vocation.

*Key question:* Does the programme's identity-formation vocabulary confirm grace as a structural precondition for selfhood, or is this a Pauline particularity?

*Priority:* Medium
