## Section 1 — The Characteristic

Compassion is the inner movement toward another person's pain. It is not a decision or an act of will, not primarily. It is a stirring — a being moved, from within, by what is suffered by another. The biblical vocabulary for compassion makes this visceral quality explicit: the Hebrew root at the heart of this word family is the same root as the word for womb (*racham*/*rechem*). This is not incidental. It names the quality of compassion as that of a mother for the child she has carried — tender, protective, instinctive, and arising from the body's most interior place. The etymological direction is unusual and worth noting: the abstract word (compassion, *rachamim*) is named *after* the concrete (womb, *rechem*), not the other way around. The somatic grounding is not a later metaphor; it is built into the word's formation.

Compassion always faces outward. It is not a state of self-absorption but an orientation toward the one who suffers. And it produces action. When the biblical texts show compassion — in God or in a person — something follows: mercy is given, healing occurs, judgment is withheld. The inner movement and its outward consequence are not separable.

The programme's own description of this registry captures it precisely: compassion is *feeling the pain of another from the inside — not observing suffering from a distance but being moved by it in the gut*. The womb-metaphor embedded in the Hebrew root marks it as something more primal than sentiment and more personal than policy. It is the response of one who cannot remain unmoved.

**Range of usage across the data**

The 58 context groups across this registry map a wide semantic territory, spanning two languages and two testaments. Reading through the group descriptions, five recognisable areas emerge.

*Visceral compassion* — the deep inward stirring toward one who suffers — is the core. This is expressed by the Greek *splanchnizō* (being moved in the bowels, gut-level compassion) and the Hebrew *ra.cham* family (compassion rooted in the womb). These groups describe both divine and human compassion as a physically-located inner movement.

*Covenantal mercy* — the steadfast, loyal love expressed in the *chesed* family — describes compassion as a settled character quality within covenantal relationship. This is compassion expressed not as a moment of feeling but as a disposition of the whole person. The *chesed* groups show compassion persisting through time, underlying all relationship with God and, by the prophets' demand, in relationship between persons.

*Comfort and consolation* — expressed through the *ni.chum* and *na.cham* family — name compassion as it comes to completion in restoration. The inner stirring reaches its goal in the comforting of grief.

*Fellow-suffering* — the Greek *sym-* compounds (*sumpatheō*, *sumpaschō*, *sumpathēs*) — name a specifically New Testament development: compassion as participation in another's experience. This is not sympathy from a safe distance but genuine entry into shared suffering, exemplified most sharply in the person of Jesus Christ.

*Gracious favour* — the *chan.nun*/*chen* cluster — describes the disposition of favour and grace as a form of compassion's expression, particularly God's. This overlaps substantially with the grace and mercy registries.

The groups that describe the *withholding* of compassion are equally significant. A substantial portion of the *chus* (to pity) corpus — fourteen of twenty-four verses — appears in judicial contexts where pity is explicitly prohibited: "your eye shall not pity." This is not a marginal observation. Compassion in Scripture is defined partly by where justice requires it to be suspended. The frequency of its prohibition establishes that compassion is the *default inner orientation* from which deliberate moral decision must sometimes deviate. Compassion that can never be withheld is indistinguishable from reflex sentiment.

**Dimensional character**

Across 22 dimension-index groups, the dominant dimension is *Relational Disposition* (16 of 22 groups). Three groups carry *Dependence / Creatureliness* (the human posture of appeal and petition before God), two carry *Divine-Human Correspondence* (the structural mirror between divine compassion and human response), one carries *Moral Character* (the settled human virtue of covenantal loyalty), and one carries *Emotion — Negative* (the pitiable condition).

## Section 2 — How It Works

**The movement-structure of compassion**

Compassion in the biblical texts operates as a movement with three phases: an inner stirring, a turn toward the sufferer, and an action that meets the need. The Hebrew and Greek both name primarily the first phase — the inner event — but the contexts consistently complete the arc. When Jesus is *splanchnizō* (moved in his gut) at the sight of the crowd, what follows is teaching and healing. When God's *rachamim* is stirred in Hosea 11, what follows is restoration. The inner movement is the spring of the action. Luke 10:33-37 makes this sequence explicit: the Samaritan is *splanchnizō* (inner visceral movement), and the result is *eleos* (mercy enacted in care) — the compassion generates the mercy.

This means compassion in Scripture is not merely an emotional state. It is a motivational structure. What is felt generates what is done.

**The deliberative quality of divine compassion**

One of the most significant findings in the verse corpus is Hosea 11:8: "How can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel?... My heart recoils within me; my compassion (*ni.chum*) grows warm and tender." The verse opens with four rhetorical questions that record God's inner deliberation between judgment and compassion. The *ni.chum* is not presented as the suppression of judgment but as the winning of an inner debate. The compassion *contends with* and *overcomes* the competing impulse. The inner life of God, as the text presents it, is not static but deliberative. And Isa 54:8 settles the question of which impulse is more fundamental: "In overflowing anger for a moment I hid my face from you, but with everlasting love (*chesed*) I will have compassion on you." Judgment is temporary; compassion is everlasting.

**Directionality: God toward humanity, humanity toward each other**

The divine dimension is primary. The core *racham* family and the intensive *splanchnon* vocabulary are used predominantly of God, and of Jesus in his earthly ministry. The divine compassion is described in terms of its freedom (God has compassion on whom he wills, Rom 9:15), its depth (the *polusplanchnos* of Jas 5:11 — *very* compassionate), and its grounding in God's own character (Exod 34:6; Ps 103:8). God withdraws compassion in judgment; he demands it from Israel; and his own compassionate character mirrors back to the person who brings compassion to him (2 Sam 22:26).

Human compassion is derived. The prophets demand it from Israel because they have experienced it from God. The New Testament calls believers to clothe themselves in *oiktirmos* (compassion, Col 3:12). The *sym-* compounds (fellow-suffering, sympathy) describe the Christian community's life together, and the incarnation establishes the theological ground for human compassion: Christ sympathises with our weaknesses because he has himself lived in the conditions of human weakness (Heb 4:15). Compassion, in its fullest form, requires shared experience of suffering.

**The womb-metaphor and its implications**

The RACHAM root binds compassion to the womb at a lexical level that most translation conceals. The word for womb (*rechem*, H7358) and the word for compassion (*rachamim*) are from the same root. The connection is not secondary metaphor but primary etymology. Isaiah 46:3 deploys this explicitly: *borne by me from before your birth, carried from the womb*. God presents himself as the one who has carried Israel from the womb — the intimate, bodily sustaining of a mother for the child she forms and bears. The compassion-vocabulary's root is not an arbitrary linguistic connection but a living image: God's compassion toward Israel is womb-love, preceding Israel's capacity to know or respond, of the body's most interior origin, implying sustained holding rather than momentary feeling.

The Greek *splanchnizō* carries the same somatic logic: the word means bowels or intestines — the body's interior visceral organs. When Jesus is *splanchnizō*, the text reports not only that he was moved emotionally, but that the movement was located in the body's deepest interior. The three terms that most vividly name compassion — *ni.chum* (the warm stirring of the heart), *splanchnizō* (the gut-movement), *ra.cham* (the womb-tenderness) — all share this structure: compassion as a physically-located inner movement from the body's interior spaces outward to action. This sub-pattern spans the divine-human boundary: both God and humans are described as *moved within* by compassion using the same body-language.

**Chesed: when compassion becomes character**

The *chesed* vocabulary introduces a different mode of compassion. Here it is not a moment of inner movement but a settled disposition of loyal love — a character quality that persists through time and undergirds all of God's dealings with his people. The three groups from the *chesed* term show this across a range: divine chesed as the ground of all covenant relationship (Exod 34:6; Lam 3:22), human chesed as the requirement of faithful relationship (Hos 6:6; Mic 6:8), and the human posture of appeal to and meditation on divine chesed as a sustaining inner orientation (Ps 25:7; 48:9).

The *chesed* vocabulary also reveals the eschatological horizon: where chesed fails, what remains is not neutral absence but its opposite pole — shame/disgrace. The same root (CHASAD) yields both *be kind* and *shame*, reflecting the way covenantal faithfulness, when violated, becomes its opposite.

**Compassion and suffering**

The New Testament introduces a dimension of compassion that the Old Testament vocabulary does not develop at the same depth: compassion as *participation in suffering*. The *sym-* compounds name this — not sympathy at a distance but genuine entry into the other's experience. And Rom 8:17 carries this to its eschatological conclusion: "if we suffer with him, we may also be glorified with him." Fellow-suffering with Christ is the condition of glorification. Compassion, in its fullest NT expression, is not only the response to another's need but the path through shared suffering toward shared glory. The God who names himself *polusplanchnos* — deeply, intensely compassionate — declares this over the backdrop of Job's suffering, holding the tension between deep compassion and permitted suffering without resolving it.

**Compassion's limits: the judicial suspension**

Not all compassion texts in this registry describe compassion expressed. A significant cluster names its deliberate withdrawal. The judicial withholding of *chus* in the law and prophets is not a failure of compassion but its structural complement — it names the moral seriousness of the inner disposition. Seven times in Ezekiel, God declares in the first person that his eye will not spare and will not pity. The repetition underscores that real compassion is being suspended — it is felt, weighty, and deliberately overridden by justice.

**H7356A** | *ra.cham* | womb | Group 729-001

*Isa 46:3 — "Listen to me, O house of Jacob, all the remnant of the house of Israel, who have been borne by me from before your birth, carried from the womb."*

This verse deploys *ra.cham* (womb) as the image through which divine carrying and sustaining are expressed. God presents himself as the one who has carried Israel from the womb — the intimate, bodily sustaining of a mother for the child she forms and bears. The etymological grounding of the compassion vocabulary in the womb is not a later metaphor but a living image in prophetic speech: God's compassion toward Israel is womb-love that preceded Israel's capacity to know or respond. The sustained holding it implies is more fundamental than momentary feeling.

**G4697** | *splanchnizō* | to pity (be moved in the gut) | Group 730-001

*Luke 7:13 — "And when the Lord saw her, he had compassion on her and said to her, 'Do not weep.'"*

The widow of Nain. *Splanchnizō* names what happens in Jesus when he sees her grief — the visceral inward movement that is his compassion. What follows immediately is a word to the grieving mother, and then the raising of her son. The sequence instructs: the inner stirring is the source of the act. Jesus speaks first to the widow before demonstrating power over death — the compassion goes to the person before it expresses itself in miracle.

*Matt 9:36 — "When he saw the crowds, he had compassion for them, because they were harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd."*

Here *splanchnizō* is directed not at a single person's grief but at the condition of a multitude. The simile is diagnostic: the suffering is structural and spiritual, not merely circumstantial. Jesus' compassion sees beneath surface behaviour to underlying destitution. The result is not healing but teaching and the calling of the twelve. *Splanchnizō* spans four semantic domains in its twelve Gospel occurrences — physical suffering, spiritual destitution, hunger, relational need. The unifying element across all four is not the type of need but the visceral, inward, movement-producing quality of the response.

**G3627** | *oikteirō* | to have compassion | Group 731-001

*Rom 9:15 — "For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.'"*

Paul quotes Exod 33:19. The doubled structure of the saying emphasises sovereignty: God's compassion is not obligated or earned — it flows from his own free determination. The verb *oikteirō* carries the sense of deep inner movement. The point is that this deep inner movement belongs to God's freedom. No external ground compels it. Compassion cannot be claimed or manipulated; it can only be received. This verse is among the clearest statements in the programme data that compassion, at its deepest level, is a divine prerogative.

**H5150** | *ni.chum* | comfort (compassion-as-comfort) | Group 733-001

*Hos 11:8 — "How can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel?… My heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender."*

One of the most humanly-expressed statements of divine inner life in all of Scripture. God is depicted in internal conflict: the demand of judgment against his own inability to hand over the people he loves. The word *ni.chum* names the compassionate warmth that wins out — grows warm and tender in the literal sense. The four rhetorical questions record a deliberation; the *ni.chum* is not the suppression of judgment but its defeat in an inner contest. This verse establishes that divine compassion is not a static attribute but a dynamic, felt, deliberative reality — the kind of compassion that contends and overcomes.

*Isa 57:18 — "I have seen his ways, but I will heal him; I will lead him and restore comfort to him and his mourners."*

The *ni.chum* here is the concrete form compassion takes when it reaches its goal — restoration, comfort, healing. God sees the straying (morally problematic) yet moves toward restoration. Compassion looks not at what the person deserves but at what they need.

**G4834** | *sumpatheō* | to sympathize (suffer-with) | Group 734-001

*Heb 4:15 — "For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin."*

The Christological anchor of the compassion vocabulary. *Sumpatheō* — to feel with, to suffer alongside — is attributed to the ascended and glorified Christ as an ongoing capacity grounded in his incarnate experience. He *can* sympathise with human weakness because he has himself lived in the conditions of human weakness. The capacity for compassion arises from shared experience. This verse establishes a principle that runs through the whole sympathy vocabulary: genuine fellow-feeling requires shared condition. The community's compassion (1 Pet 3:8; 1 Cor 12:26) participates in the same principle at the human level.

Compassion as community virtue, listed alongside unity of mind, love, tenderness, and humility. The adjective *sumpathēs* names a settled character disposition — the person who is *sumpathēs* is consistently responsive to others' suffering. Its placement in a cluster of community virtues locates sympathy as one thread in the fabric of the new humanity, inseparable from love and humility.

**G4841** | *sumpaschō* | to suffer with | Group 3986-001

*1 Cor 12:26 — "If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together."*

The mutual fellow-suffering of the body of Christ. *Sumpaschō* here is not a call to compassion — it is a description of how the body of Christ actually functions. When one suffers, the whole body suffers. This is participation, not sympathy at a distance — the capacity to suffer-with is constituted by union with the body, not achieved by individual virtue. A second occurrence worth noting: Rom 8:17 extends this further — "if we suffer with him (*sumpaschō*), we may also be glorified with him." Fellow-suffering with Christ carries an eschatological horizon: the path through shared suffering leads to shared glory.

**G3356** | *metriopatheō* | be gentle (moderate one's feeling) | Group 3988-001

*Heb 5:2 — "He can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward, since he himself is beset with weakness."*

The high priest's capacity for gentle dealing is grounded in his own shared vulnerability. *Metriopatheō* describes moderation of emotional response — a controlled, measured engagement with the weak that avoids both harshness and sentimental indulgence. The Stoic philosophical term for *metriopatheia* names the ideal of moderating the passions. The Hebrews passage gives this concept a new ground: the moderation arises not from philosophical self-mastery but from shared creaturely weakness. This is compassion shaped by wisdom — calibrated, appropriate, arising from shared condition rather than from emotional distance.

**G4184** | *polusplanchnos* | very compassionate | Group 4011-001

*Jas 5:11 — "Behold, we consider those blessed who remained steadfast. You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful."*

The compound *polusplanchnos* — *full of splanchnon*, intensely compassionate — appears only here in the NT. Its context is suffering and endurance: Job's patience is cited as the backdrop against which the Lord's *polusplanchnos* is announced. This is theologically dense: God's deep compassion is revealed precisely through suffering he permits. The verse does not resolve the tension between divine permission of suffering and divine compassion; it holds them together and names the compassion as *very* deep. The hapax compound signals that ordinary vocabulary was insufficient — the writer coined an intensification for the moment when God's compassion needed to be named beyond its normal range.

**H2587** | *chan.nun* | gracious | Group 2330-001

*Exo 34:6 — "The Lord passed before him and proclaimed, 'The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness.'"*

The foundational self-description of God in the Old Testament. *Chan.nun* (gracious) appears alongside *rachum* (compassionate), *slow to anger*, *chesed* (steadfast love), and *emet* (faithfulness). The graciousness named here is not a temporary disposition toward the deserving but a settled character attribute. This formula is cited, echoed, or alluded to more than any other Old Testament passage about God's inner character — it appears in the compassion, grace, mercy, patience, and faithfulness registries. *Psa 103:8 — "The Lord is merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love."*

A direct echo of Exod 34:6 in the Psalter. The formula becomes a pastoral resource: Israel returns to it in praise, using God's character as the ground of personal assurance.

**H2617B** | *che.sed (shame)* | Groups 1633-001, 1633-002, 1633-003

*Exo 34:6* — see above (shared anchor with H2587).

*Lam 3:22 — "The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end."*

In the context of Lamentations — the most sustained expression of grief in the canon — the declaration of unfailing *chesed* functions as the programme's clearest example of compassion persisting when all circumstantial evidence argues against it. The word *mercies* here (*rachamim*) pairs directly with *chesed*: steadfast love and tender compassion together define what keeps God's people alive when the city is destroyed. This is not optimistic sentiment but defiant confession in the face of catastrophe.

*Hos 6:6 — "For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings."*

The prophetic demand that covenantal loyalty (*chesed*) matters more to God than ritual performance. What God requires is the inner disposition of loyal love, not external acts of religion performed without it.

*Mic 6:8 — "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"*

The triad — justice, loving-kindness, humble walk — defines the character of the person who lives in right relationship with God. *Chesed* is here an object of love: one does not merely practise it; one loves it. This makes compassionate loyalty a characteristic that orients the inner life, not only a behaviour to be performed.

*Psa 25:7 — "Remember not the sins of my youth or my transgressions; according to your steadfast love remember me, for the sake of your goodness, O Lord."*

The petitioner grounds the appeal in God's *chesed* — divine compassion as the basis of prayer.

*Psa 48:9 — "We have thought on your steadfast love, O God, in the midst of your temple."*

Meditation on divine *chesed* as a sustained inner practice — God's character becoming the content of one's thinking in worship.

**H7362** | *ra.cha.ma.ni* | compassionate | Group 1616-001

*Lam 4:10 — "The hands of compassionate women have boiled their own children; they became their food during the destruction of the daughter of my people."*

The darkest use of the compassion vocabulary in Scripture. **H2347** | *chus* | to pity | Groups 3182-001 and 3182-002

*Jon 4:11 — "And should not I pity Nineveh, that great city, in which there are more than 120,000 persons who do not know their right hand from their left, and also much cattle?"*

God's rhetorical question to Jonah. The divine pity is grounded in the simple fact of creaturely need — their number and their ignorance, including the cattle. This is compassion grounded in creatureliness, not only in moral renewal.

*Psa 72:13 — "He has pity on the weak and the needy, and saves the lives of the needy."*

Royal *chus* as a political virtue: the ideal king's pity toward the vulnerable is inseparable from the act of saving their lives. Inner disposition and practical outcome are once again inseparable.

*Deu 19:13 — "Your eye shall not pity him, but you shall purge the guilt of innocent blood from Israel, so that it may be well with you."*

The judicial withdrawal of *chus*. The law explicitly prohibits compassion toward the guilty perpetrator — not because it would be understandable but because it would compromise justice and leave innocent blood unavenged.

*Eze 7:4 — "And my eye will not spare you, nor will I have pity, but I will punish you for your ways, while your abominations are in your midst."*

Divine judicial withdrawal of pity. This formula appears seven times in Ezekiel, each in God's first person. The repetition underscores that real compassion — felt, weighty — is being deliberately suspended. The moral seriousness of the suspension establishes the authenticity of the compassion that is withheld.

**G1654** | *eleēmosunē* | charity/almsgiving | Group 3167-001

*Luke 11:41 — "But give as alms those things that are within, and behold, everything is clean for you."*

*Ta entos* — the things within, the inner things — given as alms make everything clean. Compassion made visible in giving accomplishes inward cleansing that ritual washing cannot. The connection between inner quality and outward act is explicit: the purity of the alms-act depends on its origin in genuine inner mercy.

*Matt 6:2 — "Thus, when you give to the needy, sound no trumpet before you, as the hypocrites do…"*

Almsgiving can become its own corruption. The Sermon on the Mount's concern is that the inner quality of compassion is what gives the outward act its validity. The word *eleēmosunē* traces a semantic development from classical Greek *pity/mercy* to LXX/NT *almsgiving* — compassion institutionalised as its normative social form. Second Temple Judaism and early Christianity understood the inner disposition of mercy to have a normative outward form: compassion that does not give alms has not fully arrived.

**G1652** | *eleeinos* | pitiful | Group 3168-001

*Rev 3:17 — "For you say, 'I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing,' not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked."*

The Laodicean church. The *eleeinos* (pitiable condition) is masked by material prosperity — the church cannot receive the compassion it needs because it cannot perceive its own need. This is the reverse of the compassion movement: not compassion failing to arise in the one who sees need, but the need-recognising capacity failing in the one who most requires help. ## Section 4 — The Vocabulary

The vocabulary of compassion in Scripture divides into four distinct root families, with a fifth important cluster centred on solidarity and fellow-feeling. Together they cover a semantic range from raw physical movement to settled covenantal character to eschatological participation.

**The RACHAM family — Hebrew**

The foundational root is *racham*/*rechem* — womb. The etymological direction is unusual: the abstract word (compassion, *rachamim*) is named after the concrete (womb, *rechem*), not vice versa. This means the somatic grounding is constitutive of the word's meaning from its formation — not a metaphor added later. From this root come H7356A *ra.cham* (womb/compassion-ground, 6 occurrences), H7358 *re.chem* (womb, anatomical, 26 occurrences), H7362 *ra.cha.ma.ni* (compassionate, 1 occurrence), and the plural form *rachamim* (compassions/tender mercies — the primary compassion noun, owned by the mercy registry, Reg 111). The root is shared with the love registry (Reg 103), establishing a linguistic family that connects love, compassion, and womb-tenderness in a single semantic cluster.

*H7356A ra.cham* (womb): documented as connecting compassion to its physical root — womb-love. *H7358 re.chem*: the anatomical root of the compassion vocabulary. Its 26 occurrences include divine sovereignty over conception and birth (Gen 20:18; 29:31; 30:22) — language that connects the compassion vocabulary to God's creative and providential work.

**The SPLANCHN family — Greek**

The New Testament's primary compassion vocabulary centres on G4697 *splanchnizō* (to be moved in the gut, to pity) and its sibling G4698 *splanchnon* (bowels/entrails, the physical seat of compassion). The verb *splanchnizō* appears twelve times in the Gospels, used almost exclusively of Jesus. The four semantic domains it spans — physical suffering and healing, spiritual destitution, physical hunger, relational and moral need — are unified not by the type of need but by the visceral, inward, movement-producing quality of the response. In the one unambiguous human use (Luke 10:33, the Good Samaritan), compassion is the defining quality of the person who correctly answers "who is my neighbour?" G4184 *polusplanchnos* (very compassionate) intensifies this root — coined as a hapax for the statement that God is deeply, overwhelmingly compassionate (Jas 5:11). The related-words list for these terms shows the internal coherence of the family: pity, compassion, affection, entrails — a vocabulary entirely referenced to itself.

**The CHESED family — Hebrew**

H2617A *che.sed* (kindness/steadfast love — owned by the kindness registry, Reg 99) and its siblings H2616A *cha.sad* (be kind, 2 occurrences), H2617B *che.sed* (shame — the negative semantic pole, 3 occurrences) form the covenantal loyalty cluster. The *chesed* vocabulary describes compassion not as a felt movement but as a settled disposition of loyal relationship. Remarkably, the same root CHASAD yields both *be kind* and *shame/disgrace* — the positive quality and its violation. The co-presence of kindness and disgrace in a single root family signals that covenantal faithfulness, when violated, does not produce neutral absence but its opposite.

H2587 *chan.nun* (gracious, 13 occurrences) describes the *settled character disposition* of graciousness — the inner quality of being inclined toward favour. Its status note identifies it as guilt-resolution vocabulary: it is the response to guilt, positioning graciousness as the character quality that specifically addresses human failure. Its related words include beauty, favour, supplication — the semantic cluster of grace extended toward those who need it.

**The PATHĒ family — Greek**

A distinct Greek root family centres on *pathos* (experience, suffering). The active compassion terms are G4834 *sumpatheō* (to sympathise, 2 occurrences), G4835 *sumpathēs* (sympathetic, 1 occurrence), G4841 *sumpaschō* (to suffer with, 3 occurrences), G3356 *metriopatheō* (to deal gently, 1 occurrence). These terms carry the *syn-* (with/together) prefix naming fellow-suffering: suffering-*with* rather than suffering-*at*. The LSJ entry for *sumpatheō* notes its classical philosophical use describing the sympathy between soul and body — the NT reappropriates this concept for the community of faith and for Christology.

*Metriopatheō* (moderate one's feeling, G3356) draws from Stoic *metriopatheia* — the ideal of emotional moderation. The Hebrews passage gives this a new scriptural ground: the moderation arises from shared creaturely weakness rather than philosophical self-mastery, marking a significant divergence from the Stoic model.

**The ELEEIN/CHUS family — Greek and Hebrew**

G1652 *eleeinos* (pitiful, 2 occurrences) and G1654 *eleēmosunē* (charity/almsgiving, 36 occurrences) are the outer members of the mercy vocabulary family (G1653 *eleeō* and G1656 *eleos* are owned by the mercy registry, Reg 111). *Eleēmosunē* traces a semantic development from classical Greek *pity/mercy* to LXX/NT *charitable giving* — compassion institutionalised as its normative social form. The word carries the transition from inner state to outward practice built into its meaning.

H2347 *chus* (to pity, 24 occurrences) describes the *impulse* of pity — the instinctive sparing of the other. Its most striking characteristic is statistical: fourteen of twenty-four occurrences appear in prohibition contexts ("your eye shall not pity"). The term is most frequently encountered in the context of its judicial suspension. This establishes that *chus* names the *default orientation* of the inner person toward those in need — compassion is the natural state that deliberate moral decision must sometimes override.

**Synthesis observation**

The full vocabulary of compassion in Scripture moves across a spectrum from the somatic to the characterological to the eschatological. At one end, *ra.cham/re.chem* and *splanchnizō* locate compassion in the body's deepest interior — womb and bowels — as a physical resonance with another's suffering. At the other end, *chesed* and *chan.nun* describe compassion as a settled character quality, a disposition that persists through time, requiring no triggering event, shaping the whole orientation of a person toward relationship. Between these two poles, the *sym-* compounds name compassion as participation — genuine entry into shared experience, made possible by shared vulnerability. And *sumpaschō* in Rom 8:17 extends this into an eschatological frame: suffering-with-Christ as the path to glorification-with-Christ. The vocabulary as a whole rejects a purely sentimental understanding of compassion. It is at once more bodily (than sentiment), more structural (than emotion), and more costly (than sympathy from a distance).
