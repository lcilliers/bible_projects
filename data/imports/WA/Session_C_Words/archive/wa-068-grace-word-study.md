# Grace — Word Characteristic Study
## Framework B Soul Word Analysis Programme
### Registry 068 | Session C v1 | 2026-04-09

---

## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It does not arise from the quality of the recipient. It arises from within the giver, from a settled orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in two ways. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and discovering, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you. This is grace as relational position. It creates something real: access, protection, standing, the sense of being known and held. In its deepest biblical expression this is what a human being discovers in relationship with God — not mere tolerance, but active, personal goodwill that precedes any action on the recipient's part. Moses finds that he is known by name; that prior knowledge is the entire basis of his standing.

The second way grace appears is as a quality of character in the person formed by it. The one who has received grace and understood it begins to carry something of it — a gracious disposition in speech, attention to others, and the way they hold power without using it to extract. This is grace not as event but as settled character.

The human capacity to receive grace and to extend it is shaped by the experience of having received it. What a person has been shown shapes what they become able to show. But the originating source is not human character. It is divine character. Grace enters human experience from above and works its way outward from there.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The natural enemy of grace is transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on exchange, that what you receive is proportional to what you have earned. Where transaction governs, grace cannot operate. Its negation is not hostility but the closed logic of merit: you get what you deserve, no more. Wherever this logic dominates a person's inner life, it shuts down the capacity both to receive grace and to extend it.

What feeds grace in a person is primarily the experience of having received it undeservedly and knowing it. Supplication — earnest, vulnerability-based appeal — is itself a form of grace-awareness: it acknowledges that what is needed cannot be demanded, only asked for. That posture already recognises that grace is freely given. What diminishes grace is the loss of that recognition: self-sufficiency closes the space through which grace moves, as does contempt — placing others below the level where they deserve consideration.

What grace produces in the person who has it is a stability that is not earned — a standing in relationship that holds even when circumstances do not support it. From that stability comes the capacity to extend grace outward: to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt. The one who has had their own debt cancelled becomes capable of cancelling another's.

What grace produces in relationships is access and loyalty. Ruth prostrate before Boaz — astonished to be noticed — names the effect: grace creates the experience of being seen by someone with no obligation to see you. That experience generates a loyalty that contractual relationship cannot produce.

Grace does not originate in human character. The data consistently places the divine disposition as the source. Human grace is downstream of what has been received.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

Grace is the disposition of a greater toward a lesser — the free, uncompelled choice of one who has power to give or withhold, who gives. It is not a response to merit. It is not triggered by the quality of the recipient. It arises entirely from within the one who grants it, from a settled inner orientation of goodwill that moves outward into action.

> **Ephesians 2:8** — *For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God.*
>
> This verse makes the defining structural claim about grace with maximum compression: the mechanism of salvation is grace; grace is not self-generated ("not your own doing"); and it is characterised as gift. The sequence matters — grace precedes faith, not the reverse. The verse rules out any reading of grace as a reward for faith. Faith is the channel, not the basis. What the verse establishes is a one-directional flow: the initiative lies entirely with the giver.

> **2 Corinthians 12:9** — *But he said to me, "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." Therefore I will boast all the more gladly of my weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon me.*
>
> Here grace appears not as positional gift but as active, sustaining sufficiency. The context is Paul's request for a thorn to be removed — a request refused. The refusal reframes grace: it is not the removal of difficulty but the provision within it. This distinguishes grace from mere relief. It is not simply rescue from circumstances but the supply of what is needed inside them. The pairing with power and weakness is significant — grace operates precisely where human resource is exhausted, which implies that the experience of insufficiency is one of the conditions in which grace becomes most visible.

As a characteristic of the human inner life, grace shows up in two distinct but related ways. The first is as something received: the experience of being the object of another's favour — standing before someone who has authority over your life and finding, unexpectedly, that they are disposed toward you rather than against you.

> **Exodus 33:17** — *And the Lord said to Moses, "This very thing that you have spoken I will do, for you have found favor in my sight, and I know you by name."*
>
> The language here is relational and personal rather than transactional or legal. The basis of Moses' favour is not performance but knowledge — "I know you by name." Favour in this verse creates something specific: access (the thing Moses asked for is granted) and a named relational standing that is prior to any act of merit. The verse also shows grace as something that can be sought and found — Moses asked, and the asking was appropriate. This is not grace as impersonal force but as the responsive goodwill of a person toward a person.

> **Luke 1:30** — *And the angel said to her, "Do not be afraid, Mary, for you have found favor with God."*
>
> The opening phrase — "do not be afraid" — names the instinctive human posture before unexpected divine attention: fear. Favour does not come naturally to consciousness as something to expect; it arrives as interruption. The announcement that Mary has found favour precedes any statement of what she is being asked to do. The characteristic is given before the vocation. This sequence — favour, then calling — is consistent with the larger pattern in the data: grace creates before it commissions.

The second way grace appears is as a quality of character in the person who has been so formed. The one who has lived under grace begins to carry something of it — a gracious disposition in speech, in the quality of their attention to others, in the way they hold power lightly.

> **Proverbs 31:30** — *Charm is deceitful, and beauty is vain, but a woman who fears the Lord is to be praised.*
>
> This verse sits within the data's group describing favour as inner character quality — grace expressed in conduct and wisdom. The contrast is instructive: charm and beauty are the outward, performed versions of grace's effect — they produce social goodwill but they are unstable and deceptive. The fear of the Lord is named as the ground of the character quality that is genuinely praiseworthy. This implies that the kind of gracious disposition that generates lasting standing in community is not cultivated independently but is rooted in right orientation toward God. Grace as character quality is connected to the same source as grace as divine gift.

> **Ruth 2:10** — *Then she fell on her face, bowing to the ground, and said to him, "Why have I found favor in your eyes, that you should take notice of me, since I am a foreigner?"*
>
> Ruth's posture — prostrate, face to the ground — is the somatic expression of the inner disposition that grace requires in its recipient: complete vulnerability, no leverage, no claim. Her question ("why have I found favour?") is not rhetorical; it names genuine incomprehension, the experience of receiving what cannot be accounted for by anything in herself. The word "foreigner" identifies her lack of any natural claim. Grace in this exchange moves across a boundary of exclusion — the one with no standing receives standing. The physical posture of prostration expresses what grace does to the recipient: it undoes all self-constructed position.

The natural enemy of grace is transaction — the assumption that relationship operates on the basis of exchange. Grace by definition gives what has not been earned.

> **Romans 5:2** — *Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God.*
>
> The spatial language here is significant: grace is described as a sphere in which one stands rather than a moment one experiences. "Access" names the movement from outside to inside — faith is the means of entry, but what one enters is grace as a sustained condition, not a transaction that concludes. The connection to hope and the glory of God opens the eschatological dimension: standing in grace is not merely a present position but an orientation toward a future. This forward-looking quality distinguishes the grace condition from mere safety or rescue; it is a posture of life directed toward something.

> **1 Corinthians 15:10** — *But by the grace of God I am what I am, and his grace toward me was not in vain. On the contrary, I worked harder than any of them, though it was not I, but the grace of God that is with me.*
>
> This verse holds in tension two things that a merit-based framework cannot hold simultaneously: Paul's own effort and the priority of grace. Grace does not produce passivity — it produces more activity, not less. But the activity is reframed as the expression of grace rather than its cause. The phrase "I am what I am" is a statement of identity grounded not in achievement but in reception. This is grace not merely as something done to Paul but as something that has shaped what Paul has become.

What grace produces in the person who has it is stability — a standing in relationship that holds. From that stability comes the capacity to extend grace outward — to forgive, to give freely, to release others from debt.

> **Romans 8:32** — *He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things?*
>
> The logic is explicitly from greater to lesser: the supreme act of giving (the Son) is the guarantee of all subsequent giving. The word translated "graciously give" is *charizō* — grace deployed as verb, as the act of giving on the basis of grace alone. The rhetorical question makes this a matter of inner consistency in the giver: the one who gave the greater will give the lesser. Grace, once established as the mode of God's relationship with humanity, becomes the reliable orientation of all subsequent giving. The verse grounds the security of the recipient not in their own state but in the internal consistency of the giver's character.

> **Ephesians 4:32** — *Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you.*
>
> Here the flow of grace is made explicit as a circuit. The divine forgiveness is not only the model but the ground of the human act — "as God in Christ forgave you" is not merely illustrative but causal. Grace received is supposed to become grace extended. The terms "kind" and "tenderhearted" describe the inner disposition from which forgiveness flows — not a grudging release but a softened orientation toward the other. The somatic resonance of "tenderhearted" is notable: the inner quality of grace is located in the heart, the seat of will and affection, suggesting that grace as extended to others is not merely behavioural but is a matter of inner transformation.

> **Acts 20:32** — *And now I commend you to God and to the word of his grace, which is able to build you up and to give you the inheritance among all those who are sanctified.*
>
> Grace here has a communicative form: "the word of his grace." It is not only an inner state or relational posture but something that can be spoken, transmitted, and built upon. The capacity of this word to "build up" points to grace as a constructive, formative power — it creates substance in those who receive it. The inheritance language connects grace again to eschatology: what grace is building toward is not merely current wellbeing but a future possession among a people who have been set apart.

> **Luke 1:28** — *And he came to her and said, "Greetings, O favored one, the Lord is with you!"*
>
> The salutation "favored one" — the passive participle of *charitoō*, to be made the object of grace — is Mary's identity as the angel names it. She is defined not by lineage, status, or achievement, but by what has been done to her: she has been graced. The phrase "the Lord is with you" is the content of that grace — divine presence as the gift. This is the simplest and most concentrated form of the grace dynamic: the greater comes to the lesser, names them as favoured, and the content of the favour is presence itself.

> **Daniel 9:3** — *Then I turned my face to the Lord God, seeking him by prayer and pleas for mercy with fasting and sackcloth and ashes.*
>
> Daniel's supplication in this verse is inseparable from its physical expression: fasting, sackcloth, and ashes are the bodily enactment of the inner posture of the suppliant. The body is brought into alignment with what the soul is doing — the external reduction of physical comfort and beauty mirrors the inner acknowledgment of unworthiness and need. "Pleas for mercy" translates *tachanunim*, the word for earnest, vulnerability-grounded supplication. Turning the face toward God is itself a directional act — the body orienting itself in the direction of the one who can grant or withhold. Grace is approached through the body as much as through the mind.

> **Zechariah 12:10** — *"And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy, so that, when they look on me, on him whom they have pierced, they shall mourn for him, as one mourns for an only child, and weep bitterly over him, as one weeps over a firstborn son."*
>
> This verse is eschatological in register and uniquely layered: God pours out a spirit of grace that includes within it the capacity for supplication. The gift enables the response. The result is mourning — the somatic expression of recognition, described in some of the most grief-saturated language in the Old Testament. Grace here does not produce ease but a penetrating, grief-laden awareness of what has been done. The body is deeply involved: mourning, weeping, the particular sharpness of losing a firstborn. This is grace operating at the edge of repentance and restoration — it opens the capacity to feel what has been avoided.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Grace in Scripture is carried by two root families: a Hebrew family centred on the root *chen* (favour, grace) and a Greek family built from *charis* (grace, favour, gift). Together they account for 13 terms in the research inventory, 5 of which are owner terms in this registry. The sharing ratio of 0.85 indicates that this word is highly connected — only 2 terms are unique to grace; 11 are shared with adjacent word studies. This degree of sharing signals that grace is not an isolated concept but one that operates in close relationship with several neighbouring characteristics, particularly those in the supplication, mercy, and covenant-love clusters.

**Greek: charis (G5485)**

The primary New Testament term is *charis* (khar-ees), the Greek word that gives English "charity" and anchors the NT's theology of grace. It occurs 241 times in the NT alone, making it by far the highest-occurrence term in this registry. Its range of English translations is exceptionally broad: grace, favour, gift, thanks, blessing, benefit, credit, kindness. This breadth is itself significant — *charis* names not just a disposition but the full arc from disposition through act to the response the act evokes. The Mounce definition moves through at least four distinct domains: the state of favour (relational posture), the gift (the concrete expression of that favour), the gracious commission (a task given on the basis of grace), and the emotional response of gratitude (grace acknowledged and returned as thanks). No other term in this registry carries such semantic range.

Two phase 2 flags are raised on *charis*: RELATIONAL_DIRECTION, indicating that the term's meaning is inherently directed toward another — it cannot exist in isolation from a recipient — and SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH, noting that the term crosses four or more semantic domains and warrants careful attention in analysis. Both flags confirm what the data independently shows: *charis* is relational at its core and conceptually wide.

In classical Greek before the New Testament, *charis* carried the sense of that which gives pleasure or charm — the quality in a person or thing that produces delight in an observer. The word therefore already carried the dual sense of inner disposition and external expression before the NT writers appropriated it. What the NT adds is the theological weight of a God who holds this disposition toward humanity unilaterally and without obligation.

*Charis* has no somatic link recorded in the data, but the dimension index consistently assigns it to Relational Disposition — the word names an inner orientation that is directed outward.

**Greek: charizō (G5483)**

*Charizō* (khar-id-zo) is the verbal form of the grace family, occurring 24 times in the NT. It means to give grace, to bestow freely as a favour, to forgive or cancel a debt, and — in a darker usage — to hand over a person to the demands of others. The Mounce senses show the full range: giving sight freely (Luke 7:21), giving the Son for all (Romans 8:32), delivering a prisoner as a favour to accusers (Acts 25:11), and remitting debt (Luke 7:42; 2 Corinthians 2:7). The same verb that names the supreme gift can also name the unjust surrender of an innocent person — grace as free giving is morally neutral in its form; its character is determined entirely by the giver's inner disposition and the direction of the giving.

The related word *acharistos* (G884, ungrateful) is *charizō*'s semantic shadow — the one who has received grace but returns no acknowledgment. Its presence in the word family names the characteristic's negation not as the absence of grace but as the failure to receive it properly.

**Greek: charitoō (G5487)**

*Charitoō* (khar-ee-to-o) appears only twice in the NT, both in Luke's infancy narrative. Its Mounce definition is: to give graciously, to show acts of kindness by freely giving; to make an object of favour. The passive form — to be visited with free favour, to be graced — is the form that occurs: the angel's address to Mary in Luke 1:28 ("favored one") and Paul's statement that God has "lavished" grace upon believers in Ephesians 1:6. The word's rarity gives it weight: where it appears, it names the act of being constituted as an object of grace — not merely receiving a gift but being defined by having been graced.

**Hebrew: chen (H2580)**

The primary Hebrew term is *chen* (khane), occurring 69 times in the OT. BDB distinguishes three senses: favour or grace (the most common), grace or elegance (an aesthetic quality), and favour in the sense of acceptance. The first sense is the relational one — being in a state of favour before someone — and it governs the registry's use. *Chen* appears frequently in the formula "find favour in the eyes/sight of" — a relational idiom that names the experience of standing before someone and discovering their inner disposition is toward you. This formula appears with God as the one whose eyes the suppliant hopes to meet (Moses in Exodus 33, Noah in Genesis 6) and with human authority figures (Joseph before Potiphar, Esther before the king).

The aesthetic sense of *chen* — grace as beauty or elegance — is present in Proverbs 31:30, where it appears in the context of charm that can be deceptive. This usage reveals that the Hebrew word family understood grace not only as a relational posture but as a quality that can be performed and imitated — which is precisely why the verse contrasts it with the fear of the Lord as its genuine, non-deceptive form.

The related word family around *chen* is extensive. Stripping out proper names (many personal names in the Hebrew Bible are built from this root — Hannah, Hanun, Hanan, Henadad — indicating that grace was a prized characteristic whose blessing parents wished on their children), the meaningful semantic vocabulary includes: *channun* (gracious, H2587), a character description applied overwhelmingly to God in the OT; *chinnam* (for nothing, freely, H2600), the adverbial form meaning without cause or without cost; *chaninah* (favour, H2594); and *tahanun* / *techinnah* (supplication, H8469 / H8467), the words for earnest plea. The *chinnam* family — grace as "for nothing" — makes the economic logic of grace explicit: it is given without transaction.

**Hebrew: tachanum (H8469)**

*Tachanum* (takh-an-oon) and its related form *techinnah* (H8467) name supplication — specifically, earnest, grace-seeking appeal. *Tachanum* occurs 18 times in the OT and carries two sub-senses: supplication directed toward God and supplication directed toward human authority. Both senses name the same inner posture: deep need, relational vulnerability, and the recognition that what is required cannot be demanded but only requested.

Two phase 2 flags are raised on this term: ESCHATOLOGICAL_USAGE, noting that the word appears in eschatological and apocalyptic passages (Zechariah 12:10 is the primary instance), and RELATIONAL_DIRECTION, confirming the inherently directed, other-oriented nature of the term's meaning. The eschatological flag is significant: supplication appears at the end-time not as something humanity finally gets right but as something God pours out — the capacity for earnest appeal is itself a grace.

The root shared between *chen* (favour) and *tachanum* (supplication) is revealing: the same root names both the gift of grace and the inner act of seeking it. This is not coincidental. To seek grace rightly is already to be oriented in a direction that only makes sense if grace exists and if it is freely given.

**Synthesis observation**

The full vocabulary of grace reveals a semantic arch that moves from divine character through relational gift to human response and back. At one end stands the divine disposition — the inner orientation of goodwill that precedes all human action. At the other stands supplication — the human inner act that is both a recognition of need and a form of grace-awareness. Between them lies the rich middle territory: favour found, standing conferred, gifts given freely, debts cancelled, identity constituted. The word family also reveals what grace is not: *chinnam* (for nothing) makes explicit that grace operates outside the economy of merit; *acharistos* (ungrateful) names the failure mode of the recipient. Together the vocabulary defines a relational territory in which one party holds all the initiative and the other holds all the need — and yet genuine relationship is precisely what this asymmetry creates.

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Grace is among the most connected words in the programme. With a sharing ratio of 0.85 — 11 of its 13 terms shared with other word studies — it sits at the intersection of multiple relational and theological domains. Only 2 terms are unique to this registry. This degree of sharing does not indicate that grace lacks definition; it indicates that grace is a characteristic that cannot be understood in isolation. It is structurally embedded in the fabric of the biblical inner-life vocabulary.

---

**Mercy / Steadfast Love**

*Nature:* Formal and theological. The Hebrew root *chen* (favour) is closely associated with *chesed* (steadfast love, covenant loyalty), and the two words appear together frequently in the OT. While *chesed* is registered separately in this programme, the two words travel together and are often translated into the same English domain.

*Key question:* Does grace as *chen* name the disposition that *chesed* then expresses over time? Is *chen* the inner posture and *chesed* the sustained relational pattern that flows from it?

*Priority:* High

---

**Forgiveness**

*Nature:* Formal and linguistic. *Charizō* (G5483) carries "to forgive" and "to cancel a debt" among its primary senses. Ephesians 4:32 names forgiveness as the relational enactment of grace received. The connection is not inferential — forgiveness is one of the named expressions of the grace dynamic.

*Key question:* Is forgiveness a subset of grace or a distinct characteristic that requires grace as its precondition? Does the data support a hierarchical relationship?

*Priority:* High

---

**Repentance**

*Nature:* Theological. Zechariah 12:10 — an eschatological anchor verse — connects the pouring out of a spirit of grace and supplication directly to mourning and recognition of sin. Grace enables repentance; the capacity to turn appears as a gift, not a prior human achievement. This connection is strongly evidenced in the anchor material.

*Key question:* To what degree does the programme's repentance data support the sequence found in Zechariah — that grace precedes and enables the turn rather than following it?

*Priority:* High

---

**Hope**

*Nature:* Theological. Romans 5:2 places standing in grace and rejoicing in hope in the same sentence. Grace as the present condition and hope as its forward orientation appear as parts of a single inner posture. The eschatological dimension in Acts 20:32 (inheritance) reinforces this.

*Key question:* Is hope in the programme data consistently grounded in the grace-standing, or does hope appear independently of it?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Humility**

*Nature:* Inferential, with formal support. The posture of the grace-seeker in this registry — Ruth prostrate, Daniel in sackcloth, Moses as servant — is the posture of humility. The act of supplication structurally requires the inner disposition that humility names. The data implies a causal relationship but does not formally establish it.

*Key question:* Does the humility registry confirm that lowliness of self-assessment is a precondition for grace-reception, or does grace create humility as a consequence?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Peace**

*Nature:* Theological. NT epistolary greetings consistently pair "grace and peace" (*charis kai eirēnē*) as the characterisation of the believer's inner condition. The pairing is formulaic but not accidental — peace appears as what grace produces in the recipient. The programme's peace data may illuminate what the pairing implies about their relationship.

*Key question:* Does the peace registry's data support the reading that peace is grace received and settled?

*Priority:* Medium

---

**Wisdom**

*Nature:* Linguistic. *Chen* (H2580) carries an aesthetic and conduct dimension — gracious speech, gracious manner — that overlaps with the wisdom vocabulary. Proverbs uses both *chen* and wisdom language to describe the person of good character whose conduct generates community standing.

*Key question:* Is the gracious person of Proverbs the same as the wise person, or does the data distinguish them?

*Priority:* Lower

---

**Supplication / Prayer**

*Nature:* Formal. *Tachanum* (H8469) and *techinnah* (H8467) are classified in this registry as XREF terms whose ownership belongs to the supplication domain. Their presence here as cross-references confirms a formal documented connection — grace-seeking is the content of earnest prayer.

*Key question:* When the programme processes the supplication registry, what inner dispositions does it find structurally embedded in the act of prayer beyond the grace-seeking identified here?

*Priority:* Medium
