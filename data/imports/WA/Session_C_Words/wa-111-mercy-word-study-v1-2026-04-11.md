## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Mercy is the inner disposition that moves toward another person in their need, their guilt, or their helplessness — and does something about it. It is not simply sympathy, which feels the weight of another's situation but remains at a distance. Mercy crosses the distance. It acts. It relieves, covers, forgives, spares.

What makes mercy distinctive as a human characteristic is its directionality. It always moves from the one who has something — strength, standing, the power to help or to harm — toward the one who has nothing, or has forfeited whatever they once had. A person in a position of strength chooses to lay aside the claim they could press. A judge who could condemn instead shows clemency. A creditor who could demand repayment instead releases the debt. Mercy is the inner quality that makes this choice possible and natural rather than forced.

Scripture presents mercy across a rich range of expressions. In its deepest Hebrew register, mercy is womb-love — the tenderness a mother bears for the child she has carried. This is not a sentimental image; it points to the visceral, unchosen quality of genuine mercy, the attachment that will not let go. In another register, mercy is covenantal loyalty — the faithfulness that holds to another person not because they have earned it, but because a bond exists and the bond matters. In the Greek register, mercy names a compassionate quality of character — the settled disposition of the person who is open to the need of others and responds.

The range of Scripture's mercy vocabulary signals that this characteristic operates at multiple levels simultaneously: as a felt inner movement, as a settled disposition of character, as a relational commitment, and as a concrete act. These are not four different things but four dimensions of the same thing — mercy fully expressed.

What the research consistently reveals is that mercy, at its source level, is not primarily a human achievement. The primary actor in the mercy vocabulary of Scripture is God. Human mercy is described as a response to, a reflection of, and a participation in the mercy that God himself exercises. A person who shows mercy is doing something divine-shaped. But equally, the person who lacks mercy is described in Scripture not merely as unkind but as morally deficient — hardened in a way that corrupts the whole fabric of inner life.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The natural opposite of mercy is not simply harshness but a particular kind of inner closure. The merciless person is one whose inner orientation has hardened against the claims of another's need. Scripture associates this not with strength but with a catalogue of failures: foolishness, faithlessness, heartlessness. Mercilessness is not presented as a neutral choice but as a symptom of an inner life that has lost its capacity for connection. The person without mercy is the person who has become, in some fundamental sense, less human.

What feeds mercy is the experience of having received it. This is one of the most consistent dynamics in the biblical material: the person who has known mercy becomes capable of mercy. The logic is not merely moral — it is relational and experiential. The one who has stood before God in their own need, who has cried out and found that God hears, who has received forgiveness for what could not be undone — that person is changed by the encounter. They now understand need from the inside. This understanding is the seedbed of mercy.

What diminishes or blocks mercy is self-sufficiency and self-righteousness — the inner posture of a person who does not believe they need mercy themselves. Jesus identifies this as the mechanism behind the failure of the unmerciful servant: having received forgiveness of an enormous debt, he demands payment of a small one. The sequence is not accidental. The failure to receive mercy at depth produces the failure to extend it.

What mercy produces in the person who has it is a quality of openness — a heart that is not armoured against the weakness of others. It generates a willingness to bear cost, to forgo what is owed, to be inconvenienced by another's need. In the wisdom tradition, mercy is one of the marks of the wise person: wisdom from above is full of mercy and good fruits. Mercy is evidence that something essential is working correctly in the inner life.

What mercy produces in others and in relationships is the restoration of what was broken. The gesture of mercy opens a door the other person cannot open for themselves. It creates the possibility of return, of starting again, of relationship not on the basis of performance but on the basis of grace. This is why mercy is consistently linked in Scripture to forgiveness, reconciliation, and restoration — it is the inner disposition that makes all of these possible.

Where mercy ultimately comes from is not self-generated. At its source, mercy in Scripture is a divine attribute that human beings participate in, rather than an independent human virtue. God is described as compassionate and merciful, rich in mercy, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love. The call to be merciful as the Father is merciful is not simply an ethical demand but a statement about what human beings are created to reflect. Mercy, fully expressed, is the human life shaped in the image of the one who is himself its source.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

### From Section 1: Mercy as a moving, directed characteristic

Mercy is the inner disposition that moves toward another person in their need or guilt — and acts.

> **Luk 6:36** — *Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful.*
>
> This single verse encodes the whole structure of mercy as the research reveals it. The imperative "be merciful" names mercy as a settled disposition of character, not a single act — the Greek word here (*oiktirmōn*) describes the quality of being compassionate as a person. The grounding — "as your Father is merciful" — places the human disposition in explicit correspondence with the divine. Human mercy is not invented; it is modelled. The Father's mercy is the original; the human mercy is the echo. This verse also belongs to the Sermon on the Plain's most direct statement of the divine standard for inner character, which gives it programmatic weight: mercy toward others is not optional for the person formed by God.

> **Jam 5:11** — *Behold, we consider those blessed who remained steadfast. You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful.*
>
> This verse is notable for pairing steadfastness under suffering with the compassionate mercy of God. The Lord's mercy (*oiktirmōn*) is named as the theological ground for why endurance through suffering is possible and meaningful. The word here for compassionate is the plural form of the same root — suggesting abundant, layered compassion rather than a single act. James's audience is reminded that the God before whom they suffer is not indifferent but tenderly moved by their condition. The Lord's mercy is what the suffering person can hold onto.

### From Section 1 and 2: Mercy as character quality — and its absence

Mercy is a settled inner quality of character; its absence is a diagnostic sign of deeper collapse.

> **Mat 5:7** — *Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy.*
>
> The Beatitude on mercy is structurally reciprocal: the disposition you extend is the disposition you receive. This is not a contract but a statement about how mercy works in the divine-human relationship. The Greek word (*eleēmōn*) describes a person whose inner character is shaped by compassionate concern — one who feels the weight of another's need and moves toward it. The Beatitude does not say "blessed are those who occasionally show mercy" but "blessed are the merciful" — those for whom this is a defining disposition. The reciprocal promise is that mercy extended creates the condition for mercy received, both from God and from others.

> **Heb 2:17** — *Therefore he had to be made like his brothers in every respect, so that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in the service of God, to make propitiation for the sins of the people.*
>
> This verse is theologically remarkable: it grounds Christ's capacity for mercy in his having shared human experience. He became like us "in every respect" — and the result is that he became *merciful*. The Greek adjective here is the same as in Mat 5:7 — *eleēmōn*, the settled compassionate character. The verse suggests that participation in human vulnerability is what produces the inner quality of mercy at depth. This has consequences for understanding human mercy: the one who has suffered, who knows need from the inside, is the one qualified to be truly merciful. The high priest analogy is not abstract — it points to experiential formation as the mechanism by which mercy is developed and deepened.

> **Rom 1:31** — *foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless.*
>
> Paul's catalogue of inner moral collapse includes *aneleēmōn* — merciless, ruthless, without compassion. The word appears in a list that names the complete dissolution of the inner life: foolishness, faithlessness, heartlessness. The positioning of mercilessness here is significant. It is not treated as a minor failing but as part of the fundamental breakdown of the human person when they have been handed over to the consequences of abandoning God. Mercilessness is the face of inner death. It names what a person becomes when the quality of compassionate connection to others has been extinguished.

### From Section 2: The experience of receiving mercy as the source of giving it

What feeds mercy is the experience of having received it.

> **Mat 18:33** — *And should not you have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I had mercy on you?*
>
> The parable of the unmerciful servant turns on this question. The servant who was forgiven an impossible debt — ten thousand talents — refuses to forgive a hundred denarii. The master's question is not rhetorical in a simple sense; it probes the logic that should have operated. Having received mercy, the servant possessed the experiential knowledge of what it was to be in need and to be released. That knowledge should have produced mercy. The failure is not merely moral but spiritual: mercy received at depth reshapes the inner person; mercy that passes through without being truly received leaves the inner character unchanged. The parable diagnoses this as a failure of genuine encounter with mercy.

> **1Pe 2:10** — *Once you were not a people, but now you are God's people; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.*
>
> Peter frames the new identity of the believer in terms of mercy received. The before-and-after structure is not merely biographical but ontological: having received mercy is constitutive of who this person now is. The verse draws on the prophetic name Lo-Ruhamah — No Mercy — the name given to signal divine mercy withdrawn (Hos 1:6). Its reversal here is deliberate: those who were "No Mercy" are now people who have received mercy. The inner transformation from outsider to people of God is expressed in the language of mercy.

### From Section 2: Mercy shown in action — and as the ground of divine relationship

> **Mat 23:23** — *Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness. These you ought to have done, without neglecting the others.*
>
> Jesus names mercy as one of the three *weightier matters* of the law — alongside justice and faithfulness. This is a remarkable statement about the relative moral weight of religious obligations. The scrupulous performance of small ritual duties is not wrong in itself, but it cannot substitute for mercy. Jesus' use of the comparative "weightier" implies a hierarchy within the law itself: the inner dispositions of justice, mercy, and faithfulness are of greater moral weight than their ritual expressions. Mercy is not a supplement to righteousness but one of its core elements.

> **Jam 3:17** — *But the wisdom from above is first pure, then peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy and good fruits, impartial and sincere.*
>
> Wisdom from above is described as "full of mercy" — *mestē eleous*. Mercy appears here as a defining mark of genuine wisdom, alongside purity, peace, and impartiality. This positions mercy not as a tender optional add-on to wisdom but as constitutive of it. The connection between wisdom and mercy is theologically significant: the wise person understands the human condition, is not armoured by self-righteousness, and therefore remains open to the need of others. Wisdom that produces no mercy is not wisdom from above; it is the counterfeit wisdom James has just described as earthly, unspiritual, demonic.

### From Section 1: Mercy and atonement — the structures that make mercy possible

> **Lev 17:11** — *For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life.*
>
> This verse grounds the priestly atonement system in an explicit principle: life is exchanged for life. The blood of the sacrifice addresses the soul (*nephesh*) — the whole inner person standing in guilt before God. The atonement mechanism is not merely ritual but relational: it addresses the condition of the person before God, the moral accountability that prevents access. This verse belongs to the foundation of the atonement complex in the registry — it names why atonement is necessary and what it addresses. Mercy, in its full biblical expression, cannot be understood apart from this structure: it is not that God ignores the moral weight of guilt, but that he provides the means by which it is addressed.

> **Exo 25:22** — *There I will meet with you, and from above the mercy seat, from between the two cherubim that are on the ark of the testimony, I will speak with you about all that I will give you in commandment for the people of Israel.*
>
> The mercy seat (*kapporet*) is named here as the physical locus of divine speech and meeting — the place where God speaks *from*. It is not merely the cover of the ark but the defined point of divine-human encounter. The significance for mercy is structural: God's communication with his people is mediated through the place of atonement and propitiation. Access to God is mercy-seat access. The architectural and liturgical arrangement of Israel's worship placed mercy at the centre — literally, in the innermost place of the tabernacle. The mercy seat is the spatial embodiment of the claim that the human person can only stand before God on the grounds of mercy.

> **Lev 23:27** — *Now on the tenth day of this seventh month is the Day of Atonement. It shall be for you a time of holy convocation, and you shall afflict yourselves and present a food offering to the Lord.*
>
> The Day of Atonement (*Yom Kippurim*) is the formal, annual structure within which Israel's collective moral condition before God is addressed. The commanded self-affliction names the interior dimension: this is not merely a ritual but a call to inward reckoning, to the acknowledgement of need. The Day of Atonement does not allow Israel to pretend the problem of sin away. It gives it a formal occasion and a prescribed address. The institution itself embodies the mercy structure: God provides the means; the people participate with humility.

> **Psa 78:38** — *Yet he, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often and did not stir up all his wrath.*
>
> This verse presents God's atonement as driven by an inner disposition — "being compassionate" (*rachum*) — rather than by ritual compliance. The Psalmist is reflecting on Israel's repeated failure and God's repeated response. The atonement here is not priestly ceremony but direct divine action, and its source is God's inner character: compassion. He could have destroyed; he did not. He could have let his wrath be fully expressed; he restrained it. The verse reveals the interior logic of mercy: it is not the absence of judgment but its restraint, a conscious withholding of what is owed in response to an inner disposition of compassion.

### From Section 1 and 2: Propitiation — the New Testament fulfilment

> **Luk 18:13** — *But the tax collector, standing far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner!'*
>
> The tax collector's posture encodes the whole inner experience of the person who comes to God in need of mercy. He stands far off — spatial distance expressing moral distance. He will not lift his eyes — the inner quality of shame before God. He beats his breast — a physical act of self-identification with guilt, the body expressing what the inner person knows. His cry (*hilaskomai* in the Greek, the verb of propitiation) is a plea for the mercy-seat work to be done for him personally: be propitious toward me, turn your face in mercy toward me, a sinner. The verse presents mercy not as a feeling but as the whole orientation of the person who knows they have no claim and yet comes anyway.

> **Rom 3:25** — *whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith. This was to show God's righteousness, because in his divine forbearance he had passed over former sins.*
>
> Paul's use of *hilastērios* for Christ places the New Testament propitiation in explicit continuity with the mercy seat. Christ is put forward as the hilastērios — the meeting point, the atonement cover, the locus where God's mercy and human guilt are addressed simultaneously. The phrase "to be received by faith" defines how mercy-seat access now operates: not by priestly mediation and animal sacrifice but by personal faith. The mercy that was architecturally located in the innermost room of the tabernacle is now personally accessible through Christ. The structure of mercy has not changed; its form of access has.

> **1Jo 2:2** — *He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world.*
>
> John names Christ as the *hilasmos* — the propitiation, the means of forgiveness. The scope is deliberately universal: "not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world." This expansiveness is not incidental; it is the eschatological reach of mercy. The mercy structure that Israel knew in the tabernacle and temple — bounded by covenant, mediated by priests, repeated annually — is now extended without boundary. The propitiation is permanent and universal in scope. Mercy has moved from the mercy seat to the person of Christ to the whole world.

> **1Jo 4:10** — *In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins.*
>
> John locates the propitiation within the grammar of love: God's love for us is demonstrated precisely in the act of providing mercy-structure. He loved us; therefore he sent the propitiation. This places the entire atonement complex inside the category of love — mercy is not the cold mechanism by which a legal problem is resolved but the warm expression of an inner disposition of love. The sending of the Son is mercy in its most concentrated form: God himself provides what the human person cannot provide for themselves.

### Supplication: the human inner act of coming for mercy

> **Psa 51:1** — *Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love; according to your abundant mercy blot out my transgressions.*
>
> The Psalm of David opens with two parallel appeals rooted in God's inner character: steadfast love (*chesed*) and abundant mercy (*rachamim*). The plea is structured by what David knows about who God is — not by any claim David can make on God's attention. His own action (sin, adultery, murder) disqualifies him. But God's inner character — his *chesed* and his *rachamim* — remains, and that is what the prayer turns on. The verb *cha.nan* — "have mercy on me" — carries the sense of gracious turning toward, the extension of favour to one with no claim. This psalm is the foundational text of human supplication for mercy in the programme: it names the inner condition (guilt, exposure, shame), the inner act (appeal, confession, dependence), and the inner ground (God's own merciful character).

> **Num 6:25** — *The Lord make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you.*
>
> The Aaronic blessing uses *cha.nan* — "be gracious" — as the inner disposition that is invoked. The shining of God's face is the spatial metaphor for his attention and favour; graciousness is the inner quality of that attention. The blessing is priestly: it is spoken over the people, not earned by them. The verb in its causative form means both "to extend grace" and "to cause grace to be experienced." The Aaronic blessing is a structured act of directing God's gracious inner disposition toward the people who cannot secure it for themselves.

> **1Ki 8:38** — *whatever prayer, whatever plea is made by any man or by all your people Israel, each knowing the affliction of his own heart and stretching out his hands toward this house.*
>
> Solomon's prayer at the dedication of the temple identifies the physical gesture of stretched-out hands as the outward expression of the inner act of supplication (*techinah*). The phrase "knowing the affliction of his own heart" names the inward dimension: the person who comes in supplication is the person who knows their own need, who has reckoned with the inner reality of what they lack and what they have done. The stretched hands toward the house point to the mercy seat within: the gesture of supplication is directed toward the locus of mercy. The verse holds together inner acknowledgement and outward posture in a single act of appeal.

> **Dan 9:20** — *While I was speaking and praying, confessing my sin and the sin of my people Israel, and presenting my plea before the Lord my God for the holy hill of my God.*
>
> Daniel's *techinah* — supplication — is contextualised by confession: his own sin and the sin of his people. The prayer is not a request from moral confidence but a plea from moral honesty. The inner act of supplication here is explicitly communal in scope — Daniel brings not only his own need but the need of his people. The supplication moves from individual inner acknowledgement to corporate appeal, grounded in the conviction that God is disposed to hear and forgive. This verse illustrates the relational structure of mercy: it is not a mechanism but a relationship, and the relationship is entered through honest acknowledgement of need.

### The withdrawal of mercy as theological statement

> **Hos 1:6** — *She conceived again and bore a daughter. And the Lord said to him, 'Call her name No Mercy, for I will no more have mercy on the house of Israel, to forgive them at all.'*
>
> The name *Lo-Ruhamah* — No Mercy — is one of the most dramatic uses of mercy language in Scripture. God names his own inner disposition toward a covenant-breaking people in the person of a child: mercy withdrawn. The name is not a description of an event but an embodiment of a relational stance. The word *racham* (the root of *Lo-Ruhamah*) is womb-love — the deepest, most visceral form of mercy. Its withdrawal is not casual rejection but the deliberate reversal of the most intimate form of care. The extremity of the image measures the extremity of the covenantal failure. The research flag (DIM-111-SD001) correctly notes this as one pole of the mercy structure: the mercy seat as mercy granted, Lo-Ruhamah as mercy withdrawn. The human person stands between these two possibilities.

> **Eze 16:63** — *that you may remember and be confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done, declares the Lord God.*
>
> This verse presents God's atonement as a direct divine act — "when I atone for you" — beyond and fulfilling the ritual mechanism. The context is Ezekiel's extended metaphor of Jerusalem as an unfaithful wife: God's people have been comprehensively faithless. The atonement is preceded by shame and silence: the person for whom atonement has been made has nothing to say for themselves. Mercy, at this depth, silences the recipient. It does not produce self-congratulation but a profound awareness of what has been received. The inner experience of mercy fully extended is described here as shame — not the destructive shame of condemnation but the reverent shame of the person who has been forgiven for the unforgivable.

### The compassionate character of God — the ground of all mercy

> **Eph 2:4** — *But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us.*
>
> Paul names God's mercy as an inner attribute — *plousios en eleei*, rich in mercy. The richness is not a quantity but a quality of disposition: mercy is not rationed. The connection to love is immediate: mercy flows from love as its expression in the direction of need. The contrast with the preceding passage is stark — the human person is dead in trespasses, following the course of the world, by nature children of wrath. The mercy that meets this condition is the mercy of the one who is rich in it. This verse names the starting point of the mercy structure in the character of God.

> **Luk 1:78** — *because of the tender mercy of our God, whereby the sunrise shall visit us from on high.*
>
> Zechariah's Benedictus names the mercy that grounds the coming of Christ as *splagchna eleos* — the tender mercy of God, literally the mercy of his inner organs, his bowels of compassion. The bodily metaphor is deliberate: mercy at its most intimate level is felt in the body before it is expressed in action. The image of sunrise — dawn visiting from on high — names mercy as something experienced as light after darkness, warmth after cold. The mercy of God is not an abstract attribute but a felt arrival, a visitation. The somatic dimension of mercy language is here at its most theologically concentrated.

> **2Cor 1:3** — *Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort.*
>
> God is named as "the Father of mercies" — *pater tōn oiktirmōn*. The genitive is generative: he is not merely the possessor of mercy but its source and origin. The term *oiktirmos* here carries the sense of compassion that responds to the suffering of others — mercy as responsive to pain. God's fatherhood of mercies places him as the origin point of every act of mercy in the human story. This has consequences: human mercy is not self-generated but participatory, drawing from the source who is rich in it. The pairing with "God of all comfort" names the relational outcome: mercy from the Father of mercies produces comfort in those who receive it.

> **Col 3:12** — *Put on then, as God's chosen ones, holy and beloved, compassionate hearts, kindness, humility, meekness, and patience.*
>
> The call to "put on compassionate hearts" — *splagchna oiktirmou*, literally the inner organs of mercy — places mercy as the first garment of the new humanity. The metaphor of clothing is significant: mercy is something that can be consciously assumed, that shapes how a person presents and moves. The phrase *splagchna oiktirmou* is the most visceral of all the Greek mercy terms in this registry: it names mercy as originating in the body's depths, in the same register as the Lukan reference to God's tender mercy. The new humanity is called to wear what God is — to clothe their renewed inner life in the compassionate disposition that characterises God himself.

### The reciprocal and judicial dynamic of mercy

> **Jam 2:13** — *For judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy. Mercy triumphs over judgment.*
>
> This verse names the judicial dimension of mercy with complete clarity. The first clause is a warning: the person who has refused mercy to others will face judgment without mercy. This is not a punitive reversal but a structural correspondence — the inner disposition you have maintained toward others is the disposition that defines your standing before God. The second clause — "mercy triumphs over judgment" — is not a contradiction but a resolution: mercy has priority in the divine economy. It does not eliminate judgment but transcends it. The verb *katakauchaomai* — triumphs over, boasts over — images mercy as the stronger disposition, the one that has the final word when it is present.

> **Heb 8:12** — *For I will be merciful toward their iniquities, and I will remember their sins no more.*
>
> The new covenant formula, quoted from Jeremiah, names God's propitious disposition toward iniquity — *hileōs* — as the inner character of the new covenant relationship. The double promise — merciful toward iniquities, and sins no longer remembered — describes mercy not as a momentary act but as a settled stance. God's inner disposition toward the covenant people under the new covenant is one of persistent, comprehensive mercy. The forgetting of sin is not divine forgetfulness in a cognitive sense but a relational commitment: God chooses not to hold what could be held. This is mercy as a sustained inner orientation.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Registry 111 contains 67 terms in total — 31 owner terms and 34 XREF terms — distributed across Hebrew (48 terms) and Greek (19 terms). The sharing ratio of 0.71 indicates a highly connected word: the majority of the terms in this registry are shared with adjacent word studies, primarily compassion (38 shared terms), pray (14), love (13), and grace (10). This reflects the structural reality that mercy is not a self-contained characteristic but a relational and theological one — its vocabulary overlaps substantially with the vocabulary of grace, covenant love, atonement, and prayer.

**The Greek mercy family: *eleos*, *eleeō*, *eleēmōn* (G1656, G1653, G1655)**

The central Greek term is *eleos* (G1656, *eleos*), occurring 240 times in the New Testament with the primary sense of mercy, pity, and the moral quality of compassionate response to need. Mounce defines it as "the moral quality of feeling compassion and especially of showing kindness toward someone in need" — a definition that captures both the inner feeling and the outward act. *Eleos* translates the Hebrew *chesed* and *rachamim* in the Septuagint, which means it carries the full weight of covenantal steadfast love into the Greek register. Two phase 2 flags are present: RELATIONAL_DIRECTION and SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH — naming the fact that *eleos* always flows toward a recipient, and that its semantic range covers both human and divine mercy, both felt compassion and concrete relief.

The verb *eleeō* (G1653, *eleeō*) — "to have mercy, to show mercy" — occurs 131 times. Its usage range is striking: it names God's sovereign act of mercy (Rom 9:15 — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy"), the desperate cry of the supplicant to Jesus ("Son of David, have mercy on me!"), and the expected human disposition toward others (Mat 18:33). The same verb operates across all three registers — divine sovereignty, human need, and human obligation — which makes it the most dynamic term in the family.

The adjective *eleēmōn* (G1655, *eleēmōn*) — "merciful, compassionate" — occurs 20 times and describes mercy as a settled quality of character. It appears in the Beatitude on mercy (Mat 5:7) and in the description of Christ as merciful high priest (Heb 2:17), placing it at the most significant points of the New Testament mercy instruction.

**The Greek compassion family: *oiktirmos*, *oiktirmōn* (G3628, G3629)**

*Oiktirmos* (G3628, *oiktirmos*) — "compassion, mercy, pity" — occurs 36 times and names compassion primarily as a relational quality: the disposition to relieve sorrow and want, the favour or grace extended by God. Its most programmatic uses are in 2Cor 1:3 ("the Father of mercies") and Col 3:12 ("compassionate hearts"). The term *oiktirmōn* (G3629, *oiktirmōn*) — "compassionate, merciful" — occurs 15 times and appears as the adjective form naming the quality of being compassionate as a person (Luk 6:36, Jam 5:11).

**The propitiation family: *hilaskomai*, *hilasmos*, *hilastērios*, *hileōs* (G2433, G2434, G2435, G2436)**

This family names the structural-theological dimension of mercy — the mechanism by which mercy operates at the level of guilt and moral accountability before God. *Hilaskomai* (G2433, 15 occurrences) means "to make atonement for, to propitiate" (middle voice) and "to have mercy, be merciful" (passive) — the same word names both the active provision of propitiation and the experienced grace of mercy, which is theologically significant. *Hilasmos* (G2434, 8 occurrences) names Christ as "the atoning sacrifice, the means of forgiveness." *Hilastērios* (G2435, 32 occurrences) names either the mercy seat (Heb 9:5) or Christ as propitiation (Rom 3:25), with the SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH flag noting that this term carries the full weight of the mercy-seat imagery into its NT application. *Hileōs* (G2436, 29 occurrences) names the "propitious, gracious, forgiving" divine disposition — the settled inner quality of God that makes mercy possible.

The classical background of the *hileōs*/*hilaskomai* family is relevant: in classical Greek, *hilaskomai* referred to placating the gods through sacrifice or ritual. The New Testament use retains the structure — addressing the condition between a human being and the divine — but transforms its content: it is God himself who provides the propitiation, not the human person. The direction reverses.

**The Hebrew atonement family: *kip.per*, *ka.phar*, *kap.po.ret*, *kip.pu.rim*, *ko.pher* (H3722A, H3722B, H3727, H3725, H3724A)**

The Hebrew root *kap.har* — "to cover" — underlies a family of terms that together map the atonement structure. The primary verb *kip.per* (H3722A, 102 occurrences) means "to cover, purge, make atonement, make reconciliation." Its senses range from the human priestly act (Lev 4:20 — "the priest shall make atonement for them") to the direct divine act (Psa 78:38 — "he atoned for their iniquity"). The noun *kap.po.ret* (H3727, 27 occurrences) names the mercy seat — the golden cover of the ark, the specific location of divine-human encounter on the Day of Atonement. The noun *kip.pu.rim* (H3725, 8 occurrences) names the Day of Atonement as a formal institution. The noun *ko.pher* (H3724A, 13 occurrences) names the ransom price — the substitutionary payment for a life.

The root "cover" is the conceptual heart of the atonement family: guilt is addressed by covering — by the interposition of something between the guilty person and the divine gaze. The mercy seat is the place where this covering is enacted liturgically. The programmatic importance of this family for the registry is that it explains why atonement terms belong inside the mercy study: mercy, in its biblical fullness, is not merely a disposition but a structure — a system by which the moral condition that prevents the person from standing before God is addressed so that mercy can be fully extended.

**The Hebrew gracious-favour family: *cha.nan*, *te.chin.nah* (H2603A, H8467)**

*Cha.nan* (H2603A, 81 occurrences) is the verb "to be gracious, show favour, pity." Its causative form is present (CAUSATIVE_OF_INNER_STATE flag), which means it can name both the experience of grace and the act of causing another to experience grace. The multiple stems — Qal (show favour), Niphal (be pitied), Piel (make gracious), Hiphil (cause to implore favour), Hithpael (seek favour) — reveal a word that operates simultaneously from the side of the giver and the receiver of grace. God is gracious (Qal); the supplicant seeks grace (Hithpael). The same root covers the whole dynamic.

The noun *te.chin.nah* (H8467, 25 occurrences) names "supplication for favour" — the inner act of pleading for mercy. It appears in the great supplication texts: Solomon's temple dedication prayer (1Ki 8:38), Daniel's confession prayer (Dan 9:20), Jeremiah's plea before the king (Jer 37:20). The term reveals a consistent structure: supplication arises from "knowing the affliction of one's own heart," from honest inner acknowledgement of need, and it is directed toward the one — whether God or human authority — who has the disposition and the power to grant favour.

**The Hebrew compassion family: *ra.cha.mim*, *Lo-Ruhamah* (H7359, H3819)**

*Ra.cha.min* (H7359, Aramaic form, 1 occurrence in this registry) names divine compassion as the ground of appeal in Dan 2:18. It is the Aramaic cognate of the Hebrew *rachamim* — the plural of the noun derived from *rechem*, womb. The plural form suggests not a single act of compassion but an abundant, layered compassion — the multiple mercies of God.

*Lo-Ruhamah* (H3819) — "No Mercy" — is the prophetic name from Hosea 1:6. It is unique in the registry: a proper name that functions as a theological statement. The name embodies the withdrawal of divine mercy — the opposite pole of the mercy seat. Its presence in this registry signals that mercy's full meaning requires understanding its negation: mercy known in its fullness is mercy that could have been withheld.

**Synthesis observation**

The complete vocabulary of this registry maps a semantic arch from the innermost movement of mercy — womb-love, tender visceral compassion — through its expression as favour and gracious turning toward a person, through the structural mechanism that makes mercy possible in the presence of guilt (atonement, propitiation, ransom), to the formal institution (the mercy seat, the Day of Atonement) and its New Testament fulfilment in Christ as living propitiation. The vocabulary is not a collection of synonyms; it describes mercy as a complete system — an inner disposition that requires both a source (the compassionate character of God), a mechanism (atonement that addresses the condition that prevents mercy from being fully given), and an act (the specific extension of grace and forgiveness toward the person in need). The prayer vocabulary (supplication, intercession, entreaty) then names the human side of this system: the inner act by which the person in need turns toward the source of mercy and asks. The whole vocabulary, taken together, presents mercy not as one virtue among many but as the fundamental structure of the divine-human relationship.
