## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Forgiveness is the act of releasing a claim you have a right to hold. It is not the pretence that a wrong did not happen, nor the numbing of the pain it caused. It is the deliberate choice to cancel the account of injury — to stop holding the debt against the person who owes it and to relinquish the right of collection. That choice is one of the most inwardly demanding acts a human being can perform, precisely because it runs against the inner logic of what has happened: something real was taken, and the instinct of the inner life is to register and retain that loss.

The vocabulary of forgiveness in Scripture reveals its governing act-structure: the deliberate release of a legitimate hold. Whether the word is used for leaving home for a higher loyalty, for cancelling a debt, or for granting access to someone who would otherwise be turned away, the act-structure is the same — something held is let go. Forgiveness proper is the theologically and morally concentrated form of this act: the genuine cancellation of a claim against a person who genuinely owes it. The release is real because the debt is real.

The most distinctive feature of forgiveness in Scripture is where it ultimately comes from. God is its primary source and its defining model throughout both testaments. In the Old Testament, the primary Hebrew verb for divine forgiveness (*sālach*) is used exclusively of God as the acting subject — no human being is ever the subject of this verb. God forgives through sacrifice and priestly mediation in the old covenant context; through Christ's blood and authority in the new. Human forgiveness is consistently presented not as an independent moral achievement but as a response — a pattern of release in one person that mirrors and flows from having been released by God. The more profoundly a person understands how much they have been forgiven, the more freely they can forgive. That causal logic is stated explicitly in the biblical evidence and shapes the character of the word throughout.

Forgiveness is not primarily an emotion, though it may affect and transform the emotional life. It is closer to a settled inner orientation — a relational posture of release rather than retention — that has both vertical and horizontal dimensions: what is received from God and what is extended to others. At its deepest level, it is a characteristic received before it is given: grounded in an encounter with the character of God, who holds forgiveness as his own essential disposition and practises it as an act of genuine release, not of overlooking.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The opposite of forgiveness is the retention of the claim — the inner insistence that the debt remains, that the account is still open, that the person still owes. This is sometimes called unforgiveness or hardness of heart, but in the biblical evidence it appears most sharply in the image of the person who receives mercy and immediately refuses to extend it: the logic of the closed inner posture. Where forgiveness releases, its absence holds. And the evidence is pointed about the consequences: the one who withholds forgiveness is poisoned by what they retain.

What feeds and strengthens forgiveness is the prior experience of being forgiven. The grammar of the evidence is clear: the person who has understood the magnitude of what has been released in their own case has more resource to release others. The inner compassion that precedes the act of forgiving — a visceral, body-involving movement of the interior — is itself upstream of the decision. Forgiveness received opens the inner capacity for forgiveness given. Worship and atonement — the rituals and processes by which forgiveness is received — are therefore upstream of the human capacity to forgive. Prayer also feeds it: the pattern of asking God for pardon keeps the forgiver in touch with their own need, which is the ground of their compassion for the needs of others.

What diminishes or blocks forgiveness is the hardening of the inner account-keeping tendency — the cultivated sense of entitlement to the debt, the refusal to allow the record to be cleared. Pride, self-justification, and the comparison of one's own debts to those of others all close the inner hand that forgiveness requires to open. The same faculty that enables forgiveness — the capacity to release what is legitimately held — can be turned against itself, abandoning a loyalty that should have been retained or closing a door that should have been kept open.

What forgiveness produces in the person who receives it is specific and named in the evidence. Receiving forgiveness generates love as a downstream inner state: the person who has been forgiven much loves much; the one forgiven little loves little. It also produces reverential awe — not the fear of condemnation but the awe that belongs to standing before the one who held your debt and chose to release it. These are not vague feelings of relief; they are specific inner orientations that forgiveness, when genuinely received, generates.

In relationships, forgiveness makes continuation possible where continuation would otherwise be impossible. It breaks the cycle of accumulating injury and restores the relational possibility between parties who stand on opposite sides of a real wrong. It is also the enabling condition of authentic worship: a broken horizontal relationship creates an obstacle to vertical offering that must be resolved before the offering is genuine.

At its source, forgiveness is not self-generated. It is a characteristic received before it can be given.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

*Sections 1 and 2 reproduced with anchor verses inserted at relevant points.*

---

Forgiveness is the act of releasing a claim you have a right to hold. It is the deliberate choice to cancel the account of injury.

> **Ephesians 1:7** — *In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace*
>
> The noun used here (*aphesis*) is a legal and financial word — the formal releasing of a debt or the freeing of a prisoner. Paul embeds it in a chain of four terms: redemption (the buyback), blood (the price paid), forgiveness (the release of the debt), and grace (the character of God from which the whole act proceeds). The verse names what forgiveness actually costs: the account was genuinely owed and has been genuinely paid. Grace is the upstream disposition; forgiveness is the specific relational act it generates. A person who receives this is described as holding it — "we have… the forgiveness" — as a settled possession, not a moment-by-moment petition.

The vocabulary of forgiveness reveals its governing act-structure: the deliberate release of a legitimate hold. The same word that names forgiving a debt names leaving home for a higher loyalty — and the moral character of the act depends entirely on what is released and why.

> **Mark 10:29** — *Jesus said, "Truly, I say to you, there is no one who has left house or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or lands, for my sake and for the gospel"*
>
> The disciples' leaving of home and family is described with the same Greek word used for forgiveness. The inner structure is identical: something legitimately held is voluntarily released in response to a higher claim. Jesus' affirmation establishes that the act is morally significant and spiritually validated — the leaving is costly, chosen, and relationally reorienting. This structural kinship between leaving and forgiving reveals that forgiveness is not a separate kind of act from other releasing-acts; it shares their inner grammar of wilful forfeiture.

> **Revelation 2:4** — *But I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first*
>
> The same word used for forgiveness names here a spiritual defection — the releasing of something that should have been kept. The Ephesian church has maintained orthodox practice and doctrinal vigilance while its first love has quietly been let go. The verse defines the anti-pattern: the faculty that enables forgiveness, turned against itself, constitutes abandonment. What should be released (a grievance) is retained; what should be retained (a loyalty) is released. Forgiveness is the right use of the releasing act; this verse names its wrong use with the same word.

The most distinctive feature of forgiveness in Scripture is where it ultimately comes from. God is its primary source and defining model.

> **Psalm 86:5** — *For you, O Lord, are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love to all who call upon you*
>
> The only occurrence in the Old Testament of forgiveness as a divine disposition-word — naming not what God does on occasion but what God *is*. The three terms in apposition (good, forgiving, abounding in steadfast love) place forgiveness inside the essential character of God alongside his goodness and covenant faithfulness. This verse is foundational for understanding where human forgiveness ultimately comes from: the disposition to pardon belongs to God's nature before it becomes a possibility in human experience. The person who calls upon God encounters a readiness to forgive that is not generated by the request but already present in the one who is addressed.

> **Psalm 130:4** — *But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared*
>
> Forgiveness is described here as a divine possession — something located *with* God, not a general availability. The second clause is paradoxical: the existence of forgiveness produces reverential fear rather than presumption. The verse implies that without forgiveness, the appropriate response to God would be despair or flight. Forgiveness makes approach possible — and the encounter with one who holds your debt and chooses to release it produces awe rather than relief. This names one of the specific downstream inner states that forgiveness, when genuinely encountered, generates.

In the old covenant, God forgives through covenantal mediation. The mechanism is outward; the inner reality conferred is the restored standing before God.

> **Leviticus 4:20** — *And the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven*
>
> The Levitical formula is invariant across dozens of passages: sacrifice, priestly atonement, then forgiveness conferred in the passive voice — "they shall be forgiven." The worshipper does not self-achieve forgiveness; they receive it as the guaranteed outcome of a correctly executed covenantal act. The invariance of the formula matters: forgiveness is objective and covenantally administered, not dependent on the worshipper's emotional state at the moment of offering. What changes in the person is their inner standing before God — a real transformation — but the mechanism is outward and mediatory.

God's forgiveness is also genuinely conditional. He grants it in response to repentance and intercession; he can withhold it where the covenant has been finally broken.

> **Daniel 9:19** — *O Lord, hear; O Lord, forgive. O Lord, pay attention and act. Delay not, for your own sake, O my God, because your city and your people are called by your name*
>
> The four urgent imperatives addressed to God express escalating intensity — not doubt about God's character but acute awareness of the people's need. The appeal is made not to Israel's merit (the prayer has already confessed Israel's sin) but to God's own name and character. This is the posture of Dependence and Creatureliness precisely: the one praying knows two things simultaneously — that the need is real, and that only the offended party can supply the remedy. Seeking forgiveness is here an act of confidence in God's character, not a transaction between equals.

> **Jeremiah 31:34** — *For I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more*
>
> The new covenant promise joins two distinct acts: releasing the penalty of sin, and ceasing to remember it. The cessation of memory is not amnesia but covenantal resolution — the account is not deferred but genuinely closed. This verse sets the standard for what genuine forgiveness means: not mere forbearance (tolerating a debt while retaining a record) but actual cancellation (releasing the claim and expunging the account). The human analogue is the word-world's most demanding requirement: to release not only the claim but the memory of the claim as a live grievance.

Human forgiveness is consistently presented as a response to having been forgiven — not as an independent moral achievement.

> **Luke 7:47** — *Therefore I tell you, her sins, which are many, are forgiven — for she loved much. But he who is forgiven little, loves little*
>
> The most analytically direct verse in the corpus. The causal direction is crucial and must not be reversed: the woman loved much *because* she was forgiven much — not the other way around. Forgiveness received is the cause; love produced is the effect. The inverse confirms the logic: the one forgiven little loves little. This is not merely motivational — it describes an actual inner transformation. Receiving forgiveness opens the inner life and produces love as its first and characteristic fruit. The person who has genuinely encountered divine forgiveness does not merely feel relieved; they are changed in their relational orientation toward others.

> **Matthew 18:35** — *So also my heavenly Father will do to every one of you, if you do not forgive your brother from your heart*
>
> "From your heart" is the entire weight of this verse. The parable has shown a servant who verbally accepted his master's forgiveness while inwardly retaining his claim against a fellow-servant. Jesus identifies the failure precisely: the forgiveness did not reach the heart. A forgiveness that exists only in words or conduct but has not moved through the inner person is not forgiveness in the register Scripture requires. This verse names the inner locus that genuine forgiveness must occupy — not the speech, not the conduct, but the heart where the claim is either held or released.

> **Matthew 5:24** — *Leave your gift there before the altar and go. First be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift*
>
> Reconciliation takes priority over the offering already placed. The sequence is not that relationships matter more than God, but that a broken horizontal relationship creates an obstacle to vertical offering that must be resolved before the offering can be genuine. The inner posture toward the brother must be settled first. This places forgiveness and reconciliation as the enabling conditions of authentic worship — not secondary to it, but structurally prerequisite.

There is an outer limit to forgiveness in the biblical evidence — a boundary at which it becomes permanently inaccessible.

> **Mark 3:29** — *But whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit never has forgiveness, but is guilty of an eternal sin*
>
> The absolute limit: the one condition for which forgiveness is stated to be permanently unavailable. The verse does not define the mechanism of this exception in detail, but its existence qualifies any simple account of forgiveness as automatically available. The existence of a limit reveals that forgiveness is a relational act requiring something from the receiving side — and where that reception is finally and irrevocably closed, the forgiveness cannot reach. This is consistent with the rest of the corpus: the person forgiven little loves little; the person from whom forgiveness is permanently inaccessible occupies the outer boundary of a relational spectrum.

The horizontal and vertical dimensions of forgiveness are structurally linked. Receiving divine forgiveness creates the obligation and provides the resource for forgiving others.

> **Matthew 23:13** — *For you shut the kingdom of heaven in people's faces. For you neither enter yourselves nor allow those who would enter to go in*
>
> The obstructing of access and the withholding of forgiveness are structurally the same inner posture — both close what should be open. The religious leaders' refusal to permit is named with the same word-family as forgiveness. The severity of the judgement here is consistent with the pattern throughout: withholding a release that is owed is among the gravest failures of the inner life. The same faculty that enables forgiveness, exercised in its negative form, can seal off entire communities from the possibility of encounter with God.

> **Mark 10:14** — *"Let the children come to me; do not hinder them, for to such belongs the kingdom of God"*
>
> The posture of welcome — the inner orientation that opens rather than closes — is expressed with the same word-family as forgiveness. Jesus' indignation at those who obstruct marks this as a morally serious act. The forgiveness-connection is structural: to forgive is to open the relational space for someone from whom the space had been closed. The posture of welcome and the posture of forgiveness share the same inner act of non-obstruction and release.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

The vocabulary of forgiveness in Scripture divides into two language families: a Greek family centred on the verb *aphiēmi* (to release, to let go) and a Hebrew family centred on the root *sālach* (to pardon, to forgive). These two families do not share a common etymological ancestor, but they converge on the same conceptual territory: the releasing of a legitimate hold on a person or a debt. Seven terms were identified, all owned by this study with no vocabulary shared with other word studies in the programme. This self-contained character is unusual and signals that forgiveness occupies a distinctive lexical space — it does not depend on borrowing vocabulary from adjacent characteristics to name what it is.

**Note on source data:** The STEP database returned no structured word analysis for the primary Greek noun (*aphesis*, G0859) — the term's meaning data derives from Mounce and the Liddell-Scott-Jones lexicon. The three verbal sub-glosses share a single structured sense entry from STEP. This is a known data coverage limitation and does not affect the accuracy of the lexical description, which is drawn from primary sources.

**Greek terms — the *aphiēmi* family**

*Aphesis* (G0859, *afesis*) is the nominal form — the state or act of release. It occurs 62 times in the New Testament and carries no Old Testament Hebrew equivalent in this study. The Mounce range — forgiveness, pardon, release, cancellation of a debt — captures both its legal and personal registers. The Liddell-Scott-Jones data confirms that in pre-biblical Greek usage, *aphesis* named the releasing of prisoners, the cancellation of financial obligations, the dismissal of persons from service, and the starting of horses in a race (where something held is launched from a fixed point). These secular uses are not merely etymological footnotes: they show that the New Testament writers chose a word whose primary meaning in the ancient world was the releasing of something held, and applied it to the releasing of sin's hold on a person. The word carries the force of a formal legal release, not merely an emotional letting-go.

*Aphiēmi* (G0863) is the verb from which *aphesis* derives. The research has recorded it in three distinct usage registers — leave or abandon (236 instances in the research corpus), forgive (64 instances), and permit (32 instances) — reflecting the word's three primary semantic faces. All three share the same underlying act: something held is released. To leave one's home, to forgive a debt, and to permit a person to pass are all acts of non-retention. The semantic unity of the word family is instructive for understanding what forgiveness means as an inner-being act: it belongs to the same family as voluntary departure and the granting of access. This is not metaphor borrowed from other domains — it is the same act expressed in different relational registers.

The Mounce definition draws together: *to forgive, pardon, remit, cancel; to leave, abandon; to allow, permit, tolerate; to send away; to give up, yield, resign.* The full range reveals a vocabulary of release whose moral character depends entirely on what is released and why. The same word names both the forgiveness of a debt (right release) and the abandonment of first love (wrong release). Understanding forgiveness in this word family requires understanding it as a faculty — one whose exercise can be morally excellent or morally catastrophic depending on its object.

The verb has both active and passive uses: one person releases, another receives the release. This makes it capable of describing God as the acting subject (he releases the debt of sin) and the human as the recipient, and equally capable of describing the human as actor (releasing a claim against a brother) and another person as the one released. The same word-act structure in both directions is part of what makes it the natural vocabulary for the divine-human pattern of forgiveness.

**Hebrew terms — the *sālach* family**

*Sālach* (H5545, *sa.lach*) is the primary Hebrew verb for divine forgiveness, occurring 49 times in the Old Testament. Its theological profile is sharply defined: in the entire biblical corpus covered by this research, this word is used exclusively of God as the acting subject. No human being is ever the subject of *sālach* in the Old Testament. Lexical scholarship across traditions confirms this as the universal pattern. The basic senses are: Qal — to forgive, to pardon; Niphal — to be forgiven (the passive, naming the state of the person who has received forgiveness). There is no causative or intensive stem, which means the word does not structurally imply that one person causes forgiveness in another — it simply names the act of pardon, and that act belongs to God alone.

*Sallāch* (H5546, *sal.lach*) occurs exactly once in the Old Testament, in Psalm 86:5. It is the adjectival form: *forgiving*, as a stable disposition. Its single occurrence — "you, O Lord, are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love" — is the only place in the Hebrew Bible where forgiveness is named as a divine character quality rather than as a specific act. Its rarity makes it the more significant: the disposition of ready forgiveness belongs to God alone, and it appears only once because the Hebrew Bible tends to describe God's attributes through his acts rather than through abstract description.

*Selichah* (H5547, *se.li.chah*) is the nominal form, occurring three times: Psalm 130:4 ("with you there is forgiveness"), Daniel 9:9 ("to the Lord our God belong mercy and forgiveness"), and Nehemiah 9:17 (God described as "a God ready to forgive"). The noun names forgiveness as a divine possession — something that belongs to God and is located with him. The language of belonging and location ("with you," "belong to") is theologically precise: forgiveness is not a general availability in the universe but a specific possession of God's character that he makes accessible through relationship. The concentration of this noun in psalms of prayer and books of intercession is consistent with its use: *selichah* is the word one appeals to when seeking God's pardon — the resource that prayer addresses.

**Synthesis observation**

The two language families describe forgiveness from different angles that together produce a richer picture than either alone provides. The Greek vocabulary is structurally rich — the releasing-act, the permitted access, the voluntary departure — and covers both the act and the faculty: what forgiveness *does* and what kind of act it *is*. The Hebrew vocabulary is theologically concentrated — what God *is* (the disposition), what God *holds* (the possession), what God *does* (the act of pardoning) — and is almost exclusively vertical, describing the divine-human axis. Together they name forgiveness as simultaneously legal (the cancellation of a real debt), relational (the restoration of broken standing), characterological (a disposition that belongs to God and is made available to the person who encounters him), and transformative (an act that produces specific downstream inner orientations in those who receive it).

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Forgiveness shares no vocabulary with any other word study in the programme — its terms are exclusively owned by this registry. This lexical self-containment stands in contrast to its verse co-occurrence profile, where forgiveness appears alongside a wide range of inner-being characteristics across 34 other word studies. Forgiveness occupies its own lexical space but is relational in its occurrence — it rarely appears in Scripture in isolation from other inner-being states. The connections below are drawn from the correlation data and are distinguished by the type of signal that confirms them.

---

**Guilt** — verse co-occurrence (54 shared verses), shared anchor verse (Jeremiah 31:34)

The strongest connection in the research data: 54 verses shared between forgiveness and guilt. This is structurally expected — guilt names the inner state produced by moral failure, and forgiveness names its resolution. The Jeremiah 31:34 anchor is shared: new covenant forgiveness as the final cancellation of iniquity. The key question: does forgiveness *remove* guilt as an inner-being state, or does it *reframe* it without removing it? The verse evidence in Luke 7:47 suggests genuine release rather than suppression — the woman is described as loving much, not as still carrying guilt while being accepted. Whether this pattern holds across the full guilt corpus requires the two registries to be read together.

**Mercy** — verse co-occurrence (16 shared verses), shared anchor verse (Leviticus 4:20)

Mercy and forgiveness share Leviticus 4:20 as an anchor — the atonement context where both characteristics operate together. Sixteen shared verses confirm a consistent biblical pairing. Both describe what God extends to a person who is not in a position to demand it; both require a prior wrong to be meaningful. The Psalm 86:5 anchor for this registry places forgiveness alongside steadfast love (*hesed*), which is the most common Hebrew word in the mercy family. The key question: is mercy the broader disposition from which forgiveness flows as a specific act, or are they parallel expressions of the same divine character? Session D must examine whether the mercy and forgiveness registries name the same inner-being territory from different angles.

**Grace** — dimension overlap (3 shared dimensions); Ephesians 1:7 direct verse connection

Ephesians 1:7 places forgiveness "according to the riches of his grace" — grace as the upstream disposition from which the forgiving act proceeds. The dimension overlap confirms shared structural territory. Earlier analysis of the grace registry established that grace operates as an inner-being generator producing specific downstream orientations. Forgiveness appears to operate similarly. The key question: do grace and forgiveness occupy structurally different positions in an inner-being order — grace as disposition, forgiveness as specific relational act derived from it? This is the most direct structural question connecting the two most theologically significant characteristics in this cluster.

**Love** — verse co-occurrence (10 shared verses), shared anchor verse (Jeremiah 31:34), analytical finding (Luke 7:47)

Luke 7:47 establishes an explicit inner-being causal chain: forgiveness received → love produced. This is the most direct inner-being cause-and-effect statement in the corpus and has been formally noted in the research record. Ten shared verses confirm the consistent biblical pairing. The key question: is the forgiveness-to-love sequence a reproducible pattern across the full corpus, and does it represent forgiveness as a *transitional* inner-being state — one that characteristically transforms a person from one orientation to another — rather than a terminal one?

**Repentance** — verse co-occurrence (12 shared verses)

Repentance and forgiveness are consistently paired as sequence in the New Testament: a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins (Mark 1:4; Luke 3:3; Luke 24:47); repent and be baptised for forgiveness (Acts 2:38); God exalted Christ to give repentance and forgiveness (Acts 5:31). The key question: is the repentance-forgiveness relationship causal (repentance produces the inner-being conditions for forgiveness to be received), sequential (repentance precedes but does not cause), or co-generated (the same divine act that makes forgiveness available also produces repentance as a response)? Acts 5:31 hints at the third possibility: Christ gives both repentance and forgiveness.

**Faith** — verse co-occurrence (9 shared verses)

Faith is consistently the human instrument through which forgiveness is received in the New Testament: "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins" (Acts 10:43); "those who are sanctified by faith in me" (Acts 26:18); "your sins are forgiven" spoken in response to observed faith (Luke 5:20 — "when he saw their faith"). The key question: is faith a necessary inner-being precondition for receiving forgiveness, or is it the posture in which forgiveness is recognised and accepted? This matters for understanding the inner-being grammar of the transition from unforgiven to forgiven.

**Strength** — verse co-occurrence (19 shared verses)

Nineteen shared verses between forgiveness and strength — the second-highest co-occurrence signal in the registry and the most unexpected. The shared passages consistently invoke divine power alongside divine forgiveness: "God exalted him… to give repentance and forgiveness" (Acts 5:31); "according to the greatness of your steadfast love" (Numbers 14:19); "the riches of his grace" (Ephesians 1:7). Forgiveness at scale — the mass cancellation of a people's accumulated debt — appears to require an invocation of divine power as its warrant. The key question: does the biblical presentation of forgiveness depend structurally on a prior statement of divine omnipotence? Is forgiveness one of the acts that most directly expresses what divine power is *for*?

**Covenant** — dimension overlap (5 shared dimensions)

Forgiveness in both testaments is consistently embedded in the covenant relationship — it is not a general availability but a covenant-specific act. The Levitical formula, Solomon's prayer (1 Kings 8), Jeremiah 31:34, and the New Testament proclamation all locate forgiveness within the covenant framework. The key question: is forgiveness structurally covenantal (it only exists within a relationship of covenant commitment) rather than merely transactional, and does this shape its inner-being character in ways the covenant registry analysis should address?

**Authority** — verse co-occurrence (10 shared verses), dimension overlap (5 shared dimensions)

The Synoptic pronouncements of forgiveness (Luke 5:20-24; Mark 2:5-10; Matthew 9:2-6) generate a Christological argument: Jesus claims authority to forgive sins on the grounds that this authority belongs to God alone. The authority and forgiveness registries share 10 verses and 5 dimensions. The key question: is forgiveness structurally dependent on authority — not just any authority but specifically divine authority — in a way that connects the C17 relational cluster to the C20 power cluster at a deeper level than dimension overlap alone?

**Desire** — verse co-occurrence (8 shared verses)

Eight shared verses, primarily through the leaving/relinquishment passages. When forgiveness is extended, the offended party must forgo the inner satisfaction of retained grievance — itself a form of desire (for vindication, for the account to be settled). The leaving-for-discipleship passages (group 5376-001) involve a reordering of attachment. The key question: does the inner act of forgiving require a desire-level reordering — the relinquishing of the desire for the claim's satisfaction — and if so, how does the desire registry characterise this kind of forgoing?

**Compassion** — dimension overlap (4 shared dimensions); Matthew 18:27 verse connection

Matthew 18:27 — the master forgives the servant "out of pity" (*splagchnizomai*, a word naming visceral, body-involving compassion). This is the most direct somatic entry point for forgiveness in the New Testament: the compassion that generates the act of forgiving is itself body-involving. Dimension overlap of 4 confirms structural shared territory. The key question: does compassion function as a necessary inner-being precondition of the human act of forgiving, or is it one pathway among several?

**Sin** — shared anchor verse (Mark 3:29)

Mark 3:29 is shared with the sin registry. Forgiveness and sin are structural counterparts — sin is what forgiveness addresses; the outer limit of forgiveness (Mark 3:29) defines the furthest reach of the sin-state. The key question: does the sin registry address the inner-being experience of the one who has committed sin and received forgiveness, and how does that transition from guilt-bearing to forgiven characterise the inner-being change that forgiveness produces?

**Kindness, Peace, Fellowship** — dimension overlap (cluster partners in C17)

These cluster partners in C17 share dimension profiles with forgiveness. Whether they are causally connected to forgiveness — kindness and compassion as preconditions, peace as an outcome — is inferential at present and requires Session D analysis.

**Evil** — verse co-occurrence (6 shared verses)

Evil is the object of forgiveness — what forgiveness responds to. Six shared verses confirm the consistent biblical pairing. The key question: does the evil registry address the inner-being experience of the perpetrator who has done evil and received forgiveness, and how does that transition shape the inner-being grammar of being forgiven?

**Deadness (spiritual)** — dimension overlap (5 shared dimensions)

The overlap likely reflects the theologically negative groups in the forgiveness corpus — the refusal of forgiveness (Deuteronomy 29:20, Lamentations 3:42), the permanent limit (Mark 3:29). Where forgiveness is permanently inaccessible, the inner-being state of the person in that condition is worth examining across the deadness and spiritual blindness registries.
