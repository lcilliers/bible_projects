## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Mercy is the inner disposition that moves toward another person in their need, their guilt, or their helplessness — and does something about it. It is not simply sympathy, which feels the weight of another's situation but remains at a distance. Mercy crosses the distance. It acts. It relieves, covers, forgives, spares.

What makes mercy distinctive as a human characteristic is its directionality. It always moves from the one who has something — strength, standing, the power to help or to harm — toward the one who has nothing, or has forfeited whatever they once had. A person in a position of strength chooses to lay aside the claim they could press. A judge who could condemn instead shows clemency. A creditor who could demand repayment instead releases the debt. Mercy is the inner quality that makes this choice possible and natural rather than forced.

Scripture presents mercy across a rich range of expressions. In its deepest Hebrew register, mercy is womb-love — the tenderness a mother bears for the child she has carried. This is not a sentimental image; it points to the visceral, unchosen quality of genuine mercy, the attachment that will not let go. In another register, mercy is covenantal loyalty — the faithfulness that holds to another person not because they have earned it, but because a bond exists and the bond matters. In the Greek register, mercy names a compassionate quality of character — the settled disposition of the person who is open to the need of others and responds.

The range of Scripture's mercy vocabulary signals that this characteristic operates at multiple levels simultaneously: as a felt inner movement, as a settled disposition of character, as a relational commitment, and as a concrete act. These are not four different things but four dimensions of the same thing — mercy fully expressed.

What the research consistently reveals is that mercy, at its source level, is received rather than self-generated. Mercy originates in God — in his compassionate character, his covenantal faithfulness, his freely given grace. The primary actor in the mercy vocabulary of Scripture is God. Human mercy is described as a response to, a reflection of, and a participation in the mercy that God himself exercises. Specifically: mercy is received at the spirit level — it is a gift from the divine source, not a human achievement — and expressed at the soul level as compassion toward others, directed in supplication toward God, and enacted in acts of grace toward those in need. A person who shows mercy is doing something divine-shaped. Equally, the person who lacks mercy is described in Scripture not merely as unkind but as morally deficient — hardened in a way that corrupts the whole fabric of inner life.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The natural opposite of mercy is not simply harshness but a particular kind of inner closure. The merciless person is one whose inner orientation has hardened against the claims of another's need. Scripture associates this not with strength but with a catalogue of failures: foolishness, faithlessness, heartlessness. Mercilessness is not presented as a neutral choice but as a symptom of an inner life that has lost its capacity for connection. The person without mercy is the person who has become, in some fundamental sense, less human.

What feeds mercy is the experience of having received it. This is one of the most consistent dynamics in the biblical material: the person who has known mercy becomes capable of mercy. The logic is not merely moral — it is relational and experiential. The one who has stood before God in their own need, who has cried out and found that God hears, who has received forgiveness for what could not be undone — that person is changed by the encounter. They now understand need from the inside. This understanding is the seedbed of mercy. Significantly, authentic mercy-seeking itself requires self-knowledge: the supplication vocabulary of Scripture consistently presupposes that the person who comes for mercy knows the affliction of their own heart — they have reckoned honestly with their inner condition before making the appeal.

What diminishes or blocks mercy is self-sufficiency and self-righteousness — the inner posture of a person who does not believe they need mercy themselves. Jesus identifies this as the mechanism behind the failure of the unmerciful servant: having received forgiveness of an enormous debt, he demands payment of a small one. The sequence is not accidental. The failure to receive mercy at depth produces the failure to extend it.

What mercy produces in the person who has it is a quality of openness — a heart that is not armoured against the weakness of others. It generates a willingness to bear cost, to forgo what is owed, to be inconvenienced by another's need. In the wisdom tradition, mercy is one of the marks of the wise person: wisdom from above is full of mercy and good fruits. Mercy is evidence that something essential is working correctly in the inner life.

What mercy produces in others and in relationships is the restoration of what was broken. The gesture of mercy opens a door the other person cannot open for themselves. It creates the possibility of return, of starting again, of relationship not on the basis of performance but on the basis of grace. This is why mercy is consistently linked in Scripture to forgiveness, reconciliation, and restoration — it is the inner disposition that makes all of these possible. The merciful God is also the God of all comfort: mercy, received, produces comfort; comfort, given, is the pastoral output of mercy extended.

Where mercy ultimately comes from is not self-generated. At its source, mercy in Scripture is a divine attribute that human beings participate in, rather than an independent human virtue. God is described as compassionate and merciful, rich in mercy, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love. The call to be merciful as the Father is merciful is not simply an ethical demand but a statement about what human beings are created to reflect. Mercy, fully expressed, is the human life shaped in the image of the one who is himself its source.

## Section 3 — Annotated Verse Evidence

### From Section 1: Mercy as a moving, directed characteristic

Mercy is the inner disposition that moves toward another person in their need or guilt — and acts.

> **Luk 6:36** — *Be merciful, even as your Father is merciful.*
>
> This single verse encodes the whole structure of mercy as the research reveals it. The imperative "be merciful" names mercy as a settled disposition of character, not a single act — the Greek word here (*oiktirmōn*) describes the quality of being compassionate as a person. The grounding — "as your Father is merciful" — places the human disposition in explicit correspondence with the divine. Human mercy is not invented; it is modelled. The Father's mercy is the original; the human mercy is the echo. The call to reflect God's compassion follows the same pattern as the call to reflect God's holiness — it is a character-formation command, not only an ethical directive. Mercy toward others is not optional for the person formed by God.

> **Jam 5:11** — *Behold, we consider those blessed who remained steadfast. You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful.*
>
> The verse pairs steadfastness under suffering with the compassionate mercy of God as its ground. James does not say God will remove suffering quickly; he says God is compassionate in the midst of it. The word translated "compassionate" (*polysplanchnos*) is a compound — literally "many-splanchna," many inner organs of feeling — naming abundant, layered compassion rather than a single act. The plural intensity of the term reflects what the Psalmist elsewhere calls "the multitude of your mercies." Endurance through suffering is possible because the God before whom one suffers is tenderly and abundantly moved by the sufferer's condition.

### From Section 1 and 2: Mercy as character quality — and its absence

Mercy is a settled inner quality of character; its absence is a diagnostic sign of deeper collapse.

> **Mat 5:7** — *Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy.*
>
> The Beatitude addresses not acts but persons — "the merciful" (*hoi eleēmones*), those for whom mercy is a habitual character. The reciprocal promise is structurally significant: the passive "shall receive mercy" likely names divine mercy rather than human reciprocation, suggesting that merciful character creates the receptivity for divine mercy. The Beatitudes belong to an eschatological register — the future promise is not merely ethical but eschatological, naming the condition of those who stand rightly before God. Mercy is set alongside purity of heart, peacemaking, and poverty of spirit as a coherent inner-being portrait of the person formed by God.

> **Heb 2:17** — *Therefore he had to be made like his brothers in every respect, so that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in the service of God, to make propitiation for the sins of the people.*
>
> Christ becomes *eleēmōn* — the merciful person — through incarnation. This encodes a formation principle: merciful character is not innate but produced through participation in vulnerability. The pairing with *pistos* (faithful) as the two co-requisite high-priestly qualities names mercy and faithfulness as structural complements in the mediating role. Mercy without faithfulness would be indulgent; faithfulness without mercy would be harsh. The verse then immediately states the purpose: "to make propitiation" — the merciful character is the inner ground from which the atoning act flows.

> **Rom 1:31** — *foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless.*
>
> Paul's catalogue of inner moral collapse ends with *aneleēmōn* — merciless, ruthless. The four terms name the complete dissolution of inner life: foolishness (cognitive disorientation), faithlessness (broken relational loyalty), heartlessness (loss of natural affection), mercilessness (the face of relational death). Mercilessness is the terminal item — the relational face of the whole collapse. Its pairing with heartlessness (*astorgos* — without natural affection) is significant: the absence of mercy and the absence of natural family-love appear as companion failures, both forms of the inner relational capacity extinguished.

### From Section 2: The experience of receiving mercy as the source of giving it

What feeds mercy is the experience of having received it.

> **Mat 18:33** — *And should not you have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I had mercy on you?*
>
> The parable of the unmerciful servant turns on this question. The servant who was forgiven an impossible debt — ten thousand talents — refuses to forgive a hundred denarii. Having received mercy, the servant possessed the experiential knowledge of what it was to be in need and to be released. That knowledge should have produced mercy. The failure is not merely moral but spiritual: mercy received at depth reshapes the inner person; mercy that passes through without being truly received leaves the inner character unchanged. The parable diagnoses this as a failure of genuine encounter with mercy — the mercy was received but not integrated.

> **1Pe 2:10** — *Once you were not a people, but now you are God's people; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.*
>
> Peter frames the new identity of the believer in terms of mercy received. The before-and-after structure is not merely biographical but ontological: having received mercy is constitutive of who this person now is. The verse directly reverses the prophetic name Lo-Ruhamah — "No Mercy" (Hos 1:6). Those who were defined by mercy's absence are now defined by mercy received. Receiving mercy is an identity-constituting event, not simply a benefit — it changes the person's standing and their name.

### From Section 2: Mercy shown in action — and as the ground of divine relationship

> **Mat 23:23** — *Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness. These you ought to have done, without neglecting the others.*
>
> Jesus names mercy as one of three *weightier matters* of the law — alongside justice and faithfulness. The comparison to Micah 6:8 ("do justice, love mercy, walk humbly") is instructive: both passages name mercy alongside justice as co-constitutive of covenantal character, suggesting this triad is a consistent prophetic portrait of the inner life ordered rightly before God. Mercy is not a supplement to righteousness but one of its structural elements.

> **Jam 3:17** — *But the wisdom from above is first pure, then peaceable, gentle, open to reason, full of mercy and good fruits, impartial and sincere.*
>
> Wisdom from above is "full of mercy" — mercy is not an add-on to wisdom but constitutive of it. The person who is genuinely wise understands the human condition, is not armoured by self-righteousness, and therefore remains open to the need of others. Wisdom that produces no mercy is not wisdom from above; it is the counterfeit wisdom James has described as earthly, unspiritual, demonic.

### From Section 1: Mercy and atonement — the structures that make mercy possible

> **Lev 17:11** — *For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life.*
>
> The verse grounds the atonement system in an explicit principle: life (*nephesh*) addresses life (*nephesh*). The blood carrying life makes atonement for the soul-life of the person. Both the offering and its recipient are named as *nephesh* — the inner person is the specific target of the atoning blood, not merely the ritual community. God both specifies the mechanism and provides it: "I have given it for you." The atonement is a divine provision directed at the inner person's moral condition before God.

> **Lev 16:14** — *And he shall take some of the blood of the bull and sprinkle it with his finger on the front of the mercy seat on the east side, and in front of the mercy seat he shall sprinkle some of the blood with his finger seven times.*
>
> The sevenfold blood-sprinkling on the mercy seat is both liturgical precision and symbolic completeness — seven as the number of fullness names the covering as total and unreserved. The act is intensely physical: finger, blood, seven counts. But its target is the relational standing of the whole people before God. The body and its products are the instruments through which the spirit-level transaction is conducted. No covering is partial; the sevenfold sprinkling enacts comprehensiveness.

> **Exo 25:22** — *There I will meet with you, and from above the mercy seat, from between the two cherubim that are on the ark of the testimony, I will speak with you about all that I will give you in commandment for the people of Israel.*
>
> The mercy seat is not only the place of atonement but the place of divine speech. God speaks *from* above the mercy seat — divine communication is mercy-seat communication. This is architecturally significant: the innermost location of Israel's worship, where propitiation is effected, is also the location from which God's word is given. Mercy and revelation share a location. The human person can receive God's word only from within the mercy-provided standing — access to God's communication requires the mercy-seat ground.

> **Lev 23:27** — *Now on the tenth day of this seventh month is the Day of Atonement. It shall be for you a time of holy convocation, and you shall afflict yourselves and present a food offering to the Lord.*
>
> The Day of Atonement requires self-affliction of the *nephesh* — "you shall afflict your souls/lives." The fasting is bodily but its target is the inner person. The annual institution prevents Israel from ignoring the corporate inner-moral condition before God. The Day of Atonement is itself an act of mercy: God provides the occasion and the mechanism, requiring only the inner participation — acknowledgement, humility, the willingness to come.

> **Psa 78:38** — *Yet he, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often and did not stir up all his wrath.*
>
> The causal sequence is explicit: inner compassion (*rachum* — womb-love) → act of atonement → restraint of wrath. The atonement does not arise from juridical mechanism alone but from God's inner character of compassion. He could have destroyed; he did not. He could have let his wrath be fully expressed; he restrained it — not once but repeatedly. This verse is the primary evidence for the mercy-atonement causal structure: the atoning act is the expression of inner mercy, not its precondition.

### From Section 1 and 2: Propitiation — the New Testament fulfilment

> **Luk 18:13** — *But the tax collector, standing far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner!'*
>
> The tax collector's posture encodes the whole inner experience of the person who comes to God in need of mercy through three simultaneous somatic acts: standing far off (spatial distance expressing moral distance), refusing to lift his eyes (the downcast posture of shame before God), and beating his breast (the body identifying with guilt). The cry itself (*hilasthēti moi* — "let propitiation be turned toward me") is a plea for the mercy-seat work to be done for him personally. The somatic and the theological are inseparable here: the body enacts precisely what the inner person knows about itself. This posture likely recurs in the repentance and humility vocabularies as a composite inner-being posture of total dependence.

> **Rom 3:25** — *whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith. This was to show God's righteousness, because in his divine forbearance he had passed over former sins.*
>
> Christ is put forward as *hilastērios* — the mercy seat, the propitiation. Three structural moves: God puts forward (divine initiative), by his blood (the atoning instrument in the person of Christ), to be received by faith (the NT mode of mercy-seat access, replacing priestly mediation). The architectural mercy seat of Israel's worship is now the living Christ — and access is personal, immediate, and through faith. Faith is the NT mechanism of mercy-seat approach.

> **1Jo 2:2** — *He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world.*
>
> Christ is identified as the *hilasmos* — the provision of propitiation itself. The scope is deliberately universal: the mercy provision is comprehensive; its reception is personal. The eschatological reach of mercy has moved from the covenant community to the whole world. What was bounded by covenant and repeated annually is now permanent and without boundary.

> **1Jo 4:10** — *In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins.*
>
> The causal chain is stated explicitly: divine love → sending of Son → propitiation provided. Love is the motivation; mercy is love directed toward the guilty. The propitiation is not the cold mechanism by which a legal problem is resolved — it is the warm expression of an inner disposition of love. The sending of the Son is mercy in its most concentrated form: God provides what the human person cannot provide for themselves.

### Supplication: the human inner act of coming for mercy

> **Psa 51:1** — *Have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love; according to your abundant mercy blot out my transgressions.*
>
> Three mercy-terms converge in one verse: *cha.nan* (the cry for grace), *chesed* (the steadfast love appealed to), *rachamim* (the abundant mercies invoked). The supplicant addresses God using the full mercy vocabulary as the ground of appeal — three different registers of mercy simultaneously invoked. The plea is structured entirely by what David knows about who God is, not by any claim David can make. This psalm is the primary cross-registry convergence point for mercy, covenant love, and womb-compassion — the three great mercy vocabularies meeting in a single act of supplication.

> **Num 6:25** — *The Lord make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you.*
>
> The Aaronic blessing invokes *cha.nan* as the inner disposition of God's turned face. The shining face is the spatial metaphor for divine favour; graciousness is the inner quality of that attention. The blessing is a priestly speech-act directing God's gracious inner disposition toward people who cannot secure it for themselves. It encodes the mercy structure in a few words: God turns his face; grace flows from the turning; the people receive what they could not generate.

> **1Ki 8:38** — *whatever prayer, whatever plea is made by any man or by all your people Israel, each knowing the affliction of his own heart and stretching out his hands toward this house.*
>
> Solomon's prayer makes self-knowledge a structural component of authentic supplication: "knowing the affliction of his own heart" (*yada et nega levavo*) — the supplicant must reckon with the inner reality of their own condition before making the appeal. Two somatic elements accompany this: the stretched hands directed toward the temple point toward the mercy seat within. Authentic mercy-seeking requires inner self-knowledge, a somatic posture of appeal, and a theological orientation toward the locus of mercy.

> **Dan 9:20** — *While I was speaking and praying, confessing my sin and the sin of my people Israel, and presenting my plea before the Lord my God for the holy hill of my God.*
>
> Daniel's supplication is inseparable from confession — the *te.chin.nah* arises in the context of moral self-acknowledgement. The prayer is both personal and communal: Daniel brings his own sin and the sin of his people. The phrase "while I was speaking" names the supplication as continuous and active — not a crisis response but a sustained inner posture. Authentic supplication requires ongoing moral honesty, not merely an initial acknowledgement.

### The withdrawal of mercy as theological statement

> **Hos 1:6** — *She conceived again and bore a daughter. And the Lord said to him, 'Call her name No Mercy, for I will no more have mercy on the house of Israel, to forgive them at all.'*
>
> The name *Lo-Ruhamah* embodies a divine judicial act: mercy withdrawn as covenant consequence. The root *racham* — womb-love, the most intimate form of mercy — is negated. The deliberate reversal of the most visceral mercy image measures the extremity of the covenantal failure. The name establishes one pole of the mercy structure: the mercy seat as mercy granted, Lo-Ruhamah as mercy withdrawn. The human person stands between these two possibilities. The NT reversal of this name in 1Pe 2:10 is among the most concentrated summaries of what the gospel accomplishes: those who were Lo-Ruhamah are now a people who have received mercy.

> **Eze 16:63** — *that you may remember and be confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done, declares the Lord God.*
>
> God atones directly — "when I atone for you" — bypassing the priestly mechanism. The inner experience of having received comprehensive mercy for the comprehensively faithless is named here as silencing shame. This is not the shame of condemnation but the reverent silence of the fully forgiven — the person who has nothing left to say for themselves because what was unforgivable has been forgiven. Mercy at its deepest does not produce self-congratulation; it produces the quiet of the person who has been found by grace they did not deserve.

### The compassionate character of God — the ground of all mercy

> **Eph 2:4** — *But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us.*
>
> God is "rich in mercy" — not merely merciful but abundant in it. The richness is a quality of disposition: mercy is not rationed. The causal "because of the great love" states the love-mercy structural relationship: divine love is the motivation; mercy is love directed toward those in need and guilt. The contrast with the preceding passage is stark — the human person is dead in trespasses, following the course of the world, by nature children of wrath. The mercy that meets this condition is the mercy of the one who is rich in it: it is abundant enough for the depth of the condition it addresses.

> **Luk 1:78** — *because of the tender mercy of our God, whereby the sunrise shall visit us from on high.*
>
> Zechariah's Benedictus names the mercy that grounds the coming of Christ as *splagchna eleos* — the tender mercy of God, literally the mercy of his inner organs, his bowels of compassion. The most somatic mercy expression in the NT: mercy felt in the body's depths before it is expressed in action. The image of sunrise — dawn visiting from on high — names mercy as something experienced as light after darkness, warmth after cold. The somatic and the eschatological converge: this visceral divine mercy is the dawning of the messianic age.

> **2Cor 1:3** — *Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort.*
>
> God is "the Father of mercies" — *pater tōn oiktirmōn* — the generative source of all compassionate mercy. The pairing with "God of all comfort" (*paraklēsis*) names the pastoral output: the Father of mercies produces comfort in those who are afflicted. Mercy and comfort are causally connected — mercy is what God is; comfort is what he produces in others from that inner character. The one who receives mercy from the Father of mercies becomes capable of the same comfort toward others (2Cor 1:4 continues this thought).

> **Col 3:12** — *Put on then, as God's chosen ones, holy and beloved, compassionate hearts, kindness, humility, meekness, and patience.*
>
> "Compassionate hearts" — *splagchna oiktirmou*, the inner organs of mercy — is the first and foundational garment of the new humanity. Mercy originates in the body's depths (*splagchna*) and is consciously assumed (*put on*) as the characteristic posture of the person who is "holy and beloved." The identity grounding is crucial: the call to wear mercy arises from having received it. The chosen and beloved are called to clothe themselves in what God himself is — the character-formation command presupposes identity-formation by received mercy. Kindness, humility, meekness, and patience follow mercy as co-garments, but mercy comes first.

### The reciprocal and judicial dynamic of mercy

> **Jam 2:13** — *For judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy. Mercy triumphs over judgment.*
>
> The judicial dimension of mercy is stated precisely. First clause: the person who withheld mercy receives judgment without mercy (*anileōs*) — a structural correspondence between inner disposition and judicial outcome. Second clause: "mercy triumphs over judgment" (*katakauchātai* — literally "boasts over, exults over"). Mercy is named as the stronger judicial category — it does not eliminate judgment but overcomes it when it is present. This is the clearest statement in the NT of the mercy-judgment structural relationship: mercy has the final word in the divine economy.

> **Heb 8:12** — *For I will be merciful toward their iniquities, and I will remember their sins no more.*
>
> The new covenant formula names God's propitious disposition (*hileōs*) toward iniquity as the defining character of the new covenant relationship. Two promises: merciful toward iniquities, and sins remembered no more. The forgetting of sin is not divine cognitive failure but a relational commitment — God chooses not to hold what could be held. This is mercy as a sustained, settled relational posture: not a repeated transaction but a permanent stance. The new covenant is characterised by mercy that does not retract.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Registry 111 contains 67 terms in total — 31 owner terms and 34 XREF terms — distributed across Hebrew (48 terms) and Greek (19 terms). The sharing ratio of 0.71 indicates a highly connected word: the majority of the terms in this registry are shared with adjacent word studies, primarily compassion (38 shared terms), pray (14), love (13), and grace (10). This reflects the structural reality that mercy is not a self-contained characteristic but a relational and theological one — its vocabulary overlaps substantially with the vocabulary of grace, covenant love, atonement, and prayer.

**The Greek mercy family: *eleos*, *eleeō*, *eleēmōn* (G1656, G1653, G1655)**

The central Greek term is *eleos* (G1656), occurring 240 times in the New Testament with the primary sense of mercy, pity, and the moral quality of compassionate response to need. Mounce defines it as "the moral quality of feeling compassion and especially of showing kindness toward someone in need" — capturing both the inner feeling and the outward act. Crucially, *eleos* is the primary Septuagint translation of both *chesed* (Hebrew covenantal steadfast love) and *rachamim* (Hebrew womb-compassion). This LXX bridging function means the NT mercy vocabulary inherits both the covenant-loyalty dimension of *chesed* and the visceral intimacy dimension of *rachamim*. Two phase 2 flags confirm its breadth: RELATIONAL_DIRECTION (mercy always flows toward a recipient) and SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH (covering both human and divine mercy, both felt compassion and concrete relief).

The verb *eleeō* (G1653) — "to have mercy, to show mercy" — occurs 131 times. The same verb operates across three registers: God's sovereign act of mercy (Rom 9:15), the desperate cry of the supplicant to Jesus, and the expected human disposition toward others (Mat 18:33). Its range across divine sovereignty, human need, and human obligation makes it the most structurally dynamic term in the family.

The adjective *eleēmōn* (G1655) — "merciful, compassionate" — occurs 20 times and names mercy as settled character quality. Mounce notes that the word includes feelings of pity "with a focus on showing compassion to those in serious need." It appears in the Beatitude on mercy (Mat 5:7) and in the description of Christ as merciful high priest (Heb 2:17).

**The Greek compassion family: *oiktirmos*, *oiktirmōn* (G3628, G3629)**

*Oiktirmos* (G3628) — "compassion, mercy, pity" — occurs 36 times. The term derives from the verb *oiktirō* (to pity, to feel compassion for — LSJ from 5th c. BC), with *oiktirmōn* (G3629, 15 occurrences) as its adjectival form. The noun-adjective pair names the same quality as abstract character and as personal trait. In its NT plural, *oiktirmoi* names "compassionate feelings, mercies" — the aggregate of compassionate acts and dispositions. Its most significant uses are in 2Cor 1:3 ("the Father of mercies") and Col 3:12 ("compassionate hearts" — *splagchna oiktirmou*), where the compound with *splagchna* (bowels/inner organs) identifies the body's interior as the location from which compassion arises.

**The propitiation family: *hilaskomai*, *hilasmos*, *hilastērios*, *hileōs* (G2433, G2434, G2435, G2436)**

This family names the structural-theological dimension of mercy — the mechanism by which mercy operates in the presence of moral guilt.

*Hilaskomai* (G2433, 15 occurrences) has two structurally distinct senses: (1) middle voice — to make atonement for, to propitiate, providing the means for forgiveness and reconciliation (Heb 2:17); (2) passive voice — to receive mercy, to be forgiven, to have propitiation applied (Luk 18:13 — "God, be propitious toward me"). The same verb names both the active provision of propitiation and the experienced reception of mercy. This voice alternation is theologically significant, and the classical background illuminates it further: in classical Greek, *hilaskomai* always named the human act of appeasing the deity. The NT radically inverts this direction — it is God who provides the propitiation (1Jo 4:10), not the human person who offers it. Human mercy-seeking (passive) and divine mercy-provision (mediated through Christ's active atoning work) are both encoded in the same term.

*Hilasmos* (G2434, 8 occurrences) names the provision itself — the atoning sacrifice, the means of forgiveness. In 1Jo 2:2 and 1Jo 4:10, Christ is identified *as* the hilasmos: not the one who performs propitiation but the substance of it.

*Hilastērios* (G2435, 32 occurrences) bridges the spatial and the personal: it names the mercy seat in Heb 9:5, and Christ as living propitiation in Rom 3:25. The SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH flag captures this dual sense — the term carries the mercy-seat imagery directly into its NT application. The mercy seat is not replaced but fulfilled; Christ is the hilastērios received by faith rather than approached through annual priestly ritual.

*Hileōs* (G2436, 29 occurrences) names the settled disposition of being propitious — God's inner quality of being disposed toward mercy and forgiveness (Heb 8:12). Where *hilaskomai* names the act and *hilasmos* the provision, *hileōs* names the prior disposition from which both flow.

**The Hebrew atonement family: *kip.per*, *ka.phar*, *kap.po.ret*, *kip.pu.rim*, *ko.pher* (H3722A, H3722B, H3727, H3725, H3724A)**

The Hebrew root *kap.har* — "to cover" — underlies this family. The primary verb *kip.per* (H3722A, 102 occurrences) means "to cover, purge, make atonement, make reconciliation." Three groups of usage: (1) the priestly-ritual act (human covering of guilt through sacrifice); (2) the direct divine act of covering/forgiving (Psa 78:38 — God atones directly, driven by inner compassion); (3) interpersonal appeasement — covering relational offence between persons. The last usage illuminates the root's base meaning: Jacob plans to "cover the face" of Esau (Gen 32:20) — appeasement is literally face-covering, restoring the ability to face the other person. This face-vocabulary connects *kip.per* to the grace-vocabulary of *cha.nan*: the God whose face shines in grace (Num 6:25) and the person whose face is covered in appeasement (Gen 32:20) share the same spatial logic of face-direction.

The *kap.po.ret* (H3727, 27 occurrences) — the mercy seat — is architecturally singular in the programme: it names mercy as a *place*. All other terms name dispositions, acts, or qualities. The mercy seat has an address — the innermost room, accessible once yearly. In Exo 25:22, God speaks *from* above the mercy seat. The place of atonement is also the place of divine speech: the mercy-seat ground is the condition of divine-human communication.

*Ko.pher* (H3724A, 13 occurrences) names the ransom price — the substitutionary payment for a life. Two uses: (1) legitimate ransom (Job 33:24 — "I have found a ransom"), where the covering addresses moral accountability for life; (2) illicit bribe (1Sa 12:3 — bribe that "blinds the eyes"), where the covering-payment corrupts the inner moral vision of the one who receives it. The polarity is instructive: the same covering-payment can either restore justice (ransom) or corrupt it (bribe), depending on whether it is directed toward legitimate moral accountability or away from it.

**The Hebrew gracious-favour family: *cha.nan*, *te.chin.nah* (H2603A, H8467)**

*Cha.nan* (H2603A, 81 occurrences) is structurally remarkable: the same verbal root names both the granting of grace and the seeking of it. Qal: God shows favour. Hithpael: the person seeks favour, implores. The giver and the seeker share a root — the human posture of mercy-seeking is shaped by and oriented toward the divine posture of mercy-giving. The root also generates *chinnam* (H2600, "for nothing, without cause, freely") — the word used in Job 2:3 when God permits destroying Job "without reason." The gratuitousness is shared: grace is given freely; suffering, in Job's case, comes freely. The same root holds both unmerited favour and unmerited affliction.

The noun *te.chin.nah* (H8467, 25 occurrences) names "supplication for favour" — the act of earnest pleading for grace. It reveals a consistent structure: supplication arises from *yada et nega levavo* — knowing the affliction of one's own heart — and it is directed toward the one who has the power to grant favour. The form of supplication before God (Dan 9:20, 1Ki 8:38) and before human authority (Jer 37:20) are lexically identical: the inner posture of dependence before one who holds power over the supplicant's condition.

**The Hebrew compassion family: *ra.cha.mim*, *Lo-Ruhamah* (H7359, H3819)**

*Ra.cha.min* (H7359, Aramaic, 1 occurrence) names divine compassion as the ground of appeal in Dan 2:18 — the most intimate Hebrew mercy vocabulary applied to the God of all nations in a crisis of extremity. The Aramaic form carries the womb-etymology directly: *racham* from *rechem* (womb). To seek divine *ra.cha.min* is to appeal to the most visceral form of compassionate attachment.

*Lo-Ruhamah* (H3819) — "No Mercy" — is the prophetic name that defines mercy by its negation. Its presence signals that mercy's full meaning requires understanding its withdrawal. Mercy known in its fullness is mercy that could have been withheld.

**Synthesis observation**

The complete vocabulary of this registry maps a semantic arch: from the innermost movement of mercy — womb-love, tender visceral compassion — through its expression as favour and gracious turning toward a person, through the structural mechanism that makes mercy operative in the presence of guilt (atonement, propitiation, ransom, the mercy seat), to the New Testament fulfilment in Christ as the living propitiation received by faith. The vocabulary is not a collection of synonyms; it describes mercy as a complete system — an inner disposition with a source (God's compassionate character), a mechanism (atonement addressing the condition that prevents mercy from being fully given), and an act (the specific extension of grace toward the person in need). The prayer vocabulary names the human side of this system: the inner act by which the person who knows their own need turns toward the source of mercy and asks. Taken together, the vocabulary presents mercy not as one virtue among many but as the fundamental structure of the divine-human relationship — the characteristic that makes access to God possible and makes the God-shaped inner life possible in return.
