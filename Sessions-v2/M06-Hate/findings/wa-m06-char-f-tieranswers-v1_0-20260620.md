**T0.2.3** — **SILENT** — no future-fullness orientation.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Programme name: *cruelty / ruthlessness* — a dispositional character quality. The name signals a **settled trait of character**, not an episode or relation.

**T1.1.2** — `a.rits` "ruthless", `akh.zar`/`akh.za.ri`/`akh.ze.riy.yut` "cruel/cruelty", `hubrizō` "mistreat/insult", `sho.tet` "scourge", `afilagathos` "hating good". Definitionally: **the disposition to inflict harm unmoved by the victim's suffering**.

**T1.1.3** — Carries a **characterological** implication — it describes *what a person is* (a quality), engaging the relational direction only as the trait acts outward.

**T1.2.1** — A **quality** (quality 31 — the dominant type), with marginal **act** (`hubrizō`, action 5) and **status** (`sho.tet`/`akh.ze.riy.yut`, 2) forms.

**T1.2.2** — Mixed at the faculty level: `a.rits` "ruthless" is **affect**-tagged (an inner ferocity, 20), while the `akhzar`-family is **faculty-absent** (the trait named from outside). So: a quality with a partial affective core.

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: **mercy / compassion** (`rachamim`; the compassion cluster) — Pro 12:10 sets "tender mercies" against the cruelty of the wicked; `afilagathos` "hating good" inverts `philagathos` "loving good".

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes mercy, tenderness, and being-moved by another's suffering.

**T1.3.3** — It is *not* a relational role (C-E) nor a felt episode (C-A): cruelty is a **standing character trait** (quality), the disposition behind hostile acts.

**T1.4.1** — Mode: realised chiefly as **adjective/quality** ("the ruthless", "a cruel man" — quality 31), with NT verb `hubrizō` (to mistreat). Manner: **a fixed disposition expressed in harm**.

**T1.4.2** — Varies by **register**: the OT "ruthless/cruel" enemy or person (a trait — Pro 12:10; Isa 13:11; Jer 6:23) vs the NT **act** of insolent mistreatment (`hubrizō` — Luk 18:32; 1Th 2:2). Trait (OT) vs act (NT).

**T1.4.3** — Partial: `hubrizō` includes **insolent speech/mistreatment** (a communicative-aggressive mode). `afilagathos` appears in a vice-list (2Ti 3:3). Otherwise none.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response (8 occ): destruction, seeking-life, desolation, spitting/stoning/mistreatment (refs above). **T1.5.2** — **Consistent in kind** — the responses uniformly involve **infliction of harm/destruction**.

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effect: the **enduring trait itself** (a quality), and the lasting devastation it works (Jos 23:13 "until you perish"; Isa 13:9 desolation).

**T1.6.3** — The sustained effect is the **settled cruel character**; the immediate response is its episodic act (a stoning, a desolation).

**T1.7.1** — **SILENT** — no "right operation" conditions (cruelty has no righteous use; valence sinful 33 / neutral 5).

**T1.7.2** — Distortion conditions: cruelty arises where mercy is absent (Pro 12:10) and in judgement-instrument enemies (Jer 6:23; Isa 13). No adversarial-spirit tag (though `sho.tet` "scourge" names an affliction-instrument).

**T1.7.3** — **SILENT**.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms: `a.rits` "ruthless" (20), `akh.za.ri`/`akh.zar`/`akh.ze.riy.yut` "cruel/cruelty" (11), `hubrizō` "mistreat" (5). Root sense: violent/ruthless disposition; merciless infliction of harm.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: chiefly **adjective/quality** (the ruthless/cruel — quality 31), an abstract **noun** (`akh.ze.riy.yut` "cruelty"), and a **verb** (`hubrizō`). The dominance of the adjectival/quality form shows cruelty is named primarily as **a property of persons**.

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: from the ruthless conqueror/enemy (Isa 13:11; Jer 6:23) through the cruel individual (Pro 12:10) to insolent mistreatment (`hubrizō`, NT) and the vice "hating good" (`afilagathos`).

**T7.1.4** — Yes — **quality/disposition** (`a.rits`, `akhzar`) vs **act** (`hubrizō`) vs **abstract condition** (`akh.ze.riy.yut` "cruelty"). Disposition / act / condition distinguished.

**T7.1.5** — Opposite term: **mercy/compassion** (`rachamim`) — Pro 12:10 explicitly contrasts; `philagathos` "loving good" inverts `afilagathos`.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term: **yes — clearly** — `a.rits` "the ruthless one" and `akh.za.ri` "the cruel one" *are* person-type terms naming the habitual possessor of the trait. C-F is the M06 characteristic with the strongest person-type vocabulary.

**T7.1.7** — Supplication/seeking term: **none**.

**T7.1.8** — OT↔NT: the trait (`a.rits`/`akhzar`, OT) continues as the **act** (`hubrizō`, NT) and the **vice** (`afilagathos`, NT) — a shift from naming the *character* to naming the *behaviour/vice* across Testaments. 6 of 38 NT.

**T7.1.9** — Newly-coined NT term: **`afilagathos`** ("hating/without love of good", 2Ti 3:3) is a rare compound vice-term — its coinage shows cruelty conceptualised in the NT as the **privation of love-of-good**.

**T7.1.10** — The vocabulary arc shows cruelty operating from **ruthless character → cruel act → vice of hating-good** — consistently a property/behaviour opposed to mercy.

**T7.2.1** — Function: typically an **adjective qualifying a subject** ("the ruthless", "a cruel messenger", Pro 17:11) — predicating the trait of a person/nation.

**T7.2.2** — Literary form: **prophecy 17, wisdom 10, psalm 3, gospel 3, narrative-history 2, epistle 2, lament 1**. The **prophetic** (the ruthless enemy/instrument of judgement) and **wisdom** (the cruel vs the merciful) forms dominate and frame interpretation.

**T7.2.3** — Logical structure: in wisdom, *the wicked's "mercy" is itself cruel* (Pro 12:10) — a moral contrast premise; in prophecy, *because* of sin, a ruthless nation is brought as judgement (Isa 13; Jer 6).

**T7.2.4** — Settings: **wisdom-moral** (Pro 11:17; 12:10), **prophetic-judgement** (Isa 13; Jer 6; 50), **persecution-narrative** (Luk 18:32; Act 14:5; 1Th 2:2).

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor (candidate, **authorial judgement**): **Pro 12:10** — "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" (`akhzari`) — it names cruelty as a **character trait**, sets it against mercy (its opposite), and exposes it as the wicked's defining disposition, all in one wisdom saying. (Jer 6:23 anchors the ruthless-enemy image.)

**T7.2.6** — That anchor shows what no other C-F verse shows: cruelty as the **inner character of the wicked**, contrasted directly with mercy — locating it as a disposition, not merely an act.

**T7.3.1** — Field (science §3): **clinical / social psychology** — hostility, aggression, and cruelty.

**T7.3.2** — Where science illuminates: the **trait-vs-act** distinction (dispositional hostility; reactive vs proactive aggression — Berkowitz) maps C-F's quality-dominant form with its NT act-form (`hubrizō`); **Bandura's moral disengagement** illuminates how cruelty is sustained "unmoved by suffering" — matching the discovery's reading of the trait. **Baumeister** (perpetrator/victim gap) frames the cluster's persecution narratives.

**T7.3.3** — Divergence: science treats cruelty as a **disorder/dysfunction** (dark triad, psychopathy); the biblical material frames it **morally** as the privation of mercy and (uniquely) provides **no divine instance** — cruelty is the one M06 trait with no holy analogue, a categorical theological point science does not make.

**T7.3.4** — Gap surfaced: the **reactive/proactive** distinction prompts checking whether C-F's cruelty is situationally triggered or characterologically rooted — the data leans **characterological** (quality-dominant), with the NT `hubrizō` cases the more act-like; the `hubrizō`/`sho.tet` placement flags from discovery are the verse-level items to resolve.
