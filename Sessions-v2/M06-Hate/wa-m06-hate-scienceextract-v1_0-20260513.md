## Science sections

### Section 1 — Hatred as emotional state

**Primary field:** Social psychology / affective science

**Glosses covered:**
hating (sin.ah), hated (sa.ni), to hate (sa.tam), to hate (sa.ne), to hate (se.na), hatred (mas.te.mah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Sternberg's duplex theory of hate (*The Psychology of Hate*, 2003) proposes that hate is composed of three elements: negation of intimacy (disgust and revulsion that pushes the other away), passion (anger and fear in the presence of the hated object), and devaluation/diminution (contempt — cognitive devaluation of the other's worth). Different combinations produce different types of hate — from cool disdain to burning rage. Intergroup hatred research (Mackie, Devos, Smith) treats group-based hate as arising from perceived threat to in-group values, identity, or welfare. Unlike anger, which is typically activated and action-oriented, hatred is often a sustained, dispositional orientation toward its object.

**Emergence — what causes hatred to arise:**
Hatred emerges from sustained or extreme exposure to what is perceived as deeply wrong, threatening, or repugnant. It differs from anger in being more enduring and less responsive to the resolution of specific incidents — hatred becomes a stable orientation rather than a reactive episode. It typically develops through: repeated aversive encounters with the hated person or group; the interpretation of those encounters through a devaluing framework; and the social reinforcement of hate within a group context. Dehumanisation is frequently a co-condition — the hated other is deprived of full human status.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hatred to diminish:**
Hatred is among the most resistant of the negative emotions to reduction. Interventions that work for anger (reappraisal, empathy induction, conflict resolution) are less effective when the negative orientation has crystallised into stable hate. Contact theory (Allport) — structured positive contact between hated groups — shows some effectiveness under conditions of equal status, cooperative goals, institutional support, and personal acquaintance. Deradicalisation programmes address extreme group-based hate with mixed results. Genuine transformation of hatred typically involves a significant reappraisal of the hated person or group's humanity.

**Location — where hatred is seated:**
Hatred is primarily emotional and cognitive, with a strong motivational dimension (hatred inclines toward avoidance of or aggression toward its object). It is less somatically acute than rage but more cognitively entrenched — it involves a stable evaluative framework that organises perception of the hated other. The relational dimension is significant: hatred is always directed toward someone and shapes all perception of and interaction with that person.

**External influences:**
*Social and group context:* Hatred is rarely generated in isolation — it is typically sustained and amplified by group membership, shared narrative about the hated other, and social reward for expressing hate. *Developmental:* Parental and community modelling of hatred toward specific groups shapes children's hate formation from early childhood (social learning). *Circumstances:* Perceived threat (to identity, welfare, or status) by the hated group is the primary circumstantial driver. Scarcity, competition, and political manipulation of intergroup perception all increase hatred. *Hereditary:* No specific heritability for hatred as such; underlying traits (authoritarianism, low openness, high disgust sensitivity) that predispose toward hatred have some heritable components.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Hatred is less researched as a primary emotion than love, fear, or anger. Sternberg's duplex theory is the most systematic framework available.

### Section 2 — Contempt and disgust

**Primary field:** Moral psychology / emotion research

**Glosses covered:**
contempt (buz), contempt (biz.za.von), contempt (ne.a.tsah), derision (shim.tsah), scorn (she.at), to despise (ba.zah), to despise (shut), to spurn (na.ats), to stink (ba.ash), detestation (she.qets), abhorrence (de.ra.on), to abhor (bdelussomai), to abhor (ta.av), to loathe (ta.av)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Contempt and disgust are distinct emotions with different elicitors, expressions, and relational consequences. Contempt (Gottman, *Why Marriages Succeed or Fail*, 1994) is the single most destructive emotion in close relationships — it communicates the perception of the other as fundamentally inferior, beneath consideration, and not worthy of engagement. Disgust (Haidt, Rozin, *Disgust: The Body and Soul Emotion*, 2007) is rooted in contamination aversion and extends from bodily disgust to moral and social disgust. The cluster's vocabulary conflates these two distinct phenomena (contemptuous dismissal and disgust-based loathing), which is analytically important for Session B to disentangle.

**Emergence — what causes contempt and disgust to arise:**
Contempt arises from a hierarchical social evaluation in which the other is placed below the contemptuous person on a scale of worth, competence, or virtue. It does not require anger — it can be entirely cold. Disgust arises from contact with (or perceived contact with) contaminating objects, people, or acts. Moral disgust extends the contamination model to moral violations — acts or persons perceived as violating purity norms or basic human decency. The stink vocabulary (ba.ash — to stink) reflects the olfactory-disgust basis of the emotion before it extends into the moral domain.

**Reduction and absence — what causes contempt and disgust to diminish:**
Contempt is particularly resistant to reduction because it involves a stable evaluative hierarchy rather than a situational emotional reaction. It can be reduced by information that revises the hierarchical assessment (evidence of the despised person's worth or capability) or by deliberate empathy practices. Disgust reduces through habituation to previously disgusting stimuli (exposure therapy for disgust-based phobias and OCD). Moral disgust is more cognitively resistant — it is maintained by moral frameworks that define certain acts or persons as impure.

**Location — where contempt and disgust are seated:**
Contempt is primarily cognitive and social — a stable evaluative stance expressed in the face and in interaction patterns (the contemptuous eye roll, the dismissive tone). It has limited somatic intensity but profound relational consequences. Disgust is more somatically rooted — it begins in the gut (nausea, revulsion) and extends outward into the cognitive and moral domain. The cluster's dual vocabulary (both cold contempt and visceral loathing) reflects these two distinct phenomena.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Contempt norms and their social targets are heavily culturally determined — who is considered beneath consideration varies across social structures (caste, class, honour-shame systems). Disgust sensitivity varies cross-culturally, though core disgust elicitors (bodily fluids, rotting matter) are cross-culturally consistent. *Developmental:* Contempt is largely learned through social modelling. High-contempt households produce children with high contempt orientation. Disgust sensitivity develops in childhood through socialisation (parental responses to disgusting stimuli). *Circumstances:* Status hierarchies, social power differentials, and competition between groups increase contempt along power lines.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Contempt and disgust are well-researched in moral psychology and emotion science.

### Section 3 — Hostility, aggression, and antagonism

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be hostile (a.yav), cruel (akh.zar), cruel (akh.za.ri), cruel (akh.ze.riy.yut), ruthless (a.rits), opponent (antidikos), opponent (ya.riv), adversary (qim), to rival (tsa.rar), to mistreat (hubrizō), scourge (sho.tet), to watch with envy (ra.tsad)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Aggression research (Berkowitz, *Aggression*, 1993; Bandura, social learning theory) distinguishes reactive aggression (hot, impulsive, triggered by provocation) from proactive aggression (cold, instrumental, used to achieve goals). Hostility as a personality trait (the cognitive component of the Type A behaviour pattern — Dembroski, Williams) involves a chronic negative orientation toward others — the expectation that others will frustrate, deceive, or harm one. Cruelty research addresses the deliberate infliction of suffering: Baumeister (*Evil: Inside Human Violence and Cruelty*, 1997) identifies the gap between perpetrator and victim perspectives as a key analytical problem. Dark triad research (psychopathy, Machiavellianism, narcissism) addresses cold, proactive, instrumental hostility.

**Emergence — what causes hostility and cruelty to arise:**
Hostility arises from: chronic frustration and unmet needs; a dispositional negative attribution style (attributing hostile intent to others' ambiguous actions — the hostile attribution bias); experiences of victimisation that prime vigilance for threat; and social environments that reward aggressive dominance. Cruelty specifically arises when: the pain of others is perceived as deserved, satisfying, or instrumental; the victim is dehumanised; and normal moral inhibitions are disengaged (Bandura's moral disengagement mechanisms). The cluster's cruel vocabulary (akh.zar, akh.za.ri) names not just aggressive acts but the character disposition of one who is unmoved by the suffering they inflict.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hostility to diminish:**
Hostility reduces through: secure, trustworthy relationships that revise the expectation that others are threatening; cognitive restructuring of the hostile attribution bias; anger management and emotional regulation training; and community and accountability structures that sanction aggressive behaviour. Absence of hostility in provocation contexts is associated with secure attachment, high empathy, and strong moral self-regulation.

**Location — where hostility is seated:**
Hostility is primarily characterological — it is a stable orientation rather than a situational response. It is cognitively located (hostile attribution bias, negative expectation of others) with motivational and behavioural consequences (aggressive action, dominance-seeking). The cruelty dimension has an affective component — either affective coldness (psychopathic cruelty) or sadistic satisfaction (reactive cruelty) — but the defining feature is the character disposition rather than any particular feeling.

**External influences:**
*Developmental and parenting:* Harsh, punitive, and abusive parenting is among the strongest predictors of hostile and aggressive character development. Childhood experiences of victimisation — being subjected to cruelty — are associated with both trauma responses and with learning cruelty as a relational template. *Hereditary:* Genetic variants associated with the monoamine oxidase A (MAOA) gene influence aggression reactivity, particularly in the context of childhood abuse. *Cultural:* Cultures with norms of honour, retaliation, and aggressive dominance show higher rates of reactive hostility. *Circumstances:* Environments characterised by competition, scarcity, and social hierarchy with no legitimate routes to status increase hostile and aggressive behaviour.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Aggression and hostility are among the most studied negative behaviour patterns in clinical and social psychology.

### Section 4 — Social rejection and reproach

**Primary field:** Social psychology — ostracism and rejection research

**Glosses covered:**
reproach (cher.pah), to taunt (cha.raph), hating good (afilagathos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Social rejection research (Leary, Baumeister — need to belong; Williams — ostracism, *Ostracism: The Power of Silence*, 2001) demonstrates that social exclusion and rejection activate the same neural circuits as physical pain (Eisenberger — dorsal anterior cingulate cortex). Reproach and taunting function as instruments of social degradation — they communicate that the reproached person has failed by the community's standards and is thereby diminished in social standing. Taunt and reproach are relational weapons that combine cognitive devaluation with public humiliation.

**Emergence — what causes reproach and taunting to arise:**
Reproach arises when a community member is perceived as having failed a norm that the reproacher considers important. Taunting adds a performance dimension — it is public, designed to humiliate, and directed at an audience as much as at the target. Both arise from the combination of moral judgment (the other has done wrong) and social power (the reproacher has the standing to pronounce judgment publicly).

**Reduction and absence — what causes reproach to cease:**
Reproach ceases when the reproached person is perceived as having adequately responded to their failure — through acknowledgement, restitution, or rehabilitation. It can also cease when the social power dynamics shift (the reproacher loses the standing to reproach) or when the community's norms change. Taunting as an instrument of cruelty ceases when the taunter loses their audience or their target.

**Location — where reproach is seated:**
Relational and social — reproach exists in the space between persons and communities, not within an individual inner state. Its effects are felt as social pain (by the reproached person) and moral superiority (by the reproacher). It has a strong public and performative dimension.

**External influences:**
*Social and cultural context:* The power of reproach is entirely dependent on the social context — what counts as a shaming failure, who has the authority to reproach, and what consequences follow are all socially determined. Honour-shame cultures give reproach exceptional power as a social sanction.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Social rejection and ostracism research are well-developed; the specific vocabulary of reproach and taunt has thinner direct science engagement.

## Outside-science inventory

- **adversary (qim) and opponent (antidikos) in their legal and covenantal registers** — where these terms describe a party to a covenant lawsuit or a legal adversary in the divine court, the theological structure (God as prosecuting adversary, the adversary who brings accusation before God) is outside science's sociological account of opposition.
- **hatred as divine response to evil** — science addresses hate as a human psychological phenomenon; the biblical material includes divine hatred of sin and evil as a holy, ontologically grounded response. Science does not adjudicate holy hatred.

**Observation:** M06's outside-science content is concentrated at the divine-attribute end. Human hatred, contempt, hostility, and reproach are covered by science — often more thoroughly for their pathological manifestations than for the moral-relational analysis the biblical material provides.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology and affective science (hate — Section 1); moral psychology (contempt and disgust — Section 2); clinical and social psychology (hostility — Section 3); social rejection research (Section 4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Gottman's contempt research illuminates the relational destructiveness of the cluster's contempt vocabulary — contempt as a relationship-destroyer rather than merely a negative feeling. The disgust/moral disgust distinction illuminates the dual register of the cluster's abhorrence vocabulary (gut-level revulsion and moral evaluation). Moral disengagement (Bandura) illuminates how cruelty is sustained without self-condemnation — relevant to the cluster's analysis of conscience suppression or inversion.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats hatred, hostility, and contempt primarily as pathological or dysfunctional states to be reduced. The biblical material includes categories of appropriate hatred (hatred of evil, divine hatred of injustice) that science does not have a framework for evaluating as normative. The moral evaluation of hatred — when it is right and when it is wrong — is a theological question that science addresses only instrumentally (adaptive vs maladaptive).

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The distinction between reactive and proactive aggression (Berkowitz) may illuminate differences within the cluster's hostility vocabulary — is the aggression described situationally triggered or characterologically rooted? The contact theory research on reducing intergroup hatred may surface a gap: the cluster's material may include or exclude strategies for hate-reduction that are worth identifying.
