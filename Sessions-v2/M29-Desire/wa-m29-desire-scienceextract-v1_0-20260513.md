## Science sections

### Section 1 — Will, volition, and intentional action

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / philosophy of action

**Glosses covered:**
to will/desire (thelō), will/desire (thelēma), to will (tse.va), be willing (ya.al), be willing (na.dav), be willing (ne.dav), eagerness (prothumia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Heckhausen and Gollwitzer's Rubicon model of action phases (*American Psychologist*, 1987) distinguishes the motivational phase (deliberating between options and forming an intention) from the volitional phase (planning and executing the intended action). Once an intention is formed, a qualitatively different motivational state takes over — the person has 'crossed the Rubicon' and is committed. Libet's controversial neuroscience experiments (*Brain*, 1983) claimed to show that neural preparation for action precedes conscious awareness of the intention to act — sparking ongoing free will debates. Eagerness (prothumia) maps onto the early motivational phase — the state of readiness and forward-leaning engagement before action is taken.

**Emergence — what causes willingness and eagerness to arise:**
Willingness arises when a potential action is evaluated positively and the person forms a settled intention to pursue it. Eagerness arises when the intention is held with positive affect and anticipation — the forward-leaning orientation that Heckhausen distinguishes from mere cold intention. Willingness (ya.al, na.dav) often carries a voluntary and non-coerced quality — the willing response as distinct from the compelled response. In the biblical material, willingness frequently appears in the context of generous giving and worship — freely-chosen actions that exceed what is required.

**Reduction and absence — what causes willingness to diminish:**
Willingness diminishes when the evaluation of the action turns negative, when competing intentions take priority, when the cost is appraised as too high, or when self-regulatory resources are depleted. Akrasia (weakness of will — acting against one's better judgment) represents the failure of volition to execute the intended action despite a formed intention.

**Location — where volition is seated:**
Volition is primarily cognitive — it is a property of the evaluating and intending mind. Neurologically, the prefrontal cortex is the primary site of intentional action planning and executive control. However, volition has an affective dimension — eagerness and willingness are felt states, not merely cognitive computations. The will is experienced as originating from within the self — it is the inner being's own forward-directed energy.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Willingness is shaped by relational context — people are more willing to act when they feel genuinely supported and respected. Coercive pressure tends to convert willing action into reluctant compliance, eliminating the inner quality that makes willingness distinct. *Developmental:* The capacity for deliberate volitional action develops through childhood and adolescence (executive function development). *Cultural:* The value placed on autonomous willing versus obedient following varies by cultural context.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Volitional psychology is a developed field; the cluster is small.

### Section 2 — Desire, longing, and motivational orientation

**Primary field:** Motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
intention (ye.tser), acceptance (ra.tson), one who desires (epithumētēs), to long for (epithumeō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Ye.tser is the Hebrew term for the inner inclination or impulse — the rabbinic tradition developed this into the dual impulse framework (ye.tser ha-tov / ye.tser ha-ra — the good and evil inclination). Psychology's nearest analogue is the dual-process motivation framework (Evans, Kahneman) distinguishing automatic appetitive processing from deliberate value-governed processing. Acceptance (ra.tson — favour, delight, acceptance, goodwill) is a complex term that spans both human acceptance/approval and divine favour — the psychology of acceptance and approval-seeking (social belonging, Baumeister and Leary) covers the human dimension. Epithumeō in the New Testament spans positive longing (desiring spiritual gifts, longing for God) and negative desire (lusting after what is forbidden).

**Emergence — what causes inner inclination and longing to arise:**
Ye.tser as inner inclination arises from the interaction between temperament, habit, developmental history, and situational cues. The rabbinic tradition treats both the good and evil inclination as given with the human person — built-in orientational tendencies that shape desire before deliberate choice. Psychology similarly identifies stable motivational orientations (approach vs avoidance) as trait-like features that persist across situations. Longing (epithumeō in its positive register) arises from the perception of something genuinely good that is not yet possessed — a forward-reaching desire for a real and valued object.

**Reduction and absence — what causes longing to diminish:**
Positive longing is resolved by attainment of the desired object. Negative longing (M28) diminishes through Section 2's mechanisms. The acceptance-seeking dimension (ra.tson) is reduced when genuine acceptance is received — the need for approval diminishes in secure relational contexts.

**Location — where inner inclination is seated:**
Ye.tser as inner inclination is the deepest motivational location in this cluster — it names the pre-deliberate directional tendency of the inner being before it is acted on. It is neither purely cognitive nor purely somatic but represents an integrated motivational orientation. For the programme's framework, this is the closest Hebrew vocabulary comes to naming the inner being's motivational ground — the orientation from which desire, will, and action arise.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Temperamental approach/avoidance orientation has significant heritability. *Developmental:* Habitual patterns of desire are formed through repeated experience — what the person repeatedly attends to and pursues becomes what they incline toward. *Cultural:* Cultural formation shapes what is desirable and what is the appropriate direction for inner inclination.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Ye.tser is analytically significant for the programme; the rabbinic tradition's dual-inclination framework has partial psychological analogues.

### Section 3 — Choice, election, and preference

**Primary field:** Decision psychology / cognitive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
chosen (qa.ri)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Decision-making research (Kahneman — *Thinking Fast and Slow*; Ariely — *Predictably Irrational*) addresses how preferences are formed and expressed in choice behaviour. The psychology of choice paradox (Barry Schwartz — *The Paradox of Choice*) demonstrates that more options reduce satisfaction with the chosen option. Chosen (qa.ri — summoned, called) in its biblical register carries a strong divine-election dimension that exceeds the psychology of preference.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. One gloss; limited direct science coverage.

## Outside-science inventory

- **will/desire (thelēma) in its divine-will register** — God's will as the governing purpose of all things — is a theological category. Science addresses human volition; divine will is outside its scope.
- **acceptance (ra.tson) in its divine-favour register** — the experience of being accepted by God, and God's gracious disposition toward his people — is a theological category.
- **ye.tser in the rabbinic dual-inclination framework** — the good and evil inclination as theological categories of the human person's created structure — are partially accessible to psychology (via dual-process motivation theory) but are more specifically theological anthropological categories.

**Observation:** M29's most analytically significant term for the programme is ye.tser — it names the pre-deliberate motivational orientation of the inner being, which sits at the heart of the programme's interest in how the inner being is characterised and directed. The science cannot fully address this; the verse evidence will be decisive.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Volitional psychology (Heckhausen's Rubicon model) for Section 1; motivational psychology (dual-process, approach/avoidance) for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The distinction between motivated/willing action and coerced/reluctant action illuminates the cluster's consistent interest in the quality of inner disposition — not just what the person does but whether they do it freely and eagerly. Dual-process motivation theory provides the closest analogue to ye.tser as inner inclination.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Divine will (thelēma) as the governing purpose of the universe is outside science. The theological account of the transformed will — the inner being that now wills what God wills through the work of the Spirit — has no science analogue, though positive psychology's work on values-congruent motivation comes closest.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** Whether ye.tser appears in contexts that describe the inclination as directed, misdirected, or transformed will be among the most significant findings for the programme's inner-being map.
