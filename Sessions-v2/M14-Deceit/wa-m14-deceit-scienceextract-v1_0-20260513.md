## Science sections

### Section 1 — Deception and lying

**Primary field:** Social psychology — deception research

**Glosses covered:**
deceit (apatē), deceit (dolos), deceit (mir.mah), deceit (re.miy.yah), deceitful (dolios), deceitfulness (tar.mit), deception (she.qer), to deceive (dolioō), to deceive (ra.mah), to distort (doloō), slackness (re.miy.yah), untrustworthy (asunthetos), false Christ (pseudochristos), false brother (pseudadelfos), false prophet (pseudoprofētēs)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
DePaulo and colleagues (*Lying in Everyday Life*, 1996) documented the frequency and motivation of everyday deception — approximately one to two lies per day in most people, primarily social and self-serving. Vrij (*Detecting Lies and Deceit*, 2008) has examined deception detection, finding that human accuracy in detecting lies is only marginally above chance — we are poorly designed detectors despite our confidence. Levine's truth-default theory explains why: defaulting to belief is adaptive when most communications are honest. Ariely's research on dishonesty identifies the role of self-concept maintenance — people lie in amounts that still allow them to maintain a self-image as honest.

**Emergence — what causes deception to arise:**
Deception arises from: the motivation to obtain something valued (resources, status, approval) that cannot be obtained through honest means; the motivation to avoid negative consequences of truth-telling; and — at the dark triad end — the instrumental use of deception as a social tool regardless of consequences for others. The slackness/negligence dimension of re.miy.yah suggests that deception can arise from a failure of care (carelessness about accuracy) as well as from active intent to mislead.

**Reduction and absence — what causes deception to diminish:**
Deception is reduced by: accountability structures (being watched, knowing one will be audited); strong moral identity that makes lying incoherent with self-concept; communities of transparency that make honesty normative; and — for the Ariely dimension — removing the social scripts that make small deceptions seem acceptable. The absence of deception requires both the motivation toward honesty and the social conditions that make honesty viable and rewarded.

**Location — where deception is seated:**
Primarily cognitive and intentional — deception requires the deliberate management of another person's belief state, which is a cognitively demanding activity. It has motivational antecedents (the desire to be believed, to avoid consequences, or to exploit) and social consequences (the relationship damage that discovery produces). Chronic deception becomes characterological through habituation — repeated deception progressively weakens the moral inhibition against it.

**External influences:**
*Social environment:* High-deception environments (organisations where dishonesty is rewarded, communities where manipulation is normalised) produce deceptive individuals through social learning and incentive structures. *Developmental:* Children learn deception capacity early (emerging around age 3–4 as theory of mind develops); the moral inhibition against deception is a developmental achievement, not a default. *Circumstances:* High-stakes situations (survival, fear of punishment, competitive contexts) increase deception rates significantly. *Cultural:* Cultures vary in their deception norms — the degree to which white lies are socially mandated, and what contexts permit or require deception.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich coverage. Deception research is one of the most active areas in social psychology.

### Section 2 — Hypocrisy and inauthenticity

**Primary field:** Social psychology / moral psychology

**Glosses covered:**
hypocrisy (hupokrisis), hypocrite (hupokritēs), to pretend (hupokrinō), to join hypocrisy (sunupokrinomai)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Batson's hypocrisy research (*Self-Other Merging and the Empathy-Altruism Hypothesis*, 1991; and hypocrisy studies) demonstrated that people reliably exhibit moral hypocrisy — professing moral standards while failing to apply them to themselves. Stone and colleagues' induced hypocrisy paradigm shows that making the discrepancy between espoused and enacted values salient motivates behaviour change — hypocrisy aversion is a real motivational force when the discrepancy is made undeniable. The Greek hupokritēs (actor, stage performer) gives hypocrisy its core meaning — performing a self that is not the genuine self.

**Emergence — what causes hypocrisy to arise:**
Hypocrisy arises from the gap between espoused values (the standards one claims to hold) and enacted values (the standards one actually applies to oneself). This gap is maintained through: self-serving bias (applying stricter standards to others than to oneself); moral licensing (the tendency to give oneself permission to act badly after acting well); and the social reward for professing high moral standards regardless of whether one lives up to them. The theatrical etymology suggests a performed self that conceals the actual self — a managed presentation.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hypocrisy to diminish:**
Hypocrisy diminishes when: the gap between espoused and enacted values is brought to conscious awareness (induced hypocrisy paradigm); accountability structures make the gap visible and costly; and the person develops sufficient self-awareness to monitor their own inconsistency. The absence of hypocrisy — genuine alignment between professed and enacted values — is the authenticity and integrity of M13.

**Location — where hypocrisy is seated:**
Cognitive and performative — it lives in the gap between inner conviction and outer presentation. It is a social phenomenon in that it requires an audience before whom the performance is staged, but its roots are in self-deception (often the hypocrite does not fully perceive their own inconsistency).

**External influences:**
*Social reward structures:* Communities that reward moral performance rather than moral reality produce hypocrisy. *Developmental:* High-pressure moral environments that punish failure without offering grace create the conditions for hiding failure behind moral performance. *Cultural:* Cultures with strong moral display norms and high shame responses to failure produce more hypocrisy as a protective strategy.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Hypocrisy research is well-developed in social and moral psychology.

### Section 3 — Manipulation, cunning, and strategic deception

**Primary field:** Social psychology / dark triad research

**Glosses covered:**
craftiness (or.mah), crooked (ha.phakh.pakh), crookedness (se.leph), be cunning (katasofizomai), be devious (luz), charm (la.chash), plot (epiboulē), plot (me.zim.mah), plot (ma.cha.sha.vah), to plow/plot (cha.rash), smooth talk (chrēstologia), to conceal (a.lam)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Machiavellianism (Christie and Geis, *Studies in Machiavellianism*, 1970) — the second of the dark triad — is characterised by strategic manipulation, willingness to deceive for personal gain, and a cynical worldview that treats other people as tools. Dark triad research (Paulhus and Williams) examines Machiavellianism alongside psychopathy and narcissism as a constellation of subclinical antisocial traits. Charm (la.chash — whispering, enchanting) and smooth talk (chrēstologia) reflect the social skill that strategic deception often deploys — the ability to present deception in an attractive, credible form.

**Emergence — what causes strategic manipulation to arise:**
Manipulation arises from: the instrumental view of other people (as means rather than ends); the development of social intelligence that is deployed in self-serving rather than other-serving directions; and environments where manipulation is effective and not adequately sanctioned. The plotting vocabulary (me.zim.mah, epiboulē) names premeditated, concealed intent to harm or exploit — an active and sustained cognitive engagement directed toward another's harm.

**Reduction and absence — what causes manipulation to diminish:**
Manipulation is reduced by: communities of transparency where concealed scheming is noticed and named; accountability structures that make the long-term costs of manipulation high; and therapeutic interventions that address the underlying instrumental worldview. Machiavellianism as a personality orientation is resistant to change — the person's fundamental cynicism about others' motives and their comfort with exploitation does not readily yield to intervention.

**Location — where manipulation is seated:**
Primarily cognitive (the scheming, planning, and strategic management of information) and motivational (the self-serving orientation that drives the strategy). It is a cold phenomenon — lacking the arousal of rage or fear — and characterised by calculated deliberateness. The crookedness vocabulary (ha.phakh.pakh — twisted, inverted) names the character of the person whose nature has become systematically non-straight.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Exposure to environments where manipulation was modelled or rewarded in childhood. Insecure attachment may produce an instrumental view of relationships. *Hereditary:* Machiavellianism and psychopathy share genetic components. *Circumstances:* Competitive, high-stakes environments where manipulation yields significant advantage and detection is difficult increase its frequency.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Dark triad research provides solid frameworks for the manipulation vocabulary.

### Section 4 — Slander, defamation, and reputational harm

**Primary field:** Social psychology — aggression research

**Glosses covered:**
slander (dusfēmia), slander (katalalia), slander (dib.bah), slander (do.phi), slander (ra.khil), slanderous (katalalos), to conceal (a.lam)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Gossip research (Dunbar, *Grooming, Gossip, and the Evolution of Language*, 1996) proposes that social information-sharing about absent others serves the social function of norm enforcement and social bonding — it is not merely malicious. However, slander specifically — false or harmful information spread to damage another's reputation — is relational aggression (Crick and Grotpeter) deployed through the social information channel. Baumeister and colleagues have examined reputation damage as a harm with lasting psychological consequences.

**Emergence — what causes slander to arise:**
Slander arises from: hostile intent toward the target combined with the recognition that reputational damage is an effective weapon; competitive contexts where the target's diminishment benefits the slanderer; and social environments where the spreader of damaging information gains status or social reward. The vocabulary of whispering and concealment (ra.khil — gossip trader; a.lam — to conceal) reflects the covert character of slander — it operates in the absence of the slandered person and behind a facade of normal social interaction.

**Reduction and absence — what causes slander to diminish:**
Slander is reduced by: communities that actively norm against gossip and reputational harm; accountability structures that hold slanderers responsible for the damage they cause; and cultural contexts where truth is actively valued over social advantage. Its absence reflects both the presence of integrity in communication and the absence of hostile competitive motivation.

**Location — where slander is seated:**
Social and linguistic — slander operates through speech in social networks, damaging the target's reputation in the community's collective knowledge. Its effects are felt by the target as social pain and relational loss.

**External influences:**
*Social environment:* High-gossip social environments normalise and reward reputational harm. *Competitive context:* Situations where relative standing matters and the target's diminishment benefits the slanderer are the primary circumstantial drivers. *Cultural:* Some cultures maintain stronger prohibitions against slander than others; honour-shame cultures are typically highly sensitive to reputational harm in both directions.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Gossip and relational aggression research provide frameworks; the specific legal and theological weight of biblical slander has limited direct science engagement.

### Section 5 — Treachery, betrayal, and infidelity

**Primary field:** Social psychology — trust and betrayal research

**Glosses covered:**
traitor (prodotēs), treacherous (ba.god), treachery (be.ged), treachery (bo.ge.dot), treachery (tor.mah), invention (chish.sha.von)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Betrayal trauma theory (Freyd, *Betrayal Trauma*, 1996) proposes that betrayal by a trusted figure — particularly a caregiver or institution on whom one depends — produces a distinctive trauma response involving dissociation and unawareness of the betrayal. Interpersonal trust violation research (Jones and Burdette) examines betrayal as the violation of trust-based expectations. The psychological consequences of betrayal are severe and long-lasting: they reorganise the victim's model of the betrayer and of trust relationships generally.

**Emergence — what causes treachery to arise:**
Treachery involves the violation of established trust — it requires a prior relationship of commitment and dependence, which the treacherous person exploits. It arises from: the overriding of relational commitment by self-interest; the instrumental view of the trusted relationship as a resource to be exploited; and the deliberate concealment of the betrayal that gives treachery its distinctive quality (ba.god — to deal treacherously, to cover while betraying).

**Reduction and absence — what causes treachery to diminish:**
Treachery is prevented by: the maintenance of genuine commitment grounded in internalised values rather than external compliance; accountability structures that make betrayal detectable and costly; and community norms that treat treachery as among the most serious moral violations. Once treachery has occurred, the trust that was exploited is extremely difficult to restore — betrayal trauma research documents the lasting disruption to the betrayed person's trust capacity.

**Location — where treachery is seated:**
Primarily volitional and characterological — treachery requires the deliberate choice to violate an established commitment. It is located in the character of the treacherous person (ba.god names a character disposition) and expressed in specific acts of betrayal. Its effects are felt in the relational space between betrayer and betrayed.

**External influences:**
*Prior relationship:* Treachery is defined by the prior trust relationship it violates — the severity of the betrayal is proportional to the depth of the trust that was exploited. *Circumstances:* High-stakes situations where the cost of loyalty is very high and the benefit of betrayal is significant test fidelity most severely. *Social modelling:* Environments where loyalty is not rewarded and betrayal is normalised reduce the threshold for treachery.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Betrayal trauma theory and trust violation research provide solid frameworks.

## Outside-science inventory

- **false prophet (pseudoprofētēs) in its theological/covenantal register** — the false prophet is not merely someone who makes inaccurate predictions but someone who claims divine authority without having it, thereby falsifying the covenant communication from God. The theological structure of true and false prophecy is outside science's scope.
- **false Christ (pseudochristos)** — the eschatological category of one who falsely claims to be the Messiah is a theological and historical category without direct science analogue.

**Observation:** M14 has minimal outside-science content. Deception, hypocrisy, manipulation, slander, and betrayal are human phenomena with good science coverage. The theological distinctive is the ultimate seriousness of falsehood — not merely its social dysfunctionality but its opposition to the God who is truth.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology (deception — Section 1); social and moral psychology (hypocrisy — Section 2); dark triad research (manipulation — Section 3); social aggression research (slander — Section 4); trust and betrayal research (Section 5).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Truth-default theory illuminates why deception is episodically rather than continuously deployed — it exploits rather than overrides the baseline trust orientation. Moral licensing illuminates how the cluster's hypocrisy vocabulary may carry a self-permission dimension. Dark triad research illuminates the cold, characterological nature of the manipulation vocabulary. Betrayal trauma theory illuminates the relational devastation that the cluster's treachery vocabulary names.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats deception primarily as a social dysfunction — costly because it undermines cooperation. The biblical material treats falsehood as an ontological violation — opposed to the character of God who is truth. The satanic dimension (the devil as the father of lies — John 8:44) gives deception a spiritual register that science does not address.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The self-deception dimension (deceiving oneself before deceiving others) may surface a dimension of the cluster's hypocrisy vocabulary that the biblical pass should test. The social contagion of deception norms (environments where lying becomes normal) may illuminate the cluster's corruption and decay of truth vocabulary.
