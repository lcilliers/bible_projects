## Science sections

### Section 1 — Vocation, purpose, and meaningful calling

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / vocational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
to call: call (kaleō), to call: call by (qa.ra), to call to/summon (proskaleō), calling (klēsis), to call on (entunchanō), to call (on)/name (epikaleō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Dik and Duffy's vocational calling research (*The Counseling Psychologist*, 2009) identifies three components of calling: (1) a transcendent summons — a sense of being called by something beyond oneself; (2) purposeful work — the sense that one's work makes a meaningful contribution; (3) prosocial orientation — the sense that one's calling serves others. Research consistently shows that those who experience their work as a calling report higher wellbeing, engagement, and performance. Frankl's logotherapy (*Man's Search for Meaning*, 1946) addresses the discovery of unique personal meaning and vocation as the primary psychological need. The calling to name (epikaleō — to call upon the name, to name) has a specific dimension — naming as the expression of personal relationship.

**Emergence — what causes a sense of calling to arise:**
A sense of calling arises through the convergence of perceived inner gifts and strengths, the experience of meaningful work producing positive outcomes, external affirmation from community, and — in Dik and Duffy's model — the experience of transcendent summons. The transcendent dimension distinguishes calling from mere strong career interest: people who experience calling report feeling drawn by something beyond their own preferences.

**Reduction and absence — what causes a sense of calling to diminish:**
Calling diminishes through: the loss of the sense of transcendent summons (secularisation of vocation); work that fails to produce meaningful contribution (alienated work); the loss of community affirmation and confirmation; and identity disruption that disconnects the person from their sense of purpose.

**Location — where calling is seated:**
Calling is primarily cognitive and motivational — a sense of direction and purpose that organises the person's energies. It has an identity dimension (calling becomes part of who the person understands themselves to be) and a relational dimension (calling is confirmed by community and directed toward others). The summons dimension of calling — that it is received from outside rather than generated from within — is significant for the programme's framework: calling as inner-being content that is responsive to an external address.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Calling typically crystallises in adolescence and early adulthood through exploration, experience, and community confirmation. *Social:* Communities of practice that identify and affirm gifts in their members facilitate the development of a sense of calling. *Circumstances:* Significant experiences (including adversity) often catalyse the clarification of calling.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Vocational psychology provides coverage for the human-facing dimensions; the theological register substantially exceeds this.

### Section 2 — Choice, selection, and preference

**Primary field:** Decision psychology / cognitive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
to choose (haireō), to choose (hairetizō), to choose (procheirotoneō), to choose (ba.char)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Decision psychology (Kahneman, Schwartz) addresses human choice behaviour. Ba.char (to choose, elect) in the Hebrew carries both human choosing and divine election — the science covers the human dimension only. The psychology of preference and selection (Ariely, Thaler) addresses how choices are made, often irrationally, under conditions of complexity and uncertainty.

**Emergence — what causes choice to arise:**
Human choice arises when multiple options are available and evaluation of their relative value is possible. The psychology of choice paradox (Schwartz) shows that more choices do not reliably produce better outcomes — the availability of many options increases decisional difficulty and post-decisional regret.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. One primary function of this section is to establish what science covers (human preference and selection) versus what it does not (divine election as a theological category).

### Section 3 — Foreknowledge, anticipation, and prospective cognition

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
foreknowledge (prognōsis), to know/choose (proginōskō), to predetermine (proaireō), firstborn (prōtotokos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Prospective cognition research (Schacter — mental time travel; Atance and O'Neill — episodic future thinking) addresses the human capacity to mentally project into the future. Mental time travel (Tulving) — the ability to mentally re-experience the past and pre-experience the future — is a distinctively human cognitive capacity. Firstborn (prōtotokos) in its social-status register has coverage in the psychology of birth order (Sulloway — *Born to Rebel*), though its christological use is outside science.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Foreknowledge as a divine attribute is almost entirely outside science. Prospective cognition provides the human analogue; the theological content exceeds it substantially.

### Section 4 — Encounter, meeting, and relational initiation

**Primary field:** Social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
to encounter: chanced (qa.ra), to encounter: meet (qa.ra), to encounter: toward (qa.ra), to call to/summon (prosfōneō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Social psychology of first meetings (Ambady — thin slices of behaviour; Sunnafrank — predicted outcome value theory) addresses how initial encounters shape subsequent relationships. The encounter terms here describe moments of meeting that carry relational significance — they are the initiation points of relationships, which social psychology addresses through first impression research.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. The science of first meetings provides marginal coverage; the theological register of divine encounter is outside science.

## Outside-science inventory

- **election (divine choice of individuals and peoples)** — the theological category of God's sovereign, gracious choice of persons for salvation and relationship is entirely outside science. Psychology addresses human choice; divine election is a theological category without science parallel.
- **foreknowledge (prognōsis) as divine omniscience** — God's eternal knowledge of his people before their existence is a theological category.
- **firstborn (prōtotokos) as christological title** — Christ as the firstborn of all creation and of the dead is a theological category.
- **effectual calling** — the theological doctrine that God's call to salvation is efficacious and brings about the response it invites is outside science.

**Observation:** M37 has a higher proportion of outside-science content than most clusters. The gap between vocational psychology (calling as a human psychological experience) and theology (calling as divine election and effectual summons) is one of the most significant in the programme. The psychological calling construct is genuine and analytically useful; it reaches only the experiential edge of the cluster's theological core.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Vocational psychology (Dik, Duffy) for Section 1; decision psychology for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Vocational calling research illuminates the experiential dimension of the cluster's summons vocabulary — the felt sense of being called by something beyond oneself is a real psychological experience that the science addresses. This experiential dimension is genuine even if the theological claim (that the calling originates from God) is outside science's verification.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The fundamental divergence is between calling as a human psychological experience (science) and calling as a divine act (theology). The theological claim is not that people feel called but that God actually calls — an ontological claim science cannot evaluate.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** Whether the cluster's calling vocabulary predominantly describes the inner experience of the called person or the act of the calling God will be the central Session B analytical question.
