## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Relation to God: God is frequently the **possessor/agent** of indignation — "his wrath and indignation" (Psa 78:49); "the rod of my anger... the staff of my indignation" (Isa 10:5); "weapons of his indignation" (Isa 13:5). God is also the **object** of misdirected human rage — "a man's folly... his heart rages against the LORD" (Pro 19:3). Both relations occur; many human occurrences have no God-relation (1Ki 20:43).

**T0.1.2** — Across C5 indignation is **borne by God** (Psa 69:24; Isa 10:25; Mal 1:4) and by the **creature** (Ahab, 1Ki 21:4; Balaam, Num 23:7). *Interpretation:* indignation is a shared characteristic — God's settled judicial displeasure and the person's displeasure (rightly, 2Cor 7:11; or sullenly, 1Ki 21:4).

**T0.2.1** — Stated purpose/effect: God's indignation **executes judgment** — "let your burning anger / indignation overtake them" (Psa 69:24); it is the "staff" by which God strikes (Isa 10:5); it "ends" when its corrective work is done (Isa 10:25). Human sullen indignation produces **withdrawal** (Ahab "would eat no food," 1Ki 21:4). Godly indignation produces **earnest self-clearing** (2Cor 7:11). Record none where unstated.

**T0.2.2** — valence confirms **both**: neutral 29 (descriptive of God's judicial indignation, Isa 13:5) and a moral split — **righteous 4** (godly indignation, 2Cor 7:11; Psa 119:53 "hot indignation... because of the wicked"), **sinful 7 / forbidden 2** (sullen self-pity, 1Ki 21:4; rage against God, Pro 19:3). Divine indignation belongs to holy design; sullen human indignation to the fallen condition.

**T0.2.3** — Future-fullness orientation: yes — divine indignation is oriented to a **determined end / appointed time** ("the indignation... till the indignation is accomplished," Dan 11:36; Isa 10:25 "in a very little while my indignation will come to an end"; Zep 3:8 "the day when I rise up"). It points to an appointed consummation of judgment.

**T0.3.1** — Divine-likeness aspect: the aspect instantiated in the person is **righteous displeasure at evil** — "hot indignation... because of the wicked who forsake your law" (Psa 119:53), and the "indignation" of godly repentance (2Cor 7:11). Sullen self-pity (1Ki 21:4) images nothing of God.

**T0.3.2** — Shared: indignation is borne by God (Isa 10:5) and the person (2Cor 7:11) under related vocabulary; it is a divine judicial attribute the creature reflects rightly or distorts into sulking.

**T0.3.3** — Where right indignation is present it indicates **alignment with God's moral order** (Psa 119:53; 2Cor 7:11); where sullen indignation is present it indicates **wounded self-will** (1Ki 21:4 Ahab over a vineyard); where God's indignation rests on a person it indicates they stand **under judgment** (Mic 7:9 "I will bear the indignation of the LORD because I have sinned").

**T0.4.1** — Typological use: divine indignation is given an **eschatological-apocalyptic** register in Daniel — "what shall happen in the latter time of the indignation" (Dan 8:19; 11:36) — the divine instance establishing the pattern of the appointed end-time wrath. Direction: divine instance outward. Elsewhere none.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Programme name: **Indignation / displeasure / sullenness**. The name signals a **cooler, settled displeasure** — moral indignation, judicial fury, or sullen vexation — distinct from hot kindled rage. Senses: indignation 18, fury 5, sullen 2, "denounce."

**T1.1.2** — Primary terms: zaˈam (noun/verb, "indignation; to be indignant / to denounce, curse") — its root carries **to foam/be enraged** and **to denounce/curse**; zaˈaph ("rage, be vexed/sullen"); zalˈaˈphah ("scorching/burning heat"); aganaktēsis (Greek, "indignation"). Definitionally a **displeasure that condemns** — and at one edge **curses** (Num 23:7–8).

**T1.1.3** — Directional/relational: indignation is directed **against** wrongdoers (Psa 69:24; 119:53), expressed **toward** a denounced party (Num 23:7), or turned **inward** as sullenness (1Ki 21:4). It carries a condemnatory direction.

**T1.2.1** — Mainly a **condition/status** (noun forms, 32 — "the indignation of the LORD," Mic 7:9) with an **act** strand (verb forms, 15 — "denounce," Num 23:8; "be indignant") and a small **quality** (2 — "sullen," zaˈeph, 1Ki 20:43 as a descriptive state).

**T1.2.2** — **Compound**: indignation fuses with **wrath/anger** ("his wrath, indignation, and distress," Psa 78:49; "fury... indignation," Isa 30:27 context) and with **grief** in the godly case (2Cor 7:11 "godly grief... indignation"). The sullen form fuses displeasure with **dejection** (1Ki 21:4). Rarely simple.

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: **favour / good pleasure** — implied where indignation is the negative of God's favour ("his indignation" vs the restored "his hand... known to his servants," Isa 66:14 context). Contentment stands against sullenness.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes **acceptance/blessing**: Balaam cannot "denounce" (zaˈam) whom God has not denounced — "How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?" (Num 23:8). Indignation resists where God has blessed.

**T1.3.3** — It ends where **hot rage** (C1/C2) begins (indignation is cooler, often moral or sulking) and where **grief** (M03) begins (the sullen/dejected edge, 1Ki 21:4; 2Cor 7:11 pairs it with grief). It is displeasure-with-condemnation, not mere heat.

**T1.4.1** — Modes: as **settled judicial fury** (a "staff/rod," Isa 10:5); as a **denouncing speech-act** (Num 23:7–8 "come, denounce"); as **scorching heat** (zalˈaˈphah, Psa 11:6 "scorching wind"); as **sullen withdrawal** (Ahab "vexed and sullen," 1Ki 20:43; 21:4); as **seizing affect** ("hot indignation seizes me," Psa 119:53). Multiple modes.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies: **divine** indignation as judicial instrument (Isa 10:5); **human moral** indignation as righteous heat (Psa 119:53); **human sullen** indignation as dejected withdrawal (1Ki 21:4); **denunciatory** indignation as curse-speech (Num 23:7). Distinct registers.

**T1.4.3** — Communicative/spoken mode: yes, prominently — indignation is exercised as **denunciation/cursing** (Num 23:7–8 "denounce"), and God **declares** his indignation (Eze 21:31 "I will pour out my indignation"; Mal 1:4). Recorded.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response: Ahab's indignation issues in **lying down, turning his face away, refusing food** (1Ki 21:4); divine indignation issues in **judgment let loose** (Psa 78:49); godly indignation issues in **earnestness and self-clearing** (2Cor 7:11). Record none where unstated (Num 23:7).

**T1.5.2** — Varied: sullen withdrawal (1Ki 21:4), executed judgment (Psa 69:24), or zealous self-correction (2Cor 7:11). No single immediate response.

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effect: God's indignation, sustained, **devastates** ("the indignation of the LORD... his fury," Jer 50:25; Lam 2 context) and is bounded to an **appointed end** (Isa 10:25; Dan 11:36). Sullen human indignation, sustained, festers into **plotting** (Ahab's sullenness → Jezebel's plot, 1Ki 21:4–7). Record none where unstated.

**T1.6.3** — Immediate response is the flash (withdrawal/judgment); sustained effect is the **accumulated devastation or festering scheme** (Isa 10:25; 1Ki 21:7). Episode vs settled consequence.

**T1.7.1** — It "takes hold rightly" (valence righteous) where it is **moral indignation at evil** — "hot indignation... because of the wicked who forsake your law" (Psa 119:53) and the indignation of genuine repentance (2Cor 7:11). The condition is alignment with God's standard.

**T1.7.2** — It is **distorted** (valence sinful/forbidden) as **sullen self-pity** (Ahab over a denied vineyard, 1Ki 21:4) or **rage against God** for one's own folly (Pro 19:3 "his heart rages against the LORD"). The denunciation form is **blocked** where God has blessed — Balaam cannot curse Israel (Num 23:8). No verse attributes the indignation to another spirit — **silent** on that.

**T1.7.3** — Where indignation is present but does not take hold rightly, the person is shown **sullen, withdrawn, and self-harming** (Ahab, 1Ki 21:4) or **blaming God for self-caused ruin** (Pro 19:3).

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — Locations: **heart** — "his heart rages against the LORD" (Pro 19:3); the restored "hand of the LORD... his indignation against his enemies" set against the servants whose "heart shall rejoice" (Isa 66:14); **flesh** — "there is no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation" (Psa 38:3). 46/49 locate no seat (Num 23:7) — record none.

**T2.1.2** — The pattern: where located, indignation reaches the **heart** (its raging seat, Pro 19:3) and even the **flesh/body** under divine indignation ("no soundness in my flesh," Psa 38:3) — a notable somatic link. The body link is **expressive/indicative** of the inner displeasure or of judgment felt bodily.

**T2.10.1** — Movement: divine indignation moves **onto the person from God** — "let loose... his indignation" (Psa 78:49), "poured out" (Eze 21:31), and is **received-from-outside** (Dan 1:10 the official's fear of the king's displeasure). Sullen indignation arises **within** (1Ki 21:4). No angelic introduction. Record the onsets shown.

**T2.7.1** — Body direction: where a body link exists it runs **inner displeasure / divine indignation expressing into the body** — "no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation" (Psa 38:3), the heart that "rages" (Pro 19:3). Inner/divine-to-body. No body-to-soul feedback — **silent**.

**T2.9.1** — Origin: **within the person** for sullen/moral indignation (1Ki 21:4; Psa 119:53); **from God** for divine indignation (Psa 78:49 "his indignation"); and, in the godly case, produced **by godly grief** ("godly grief produces... what indignation," 2Cor 7:11). Multiple origins.

**T2.9.2** — Origin is **multiple** and context-dependent — within-person sullenness, divine bestowal of judicial indignation, and grief-produced righteous indignation (2Cor 7:11). It changes with context.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1** — Perception: indignation is triggered by what is **seen/heard** — Psalmist's hot indignation "because of the wicked" he observes (Psa 119:53); Ahab's sullenness on **hearing** Naboth's refusal (1Ki 21:4); the king's "displeasure" feared by sight (Dan 1:10). Perception is the gate.

**T3.1.2** — Effect on perception: sullen indignation **narrows** perception to the grievance (Ahab fixed on the vineyard, 1Ki 21:4). No verse states it distorting the senses generally — otherwise **silent**.

**T3.1.3** — Pattern: indignation is **perception-triggered** (a perceived wrong or denial), confirming its responsive, evaluative nature.

**T3.2.1** — Cognition: engaged as **moral appraisal** — the indignation "because of the wicked who forsake your law" is a judgment of their wrong (Psa 119:53); divine indignation follows God's reckoning of sin (Mic 7:9 "because I have sinned"). 

**T3.2.2** — Effect on cognition: sullen indignation **clouds** judgment toward self-pity and plotting (1Ki 21:4–7); godly indignation **sharpens** moral resolve (2Cor 7:11 "what earnestness... what indignation"). Both directions.

**T3.2.3** — Pattern: indignation is **appraisal-bound** — it rests on a judgment of wrong and reshapes the mind toward either festering or earnestness.

**T3.3.1** — Memory: thin — sullen indignation **holds** a grievance (Ahab, 1Ki 21:4); divine indignation recalls covenant sin (Mic 7:9). Direct memory-faculty engagement is limited — mostly **silent**.

**T3.3.2** — **Silent** on indignation acting on a memory faculty.

**T3.3.3** — The thinness indicates indignation is framed as **present displeasure**, with remembered grievance as backdrop.

**T3.4.1** — Affect: the core faculty (45/49 affect) — indignation is an **emotional state of condemnatory displeasure** (Num 23:7; 1Ki 21:4; Psa 119:53).

**T3.4.2** — Effect on affect: it can **darken** the affective field into sullenness (1Ki 21:4) or **intensify** moral feeling (Psa 119:53 "hot indignation"); paired with grief in 2Cor 7:11. 

**T3.4.3** — That affect dominates confirms indignation as a genuine **emotion**, distinct from hot rage by its cooler, often sulking or judicial register.

**T3.5.1** — Creativity: not engaged. **Silent.**

**T3.5.2** — **Silent.**

**T3.5.3** — Absence indicates no relation to the originative faculty.

**T3.6.1** — Volition: engaged where indignation **drives or withholds** action — Balaam is summoned to *choose* to denounce but cannot (Num 23:8); Ahab's sullen will **refuses** food and action (1Ki 21:4); godly indignation **mobilises** the will to set things right (2Cor 7:11). 

**T3.6.2** — Effect on volition: sullen indignation **paralyses/redirects** the will toward self-pity or scheming (1Ki 21:4–7); righteous indignation **energises** the will toward correction (2Cor 7:11).

**T3.6.3** — Pattern: indignation strongly engages the **will** at both poles — it must be governed lest it sour into sullen scheming.

**T3.7.1** — Agency: divine indignation is intensely **action-issuing** (the "staff" that strikes, Isa 10:5; "let loose" judgment, Psa 78:49); human indignation issues in **denunciation** (Num 23:7) or, sullenly, in **withdrawal/plotting** (1Ki 21:4–7).

**T3.7.2** — Effect on agency: it **drives** judgment (Isa 10:5), curse-speech (Num 23:7), corrective zeal (2Cor 7:11), or scheming injustice (1Ki 21:7).

**T3.7.3** — The indignation→act pattern marks it as **agency-generating**, for judgment, denunciation, or (sullenly) plotting.

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation: directly engaged — indignation *is* the affective form of a **condemnatory verdict** (Psa 119:53 against lawbreakers; God's indignation against sin, Mic 7:9; godly indignation, 2Cor 7:11). valence: righteous 4, sinful 7, forbidden 2.

**T3.8.2** — Effect: it **enacts** a moral verdict — righteous indignation condemns evil rightly (Psa 119:53; 2Cor 7:11); sullen indignation passes a **self-serving** false verdict (Ahab feels wronged over a just refusal, 1Ki 21:4).

**T3.8.3** — Pattern: indignation is **morally tri-valued** — God's righteous judicial indignation (Isa 10:5), the believer's righteous moral indignation (Psa 119:53; 2Cor 7:11), and sinful sullen self-pity (1Ki 21:4). Its morality depends on the justice of its grievance.

**T3.9.1** — Conscience: engaged where indignation is **borne as deserved judgment** under a guilty conscience — "I will bear the indignation of the LORD because I have sinned against him" (Mic 7:9). The acute witness of guilt accepts the indignation.

**T3.9.2** — Effect on conscience: the guilty conscience **submits** to the indignation as just (Mic 7:9). Recorded; elsewhere not stated.

**T3.9.3** — The Mic 7:9 case indicates indignation can engage conscience as **acknowledged just desert**, though this is a single clear instance.

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness: engaged in the godly case — indignation as part of the **integrated** repentant response ("godly grief produces... earnestness... indignation... zeal," 2Cor 7:11). Sullen indignation is the **failure** of integration (self-pity over a just denial, 1Ki 21:4).

**T3.10.2** — Effect: integrated righteous indignation **drives reform** (2Cor 7:11); unintegrated sullen indignation **drives injustice** (1Ki 21:7).

**T3.10.3** — The pattern marks indignation as a **moral test-site** — rightly integrated it is the indignation of repentance; wrongly, the sulk that schemes.

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity: indignation is **relational** — directed against wrongdoers (Psa 119:53), expressed in denunciation of a people (Num 23:7), or souring a relationship (Ahab toward Naboth, 1Ki 21:4). It engages the bond.

**T3.11.2** — Effect on relational capacity: sullen indignation **damages** relationships (it leads to Naboth's judicial murder, 1Ki 21:4–13); righteous indignation **restores** community integrity (2Cor 7:11 the church's self-clearing). Both poles.

**T3.11.3** — The pattern shows indignation is **relationally embedded** — it arises against another and bears on the relationship for ill (sullen scheming) or good (corrective indignation).

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1** — God-toward-human: yes — God's indignation operates from God toward persons/nations — "let your indignation overtake them" (Psa 69:24); the Assyrian "rod of my anger... staff of my indignation" sent against a godless nation (Isa 10:5–6); "weapons of his indignation" (Isa 13:5).

**T4.1.2** — Basis: **responsive/judicial** — extended in response to sin and covenant violation (Mic 7:9 "because I have sinned"; Isa 10:6 against "a godless nation"). Conditional on wrong; bounded to an appointed end (Isa 10:25).

**T4.1.3** — What it shows of God's disposition: God is **morally reactive to evil** — His indignation measures His opposition to sin, yet is **bounded and purposive** (Isa 10:25 "in a very little while... my indignation will come to an end"), showing restraint within judgment.

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God: yes — the person **bears** God's indignation penitently (Mic 7:9), and (wrongly) **rages against** God for self-caused ruin (Pro 19:3 "his heart rages against the LORD"). The movement is the person's posture under or against God's displeasure.

**T4.2.2** — Inner posture required: **submission and confession** ("I will bear the indignation of the LORD... until he pleads my cause," Mic 7:9) — the right posture; the wrong posture is **blame-shifting rage** (Pro 19:3).

**T4.2.3** — What it shows: penitent bearing of indignation shows a **hope-filled, submissive** relationship (Mic 7:9 expects vindication); raging against God shows a **disordered** one (Pro 19:3).

**T4.3.1** — Person-to-person extension: yes — indignation/denunciation is extended toward another (Balaam hired to "denounce" Israel, Num 23:7); sullen indignation is "extended" as cold withdrawal/scheming toward Naboth (1Ki 21:4 ff.).

**T4.3.2** — Inner conditions accompanying it: **moral judgment** in the righteous case (Psa 119:53), **wounded entitlement** in the sullen case (1Ki 21:4), **hireling malice** in the denunciation case (Num 23:7 Balak's purpose).

**T4.3.3** — What the giver must have/become: the evidence states no required prior state for indignation; the denouncer's curse is **void unless God concurs** (Num 23:8 — one cannot denounce whom God has not denounced). Recorded.

**T4.4.1** — Receiving: the object of indignation **receives** judgment/denunciation — Israel is the intended object of Balaam's denunciation (Num 23:7), the wicked the object of divine indignation (Psa 69:24). Uptake is endured, not adopted.

**T4.4.2** — Conditions of uptake: **guilt** draws deserved indignation (Mic 7:9); **God's protection** blocks undeserved denunciation (Num 23:8 Israel cannot be cursed). 

**T4.4.3** — The person who meets indignation justly bears it **penitently and hopefully** (Mic 7:9); the one wrongly denounced is **shielded** (Num 23:8).

**T4.5.1** — Within bonds vs across distance: indignation operates **within bonds** (Ahab toward his subject Naboth, 1Ki 21:4; God toward His covenant people, Mic 7:9) and **across** them (God's indignation on the nations, Isa 13:5; 34:2 context). Both.

**T4.5.2** — Covenantal scope: divine indignation operates **within the covenant** (against His sinning people, Mic 7:9) and **crosses** to the nations (Isa 13:5; Mal 1:4 against Edom). Both.

**T4.5.3** — Relational scope: **wide** — God (Pro 19:3 as object), His people (Mic 7:9), the nations (Isa 13:5; Mal 1:4), a subject (1Ki 21:4), a hostile people (Num 23:7). Indignation reaches across the relational field.

**T4.6.1** — Spiritual beings: not shown operating toward angelic/adversarial beings. **Silent.**

**T4.6.2** — Adversarial site: not evidenced as a site of adversarial weaponising. **Silent** (recorded as not evidenced).

**T4.6.3** — Angelic mediation: divine indignation is at times executed **through a human instrument** (Assyria as the "staff of my indignation," Isa 10:5) — an instrumental mediation of judgment, though human, not angelic. Recorded; otherwise silent on angelic mediation.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1** — Transformation: indignation transforms **condition** — divine indignation lays a nation low (Isa 10:5–6); godly indignation transforms a repentant community ("at every point you have proved yourselves innocent," 2Cor 7:11). Record none where unstated.

**T5.1.2** — Reversible/irreversible: divine indignation is **bounded/reversible** ("in a very little while my indignation will come to an end," Isa 10:25; "until he pleads my cause," Mic 7:9); sullen indignation hardens into **executed** injustice once acted on (1Ki 21:13). Both.

**T5.2.1** — Sequence: **perceived wrong → sullen indignation → withdrawal → scheme/act** (Ahab, 1Ki 21:4–7,13); **godly grief → indignation → earnest reform** (2Cor 7:11); **sin → divine indignation → judgment → appointed end** (Isa 10:5–6,25). Record none where no sequence appears.

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism of change: **judgment** (divine indignation as the instrument, Isa 10:5) and **repentant grief** (the mechanism producing righteous indignation, 2Cor 7:11). Record none where unstated.

**T5.3.2** — Differs: divine indignation works change by **judicial striking** (Isa 10:5); godly indignation by **grief-driven self-correction** (2Cor 7:11); sullen indignation by **festering into scheme** (1Ki 21:7). Context-dependent.

**T5.4.1** — Relation to suffering: indignation **produces suffering** (divine indignation as affliction — "no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation," Psa 38:3) and is **produced by** suffering/grievance (Ahab's denied desire, 1Ki 21:4; the prophet "sat alone... filled with indignation," Jer 15:17 amid his affliction).

**T5.4.2** — What suffering does to it: affliction under God's indignation drives the sufferer to **confession and hope** (Mic 7:9; Psa 38:3–4,18 confession); the prophet's suffering-borne indignation drives him to **lament before God** (Jer 15:17–18). Suffering can turn indignation Godward.

**T5.5.1** — Sanctification arc: indignation participates — godly indignation is part of the **repentant transformation** that sanctifies a community (2Cor 7:11); the believer's hot indignation at lawlessness is a mark of **formed loyalty** to God's law (Psa 119:53). The sullen form is the anti-formative failure (1Ki 21:4).

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological orientation: yes — divine indignation is oriented to the **appointed "time of the indignation"** (Dan 8:19; 11:36) and the **day** of God's rising (Zep 3:8). Its present operation anticipates that consummation.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1** — Co-occurring characteristics: **wrath/anger** ("his wrath, indignation, and distress," Psa 78:49; indignation beside fury, Isa 30:27 context — C1/C2), **grief** ("godly grief... indignation," 2Cor 7:11 — M03), and **denunciation/cursing** (Num 23:7–8). Strong pairing with wrath and, in the godly case, grief.

**T6.1.2** — Co-occurrence places C5 **adjacent to wrath** (as a cooler/judicial member) and bridging toward **grief** (the sullen/dejected and godly-grief edges) — it sits between hot anger and sorrow.

**T6.2.1** — Sequence: indignation **follows** a perceived/actual wrong and **precedes** judgment, withdrawal, or reform (Isa 10:5–6; 1Ki 21:4–7; 2Cor 7:11). It also **accompanies** wrath as a paired term (Psa 78:49).

**T6.2.2** — The relationship is **causal/developmental**: grievance causes indignation; indignation causes judgment/scheme/reform (Isa 10:6; 1Ki 21:7; 2Cor 7:11).

**T6.3.1** — Produces: indignation **produces** judgment (Isa 10:6), bodily affliction (Psa 38:3), corrective zeal (2Cor 7:11), or sullen scheming (1Ki 21:7).

**T6.3.2** — Produced by: indignation is **produced by** perceived wrong (Psa 119:53), denied desire (1Ki 21:4), godly grief (2Cor 7:11), and divine reckoning of sin (Mic 7:9).

**T6.3.3** — Constituent relation: indignation is a **constituent** of the wrath-complex (paired with wrath/distress, Psa 78:49) and of the godly-repentance complex (a constituent alongside earnestness and zeal, 2Cor 7:11).

**T6.4.1** — Shared vocabulary: the zaˈam/zaˈaph family is **distinct** from the burning-anger lemmas but is **collocated** with cheˈmah/aph/qeˈtseph (Psa 78:49; Isa 30:27 context) — shared collocation, not shared lemma. aganaktēsis is the NT indignation term, distinct from orgē.

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms/roots: **zaˈam** (H2194/H2195, "to be indignant; to denounce/curse"; noun "indignation") — root sense of **foaming/denouncing**; **zaˈaph** (H2196/H2197, "to rage, be vexed/sullen"); **zalˈaˈphah** (H2152, "scorching heat"); Greek **aganaktēsis** ("indignation"). **T7.1.4** — Distinguishing terms: **condition vs act** — zaˈam (the state of indignation) vs the verb "denounce"; **mood/quality** — zaˈeph "sullen" (1Ki 20:43). The denunciation sense is a distinct aspect within the same root.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term: the adjective **zaˈeph "sullen"** describes a person's state (1Ki 20:43; 21:4) — a quality/mood-term rather than a fixed person-type noun. Record: a mood-adjective, no settled person-type noun.

**T7.1.7** — Supplication/seeking term: none — no term for seeking indignation. Record none.

**T7.1.8** — OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew zaˈam/zaˈaph indignation family is represented in the NT by **aganaktēsis** (2Cor 7:11), where indignation appears in a **positive, sanctifying** light (the fruit of godly grief) — a notable development from the predominantly judicial/sullen OT range. Continuity of the concept; development toward a redeemable register.

**T7.1.9** — NT-coined term: **aganaktēsis** (and the verb aganakteō) is the NT indignation term; not newly coined but applied here to **righteous repentant indignation** (2Cor 7:11). Record the refined positive use.

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc: from OT **judicial/denunciatory/sullen** displeasure (zaˈam/zaˈaph) to NT **righteous repentant** indignation (aganaktēsis) — an arc from condemnation and sulking to sanctified moral indignation.

**T7.2.1** — Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Num 23:7–8 zaˈam ("denounce") is the **verb Balaam is hired to perform and cannot** — the term carries the whole drama of the failed curse ("How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?"). **T7.2.2** — Literary forms: **narrative** (Gen 40:6; 1Ki 20:43; 21:4; Num 23), **psalm** (Psa 69:24; 78:49; 119:53), **wisdom** (Pro 19:3; 22:14), **prophecy** (Isa 10:5; 13:5; Mic 7:9; Mal 1:4), **apocalyptic** (Dan 8:19; 11:36), **NT epistle** (2Cor 7:11). **T7.2.3** — Logical structure: **judicial-consequential** in the divine texts — sin/godlessness → the staff of indignation → bounded judgment (Isa 10:5–6,25); **narrative-causal** in the sullen texts — denial → sullen indignation → scheme (1Ki 21:4–7); **fruit-structure** in the NT — godly grief → indignation among its fruits (2Cor 7:11).

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: **judicial/prophetic** (Isa 10:5; Mic 7:9), **royal-narrative** (1Ki 21:4), **liturgical-imprecatory** (Psa 69:24; 119:53), **apocalyptic** (Dan 11:36), **pastoral-epistolary** (2Cor 7:11). The settings show indignation as judicial instrument, sulking royal vice, imprecatory zeal, and repentant fruit.

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor verse: **2 Corinthians 7:11** for the redeemed pole ("godly grief... what indignation, what fear, what longing, what zeal") and **Isaiah 10:5** for the divine-judicial pole ("the rod of my anger... the staff of my indignation"). Together they most fully express C5's range — righteous repentant indignation and God's bounded judicial indignation.

**T7.2.6** — These anchors show what no single verse shows alone: 2Cor 7:11 that indignation can be a **sanctifying fruit of grief**; Isa 10:5 that divine indignation is a **commissioned, bounded instrument of judgment** — the cooler, judicial-and-moral character that distinguishes C5 from hot rage.

**T7.3.1** — Most useful human-science lens: per the science extract, **moral psychology** — indignation is treated there as "moral anger... triggered by norm violation rather than personal frustration," a distinct moral emotion (Haidt; third-party punishment; justice sensitivity).

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates: the moral-anger account illuminates the **righteous** indignation texts — "hot indignation... because of the wicked who forsake your law" (Psa 119:53) and the repentant indignation of 2Cor 7:11 are precisely norm-violation-triggered moral anger; justice sensitivity illuminates why such indignation is commended. The trait-mood account illuminates the **sullen** disposition (Ahab, 1Ki 20:43).

**T7.3.3** — Where they diverge: science treats indignation as a human moral emotion only; the C5 evidence makes its primary register **divine judicial indignation** (Isa 10:5; Psa 78:49) — God's holy displeasure, a covenant/theological category science does not adjudicate (per the outside-science inventory). The **denunciation/curse** edge (Num 23:7) also has no clean scientific analogue.

**T7.3.4** — Gaps surfaced by science: moral-disengagement research (absence of indignation at genuine injustice = deactivated moral self-sanction) prompts examination of texts where indignation is **lacking** or **misdirected** (Ahab's sulking over a *just* refusal, 1Ki 21:4, is misdirected indignation) — a prompt for further verse work on disordered vs righteous indignation.
