## T0.1.1 — In this verse, is the characteristic predicated of God or otherwise related to God; if so, in what relation (God as the one who bears it, acts, gives it, or is its object)? Record the relation, or record that it is not related to God here.

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** In the C1 set the characteristic is borne by the human person, not God; the divine role resolves to NONE in 227 of 228 occurrences. The single God-relation is God as **addressee** of the angry person — the LORD speaking to angry Cain (Gen 4:6). *Flag:* a few divine-subject verses leaked into C1 by field resolution (Exo 4:14; 2Ki 13:3) and properly belong to C2.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Predicated of God directly: the wrath is God's, with God as **possessor** ("the wrath of the LORD," Exo 15:7; Num 32:14; Deu 29:23) or **agent** ("the LORD's anger was kindled," Gen 18:30; Exo 22:24; Num 11:1). This is the defining relation of C2.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Relation to God: in the dominant strand God is the **object** provoked — "they provoked him to anger with their high places" (1Ki 14:9; Deu 4:25; Jer 25:6–7). God is not the bearer but the one whose anger is aroused. In the human strand God is not in view (1Sa 1:6).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Relation to God: God is the **bearer** of jealousy — "the LORD your God is a jealous God" (Exo 20:5; Deu 4:24; 6:15; possessor/agent), the **object** of jealousy provoked ("they moved him to jealousy," Deu 32:16,21; Psa 78:58), and the one **for whom** human zeal is exercised (Num 25:11 "jealous with my jealousy"; 1Ki 19:10). All three relations occur.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Relation to God: God is frequently the **possessor/agent** of indignation — "his wrath and indignation" (Psa 78:49); "the rod of my anger... the staff of my indignation" (Isa 10:5); "weapons of his indignation" (Isa 13:5). God is also the **object** of misdirected human rage — "a man's folly... his heart rages against the LORD" (Pro 19:3). Both relations occur; many human occurrences have no God-relation (1Ki 20:43).

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Relation to God: God **has a controversy/indictment (rîb)** with His people and the nations — "the LORD has a controversy with the inhabitants of the land" (Hos 4:1); "the LORD has an indictment against his people" (Mic 6:2); "an indictment against the nations" (Jer 25:31) — div_role agent. God is also the one to whom a person **commits his cause** (Psa 35:23; Lam 3:58 "you have taken up my cause") and who **pleads the cause** of the oppressed (Pro 22:23; Psa 74:22). Most human-strife occurrences have no God-relation (Gen 13:7).

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Relation to God: none of the four verses predicates bitterness of God; God does not bear pikria. In Heb 12:15 the "root of bitterness" is set in explicit contrast to "the grace of God" that the community must not fail to obtain. So bitterness stands **opposed to grace**, never borne by God. Record: no divine-bearer relation.

## T0.1.2 — Across the characteristic's verses, is it ever borne by God himself or only by the creature, and what does that pattern of presence or absence indicate for its place in the human person and in the divine image?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Across C1 the characteristic is borne by the creature (human): Gen 4:5 (Cain); Gen 30:2 (Jacob); 1Sa 20:30 (Saul); Jon 4:1 (Jonah). Divine bearing of the same affect is held separately as C2 (researcher decision DEC-2). *Interpretation (inferential):* the same vocabulary borne by God in C2 (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē) being borne here by the person indicates anger is a shared affect, not an exclusively creaturely one — its place in the divine image is via that correspondence (see T0.3.2, T6.4).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Across C2 the characteristic is **borne by God himself** throughout (possessor/agent in 144 of 145). *Interpretation:* that the same vocabulary borne by the creature (C1) is here predicated of God establishes anger as belonging to the divine nature as a righteous response — the human instance (C1) is the creaturely correspondent (Divine-Human Correspondence dimension).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Across C3 God appears chiefly as the **provoked party** (object, 60 occ), not the bearer. *Interpretation:* the characteristic names the human capacity to **arouse anger in another** — and, gravely, in God; its place in the person is as the relational act that draws covenant wrath (a fallen-direction capacity), with a redeemed counterpart (stirring to good, Heb 10:24).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Across C4 the characteristic is **borne by God himself** (Exo 34:14; Isa 42:13 "he stirs up his zeal/jealousy") and by the creature (Gen 30:1; Num 25:11). *Interpretation:* jealousy/zeal is a **shared** characteristic — supremely a divine covenant-attribute that the creature bears both rightly (zeal, Num 25:11) and wrongly (envy, Gen 30:1).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Across C5 indignation is **borne by God** (Psa 69:24; Isa 10:25; Mal 1:4) and by the **creature** (Ahab, 1Ki 21:4; Balaam, Num 23:7). *Interpretation:* indignation is a shared characteristic — God's settled judicial displeasure and the person's displeasure (rightly, 2Cor 7:11; or sullenly, 1Ki 21:4).

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Across C6, contention is **borne by God** as covenant-lawsuit (Hos 4:1; Mic 6:2) and by the **creature** as quarrel/strife (Gen 13:7; 1Cor 3:3). *Interpretation:* the divine instance is **judicial covenant-contention**; the creaturely instance ranges from righteous cause-pleading (1Sa 24:15) to sinful quarrelling (1Cor 3:3). The shared term riv links God's just controversy and human strife.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Across C7 bitterness is borne only by the **creature**, and always as a **disorder** (Act 8:23 Simon; Rom 3:14 the depraved mouth; Eph 4:31 the believer to put it off; Heb 12:15 the defiling root). *Interpretation:* bitterness is a wholly fallen-condition characteristic — it images nothing of God and has no righteous form in this set.

## T0.2.1 — Does this verse state any purpose, role, or effect the characteristic serves in the person — what it leads the person to be, do, or become? Record it if stated; otherwise record none.

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Stated effects in-verse: anger **stirs up strife / contention** (Pro 15:18; Pro 29:22); a harsh word **stirs up** anger while a soft answer turns it away (Pro 15:1); anger **lodges in the bosom of fools** (Ecc 7:9); "much vexation" accompanies much wisdom/knowledge (Ecc 1:18). Where no effect is stated (most narrative occurrences, e.g. Gen 31:35), record none.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Stated purpose/effect: God's wrath **consumes/destroys** the guilty (Num 16:46 plague; Deu 9:19–20; Isa 34:2 "devoted them to destruction"), **executes covenant judgment** (Deu 29:28 "uprooted... in anger"), and is repeatedly shown as **turnable away** by repentance/intercession (Num 25:4; 2Ch 29:10; Jon 3:9 — its purpose is corrective, not merely punitive).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Stated purpose/effect: provoking God **brings down wrath/judgment** ("to provoke me to anger... I will set my face against," Eze 8:17–18 context; "provoked the LORD... so that he removed them," 2Ki 17:18-type). The positive strand states a constructive effect — "stir up one another to love and good works" (Heb 10:24). Record none where unstated.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Stated purpose/effect: divine jealousy **accomplishes** salvation/judgment — "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this" (Isa 9:7; 37:32); Phinehas' jealousy **turns away wrath and makes atonement** (Num 25:11,13). Human envy, by contrast, **rots/consumes** (Pro 14:30 "envy makes the bones rot"; Job 5:2 context). Record none where unstated.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Stated purpose/effect: God's indignation **executes judgment** — "let your burning anger / indignation overtake them" (Psa 69:24); it is the "staff" by which God strikes (Isa 10:5); it "ends" when its corrective work is done (Isa 10:25). Human sullen indignation produces **withdrawal** (Ahab "would eat no food," 1Ki 21:4). Godly indignation produces **earnest self-clearing** (2Cor 7:11). Record none where unstated.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Stated purpose/effect: human strife **divides and harms** — herdsmen's strife forces separation (Gen 13:7–9); "pressing anger produces strife" (Pro 30:33); strife of the flesh marks immaturity (1Cor 3:3). God's controversy **summons to judgment/repentance** (Mic 6:2). The committed cause **secures vindication** (Lam 3:58–59; 1Sa 24:15). Record none where unstated.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Stated purpose/effect: bitterness **"causes trouble" and "defiles many"** (Heb 12:15); it fills the mouth with "curses" (Rom 3:14); it accompanies "the bond of iniquity" (Act 8:23). Its effect is corrupting and contaminating. Record none beyond these.

## T0.2.2 — Across the evidence, does the characteristic's role read as belonging to created design, to the fallen condition, to both, or as not determinable?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** The evidence leans toward the **fallen condition**: anger linked to murder (Gen 4:5–8), to strife and transgression (Pro 29:22), to folly (Ecc 7:9). But the NT shows anger engaging moral evaluation — Jesus angry at hardness of heart (Mar 3:5) — pointing to **created design** for moral response. *Conclusion:* both, with the data weighting the fallen/distorted side (the created-design reading is inferential, grounded only at Mar 3:5).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** God's wrath reads as belonging to God's **holy character** responding to covenant violation — not to a fallen condition (it is His by nature). The causes are consistently sin/idolatry (Deu 6:15; 9:19; 1Ki 11:9). *Conclusion:* belongs to created/uncreated design as a righteous response to evil (it has no "fallen" register; the fallenness is in its objects, not in it).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** The provoke-God strand belongs to the **fallen condition** (idolatry, rebellion — Deu 4:25; 31:29 "do evil... provoking him to anger"); the "stir up to good" strand belongs to **created/redeemed design** (Heb 10:24; 2Cor 9:2). Both — sharply divided by direction.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Both: **zeal and divine jealousy** belong to created/holy design (Num 25:11; Isa 9:7), while **envy** belongs to the fallen condition (Gen 30:1; 37:11; Pro 14:30; Ecc 4:4 "all toil... comes from envy"). The same root spans the right and the disordered forms.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** valence confirms **both**: neutral 29 (descriptive of God's judicial indignation, Isa 13:5) and a moral split — **righteous 4** (godly indignation, 2Cor 7:11; Psa 119:53 "hot indignation... because of the wicked"), **sinful 7 / forbidden 2** (sullen self-pity, 1Ki 21:4; rage against God, Pro 19:3). Divine indignation belongs to holy design; sullen human indignation to the fallen condition.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** valence confirms the split: **sinful 33 / forbidden 1** (the quarrel/strife pole — Pro 17:14; 1Cor 3:3; Tit 3:9) vs **neutral 41** (the judicial-cause and divine-lawsuit pole — Exo 23:2; Mic 6:2). Human quarrelsome strife belongs to the fallen condition; just cause-pleading and the divine lawsuit belong to the moral order of justice.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** valence (sinful 3, forbidden 1) confirms bitterness belongs **wholly to the fallen condition** — it is to be "put away" (Eph 4:31, the forbidden case) and is a mark of the unregenerate mouth (Rom 3:14) and of a heart "not right before God" (Act 8:21,23). No created/holy register appears.

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Where anger engages moral evaluation (Mar 3:5; the master's wrath at the unforgiving servant, Mat 18:34), the aspect of likeness instantiated is **moral responsiveness to wrong**. Inferential; not instantiated where the verses show only sinful anger (Gen 4:5; 1Sa 20:30).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Aspect of divine likeness it grounds in the person: **righteous moral response to evil** — where human anger engages true moral evaluation (C1, Mar 3:5) it images this divine wrath. Inferential link via shared vocabulary.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Divine-likeness aspect: the redeemed capacity to **rouse another toward good** (Heb 10:24) images God's own rousing of His people; the negative capacity images nothing of God (it is the abuse of relational influence).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Divine-likeness aspect: the aspect instantiated in the person is **covenant-protective ardour / zeal for God's honour** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9; 1Ki 19:10) — a true image of God's jealousy; envy is the **counterfeit** that images nothing of God.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Divine-likeness aspect: the aspect instantiated in the person is **righteous displeasure at evil** — "hot indignation... because of the wicked who forsake your law" (Psa 119:53), and the "indignation" of godly repentance (2Cor 7:11). Sinful quarrelling images nothing of God.

## T0.3.2 — Across the evidence, is the characteristic shared between God and the person, or an exclusively creaturely analogue to something in God?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Shared between God and the person: the affect and its core vocabulary (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē) are borne by both (C1 here; C2 for God). It is not an exclusively creaturely analogue (T6.4.1).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Shared: the affect is borne by God (here) and by the person (C1) under the same terms (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē). Not an exclusively creaturely analogue — it is primarily a divine attribute the creature reflects.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Shared/creaturely: the *act* of provoking is creaturely (a person provokes); what is aroused (anger) is shared with God (C2). The capacity to influence another's affect is a creaturely analogue of relational agency.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Shared: jealousy/zeal is borne by both God and the person under the same root (qinˈah/qaˈna). It is **primarily a divine attribute** that the creature reflects (rightly as zeal, wrongly as envy).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Shared: indignation is borne by God (Isa 10:5) and the person (2Cor 7:11) under related vocabulary; it is a divine judicial attribute the creature reflects rightly or distorts into sulking.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Shared/creaturely: just cause-contention is a **creaturely analogue** of God's righteous controversy; quarrelsome strife is a purely creaturely disorder. The rîb vocabulary is shared between God's lawsuit and human dispute.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Shared/creaturely: a purely **creaturely disorder** — not a shared attribute. Record: not borne by God.

## T0.3.3 — Where the characteristic is present or absent in a person, what does that indicate about the condition of the divine image in them — or is no such indication evidenced?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** The verses tie unregulated anger to folly and sin (Pro 29:22 "a man of wrath stirs up strife"; Ecc 7:9 anger resting in the fool's bosom) and restraint to wisdom (Pro 15:18 "slow to anger quiets contention"; Pro 16:32 implied). *Interpretation:* the **regulation** of anger, not its mere presence, is what the evidence treats as indicating the inner condition.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Where God's wrath rests on a person it indicates the person stands under judgment for sin (Deu 9:19; Joh 3:36 "the wrath of God remains on him"); where it is **turned away** (Num 25:11; 2Ch 29:10) it indicates restored covenant standing. The condition indicated is the person's covenant relation, not the image per se.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Where a person habitually provokes God, it indicates a **rebellious covenant condition** (Deu 31:29; 2Ki 17:11 "did wicked things, provoking the LORD"); where a person stirs others to love, it indicates a **formed, edifying disposition** (Heb 10:24).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Where right jealousy/zeal is present it indicates **covenant loyalty** (Phinehas, Num 25:11–13; Elijah, 1Ki 19:10); where envy is present it indicates a **disordered, rotting inner state** (Pro 14:30); where divine jealousy rests against a person it indicates they have **violated the covenant** (Deu 29:20 "the anger and jealousy of the LORD will smoke against that man").

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Where right indignation is present it indicates **alignment with God's moral order** (Psa 119:53; 2Cor 7:11); where sullen indignation is present it indicates **wounded self-will** (1Ki 21:4 Ahab over a vineyard); where God's indignation rests on a person it indicates they stand **under judgment** (Mic 7:9 "I will bear the indignation of the LORD because I have sinned").

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Where a person commits their cause to God it indicates **faith in the righteous Judge** (Jer 11:20); where a person is given to strife it indicates a **fleshly, immature condition** (1Cor 3:3 "you are still of the flesh... there is jealousy and strife among you").

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Where bitterness is present it indicates a **heart not right before God** (Act 8:21,23 "your heart is not right... you are in the gall of bitterness") and a condition that **defiles** community (Heb 12:15). It marks corrupted inner standing.

## T1.1.1 — What is the characteristic called in the programme, and what does the name signal about its essential nature?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** In the programme the characteristic is **Human kindled / burning anger**. The name signals a hot, kindled inner affect — its essential nature is thermal arousal: the dominant senses are "wrath" (69), "angry" (48), "kindled" (18), "hot/heat/burning" (Gen 39:19; Est 1:12; Psa 39:3 vocabulary), and even "poison/venom" (Deut 32:33; Psa 58:4).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Programme name: **Divine wrath**. The name signals God's holy, kindled response to evil; essential nature is **burning judgment** — dominant senses "wrath" (48), "fierce" (19), "kindled" (16), "burning" (7), "fury" (10).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Programme name: **Provoking to anger**. The name signals a **causative, relational act** — arousing anger in another; essential nature is elicitation, not the affect itself. Dominant senses "anger/provoke/provoked/provoking" (kaˈas).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Programme name: **Jealousy / zeal**. The name signals a **passionate, possessive ardour** over what is one's own or one's due; essential nature is intense protective/competitive heat. Senses cluster as jealousy/zeal/envy.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Programme name: **Indignation / displeasure / sullenness**. The name signals a **cooler, settled displeasure** — moral indignation, judicial fury, or sullen vexation — distinct from hot kindled rage. Senses: indignation 18, fury 5, sullen 2, "denounce."

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Programme name: **Strife / quarrel / contention**. The name signals an **external relational/forensic conflict** — strife, quarrel, dispute, lawsuit — not an inner affect. Senses: strife 18, cause 16, dispute 9, case/lawsuit 9, quarrel(ing) 7, indictment 3.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Programme name: **Bitterness**. The name signals a **settled inner sourness/resentment** that corrupts speech and community. Sense: "bitterness" (pikria) in all four.

## T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms show at the definitional level?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** The primary terms show: cheˈmah = heat/rage (root sense "to be hot"); chaˈrah = to burn / be kindled (Gen 4:5–6); orgē = settled wrath (Mat 3:7); qaˈtsaph = to be angry / break out (Num 16:22); aˈnaph = to be angry (lit. of the nostril). At the definitional level the cluster is built on a heat/burning metaphor for an aroused inner state.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Primary terms: cheˈmah (heat/fury), chaˈron "burning anger" (the most God-specific term, Exo 15:7; Num 25:4), chaˈrah (kindled, Exo 22:24), qeˈtseph (wrath/overflow), orgē (NT settled wrath). The definitional core is **intense, kindled heat** belonging to God.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Primary terms: kaˈas (Hebrew, "to provoke/vex to anger," hiphil causative — Deu 4:25; 1Ki 14:9); Greek paroxunō / parorgizō ("provoke, exasperate," Eph 6:4) and parorgismos (Eph 4:26); paroxusmos (a "provoking/stirring," Heb 10:24; Act 15:39). Definitionally the cluster is **causative** — the morphology is largely hiphil (HVhi…), the grammar of *making* another angry.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Primary terms: qinˈah (noun, "jealousy/zeal/ardour") and qaˈna (verb, "to be jealous / envy / be zealous"); the adjective qanˈna/qanˈno ("jealous"), used only of God. The root carries **ardour to the point of heat** — "jealousy is fierce as the grave... a most vehement flame" (Song 8:6).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Primary terms: zaˈam (noun/verb, "indignation; to be indignant / to denounce, curse") — its root carries **to foam/be enraged** and **to denounce/curse**; zaˈaph ("rage, be vexed/sullen"); zalˈaˈphah ("scorching/burning heat"); aganaktēsis (Greek, "indignation"). Definitionally a **displeasure that condemns** — and at one edge **curses** (Num 23:7–8).

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Primary terms: riv (Hebrew, "strife, dispute, legal case, controversy"; verb "to contend/plead a cause") — the dominant term, spanning quarrel and lawsuit; Greek eris ("quarrel, strife," a work of the flesh, Gal 5:20), antilogia ("dispute, hostility"), logomachia ("quarrel about words"). Definitionally a **contention between parties** — interpersonal or forensic.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Primary term: **pikria** (Greek, "bitterness") — from pikros ("sharp, bitter" to taste), used metaphorically for **resentful, harsh inner disposition** (Eph 4:31) and corrupt speech (Rom 3:14). The single term carries the whole characteristic in this set.

## T1.1.3 — What directional, relational, or constitutional implication does the name carry?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** The name carries a **directional and relational** implication: anger is typically *against* or *with* someone (relational markers "against" Num 24:10; "with" Gen 40:2; "upon" 2Ch 32:25). It also carries a **somatic** implication through the co-term aph "nose/anger" (co-occurs as qualifier ×8, e.g. Gen 30:2) and the heat vocabulary.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Directional/relational: God's wrath is **against** persons and nations (Num 11:1; Deu 6:15) and **poured out / sent forth** (Jer 4:4 "go forth like fire"; Lam 2:4; Eze 22:22) — an outward-moving, judicial force.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** The name carries an inherently **relational/directional** implication: provocation is always *of someone* — toward God (Deu 4:25) or toward a person (1Sa 1:6; Eph 6:4). It presupposes a second party.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Directional/relational: jealousy/zeal is inherently **toward** something valued — *for* God/His house (Psa 69:9), *over* a spouse (Num 5:14), *against* a rival (Gen 30:1). It presupposes a valued bond or possession and a perceived threat to it.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Directional/relational: indignation is directed **against** wrongdoers (Psa 69:24; 119:53), expressed **toward** a denounced party (Num 23:7), or turned **inward** as sullenness (1Ki 21:4). It carries a condemnatory direction.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Directional/relational: contention is inherently **between two parties** — "a quarrel between" herdsmen (Gen 13:7), a "cause between a man and his neighbour" (Deu 25:1), God's controversy "with" His people (Hos 4:1). It is irreducibly two-party.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Directional/relational: bitterness is implicitly **against** others — it issues in "curses" against people (Rom 3:14) and "defiles many" relationally (Heb 12:15); it is listed among relationship-destroying vices to put off (Eph 4:31 "bitterness... wrath... anger... clamor... slander"). Record the relational, corrupting direction.

## T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is the characteristic — an act, a disposition, a condition/status, a quality, or something else?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Mixed: it is borne both as a **condition/status** (noun forms cheˈmah/qeˈtseph/orgē — 129 status occurrences, e.g. Est 2:1) and as an **act** (verb forms chaˈrah/qaˈtsaph/aˈnaph — 93 action occurrences, e.g. Gen 4:5); a small dispositional **quality** strand appears (orgilos "quick-tempered", Pro 22:24-type; 5 quality occurrences).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Mainly a **condition/status** (noun forms, 103 occurrences — "the wrath of the LORD," Num 32:14) and an **act** (verb forms, 42 — "was kindled," Gen 18:30). No quality/disposition strand for God here.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** An **act** predominantly (69 action occurrences — "they provoked," 1Ki 14:9) with a residual **status/abstract** strand (16; "provocation," 2Ki 23:26). It is a doing, not a state.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Mixed: a **status/condition** (noun qinˈah, 40 occurrences — "jealousy," Pro 27:4), an **act** (verb qaˈna, 29 — "envied," Gen 30:1), and a **quality** (adjective qanˈna, 8 — "jealous God," Exo 20:5). It is borne as a state, exercised as an act, and predicated as an attribute.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Mainly a **condition/status** (noun forms, 32 — "the indignation of the LORD," Mic 7:9) with an **act** strand (verb forms, 15 — "denounce," Num 23:8; "be indignant") and a small **quality** (2 — "sullen," zaˈeph, 1Ki 20:43 as a descriptive state).

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Predominantly a **condition/status** (noun, 80 of 83) — strife/a dispute/a cause is a *thing* that exists between parties (Pro 17:14 "the beginning of strife"), not chiefly an act of the inner being. (The single affect/act occurrence is the prideful "dispute" of Luk 22:24.)

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** A **condition/status** (all four are status-type nouns) — bitterness is a settled state, not an act. "Gall of bitterness" (Act 8:23), "full of... bitterness" (Rom 3:14), "all bitterness" (Eph 4:31), "root of bitterness" (Heb 12:15) all name a state.

## T1.2.2 — Is the characteristic simple in structure, or does it combine constituent elements; if compound, which?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** It can be **compound**: anger co-occurs with jealousy (qinˈah — co-term ×5, e.g. Pro 27:4), with provocation (kaˈas ×5), with "harm/distress" (raˈah ×7), and with the somatic nose-word aph (×8). Where it stands alone (Gen 31:35) it is simple. The vexation strand (kaˈaˈs) even fuses anger with **grief/sorrow** (senses "grief", "Sorrow" — Ecc 1:18; 1Sa 1:16), a compound with M03.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Often **compound**: wrath fused with **jealousy** (chaˈron alongside qinˈah, Num 25:11; Deu 29:20; Zep 1:18), with **fury/indignation** terms (cheˈmah + zaˈam contexts), and paired with **fire** imagery (Jer 4:4; Deu 32:22). Where it stands alone (Gen 18:30) it is simple.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** **Compound**: provocation joins to **idolatry/evil works** as its means (Deu 4:25 "make a carved image... provoke him"; Jer 25:6 "the work of your hands... to provoke me"); in the positive strand it joins to **zeal/love** (Heb 10:24; 2Cor 9:2). It is rarely simple — it carries its instrument.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** **Compound** — jealousy fuses with **anger/wrath** (Pro 27:4 "wrath... anger... but who can stand before jealousy?"; Deu 29:20 "anger and jealousy"; Zep 1:18 "fire of his jealousy"), and at the negative pole with **envy/covetousness** (Ecc 4:4; Pro 14:30). It is rarely simple — it carries an anger or envy element.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** **Compound**: indignation fuses with **wrath/anger** ("his wrath, indignation, and distress," Psa 78:49; "fury... indignation," Isa 30:27 context) and with **grief** in the godly case (2Cor 7:11 "godly grief... indignation"). The sullen form fuses displeasure with **dejection** (1Ki 21:4). Rarely simple.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** **Compound** where it engages the inner being: strife joins **anger** ("pressing anger produces strife," Pro 30:33), **jealousy** ("jealousy and strife," 1Cor 3:3), **pride** (the dispute over greatness, Luk 22:24), and a cluster of **works of the flesh** (eris, Gal 5:20 context; Rom 1:29). As a bare legal term it is simple (Deu 17:8).

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** **Compound** in three of four — bitterness is listed alongside partner vices: with **iniquity** (Act 8:23 "bond of iniquity"); with **cursing** (Rom 3:14); with **wrath, anger, clamor, slander, malice** (Eph 4:31, co-terms orgē/kakia/blasfēmia); and it "defiles" (miainō, Heb 12:15). It rarely stands alone — it clusters with anger and malice.

## T1.3.1 — What stands against the characteristic as its structural opposite — the inner-being reality that excludes it?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** The structural opposite shown in the evidence is **being slow to anger / a soft answer that turns away wrath** (Pro 15:1; Pro 15:18 "slow to anger quiets contention"). Patience/forbearance stands against kindled anger.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Structural opposite shown: God's wrath **turning away** into mercy/favour — "the LORD turned from his burning anger" (Jos 7:26; 2Ki 23:26; Jon 3:9; Psa 85:3 "you withdrew all your wrath"). Mercy/favour stands against wrath.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Structural opposite: **pleasing/obeying** (the cessation of provocation) — implied where the alternative is to "do what is right in the eyes of the LORD" rather than "provoke" (1Ki 14:8–9 contrast with David). Not lexicalised as a single antonym.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Structural opposite: **security/contentment** (the absence of perceived threat) — implied where envy is contrasted with a "tranquil heart" ("a tranquil heart gives life... but envy makes the bones rot," Pro 14:30). Contentment stands against envy; **indifference** stands against zeal.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Structural opposite: **favour / good pleasure** — implied where indignation is the negative of God's favour ("his indignation" vs the restored "his hand... known to his servants," Isa 66:14 context). Contentment stands against sullenness.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Structural opposite: **peace / a quiet, unifying disposition** — "it is an honour for a man to keep aloof from strife" (Pro 20:3); slowness to anger that "quiets contention" (Pro 15:18). Peace stands against strife.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Structural opposite: **the grace of God** and the kindness that replaces it — Heb 12:15 opposes the bitter root to "the grace of God"; Eph 4:32 opposes the bitterness of 4:31 to "be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving." Grace/kindness/forgiveness stands against bitterness.

## T1.3.2 — What does the characteristic exclude or resist at its edge?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** At its edge it excludes and resists **calm/peaceable speech**: the "soft answer" turns it away (Pro 15:1); restraint "quiets" the contention it would otherwise raise (Pro 15:18).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** At its edge it excludes **continuance** when repentance comes: it is "quenched" (2Ki 22:17; 2Ch 34:25) and resists persisting where the people humble themselves (2Ch 12:7).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** At its edge it excludes **conciliation/obedience**: where the people turn and obey, the provoking ceases (2Ch 33:12–13 Manasseh's repentance ends the provocation). In the human strand the positive opposite of "provoke to anger" is to "provoke to love" (Heb 10:24).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** At its edge it excludes **contentment and trust**: envy resists a tranquil heart (Pro 14:30); divine jealousy excludes **rival worship** ("you shall worship no other god, for the LORD... is a jealous God," Exo 34:14).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** At its edge it excludes **acceptance/blessing**: Balaam cannot "denounce" (zaˈam) whom God has not denounced — "How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?" (Num 23:8). Indignation resists where God has blessed.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** At its edge it excludes **forbearance and unity**: the wise "keep aloof" (Pro 20:3); believers are charged to **avoid** quarrels (Tit 3:9; the Lord's servant "must not be quarrelsome," 2Ti 2:24). Strife resists peaceable forbearance.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** At its edge it excludes **kindness and forgiveness** — the remedy is to "put away... and be kind... forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you" (Eph 4:31–32). Bitterness resists forgiveness.

## T1.3.3 — Where does the characteristic end and another thing begin — what is it not?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** It ends where **grief** begins on one side (the kaˈaˈs vexation/sorrow occurrences sit on that boundary, Ecc 1:18) and where **settled jealousy** begins on another (qinˈah co-occurs but is its own characteristic, C4; Pro 27:4 sets "wrath" beside "jealousy"). It is not mere displeasure (cf. C5 indignation) and not contention itself (C6).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** It ends where **chastening love** and **jealousy** begin (it is bound to covenant, Deu 29:20), and is not arbitrary rage — it is not the human disordered anger of C1 (T6.5).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** It ends where the **resulting anger** (C1/C2) begins (C3 is the eliciting act, the anger is the response) and where mere **contention** (C6) begins (provocation aims at arousing affect, not at the dispute itself).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** It ends where **simple anger** begins (jealousy is anger *over a threatened bond*, Pro 27:4 distinguishes them) and where **covetousness** begins (envy shades into coveting what another has, Ecc 4:4). Divine jealousy is not envy — it is rightful covenant-protectiveness.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** It ends where **hot rage** (C1/C2) begins (indignation is cooler, often moral or sulking) and where **grief** (M03) begins (the sullen/dejected edge, 1Ki 21:4; 2Cor 7:11 pairs it with grief). It is displeasure-with-condemnation, not mere heat.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** It ends where **anger/jealousy** (its inner cause, C1/C4) begins — strife is the *outward event* those affects produce (Pro 30:33; 1Cor 3:3) — and where mere **legal procedure** begins (the inner-being-peripheral "cause/lawsuit," Deu 17:8). It is contention itself, not the affect behind it.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** It ends where **wrath/anger** (C1) shade into a *settled* resentment — bitterness is the **chronic, settled** form listed beside the more acute "wrath and anger" (Eph 4:31), distinguished as the sour residue rather than the hot flare.

## T1.4.1 — In what distinct mode(s) does the characteristic operate within the inner person in this verse — including its grammatical/stem form and the manner of functioning?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Modes in-verse: as a **kindling verb** (qal of chaˈrah, "was kindled", Gen 4:5; morph HVqw3ms); as a **poured-out / executed** state (Eze 7:8 "pour out"; Hos 11:9 "execute"); as a state that **fills** a person (Est 3:5; 5:9 "filled with fury"); as a **burning** (Est 1:12). Grammatically it spans qal verbs and noun-states.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Modes: **kindled** (qal/niphal of chaˈrah, Exo 22:24); **poured out** (Lam 2:4; Eze 22:22; morph of natak/shaphak); **gone forth like fire** (Jer 4:4; 21:12); **sent** (Deu 32:24; Job 20:23); **cast** (Deu 29:28). It operates as both a sudden kindling and a deliberate pouring-out.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Modes: causative **hiphil** "to provoke/make angry" (Deu 4:25, morph HVhi…); a **stirring-up** (paroxusmos, Heb 10:24; Act 15:39); an **exasperating** (parorgizō/erethizō of children, Eph 6:4; Col 3:21). It operates as deliberate or careless arousal of another's affect.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Modes: as a **possessive attribute** (qanˈna "jealous God," Exo 20:5); as a **kindled act** ("envied," Gen 30:1); as a **consuming fire** ("the fire of his jealousy," Zep 1:18; "consumed by my jealousy," Eze 36:5); as a **spirit that comes over** a person ("the spirit of jealousy comes over him," Num 5:14). Grammatically piel/qal verbs and an adjective.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Modes: as **settled judicial fury** (a "staff/rod," Isa 10:5); as a **denouncing speech-act** (Num 23:7–8 "come, denounce"); as **scorching heat** (zalˈaˈphah, Psa 11:6 "scorching wind"); as **sullen withdrawal** (Ahab "vexed and sullen," 1Ki 20:43; 21:4); as **seizing affect** ("hot indignation seizes me," Psa 119:53). Multiple modes.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Modes: a **quarrel/strife** between parties (Gen 13:7); a **legal cause/lawsuit** (Exo 23:2–3; Deu 25:1); a **covenant-lawsuit** God brings (Mic 6:2); a **cause committed to God** for vindication (Jer 11:20; Lam 3:58); **fleshly quarrelling/dissension** in the church (1Cor 3:3; Tit 3:9). Multiple modes.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Modes: as **"gall of bitterness"** (a poisoning condition, Act 8:23); as **bitter speech** ("mouth full of... bitterness," Rom 3:14); as a **disposition to be put off** (Eph 4:31); as a **"root" that springs up** and spreads (Heb 12:15). The root-mode (latent then springing up and defiling) is the most developed.

## T1.4.2 — Does the mode of operation vary by context, direction, or constitutional level; if so, how?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Yes — mode varies by direction and level: directed *upon/against* others (Jos 9:20 "upon"; Num 24:10 "against"), *with* a person (Gen 40:2), or *from* a person/God outward (Exo 32:12 "from"). At the heart level it is an inner seething (Est 5:9 "filled with anger"); as an outward act it is poured out (Eze 14:19).

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Yes — mode varies: kindled *against* a person (Exo 32:10), poured *on* a place (Eze 22:22 Jerusalem), sent *as* plague/sword (Deu 32:24); intensified ("great wrath," Deu 29:28; "hotly," Num 11:10).

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Yes — mode varies by direction: toward **God** it is idolatrous provocation (Deu 4:25); toward **a person** it can be cruel taunting (1Sa 1:6 Peninnah), parental exasperation (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21), heated dispute (Act 15:39), or **constructive incitement to good** (Heb 10:24). The same causative form serves opposite ends.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Yes — mode varies by direction and level: **divine** jealousy as consuming covenant-fire (Zep 1:18); **zealous** ardour acting for God (Isa 9:7 "will do this"); **human** envy as an inward rot (Pro 14:30); the **marital** "spirit of jealousy" rising over a husband (Num 5:14). Same root, distinct modes.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Yes — mode varies: **divine** indignation as judicial instrument (Isa 10:5); **human moral** indignation as righteous heat (Psa 119:53); **human sullen** indignation as dejected withdrawal (1Ki 21:4); **denunciatory** indignation as curse-speech (Num 23:7). Distinct registers.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Yes — mode varies sharply by context: **interpersonal** quarrel (Gen 13:7), **forensic** case (Deu 25:1), **divine** covenant-lawsuit (Hos 4:1; Mic 6:2), **prayerful** cause-committing (Jer 11:20), **ecclesial** dissension (1Cor 3:3). Same term, very different registers.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Yes — mode varies across the four: a present poisoning state (Act 8:23), corrupt speech (Rom 3:14), a vice to renounce (Eph 4:31), a latent-then-spreading root (Heb 12:15). Same noun, four facets.

## T1.4.3 — Does the characteristic operate through a communicative or speech-based mode (commanded, addressed, spoken); if so, how? Record it, or record none.

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Yes — it operates in a **commanded / spoken** mode: "Fret not" / "refrain from anger" addressed as command (Psa 37:1, 37:7, 37:8); the master "in anger said/handed over" (Mat 18:34); anger expressed in denouncing speech (Psa 79:6 "pour out your anger... call on your name"). Recorded where present; elsewhere none.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes — God **declares** his wrath through prophets ("thus says the LORD... my wrath," Eze 5:13 context; Jer 4:4 spoken oracle) and **swears** in wrath ("as I swore in my wrath," Psa 95:11 / Heb 3:11; 4:3). Recorded; elsewhere none.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes — provocation is often **verbal** — Peninnah's taunting (1Sa 1:6–7), the taunt "in the presence of the builders" (Neh 4:1–5), and exhortation "to stir up" (Heb 10:24). Recorded; elsewhere none.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes — God **declares** his jealousy ("I have spoken in my jealous wrath," Eze 36:5–6; Zec 1:14 "I am jealous for Jerusalem... with great jealousy," spoken oracle). Recorded; elsewhere none.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes, prominently — indignation is exercised as **denunciation/cursing** (Num 23:7–8 "denounce"), and God **declares** his indignation (Eze 21:31 "I will pour out my indignation"; Mal 1:4). Recorded.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes, intrinsically — contention is conducted in **speech** (pleadings; "quarrels about words," 1Ti 6:4; the dispute "arose among them," Luk 22:24); God's controversy is a **spoken indictment** (Mic 6:1–2). Recorded.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Communicative/spoken mode: yes — bitterness issues in **speech** ("their mouth is full of curses and bitterness," Rom 3:14). Recorded; the other three are dispositional.

## T1.7.1 — Under what inner conditions does the characteristic take hold or operate rightly?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** It "takes hold rightly" where it engages **moral evaluation** of genuine wrong — Jesus' anger at hardness of heart (Mar 3:5); Moses' anger at the calf (Exo 32:19); anger at outrage/injustice (Gen 34:7; 1Sa 20:34 over Saul's shameful act). The inner condition is a true perception of wrong.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** It "operates rightly" — i.e. is provoked — where there is **covenant violation/idolatry**: other gods (Deu 6:15; 7:4), the golden calf (Deu 9:19), forsaking the LORD (Deu 31:17; 1Ki 11:9). The condition is real sin.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** It "operates" (succeeds in provoking) where there is **idolatry/evil before God** (Deu 4:25; 31:29) or **cruelty/insult between persons** (1Sa 1:6; Neh 4:1). The condition is a real offence.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** It "takes hold rightly" where there is a **genuine threat to a rightful bond** — God's covenant honour (Num 25:11; Exo 34:14), a husband's exclusive marriage (Num 5:14), God's house (Psa 69:9). The condition is a valued, rightful relation under threat.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** It "takes hold rightly" (valence righteous) where it is **moral indignation at evil** — "hot indignation... because of the wicked who forsake your law" (Psa 119:53) and the indignation of genuine repentance (2Cor 7:11). The condition is alignment with God's standard.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** It "operates" (rightly, valence neutral) as **just cause-pleading** — the oppressed whose cause God pleads (Pro 22:23; 23:11), the innocent committing their cause to God (Jer 11:20; 1Sa 24:15). The condition is a genuine claim of justice.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Conditions where it "takes hold": bitterness takes root in a **heart not right before God** (Act 8:21,23) and where the community **"fails to obtain the grace of God"** (Heb 12:15). The condition is a grace-deficient, unforgiving heart.

## T1.7.2 — Under what inner conditions is the characteristic blocked, distorted, resisted, or not taken up — including, where the evidence shows it, distortion or interference by another spirit (adversarial or angelic)?

No verse in the C1 set attributes the distortion to another (adversarial/angelic) spirit — **silent** on that sub-point.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** It is **turned away / blocked** by intercession (Moses, Psa 106:23 context; Num 16:46–48 Aaron's atonement), by repentance and humbling (2Ch 12:7; Jon 3:9–10), and by zealous covenant-action (Phinehas, Num 25:11). - **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** It is **blocked** where the would-be provoked party does not take it up — Hannah ultimately pours out her soul to God rather than retaliate (1Sa 1:15–16); the wise overlook an insult (Pro 12:16-type). - **[C7 — Bitterness]** Where bitterness takes hold, the person is shown **bound in iniquity and unforgiven** until repentance (Act 8:23) and the community **defiled** (Heb 12:15). It is a corrupting, spreading condition.

## T7.1.8 — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew and NT Greek vocabulary show about continuity or development of the characteristic across the Testaments?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew heat-cluster (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, qeˈtseph) is carried into Greek by **orgē / orgizō** (settled wrath) with the NT adding the explicit pairing of anger with **moral evaluation** (Mar 3:5; Mat 18:34, faculty affect|moral_evaluation) and the **eschatological "wrath to come"** register (Mat 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16). Continuity of the affect; development toward moral-judgment and eschatological framing.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew burning-cluster (cheˈmah/chaˈron/qeˈtseph) is rendered by **orgē** in the NT, where divine wrath becomes the **eschatological "the wrath"** (Rom 9:22; Rev 14:10) and is grounded in the revelation of God's righteousness (Rom 1:18). Strong continuity; development toward eschatological consummation.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew causative kaˈas (provoke God to anger) carries into Greek parorgizō/parorgismos (Eph 4:26; 6:4), but the NT notably **broadens** the root toward the positive **paroxusmos** "stir up to good" (Heb 10:24) and the redemptive "provoke to jealousy" (Rom 10:19). Continuity of the causative idea; development toward a redeemable/positive use.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** OT→NT relationship: C4's data is **OT-only** (qinˈah/qaˈna). The NT counterpart (zēlos/zēloō, and the rivalry terms eritheia/phthonos) is carried in the **C6 strife** and broader cluster vocabulary rather than in C4's focus set; per the science extract the NT continues both the zeal and envy poles. Within C4 proper, continuity is **inferred**, not present in the OT-only set — labelled inferential.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew zaˈam/zaˈaph indignation family is represented in the NT by **aganaktēsis** (2Cor 7:11), where indignation appears in a **positive, sanctifying** light (the fruit of godly grief) — a notable development from the predominantly judicial/sullen OT range. Continuity of the concept; development toward a redeemable register.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew riv (covenant-controversy and just cause) gives way in the NT to **eris/eritheia/logomachia** as **vices of the flesh** to be put off (Gal 5:20; 1Cor 3:3; Tit 3:9), while righteous advocacy is carried by other vocabulary. Continuity of "contention"; development that **moralises** quarrelling as a fleshly vice and relocates righteous advocacy.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** OT→NT relationship and **scope gap**: this focus set is **NT-only (pikria)**. The **Hebrew bitterness field is absent from the set** — marah/mar ("bitter; bitterness of soul"): Naomi "call me Mara, for the Almighty has dealt very bitterly" (Ruth 1:20–21); Hannah "bitterness of soul" (1Sa 1:10); Job "I will speak in the bitterness of my soul" (Job 7:11; 10:1); "the bitter in soul" (Job 3:20); Hezekiah (Isa 38:15,17). That field includes **grief-laden, non-sinful** bitterness (bitterness of affliction), which the NT pikria set does **not** capture. **This is a known gap**: C7 as constituted reflects only the NT resentment-bitterness, not the OT affliction-bitterness. Flagged for researcher review and possible cross-cluster check with M03 (Grief). *(Inferential/flagged — not in the focus set.)*

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** NT-coined term: **parorgismos** ("provocation/anger," Eph 4:26 context) is a rare, largely NT/biblical-Greek formation; **orgilos** is the trait-adjective. The coinage marks a sharpened concern with **provoked anger and the angry disposition** in the NT.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** NT-coined term: **orgē** is not newly coined but is given a fixed eschatological-technical sense ("the wrath," ho orgē, Rom 5:9; 1Th 1:10). Record: semantic specialisation rather than coinage.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** NT term: **paroxusmos** used positively (Heb 10:24) and as "sharp disagreement" (Act 15:39); **parorgismos** (Eph 4:26). These mark a NT refinement distinguishing destructive from constructive provocation.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** NT-coined term: none in C4's OT-only set. Record none (the NT zēlos vocabulary lies outside this focus set).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** NT-coined term: **aganaktēsis** (and the verb aganakteō) is the NT indignation term; not newly coined but applied here to **righteous repentant indignation** (2Cor 7:11). Record the refined positive use.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** NT term: **logomachia/logomacheō** ("word-battle," 1Ti 6:4; 2Ti 2:14) is a distinctively NT-era formation marking **doctrinal quarrelling**; **eritheia** ("selfish rivalry/factionalism") likewise. Their coinage marks a NT concern with **church-dividing strife**. Recorded.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** NT-coined term: pikria is not NT-coined (classical Greek), but its **metaphorical-ethical use** for resentful disposition (Eph 4:31) and the **"root of bitterness"** covenant-allusion (Heb 12:15, echoing Deu 29:18 LXX) are characteristic NT/LXX developments. Recorded.

## T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc show about the characteristic's complete semantic range?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Full vocabulary arc: from a **heat/burning** core (chaˈrah, cheˈmah) the range fans out to settled **wrath** (orgē), a **trait** (orgilos), a **toxic** extreme (venom, Deut 32:33), and a **grief-tinged** edge (kaˈaˈs vexation) — a complete range from momentary kindling to settled disposition to eschatological judgment.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Full vocabulary arc: from **kindled burning** (chaˈrah/chaˈron) through **settled fury** (cheˈmah/qeˈtseph) to the NT **eschatological wrath** (orgē) — a complete arc from covenant-judgment in history to final judgment.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Full vocabulary arc: from the OT **causative provocation of God to wrath** (kaˈas, Deu 4:25) through interpersonal **exasperation** (Eph 6:4) to the NT **constructive stirring** (Heb 10:24) — a complete arc from covenant-rebellion to relational edification.

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Full vocabulary arc: a **single root** holding the full range from **divine covenant-jealousy** (Exo 34:14) through **human zeal for God** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9) to **rivalrous envy** (Gen 30:1; Pro 14:30) — the most semantically unified term in the cluster, its valence set entirely by direction and object.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Full vocabulary arc: from OT **judicial/denunciatory/sullen** displeasure (zaˈam/zaˈaph) to NT **righteous repentant** indignation (aganaktēsis) — an arc from condemnation and sulking to sanctified moral indignation.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Full vocabulary arc: from the OT **riv** holding quarrel, lawsuit, covenant-controversy, and just advocacy in one root, to the NT splitting of **fleshly strife** (eris/eritheia/logomachia, to be put off) from righteous advocacy — an arc from a unified contention-term to a moralised division between sinful quarrelling and just contention.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Full vocabulary arc: within this set, a **single noun** (pikria) presenting bitterness as a **uniformly sinful, settled, contaminating disposition** — poison (Act 8:23), corrupt speech (Rom 3:14), vice to renounce (Eph 4:31), defiling root (Heb 12:15). The fuller biblical arc (including OT affliction-bitterness) lies outside the set (T7.1.8).

## T7.2.1 — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Gen 4:5 chaˈrah is the **main predicate of Cain's state** ("Cain was very angry"), driving the narrative toward the first murder; the term is the hinge of the episode. In Pro 15:18 cheˈmah is the **subject** whose action ("stirs up strife") the proverb contrasts with the slow-to-anger.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Exo 32:10 "let my wrath burn hot against them that I may consume them" — chaˈrah/cheˈmah is the **decisive predicate** of God's judicial intent, which Moses' intercession then turns (Exo 32:11–14). The term carries the covenant crisis.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Deu 4:25 kaˈas is the **purpose-clause verb** ("make a carved image... to provoke him to anger") — the term names the offence that the whole warning turns on. In Heb 10:24 paroxusmos is the **goal** of mutual consideration ("how to stir one another up").

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Exo 34:14 qanˈna is the **predicate nominative defining God** ("the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God") — the term *names* God and grounds the prohibition of other gods. In Num 25:11 qinˈah is the **causal ground** of Phinehas' commended act and reward.

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Num 23:7–8 zaˈam ("denounce") is the **verb Balaam is hired to perform and cannot** — the term carries the whole drama of the failed curse ("How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?"). In Isa 10:5 "indignation" is the **noun defining the Assyrian's commission** as God's instrument.

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Mic 6:2 riv ("indictment/controversy") is the **governing legal metaphor** of the oracle. In Pro 30:33 riv ("strife") is the **result clause** — "pressing anger produces strife" — naming the social fruit of the inner affect.

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Eph 4:31 pikria **heads the list** of vices to "put away," functioning as the **root resentment** from which the following (wrath, anger, clamor, slander) flow — the term governs the call to renounce relational sin. In Heb 12:15 it is the **subject of the warning** ("root of bitterness... defiles many").

## T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of the key arguments in the verse evidence — premises and conclusions?

- **[C1 — Human kindled / burning anger]** Logical structure of the key arguments: the wisdom argument is **conditional-consequential** — *if* a man is wrathful/hasty, *then* strife and transgression follow (Pro 15:18; 29:22); the narrative argument is **sequential-causal** — provocation → anger → act (Gen 4:5–8). Premise: anger perceives/responds to wrong; conclusion: its unrestrained issue is destructive.

- **[C2 — Divine wrath]** Logical structure: **covenant-conditional** — *if* the people forsake the LORD / serve other gods, *then* His wrath is kindled (Deu 6:14–15; 7:4; 31:16–17). Premise: God is holy and covenant-committed; conclusion: covenant breach draws consuming judgment, relentable on repentance.

- **[C3 — Provoking to anger]** Logical structure: **conditional-consequential** in the covenant strand — *if* you provoke God by idolatry, *then* wrath/judgment follows (Deu 4:25–26; 31:29); **imperatival** in the epistolary strand — *do not* provoke (Eph 6:4), *do* stir up to good (Heb 10:24).

- **[C4 — Jealousy / zeal]** Logical structure: **covenant-exclusive** — *because* the LORD is a jealous God, *therefore* no other gods/idols (Exo 20:3–5; 34:14); **wisdom-consequential** — *if* the heart envies, *then* it rots / strays (Pro 14:30; 23:17); **narrative-exemplary** — Phinehas' jealousy → atonement and covenant (Num 25:11–13).

- **[C5 — Indignation]** Logical structure: **judicial-consequential** in the divine texts — sin/godlessness → the staff of indignation → bounded judgment (Isa 10:5–6,25); **narrative-causal** in the sullen texts — denial → sullen indignation → scheme (1Ki 21:4–7); **fruit-structure** in the NT — godly grief → indignation among its fruits (2Cor 7:11).

- **[C6 — Strife / quarrel]** Logical structure: **wisdom-consequential** — *if* anger/hatred presses, *then* strife results; *therefore* keep aloof (Pro 30:33; 20:3; 17:14); **covenant-forensic** — premise: covenant unfaithfulness; God's indictment; call to repentance (Mic 6:1–8); **vice-list** — strife among the works of the flesh to be put off (Gal 5:19–21; 1Cor 3:3).

- **[C7 — Bitterness]** Logical structure: **imperatival** (Eph 4:31 "put away"; Heb 12:15 "see to it... that no root"), **diagnostic** (Act 8:23 "you are in the gall of bitterness"), and **indictment-by-citation** (Rom 3:14, evidencing universal depravity). The structure is consistently **condemnatory/remedial**.

# Cluster-wide synthesis findings (M02)

## F1 — Two vocabulary architectures in one cluster *(guide: T1, T7)*

**Observation.** The cluster does not run on a single vocabulary. A **shared burning-heat family** — cheˈmah, chaˈrah, chaˈron, qaˈtsaph, qeˈtseph, kaˈaˈs, aˈnaph, orgē — supplies **C1 (human anger), C2 (divine wrath), and C3 (provoking)** and *only* those three. The lemma map is unambiguous: cheˈmah appears in C1/C2/C3 (60/51/4), chaˈrah in C1/C2/C3 (56/26/6), orgē in C1/C2/C3 (23/5/5), and so on for every burning-heat term. By contrast **C4 (jealousy/zeal: qinˈah/qaˈna), C5 (indignation: zaˈam/zaˈaph), C6 (strife: riv/eris), and C7 (bitterness: pikria) each run on a distinct root that is shared with no other characteristic.**

**Interpretation.** M02 is therefore **one affect under three relations plus four distinct affects/events**. C1/C2/C3 are not three vocabularies but one heat-architecture differentiated by *relation to the subject*: the same kindling is **experienced** (C1, human bearer), **borne** (C2, divine bearer), or **aroused** (C3, God or a person as object). The boundary between them is relational, not lexical — which is why their edges leak (see F10). C4–C7, having their own roots, are genuinely separate inner phenomena that sit in the cluster by conceptual kinship and co-occurrence, not by shared word-stock. The "burning/heat" root meaning (chaˈron "burning"; cheˈmah "heat, fury, also venom/poison" — Deu 32:33; Psa 58:4) is the conceptual centre of gravity; jealousy adds "ardour," indignation "denunciation/scorching," strife "contention," bitterness "sourness."

## F2 — The Divine–Human correspondence runs through the whole cluster *(guide: T0, T4)*

**Observation.** God appears in a defined role in a large share of occurrences: as **bearer** (possessor/agent) of wrath (C2: 144), jealousy (C4: 22), and indignation (C5: 18); as the **object** provoked to anger (C3: 60) and to jealousy (C4: 8); as **contending party** in the covenant-lawsuit (C6: agent 4, object 5); and as **addressee** in prayer across several (C1, C5, C6). The same vocabulary that names God's wrath (cheˈmah/chaˈron) names human anger (C1); the same root that names God's jealousy (qanˈna, used *only* of God — Exo 20:5; 34:14) names human zeal and envy (C4).

**Interpretation.** Anger, wrath, jealousy, and indignation are presented **primarily as divine attributes that the creature images** — rightly (Phinehas' zeal "with my jealousy," Num 25:11; Moses' anger at the calf, Exo 32:19; righteous indignation, Psa 119:53) or in distortion (envy, Gen 30:1; sullen indignation, 1Ki 21:4). This is the cluster's strongest contribution to the programme's Divine-Human Correspondence dimension: the inner capacity for kindled moral response is a point of likeness to God, and its disorder is the corruption of that likeness. **Bitterness (C7) is the one exception** — never borne by God, set explicitly against "the grace of God" (Heb 12:15); it images nothing of God and has no righteous form.

## F3 — One cluster, three constitutional kinds *(guide: T2, T3)*

**Observation.** By the term-intrinsic **faculty** field the cluster splits sharply:
- **C1–C5 are affects** — faculty=affect dominates (C1 196/228, C2 140/145, C3 78/85, C4 70/77, C5 45/49), frequently overlapping **moral_evaluation** (C1 31; C4's "jealous God" tagged moral_evaluation).
- **C6 is a relational event** — faculty-NONE in 82/83 (strife is not an inner state but a contention between parties).
- **C7 is a settled disposition** — faculty-NONE in all 4 (bitterness named as a condition, not analysed as a faculty-act).

Where a **constitutional seat** is named (sparse — 45/671), it is the **heart** (dominant), **soul**, and notably the **body**: envy "makes the bones rot" (Pro 14:30, heart|flesh); divine indignation leaves "no soundness in my flesh" (Psa 38:3); zeal reaches the "inward parts" (Isa 63:15). By **type**, the cluster is **status-heavy** (the affects are named as states more than acts), the exception being **C3 (provoking), which is action-dominant** (69/85) because it names a *doing*.

**Interpretation.** M02 is not a uniform field of "emotions." It holds (i) genuine **inner affects** seated in the heart/soul and at times felt in the body, (ii) an **external relational event** (strife) that the affects *produce* but which is not itself an inner state, and (iii) a **settled resentful disposition** (bitterness). This tripartite shape governs how the cluster relates to the inner being: C1–C5 are constitutive of it; C6 is downstream of it; C7 is a chronic corruption of it. The status-heavy typing suggests Scripture treats anger more as a **condition one is in** than an act one does — except provocation, which is inherently an act done to another.

**Interpretation.** The valence is patterned, not random. **Divine-borne** characteristics (C2) are overwhelmingly **neutral/righteous** — God's wrath is never sin. **Human-Godward** provocation (C3) is overwhelmingly **sinful** — provoking God is the gravest misuse. **Jealousy/zeal (C4)** is the most balanced — its single root carries righteous zeal (24), sinful envy (16), and neutral/legal jealousy (28), confirming its tri-valent character. **Strife (C6)** divides between neutral just-cause and sinful quarrelling. **Bitterness (C7)** is uniquely **wholly negative**. The cluster as a whole demonstrates that the *same* inner capacity (kindled response) is righteous, neutral, or sinful **depending on who bears it and toward whom it is directed** — the programme's clearest case that moral valence is set by relation, not by the affect itself.

## F5 — The cluster has an internal causal structure *(guide: T6)*

**Observation.** The characteristics are not a flat list; the co-occurrence and "produced-by" data show a chain:
- **Provocation (C3) → anger/jealousy (C1/C2/C4):** "provoked him to jealousy... to anger" (Deu 32:16,21); "provoked him... full of wrath" (Psa 78:58–59). C3 is action-typed and causally prior.
- **Anger/jealousy/hatred/pride → strife (C6):** "pressing anger produces strife" (Pro 30:33); "jealousy and strife" of the flesh (1Cor 3:3); "hatred stirs up strife" (Pro 10:12); the prideful dispute over greatness (Luk 22:24). C6 is faculty-NONE and causally downstream — the relational *output* of the affects.
- **Bitterness (C7)** sits as the **settled root** heading the anger list — "bitterness and wrath and anger and clamor" (Eph 4:31), "root of bitterness springs up" (Heb 12:15) — i.e. the chronic residue underlying the louder forms (inferential, from word-order and the root-image).
- **Indignation (C5)** is the **cooler/judicial sibling** of wrath, paired with it ("wrath, indignation," Psa 78:49) and shading toward grief at its sullen edge (1Ki 21:4) and its godly-grief edge (2Cor 7:11).

**Interpretation.** Read structurally, M02 maps a **lifecycle of kindled response**: a provocation arouses an affect (anger, jealousy, or the cooler indignation), the affect is borne rightly or sinfully, and — if unmastered — it issues outward as strife or settles inward as bitterness. The cluster is therefore not seven parallel topics but a **connected system** with provocation at the front, the affects at the centre, and strife/bitterness as the two terminal outputs (outward and inward). This is the natural spine for any Session B/C synthesis and for the eventual constitutional mapping.

## F6 — The relational interfaces are symmetrical and reversible *(guide: T4, T5)*

**Observation.** Every direction of the inner-being relational map is populated:
- **God → human:** wrath (Exo 22:24), jealousy *for* and *against* (Zec 1:14; Deu 29:20), indignation (Isa 10:5), covenant-lawsuit (Mic 6:2).
- **Human → God:** provoking to anger/jealousy (Deu 4:25; 32:21), zeal *for* God (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9), bearing God's indignation penitently (Mic 7:9), committing one's cause to God (Jer 11:20).
- **Human → human:** anger (Gen 4:5), envy (Gen 30:1), strife (Gen 13:7), provocation (1Sa 1:6), bitterness/cursing (Rom 3:14) — and the **positive** "stir up to love" (Heb 10:24).

A recurring feature is **reversibility/turnability**: divine wrath and indignation are repeatedly "turned away," "quenched," or "relented" on repentance/intercession (Jos 7:26; 2Ki 22:17; Jon 3:10; Exo 32:14); envy is corrected "in the sanctuary" (Psa 73:17); bitterness is "put away" and replaced by kindness (Eph 4:31–32).

**Interpretation.** The cluster portrays the inner being as **relationally embedded and morally plastic**: these affects arise *within* relationships (covenant, family, community) and *bear on* them, and most are presented as **changeable** — God's by His own will toward the penitent, the human's by repentance, reframing, or deliberate put-off. The affect is rarely treated as fixed fate; it is something turned, quenched, or put away. (Strife and bitterness, the terminal outputs, are correspondingly addressed by *prevention* — keep aloof, watch the root — Pro 20:3; Heb 12:15.)

## F7 — Anger in M02 is densely bound to sin and to the moral order *(guide: T3.8, T6, T7)*

**Observation.** Beyond the anger lemmas, the most frequent cluster-wide co-occurrence partners are **sin and evil vocabulary**: chaˈta "to sin" (15), chatˈtat "sin" (13), raˈah "evil/distress/harm" (12+9). Anger also co-occurs heavily with **the heart** (lev "co-seated," 10), **the face** (aph, the anger idiom, 14), **destruction** (shaˈchat "to ruin" 10; kaˈlah "to consume/expend" 10), and **hearing** (shaˈma "to hear," 13) and **serving** (aˈvad, 11 — idolatrous service). The faculty overlap **affect|moral_evaluation** is itself substantial (C1 31; C4 5; C3 5).

**Interpretation.** The cluster's affects are not free-floating feelings; they are **welded to the moral order**. Anger appears as a response *to* sin (provocation by idolatry/evil, aˈvad/chaˈta), as God's verdict *against* sin (wrath, chatˈtat), and as itself morally evaluated (the affect|moral_evaluation overlap). The "hearing" co-occurrence ties anger to the covenant word heard and disobeyed (and resonates with Registry 213 *listen*). The destruction co-occurrence (shaˈchat/kaˈlah) shows the affects' frequent terminal in judgment. This is the cluster's deepest theological observation: **M02's inner states are the affective dimension of the covenant moral order** — kindled by its breach, expressed as its enforcement, and themselves judged by it.

## F8 — Developmental and eschatological orientation *(guide: T5)*

**Observation.** Several characteristics carry an explicit forward horizon: divine wrath as the coming "wrath" (Rom 9:22; Rev 14:10; 16:19) and bounded "indignation" with an appointed end (Isa 10:25; Dan 11:36); jealousy/zeal as the "zeal of the LORD" that "will do this" (Isa 9:7; 37:32) and the consuming "fire of my jealousy" at the end (Zep 3:8); strife as awaiting the "day of recompense for the cause of Zion" (Isa 34:8). Within the sanctification arc, the cluster supplies both vices to put off (anger, bitterness, strife — Eph 4:31; Col 3:8) and a righteous indignation that is *fruit* of repentance (2Cor 7:11).

**Interpretation.** The cluster is **eschatologically loaded on its divine side and formatively addressed on its human side**: God's wrath/jealousy/indignation point to a consummation of judgment; the human affects are placed under a programme of formation (put off the sinful forms, exercise the righteous — zeal, godly indignation). The same word (e.g. indignation) is at once a divine end-time instrument and a sanctifying human fruit — a compact instance of the cluster's divine-human correspondence across time.

## F10 — Data-quality and method observations *(guide: T7.3.4; carried for researcher review)*

**Flags.**
1. **C1/C2 boundary leakage.** Because C1↔C2 are one vocabulary split only by divine_involvement (a read-resolved field), their edges leak: divine-subject verses (Exo 4:14; 2Ki 13:3) fell into C1, and C2 carries **7 occurrences tagged valence=sinful** — anomalous for God's wrath. These need verse-confirmation; the split rests on a read-resolved field and so carries a **circularity caution** (the boundary cannot be validated from the same read field without independent verse re-reading).
