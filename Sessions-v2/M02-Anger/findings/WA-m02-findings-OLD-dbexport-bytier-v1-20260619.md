The name signals three distinct registers within one essential phenomenon: (1) anger as the broad category of the reactive morally-charged inner state; (2) wrath, implying the heavier judicial and covenantal register — settled, purposeful, and consequential; (3) indignation, implying the more principled and sustained moral-displeasure register. The name correctly signals that this cluster is not a simple single phenomenon but a family of related states sharing a common root in moral reactive passion.

## T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms show at the definitional level?

- **[consolidated — whole cluster]** The primary Hebrew terms: *cha.rah* (to kindle/be incensed) — a verb root meaning to burn or kindle, foregrounding the heat metaphor as definitional; *che.mah* (rage/wrath) — related to heat, the noun of the burning interior state; *cha.ron* (burning anger) — an intensifying noun specifically for the burning quality of anger at its most intense; *orgē* (Greek, wrath) — at the definitional level, a settled, purposeful inner disposition; *orgizō* (Greek, to anger) — the verb of the anger-event, the eruption. These terms reveal that the characteristic is definitionally thermal and reactive at root — it is the inner being heating up in response to moral violation. The Greek *orgē* adds the judicial-structural dimension absent from the pure-heat Hebrew vocabulary.

## T1.1.3 — What directional, relational, or constitutional implication does the name carry?

- **[consolidated — whole cluster]** The name carries relational implications from the outset: anger, wrath, and indignation are inherently directed — they require an object (the wrongdoing, the person who committed it, the situation that is wrong). The cluster is not about a free-floating inner temperature but about a reactive state with a specific directional structure: something is perceived as wrong and the inner being fires against it. The directionality is intrinsic to the characteristic's definition.

## T7.3.1 — Which human-science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) serves as the most useful interpretive lens for this characteristic?

- **[consolidated — whole cluster]** Three frameworks are relevant and identified in the science extract: (1) Clinical and social psychology (emotion theory) for personal anger — Averill, Berkowitz, Tavris, Spielberger, Lakoff-Kövecses. (2) Moral psychology for indignation — Haidt, Aristotle's orge, third-party punishment research. (3) Evolutionary and social psychology for jealousy — Buss, Parrott and Smith, Festinger. The most useful single framework for the cluster as a whole is the appraisal theory of emotion (Lazarus, Smith and Ellsworth), which specifies that anger is produced by appraising an event as negative, caused by another agent, and deserving of blame. This framework captures the blame-attribution structure that underlies virtually all M02's provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-01: idolatry is blameworthy, it causes God's anger, it has a responsible agent).

## T7.3.2 — Where the framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it show?

- **[char 72 · Burning Rage and Inner Heat]** The heat metaphor research (Lakoff-Kövecses) illuminates M02-B: the thermal vocabulary (burning, kindling, scorching, pouring) is not merely figurative but captures the physiological reality of anger — increased body temperature, arousal, heat. The science extract confirms: "anger is a hot, high-arousal state. Cognitively, it is constituted by the appraisal of wrongdoing and the attribution of blame. The cluster's thermal vocabulary sits at the intersection of the somatic and metaphorical dimensions of anger — both are real." The thermal vocabulary in M02-B is simultaneously metaphorical and physically accurate.

- **[char 74 · Provocation — Anger Aroused]** Appraisal theory illuminates M02-D-VCG-01 (idolatry provoking divine anger): the blame-attribution structure is explicit across the 47-verse corpus — the wrongdoing is assessed as blameworthy (wilful, deliberate, in defiance of repeated warnings: 2Ki 22:17; Jer 44:3), caused by identifiable agents (Israel's kings, the people), and deserving of consequence. This is the full appraisal structure Lazarus describes. The science framework makes explicit what the biblical text presupposes.

- **[char 75 · Jealousy, Zeal and Possessive Passion]** Evolutionary psychology's jealousy research (Buss) illuminates M02-E-VCG-04: spousal jealousy (Num 5:14–30) is explained by evolutionary psychology as an adaptation protecting valued relationships — the biblical law creates formal adjudication procedures for jealousy because it is treated as a real, judicially significant inner state whose validity God must adjudicate. The science framework illuminates why the law takes this seriously: jealousy over a valued relational bond is a cross-culturally consistent, deep psychological response.

## T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the framework diverge, what does the divergence show?

- **[char 71 · Divine Wrath as Judicial Force]** Three key divergences: (1) Divine wrath as holy, ontologically grounded response: science has no category for wrath as the morally appropriate response of a holy being to evil — science treats divine wrath either as a projection of human anger or as a theological category outside its scope. The science extract notes: "divine wrath as a category is the deepest divergence — science has no analogue for holy wrath as a morally appropriate, ontologically grounded response to evil." (2) Righteous anger as virtue: science more readily pathologises anger (treatment typically aims at reduction); Scripture presents certain anger as not only permissible but required by moral seriousness. Aristotle's orge is the closest science comes to righteous anger as virtue, and the science extract acknowledges this explicitly. (3) Catharsis model failure: Tavris's research shows that venting anger typically amplifies rather than reduces it — this is consistent with some biblical wisdom (anger stored and managed rather than vented), but the biblical pattern of God's anger being "poured out" and "spent" describes a divine anger whose expression resolves it, not amplifies it. The human catharsis-model failure does not straightforwardly apply to the divine-anger register.

## T7.3.4 — Does the framework surface any aspect of the characteristic the verse evidence has not yet addressed, and does that absence call for further verse investigation?

- **[char 72 · Burning Rage and Inner Heat]** Yes. The science extract notes two potential gaps: (1) "The role of rumination in sustaining anger (Nolen-Hoeksema applied to anger) may surface gaps in how the cluster's sustained-wrath vocabulary is classified." The sustained-wrath corpus (M02-B-VCG-05: Psa 37:1–8 warning against fretting/agitation) addresses the rumination-adjacent phenomenon — "fretting" is a form of sustained mental engagement with the anger-producing stimulus. The biblical counsel to cease fretting is consistent with rumination research showing that sustained mental re-engagement with the anger-stimulus prolongs the anger. This suggests M02-B-VCG-05 may be partially about breaking the rumination cycle, a connection the verse evidence supports but does not name explicitly.

(2) "Jealousy's attachment dimension (anxious versus secure attachment predicting jealousy sensitivity) may illuminate differences in the cluster's jealousy terms." M02-E-VCG-04's possessive spousal jealousy (Num 5:14 — jealousy as a spirit coming over a man regardless of wife's guilt or innocence) is consistent with anxious-attachment-driven jealousy in the science literature — the jealousy is not proportionate to actual threat but to the internal attachment anxiety. This connection is noted as a gap for further verse investigation: are there indicators in the corpus that distinguish secure-attachment jealousy (appropriate protective response) from anxious-attachment jealousy (disproportionate possessive fear)?
