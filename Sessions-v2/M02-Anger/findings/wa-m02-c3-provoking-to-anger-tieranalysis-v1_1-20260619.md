## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Relation to God: in the dominant strand God is the **object** provoked — "they provoked him to anger with their high places" (1Ki 14:9; Deu 4:25; Jer 25:6–7). God is not the bearer but the one whose anger is aroused. In the human strand God is not in view (1Sa 1:6).

**T0.1.2** — Across C3 God appears chiefly as the **provoked party** (object, 60 occ), not the bearer. *Interpretation:* the characteristic names the human capacity to **arouse anger in another** — and, gravely, in God; its place in the person is as the relational act that draws covenant wrath (a fallen-direction capacity), with a redeemed counterpart (stirring to good, Heb 10:24).

**T0.2.1** — Stated purpose/effect: provoking God **brings down wrath/judgment** ("to provoke me to anger... I will set my face against," Eze 8:17–18 context; "provoked the LORD... so that he removed them," 2Ki 17:18-type). The positive strand states a constructive effect — "stir up one another to love and good works" (Heb 10:24). Record none where unstated.

**T0.2.2** — The provoke-God strand belongs to the **fallen condition** (idolatry, rebellion — Deu 4:25; 31:29 "do evil... provoking him to anger"); the "stir up to good" strand belongs to **created/redeemed design** (Heb 10:24; 2Cor 9:2). Both — sharply divided by direction.

**T0.2.3** — Future-fullness orientation: in the positive strand, "provoke to jealousy" is oriented toward Israel's future **salvation** (Rom 10:19; cf. 11:11 context — provoking to jealousy "to make Israel jealous"). In the negative strand, none. Record the positive orientation where present.

**T0.3.1** — Divine-likeness aspect: the redeemed capacity to **rouse another toward good** (Heb 10:24) images God's own rousing of His people; the negative capacity images nothing of God (it is the abuse of relational influence).

**T0.3.2** — Shared/creaturely: the *act* of provoking is creaturely (a person provokes); what is aroused (anger) is shared with God (C2). The capacity to influence another's affect is a creaturely analogue of relational agency.

**T0.3.3** — Where a person habitually provokes God, it indicates a **rebellious covenant condition** (Deu 31:29; 2Ki 17:11 "did wicked things, provoking the LORD"); where a person stirs others to love, it indicates a **formed, edifying disposition** (Heb 10:24).

**T0.4.1** — Typological use: "provoke to jealousy" is used **redemptive-historically** — the Gentiles' inclusion to provoke Israel to jealousy unto salvation (Rom 10:19), pointing beyond the immediate. Direction: human action serving the divine purpose. Elsewhere none.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Programme name: **Provoking to anger**. The name signals a **causative, relational act** — arousing anger in another; essential nature is elicitation, not the affect itself. Dominant senses "anger/provoke/provoked/provoking" (kaˈas).

**T1.1.2** — Primary terms: kaˈas (Hebrew, "to provoke/vex to anger," hiphil causative — Deu 4:25; 1Ki 14:9); Greek paroxunō / parorgizō ("provoke, exasperate," Eph 6:4) and parorgismos (Eph 4:26); paroxusmos (a "provoking/stirring," Heb 10:24; Act 15:39). Definitionally the cluster is **causative** — the morphology is largely hiphil (HVhi…), the grammar of *making* another angry.

**T1.1.3** — The name carries an inherently **relational/directional** implication: provocation is always *of someone* — toward God (Deu 4:25) or toward a person (1Sa 1:6; Eph 6:4). It presupposes a second party.

**T1.2.1** — An **act** predominantly (69 action occurrences — "they provoked," 1Ki 14:9) with a residual **status/abstract** strand (16; "provocation," 2Ki 23:26). It is a doing, not a state.

**T1.2.2** — **Compound**: provocation joins to **idolatry/evil works** as its means (Deu 4:25 "make a carved image... provoke him"; Jer 25:6 "the work of your hands... to provoke me"); in the positive strand it joins to **zeal/love** (Heb 10:24; 2Cor 9:2). It is rarely simple — it carries its instrument.

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: **pleasing/obeying** (the cessation of provocation) — implied where the alternative is to "do what is right in the eyes of the LORD" rather than "provoke" (1Ki 14:8–9 contrast with David). Not lexicalised as a single antonym.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes **conciliation/obedience**: where the people turn and obey, the provoking ceases (2Ch 33:12–13 Manasseh's repentance ends the provocation). In the human strand the positive opposite of "provoke to anger" is to "provoke to love" (Heb 10:24).

**T1.3.3** — It ends where the **resulting anger** (C1/C2) begins (C3 is the eliciting act, the anger is the response) and where mere **contention** (C6) begins (provocation aims at arousing affect, not at the dispute itself).

**T1.4.1** — Modes: causative **hiphil** "to provoke/make angry" (Deu 4:25, morph HVhi…); a **stirring-up** (paroxusmos, Heb 10:24; Act 15:39); an **exasperating** (parorgizō/erethizō of children, Eph 6:4; Col 3:21). It operates as deliberate or careless arousal of another's affect.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by direction: toward **God** it is idolatrous provocation (Deu 4:25); toward **a person** it can be cruel taunting (1Sa 1:6 Peninnah), parental exasperation (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21), heated dispute (Act 15:39), or **constructive incitement to good** (Heb 10:24). The same causative form serves opposite ends.

**T1.4.3** — Communicative/spoken mode: yes — provocation is often **verbal** — Peninnah's taunting (1Sa 1:6–7), the taunt "in the presence of the builders" (Neh 4:1–5), and exhortation "to stir up" (Heb 10:24). Recorded; elsewhere none.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response following provocation: the provoked party becomes **angry/grieved** — Hannah "wept and would not eat" under provocation (1Sa 1:7); God's anger is kindled (Deu 9:18–19); the dispute "became so sharp they parted" (Act 15:39). Record none where unstated.

**T1.5.2** — Varied: the immediate response ranges from divine wrath (Deu 9:19) to human grief/weeping (1Sa 1:7) to relational rupture (Act 15:39) to — in the positive strand — **love and good works** (Heb 10:24).

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effect: persistent provocation of God yields **settled judgment/exile** ("provoked... so the LORD removed Israel," 2Ki 17:11,18-type); persistent provocation of a person yields **lasting grief/estrangement** (1Sa 1:6–7 "year by year"). The positive strand yields sustained **mutual edification** (Heb 10:24). Record none where unstated.

**T1.6.3** — Immediate response is the flash (anger kindled, weeping); sustained effect is the **accumulated judgment or estrangement** (exile; year-by-year grief). Episode vs settled consequence.

**T1.7.1** — It "operates" (succeeds in provoking) where there is **idolatry/evil before God** (Deu 4:25; 31:29) or **cruelty/insult between persons** (1Sa 1:6; Neh 4:1). The condition is a real offence.

**T1.7.2** — It is **blocked** where the would-be provoked party does not take it up — Hannah ultimately pours out her soul to God rather than retaliate (1Sa 1:15–16); the wise overlook an insult (Pro 12:16-type). And it is restrained by command — "fathers, do not provoke your children" (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21). No verse attributes the provoking to another spirit — **silent** on that.

**T1.7.3** — Where provocation meets a person who does not take it up, that person is shown **grieved but self-controlled / turning to God** (1Sa 1:15–16 Hannah). Where it meets God, God is provoked but **relents on repentance** (2Ch 33:12–13).

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — Location: largely **NONE** (82/85), since provocation is a relational act, not a seated state. Where a seat appears it is the **heart** of the provoked or the provoker (2Ch 32:26 Hezekiah's heart proud; Eze 32:9 "I will trouble the hearts of many"), or the **flesh** (Eze 16:26 context). Record none elsewhere (Deu 4:25).

**T2.1.2** — The near-total absence of a seat confirms provocation is an **interpersonal act**, not a faculty housed in a level; the rare heart-references locate the pride/offence behind it (2Ch 32:26).

**T2.10.1** — Movement: provocation moves **from one party onto another** — the provoker acts on God or a person (the defining external direction; Deu 4:25 onto God; 1Sa 1:6 onto Hannah). Provocation of God is **received-from-outside** by God's people as the wrath it draws (Deu 9:7; 2Ki 21:15). No internal spirit→soul→body sequence; no angelic source. Record the party→party movement.

**T2.7.1** — Body direction: thin — where the flesh appears (Eze 16:26) it is the **means** of provocation (idolatrous "lust"), an outward act expressing inner rebellion. Mostly **silent** on a body-direction proper to the characteristic.

**T2.9.1** — Origin: provocation **originates in the provoking party** (generated within the provoker — their idolatry, 1Ki 14:9; their cruelty, 1Sa 1:6). For God's people it is also depicted as a **persistent generational pattern** ("you have been rebellious... provoking," Deu 9:7,24). Stated source = the provoker.

**T2.9.2** — Origin can be **single** (an individual's act, 1Sa 1:6) or **corporate/generational** (Israel "from the day... to this day," Deu 9:7) — it varies with context.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1** — Perception: provocation is triggered by what the provoked party **sees/hears** — God "sees" the high places (1Ki 14:9 context), Hannah hears the taunt (1Sa 1:6–7), the dispute follows perceived wrong (Act 15:38–39). Perception is the gate.

**T3.1.2** — Effect on perception: the provoking taunt **fixes attention** on the offence (1Sa 1:6–7 year by year). No verse states it distorting the senses — otherwise **silent**.

**T3.1.3** — Pattern: provocation works **through perception** (it must be perceived to provoke) — confirming its relational, communicated nature.

**T3.2.1** — Cognition: engaged as the provoked party's **appraisal of the offence** (faculty affect|moral_evaluation in the wrath-of-God texts, Rom 1:18; Eph 5:6 "because of these the wrath of God comes"). The provocation is judged blameworthy.

**T3.2.2** — Effect on cognition: in the children-strand, provocation **discourages** ("lest they become discouraged," Col 3:21) — it clouds and dispirits the mind of the provoked. In the positive strand it **stimulates** good intent (Heb 10:24).

**T3.2.3** — Pattern: provocation is **cognition-engaging** at both ends — it presupposes the provoked party's appraisal and reshapes their disposition (discouragement or zeal).

**T3.3.1** — Memory: provocation of God is tied to **remembered covenant breach accumulated over time** ("they have provoked me... since the day," Deu 9:7; 2Ki 21:15 "since the day their fathers came out of Egypt"). It is recorded against the people.

**T3.3.2** — Effect on memory: it builds a **remembered record of rebellion** that God recalls in judgment (Deu 9:7). Recorded.

**T3.3.3** — The memory dimension (accumulated provocation) indicates the provoke-God strand is **historical and cumulative**, not merely momentary.

**T3.4.1** — Affect: the core faculty (78/85 affect) — provocation is defined by the **anger it arouses** (Deu 9:18; 1Sa 1:6–7 grief).

**T3.4.2** — Effect on affect: it **arouses** anger (in God, Deu 9:19; in a person, the grief/anger of the provoked) — and in the positive strand **rouses** love (Heb 10:24). It is an affect-mover.

**T3.4.3** — That affect dominates confirms the characteristic is fundamentally about **moving another's affect** — its whole point is the affective response it produces.

**T3.5.1** — Creativity: not engaged. **Silent.**

**T3.5.2** — **Silent.**

**T3.5.3** — Absence indicates no relation to the originative faculty.

**T3.6.1** — Volition: engaged as the **will to provoke** (deliberate idolatry, 1Ki 14:9; deliberate taunting, 1Sa 1:6) and as the will summoned **not** to provoke ("do not provoke your children," Eph 6:4) or to provoke **toward good** (Heb 10:24 "let us consider how to stir up").

**T3.6.2** — Effect on volition: provocation **presses** the provoked party's will toward a reaction (anger, withdrawal — Act 15:39); the command-strand calls the provoker's will to restraint or redirection (Eph 6:4; Heb 10:24).

**T3.6.3** — Pattern: provocation is a **volitional act** with a volitional target — it is chosen, and it is morally accountable ("do not," Eph 6:4).

**T3.7.1** — Agency: provocation is itself an **act of relational agency** — the provoker *does* something to another (idolatry "to provoke," Deu 4:25; taunting, 1Sa 1:6; "stirring up," Heb 10:24).

**T3.7.2** — Effect on agency: it **moves the provoked party to act** — God to judge (Deu 9:19), Hannah to pray (1Sa 1:10), Paul and Barnabas to part (Act 15:39), believers to love and good works (Heb 10:24).

**T3.7.3** — The provoke→act pattern marks the characteristic as **agency directed at another's agency** — it is influence that produces action.

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation: directly engaged — provoking God is judged **evil** ("did evil... provoking him to anger," Deu 31:29; 1Ki 14:9 "you have done evil above all"); the wrath it draws is God's moral verdict (Rom 1:18; Eph 5:6).

**T3.8.2** — Effect: it **incurs** a moral verdict (judgment, Deu 31:29) on the provoker; the positive strand earns commendation ("good works," Heb 10:24).

**T3.8.3** — Pattern: provocation is **morally double-valued by direction** — provoking God/oppressing a person is evil; provoking to love is good. Its morality is wholly directional.

**T3.9.1** — Conscience: thin — the children-strand's "discouragement" (Col 3:21) touches the dispiriting of the inner person, but the acute witness of guilt is not in view. Mostly **silent**.

**T3.9.2** — **Silent** on provocation operating on conscience.

**T3.9.3** — The thinness indicates provocation is primarily an **interpersonal/affective** act, not a faculty of self-conviction.

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness: engaged in the command-strand — the integrated moral call **not to provoke** (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21) and to **provoke toward good** (Heb 10:24) is a matter of formed moral practice in relationships.

**T3.10.2** — Effect: failed conscientiousness **discourages/embitters** the other (Col 3:21); exercised, it **builds up** (Heb 10:24).

**T3.10.3** — The command-pattern marks provocation as a **governed relational practice** — Scripture holds the provoker responsible for its direction.

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity: provocation is **constitutively relational** — it requires and acts upon a bond (parent-child, Eph 6:4; rival wives, 1Sa 1:6; covenant people-God, Deu 4:25; co-workers, Act 15:39).

**T3.11.2** — Effect on relational capacity: negatively it **damages** the bond (estrangement, Act 15:39; embittered children, Col 3:21; God forsaking, Deu 31:17); positively it **strengthens** it (mutual stirring to love, Heb 10:24).

**T3.11.3** — The pattern shows the characteristic is **purely relational** — it cannot exist without a second party and always bears on the relationship's health.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1** — God-toward-human: not the direction of C3 (God is the provoked, not the provoker). The wrath that provocation draws *from* God is C2. Record none for C3 operating from God.

**T4.1.2** — **Silent** (a C2 question).

**T4.1.3** — **Silent.**

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God: this is C3's primary direction — the person **provokes God** by idolatry and evil (Deu 4:25; 1Ki 14:9; Jer 25:6–7; Psa 78:58 "provoked him... with their high places"). The movement is the person acting upon God's covenant honour.

**T4.2.2** — Inner posture it reveals: **rebellion and contempt for the covenant** — "they did evil... to provoke me to anger" (1Ki 14:9; Deu 31:29). It is the opposite of the seeking/worshipping posture.

**T4.2.3** — What it shows about the relationship: a **broken, contemptuous** covenant standing — the person treats the covenant Lord as one to be defied (Deu 32:16,21). The relationship is real but assaulted by the person.

**T4.3.1** — Person-to-person extension: yes — provocation is extended by one person toward another (Peninnah → Hannah, 1Sa 1:6; fathers → children, Eph 6:4; one believer → another for good, Heb 10:24).

**T4.3.2** — Inner conditions accompanying it: cruelty/rivalry in the negative case (1Sa 1:6 the rival "provoked her grievously to irritate her"); love and care in the positive case (Heb 10:24 "consider how to stir up one another to love").

**T4.3.3** — What the giver must have become: the positive strand implies the provoker must **love** before stirring to love (Heb 10:24); the negative requires only malice (1Sa 1:6). Recorded for the positive; the negative states no precondition.

**T4.4.1** — Receiving: the provoked party **takes up** the provocation as anger/grief (Hannah's grief, 1Sa 1:7) or, in the positive strand, as **stirred love/good works** (Heb 10:24) or jealousy (Rom 10:19 Israel "made jealous").

**T4.4.2** — Conditions of uptake: a **valued stake** makes uptake likely (Hannah's longing for a child, 1Sa 1:6; Israel's identity, Rom 10:19); **self-control / turning to God** blocks the negative uptake (1Sa 1:15–16).

**T4.4.3** — The person who meets provocation but does not take it up is shown **pained yet composed, redirecting to God** (Hannah, 1Sa 1:15–16) — grief without retaliation.

**T4.5.1** — Within bonds vs across distance: provocation operates **within close bonds** (co-wives, 1Sa 1:6; parent-child, Eph 6:4; covenant people-God, Deu 4:25) and across rivalry/conflict (Neh 4:1 enemies; Act 15:39 colleagues). Both.

**T4.5.2** — Covenantal scope: the provoke-God strand is **covenant-internal** (Israel against its own God, Deu 4:25); the human strand crosses any relational line (rivals, parents, co-workers, opponents). Both.

**T4.5.3** — Relational scope: **wide** — God (Deu 4:25), spouse-rivals (1Sa 1:6), children (Eph 6:4), co-workers (Act 15:39), fellow believers (Heb 10:24), enemies (Neh 4:1). Provocation reaches across the relational field.

**T4.6.1** — Spiritual beings: not shown operating toward angelic/adversarial beings. **Silent.**

**T4.6.2** — Adversarial site: not evidenced as a site of adversarial weaponising in the C3 verses (the nearest is idolatry "to provoke," which involves false gods as the *means*, Deu 32:16 "with strange gods... they provoked," and Deu 32:17 "sacrificed to demons"). Recorded: idolatrous provocation involves false-god/"demon" objects (Deu 32:16–17) — a partial link; otherwise silent on adversarial weaponising of the characteristic itself.

**T4.6.3** — Angelic mediation: not evidenced. **Silent.**

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1** — Transformation: provocation transforms **relationship and condition** — it moves God to judgment and the people to exile (2Ki 17:11,18-type), moves co-workers to separation (Act 15:39), and, positively, moves believers toward love and good works (Heb 10:24). Record none where unstated.

**T5.1.2** — Reversible/irreversible: the provoke-God pattern is **reversible** on repentance (Manasseh, 2Ch 33:12–13, who had provoked greatly yet was restored); persistent provocation leads to **executed** judgment (exile). Both.

**T5.2.1** — Sequence: **provocation → anger kindled → judgment / grief** (Deu 9:18 "you provoked... the LORD was angry") and, positively, **consideration → stirring → love/good works** (Heb 10:24). Record none where no sequence appears.

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism of change: **arousal of the other's affect** — idolatry rouses God's wrath (Deu 4:25); taunting rouses grief (1Sa 1:6); exhortation rouses zeal (Heb 10:24). The mechanism is affective elicitation.

**T5.3.2** — Differs: the mechanism is **destructive elicitation** (idolatry/cruelty) versus **constructive elicitation** (exhortation to good) — the same causative act with opposite mechanisms by direction.

**T5.4.1** — Relation to suffering: provocation **produces suffering** in the provoked — Hannah's distress under year-by-year provocation (1Sa 1:6–7,10 "deeply distressed"); and the people's suffering as the judgment provocation draws (Deu 9:19). It is a producer and context of affliction.

**T5.4.2** — What suffering does to it: under provocation-induced suffering Hannah is driven to **prayer** (1Sa 1:10) — suffering can redirect the provoked toward God rather than retaliation. Recorded.

**T5.5.1** — Sanctification arc: the positive strand explicitly participates — believers "stir up one another to love and good works" as ongoing mutual formation (Heb 10:24); the command not to provoke children serves their formation (Eph 6:4 "bring them up in the discipline... of the Lord"). The negative strand is the anti-formative pattern of rebellion (Deu 9:7).

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological orientation: "provoke to jealousy" is oriented to Israel's **eschatological salvation** (Rom 10:19; cf. 11:11,26 context); the negative strand draws the eschatological **wrath** (the "wrath of God comes," Eph 5:6; Col 3:6). Record the orientation where present.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1** — Co-occurring characteristics: **anger/wrath** (the response it arouses — C1/C2; Deu 9:18–19), **jealousy** ("provoked him to jealousy... to anger," Deu 32:16,21 — C4), **idolatry/evil** as instrument, and in the positive strand **love** (Heb 10:24). Strong pairing with jealousy and anger.

**T6.1.2** — Co-occurrence places C3 as the **hinge** between human action and divine/relational anger — it is the cause-term that produces the affect-terms (C1/C2) and is twinned with jealousy (C4).

**T6.2.1** — Sequence: C3 consistently **precedes** anger/wrath (provocation → anger, Deu 9:18; Psa 78:58–59 "provoked him to jealousy... God heard and was full of wrath"). It is the antecedent.

**T6.2.2** — The relationship is **causal**: provocation causes the anger/jealousy that follows (Deu 32:21; Psa 78:58–59). Cause, not mere correlation.

**T6.3.1** — Produces: C3 **produces** divine wrath (C2; Deu 9:19), divine jealousy (C4; Deu 32:16), human anger/grief (C1; 1Sa 1:7), and (positively) love/good works (Heb 10:24). Mechanism: affective elicitation.

**T6.3.2** — Produced by: provocation is itself produced by **idolatry/sin** (its instrument, Deu 4:25) or by **malice/rivalry** (1Sa 1:6) — and, positively, by love (Heb 10:24). 

**T6.3.3** — Constituent relation: provocation is the **causal constituent** of the anger/wrath/jealousy complex — it is the eliciting member of the provoke→anger structure (Deu 32:21).

**T6.4.1** — Shared vocabulary: kaˈas overlaps with **C1** (kaˈaˈs "vexation") and the wrath-lemmas chaˈrah/qaˈtsaph/cheˈmah appear here in their **provoked** sense (shared with C1/C2). Greek parorgismos/parorgizō link to orgē (C1/C2).

**T6.4.2** — Root-level: yes — kaˈas (provoke) and kaˈaˈs (vexation) share a root spanning C3 and C1; the chaˈrah burning-root spans C1/C2/C3 in its provoked use.

**T6.4.3** — The vocabulary sharing shows C3 is the **causative face** of the same anger-architecture — the act that arouses the affect named by C1/C2/C4.

**T6.5.1** — Nearest neighbour: **C4 jealousy** (the two are repeatedly paired and parallel — "provoked him to jealousy... provoked him to anger," Deu 32:16,21). Distinction: C3 is the **act of arousing**; C4 is the **affect aroused** (jealousy).

**T6.5.2** — Apparent overlap with C1/C2: where chaˈrah/qaˈtsaph appear with God as **object** (provoked) they are C3; with God as **bearer** they are C2; with a human bearer they are C1. The deciding criterion is **the term's relation to its subject/object** (arousing vs bearing).

**T6.5.3** — Distinction from C1/C2 is of **direction** (eliciting anger vs experiencing it); from C4 of **kind** (an act of provocation vs the affect of jealousy), though they co-occur.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms/roots: **kaˈas** (H3707, "to provoke, vex to anger"; the related noun kaˈaˈs "vexation/provocation"); Greek **paroxunō** ("provoke, sharpen"), **parorgizō** ("provoke to anger"), **erethizō** ("provoke, irritate"), **paroxusmos** ("a provoking/stirring"). Roots converge on **stirring/sharpening** another to a reaction.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: predominantly **verb/action**, characteristically **causative (hiphil)** in Hebrew — "to make angry" (Deu 4:25, morph HVhi…) — with a small noun strand ("provocation," 2Ki 23:26; paroxusmos, Heb 10:24). The causative grammar shows the characteristic is intrinsically about *making* another feel.

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: from "provoke to anger" (Deu 4:25) through "exasperate/embitter" (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21) and "sharp disagreement" (Act 15:39) to the **positive** "stir up to love" (Heb 10:24) and "provoke to jealousy/zeal" (Rom 10:19; 2Cor 9:2). Breadth: hostile arousal → exasperation → constructive incitement.

**T7.1.4** — Distinguishing terms: **act vs result** — kaˈas (the act of provoking) vs the anger (cheˈmah/chaˈron) it produces; the noun kaˈaˈs marks the **provocation as a thing**. Negative (parorgizō) vs positive (paroxusmos "to good," Heb 10:24) directions are lexically the same root, distinguished by object.

**T7.1.5** — Opposite/absence term: not a single lexeme; the opposite is **pleasing/obeying** God (1Ki 14:8 contrast) or, in the human strand, **not provoking** / **stirring to love** (Eph 6:4; Heb 10:24).

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term: none — there is no noun for "a provoker-type person" in the C3 vocabulary. Record none.

**T7.1.7** — Supplication/seeking term: none. Record none.

**T7.1.8** — OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew causative kaˈas (provoke God to anger) carries into Greek parorgizō/parorgismos (Eph 4:26; 6:4), but the NT notably **broadens** the root toward the positive **paroxusmos** "stir up to good" (Heb 10:24) and the redemptive "provoke to jealousy" (Rom 10:19). Continuity of the causative idea; development toward a redeemable/positive use.

**T7.1.9** — NT term: **paroxusmos** used positively (Heb 10:24) and as "sharp disagreement" (Act 15:39); **parorgismos** (Eph 4:26). These mark a NT refinement distinguishing destructive from constructive provocation.

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc: from the OT **causative provocation of God to wrath** (kaˈas, Deu 4:25) through interpersonal **exasperation** (Eph 6:4) to the NT **constructive stirring** (Heb 10:24) — a complete arc from covenant-rebellion to relational edification.

**T7.2.1** — Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Deu 4:25 kaˈas is the **purpose-clause verb** ("make a carved image... to provoke him to anger") — the term names the offence that the whole warning turns on. In Heb 10:24 paroxusmos is the **goal** of mutual consideration ("how to stir one another up").

**T7.2.2** — Literary forms: **covenant law/exhortation** (Deu 4:25; 31:29), **Deuteronomistic narrative** (1Ki 14:9; 2Ki 17:11), **prophecy** (Jer 25:6–7), **NT epistle** (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21; Heb 10:24), **NT narrative** (Act 15:39). Responsible reading weighs the covenant-warning form (conditional consequence) and the epistolary paraenesis (command/exhortation) (BOOK distribution: Jer 13, 1Ki 11, Deu 8).

**T7.2.3** — Logical structure: **conditional-consequential** in the covenant strand — *if* you provoke God by idolatry, *then* wrath/judgment follows (Deu 4:25–26; 31:29); **imperatival** in the epistolary strand — *do not* provoke (Eph 6:4), *do* stir up to good (Heb 10:24).

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: **covenantal** (Deu 4:25), **judicial-historical** (the kings "provoking the LORD," 1Ki/2Ki), **familial/communal** (Eph 6:4; Col 3:21; Heb 10:24), **missional-eschatological** (provoke to jealousy unto salvation, Rom 10:19). The settings show provocation as covenant betrayal at one pole and relational edification at the other.

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor verse: **Deuteronomy 4:25** — "you... act corruptly by making a carved image... and by doing what is evil... so as to provoke him to anger" — most fully and directly expresses the dominant characteristic (the deliberate human act of arousing God's covenant wrath).

**T7.2.6** — Deu 4:25 shows what no other verse shows together: the **deliberate act** (carved image), the **moral character** (evil), and the **explicit purpose** (to provoke God to anger) — the complete anatomy of provoking God in one warning. (The positive pole's anchor is Heb 10:24.)

**T7.3.1** — Most useful human-science lens: per the science extract, **moral psychology** — provocation terms (paroxunō, kaˈas, erethizō) are treated there as "the external stimulus that triggers the moral-anger response — the wrongdoing that elicits it."

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates: appraisal/elicitor research illuminates provocation as the **external trigger** whose perceived wrongdoing arouses anger (Deu 4:25 idolatry as the elicitor; 1Sa 1:6 taunting as the elicitor) — and the social-norm-enforcement account illuminates why provoking God's covenant honour draws judgment.

**T7.3.3** — Where they diverge: science treats provocation only as a **stimulus to a human emotion**; the C3 evidence makes its gravest instance **provoking God** — a covenant/theological category science does not address (outside-science inventory). Science also lacks a clean place for the **positive** provocation "to love and good works" (Heb 10:24), which is constructive rather than aggressive.

**T7.3.4** — Gaps surfaced by science: the **honour-culture** finding (lower thresholds for anger at insult to status/dignity) may illuminate the covenant-honour logic of provoking God (idolatry as insult to the covenant Lord, Deu 32:16,21) — a prompt for further verse work on provocation-as-dishonour. Labelled inferential.
