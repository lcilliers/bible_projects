## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Relation to God: God is the **bearer** of jealousy — "the LORD your God is a jealous God" (Exo 20:5; Deu 4:24; 6:15; possessor/agent), the **object** of jealousy provoked ("they moved him to jealousy," Deu 32:16,21; Psa 78:58), and the one **for whom** human zeal is exercised (Num 25:11 "jealous with my jealousy"; 1Ki 19:10). All three relations occur.

**T0.1.2** — Across C4 the characteristic is **borne by God himself** (Exo 34:14; Isa 42:13 "he stirs up his zeal/jealousy") and by the creature (Gen 30:1; Num 25:11). *Interpretation:* jealousy/zeal is a **shared** characteristic — supremely a divine covenant-attribute that the creature bears both rightly (zeal, Num 25:11) and wrongly (envy, Gen 30:1).

**T0.2.1** — Stated purpose/effect: divine jealousy **accomplishes** salvation/judgment — "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this" (Isa 9:7; 37:32); Phinehas' jealousy **turns away wrath and makes atonement** (Num 25:11,13). Human envy, by contrast, **rots/consumes** (Pro 14:30 "envy makes the bones rot"; Job 5:2 context). Record none where unstated.

**T0.2.2** — Both: **zeal and divine jealousy** belong to created/holy design (Num 25:11; Isa 9:7), while **envy** belongs to the fallen condition (Gen 30:1; 37:11; Pro 14:30; Ecc 4:4 "all toil... comes from envy"). The same root spans the right and the disordered forms.

**T0.2.3** — Future-fullness orientation: yes — "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this" establishes a **future accomplishment** (Isa 9:7 the messianic kingdom; 37:32 the remnant). Divine jealousy is oriented to covenant consummation. Human envy has no such orientation. Record where present.

**T0.3.1** — Divine-likeness aspect: the aspect instantiated in the person is **covenant-protective ardour / zeal for God's honour** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9; 1Ki 19:10) — a true image of God's jealousy; envy is the **counterfeit** that images nothing of God.

**T0.3.2** — Shared: jealousy/zeal is borne by both God and the person under the same root (qinˈah/qaˈna). It is **primarily a divine attribute** that the creature reflects (rightly as zeal, wrongly as envy).

**T0.3.3** — Where right jealousy/zeal is present it indicates **covenant loyalty** (Phinehas, Num 25:11–13; Elijah, 1Ki 19:10); where envy is present it indicates a **disordered, rotting inner state** (Pro 14:30); where divine jealousy rests against a person it indicates they have **violated the covenant** (Deu 29:20 "the anger and jealousy of the LORD will smoke against that man").

**T0.4.1** — Typological use: yes — "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this" (Isa 9:7) functions **messianically/eschatologically**, the divine instance establishing the pattern of God's saving zeal. Direction: divine instance outward. Elsewhere narrative/wisdom, no typology.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Programme name: **Jealousy / zeal**. The name signals a **passionate, possessive ardour** over what is one's own or one's due; essential nature is intense protective/competitive heat. Senses cluster as jealousy/zeal/envy.

**T1.1.2** — Primary terms: qinˈah (noun, "jealousy/zeal/ardour") and qaˈna (verb, "to be jealous / envy / be zealous"); the adjective qanˈna/qanˈno ("jealous"), used only of God. The root carries **ardour to the point of heat** — "jealousy is fierce as the grave... a most vehement flame" (Song 8:6).

**T1.1.3** — Directional/relational: jealousy/zeal is inherently **toward** something valued — *for* God/His house (Psa 69:9), *over* a spouse (Num 5:14), *against* a rival (Gen 30:1). It presupposes a valued bond or possession and a perceived threat to it.

**T1.2.1** — Mixed: a **status/condition** (noun qinˈah, 40 occurrences — "jealousy," Pro 27:4), an **act** (verb qaˈna, 29 — "envied," Gen 30:1), and a **quality** (adjective qanˈna, 8 — "jealous God," Exo 20:5). It is borne as a state, exercised as an act, and predicated as an attribute.

**T1.2.2** — **Compound** — jealousy fuses with **anger/wrath** (Pro 27:4 "wrath... anger... but who can stand before jealousy?"; Deu 29:20 "anger and jealousy"; Zep 1:18 "fire of his jealousy"), and at the negative pole with **envy/covetousness** (Ecc 4:4; Pro 14:30). It is rarely simple — it carries an anger or envy element.

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: **security/contentment** (the absence of perceived threat) — implied where envy is contrasted with a "tranquil heart" ("a tranquil heart gives life... but envy makes the bones rot," Pro 14:30). Contentment stands against envy; **indifference** stands against zeal.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes **contentment and trust**: envy resists a tranquil heart (Pro 14:30); divine jealousy excludes **rival worship** ("you shall worship no other god, for the LORD... is a jealous God," Exo 34:14).

**T1.3.3** — It ends where **simple anger** begins (jealousy is anger *over a threatened bond*, Pro 27:4 distinguishes them) and where **covetousness** begins (envy shades into coveting what another has, Ecc 4:4). Divine jealousy is not envy — it is rightful covenant-protectiveness.

**T1.4.1** — Modes: as a **possessive attribute** (qanˈna "jealous God," Exo 20:5); as a **kindled act** ("envied," Gen 30:1); as a **consuming fire** ("the fire of his jealousy," Zep 1:18; "consumed by my jealousy," Eze 36:5); as a **spirit that comes over** a person ("the spirit of jealousy comes over him," Num 5:14). Grammatically piel/qal verbs and an adjective.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by direction and level: **divine** jealousy as consuming covenant-fire (Zep 1:18); **zealous** ardour acting for God (Isa 9:7 "will do this"); **human** envy as an inward rot (Pro 14:30); the **marital** "spirit of jealousy" rising over a husband (Num 5:14). Same root, distinct modes.

**T1.4.3** — Communicative/spoken mode: yes — God **declares** his jealousy ("I have spoken in my jealous wrath," Eze 36:5–6; Zec 1:14 "I am jealous for Jerusalem... with great jealousy," spoken oracle). Recorded; elsewhere none.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response following jealousy: it issues in **action** — Phinehas' jealousy → he acts to stay the plague (Num 25:7–8,11); Rachel's envy → "Give me children, or I shall die!" (Gen 30:1); divine jealousy → judgment declared (Eze 36:5–6). Record none where unstated.

**T1.5.2** — Varied: the immediate response ranges from zealous decisive action (Num 25:7–8) to desperate demand (Gen 30:1) to consuming judgment (Zep 1:18). No single response.

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effect: right jealousy/zeal **establishes covenant outcomes** — Phinehas' jealousy secures "a covenant of perpetual priesthood" (Num 25:13); divine zeal "will do this" lastingly (Isa 9:7). Envy's sustained effect is **bodily/inner decay** ("rots the bones," Pro 14:30). Record none where unstated.

**T1.6.3** — Immediate response is the act/flash (Num 25:7–8; Gen 30:1); sustained effect is the **established covenant/decay** (perpetual priesthood, Num 25:13; rotted bones, Pro 14:30). Episode vs enduring result.

**T1.7.1** — It "takes hold rightly" where there is a **genuine threat to a rightful bond** — God's covenant honour (Num 25:11; Exo 34:14), a husband's exclusive marriage (Num 5:14), God's house (Psa 69:9). The condition is a valued, rightful relation under threat.

**T1.7.2** — It is **distorted** where the threatened "possession" is not rightfully one's own or the response is rivalrous resentment — envy of a sibling's children (Gen 30:1), of a brother's favour (Gen 37:11), of the wicked's prosperity (Psa 73:3; Pro 23:17 "let not your heart envy sinners"). No verse attributes the jealousy to another spirit (the "spirit of jealousy," Num 5:14, is a disposition that "comes over," not an external being) — **silent** on adversarial origin.

**T1.7.3** — Where envy is present and unmastered, the person is shown **inwardly decaying and self-destructive** ("envy makes the bones rot," Pro 14:30; "a fool... envy slays the simple," Job 5:2 context) — and counselled away ("let not your heart envy sinners," Pro 23:17).

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — Locations: **soul/nephesh** (1Ki 19:10,14 Elijah "I have been very jealous for the LORD," lit. of the soul's ardour); **heart** (Pro 23:17 "let not your heart envy"; Song 8:6 "jealousy... set me as a seal upon your heart"); **heart|flesh** (Pro 14:30 envy reaching the bones/flesh); **inward parts** (Isa 63:15 "the stirring of your inner parts and your compassion / zeal"); **heart|soul** (Eze 36:5). 70/77 occurrences locate no seat — record none (Gen 30:1).

**T2.1.2** — The pattern: where located, jealousy/zeal is seated in the **heart and soul** (the deep affective-volitional centre) and is uniquely shown reaching the **body** — "rots the bones" (Pro 14:30). It is a deep-seated, even somatically-felt ardour; the body link is **expressive/indicative** (envy's bodily corrosion).

**T2.10.1** — Movement: the **marital** form moves **onto the person from outside the will** — "the spirit of jealousy comes over him" (Num 5:14,30), an external-feeling onset; divine jealousy moves **from God onto** the covenant-breaker (Deu 29:20). Envy is generated **within** at the sight of another (Gen 30:1). No angelic introduction is named. Record the onsets shown.

**T2.7.1** — Body direction: where a body link exists it runs **soul/heart expressing into the body** — envy "rots the bones / makes the flesh waste" (Pro 14:30), the inner ardour felt in the "inward parts" (Isa 63:15). Inner-to-body. No body-to-soul feedback shown — **silent** on that direction.

**T2.9.1** — Origin: **generated within the person** at the sight of a rival (Gen 30:1 "Rachel envied her sister"; Ecc 4:4 from comparison; Pro 23:17); **borne by God** intrinsically (Exo 20:5); and, in the marital case, arising as a **"spirit" that comes over** the husband (Num 5:14). Multiple origins.

**T2.9.2** — Origin is **multiple** and context-dependent: within-person comparison (Gen 30:1), intrinsic to God (Exo 34:14), and the onset-"spirit" of the marriage law (Num 5:14). It changes with context.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1** — Perception: jealousy/envy is **triggered by sight** — "Rachel saw that she bore no children... and envied" (Gen 30:1); the comparison arises from "seeing a neighbour's skill" (Ecc 4:4) and "seeing sinners" (Pro 23:17). Perception is the gate.

**T3.1.2** — Effect on perception: envy **fixes the gaze** on the rival/the wicked's prosperity (Psa 73:3 "I was envious... when I saw the prosperity of the wicked") — distorting perception until corrected "in the sanctuary" (Psa 73:17). Recorded.

**T3.1.3** — Pattern: jealousy/envy is **perception-driven** — it arises from and skews what is seen (the rival, the prosperity), confirming its comparative, relational nature.

**T3.2.1** — Cognition: engaged as the **appraisal of threat/comparison** (Psa 73:3 the reasoning about the wicked's prosperity; the "jealous God" of moral evaluation, Exo 20:5; Deu 4:24, faculty moral_evaluation).

**T3.2.2** — Effect on cognition: envy **troubles and embitters** thought until reframed (Psa 73:2–3,16–17 "it was a wearisome task... until I went into the sanctuary"); right zeal **clarifies** purpose (Num 25:11–13). 

**T3.2.3** — Pattern: jealousy/zeal is **appraisal-bound** — it both rests on a cognitive comparison and reshapes judgment (distorted by envy, clarified by zeal).

**T3.3.1** — Memory: thin — zeal is tied to **remembered covenant** (Elijah's "they have forsaken your covenant," 1Ki 19:10,14) and divine jealousy to remembered idolatry (Psa 78:58 context). Direct memory-faculty engagement is limited. Mostly **silent**.

**T3.3.2** — **Silent** on jealousy acting on a memory faculty.

**T3.3.3** — The thinness indicates jealousy/zeal is framed as **present, perception-triggered ardour**, with covenant memory as backdrop rather than mechanism.

**T3.4.1** — Affect: the core faculty (70/77 affect) — jealousy/envy/zeal is first an **intense feeling** (Gen 30:1; Pro 27:4 "who can stand before jealousy?"; Song 8:6 "vehement flame").

**T3.4.2** — Effect on affect: it is among the **most intense** affects — "fierce as the grave" (Song 8:6), unbearable ("more than wrath or anger," Pro 27:4), and consuming ("the fire of his jealousy," Zep 1:18). It overwhelms the affective field.

**T3.4.3** — That affect dominates, with the highest intensity language in the cluster (Song 8:6; Pro 27:4), confirms jealousy/zeal as a **peak-intensity emotion**.

**T3.5.1** — Creativity: not engaged. **Silent.**

**T3.5.2** — **Silent.**

**T3.5.3** — Absence indicates no relation to the originative faculty.

**T3.6.1** — Volition: engaged where jealousy **drives decisive choice** (Phinehas chooses to act, Num 25:7–8,11; Elijah's zealous resolve, 1Ki 19:10) and where envy is **commanded against** ("let not your heart envy sinners," Pro 23:17; "fret not... do not be envious," Psa 37:1).

**T3.6.2** — Effect on volition: right zeal **mobilises** the will for God (Num 25:11); envy **disorders** it toward rivalry and discontent (Gen 30:1; Ecc 4:4). It strongly engages the will at both poles.

**T3.6.3** — Pattern: jealousy/zeal is a **powerfully volitional** characteristic — it propels action (zeal) and must be governed (envy), repeatedly placed under command (Pro 23:17; Psa 37:1).

**T3.7.1** — Agency: jealousy/zeal is intensely **action-issuing** — Phinehas acts (Num 25:7–8); the LORD's zeal "will do this" (Isa 9:7; 37:32); divine jealousy executes judgment (Eze 36:5–6; Zep 1:18).

**T3.7.2** — Effect on agency: right zeal **produces atoning/saving action** (Num 25:13; Isa 9:7); envy **produces rivalrous harm** (the brothers' envy → selling Joseph, Gen 37:11 → 37:28). It is a strong motor of action.

**T3.7.3** — The jealousy→act pattern marks it as **highly agency-generating**, for good (zeal) or ill (envy).

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation: directly engaged — God's jealousy *is* a moral attribute defending exclusive worship (Exo 20:5; 34:14; Deu 4:24, faculty moral_evaluation); envy is morally condemned ("let not your heart envy," Pro 23:17); zeal is morally commended (Num 25:11,13).

**T3.8.2** — Effect: it **expresses and enacts** a moral verdict — God's jealousy condemns idolatry (Exo 34:14); the wise condemn envy (Pro 14:30; 23:17). 

**T3.8.3** — Pattern: jealousy/zeal is **morally tri-valued** — righteous (divine jealousy, zeal), sinful (envy), and a regulated marital safeguard (Num 5). Its morality depends on the rightness of the threatened bond and the response.

**T3.9.1** — Conscience: thin — the marital "spirit of jealousy" ordeal (Num 5:14–30) engages a quasi-juridical inner suspicion, but the acute witness of guilt is not the faculty in view. Mostly **silent**.

**T3.9.2** — **Silent** on jealousy operating on conscience as such.

**T3.9.3** — The thinness indicates jealousy/zeal is primarily an **affective-relational** characteristic, not a faculty of self-conviction.

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness: engaged where zeal is the **integrated moral-volitional-active** defence of the covenant (Phinehas, Num 25:11 perceives, resolves, acts; Elijah, 1Ki 19:10) and where envy must be integrally **resisted** (Pro 23:17; Psa 37:1).

**T3.10.2** — Effect: right zeal **secures** covenant good (Num 25:13); unmastered envy **corrodes** the whole person (Pro 14:30).

**T3.10.3** — The pattern marks jealousy/zeal as a **test-site of integrated moral character** — rightly directed it is exemplary virtue (Num 25:11), wrongly directed it is corrosive vice.

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity: jealousy/zeal is **constitutively relational** — over a bond (marriage, Num 5:14; God's covenant, Exo 34:14) or against a rival (Gen 30:1; 37:11). It cannot exist without a relationship and a third party/threat.

**T3.11.2** — Effect on relational capacity: envy **fractures** bonds (the brothers sell Joseph, Gen 37:11,28; Rachel's strife with Leah, Gen 30:1); right jealousy **guards** them (the marriage, Num 5; the covenant, Exo 34:14). It both protects and ruptures, by pole.

**T3.11.3** — The strongly relational pattern shows jealousy/zeal is **inherently social** — the science extract's point that it "cannot arise in isolation" matches the evidence (always a bond and a threat, Gen 30:1; Num 5:14).

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1** — God-toward-human: yes — God's jealousy operates from God toward the person, **for** them (jealous *for* Jerusalem/Zion, Zec 1:14; 8:2; Joe 2:18 "the LORD became jealous for his land") and **against** them in covenant breach (Deu 29:20; Eze 36:5–6).

**T4.1.2** — Basis: **covenantal** — God's jealousy is grounded in the exclusive covenant ("you shall have no other gods... for I the LORD your God am a jealous God," Exo 20:3–5; 34:14). It is responsive to covenant fidelity/breach.

**T4.1.3** — What it shows of God's disposition: God is **passionately committed** to His people — His jealousy is the measure of His covenant love (jealous *for* Zion, Zec 1:14; Joe 2:18), and of His refusal to share their worship (Exo 34:14).

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God: yes — the person's **zeal for God** moves toward God ("I have been very jealous for the LORD," 1Ki 19:10,14; "zeal for your house has consumed me," Psa 69:9; Phinehas "jealous with my jealousy," Num 25:11). And the person can **provoke God to jealousy** (Deu 32:16,21 — overlaps C3).

**T4.2.2** — Inner posture: zeal for God requires **wholehearted covenant loyalty and indignation at His dishonour** (1Ki 19:10; Psa 69:9). Provoking-to-jealousy reveals the opposite — covenant betrayal (Deu 32:21).

**T4.2.3** — What it shows: right zeal shows a person **identified with God's honour** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9); envy of the wicked, corrected in God's presence, shows the relationship **restoring right perception** (Psa 73:17).

**T4.3.1** — Person-to-person extension: yes (negatively) — envy is "extended" toward another as **rivalrous resentment** (Rachel toward Leah, Gen 30:1; brothers toward Joseph, Gen 37:11; envy of neighbour's skill, Ecc 4:4).

**T4.3.2** — Inner conditions accompanying it: **comparison and perceived disadvantage** (Gen 30:1 the barren sister; Ecc 4:4; Psa 73:3). Right jealousy in the marital case accompanies **rightful exclusive claim** (Num 5:14).

**T4.3.3** — What the giver must have: envy requires only a **valued, threatened or coveted** good (Gen 30:1); the evidence states no virtue the envier must first have. Record none for a precondition of envy.

**T4.4.1** — Receiving: the object of envy **receives harm** — Joseph is sold by his envious brothers (Gen 37:11,28); the object of marital jealousy undergoes the ordeal (Num 5:15–28). Uptake is endured consequence, not adoption.

**T4.4.2** — Conditions of uptake: the envied party's **favour/prosperity** is what draws it (Joseph's favour, Gen 37:11; the wicked's prosperity, Psa 73:3). 

**T4.4.3** — The person who is the object of envy is shown **endangered** (Joseph, Gen 37:11→28) — the inner-being state of the *envier*, by contrast, is the rotting one (Pro 14:30).

**T4.5.1** — Within bonds vs across distance: jealousy/zeal operates **within close bonds** (siblings, Gen 30:1; 37:11; marriage, Num 5:14; God's covenant, Exo 34:14) and across rivalry (envy of strangers' prosperity, Psa 73:3). Both.

**T4.5.2** — Covenantal scope: divine jealousy is **covenant-grounded** (Exo 34:14) yet reaches the nations in judgment (Zep 3:8 "all the earth shall be consumed in the fire of my jealousy"). Human envy crosses any line. Both.

**T4.5.3** — Relational scope: **wide** — God (Exo 20:5), spouse (Num 5:14), siblings (Gen 30:1; 37:11), the wicked/strangers (Psa 73:3), the nations (Zep 3:8). Jealousy/zeal reaches across the relational field, always over a valued stake.

**T4.6.1** — Spiritual beings: not shown operating toward angelic/adversarial beings (the rival objects of divine jealousy are **idols/"other gods,"** Exo 34:14; Deu 32:16,21 — false gods as the rival, Deu 32:17 "demons"). Recorded: idolatry as the rival drawing divine jealousy involves false-god objects; otherwise **silent** on angelic interface.

**T4.6.2** — Adversarial site: not evidenced as a site of adversarial weaponising. **Silent** (recorded as not evidenced).

**T4.6.3** — Angelic mediation: not evidenced. **Silent.**

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1** — Transformation: right jealousy/zeal **transforms condition** — Phinehas' jealousy turns a plague and establishes a covenant (Num 25:11–13); envy transforms the person toward **decay** (Pro 14:30) and the envied toward harm (Gen 37:28). Record none where unstated.

**T5.1.2** — Reversible/irreversible: envy is shown **correctable** — Asaph's envy of the wicked is reversed "in the sanctuary" (Psa 73:3,17); the wise are counselled out of it (Pro 23:17). Divine jealousy's judgment, once executed, stands (Zep 3:8). Both, by pole.

**T5.2.1** — Sequence: **seeing → envy → harmful act** (Gen 30:1; 37:11→28) and **covenant threat → zeal → atoning action** (Num 25:6–13). For envy corrected: **envy → near-stumbling → reframing in God's presence → restored footing** (Psa 73:2–3,16–17,28). Record none where no sequence appears.

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism of change: **encounter/reframing** corrects envy (the sanctuary, Psa 73:17); **decisive zealous act** effects covenant change (Num 25:11–13). Record none where unstated.

**T5.3.2** — Differs: envy is changed by **reappraisal/encounter** (Psa 73:17), zeal effects change by **action** (Num 25:13). Context-dependent.

**T5.4.1** — Relation to suffering: envy **produces inner suffering** (the rotting bones, Pro 14:30; Asaph's "wearisome task," Psa 73:16) and arises amid the suffering of perceived disadvantage (Gen 30:1 barrenness; Psa 73:3 the contrast of the afflicted righteous and prospering wicked). Right zeal arises amid covenant affliction (Elijah's despair, 1Ki 19:10).

**T5.4.2** — What suffering does to it: the suffering of envy, brought into God's presence, is **dissolved/reframed** (Psa 73:17,28); the affliction of covenant betrayal **stirs** God's jealousy for His people (Joe 2:18). Suffering can both feed envy and, redirected, end it.

**T5.5.1** — Sanctification arc: jealousy/zeal participates — the formed person **puts away envy** ("let not your heart envy sinners, but continue in the fear of the LORD," Pro 23:17) and grows in **rightly-directed zeal** for God (Psa 69:9; Num 25:11 as exemplar). It is a long-term work of redirecting ardour from rivalry to covenant loyalty.

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological orientation: yes — "the zeal of the LORD of hosts will do this" (Isa 9:7; 37:32) orients divine jealousy/zeal toward the **consummated kingdom/remnant**; "all the earth consumed in the fire of my jealousy" (Zep 3:8) toward final judgment. Present zeal anticipates that accomplishment.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1** — Co-occurring characteristics: **anger/wrath** (Pro 27:4 jealousy beside wrath/anger; Deu 29:20 "anger and jealousy"; Zep 1:18 "fire of his jealousy" = wrath), **provocation** ("provoked him to jealousy," Deu 32:16,21 — C3), **love** (Song 8:6 jealousy beside love "strong as death"), and **envy/covetousness** at the negative pole (Ecc 4:4). Strong pairing with anger and provocation.

**T6.1.2** — Co-occurrence places C4 **adjacent to and fused with divine wrath** (Zep 1:18) and as the **affect that provocation arouses** (Deu 32:21), while at the human pole it sits beside love (Song 8:6) and envy.

**T6.2.1** — Sequence: jealousy is **aroused by provocation** ("provoked him to jealousy," Deu 32:16,21) and **precedes wrath/action** (Deu 29:20 jealousy → smoking anger; Num 25:11 jealousy → atoning act). 

**T6.2.2** — The relationship is **causal**: provocation causes jealousy; jealousy causes wrath/action (Deu 32:16,21; Num 25:11). Causal chain.

**T6.3.1** — Produces: jealousy **produces** wrath/judgment (Deu 29:20; Zep 1:18), atoning/saving action (Num 25:13; Isa 9:7), and, as envy, inner decay and interpersonal harm (Pro 14:30; Gen 37:28). 

**T6.3.2** — Produced by: jealousy is **produced by** provocation (C3; Deu 32:21), by perceived threat/comparison (Gen 30:1; Psa 73:3), and by covenant betrayal (Exo 34:14).

**T6.3.3** — Constituent relation: divine jealousy and divine wrath **interpenetrate** — "the fire of his jealousy" *is* a wrathful consuming (Zep 1:18; 3:8); jealousy is thus a constituent of the divine-wrath response (cf. C2 T6.3.3).

**T6.4.1** — Shared vocabulary: qinˈah/qaˈna is **distinct** from the anger-lemmas (it has its own root) but is repeatedly **collocated** with cheˈmah/chaˈron/aph (Pro 27:4; Deu 29:20; Zep 1:18) — shared collocation, not shared lemma.

**T6.4.2** — Root-level: the qnˈ root generates qinˈah/qaˈna/qanˈna across the jealousy/zeal/envy senses; it does **not** share a root with the burning-anger family — the connection is collocational and conceptual, not etymological.

**T6.4.3** — The vocabulary picture shows C4 is a **distinct affect** (its own root) that **fuses with** anger/wrath in operation (especially in God) — explaining why it sits in the anger cluster (DEC-4 retained it) yet remains lexically its own.

**T6.5.1** — Nearest neighbour: **C2 divine wrath** (they fuse in God — "fire of his jealousy," Zep 1:18) and **C3 provocation** (jealousy is what provocation arouses, Deu 32:21). The distinction from wrath: jealousy adds the **relational-possessive** note (over a *valued bond*), which bare wrath lacks.

**T6.5.2** — Apparent overlap: in Zep 1:18 / Deu 29:20 wrath and jealousy run together; the **precise boundary** is the *object* — jealousy is anger specifically **over a threatened exclusive bond** (covenant, marriage), whereas wrath is the broader judicial heat. The marital "spirit of jealousy" (Num 5) marks jealousy's distinct relational-possessive core.

**T6.5.3** — Distinction from C2 is of **kind/relational-object** (possessive ardour over a bond vs judicial heat), not of vocabulary-root; from envy-as-vice it is one of **direction/rightness** (zeal for the rightful vs resentment of the rival).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms/roots: **qinˈah** (noun) and **qaˈna** (verb), from a root meaning "to be intensely red/ardent" — ardour, zeal, jealousy, envy; the adjective **qanˈna/qanˈno** ("jealous"), reserved for God. The root meaning centres on **burning ardour over what is one's own/one's due**.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: **noun/status** (qinˈah, 40 — "jealousy," Pro 27:4), **verb/action** (qaˈna, 29 — "envied/was jealous," Gen 30:1), **adjective/quality** (qanˈna, used only of God, Exo 20:5). The range shows jealousy as a **state, an act, and a divine attribute**.

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: a single root spans **zeal** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9), **(righteous) jealousy** (Exo 34:14; Num 5:14), and **envy** (Gen 30:1; Ecc 4:4; Pro 14:30). Breadth: passionate devotion → protective jealousy → rivalrous envy — the positive and negative held in one word.

**T7.1.4** — Distinguishing terms: the **same root** carries disposition (qanˈna "jealous," an attribute) and act (qaˈna "envied"); condition (qinˈah "jealousy") and quality (qanˈna). The notable feature is that **zeal, jealousy, and envy are not lexically separated** — context, not vocabulary, distinguishes them.

**T7.1.5** — Opposite/absence term: no single antonym lexeme; the contrast is carried by **"a tranquil heart"** (Pro 14:30) for envy and by **indifference/lukewarmness** for zeal (cf. zestos "hot" vs its opposite elsewhere). Idiomatic.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term: the adjective **qanˈna "jealous"** functions as a fixed attribute of God ("a jealous God," Exo 20:5; 34:14) — a quality-term, used of God rather than a human person-type. Record: a divine-attribute adjective, not a human person-type noun.

**T7.1.7** — Supplication/seeking term: none — there is no term for seeking jealousy/zeal from another. Record none.

**T7.1.8** — OT→NT relationship: C4's data is **OT-only** (qinˈah/qaˈna). The NT counterpart (zēlos/zēloō, and the rivalry terms eritheia/phthonos) is carried in the **C6 strife** and broader cluster vocabulary rather than in C4's focus set; per the science extract the NT continues both the zeal and envy poles. Within C4 proper, continuity is **inferred**, not present in the OT-only set — labelled inferential.

**T7.1.9** — NT-coined term: none in C4's OT-only set. Record none (the NT zēlos vocabulary lies outside this focus set).

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc: a **single root** holding the full range from **divine covenant-jealousy** (Exo 34:14) through **human zeal for God** (Num 25:11; Psa 69:9) to **rivalrous envy** (Gen 30:1; Pro 14:30) — the most semantically unified term in the cluster, its valence set entirely by direction and object.

**T7.2.1** — Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Exo 34:14 qanˈna is the **predicate nominative defining God** ("the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God") — the term *names* God and grounds the prohibition of other gods. In Num 25:11 qinˈah is the **causal ground** of Phinehas' commended act and reward.

**T7.2.2** — Literary forms: **covenant law** (Exo 20:5; 34:14; Deu 4:24), **narrative** (Gen 30:1; 37:11; Num 25; 1Ki 19), **ritual law** (Num 5:14–30, the jealousy ordeal), **wisdom** (Pro 14:30; 23:17; Ecc 4:4), **psalm** (Psa 69:9; 73:3), **prophecy** (Eze 36:5; Zec 1:14; Zep 1:18; Isa 9:7). Responsible reading distinguishes the covenant-attribute texts, the narrative exemplars, the ritual-legal ordeal, and the wisdom warnings (BOOK distribution: Eze 13, Num 12, Isa 8).

**T7.2.3** — Logical structure: **covenant-exclusive** — *because* the LORD is a jealous God, *therefore* no other gods/idols (Exo 20:3–5; 34:14); **wisdom-consequential** — *if* the heart envies, *then* it rots / strays (Pro 14:30; 23:17); **narrative-exemplary** — Phinehas' jealousy → atonement and covenant (Num 25:11–13).

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: **covenantal/cultic** (Exo 34:14; Deu 4:24), **ritual-juridical** (the marriage ordeal, Num 5), **communal/familial** (Gen 30:1; 37:11), **liturgical-wisdom** (Psa 73; Pro 23:17), **eschatological-prophetic** (Isa 9:7; Zep 3:8). The settings show jealousy as covenant-attribute, marital safeguard, family fault-line, and eschatological zeal.

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor verse: **Exodus 34:14** (with 20:5) — "the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God" — most fully and directly expresses the characteristic's essential character: jealousy as God's covenant-exclusive, possessive ardour. (The human-zeal anchor is Num 25:11; the envy anchor Pro 14:30.)

**T7.2.6** — Exo 34:14 shows what no other verse shows: that jealousy is **God's very name/attribute** — not a passing affect but a defining covenant characteristic that grounds the whole demand of exclusive worship. No other verse predicates jealousy of God's *name* this directly.

**T7.3.1** — Most useful human-science lens: per the science extract, **social and evolutionary psychology** — jealousy as "protective of valued bonds and resources" (mate-guarding, social comparison), with the noted caveat that **divine jealousy lies outside science's scope**.

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates: the evolutionary account (jealousy = response to threat against a *valued* relationship/resource) illuminates why qinˈah arises only over **valued, rightful bonds** — the covenant (Exo 34:14), the marriage (Num 5:14), and why God's jealousy carries **positive** valence as covenant-protection (per the science extract's explicit point). Social-comparison theory illuminates the envy pole (Gen 30:1; Psa 73:3; Ecc 4:4 "from a man's envy of his neighbour").

**T7.3.3** — Where they diverge: clinical accounts **pathologise** jealousy, whereas the C4 evidence presents **divine jealousy and righteous zeal as positive** (Exo 34:14; Num 25:11–13) — "a genuine divergence from the pathologising tendency of clinical accounts" (science extract). Science also does not adjudicate the **covenantal-ontological** claim that God is rightly jealous.

**T7.3.4** — Gaps surfaced by science: the **attachment dimension** (secure vs anxious attachment predicting jealousy sensitivity) may illuminate differences between the cluster's jealousy terms — e.g. whether the marital "spirit of jealousy" (Num 5:14) and covenant jealousy encode a security/insecurity axis the biblical pass should test (per the science extract's T7.3.4). Labelled inferential — a prompt for further verse work.
