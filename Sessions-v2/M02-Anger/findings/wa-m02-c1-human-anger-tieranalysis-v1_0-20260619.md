## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — In the C1 set the characteristic is borne by the human person, not God; the divine role resolves to NONE in 227 of 228 occurrences. The single God-relation is God as **addressee** of the angry person — the LORD speaking to angry Cain (Gen 4:6). *Flag:* a few divine-subject verses leaked into C1 by field resolution (Exo 4:14; 2Ki 13:3) and properly belong to C2.

**T0.1.2** — Across C1 the characteristic is borne by the creature (human): Gen 4:5 (Cain); Gen 30:2 (Jacob); 1Sa 20:30 (Saul); Jon 4:1 (Jonah). Divine bearing of the same affect is held separately as C2 (researcher decision DEC-2). *Interpretation (inferential):* the same vocabulary borne by God in C2 (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē) being borne here by the person indicates anger is a shared affect, not an exclusively creaturely one — its place in the divine image is via that correspondence (see T0.3.2, T6.4).

**T0.2.1** — Stated effects in-verse: anger **stirs up strife / contention** (Pro 15:18; Pro 29:22); a harsh word **stirs up** anger while a soft answer turns it away (Pro 15:1); anger **lodges in the bosom of fools** (Ecc 7:9); "much vexation" accompanies much wisdom/knowledge (Ecc 1:18). Where no effect is stated (most narrative occurrences, e.g. Gen 31:35), record none.

**T0.2.2** — The evidence leans toward the **fallen condition**: anger linked to murder (Gen 4:5–8), to strife and transgression (Pro 29:22), to folly (Ecc 7:9). But the NT shows anger engaging moral evaluation — Jesus angry at hardness of heart (Mar 3:5) — pointing to **created design** for moral response. *Conclusion:* both, with the data weighting the fallen/distorted side (the created-design reading is inferential, grounded only at Mar 3:5).

**T0.2.3** — For human anger as such, no orientation toward future fullness is stated. The "wrath to come" (Mat 3:7; Luk 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16) is divine wrath received-from-outside, not the person's own anger maturing. **Silent** for C1 (pointer: C2).

**T0.3.1** — Where anger engages moral evaluation (Mar 3:5; the master's wrath at the unforgiving servant, Mat 18:34), the aspect of likeness instantiated is **moral responsiveness to wrong**. Inferential; not instantiated where the verses show only sinful anger (Gen 4:5; 1Sa 20:30).

**T0.3.2** — Shared between God and the person: the affect and its core vocabulary (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē) are borne by both (C1 here; C2 for God). It is not an exclusively creaturely analogue (T6.4.1).

**T0.3.3** — The verses tie unregulated anger to folly and sin (Pro 29:22 "a man of wrath stirs up strife"; Ecc 7:9 anger resting in the fool's bosom) and restraint to wisdom (Pro 15:18 "slow to anger quiets contention"; Pro 16:32 implied). *Interpretation:* the **regulation** of anger, not its mere presence, is what the evidence treats as indicating the inner condition.

**T0.4.1** — Human anger is carried in narrative and wisdom forms with no typological use in the C1 set; the typological/eschatological register attaches to divine wrath (C2). **Silent** for C1.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — In the programme the characteristic is **Human kindled / burning anger**. The name signals a hot, kindled inner affect — its essential nature is thermal arousal: the dominant senses are "wrath" (69), "angry" (48), "kindled" (18), "hot/heat/burning" (Gen 39:19; Est 1:12; Psa 39:3 vocabulary), and even "poison/venom" (Deut 32:33; Psa 58:4).

**T1.1.2** — The primary terms show: cheˈmah = heat/rage (root sense "to be hot"); chaˈrah = to burn / be kindled (Gen 4:5–6); orgē = settled wrath (Mat 3:7); qaˈtsaph = to be angry / break out (Num 16:22); aˈnaph = to be angry (lit. of the nostril). At the definitional level the cluster is built on a heat/burning metaphor for an aroused inner state.

**T1.1.3** — The name carries a **directional and relational** implication: anger is typically *against* or *with* someone (relational markers "against" Num 24:10; "with" Gen 40:2; "upon" 2Ch 32:25). It also carries a **somatic** implication through the co-term aph "nose/anger" (co-occurs as qualifier ×8, e.g. Gen 30:2) and the heat vocabulary.

**T1.2.1** — Mixed: it is borne both as a **condition/status** (noun forms cheˈmah/qeˈtseph/orgē — 129 status occurrences, e.g. Est 2:1) and as an **act** (verb forms chaˈrah/qaˈtsaph/aˈnaph — 93 action occurrences, e.g. Gen 4:5); a small dispositional **quality** strand appears (orgilos "quick-tempered", Pro 22:24-type; 5 quality occurrences).

**T1.2.2** — It can be **compound**: anger co-occurs with jealousy (qinˈah — co-term ×5, e.g. Pro 27:4), with provocation (kaˈas ×5), with "harm/distress" (raˈah ×7), and with the somatic nose-word aph (×8). Where it stands alone (Gen 31:35) it is simple. The vexation strand (kaˈaˈs) even fuses anger with **grief/sorrow** (senses "grief", "Sorrow" — Ecc 1:18; 1Sa 1:16), a compound with M03.

**T1.3.1** — The structural opposite shown in the evidence is **being slow to anger / a soft answer that turns away wrath** (Pro 15:1; Pro 15:18 "slow to anger quiets contention"). Patience/forbearance stands against kindled anger.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes and resists **calm/peaceable speech**: the "soft answer" turns it away (Pro 15:1); restraint "quiets" the contention it would otherwise raise (Pro 15:18).

**T1.3.3** — It ends where **grief** begins on one side (the kaˈaˈs vexation/sorrow occurrences sit on that boundary, Ecc 1:18) and where **settled jealousy** begins on another (qinˈah co-occurs but is its own characteristic, C4; Pro 27:4 sets "wrath" beside "jealousy"). It is not mere displeasure (cf. C5 indignation) and not contention itself (C6).

**T1.4.1** — Modes in-verse: as a **kindling verb** (qal of chaˈrah, "was kindled", Gen 4:5; morph HVqw3ms); as a **poured-out / executed** state (Eze 7:8 "pour out"; Hos 11:9 "execute"); as a state that **fills** a person (Est 3:5; 5:9 "filled with fury"); as a **burning** (Est 1:12). Grammatically it spans qal verbs and noun-states.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by direction and level: directed *upon/against* others (Jos 9:20 "upon"; Num 24:10 "against"), *with* a person (Gen 40:2), or *from* a person/God outward (Exo 32:12 "from"). At the heart level it is an inner seething (Est 5:9 "filled with anger"); as an outward act it is poured out (Eze 14:19).

**T1.4.3** — Yes — it operates in a **commanded / spoken** mode: "Fret not" / "refrain from anger" addressed as command (Psa 37:1, 37:7, 37:8); the master "in anger said/handed over" (Mat 18:34); anger expressed in denouncing speech (Psa 79:6 "pour out your anger... call on your name"). Recorded where present; elsewhere none.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate responses shown: Cain's **face fell** (Gen 4:5–6, the immediate downcast face of anger); anger **grieved** the one it struck (1Sa 20:34); a soft answer immediately **turns away** wrath (Pro 15:1); anger immediately **stirs up** strife (Pro 15:18; 29:22). Most occurrences state no immediate response (Gen 31:35) — record none there.

**T1.5.2** — Varied. The immediate response ranges from somatic (fallen face, Gen 4:6) to relational rupture (grief in another, 1Sa 20:34) to escalation (stirring strife, Pro 29:22) to verbal outburst (1Sa 20:30). No single consistent immediate response.

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effects: anger **lodges/rests** in the fool over time (Ecc 7:9, settled disposition); a man of wrath **abounds in transgression** (Pro 29:22, established pattern of sin); wrath **stirs up continual strife** (Pro 15:18; 30:33 implied). Record none where the verse shows only a momentary episode (Gen 31:36).

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is the first flash (fallen face, Gen 4:6; outburst, 1Sa 20:30); the sustained effect is the **settled lodging** of anger and its harvest of strife/transgression (Ecc 7:9; Pro 29:22). The difference is episode versus established disposition.

**T1.7.1** — It "takes hold rightly" where it engages **moral evaluation** of genuine wrong — Jesus' anger at hardness of heart (Mar 3:5); Moses' anger at the calf (Exo 32:19); anger at outrage/injustice (Gen 34:7; 1Sa 20:34 over Saul's shameful act). The inner condition is a true perception of wrong.

**T1.7.2** — It is blocked or turned away by a **soft answer** and by being **slow to anger** (Pro 15:1; 15:18), and counselled away by command (Psa 37:8 "refrain from anger"). Distortion appears where it is kindled over self-interest or wounded pride (1Sa 20:30; Est 1:12; Jon 4:1 over a plant). No verse in the C1 set attributes the distortion to another (adversarial/angelic) spirit — **silent** on that sub-point.

**T1.7.3** — Where anger is present but does not take hold rightly, the person is shown as **foolish and self-harming** — anger resting in the fool's bosom (Ecc 7:9), Jonah angry "enough to die" over a plant (Jon 4:9), Cain mastered by a sin "crouching at the door" he was told to rule (Gen 4:6–7).

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — Locations evidenced: **heart** (Exo 4:14 "glad in his heart"; 1Sa 17:28 "the evil of your heart"; 2Ch 32:25 "his heart was proud"→wrath; Est 5:9 anger "filled... in his heart"; Ecc 11:10 heart|flesh); **soul/nephesh** (Est 7:7 "in his wrath... [of soul]"; Job 32:2 "kindled"; Psa 31:9 "my soul... wasted with grief/anger"; Jer 18:20); **conscience** once (Rom 13:5, subjection "for the sake of... wrath/conscience"); **flesh/mind** once (Eph 2:3 "children of wrath... desires of the flesh and mind"). 212 of 228 occurrences locate no seat — record none there (Gen 4:5).

**T2.1.2** — The dominant pattern is **unlocated** (212/228): anger is named without a stated seat. Where located, the seat is chiefly the **heart** (9) and **soul** (4) — the volitional-affective centre — with isolated conscience (Rom 13:5) and flesh/mind (Eph 2:3) links. The seldom-named seat suggests anger is treated as a whole-person affect rather than housed in one faculty; the body link is **expressive** (heat, fallen face) rather than emphatic.

**T2.10.1** — Movement appears as anger **received from outside** in the eschatological strand — "the wrath to come" upon persons (Mat 3:7; Luk 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16) — i.e. onto the person from God. Within the person, no spirit→soul→body sequence is described; most occurrences show no movement (record none, Gen 31:35). No angelic/adversarial introduction of anger is evidenced — **silent** on that.

**T2.7.1** — Where a body link exists it runs **soul/spirit expressing through the body**: the heat/burning vocabulary and Cain's **fallen face** (Gen 4:6) are the inner state showing outwardly; Ecc 11:10 pairs heart and flesh ("remove vexation from your heart, put away pain from your flesh"). No verse shows the body feeding anger back to the soul — that direction is **silent**.

**T2.9.1** — Origin is generated **within the person** in the great majority (no source marker; Gen 30:2; 1Sa 20:30) and is stated as **received from outside** (from God) only in the eschatological wrath verses (Mat 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16). No verse attributes the person's anger to a generational source or to another spirit — record not stated for those.

**T2.9.2** — Origin is effectively **single** (within-person) for human anger, with the one contextual shift to received-from-God in the eschatological-judgment register (Mat 3:7; Luk 3:7). It does change with that context.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1** — Anger engages perception where it follows on **hearing**: it is kindled when one *hears* a report (shaˈma co-occurs ×6; e.g. Exo 16:20 Moses angry on seeing/hearing; 1Sa 11:6 Saul's anger kindled "when he heard"; Est 3:5 Haman saw Mordecai and was filled with fury). The inner sense engaged is hearing/sight as the trigger-gate.

**T3.1.2** — Effect on perception: the evidence does not describe anger altering the senses themselves; it shows perception **feeding** anger (Est 3:5 seeing → fury). No verse states anger distorting perception — **silent** on that effect.

**T3.1.3** — The pattern (perception as trigger, not as object of anger's effect) indicates anger is a **reactive affect** — aroused by what is perceived (heard/seen) rather than itself reshaping the senses.

**T3.2.1** — Cognition is engaged where anger pairs with **moral evaluation/appraisal of wrong** (faculty "affect|moral_evaluation" in 31 NT occurrences, e.g. Mat 3:7; Mat 18:34; Mar 3:5). The angry person has appraised an act as blameworthy (Gen 34:7 "outrage"; 1Sa 20:34 "disgrace").

**T3.2.2** — Effect on cognition: wisdom literature presents anger as **impairing** sound thought — it rests in the fool (Ecc 7:9), is owned by "a man of wrath" who "abounds in transgression" (Pro 29:22), and is set against the prudent who is "slow to anger" (Pro 15:18). So anger is shown degrading good judgment.

**T3.2.3** — The pattern: anger both **presupposes** a cognitive appraisal of wrong (its trigger) and **erodes** cognition when it settles (wisdom texts). This double relation marks anger as appraisal-driven yet judgment-clouding.

**T3.3.1** — Memory: a few verses tie anger to **remembered/recorded grievance** — the anger that "entered the chronicles" / was recorded (1Ch 27:24 context), and the stored-up "wrath" against a day (Rom 2:5-type; in C1, Pro 19:19 "a man of great wrath"). Direct engagement of the memory faculty is thin. Mostly **silent**.

**T3.3.2** — No C1 verse states anger acting on the memory faculty. **Silent.**

**T3.3.3** — The near-absence of memory engagement indicates human anger here is portrayed as **present-tense arousal** rather than a faculty of recollection.

**T3.4.1** — Affect is the **core** faculty: 196 of 228 occurrences are faculty=affect (Gen 4:5; Gen 30:2; Est 1:12; Jon 4:1). Anger is first and most an emotional state of the inner being.

**T3.4.2** — Effect on affect: anger **displaces** and **co-occurs** with other affects — it grieves (1Sa 20:34), pairs with jealousy (Pro 27:4) and with vexation/sorrow (Ecc 1:18; 1Sa 1:16), and can reach the pitch of wishing death (Jon 4:9). It intensifies the affective field.

**T3.4.3** — That affect dominates almost every occurrence confirms the characteristic's nature as a **high-arousal emotion** rather than a primarily cognitive or volitional one.

**T3.5.1** — Creativity / imagination: no C1 verse engages it. **Silent.**

**T3.5.2** — **Silent.**

**T3.5.3** — The complete absence indicates anger is not connected to the originative/imaginative faculty in this evidence.

**T3.6.1** — Volition is engaged where anger is **commanded or restrained by choice**: "refrain from anger, forsake wrath" (Psa 37:8); "fret not" (Psa 37:1, 7); the choice to be "slow to anger" (Pro 15:18). Anger is presented as something the will is summoned to govern.

**T3.6.2** — Effect on volition: unrestrained anger **overrides** the will toward destructive action (Cain told to rule the sin but does not, Gen 4:7–8; Saul's anger drives an oath/attack, 1Sa 20:30–33). Conversely the will can quiet it (Pro 15:1, 18). It constrains and is constrained by volition.

**T3.6.3** — The pattern shows anger as a **contested** faculty — repeatedly placed under volitional command (Psa 37:8) — indicating Scripture treats it as governable, not merely suffered.

**T3.7.1** — Agency is engaged where anger **issues in action**: it executes judgment/violence (Mat 22:7 the king "sent troops"; 2Ki 3:27 "great wrath"→withdrawal; Est 7:7–10 the king's wrath → execution), and "stirs up strife" as an act (Pro 29:22).

**T3.7.2** — Effect on agency: anger **drives and distorts** action — Cain to murder (Gen 4:8), Moses to break the tablets (Exo 32:19), Haman to plot (Est 3:5–6). It is a powerful motor of agency, often harmful.

**T3.7.3** — The recurrent anger→act sequence (Gen 4:5→8; Est 3:5→6) indicates anger is strongly **action-generating**: it rarely stays inert.

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation is directly engaged in 31 occurrences (faculty affect|moral_evaluation): Jesus' anger at hardness of heart (Mar 3:5); anger at the "outrage" (Gen 34:7); at "disgrace" (1Sa 20:34); the master's wrath at unforgiveness (Mat 18:34).

**T3.8.2** — Effect: anger **expresses** a moral verdict — it is the affective form of judging an act wrong (Mar 3:5; Gen 34:7). Where the verdict is just, the anger is righteous; where it is over wounded pride (1Sa 20:30) or trivial loss (Jon 4:9), the moral evaluation is shown to be defective.

**T3.8.3** — The pattern: anger is **morally double-valued** — sometimes the right response to evil (Mar 3:5), sometimes itself the sin (Gen 4:5; Pro 29:22). Its nature is bound to the justice of its trigger.

**T3.9.1** — Conscience: engaged once, where subjection is urged "for the sake of conscience" alongside wrath (Rom 13:5). Otherwise the acute witness of guilt is not the faculty in view. Largely **silent**.

**T3.9.2** — No C1 verse shows anger acting on the conscience as such (Rom 13:5 sets wrath beside conscience rather than anger operating on it). **Silent** on effect.

**T3.9.3** — The near-absence indicates human anger here is not primarily a faculty of self-conviction (contrast guilt/conscience characteristics elsewhere).

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness (integrated moral awareness + will + action): engaged where the verses press the person to **govern** anger by sustained discipline — "refrain... forsake" (Psa 37:8), "slow to anger" as settled practice (Pro 15:18), Cain summoned to "rule over" the crouching sin (Gen 4:7).

**T3.10.2** — Effect: failure of conscientiousness lets anger **issue in transgression** (Pro 29:22; Gen 4:8); its exercise **quiets** the strife anger would raise (Pro 15:18).

**T3.10.3** — The pattern marks anger as a principal **test-site of self-governance** — repeatedly the thing the integrated moral person must master.

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity is engaged because anger is overwhelmingly **interpersonal**: experiencer "other" in 134 occurrences and directed at a person (object_type person 43): Gen 4:5 (toward Abel-context); Gen 30:2 (Jacob at Rachel); 1Sa 20:30 (Saul at Jonathan).

**T3.11.2** — Effect on relational capacity: anger **ruptures** bonds — it grieves the other (1Sa 20:34), drives fratricide (Gen 4:8), and breaks fellowship (Gen 27:44–45, Esau's anger sends Jacob away). It damages connection.

**T3.11.3** — The strong interpersonal pattern indicates anger is constitutively **relational** — it arises within and acts upon the bond between persons (and with God, T4.2).

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1** — God-toward-human operation of the characteristic belongs to C2 (divine wrath); within the C1 set the human is the bearer, so God→human anger is **not in view except** in the leaked/eschatological verses (Exo 4:14; Mat 3:7; Rom 5:9). For C1 proper, record none.

**T4.1.2** — **Silent** for C1 (basis of God's extension is a C2 question).

**T4.1.3** — **Silent** for C1 (pointer: C2).

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God: the person's anger is directed **at God** in several verses — Jonah "angry" at the LORD over the plant and the city (Jon 4:1, 4:9); Moses' anger before the LORD (Num 16:15); contexts where people charge God in their wrath (object_type=God, e.g. Judg 6:39 context; 1Sa 15:11 Samuel angry and crying to the LORD).

**T4.2.2** — The posture revealed is one of **complaint/contention** with God rather than supplication — Jonah's self-justifying "I do well to be angry" (Jon 4:9). The evidence shows anger as a (disordered) posture toward God, not worship.

**T4.2.3** — That a person can be angry *at* God (Jon 4) shows the relationship is **real and contestable** — the person engages God directly even in resentment; but the verses frame such anger as misplaced (Jon 4:9–11).

**T4.3.1** — Person-to-person extension: anger is "extended" toward another constantly (Gen 30:2 at Rachel; 1Sa 20:30 at Jonathan; 2Sa 13:21 David's anger). It operates as **directed hostility** within the bond.

**T4.3.2** — Genuine (righteous) extension accompanies a true perception of **wrong/outrage** in the giver (Gen 34:7; Exo 32:19; 1Sa 20:34); defective extension accompanies wounded **pride or self-interest** (1Sa 20:30; Est 1:12).

**T4.3.3** — The evidence does not specify a prior inner state a person must "have received" before extending anger (anger is shown as immediate arousal, Est 3:5). **Silent** on a precondition of giving.

**T4.4.1** — Receiving anger from another: persons are **objects** of another's anger (Jonathan grieved by Saul's anger, 1Sa 20:34; Jacob fearing Esau's wrath, Gen 27:44–45). Uptake is shown as **fear/grief**, not adoption of the anger.

**T4.4.2** — What accompanies the meeting of another's anger is **fear and withdrawal** (Gen 27:44 "until your brother's fury turns away"; Jacob flees). No verse shows a positive uptake of anger from another. 

**T4.4.3** — The person who meets another's anger is shown **grieved and endangered** (1Sa 20:34 Jonathan; Gen 27:45 Jacob must flee) — the inner state is wounded and self-protective.

**T4.5.1** — Anger operates both **within bonds** (brother to brother, Gen 4:5; 27:44; father to son context) and **across distance/difference** (toward enemies/nations, Psa 79:6 "pour out your anger on the nations"; Mat 22:7). It is not confined to either.

**T4.5.2** — It **crosses covenantal boundaries**: directed within Israel (1Sa 20:30) and at the nations outside covenant (Psa 79:6; Jer 10:25). Not covenant-internal only.

**T4.5.3** — Relational scope is **wide** — kin (Gen 4; 27:44), rivals (Gen 30:2), kings and subjects (Est 1:12; 7:7), Israel and the nations (Psa 79:6). No relational category is shown immune from being its object.

**T4.6.1** — Operation in relation to other spiritual beings: not shown for human anger. **Silent.**

**T4.6.2** — Whether the characteristic is a site of adversarial activity: the C1 verses do not say (the nearest is Gen 4:7 "sin is crouching at the door," which names sin, not an adversarial power acting on Cain's anger). **Silent** (recorded as not evidenced).

**T4.6.3** — Angelic mediation/strengthening of anger: not evidenced. **Silent.**

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1** — Transformation: anger changes the person's **condition and conduct** — Cain moves from angry to murderer (Gen 4:5–8); it hardens into the fool's settled state (Ecc 7:9). Most single verses show an episode, not a transformation (record none, Gen 31:35).

**T5.1.2** — Reversibility: shown reversible where anger **turns away / cools** — Esau's fury is expected to "turn away" (Gen 27:44–45); a soft answer turns it (Pro 15:1). Shown to harden where it "rests/lodges" (Ecc 7:9). Both appear; the evidence does not treat it as inherently irreversible.

**T5.2.1** — A sequence appears: **perception → kindling → outburst/act** (Cain: regard withheld → "very angry," face fell → kills, Gen 4:5–8; Saul: hears → "anger kindled" → oath/spear, 1Sa 20:30–33). Where no sequence is shown, record none (Gen 39:19).

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism of change: chiefly **sudden kindling** (chaˈrah "was kindled," Gen 4:5; 1Sa 11:6) and, on the cooling side, **restraint/soft answer** (Pro 15:1). Discipline appears where wrath is the LORD's instrument (Lev 26:28 — but that is the divine/leaked register). Record none where unstated.

**T5.3.2** — The mechanism differs: **arousal** mechanisms (a perceived wrong, Est 3:5) versus **attenuation** mechanisms (soft answer/slowness, Pro 15:1, 18; temporal turning-away, Gen 27:44). Context-dependent.

**T5.4.1** — Relation to suffering/affliction: anger appears as a **product of affliction and provocation** — Hannah's anger/vexation out of her rival's provocation and her "great anxiety and vexation" (1Sa 1:6, 1:16); Job's anger amid his suffering (Job 32:2 context). It is also a **response to** wrong done (Gen 34:7).

**T5.4.2** — What suffering does to it: suffering/provocation **kindles and sustains** anger (1Sa 1:6 the rival "provoked her grievously"; Psa 31:9 "my soul... wasted" amid grief/anger). Suffering amplifies rather than dissolves it.

**T5.5.1** — Participation in the sanctification arc: the wisdom material places anger inside the long work of **character** — "whoever is slow to anger has great understanding, but he who has a hasty temper exalts folly" (Pro 14:29-type; Pro 15:18); the call to "put away" anger (Ecc 11:10; cf. Eph 4:31 context). Anger is something the formed character learns to govern.

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological orientation: for human anger as such, none; the eschatological horizon ("wrath to come," Mat 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16) is divine wrath the person is warned to flee, not the maturing of human anger. **Silent** for C1 (pointer: C2).

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1** — Adjacent characteristics co-occurring in the C1 verses (from co-terms): **jealousy** (qinˈah ×5; Pro 27:4 "wrath... jealousy"; Num 25:11 context), **provocation** (kaˈas ×5; Deut-type), the somatic **nose-anger** aph (×8; Gen 30:2), **harm/distress** raˈah (×7) and **evil** raˈa (×5), and **hearing** shaˈma (×6) as trigger. Grief/vexation co-occurs via kaˈaˈs (Ecc 1:18).

**T6.1.2** — The co-occurrence places C1 at the **hot, reactive centre** of the cluster: tightly bound to provocation (its cause, C3) and jealousy (C4), expressed through the somatic aph, and gated by perception (shaˈma). It sits between provocation that arouses it and the strife it produces.

**T6.2.1** — Sequence: anger consistently **follows provocation/perception** and **precedes strife/harm** — provoked/heard → angry → strife (Pro 15:18; 29:22; Est 3:5–6). 

**T6.2.2** — The relationship reads as **causal**: provocation causes anger (kaˈas → anger), and anger causes strife/transgression (Pro 29:22 "stirs up strife... abounds in transgression"). A causal chain, not mere correlation.

**T6.3.1** — Anger **produces** strife/contention (Pro 15:18; 29:22), grief in another (1Sa 20:34), and violent action (Gen 4:8; Mat 22:7) — by the mechanism of issuing the aroused state into speech and act.

**T6.3.2** — Anger **is produced by** provocation (C3; kaˈas co-term), by jealousy/rivalry (C4; Gen 30:1–2 Rachel's envy → Jacob's anger; Pro 27:4), and by perceived outrage/affliction (Gen 34:7; 1Sa 1:6).

**T6.3.3** — Anger is a **constituent of** "vexation" that also contains grief (kaˈaˈs, Ecc 1:18 — anger+sorrow compound) and stands as a constituent in the broader provocation→anger→strife structure; jealousy can contain an anger element (Pro 27:4 pairs them).

**T6.4.1** — Vocabulary shared with other characteristics: cheˈmah/chaˈrah/orgē are shared with **C2 divine wrath** (same lemmas, different bearer) and with **C3 provoking-to-anger** (chaˈrah/qaˈtsaph where God is provoked); kaˈaˈs "vexation" is shared with **C5 indignation** and bridges **M03 grief**.

**T6.4.2** — Root-level sharing: yes — the chaˈrah / chaˈron / choˈri root (to burn) generates terms across C1 (human), C2 (divine wrath), and C3 (provoke to burning anger). One burning-root architecture spans the bearer-distinctions.

**T6.4.3** — The shared vocabulary shows the characteristics are **one affect under different relations** — the boundary between C1, C2, C3 is the *bearer/relation* (who is angry, who provokes) rather than a different word-stock. This confirms the characteristic-perspective basis of the split.

**T6.5.1** — The nearest neighbour is **C2 divine wrath**: it most closely resembles C1 (same lemmas, same heat). The distinction is the **bearer and its moral standing** — God's wrath is righteous by definition (C2), human anger is morally double-valued (T3.8.3).

**T6.5.2** — Apparent overlap (the leaked verses Exo 4:14; 2Ki 13:3) marks the **precise boundary**: the deciding criterion is whether God is the angry subject (→C2) or the human is (→C1). Where the read-resolved divine role failed, verses crossed the line — these require verse-confirmation (boundary caveat).

**T6.5.3** — The distinction from C2 is one of **bearer/direction and moral valence**, not of kind or constitutional level (same affect, same vocabulary, same heat). From C3 it is one of **direction** (experiencing anger vs arousing it in another); from C5 (indignation) one of **degree/temperature** (hot rage vs cooler displeasure).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms and roots: **cheˈmah** (H2534, "heat/rage," from the root "to be hot"); **chaˈrah** (H2734, "to burn / be kindled"); **orgē** (G3709, "wrath," from a root of swelling/natural impulse); supporting **qaˈtsaph** (to break out in anger), **aˈnaph** (to be angry, of the nostril), **qeˈtseph** (wrath). The roots converge on **heat/burning and swelling**.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: **noun/status** (cheˈmah, qeˈtseph, orgē — 129 occurrences, e.g. Est 2:1), **verb/action** (chaˈrah, qaˈtsaph, aˈnaph, orgizō — 93, e.g. Gen 4:5), and **adjective/quality** (orgilos "quick-tempered"; zestos "hot" — 5). The range shows anger operates as a **state one is in**, an **act one does**, and a **dispositional trait** one has.

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range of the primary term: from "wrath/fury" (Est 1:12) through "kindled/hot/heat/burning" (Gen 4:5; 39:19; Est 1:12) to "poison/venom" (Deut 32:33; Psa 58:4) and even "vexation/grief" (kaˈaˈs, Ecc 1:18). Breadth: intense settled wrath → momentary heat → toxic outflow → sorrowful vexation.

**T7.1.4** — Distinguishing terms: **disposition vs act** — orgilos "quick-tempered" (disposition) vs chaˈrah "was kindled" (act); **condition vs quality** — cheˈmah (a state, status noun) vs orgilos (a quality). Received-vs-given is not lexicalised here. Recorded: disposition/act and condition/quality distinctions present.

**T7.1.5** — Term for the opposite/absence: no single antonym lemma in the C1 vocabulary; the opposite is carried by an **idiom** — "slow to anger" (erek aphayim) and the "soft answer" (Pro 15:1, 18). Record: idiomatic, not a single lexeme.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term: **yes** — "a man of wrath / hot-tempered man" (baˈal cheˈmah, Pro 22:24-type; "a man of great wrath," Pro 19:19) and Greek **orgilos** "quick-tempered [person]." Recorded.

**T7.1.7** — Supplication/seeking term (a term for seeking this characteristic from another): **none** — one does not seek anger from another in the vocabulary. Record none.

**T7.1.8** — OT→NT relationship: the Hebrew heat-cluster (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, qeˈtseph) is carried into Greek by **orgē / orgizō** (settled wrath) with the NT adding the explicit pairing of anger with **moral evaluation** (Mar 3:5; Mat 18:34, faculty affect|moral_evaluation) and the **eschatological "wrath to come"** register (Mat 3:7; Rom 5:9; Rev 6:16). Continuity of the affect; development toward moral-judgment and eschatological framing.

**T7.1.9** — NT-coined term: **parorgismos** ("provocation/anger," Eph 4:26 context) is a rare, largely NT/biblical-Greek formation; **orgilos** is the trait-adjective. The coinage marks a sharpened concern with **provoked anger and the angry disposition** in the NT.

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc: from a **heat/burning** core (chaˈrah, cheˈmah) the range fans out to settled **wrath** (orgē), a **trait** (orgilos), a **toxic** extreme (venom, Deut 32:33), and a **grief-tinged** edge (kaˈaˈs vexation) — a complete range from momentary kindling to settled disposition to eschatological judgment.

**T7.2.1** — Function of the primary term in its primary verse: in Gen 4:5 chaˈrah is the **main predicate of Cain's state** ("Cain was very angry"), driving the narrative toward the first murder; the term is the hinge of the episode. In Pro 15:18 cheˈmah is the **subject** whose action ("stirs up strife") the proverb contrasts with the slow-to-anger.

**T7.2.2** — Literary forms carrying C1: **narrative** (Genesis, Samuel, Esther — Gen 4:5; 1Sa 20:30; Est 1:12), **wisdom** (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes — Pro 15:18; Ecc 7:9), **psalm** (Psa 6:1; 37:8), **prophecy** (Eze; Jer), and **epistle/apocalyptic** (Eph 2:3; Rev 6:16). Responsible interpretation requires reading narrative for character/consequence, wisdom for general maxim, and psalm/prophecy for their poetic-covenantal frame (BOOK distribution: Psa 23, Eze 17, Pro 14, Gen 12).

**T7.2.3** — Logical structure of the key arguments: the wisdom argument is **conditional-consequential** — *if* a man is wrathful/hasty, *then* strife and transgression follow (Pro 15:18; 29:22); the narrative argument is **sequential-causal** — provocation → anger → act (Gen 4:5–8). Premise: anger perceives/responds to wrong; conclusion: its unrestrained issue is destructive.

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: **covenantal** (Israel's history, Num/Deu), **judicial/royal** (the king's wrath executing judgment, Est 7:7–10; Mat 22:7), **communal** (strife among brothers/people, Gen 13 context; 2Sa 19:42), and **liturgical/lament** (Psa 6; 37). The settings show anger as both a private affect and a public, judicial force.

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor verse: **Genesis 4:5–6** — Cain "was very angry, and his face fell," with the LORD's question "Why are you angry?" most fully and directly expresses the characteristic's essential character (kindled affect, somatic expression, moral summons to mastery).

**T7.2.6** — Gen 4:5–6 shows what no other C1 verse shows together: the **kindling**, the **somatic sign** (fallen face), the **divine address** naming and questioning the anger, and the immediate **summons to rule it** before it issues in act (Gen 4:7) — the full anatomy of human anger in one scene.

**T7.3.1** — Most useful human-science lens (per science extract): **clinical and social psychology — emotion theory** for personal anger (Averill, Berkowitz, Tavris, Spielberger's trait/state distinction), supplemented by **moral psychology** where anger engages moral evaluation.

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates: appraisal theory's **blame-attribution** structure illuminates the cluster's binding of anger to perceived wrongdoing (Gen 34:7; Mar 3:5); the **heat/arousal physiology** illuminates the thermal vocabulary (chaˈrah "kindled," "hot," "burning," Gen 4:5; Est 1:12); the **trait/state** distinction maps onto C1's split between "a man of wrath" (trait, Pro 19:19; orgilos) and "was kindled" (state, Gen 4:5); Tavris's finding that **venting amplifies** anger coheres with the wisdom counsel that wrath "stirs up" more strife (Pro 15:18; 29:22).

**T7.3.3** — Where they diverge: science tends to **pathologise** anger, whereas the C1 evidence presents some anger as **righteous and appropriate** (Mar 3:5 Jesus' anger; Exo 32:19) — a legitimate moral response, not merely a state to be managed. Science also has no analogue for anger framed within **covenant judgment** (the leaked/eschatological register).

**T7.3.4** — Gaps surfaced by science: the role of **rumination** in sustaining anger (Nolen-Hoeksema) may sharpen how C1's "lodging/resting" sustained-anger texts (Ecc 7:9; Pro 19:19) are classified — whether they encode rumination as the maintenance mechanism. This is a prompt for further verse work, not a verse finding (labelled inferential).
