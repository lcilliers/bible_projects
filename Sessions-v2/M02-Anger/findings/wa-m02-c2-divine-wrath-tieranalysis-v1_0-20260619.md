## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Predicated of God directly: the wrath is God's, with God as **possessor** ("the wrath of the LORD," Exo 15:7; Num 32:14; Deu 29:23) or **agent** ("the LORD's anger was kindled," Gen 18:30; Exo 22:24; Num 11:1). This is the defining relation of C2.

**T0.1.2** — Across C2 the characteristic is **borne by God himself** throughout (possessor/agent in 144 of 145). *Interpretation:* that the same vocabulary borne by the creature (C1) is here predicated of God establishes anger as belonging to the divine nature as a righteous response — the human instance (C1) is the creaturely correspondent (Divine-Human Correspondence dimension).

**T0.2.1** — Stated purpose/effect: God's wrath **consumes/destroys** the guilty (Num 16:46 plague; Deu 9:19–20; Isa 34:2 "devoted them to destruction"), **executes covenant judgment** (Deu 29:28 "uprooted... in anger"), and is repeatedly shown as **turnable away** by repentance/intercession (Num 25:4; 2Ch 29:10; Jon 3:9 — its purpose is corrective, not merely punitive).

**T0.2.2** — God's wrath reads as belonging to God's **holy character** responding to covenant violation — not to a fallen condition (it is His by nature). The causes are consistently sin/idolatry (Deu 6:15; 9:19; 1Ki 11:9). *Conclusion:* belongs to created/uncreated design as a righteous response to evil (it has no "fallen" register; the fallenness is in its objects, not in it).

**T0.2.3** — Orientation toward future fullness: yes — the wrath is oriented to a **day/coming** of judgment (Isa 34:2; the prophetic "day" texts; NT orgē "the wrath" Rom 9:22; Rev 14:10; 16:19). It points beyond the present to an eschatological consummation.

**T0.3.1** — Aspect of divine likeness it grounds in the person: **righteous moral response to evil** — where human anger engages true moral evaluation (C1, Mar 3:5) it images this divine wrath. Inferential link via shared vocabulary.

**T0.3.2** — Shared: the affect is borne by God (here) and by the person (C1) under the same terms (cheˈmah, chaˈrah, orgē). Not an exclusively creaturely analogue — it is primarily a divine attribute the creature reflects.

**T0.3.3** — Where God's wrath rests on a person it indicates the person stands under judgment for sin (Deu 9:19; Joh 3:36 "the wrath of God remains on him"); where it is **turned away** (Num 25:11; 2Ch 29:10) it indicates restored covenant standing. The condition indicated is the person's covenant relation, not the image per se.

**T0.4.1** — Typological/eschatological use: yes — divine wrath functions as the pattern the NT "wrath to come" fulfils (Rom 9:22; Rev 14:10; 16:19; 19:15), the **divine instance establishing the eschatological pattern**. Direction: from the divine instance outward to the coming judgment.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Programme name: **Divine wrath**. The name signals God's holy, kindled response to evil; essential nature is **burning judgment** — dominant senses "wrath" (48), "fierce" (19), "kindled" (16), "burning" (7), "fury" (10).

**T1.1.2** — Primary terms: cheˈmah (heat/fury), chaˈron "burning anger" (the most God-specific term, Exo 15:7; Num 25:4), chaˈrah (kindled, Exo 22:24), qeˈtseph (wrath/overflow), orgē (NT settled wrath). The definitional core is **intense, kindled heat** belonging to God.

**T1.1.3** — Directional/relational: God's wrath is **against** persons and nations (Num 11:1; Deu 6:15) and **poured out / sent forth** (Jer 4:4 "go forth like fire"; Lam 2:4; Eze 22:22) — an outward-moving, judicial force.

**T1.2.1** — Mainly a **condition/status** (noun forms, 103 occurrences — "the wrath of the LORD," Num 32:14) and an **act** (verb forms, 42 — "was kindled," Gen 18:30). No quality/disposition strand for God here.

**T1.2.2** — Often **compound**: wrath fused with **jealousy** (chaˈron alongside qinˈah, Num 25:11; Deu 29:20; Zep 1:18), with **fury/indignation** terms (cheˈmah + zaˈam contexts), and paired with **fire** imagery (Jer 4:4; Deu 32:22). Where it stands alone (Gen 18:30) it is simple.

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite shown: God's wrath **turning away** into mercy/favour — "the LORD turned from his burning anger" (Jos 7:26; 2Ki 23:26; Jon 3:9; Psa 85:3 "you withdrew all your wrath"). Mercy/favour stands against wrath.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes **continuance** when repentance comes: it is "quenched" (2Ki 22:17; 2Ch 34:25) and resists persisting where the people humble themselves (2Ch 12:7).

**T1.3.3** — It ends where **chastening love** and **jealousy** begin (it is bound to covenant, Deu 29:20), and is not arbitrary rage — it is not the human disordered anger of C1 (T6.5).

**T1.4.1** — Modes: **kindled** (qal/niphal of chaˈrah, Exo 22:24); **poured out** (Lam 2:4; Eze 22:22; morph of natak/shaphak); **gone forth like fire** (Jer 4:4; 21:12); **sent** (Deu 32:24; Job 20:23); **cast** (Deu 29:28). It operates as both a sudden kindling and a deliberate pouring-out.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies: kindled *against* a person (Exo 32:10), poured *on* a place (Eze 22:22 Jerusalem), sent *as* plague/sword (Deu 32:24); intensified ("great wrath," Deu 29:28; "hotly," Num 11:10).

**T1.4.3** — Communicative/spoken mode: yes — God **declares** his wrath through prophets ("thus says the LORD... my wrath," Eze 5:13 context; Jer 4:4 spoken oracle) and **swears** in wrath ("as I swore in my wrath," Psa 95:11 / Heb 3:11; 4:3). Recorded; elsewhere none.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response following God's wrath: **judgment falls** — plague (Num 11:33; 16:46), fire (Num 11:1), destruction/slaughter (Isa 34:2 "given to slaughter"), or, where averted, the wrath **quenched** (2Ki 22:17). Most occurrences name no immediate response (Gen 18:30) — record none.

**T1.5.2** — Varied: the immediate response is either **executed judgment** (Num 11:1; 16:46) or, on repentance/intercession, **withheld/quenched judgment** (Num 25:11; 2Ch 34:25). Two consistent poles.

**T1.6.1** — Sustained effect: God's wrath, when it runs its course, leaves a land **desolate** (Deu 29:23–28 "uprooted... cast into another land"; Lam 4:11 "kindled a fire... consumed its foundations"); when turned away, it establishes **restored covenant** (Psa 85:3–4). Record none where unstated.

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is the falling blow (plague/fire, Num 11:1); the sustained effect is the **enduring desolation or restored standing** (Deu 29:28; Psa 85:3). Episode of judgment versus its lasting result.

**T1.7.1** — It "operates rightly" — i.e. is provoked — where there is **covenant violation/idolatry**: other gods (Deu 6:15; 7:4), the golden calf (Deu 9:19), forsaking the LORD (Deu 31:17; 1Ki 11:9). The condition is real sin.

**T1.7.2** — It is **turned away / blocked** by intercession (Moses, Psa 106:23 context; Num 16:46–48 Aaron's atonement), by repentance and humbling (2Ch 12:7; Jon 3:9–10), and by zealous covenant-action (Phinehas, Num 25:11). No verse attributes interference with God's wrath to another spirit — **silent** on that.

**T1.7.3** — Not applicable as posed (God's wrath is never "present but failing to take hold" in a person — it is God's, not a creaturely capacity). Where God **restrains** it, the people are spared though guilty (2Ch 12:7 "my wrath shall not be poured out... by Shishak"). Recorded as the nearest evidenced case.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — As predicated of God, constitutional location is largely **not applicable / NONE** (136/145). Where the term touches a human seat it is the **heart** of the one against whom/whom God acts (1Ki 11:9 Solomon's heart turned → wrath; Isa 57:17 "I struck him... he went on backsliding in the way of his heart"; Jer 4:4 "circumcise... your heart, lest my wrath go forth"), **soul** (Jer 51:45), **flesh** (Ecc 5:6). These locate the *human* covenant-condition that provokes wrath, not a seat of wrath in God.

**T2.1.2** — The pattern (overwhelmingly unlocated) confirms divine wrath is **not seated in a creaturely faculty**; the few seat-references attach to the human heart as the locus of the sin that draws wrath (Jer 4:4). Any body link is **indicative** (of human guilt), not a seat of the wrath itself.

**T2.10.1** — Movement: God's wrath moves **onto the person/nation from God** (the defining external direction) — "my wrath will go forth" (Jer 4:4), "poured out on this place" (2Ch 34:25), received-from-outside (2Ch 29:10; Jer 32:31). No spirit→soul→body internal sequence; no angelic introduction. Record the God→person movement.

**T2.7.1** — Body direction: where a body link exists it runs from the **human heart/flesh** as the site of provoking sin (Jer 4:4; Ecc 5:6) — i.e. the body indicates the guilt; it does not house God's wrath. No soul→body expression *of God* is in view. Largely **silent** on a body-direction proper to the characteristic.

**T2.9.1** — Origin: the wrath **originates in God** (it is His by possession, Exo 15:7; agency, Gen 18:30) — not generated in the person, received from another, or carried generationally. Stated source = God himself.

**T2.9.2** — Origin is **single** (God) and does not change with context — every occurrence has God as source.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1** — Perception: God's wrath responds to what God **sees/hears** of sin ("the LORD saw... his anger," contexts of Num 11:1, 11:10; Deu 32:19 "the LORD saw it and spurned them"). The trigger is divine perception of evil.

**T3.1.2** — Effect on (human) perception: God's wrath leaves the people **blind/hardened** in some texts (Isa 42:25 "it set him on fire... but he did not understand"). Recorded; elsewhere not stated.

**T3.1.3** — Pattern: divine wrath is **perception-triggered** (God sees the sin) and can **dull** human perception under judgment (Isa 42:25).

**T3.2.1** — Cognition: engaged as God's **judicial appraisal** — wrath follows God's reckoning of covenant-breach (Deu 9:19; Rom 1:18 "the wrath of God is revealed against... unrighteousness," faculty affect|moral_evaluation).

**T3.2.2** — Effect on human cognition: under wrath the people **do not lay it to heart / do not understand** (Isa 42:25). Wrath as judgment can accompany cognitive hardening.

**T3.2.3** — Pattern: divine wrath is itself **appraisal-grounded** (a considered response to evil), distinguishing it from blind rage.

**T3.3.1** — Memory: God's wrath relates to **remembered covenant** — kindled when the people forget Him (Deu 32:18 context) and stored against "the day of wrath" (Rom 2:5-type; not in set). Direct memory-faculty engagement is thin — mostly **silent**.

**T3.3.2** — **Silent** on wrath acting on a memory faculty.

**T3.3.3** — The thinness indicates divine wrath is framed as **present judicial response**, not as a function of recollection (though covenant memory is its backdrop).

**T3.4.1** — Affect: the core faculty — 140/145 occurrences are affect (Exo 22:24; Num 11:1). Divine wrath is a genuine affective response in God (anthropopathically expressed).

**T3.4.2** — Effect on affect: it is the **intensified** affective register of God's holiness — "fierce" and "burning" (chaˈron, Exo 15:7; Num 25:4). On the human side it evokes **terror** (the people fear, Deu 9:19 "I was afraid of the anger").

**T3.4.3** — That affect dominates confirms divine wrath is a **real disposition** of God toward evil, not a mere metaphor of action.

**T3.5.1** — Creativity/imagination: not engaged. **Silent.**

**T3.5.2** — **Silent.**

**T3.5.3** — The absence indicates wrath has no relation to the originative faculty.

**T3.6.1** — Volition: engaged as God's **sovereign choice** to execute or relent — "the LORD turned from his burning anger" (Jos 7:26; 2Ki 23:26), He "swore in wrath" (Psa 95:11/Heb 3:11). Wrath is exercised and withheld by God's will.

**T3.6.2** — Effect on (human) volition: the threat of wrath summons the human will to **repent / turn** (Jer 4:4 "circumcise your heart, lest my wrath go forth"; 2Ch 30:8 "that his fierce anger may turn away").

**T3.6.3** — Pattern: divine wrath is **will-governed** in God (relentable, Jon 3:9–10) — never an uncontrolled overflow.

**T3.7.1** — Agency: divine wrath is intensely **action-issuing** — it sends plague (Num 11:33), fire (Num 11:1), sword/famine (Deu 32:24; Eze 5:12 context), exile (Deu 29:28). Wrath is God's executive judgment.

**T3.7.2** — Effect on agency: it **acts upon** the objects decisively (Isa 34:2 destruction); on the human side it can **provoke counter-action** of repentance (2Ch 29:10 "make a covenant... that his fierce wrath may turn").

**T3.7.3** — The constant wrath→act pattern marks it as the **agency of holy judgment**.

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation: directly engaged — wrath is God's verdict on covenant-breach and unrighteousness (Rom 1:18; 12:19 "leave it to the wrath of God"; 13:4 the magistrate "carries out God's wrath on the wrongdoer").

**T3.8.2** — Effect: it **enacts** the moral verdict — justice executed (Deu 9:19; Rom 13:4). It is the affective-executive form of God's righteousness against evil.

**T3.8.3** — Pattern: divine wrath is **wholly bound to moral evaluation** — always a response to real wrong (the causes are uniformly sin/idolatry), distinguishing it categorically from the morally double-valued human anger of C1.

**T3.9.1** — Conscience: engaged on the human side as the wrath that **vindicates** the magistrate's role "for the sake of conscience" (Rom 13:5). Otherwise the acute inner witness is not the faculty in view — mostly **silent**.

**T3.9.2** — **Silent** on wrath operating on conscience as such (Rom 13:5 sets them side by side).

**T3.9.3** — The thinness indicates divine wrath is **objective judgment** rather than a faculty of inner self-conviction.

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness (integrated moral response): God's wrath is the supreme instance of **integrated moral awareness, will, and act** — He perceives sin, judges it, and acts (Deu 9:19; Rom 1:18). On the human side it summons the integrated response of repentance (2Ch 30:8).

**T3.10.2** — Effect: it presses the person/nation toward **covenant integrity** (turn and live, Eze 18-type; 2Ch 30:8).

**T3.10.3** — Pattern: divine wrath models conscientiousness perfectly integrated in God.

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity: divine wrath is intensely **relational/covenantal** — directed at *His people* for breaking *His covenant* (Deu 6:15 "for the LORD your God... is a jealous God... lest the anger of the LORD be kindled against you").

**T3.11.2** — Effect on the relationship: wrath signals **covenant rupture** (Deu 31:17 "I will forsake them... my anger") and, when turned away, **restored relationship** (Psa 85:3–4).

**T3.11.3** — The covenant-bound pattern shows divine wrath is constitutively **relational** — the wounded response of the covenant Lord, not impersonal force.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1** — God-toward-human: this is the defining operation of C2 — God's wrath moves from God toward the human person/nation (Exo 22:24; Num 11:1; Deu 6:15).

**T4.1.2** — Basis: **covenantal and responsive** — extended in response to covenant violation (Deu 6:15; 9:19; 1Ki 11:9), not arbitrary; conditional on sin and relentable on repentance (Jon 3:9–10; 2Ch 12:7).

**T4.1.3** — What it shows of God's disposition: God is **holy and committed** to the covenant — His wrath is the measure of how seriously He takes covenant betrayal, and His readiness to turn it away (Psa 85:3) shows mercy as its horizon.

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God movement of *this* characteristic: not applicable — wrath here is God's, not the person's toward God. The human movement in view is **repentance/intercession that turns wrath away** (Num 25:11; 2Ch 29:10). Record none for the characteristic operating *from* human to God.

**T4.2.2** — The posture that meets divine wrath is **humbling, repentance, intercession** (2Ch 12:6–7 "they humbled themselves"; Num 16:47–48 atonement).

**T4.2.3** — That wrath is **turnable** by human repentance shows the relationship is genuinely responsive — God relents toward the penitent (Jon 3:10).

**T4.3.1** — Person-to-person extension: not proper to C2 (wrath is God's). **Silent** (human-human anger is C1).

**T4.3.2** — **Silent.**

**T4.3.3** — **Silent.**

**T4.4.1** — Receiving: the human/nation **receives** God's wrath as judgment (Joh 3:36 "the wrath of God remains on him"; Rom 5:9 "saved... from the wrath"). Uptake is endured judgment, not adoption.

**T4.4.2** — What accompanies receiving it: **unrepentance** keeps it remaining (Joh 3:36); **faith/repentance** removes it (Rom 5:9 "saved... through him").

**T4.4.3** — The person who stands under wrath without repentance is shown as **condemned/abiding under it** (Joh 3:36).

**T4.5.1** — Within bonds vs across distance: divine wrath operates chiefly **within the covenant bond** (against His own people, Deu 6:15) and also **across** it against the nations (Isa 34:2; Jer 10:25 context). Both.

**T4.5.2** — It operates within the **covenant** (its primary frame, Deu 29:24–28) and **crosses** to the nations in judgment (Isa 34:2). Not covenant-internal only.

**T4.5.3** — Relational scope: **universal** — His covenant people (Deu 9:19), the nations (Isa 34:2), individuals (1Ki 11:9). None is exempt as a potential object.

**T4.6.1** — In relation to spiritual beings: thin — wrath is poured out in contexts including the heavenly host's judgment (Isa 34:2 "all the host of heaven shall rot"; object spiritual-being Eze 3:14 context). Mostly **silent** on operation toward angelic/adversarial beings directly.

**T4.6.2** — Site of adversarial activity: not shown — God's wrath is not depicted as attacked or distorted by adversarial powers. **Silent** (recorded as not evidenced).

**T4.6.3** — Angelic mediation: God's wrath is at times **executed through** agents (the plague/"destroyer" tradition, e.g. Num 16:46–48 atonement halts it; the sword, Deu 32:24). Where a mediating agent of judgment appears it is the instrument of wrath. Recorded; otherwise none.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1** — Transformation: God's wrath effects transformation of **condition** — it lays waste (Deu 29:28; Lam 4:11) and, when turned away, **restores** (Psa 85:3–4). On the person it can change orientation by driving repentance (2Ch 30:8).

**T5.1.2** — Reversible/irreversible: shown **reversible** — repeatedly "turned away" / "quenched" (Jos 7:26; 2Ki 22:17; Jon 3:10); but where judgment falls it is **executed** and the desolation stands (Deu 29:28). Both, depending on response.

**T5.2.1** — Sequence: **sin → kindling of wrath → judgment (or repentance → turning away)** (Deu 9:8–19 the calf → wrath → intercession → relenting; Jon 3 sin → threatened wrath → repentance → relenting). Where no sequence appears, record none (Exo 15:7 poetic).

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism of change: **judgment/discipline** (the wrath itself as corrective, Lev 26:28 context; 2Ki 22:13) and **intercession/atonement** as the mechanism that turns it (Num 16:46–48; Psa 106:23). Record none where unstated.

**T5.3.2** — Differs: wrath works change by **destruction** on the unrepentant (Isa 34:2) and by **threat-driving-repentance** on the responsive (2Ch 30:8; Jon 3). Context-dependent.

**T5.4.1** — Relation to suffering/affliction: divine wrath **produces** affliction as judgment (Lam 2:4 "poured out his fury like fire"; Psa 88:7 "your wrath lies heavy on me"; Psa 38:1–3). It is a cause of suffering for its objects.

**T5.4.2** — What suffering does to it: the suffering of judgment is the **outworking** of wrath; where the afflicted repent/cry out, the wrath is **relented** (Psa 85; 2Ch 12:7). Suffering under wrath is meant to turn the sufferer back.

**T5.5.1** — Participation in the sanctification arc: God's wrath functions as **covenant discipline** shaping the people over time — chastening (Lev 26:28 "discipline you... for your sins"; Isa 57:17 "I was angry... I struck him"). It is part of the formative dealing of God with His people.

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological orientation: strongly yes — divine wrath is oriented to **"the wrath to come" / the day of judgment** (Rom 9:22 "vessels of wrath"; Rev 14:10; 16:19; 19:15). Its present operation anticipates the final judgment.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1** — Co-occurring characteristics: **jealousy** (qinˈah with chaˈron, Num 25:11; Deu 29:20; Zep 1:18 "fire of his jealousy"), **fury/indignation** (cheˈmah with zaˈam, Isa 34:2 context), and **fire** imagery throughout (Jer 4:4; Deu 32:22). Frequent pairing with jealousy especially.

**T6.1.2** — Co-occurrence places divine wrath at the **judicial-covenantal core** of the cluster — inseparable from God's jealousy (C4) over the covenant and expressed as consuming fire.

**T6.2.1** — Sequence: divine wrath consistently **follows** covenant sin/idolatry and **precedes** judgment (Deu 9:16–19; 1Ki 11:9–11). It also follows divine **jealousy** being provoked (Deu 32:16,21 "provoked him to jealousy... to anger").

**T6.2.2** — The relationship is **causal**: sin/idolatry causes wrath; provoked jealousy causes wrath (Deu 32:21). Wrath causes judgment.

**T6.3.1** — Produces: divine wrath **produces** judgment, desolation, exile (Deu 29:28; Lam 4:11) and, by reaction, repentance (2Ch 30:8). Mechanism: executed or threatened judgment.

**T6.3.2** — Produced by: **divine jealousy provoked** (C4/C3; Deu 32:16,21; Psa 78:58 "provoked him... moved him to jealousy... full of wrath," Psa 78:58–59) and by **human provocation** (C3; 1Ki 14:9; 2Ki 21:6).

**T6.3.3** — Constituent relation: divine wrath is a constituent of God's **covenant-jealousy** response (the "fire of jealousy" *is* wrathful, Zep 1:18; 3:8) — jealousy and wrath interpenetrate at the divine end.

**T6.4.1** — Shared vocabulary: cheˈmah/chaˈrah/qeˈtseph/orgē shared with **C1 human anger** (same lemmas, different bearer) and with **C3 provoking-to-anger** (the wrath that provocation arouses).

**T6.4.2** — Root-level: yes — the chaˈrah/chaˈron burning-root spans C1, C2, C3; one heat-architecture underlies human anger, divine wrath, and provoked anger.

**T6.4.3** — The shared root shows C1/C2/C3 are **one affect under different relations** — divine wrath is distinguished not by a separate vocabulary but by its **bearer (God) and its unfailing righteousness**.

**T6.5.1** — Nearest neighbour: **C1 human anger** (identical core vocabulary). The precise distinction: **bearer and moral standing** — God's wrath is righteous by nature and covenant-bound; human anger is morally variable (C1, T3.8.3).

**T6.5.2** — Apparent overlap: the leaked verses (Exo 4:14; 2Ki 13:3, held in C1) sit exactly on the boundary; the deciding criterion is **whether God is the angry subject** (→C2). The other near-neighbour is **C4 divine jealousy**, which co-occurs and overlaps in the "fire of jealousy/wrath" (Zep 1:18) — distinguished by jealousy's relational-possessive note vs wrath's judicial note.

**T6.5.3** — Distinction from C1 is of **bearer and moral valence** (not kind or vocabulary); from C3 of **direction** (bearing wrath vs being provoked to it); from C4 of **register** (judicial wrath vs possessive jealousy), though they fuse at the divine end.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms/roots: **cheˈmah** (heat/fury), **chaˈron** (burning, the term most distinctively used of God's anger, from chaˈrah "to burn"), **chaˈrah** (to be kindled), **qeˈtseph** (wrath/overflow), **orgē** (NT). Roots converge on **burning heat** and **overflow**.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: **noun/status** dominant (103; "the wrath of the LORD," Num 32:14) with **verb/action** (42; "was kindled," Gen 18:30). The noun-heavy range shows divine wrath named as an **abiding attribute/state**, not only an episode.

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: from "wrath/anger" (Exo 32:10) through "fierce/burning" (chaˈron, Exo 15:7) to "fury/heat" (cheˈmah, Jer 4:4) and "venom" once (Deu 32:33 context near divine judgment). Breadth: kindled heat → settled fury → consuming fire.

**T7.1.4** — Distinguishing terms: **condition vs act** — cheˈmah/qeˈtseph (state) vs chaˈrah (act of kindling); chaˈron specifically marks **intensity** ("fierce/burning"). Received-vs-given not lexicalised (it is always God's own).

**T7.1.5** — Opposite/absence term: carried by the **turning-away** idiom and by **mercy/favour** vocabulary (Psa 85:3 "you withdrew all your wrath"; "slow to anger," erek aphayim, Exo 34:6). Not a single antonym lexeme.
