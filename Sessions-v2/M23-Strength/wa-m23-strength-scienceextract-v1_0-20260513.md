## Purpose

This document is the cluster science review for M23. It is produced as a pre-analytical asset for use during Phase 8 of Session B when T7.3 prompts are reached. This is the programme's largest cluster (105 terms) and spans a wide range from physical physiology to political psychology. A key analytical note: most strength vocabulary in biblical usage functions metaphorically rather than literally — the science of physical strength has limited direct application, while the science of psychological and social power is more directly relevant.

## Science sections

### Section 1 — Physical strength and physiological capacity

**Primary field:** Physiology / exercise science

**Glosses covered:**
strength (ischus), strength (ul), strength (on), strength (e.yal), strength (e.ya.lut), strength (o.mets), strength (am.tsah), strength (do.ve), strength (che.zeq), strength (cho.zeq), strength (chez.qah), strength (cha.yil), strength (ko.ach), strength (e.zuz), strength (oz), strength (o.tsem), strength (ots.mah), be strong (ischuō), be strong (a.zaz), be vast (a.tsom), mighty (ul), mighty (cha.sin), mighty (a.tsum), strong (ischuros), strong (stereos), strong (e.tan), strong (am.mits), strong (az), stronger (cha.zeq), to strengthen (endunamoō), to strengthen (sthenoō), to strengthen (stereoō), to strengthen (a.mets), to strengthen (cha.zaq — all senses)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Exercise physiology addresses physical strength as muscular capacity — the ability to exert force against resistance. The psychology of physical strength is addressed through self-efficacy research (Bandura — the belief in one's capacity to perform physical tasks) and through the psychology of physical toughness. However, the primary analytical note is that most of these glosses function metaphorically in their biblical context — strength vocabulary is applied to God, to character, to courage, and to emotional resilience far more than to literal muscular capacity. The physiology is the source domain of the metaphor, not its primary referent.

**Emergence — what causes a sense of strength and physical capacity to arise:**
Physical strength increases through exercise, adequate nutrition, rest, and developmental maturation. The psychological sense of strength (feeling strong, empowered) is produced by successful performance experiences, social encouragement, and the observation of models (Bandura's four sources of self-efficacy). The strengthening terms in this section (endunamoō, cha.zaq) are frequently relational — strength that comes from outside the individual, through encouragement or divine empowerment.

**Reduction and absence — what causes strength to diminish:**
Physical strength diminishes through illness, injury, age, fatigue, deprivation, and sustained high output without recovery. The psychological sense of strength diminishes through failure experiences, social discouragement, and the withdrawal of relational support. Fear, grief, and shame are among the most powerful psychological strength-reducers — they suppress the motivation to act even when physical capacity remains.

**Location — where physical strength is seated:**
The primary physiological location is musculoskeletal — physical strength resides in muscles, tendons, bones, and the neuromuscular system. The psychological sense of strength is more diffuse: it involves the whole person's self-assessment of capacity. The biblical cluster's vocabulary spans both — some terms name literal physical capacity, most name an inner quality of being capable, resolute, and effective that transcends literal muscularity.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Physical strength is shaped by nutrition, health, and physical activity across development. *Social:* The psychological sense of strength is heavily shaped by relational support — 'strengthening one another' (cha.zaq applied relationally) is a consistent biblical pattern with direct psychological parallels in social support research. *Hereditary:* Physical capacity has significant genetic components (muscle fibre type, baseline hormonal levels). Cultural contexts shape what counts as strength and who is expected to demonstrate it.

**Coverage assessment:** Limited for direct physical application; moderate for psychological application. Most value is in how the physical source domain maps metaphorically onto the cluster's wider content.

### Section 2 — Power, authority, and social dominance

**Primary field:** Social psychology / evolutionary psychology — dominance research

**Glosses covered:**
authority (exousia), authority (huperochē), dominion (mim.shal), dominion (mem.sha.lah), dominion (mo.shel), dominion (mis.rah), dominion (shol.tan), rule (mish.tar), ruling (shal.lit), be higher (huperechō), to have authority (exousiazō), to lord over (kurieuō), to master (katakurieuō), to domineer (authenteō), to domineer (sha.lat), to rule (katexousiazō), to rule (ma.shal), to rule (ra.dah), to rule (she.let), lord: master (kurios), lordship (kuriotēs)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Keltner, Gruenfeld and Anderson's approach-inhibition theory of power (*Psychological Review*, 2003) proposes that high power produces an approach-oriented psychological state (action, optimism, attention to rewards) while low power produces an inhibition-oriented state (caution, attention to threats). French and Raven's five bases of social power (1959) — reward, coercive, legitimate, referent, expert — remain foundational. Milgram's obedience research demonstrated the extraordinary extent to which legitimate authority shapes behaviour. Evolutionary psychology addresses dominance hierarchies as a cross-species phenomenon with both social and neurobiological roots.

**Emergence — what causes power and authority to arise:**
Power arises through social recognition of capacity, status, or legitimacy. Legitimate authority arises through formal structures (appointment, election, inheritance) or through earned reputation for competence and reliability. Dominance in evolutionary terms arises through successful competition for resources and mates. The biblical terms span legitimate authority (exousia — delegated authority) and raw dominance (ra.dah — to tread down) — a distinction that the science of power also addresses.

**Reduction and absence — what causes power to diminish:**
Power diminishes through the withdrawal of recognition, through failure of the competence or reliability that supports it, through legitimate challenge, or through forcible displacement. The corrupting effect of power (Keltner's 'power paradox' — power reduces the empathy and social attunement that produced it) is among the more counterintuitive findings.

**Location — where power is seated:**
Power is relational — it does not exist in an individual alone but in the relationship between an authority and those who recognise it. Neurologically, power experience activates reward circuitry and suppresses the social cognition systems associated with perspective-taking. Psychologically, power is located in the self's capacity to act on the world without being constrained by others' responses.

**External influences:**
*Social structures:* Authority structures are institutionally and culturally determined — what counts as legitimate power varies enormously. *Developmental:* Early experiences of efficacy and social recognition shape adult power orientation. *Hereditary:* Dominance-related traits (assertiveness, physical size, testosterone-linked behaviour) have heritable components.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Social psychology of power is well-developed and directly relevant to this section.

### Section 3 — Kingdom, governance, and political power

**Primary field:** Political psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
kingdom (basileia), kingdom (mam.la.khut), kingly (basileios), palace (basileion), queen (basilissa), royal (basilikos), hosts (sabaōth), greater (kreissōn)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Political psychology addresses the psychology of governance, leadership, and political authority. The sociology of the state addresses kingship as a social institution. Leadership research (Bass — transformational vs transactional leadership; Burns) is relevant to the governance dimension. Hosts (sabaōth) in its military dimension has coverage in the social psychology of military organisations and collective power.

**Emergence — what causes royal/political power to arise:**
Political authority arises through a combination of legitimation (the recognised right to rule), force (the capacity to compel compliance), and consent (the willingness of subjects to recognise and follow). The institution of kingship is a cultural universal in complex societies.

**Location — where political power is seated:**
Primarily social and institutional — political power is a property of social structures and relationships, not of individuals in isolation. It is enacted through institutions, symbols, rituals, and the ongoing consent of subjects.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* The form, legitimation, and limits of political power are entirely culturally and historically shaped. *Circumstances:* War, economic disruption, and social crisis are the primary conditions under which existing power structures are challenged or collapse.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin for direct psychology; the political science dimension is broader than the psychological. Outside-science content is proportionally large for this sub-group.

### Section 4 — Energy, active power, and efficacy

**Primary field:** Motivational psychology / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
active energy (energeia), be active (energeō), effective (energēs enargēs), working (energēma), power (dunamis), power (kratos), power (ma.a.mats), power (shil.ton), power (ta.a.tsu.mah), power (to.qeph), might (ge.vu.rah), might (te.qaph), force (choz.qah), growing (ga.del), to empower (dunamoō), to enlarge (ra.chav), to grow great (re.vah), to grow strong (te.qeph), to prevail (katischuō), to prevail (ga.var), to prevail (ta.qeph)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Bandura's self-efficacy theory (*Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control*, 1997) is the primary framework — the belief in one's capacity to perform specific tasks is among the strongest predictors of actual performance and persistence. Ryan and Deci's self-determination theory (*Psychological Review*, 2000) addresses psychological energy (vitality) as produced by environments that support autonomy, competence, and relatedness. The energeia vocabulary in this section maps onto these motivational constructs — active, effective working as a psychological as well as physical state.

**Emergence — what causes active energy and efficacy to arise:**
Psychological energy and efficacy arise through successful performance experiences, through encouragement from credible others, through physiological arousal, and through positive emotional states. Empowerment (dunamoō) is relational — it arises through another's action on the individual, producing increased capacity. This is structurally significant for the programme: the strengthening and empowering terms in this cluster are often explicitly relational or divine — capacity that comes from outside the self.

**Reduction and absence — what causes energy and efficacy to diminish:**
Failure experiences, social discouragement, physiological depletion, and the withdrawal of relational support all reduce efficacy and energy. Ego depletion (Baumeister — decision fatigue and self-control depletion) reduces the capacity for effective action even when no physical constraint exists.

**Location — where energy and efficacy are seated:**
Primarily motivational and cognitive, with physiological expressions. Efficacy is a belief state (cognitive); energy is experienced as a felt capacity (emotional and physiological). The energeia terms describe effective working as an observable outcome, not merely a subjective feeling — active energy that produces results in the world.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Encouragement, modelling, and social support are primary producers of efficacy. *Cultural:* The domains in which efficacy is valued and cultivated vary by culture. *Developmental:* Early mastery experiences are foundational to adult efficacy beliefs.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Self-efficacy and motivational psychology provide direct coverage for this section's vocabulary.

### Section 5 — Subjection, coercion, and the exercise of force

**Primary field:** Social psychology — coercion and compliance

**Glosses covered:**
to subject (hupotassō), to compel (anankazō), to confine (tsur), to conquer (katagōnizomai), to hold/oppress (sunechō), to bear/lead (ferō), to govern (hēgeomai), almighty (pantokratōr)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Cialdini's compliance research (*Influence*, 1984) addresses the mechanisms by which people are moved to comply with requests or demands. Seligman's learned helplessness research addresses the psychological consequences of prolonged coercive control. Governance psychology (leadership and followership research) addresses the relational dynamics of leading and being led.

**Emergence — what causes coercive force to arise:**
Coercion arises when the controlling party possesses greater power and is willing to use it to compel compliance regardless of the subject's preference. It is the exercise of power without the consent dimension that distinguishes authority (Section 2) from coercion.

**Location — where this characteristic is seated:**
Like Section 3 in M01, the glosses in this section primarily describe relationships and actions rather than inner states. The inner experience of the coerced — constraint, confinement, subjection — is in the same physical-somatic and emotional domain as M01's coercion vocabulary.

**External influences:**
*Social and political:* The conditions that enable coercive force are structural — war, occupation, slavery, and oppressive governance create the contexts in which these terms are deployed.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. This section's content is primarily descriptive of relational power; science coverage is partial.

## Outside-science inventory

- **almighty (pantokratōr)** — divine omnipotence as a theological category is outside science entirely.
- **dominion in its creation-mandate register** — humanity's delegated dominion over creation (Gen 1:26–28) is a theological category of vocation and responsibility.
- **hosts (sabaōth)** — the LORD of armies as divine title, commanding heavenly hosts, is a theological category.
- **kingdom (basileia)** in its eschatological register — the kingdom of God as a theological reality that transforms and transcends ordinary political structures.
- **power (dunamis)** in its Pauline register — the power of God in the gospel and in the resurrection — is a theological category.

**Observation:** This cluster spans the widest scientific range of any cluster to date — from exercise physiology to political psychology. The outside-science content is concentrated at the divine-attribute end. The analytical challenge for Session B will be distinguishing the cluster's inner-being content from its extensive political and social content — the programme's interest is in how strength, power, and efficacy are experienced and located within the human inner being, not in the political structures they describe.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology of power (Sections 2 and 3); motivational psychology and self-efficacy (Section 4); physiology provides the source domain for the metaphorical vocabulary (Section 1).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Self-efficacy research illuminates the experiential dimension of the cluster's empowerment vocabulary — strengthening and empowering terms describe a change in perceived capacity, not merely literal physical change. The social and relational character of empowerment in this section (dunamoō — empowered by another) maps onto what Bandura identifies as vicarious experience and social encouragement as efficacy sources. The power paradox (power reduces empathy) may illuminate why the cluster's biblical material so frequently contrasts divine power with humble service.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats power as primarily horizontal — a property of social relationships among humans. Scripture introduces a vertical dimension: divine power that is absolute, that created and sustains, and that is ontologically different in kind from human power. The cluster's most theologically significant content (almighty, hosts, kingdom) cannot be reached by science's horizontal account of power.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The distinction between power over (coercive dominion) and power with (empowering strengthening) may surface as a significant structural distinction in Session B — science identifies these as different psychological phenomena (approach vs facilitative power), and the biblical vocabulary may show the same distinction.
