## Science sections

### Section 1 — Work, labour, and occupational psychology

**Primary field:** Organisational psychology / occupational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
service (a.vo.dah), service (a.vud.dah), service: work (a.vo.dah), to make (a.vad), to serve (a.vad), to serve: labour (a.vad)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Hackman and Oldham's job characteristics model (*Organisational Behaviour and Human Performance*, 1976) identifies five core job characteristics that produce meaningful work: skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback. Work that scores high on these dimensions produces motivation, satisfaction, and performance. Steger's work meaning research (*Journal of Vocational Behaviour*, 2012) distinguishes work that is meaningful (contributing to something larger than oneself) from work that is merely pleasant or well-paid. The a.vo.dah root spans both worship (service rendered to God) and labour (work rendered in the world) — the dual meaning is theologically significant.

**Emergence — what causes the experience of meaningful work to arise:**
Meaningful work arises when the work connects to the person's values and identity, produces tangible positive outcomes for others, requires genuine skill, and is embedded in a relational context of accountability and recognition. The theological dimension of a.vo.dah — service as worship — suggests that the source of meaning for the biblical concept of work exceeds the occupational psychology account: work is meaningful not merely because of its human consequences but because it is service rendered to God.

**Reduction and absence — what causes work to become meaningless:**
Work becomes meaningless through disconnection from outcomes (bureaucratic fragmentation), through the suppression of skill and autonomy (deskilling), through the loss of a sense of contribution, and through the absence of relational context. Alienated labour (Marx) — work experienced as external constraint rather than self-expression — is the negative pole of meaningful work.

**Location — where the experience of meaningful work is seated:**
Primarily cognitive and motivational — meaning is located in the person's interpretation of their work's significance. It has an emotional dimension (engagement, satisfaction, or conversely boredom and alienation) and a relational dimension (embedded in a community of purpose).

**External influences:**
*Organisational:* Job design, management quality, and organisational culture are the primary determinants of whether work is experienced as meaningful. *Cultural:* The theological grounding of work as vocation (Luther's doctrine of vocation) is a cultural formation that shapes the experience of work. *Developmental:* The formation of a sense of vocation and calling (M37) shapes what kinds of work are experienced as meaningful.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Occupational psychology provides coverage; the worship dimension of a.vo.dah is outside science.

### Section 2 — Slavery, coercion, and powerlessness

**Primary field:** Social psychology / trauma psychology

**Glosses covered:**
slave (doulos), slave (oiketēs), slavery (douleia), slavery (av.dut), female slave (doulē), be a slave (douleuō), to enslave (doulagōgeō), to enslave (douloō), to enslave (katadouloō), to serve: burden (a.vad)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Van der Kolk's trauma research addresses the psychological effects of captivity and coercive control. Seligman's learned helplessness framework applies directly to slavery contexts — exposure to inescapable control produces passive, helpless, and cognitively impaired states. Patterson's coercive control research and Herman's analysis of complex trauma (*Trauma and Recovery*, 1992) address the long-term psychological effects of sustained captivity and powerlessness. The psychological effects of slavery include: identity disruption, chronic trauma responses, learned helplessness, and the internalisation of the enslaver's view of the enslaved person.

**Emergence — what causes the enslaved state to arise:**
Slavery as a social institution arises from the exercise of coercive power — one party using force, threat, or structural advantage to compel another's service without consent. The psychological experience of being enslaved arises from the combination of powerlessness, identity assault, and the ongoing threat of harm. Paul's use of doulos as a chosen identity ('bondservant of Christ Jesus') is a deliberate appropriation of slavery language into a theological context of chosen, honoured service — the opposite of coerced bondage.

**Reduction and absence — what causes the enslaved state to diminish:**
Liberation from coercive slavery requires the removal of the coercive power. Psychological recovery from slavery trauma follows the same trajectory as complex trauma recovery: safety, reconnection, integration. The Pauline 'slavery to Christ' is paradoxically described as the only genuine freedom — slavery to sin as the alternative slavery that produces death.

**Location — where this is seated:**
Coercive slavery produces effects across all inner-being dimensions — emotional (chronic fear, shame, grief), cognitive (restricted agency, disrupted self-concept), relational (damaged trust, disrupted attachment), and somatic (chronic stress physiology). It is the most comprehensive inner-being assault in the programme's vocabulary.

**External influences:**
*Social and structural:* Slavery is entirely a social institution — it arises from structural power arrangements, not from any individual characteristic of the enslaved. *Historical:* The biblical world was pervasively shaped by slavery as a social reality; the cluster's vocabulary must be read in that context.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Trauma psychology and social psychology provide relevant coverage. The theological reappropriation of slavery language (Paul as doulos of Christ) is outside science.

### Section 3 — Service, ministry, and prosocial motivation

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
service: ministry (a.vo.dah), to serve: minister (a.vad)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Servant leadership research (Greenleaf, *Servant Leadership*, 1977) addresses the psychology of leading through service rather than authority — the leader who places the needs of those led above their own. Prosocial motivation research (Grant — *Give and Take*, 2013) demonstrates that other-oriented motivation — wanting to benefit others — is among the strongest predictors of sustained high performance in helping roles. Volunteer motivation research (Penner) addresses the psychological drivers of sustained service without coercion or payment.

**Emergence — what causes prosocial service motivation to arise:**
Other-oriented service arises from genuine care for others' wellbeing, from the identity integration of 'helper' or 'servant' as central to one's self-concept, and from the relational satisfaction derived from effective service. Prosocial motivation is sustained when service is freely chosen (as opposed to coerced), when outcomes are visible, and when the server is embedded in a community that values service.

**Location — where prosocial service motivation is seated:**
Primarily relational and motivational. Other-oriented service is inherently other-directed — it is seated in the person's orientation toward others' wellbeing rather than in any single inner faculty.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Servant leadership and prosocial motivation research provide partial coverage.

## Outside-science inventory

- **slavery to God as a chosen theological identity** — the Pauline use of doulos as a voluntary and honoured status is a theological category. Science addresses slavery as coercive; chosen, honoured service-identity is a theological reappropriation.
- **service as worship (latreia) in its cultic register** — liturgical service rendered to God is a theological category.
- **a.vo.dah as simultaneously worship and work** — the theological collapse of the sacred/secular work distinction is outside science's scope.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Occupational psychology (Section 1); trauma psychology (Section 2); prosocial motivation research (Section 3).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Meaningful work research illuminates why the cluster's service vocabulary consistently carries positive valence in its voluntary registers — service that is chosen and connected to larger purpose produces the exact conditions that occupational psychology associates with meaning. Trauma psychology illuminates the depth of the enslaved condition's inner-being effects.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The theological collapse of the distinction between worship and work (a.vo.dah spanning both) has no science parallel. Science treats work and worship as categorically distinct; the cluster treats them as structurally related forms of service rendered to the same Lord.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The distinction between voluntary service (chosen doulos) and coerced service (enslaved doulos) will be a critical Session B classification question that the verse evidence must resolve — the same word family covers both, but the inner-being registers are opposite.
