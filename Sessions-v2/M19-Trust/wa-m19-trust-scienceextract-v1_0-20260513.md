## Science sections

### Section 1 — Trust and interpersonal confidence

**Primary field:** Social psychology — trust research

**Glosses covered:**
confidence (pepoithēsis), confidence (kis.lah), confidence (miv.tach), firm (bebaios), firmness (stereōma), to trust (a.man), to trust (ba.tach), to trust (re.chats), trust (bit.chah), trust (bit.ta.chon), be convinced (pistoō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman's integrative model of trust (*An Integrative Model of Organizational Trust*, 1995) defines trust as the willingness to be vulnerable to the actions of another party based on the expectation that the other will perform a particular action important to the trustor. The three antecedents of perceived trustworthiness are: ability (the other has the competence to deliver), benevolence (the other cares about the trustor's wellbeing), and integrity (the other adheres to acceptable principles). Rotter's interpersonal trust scale (1967) established that dispositional trust is a stable individual difference. The ba.tach vocabulary (to trust, to lean upon, to feel secure) suggests trust as a posture of the whole person — not merely a cognitive judgment but a leaning of the self upon the trusted other.

**Emergence — what causes trust to arise:**
Trust arises from: positive prior experience with the trustee; perceived alignment between trustor and trustee values and interests; the demonstrated competence of the trustee; and — particularly in high-stakes contexts — the experience of having been genuinely cared for by the trustee. The leaning/resting vocabulary (ba.tach — to trust, to lean; sha.an — to lean) suggests that trust, once established, involves a whole-body orientation of weight-bearing on another.

**Reduction and absence — what causes trust to diminish:**
Trust is damaged by: betrayal (violation of trust-based expectations); demonstrated incompetence or unreliability; the perception of misalignment between trustor and trustee interests; and the absence of adequate knowledge of the trustee. The recovery of trust after betrayal is well-studied (Kim and colleagues — trust repair) and consistently shows that the process is long, requires sustained reliability, and may never fully restore to pre-betrayal levels. Dispositionally low trust (low Rotter trust) is associated with insecure attachment history.

**Location — where trust is seated:**
Both cognitive (the judgment that the trustee is reliable, competent, and benevolent) and emotional (the felt sense of security that accompanies genuine trust). The ba.tach vocabulary suggests it is also postural — embodied in the way one holds oneself in relation to the trusted other. Genuine trust produces relaxation; its absence produces vigilance and tension.

**External influences:**
*Attachment history:* Secure early attachment produces dispositional trust; insecure and disorganised attachment produces dispositional mistrust. This is one of the most robust findings in developmental psychology. *Cultural:* Generalised social trust (trust of strangers and institutions) varies substantially across cultures — Scandinavian cultures show very high generalised trust; other cultures show low generalised trust. *Prior experience:* Specific trust is shaped by the specific track record of the trustee. *Circumstances:* Institutional transparency, accountability structures, and track records of reliability build institutional trust.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich. Trust research is one of the most developed areas in social and organisational psychology.

### Section 2 — Self-regulation, self-control, and discipline

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / developmental psychology

**Glosses covered:**
self-control (enkrateia), self-controlled (enkratēs), self-controlled (sōfrōn), self-discipline (sōfronismos), to self-control (enkrateuomai), sober (nēfaleos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Self-regulation research (Baumeister and Vohs, *Handbook of Self-Regulation*, 2004) identifies self-control as among the most important personality variables for life outcomes — education, work, relationships, health, and criminal behaviour are all significantly predicted by self-control. The ego depletion model (Baumeister — self-control as a limited resource that is depleted by use) has been partially revised by later research suggesting that beliefs about self-control matter as much as its actual resource character. Mischel's delay of gratification research demonstrated the long-term predictive power of self-control measured in early childhood.

**Emergence — what causes self-control to arise:**
Self-control develops through: practice (like a muscle — Muraven and Baumeister); the internalisation of values that make self-control intrinsically motivated rather than merely externally required; the support of accountability relationships; and adequate sleep and positive affect (both of which replenish self-regulatory capacity). Soberness (nēfaleos — calm, clear-headed) suggests a stable mental composure that supports self-regulation.

**Reduction and absence — what causes self-control to diminish:**
Self-control is reduced by: ego depletion (the depletion model suggests that prior acts of self-control reduce capacity for subsequent acts); sleep deprivation; emotional arousal; substance use; and the absence of values that make restraint worth the cost. Absence of self-control (impulsivity) is associated with a wide range of negative outcomes and is a core feature of several personality and clinical conditions.

**Location — where self-control is seated:**
Primarily in the prefrontal cortex and its regulatory relationships with subcortical motivation systems. Self-control is the capacity to override automatic, dominant response tendencies in favour of controlled, valued alternatives. It is characterological in its developed form — not merely a situational effort but a stable capacity built through repeated practice.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Self-control develops substantially through childhood and adolescence with the maturation of the prefrontal cortex, reaching adult levels only in the mid-20s. Early parenting that requires and supports self-regulation (age-appropriate demands, consistent consequences) builds the capacity. *Hereditary:* Self-control capacity has significant heritability. *Cultural:* Cultures that value and expect self-regulation (industriousness, sobriety, discipline) cultivate it more effectively.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich. Self-regulation is one of the most extensively researched psychological capacities.

### Section 3 — Security, refuge, and felt safety

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / attachment theory

**Glosses covered:**
refuge (cha.sut), refuge (mach.s.seh), secure (sha.a.nan), security (be.tach), security (bat.tu.chot), security (ma.oz), support (mish.an), support (mish.en), to lean (sha.an), to seek refuge (cha.sah), to lift: trust (na.sa)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Maslow's hierarchy of needs places safety as the second level — immediately above physiological survival — establishing security as a fundamental human need whose satisfaction is prerequisite to higher functioning. Attachment theory (Bowlby — the secure base and safe haven functions of attachment) provides the most developed account of felt security: the attachment figure functions both as a safe haven (providing comfort under threat) and as a secure base (providing the confidence from which exploration can occur). Psychological safety research (Edmondson — team psychological safety; Brown — safe vulnerability) examines the conditions under which people feel safe enough to take interpersonal risks.

**Emergence — what causes felt security to arise:**
Felt security arises from: the consistent availability of a trusted, responsive attachment figure (or institution); the demonstrated reliability and power of the protection source; and the internalisation of a secure base that persists when the physical attachment figure is absent. The refuge vocabulary (mach.s.seh — shelter, covering; ma.oz — stronghold, fortress) suggests that security is provided by something stronger than the person — a protection they take shelter within rather than generate themselves.

**Reduction and absence — what causes security to diminish:**
Security is undermined by: the unavailability or unreliability of the protection source; perceived threat that exceeds the capacity of the protection source; and the erosion of the internalised secure base through trauma or sustained threat. The cluster's vocabulary for seeking refuge (cha.sah — to take shelter under the wing) suggests that security-seeking is an active movement toward the protection source, not merely a passive resting.

**Location — where security is seated:**
Emotional and neurological — felt security is associated with parasympathetic nervous system dominance (Porges' polyvagal theory — the social engagement system that operates when safety is signalled). It is experienced as a felt state of calm, settledness, and openness. The bodily metaphor of leaning (sha.an) and taking shelter (cha.sah) suggests that security has a somatic quality — the physical release of tension that accompanies genuine trust.

**External influences:**
*Attachment history:* Secure early attachment is the primary developmental source of felt security. *Cultural:* Social structures that provide institutional safety (rule of law, predictable government, functioning healthcare) provide generalised security. *Circumstances:* Genuine safety from physical threat is a prerequisite for the psychological security that enables flourishing.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich. Attachment research and psychological safety research provide extensive frameworks.

### Section 4 — Diligence, eagerness, and motivational engagement

**Primary field:** Motivational psychology / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be careful (frontizō), be eager (zēloō), be eager (spoudazō), be fervent (zeō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Flow research (Csikszentmihalyi, *Flow*, 1990) identifies the state of optimal engagement — when challenge and skill are well matched, producing absorbed, intrinsically motivated activity. Zeal and eagerness are high-arousal positive motivational states that drive sustained engagement. Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan) addresses the intrinsic motivation that underlies genuine eagerness — the orientation toward activity for its own sake or for deeply held values, rather than for external reward.

**Emergence — what causes eagerness and diligence to arise:**
Eagerness arises from: genuine valuing of the goal or activity; the experience of competence and progress; and the alignment between the activity and one's core values and identity. Zeal (zeō — to boil, to bubble) names the high-arousal end of motivational engagement — not merely steady diligence but intense, wholehearted pursuit.

**Reduction and absence:** Eagerness diminishes with: burnout (sustained high engagement without adequate recovery); goal abandonment; loss of meaning; and the absence of progress signals. Diligence as steady engagement requires sustained values-alignment even when the arousal of eagerness has subsided.

**Location:** Primarily motivational and affective — eagerness is a high-arousal positive state that energises action. Diligence is the more stable, lower-arousal character disposition of sustained effort.

**External influences:** Meaningful goals that align with core values are the primary driver. Community that models and reinforces sustained engagement sustains it in the individual.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Flow and motivation research provide frameworks; the gloss count is small.

## Outside-science inventory

- **refuge (mach.s.seh) and security (ma.oz) in their theological register** — where these terms name God as the refuge and stronghold of his people (Psalm 46; Psalm 91), they describe a specific theological relationship — the person who shelters in the living God. Science addresses the psychology of felt security; it does not address God as the actual source of that security. The theological claim is not that trust in God produces the psychological benefits of felt security (though it does), but that God is genuinely trustworthy and genuinely protective — a reality that transcends the psychology.

**Observation:** M19 has focused outside-science content at the theological edge. The human psychology of trust, self-control, security, and diligence are well covered. The theological distinctive is the identity of the One trusted — trust in God is not trust in an uncertain other but in the One who has the ability (omnipotence), benevolence (covenant love), and integrity (faithfulness) to be fully trusted.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology (trust — Section 1); clinical and developmental psychology (self-control — Section 2); attachment theory (security — Section 3); motivational psychology (Section 4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The three-component trustworthiness model (ability, benevolence, integrity) illuminates the biblical portrait of God as fully trustworthy — he has the ability (omnipotence), the benevolence (covenant love), and the integrity (faithfulness) that the model identifies as the preconditions for warranted trust. Attachment theory illuminates the ba.tach and sha.an vocabulary — trust as leaning, as taking the weight of oneself on another, as settling into the security of the trusted one.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats trust as probabilistic — warranted trust is calibrated to the track record and characteristics of the trustee. Biblical trust in God is calibrated to God's character and covenant promise — which is absolutely reliable — producing a form of trust that is not probabilistic but covenantal. The self-control vocabulary also diverges: science treats self-control as a psychological resource; the biblical account grounds self-discipline in the Spirit's work (fruit of the Spirit — Galatians 5:23) rather than in human willpower alone.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The attachment theory distinction between the secure base (confidence for exploration) and the safe haven (comfort under threat) may surface two distinct registers in the cluster's security vocabulary — does the biblical material address God as safe haven (refuge under threat) and as secure base (the confidence for mission)? This may be worth testing in the biblical pass.
