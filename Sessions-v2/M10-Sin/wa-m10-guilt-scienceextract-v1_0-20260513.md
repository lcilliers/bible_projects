## Science sections

### Section 1 — Guilt as emotional and moral state

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / moral psychology — guilt research

**Glosses covered:**
be guilty (a.sham), guilty (a.shem), guiltiness (ash.mah), sin (hamartēma), sin (hamartia), sin (chet), sin (cha.ta.ah), sin (chat.ta.ah), sin (chat.tat), sinful (hamartōlos), sinner (chat.ta), to sin (hamartanō), to sin (cha.ta), wrong (atopos), wrongdoing (kakopoios)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Lewis's foundational distinction (1971) between shame (the self is bad) and guilt (the behaviour was bad) established the primary research framework. Tangney and Dearing (*Shame and Guilt*, 2002) demonstrated through extensive empirical work that guilt is associated with more adaptive outcomes than shame: guilt motivates reparative behaviour, empathy, and apology; shame motivates concealment, externalisation of blame, and aggression. Baumeister's guilt research (*Escaping the Self*, 1991) identifies guilt as a socially functional emotion — it regulates interpersonal behaviour by motivating the repair of damaged relationships. The sin terminology in this cluster spans guilt (the subjective emotional state) and sin (the moral-theological category of wrong before God), a distinction that science partially captures in its guilt/transgression research.

**Emergence — what causes guilt to arise:**
Guilt arises from the appraisal that one has done something wrong — specifically, that one's behaviour has violated an internalised moral standard and has caused harm to another. The key conditions are: the person must have internalised the moral standard being violated (external rule compliance without internalisation produces compliance but not guilt); the person must attribute the violation to their own agency (guilt requires responsibility); and the behaviour — not the whole self — must be the locus of the negative evaluation (otherwise shame is produced instead). Moral foundations theory (Haidt) suggests that different moral concerns (harm, fairness, loyalty, authority, purity) have different elicitor profiles for guilt.

**Reduction and absence — what causes guilt to diminish:**
Guilt is relieved by: confession and acknowledgement of the wrongdoing; reparative action that addresses the harm done; forgiveness received from the wronged party; and — in the theological context — divine forgiveness that addresses the vertical dimension of the guilt (before God, not merely before the human victim). Research distinguishes healthy guilt (motivating repair) from neurotic guilt (self-punishing, excessive, without reparative action). Guilt is absent in psychopathy (the absence of empathic concern that generates guilt) and is suppressed by moral disengagement mechanisms (Bandura) that prevent the attribution of wrongdoing to oneself.

**Location — where guilt is seated:**
Primarily emotional and cognitive. Guilt is felt as an uncomfortable, self-directed negative state focused on a specific behaviour. It is less global and less physically collapsing than shame — it motivates approach (toward repair) rather than avoidance (from exposure). Neurologically, guilt activates prefrontal and anterior cingulate circuits involved in moral evaluation and conflict monitoring. Somatically it produces less dramatic physical changes than shame but carries a characteristic heaviness and restlessness — the drive to make things right.

**External influences:**
*Parenting:* The development of healthy guilt requires parenting that models and teaches moral standards, responds to violations with empathy for the harmed party (rather than shame-inducing global criticism), and affirms the child's capacity to repair. *Cultural:* Guilt-culture frameworks (individualist, Protestant) emphasise internal moral states; shame-culture frameworks (collectivist, honour-based) emphasise social standing. The biblical material spans both contexts. *Developmental:* Guilt capacity develops alongside empathy and moral understanding — typically from around age 3–4, expanding through middle childhood as moral reasoning develops. *Hereditary:* Empathy (which underlies guilt) has moderate heritability. Low guilt capacity (as in psychopathy) has genetic as well as developmental components.

**Coverage assessment:** Rich coverage. Guilt is one of the most studied moral emotions.

### Section 2 — Moral transgression and wrongdoing

**Primary field:** Moral psychology — moral foundations research

**Glosses covered:**
transgression (parabasis), transgression (pe.sha), transgressor (parabatēs), trespass (paraptōma), iniquity: crime (a.von), iniquity: guilt (a.von), iniquity: punishment (a.von), injustice (a.vel), injustice (av.lah), crime (adikēma), to transgress (parabainō), to transgress (pa.sha), unjust (adikos), unjust (av.val), unjust one (a.vil), unrighteousness (adikia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Moral foundations theory (Haidt and Graham, *The Righteous Mind*, 2012) proposes that human moral psychology operates on multiple independent foundations — harm/care, fairness/reciprocity, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, and purity/degradation. Transgression involves the violation of one or more of these foundations, with different foundations having different emotional and motivational profiles. The boundary violation framing (parabasis — stepping across a line; pe.sha — rebellion, stepping over a boundary deliberately) captures the structural understanding of wrongdoing as boundary-crossing that moral psychology operationalises in norm-violation research.

**Emergence — what causes transgression to occur:**
Transgression arises from: the weakening of moral inhibition (through temptation, self-regulatory depletion, or moral disengagement); deliberate rebellion against authority (pe.sha specifically carries the connotation of wilful defiance); and the absence of adequate moral development or internalisation of norms. Situational factors (Zimbardo's situationist account) demonstrate that ordinary people can be induced to transgress under the right (or wrong) situational pressures — undermining purely dispositional accounts of wrongdoing.

**Reduction and absence — what causes transgression to diminish:**
Transgression is reduced by: strong moral identity internalisation (Aquino and Reed); moral self-regulation capacity; environmental design that reduces opportunity; accountability structures; and community norms that make transgression costly. The absence of transgression may reflect genuine virtue, adequate self-regulation, insufficient opportunity, or fear of consequences — the biblical material distinguishes these possibilities.

**Location — where transgression is seated:**
Primarily volitional and behavioural — transgression is defined by the act of crossing a boundary, not merely by feeling that one might. It has inner antecedents (temptation, intention, deliberate defiance) and outer expressions (the action itself). The biblical vocabulary distinguishes between inadvertent transgression and wilful rebellion — a distinction with significant moral and psychological content.

**External influences:**
*Moral community:* The moral norms that define transgression are communally held and transmitted. Moral communities that maintain clear standards and accountability reduce transgression rates. *Circumstances:* Situational pressures, opportunity structures, and the immediate costs and benefits of transgression significantly shape whether it occurs. *Developmental:* Moral development research (Kohlberg, Gilligan) tracks the development of the moral reasoning capacity that governs transgression judgment.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Moral foundations theory and norm violation research provide solid frameworks.

### Section 3 — Evil, wickedness, and moral character pathology

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / personality psychology

**Glosses covered:**
evil (kakia), evil (ponēria), evil (faulos), evil (ro.a), evil/bad (ponēros), evil: trouble (a.ven), wicked (ra.sha), wickedness (mir.sha.at), wickedness (re.sha), wickedness (rish.ah), perversion (mut.teh), perversion (te.vel), perversity (ho.phekh), perversity (la.zut), perversity (tah.pu.khah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Baumeister (*Evil: Inside Human Violence and Cruelty*, 1997) identifies the gap between perpetrator self-justification and victim experience as the central analytical problem in the psychology of evil. Zimbardo (*The Lucifer Effect*, 2007) demonstrates the situational roots of evil behaviour — normal people placed in corrupting situational structures can do evil things. Bandura's moral disengagement research describes the cognitive mechanisms by which moral self-sanctions are deactivated. Dark triad research (Paulhus and Williams) examines the personality constellation of psychopathy, Machiavellianism, and narcissism that underlies persistent evil-oriented behaviour. The Hebrew ra.sha names a character orientation — wickedness as a stable disposition, not merely a series of wicked acts.

**Emergence — what causes wickedness to develop:**
Wickedness as a character orientation develops through: repeated moral disengagement and transgression without repair (each unrepaired violation makes the next easier); the normalisation of treating others as means; the progressive suppression of empathy and conscience; and immersion in social environments that reward or normalise cruelty and exploitation. Situationist research emphasises external factors; character psychology emphasises the cumulative formation of a character orientation through habitual choices.

**Reduction and absence — what causes wickedness to diminish:**
The most clinically resistant presentations involve the darkest triad personality structures — psychopathy in particular shows limited response to standard therapeutic intervention. Moral restoration requires: confrontation with the reality of harm done; restoration of empathic connection; accountability; and a changed social environment. The biblical material adds a theological dimension — transformation of the wicked character is possible but requires something beyond human therapeutic resources.

**Location — where wickedness is seated:**
Characterological — it is a stable dispositional orientation rather than a series of isolated acts. The perversity vocabulary (things turned, twisted, inverted from their proper orientation) suggests a character that has been systematically distorted from its proper direction. This is a whole-person assessment, not an emotion or a mood.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Severe childhood adversity — abuse, neglect, exposure to cruelty — is associated with the development of dark triad traits and moral disengagement. *Social context:* Institutions, groups, and ideologies that permit or mandate cruel treatment of out-groups produce evil behaviour in ordinarily moral individuals. *Hereditary:* Psychopathy has significant heritable components (particularly the affective callousness dimension). *Circumstances:* Power without accountability is among the most reliable producers of wickedness in ordinarily moral individuals (Keltner).

**Coverage assessment:** Good. The psychology of evil is well-addressed by multiple research traditions, though it remains a complex and contested domain.

### Section 4 — Defilement, impurity, and contamination

**Primary field:** Moral psychology — purity/disgust research

**Glosses covered:**
defilement (miasmos), defilement (molusmos), impurity (akatharsia), impurity (nid.dah), unclean (akathartos), unclean (ta.me), uncleanness (tum.ah), abominable (bdeluktos), abomination (bdelugma), abomination (shiq.quts), abomination (to.e.vah), to defile (molunō), to defile (ta.me), to stain (miainō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Haidt and Rozin's disgust research (*The Disgust Scale*, 1994; *Disgust: The Body and Soul Emotion*, 2007) establishes that disgust began as a food-rejection emotion and has been recruited for social and moral regulation across cultures. Moral purity intuitions are cross-culturally present but vary in their specific content. Contamination sensitivity — the tendency to perceive contact with impure objects or persons as polluting — varies as an individual difference and is associated with harm avoidance temperament and scrupulosity in clinical contexts. The purity foundation in moral foundations theory addresses the moral intuitions that produce abomination judgments.

**Emergence — what causes feelings of defilement and impurity to arise:**
Purity violations trigger disgust and contamination sensitivity through: contact with core disgust elicitors (bodily substances, decay, sexual violations); the perception that moral or social boundaries have been crossed in ways that pollute; and — in the biblical context — the specific cultic-covenantal framework in which certain acts and states render the person unfit for contact with the holy. The cluster's abomination vocabulary (to.e.vah, shiq.quts) reflects the strongest end of the moral disgust response — things that are experienced as utterly repugnant to moral sensibility.

**Reduction and absence — what causes impurity to be addressed:**
Defilement is addressed by: purification rituals (which have both psychological — cathartic — and social — boundary-restoring — functions); genuine moral repair where the defilement is moral rather than ritual; and — in the theological register — divine cleansing that addresses defilement before God. The psychology of ritual cleansing (including the efficacy of physical washing for moral feelings — Zhong and Liljenquist's Lady Macbeth effect) demonstrates that embodied purification acts have genuine psychological effects.

**Location — where impurity feelings are seated:**
Viscerally somatic in origin (disgust is fundamentally a gut-level response) and socially extended through moral and religious frameworks. Defilement is experienced as bodily (contact pollution) and moral-social (one is now in a state of impurity that affects one's standing). The cluster's vocabulary covers both the inner state (feeling defiled) and the objective condition (being defiled in the community's judgment).

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Purity norms are among the most culturally variable of all moral intuitions. The specific content of what counts as defiling is culturally and religiously determined, though the disgust substrate is cross-culturally present. *Developmental:* Disgust sensitivity develops in early childhood through socialisation. Contamination sensitivity is amplified in OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder) and in scrupulosity (a religiously-framed form of contamination anxiety). *Hereditary:* Disgust sensitivity has heritable components.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Purity and disgust research is well-developed in moral psychology.

### Section 5 — Corruption, destruction, and moral decay

**Primary field:** Social psychology / developmental psychology

**Glosses covered:**
corruption (fthora), corruption (ma.she.chat), destruction (che.vel), destruction (cha.loph), destruction (mash.chit), destruction (mash.chet), degenerate (sur), revolt (sa.rah), apostasy (apostasia), to destroy (diaftheirō), to destroy (ftheirō), to destroy (cha.val), lewdness (nav.lut), to act treacherously (ba.gad), to join hypocrisy (sunupokrinomai), to pervert (sa.laph), to pervert (a.vah), to twist (a.vah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Moral disengagement (Bandura, *Moral Disengagement*, 2016) describes the systematic mechanisms by which moral self-sanctions are deactivated — allowing people to engage in or support destructive behaviour without moral distress. Social corruption research addresses how institutions and communities can become progressively more tolerant of and involved in wrongdoing. Apostasy and moral defection involve the deliberate departure from a prior moral community's standards — involving the volitional dimension of revolt (sa.rah) rather than mere drift.

**Emergence — what causes moral decay and corruption to develop:**
Corruption develops progressively through: small compromises that normalise slightly larger ones; social environments that reward or require moral disengagement; and the gradual erosion of accountability structures. The progressive character of moral decay (each step making the next easier) is documented in moral disengagement research and in the incremental commitment literature (foot-in-the-door dynamics applied to ethical behaviour).

**Reduction and absence:** Corruption is reduced by: maintaining accountability structures; social environments with strong integrity norms; and the recovery of connection to the moral community whose standards were abandoned. Reversal of moral decay is difficult precisely because it has restructured character and social relationships.

**Location:** Primarily characterological and social — moral decay is a process that affects the person's whole orientation and their community embeddedness simultaneously.

**External influences:** Social environments are the primary driver — corrupt institutions produce corrupt individuals more reliably than corrupt individuals corrupt institutions (Zimbardo's situationist finding). Political and economic contexts that make corruption adaptive are significant amplifiers.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Moral disengagement and social corruption research provide frameworks; the specific biblical vocabulary of apostasy and revolt has limited direct science engagement.

### Section 6 — Temptation and enticement

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — self-regulation / temptation research

**Glosses covered:**
to entice (deleazō), blasphemous (blasfēmos), blasphemy (blasfēmia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Temptation research within self-regulation science (Baumeister and Vohs, *Handbook of Self-Regulation*, 2004; Hofmann and colleagues) examines the conditions under which people successfully resist or yield to tempting impulses. The tension between immediate desire and valued long-term goals is the core structure. Blasphemy (as a category of speech-act that violates the sacred) has limited direct psychological research but connects to the moral purity foundation (Haidt) and to the psychology of taboo violation.

**Emergence — what causes enticement to operate:** Temptation is maximally effective when the desired object is proximate, the self-regulatory resources are depleted, and the anticipated consequences of yielding are temporally distant. Enticement (deleazō — to bait, to lure) describes the external dimension — the situation or agent that makes yielding attractive.

**Reduction and absence:** Resisting temptation is supported by: implementation intentions (pre-decided responses to anticipated temptation); removal of the tempting stimulus from the environment; and the maintenance of self-regulatory resources through adequate rest and positive affect. Moral identity strength (how central to self-concept one's moral commitments are) also predicts resistance.

**Location:** Primarily motivational — temptation operates at the interface between desire and self-regulation capacity. It has both somatic (craving, arousal) and cognitive (imagining the desired outcome) dimensions.

**External influences:** Environmental design is the primary lever — removing or reducing access to tempting stimuli is the most reliable temptation-resistance strategy. Social accountability also reduces yielding to temptation significantly.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Self-regulation research is extensive; the specific enticement vocabulary has thinner coverage.

## Outside-science inventory

- **atonement (kip.pu.rim)** — the cultic/covenantal category of atonement as the covering of sin through sacrificial substitution; science addresses moral repair but not vicarious atonement.
- **sin: sin offering (chat.tat) in its sacrificial register** — where chat.tat names the specific offering prescribed for inadvertent sin, it is a cultic-ritual category beyond science's moral psychology.
- **unfaithfulness (ma.al) as covenant violation** — where ma.al names the breach of covenant relationship with God (covenant infidelity), the theological structure of covenant is the frame science does not have.
- **be unfaithful (ma.al)** — same as above.
- **sin as vertical offence against God** — science addresses the interpersonal dimension of moral violation (harm to others, violation of social norms) but not the vertical dimension (offence against God as the primary party wronged by all sin). The theological claim that all sin is ultimately against God (Psalm 51:4) is outside science's descriptive reach.

**Observation:** M10 has the most theologically loaded outside-science content of any cluster so far. The cluster's core vocabulary of guilt and transgression is well covered by science; the theological distinctive — that sin is primarily vertical (against God), that all wrongdoing has a covenantal dimension, and that atonement addresses not merely interpersonal harm but cosmic moral violation — is substantially outside science's scope.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Clinical and moral psychology (guilt — Section 1); moral foundations research (transgression — Section 2); clinical and personality psychology (wickedness — Section 3); moral psychology/disgust research (defilement — Section 4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The shame/guilt distinction illuminates the cluster's vocabulary — terms that name the behaviour-focused response of guilt versus those that implicate the whole self. Moral disengagement (Bandura) illuminates the mechanisms by which the cluster's wickedness and corruption vocabulary develops progressively. The contamination psychology (Zhong and Liljenquist's physical-moral cleansing connection) illuminates the embodied nature of the cluster's defilement vocabulary.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats guilt and moral transgression in an entirely horizontal register (harm to other people, violation of social norms). The biblical material's deepest structure for sin is vertical — all sin is ultimately against God, all guilt has a theological dimension. This is not merely an extension of the science account; it is a fundamental reframing of the entire moral domain. Atonement — the resolution of the vertical guilt before God — has no science analogue.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The progressive character of moral decay (moral disengagement research) may surface gaps in how the cluster's corruption and perversity vocabulary is classified — does the biblical material include terms that mark different stages of moral deterioration? The dark triad research's finding of heritable psychopathic traits may surface a dimension of the cluster's wickedness vocabulary that touches on constitutionally shaped rather than environmentally shaped moral failure.
