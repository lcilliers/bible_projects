## Science sections

### Section 1 — Social bonding, belonging, and community

**Primary field:** Social psychology / community psychology

**Glosses covered:**
brotherhood (adelfotēs), companion (cha.ver), community (chay.yah), fellow slave (sundoulos), sister (adelfē), to share (metadidōmi), to share (summerizō), unity (henotēs), to connect (cha.shaq), to agree (exomologeomai), to betroth (a.ra.s)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Baumeister and Leary's need to belong (*Psychological Bulletin*, 1995) proposes that belonging is a fundamental human motivation — people have an innate drive to form and maintain lasting, positive relationships. Deprivation of belonging produces predictable negative psychological effects (loneliness, depression, physical health deterioration). Dunbar's social brain hypothesis (*Proceedings of the Royal Society*, 1992) addresses the cognitive demands of maintaining social relationships and the corresponding constraint on social group size (the 'Dunbar number' of approximately 150). Community psychology (Sarason — *The Psychological Sense of Community*) addresses the subjective experience of belonging — the sense of membership, influence, integration, and shared emotional connection. Brotherhood (adelfotēs) and companion (cha.ver) name the relational orientation of genuine fellowship — not merely proximity but shared identity and mutual commitment.

**Emergence — what causes social bonding and belonging to arise:**
Social bonding arises through: repeated positive interaction (proximity and familiarity — Zajonc's mere exposure effect); shared experience, especially shared adversity (Festinger — group cohesion); mutual vulnerability and disclosure (Aron — self-disclosure and the expansion of self through relationship); and shared identity (social identity theory — Tajfel and Turner). The betrothal term (a.ra.s) describes the formalisation of a relational bond — the commitment that moves relationship from provisional to covenanted.

**Reduction and absence — what causes belonging to diminish:**
Belonging diminishes through: relational rupture and betrayal; social exclusion and ostracism (Leary — ostracism research); the loss of shared identity; and sustained absence of positive interaction. Loneliness — the subjective sense of insufficient belonging — is among the most powerful predictors of psychological and physical health deterioration. Cacioppo's loneliness research establishes that perceived social isolation is as damaging to health as smoking 15 cigarettes per day.

**Location — where social bonding is seated:**
Social bonding is relational — it is a property of the space between persons rather than located within any single person. However, the psychological experience of belonging is an inner state: felt security, warmth, identity, and the sense of being known and valued. Neurologically, social bonding involves the oxytocin and dopaminergic systems. The inner-being dimension for the programme is the orientation of the person toward community — the relational disposition of openness and commitment.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Secure attachment in childhood is the primary developmental foundation for adult social bonding capacity. Those with insecure attachment bring defensive or anxious strategies to adult relationships. *Cultural:* Collectivist cultures cultivate strong in-group belonging; individualist cultures cultivate more bounded, selective bonding. *Circumstances:* Shared adversity (war, natural disaster, persecution) is among the most powerful community-building forces.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Social psychology of belonging is well-developed and directly relevant.

### Section 2 — Enmity, conflict, and relational opposition

**Primary field:** Social psychology — conflict research

**Glosses covered:**
enemy (o.yev), enemy (tsar), enmity (e.vah), to contend (epagōnizomai), to contend (riv), to contend (ta.cha.rah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment (*Intergroup Conflict and Cooperation*, 1961) demonstrated that intergroup conflict arises readily from competition for scarce resources and is reduced by superordinate goals requiring cooperation. Dehumanisation research (Bandura, Leyens — infrahumanisation) addresses how enmity is sustained through the denial of full human status to the enemy — making harm more psychologically permissible. Enemy psychology (Sjoberg; Rieber) addresses how the image of the enemy functions psychologically — enemies provide a negative identity anchor (we are defined partly by who we are not). The contention vocabulary (riv, eris — quarrel, legal dispute) spans interpersonal conflict from structured legal contest to relational hostility.

**Emergence — what causes enmity to arise:**
Enmity arises through: competition for scarce or valued resources; perceived injustice or offense that triggers retributive hostility; ideological/identity incompatibility; and the social categorisation of the other as out-group (which automatically triggers reduced empathy and increased readiness to harm). The persistent enemy (o.yev — the ongoing enemy, not merely the situational opponent) names a stable relational orientation of hostility — enmity as a settled disposition rather than an episodic conflict.

**Reduction and absence — what causes enmity to diminish:**
Intergroup enmity is reduced by: superordinate goals that require cooperation; contact under conditions of equal status (Allport's contact hypothesis); personalisation of the enemy (reducing dehumanisation); and the removal of the conditions (scarce resources, perceived injustice) that produced it. Interpersonal enmity is reduced through the restoration of justice, forgiveness (M11), and the reconstruction of trust.

**Location — where enmity is seated:**
Enmity as a relational orientation is seated in the inner being's evaluation of and attitude toward the other — it is a combination of negative evaluation (contempt, hostility — M06), active hostility, and the settled opposition of the will. It is relational in character but has a clear inner-being locus: the person's disposition toward the enemy.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Group membership and social norms powerfully shape who is designated as enemy. *Cultural:* Cultural and historical traditions of enmity can sustain hostility for generations beyond the original conflict. *Circumstances:* Genuine harm done by the other is a primary driver.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Intergroup conflict research provides coverage; the theological register (enmity between humanity and God — Rom 5:10; the enmity of Gen 3:15) is outside science.

## Outside-science inventory

- **Unity (henotēs) in its ecclesial register** — the unity of the body of Christ as a theological category — is outside science. Social psychology addresses group cohesion; the theological claim is that the church's unity is grounded in the unity of the Triune God.
- **Betrothal (a.ra.s) in its covenantal dimension** — marriage as a covenantal image of God's relationship with Israel (Hosea, Eph 5) — is a theological category.
- **Enmity (e.vah) in its theological register** — the enmity between the seed of the woman and the seed of the serpent (Gen 3:15) — is a theological category that shapes the programme's understanding of spiritual conflict.
- **Fellow slave (sundoulos)** in its shared-servanthood-to-Christ register — a theological category of mutual identity before God.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology of belonging (Baumeister, Leary, Dunbar) for Section 1; social psychology of conflict (Sherif, Allport) for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Need-to-belong research illuminates why the cluster's fellowship and community vocabulary is so consistently positive and motivationally significant — belonging is a genuine human need, not merely a preference. Dehumanisation research illuminates why enmity vocabulary in Scripture so often involves the total othering of the enemy — the psychological mechanism of enemy-hatred involves the suppression of shared humanity.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The covenantal grounding of both fellowship (as participation in the covenant community) and enmity (as opposition to the covenant God and his people) is a theological frame that exceeds social psychology's horizontal account. Science treats both belonging and enmity as social phenomena; Scripture roots them in the vertical relationship with God.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** Whether the cluster's relational terms describe primarily the inner-being disposition (the person's orientation toward others) or the external social relationship (the objective fact of fellowship or enmity) will be the key Session B analytical question.
