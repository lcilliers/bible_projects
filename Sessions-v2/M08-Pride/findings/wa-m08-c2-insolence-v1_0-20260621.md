## 1. Definition (working)

Pride turned **outward against other people** as insolent contempt, derision, and oppression — the haughty bearing down on neighbour, elder, and the lowly. The outward, relational pole of pride.

## 2. Evidence base

**~13 core occurrences** (plus cross-listed oppression from B): zed (H2086 — Psalm 119:21, 51, 69, 78, 85, 122; Psalm 86:14), zid (H2102 — Exodus 18:11; Nehemiah 9:10; Exodus 21:14), za.don (H2087 — Proverbs 13:10), ra.hav (H7292 — Isaiah 3:5), shal.le.tet (H7986 — Ezekiel 16:30). Cross-listed from B (settled pride expressed as oppression): ga.a.vah (H1346 — Psalm 10:2; 31:18; 73:6; 140:5) and ge.ut (H1348 — Psalm 17:10).

## 3. Analysis (observation → interpretation)

**The dominant scene is the insolent oppressing the righteous.** The Psalm 119 cluster sets it out: "The insolent utterly deride me" (119:51); "The insolent smear me with lies" (119:69); "The insolent have dug pitfalls for me" (119:85); "let not the insolent oppress me" (119:122); "insolent men have risen up against me… seek my life" (Psalm 86:14). The insolent are the agents; the righteous sufferer is the one acted upon.

**Insolence and disregard of God's law go together.** The same insolent ones "wander from your commandments" (Psalm 119:21) and "do not live according to your law" (Psalm 119:85). **Interpretation:** in the wicked, contempt toward people (C2) and disregard of God (C1) are fused — the same pride faces both directions. The split is analytic; in the person it is one disposition.

**It breaks proper social order.** "the youth will be insolent to the elder, and the despised to the honorable" (Isaiah 3:5) — insolence as the collapse of due regard.

**Its root is the settled pride of B.** The cross-listed evidence shows the mechanism: "In arrogance the wicked hotly pursue the poor" (Psalm 10:2); "pride is their necklace; violence covers them" (Psalm 73:6); "They close their hearts to pity; with their mouths they speak arrogantly" (Psalm 17:10). The settled height (B) issues in contemptuous action (C2).

**The sufferer's posture is fidelity under contempt.** The righteous keep God's word despite the insolent — "The insolent utterly deride me, but I do not turn away from your law" (Psalm 119:51); "with my whole heart I keep your precepts" (Psalm 119:69). C2 is largely framed from the victim's side.

## 4. Synthesis

Insolence is pride aimed at people: the haughty deriding, defrauding, and oppressing the lowly, and the young despising the honourable. It is the outward, relational discharge of the settled pride of B, and in the wicked it is inseparable from their disregard of God (C1). The corpus most often shows it from beneath — as contempt endured by the faithful, who answer it not in kind but by keeping God's word.

## 5. Expected but absent — special attention

1. **The proud's *inner* state is thin by nature — a genuine shape, not a gap.** C2 is described overwhelmingly through *action* (deride, smear, oppress, dig pitfalls, pursue the poor) rather than through an inner condition of the proud agent. Its inner-being content is largely supplied by B (settled pride) and by the *victim's* interior (the faithful heart that keeps the law). **Interpretation:** C2 is the **faculty-absent outward pole** of pride — its analysis legitimately leans on the agent's settled disposition (B) and the sufferer's response, because the lexical itself locates little interior in the insolent act. This mirrors the M06 finding that a substantial share of outward hostility is faculty-absent. Genuine silence, recorded as a finding.
2. **Experiencer is frequently the *sufferer*, not the proud.** The `experiencer=self` tags (Psalm 119:51, 85, 122) mark the psalmist-victim, not the insolent. Read carelessly this could be miscounted as the proud person's self-experience. → caution flag for any aggregation.
3. **Seat rarely named** (heart, Ezekiel 16:30 "brazen"; soul, Psalm 86:14 of the victim). The proud agent's seat is mostly unstated; the named seats often belong to the sufferer. Do not over-read.
4. **Cause largely silent**, consistent with B: the contempt flows from a settled disposition rather than a stated elicitor.

## 6. Reflection (labelled — not a conclusion)

- C2 carries direct social-ethical resonance (contempt for the poor, the elder, the honourable); the "from beneath" framing — fidelity under contempt — is pastorally significant but stated descriptively here.
- The fusion of C1 and C2 in the wicked (contempt for God and for people as one disposition) is a candidate observation for the cluster synthesis.
- **Cross-cultural:** Isaiah 3:5 (youth insolent to elder) will read with particular force in age-/honour-ordered cultures. → flag for reader-facing.
