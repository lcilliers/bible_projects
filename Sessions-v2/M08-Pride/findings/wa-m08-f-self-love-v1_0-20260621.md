## 1. Definition (working)

Pride turned fully inward and upward at once — the **self made its own centre and, at the limit, its own god**. The displacement of God by the self.

## 2. Evidence base

**Direct: 2 occurrences.**
- filautos (G5367, 1) — "people will be **lovers of self**, lovers of money, proud, arrogant…" (2 Timothy 3:2). Heads the catalogue of the last days.
- a.din (H5719, 1) — "you say in your heart, '**I am, and there is no one besides me**'" (Isaiah 47:8, seated in heart).

## 3. Analysis (observation → interpretation)

**F is the terminus, not a dense word-group.** Named directly only twice, its substance appears as the endpoint toward which the other characteristics run: the self-exalting heart (A) that ascends "above the stars"; the settled pride (B) whose face says "There is no God"; the defiant will (C1) that refuses all authority. Each, pressed to its limit, arrives at the self enthroned in God's place.

**Its speech is the usurped divine "I am."** "I am, and there is no one besides me" (Isaiah 47:8) takes onto the lips of Babylon the self-predication that belongs to God alone ("I am… besides me there is no god," Isaiah 45). Pride here is not merely high but **idolatrous of the self** — practical atheism in its negative face ("There is no God," Psalm 10:4) and self-deification in its positive face ("I am a god," Ezekiel 28:2).

**Its root word is mis-directed love.** filautos = self-*love*: the catalogue of 2 Timothy 3 opens with disordered love (lovers of self, lovers of money, lovers of pleasure "rather than lovers of God," v4). The disorder is one of love's object — the self loved where God should be.

**Interpretation.** F is the cluster's vanishing point: where A/B/C/E are facets of pride, F is what pride finally *is* when complete — the self in the place of God. Its thin direct lexicalisation reflects that it is more a terminus than a discrete state.

## 4. Synthesis

Self-love is pride turned fully inward and upward: the self made its own centre and its own god. Directly it is named only twice — the "lover of self" heading the catalogue of the last days, and the "lover of pleasures" who says in her heart "I am, and there is no one besides me" — but it is the logical end toward which the cluster runs. Its speech usurps the divine "I am"; its negative face is the practical atheism of the proud ("There is no God"); its essence is the displacement of God by the self, the disorder of a love that rests finally on itself.
