## Science sections

### Section 1 — Threat detection and fear response

**Primary field:** Neuroscience — affective neuroscience

**Glosses covered:**
fear (yir.ah), fear (fobos), fear (deos), fear (me.go.rah), fear (me.gu.rah), fear (mo.rah), fear (mo.ra), dread (pa.chad), dread (pach.dah), terror (e.mah), terror (bal.la.hah), terror (be.a.tah), terror (chit.tah), terror (chat.chat), terror (chit.tit), terror (cha.tat), terror (ma.gor), terror (me.chit.tah), terror (ma.a.ra.tsah), terror (tiph.le.tset), fearful (foberos), terrified (ekfobos), dreadful (a.ruts), timid (deilos), timidity (deilia), to fear (fobeō), to fear (ya.re), to fear (de.chal), to fear (za.chal), to fear (ya.gor), to fear (ra.hah), to fear: revere (ya.re), to frighten (ptoeō), to frighten (pturomai), afraid (emfobos), afraid (ya.re), fearing (ya.gor)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
The amygdala-centred model of fear processing (LeDoux, *The Emotional Brain*, 1996) established that fear responses can be initiated by subcortical pathways that bypass conscious appraisal — a rapid, automatic threat-detection system operating below conscious awareness. Affective neuroscience (Panksepp, *Affective Neuroscience*, 1998) identifies FEAR as one of seven primary emotional systems, hard-wired and cross-species, associated with specific subcortical circuitry. Öhman's preparedness theory demonstrates that certain threat stimuli (predators, angry faces) activate fear circuitry more readily than others. The distinction between fear (present, definite threat) and anxiety (anticipated, diffuse threat) is foundational (Barlow, *Anxiety and Its Disorders*, 2002).

**Emergence — what causes fear to arise:**
Fear is triggered by perceived threat to survival, safety, or wellbeing. At the neurological level, threat stimuli activate the amygdala through two pathways: a fast subcortical route (direct from thalamus, before cortical processing) and a slower cortical route that allows for appraisal and context. The fast route produces an immediate fear response before the person is consciously aware of the threat — explaining why fear can feel involuntary and instantaneous. Threat appraisal (Lazarus and Folkman) adds a cognitive layer: fear is shaped by the assessment of a stimulus as threatening and of one's own capacity to cope. Where threat is appraised as high and coping capacity as low, fear intensifies. Learned fear — through conditioning (Pavlovian fear conditioning is among the most robust findings in psychology) — means that previously neutral stimuli can acquire fear-eliciting properties through association with genuinely threatening events.

**Reduction and absence — what causes fear to diminish or not exist:**
Extinction learning (the repeated exposure to a feared stimulus without negative outcome) is the primary mechanism by which conditioned fear is reduced. This is the basis of exposure therapy, the most evidence-supported treatment for fear-based disorders. However, extinction does not erase the original fear memory — it creates a competing inhibitory memory. Fear can return spontaneously, particularly in contexts resembling the original conditioning environment. Cognitive reappraisal (Beck's cognitive therapy) reduces fear by altering the threat appraisal — changing the assessment of either the threat's severity or one's capacity to cope. Safety signals (stimuli reliably associated with the absence of threat) reliably reduce fear in experimental settings. At the neurological level, prefrontal cortical activity (particularly the ventromedial prefrontal cortex) inhibits amygdala responding and underlies the experience of feeling safe.

**Location — where fear is seated:**
Fear is simultaneously neurological, physical, and emotional. Neurologically, it is centred in subcortical limbic structures, primarily the amygdala, with regulatory input from prefrontal cortex. Physiologically, it is expressed throughout the body via the autonomic nervous system (sympathetic arousal: elevated heart rate, muscle tension, perspiration, trembling). Emotionally, it is experienced as a felt state of threat or alarm. Cognitively, it involves appraisal of threat and assessment of coping capacity. Fear is therefore not exclusively located in any one domain — it is a systemic response that integrates neurological, physiological, cognitive, and affective dimensions. The somatic dimension (Section 2) is the most visible aspect of this integration.

**External influences — circumstances, hereditary factors, parenting, social environment:**
*Hereditary:* Twin studies consistently show that fear and anxiety-related traits have moderate heritability (approximately 30–50%), with specific phobias showing higher heritability than generalised anxiety. Temperament — particularly behavioural inhibition (Kagan) — is a heritable trait that predisposes individuals to fearful responses in novel or uncertain situations. *Parenting and early environment:* Insecure attachment in infancy (Bowlby, Ainsworth) predisposes toward heightened fear responses. Parental modelling of fearful responses directly shapes children's fear responses through observational learning. Overprotective parenting that prevents normal exposure to manageable threat can maintain and amplify fear. *Circumstances:* Traumatic events — particularly unpredictable and uncontrollable ones — are among the strongest environmental triggers for chronic fear responses (post-traumatic stress). Adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) significantly elevate the risk of persistent fear-based disorders into adulthood. *Social and cultural environment:* Fear can be socially transmitted (social referencing in infants; mass fear contagion in adults). Cultural norms shape which fears are considered normative or shameful, affecting whether fear is expressed or suppressed.

**Coverage assessment:** The most science-rich section of the cluster. Extensive coverage across neuroscience, experimental psychology, and clinical research.

### Section 2 — Physiological arousal and somatic fear expression

**Primary field:** Physiology / psychophysiology

**Glosses covered:**
trembling (entromos), trembling (tromos), trembling (ze.va.ah), trembling (cha.red), trembling (cha.ra.dah), trembling (ra.ad), trembling (re.a.dah), trembling (re.tet), to tremble (cha.rad), to tremble (a.rats), to tremble (ra.gaz), to tremble (ra.ad), to shudder (pa.lats), to shudder (sa.ar), shuddering (pal.la.tsut), shuddering (sa.ar), quivering (rag.gaz), quivering (rog.zah), staggering (pu.qah), faint (dav.va), to be dismayed (cha.tat), panic (re.tet), tossing (ne.dud)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Cannon's early description of the sympathetic nervous system's role in emergency arousal (*Bodily Changes in Pain, Hunger, Fear and Rage*, 1915) remains foundational. James-Lange theory (William James, 1884) proposed that emotional experience follows somatic response rather than precedes it — the experience of fear is partly constituted by the awareness of one's own trembling, racing heart, and heightened arousal. Damasio's somatic marker theory (*Descartes' Error*, 1994) similarly argues that bodily states are constitutive of emotional experience. Porges' polyvagal theory distinguishes three autonomic states: the social engagement system (calm), the sympathetic fight-flight system (aroused fear), and the dorsal vagal freeze system (shutdown, collapse) — making trembling and staggering potentially distinct from each other in their underlying physiology.

**Emergence — what causes somatic fear expression to arise:**
Somatic fear responses are produced by sympathetic nervous system activation under conditions of perceived threat. The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis releases cortisol and adrenaline; these hormones produce the cascade of physical responses — elevated heart rate, muscle tension, tremor, pallor, perspiration — that the biblical vocabulary records in exceptional density. Trembling specifically arises from muscle tension and adrenaline-driven neuromuscular activation. Fainting (vasovagal syncope) can paradoxically arise from extreme fear through a different mechanism — a sudden drop in blood pressure following initial arousal. The freeze response (Porges) is triggered by threat perceived as inescapable, producing immobility, reduced heart rate, and collapse rather than aroused trembling.

**Reduction and absence — what causes somatic arousal to diminish:**
The parasympathetic nervous system (rest-and-digest) counteracts sympathetic arousal and returns the body to baseline following the cessation of threat. This downregulation is impaired in chronic stress and trauma conditions — where the HPA axis remains elevated and the body does not fully return to baseline. Slow, controlled breathing directly activates the parasympathetic system and is among the most effective immediate interventions for reducing somatic fear arousal. The absence of somatic fear expression can result from: habituation (repeated exposure reduces autonomic responding over time); dissociation (shutdown of somatic awareness under extreme threat — a defence mechanism); or cultural suppression (learned inhibition of visible somatic expression).

**Location — where somatic fear is seated:**
Explicitly physical — the body is the primary site. The autonomic nervous system (sympathetic branch), the HPA axis, skeletal musculature, cardiovascular system, and respiratory system are all involved. The biblical cluster's vocabulary for trembling, shuddering, quivering, and staggering maps directly onto the peripheral nervous system and musculoskeletal expression of central fear processing. The cluster's somatic vocabulary is unusually dense by comparison with other ancient emotional vocabularies — this density is itself analytically significant for inner-being research, suggesting that the bodily dimension of fear is foregrounded in the scriptural witness.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Autonomic reactivity — how quickly and strongly the sympathetic nervous system responds to threat — has a hereditary component. High autonomic reactivity is a stable individual difference trait. *Developmental:* Early trauma produces lasting changes in HPA axis regulation, with elevated baseline cortisol and heightened somatic reactivity in adulthood (van der Kolk, *The Body Keeps the Score*, 2014). *Circumstances:* Physical exhaustion, illness, and sleep deprivation amplify somatic fear responses. Chronically threatening environments (war zones, domestic violence contexts) produce persistent HPA dysregulation. *Social:* The expression of somatic fear is culturally shaped — some cultural contexts suppress visible trembling as shameful, others permit or ritualise it. This affects whether somatic fear expression is available as a data point in self-report studies, but does not change the underlying physiology.

**Coverage assessment:** Good coverage. Psychophysiology and somatic research provide direct analogues to this vocabulary. The density of somatic terms in M01 exceeds what is typical in ancient emotional vocabulary compilations and is worth carrying as a finding into Session B.

### Section 3 — Anxiety, anticipatory dread, and worry

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — anxiety research

**Glosses covered:**
anxiety (de.a.gah), anxiety (sar.ap.pim), be distressed (ademoneo), be distressed (ke.ra), be timid (deiliaō), hardship (stenochoria), hardship (te.la.ah), pressure (thlipsis), pressure (a.qah), be perplexed (aporeō), be perplexed (diaporeō), be perplexed (she.vash)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Beck's cognitive model of anxiety (Beck and Emery, *Anxiety Disorders and Phobias*, 1985) identifies threat appraisal and perceived inability to cope as the cognitive architecture underlying anxiety. Borkovec's research on worry identifies it as verbal-linguistic, repetitive, and future-oriented — a sustained cognitive engagement with possible threat that is functionally different from acute fear. Barlow's unified model (*Anxiety and Its Disorders*, 2002) treats anxiety as a distinct emotional state from fear — sharing some neural substrates but with different temporal profiles, different cognitive contents, and different functional roles.

**Emergence — what causes anxiety to arise:**
Anxiety arises when threat is anticipated rather than present, and when the outcome is uncertain. The key cognitive ingredients are: appraisal of future threat as possible and significant; appraisal of one's own coping resources as insufficient; and intolerance of uncertainty (Dugas — intolerance of uncertainty is among the strongest predictors of generalised anxiety). Anxiety can arise without any conscious identification of its object — free-floating anxiety — which the biblical terms for vague distress and perplexity (aporeō, she.vash) may partially capture. Anxiety is also produced by the memory of past threat, sustaining arousal beyond the resolution of the original danger.

**Reduction and absence — what causes anxiety to diminish:**
Certainty, predictability, and a sense of control are the primary anxiety-reducers. Information that resolves uncertainty reduces anxiety even when the information is negative (the known is less anxiety-provoking than the unknown). Cognitive reappraisal of the threat or of one's coping resources reduces anxiety through the same mechanisms as in Section 1. The presence of trusted relational support (social support as a buffer) is among the most consistent findings in the anxiety literature. Mindfulness-based approaches reduce anxiety by decreasing the tendency to engage in worry cognition. Anxiety is absent or minimal in individuals with high perceived self-efficacy (Bandura), secure attachment, and low intolerance of uncertainty.

**Location — where anxiety is seated:**
Primarily cognitive and emotional, with somatic accompaniments. Unlike the acute fear response (Section 1), which is primarily a subcortical neurological and somatic event, anxiety is more heavily mediated by cortical processes — the prefrontal cortex is involved in the anticipatory and evaluative dimensions. Anxiety is felt as a sustained emotional state (dread, unease, tension) and experienced cognitively as rumination and worry. Its somatic expressions (muscle tension, unsettled stomach, sleep disruption) are less acute than those of fear but more persistent. The perplexity terms in this section (aporeō, she.vash) suggest a cognitive dimension — the mind at a loss, unable to find a path — that places this section's content more in the cognitive domain than Section 1.

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Generalised anxiety has significant heritability (approximately 30–40% from twin studies). Shared genetic variance with depression (the internalising spectrum) is well-established — anxiety and depression frequently co-occur and share heritable vulnerability. *Parenting:* Overprotective or anxious parenting is among the strongest environmental risk factors for childhood anxiety disorders. Parental modelling of anxious appraisal directly teaches threat-focused cognitive patterns. *Adverse experiences:* Unpredictable and uncontrollable life events (loss of employment, bereavement, chronic illness) are strong situational triggers. Social adversity — poverty, marginalisation, discrimination — produces chronic low-level anxiety through sustained uncertainty and reduced control. *Cultural:* Collectivist cultures show different anxiety profiles than individualist cultures, suggesting that the social environment shapes what is experienced as threatening and how anxiety is expressed.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. The gloss count is smaller than Sections 1–2. Science coverage for anxiety is rich and well-developed; the cluster's contribution here is proportionally smaller.

### Section 4 — Awe, wonder, and reverential fear

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion / positive psychology — psychology of awe

**Glosses covered:**
be awe-struck (ekthambeo), astonished (ekthambos), bewilderment (tim.ma.hon), amazement (thambos), to be astounded (ta.mah), to dismay (ba.hal), to dismay (be.hal), horror (za.a.vah), horror: destroyed (sham.mah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Keltner and Haidt's foundational paper on awe (2003) identifies two core features — perceived vastness and a need for accommodation (existing mental structures do not adequately fit the experience). Empirical research on awe (Stellar, Gordon, Piff and colleagues, 2015 onwards) reliably shows that awe produces self-diminishment, slowed time perception, prosocial orientation, and increased openness to new information. The psychology of religion has addressed reverential fear through the numinous experience (Otto, *Das Heilige*, 1917 — operationalised in Hood's Mysticism Scale and the Religious Experience Questionnaire), which combines elements of dread (tremendum) and fascination (fascinans).

**Emergence — what causes awe to arise:**
Awe is triggered by encounters with stimuli perceived as vast — physically large, conceptually overwhelming, morally sublime, or socially powerful — that exceed existing mental frameworks. Natural phenomena (storms, mountains, vast spaces), great music, encounters with moral greatness, and religious experiences are the primary elicitors. The vastness need not be physical: moral vastness (encountering exceptional virtue or exceptional evil) and conceptual vastness (encountering a reality too large to comprehend) both produce awe. In the biblical vocabulary, the proximity of the divine — the divine presence, divine acts of power — is the primary awe-elicitor, consistent with the science account of awe as triggered by something perceived as exceeding ordinary human scale.

**Reduction and absence — what causes awe to diminish:**
Familiarity is the primary reducer of awe — repeated exposure to the same stimulus reduces its capacity to overwhelm existing frameworks (habituation). Awe is also reduced by a sense of mastery or comprehension: once a phenomenon is explained and assimilated, the need for accommodation that awe creates is resolved. Contexts that prioritise control, efficiency, or instrumentalism tend to suppress awe by framing the world as manageable and comprehensible. The absence of awe correlates with narcissism (the self as the largest thing in one's world) and with what psychologists have called a diminished sense of the sublime.

**Location — where awe is seated:**
Awe is primarily emotional and cognitive, with significant physiological accompaniments (goosebumps, tingling, a sense of physical smallness, altered time perception). It is distinct from fear in that its primary valence is not survival-threat but overwhelm of a different kind — the sense that one's existing categories are insufficient. Keltner identifies awe as involving both the body (the physical response of being struck) and the mind (the cognitive need for accommodation). Its relational dimension is significant: awe reliably orients the self toward something beyond the self, producing prosocial and self-transcendent effects that are not characteristic of threat-fear.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Childhood exposure to natural environments, religious practice, music, and great art cultivates the capacity for awe. Conversely, environments that are exclusively functional or technological may reduce the development of awe-responsiveness. *Cultural:* Cultures vary significantly in the elicitors and expressions of awe. Some cultures ritualise awe-inducing encounters (pilgrimage, initiation, liturgy) as formative events; others have no equivalent practice. *Individual differences:* Openness to experience (Big Five personality) is among the strongest predictors of awe-proneness. Mindfulness practices increase awe responsiveness. *Circumstances:* Direct encounters with vastness — bereavement, natural disaster, the birth of a child — commonly produce awe as an unsought response, independent of individual disposition.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate and growing. Awe research is one of the more active areas of positive psychology. The overlap with Section 1 is real — horror and dismay sit at the boundary between threat-fear and overwhelm-awe.

**Flag for Session B:** Several glosses in this section (ba.hal, be.hal, za.a.vah, sham.mah) carry strong negative weight and may sit between Sections 1 and 4 depending on the verse evidence. The science grouping places them here because of their overlap with the overwhelm/vastness register, but the biblical analysis should determine their primary classification independently.

### Section 5 — Constraint, coercion, and external pressure

**Primary field:** Behavioural science / social psychology — stress and coercion research

**Glosses covered:**
coercion (o.tser), constraint (mu.tsaq), terrible (a.yom), ruthless (a.ruts), wave (mish.bar), burning (cho.ri)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Lazarus and Folkman's stress and coping model (*Stress, Appraisal, and Coping*, 1984) identifies controllability and predictability as the two dimensions that most determine whether an external stressor produces acute fear, chronic anxiety, or learned helplessness. Seligman's learned helplessness research demonstrated that uncontrollable, inescapable aversive events produce a characteristic profile of passivity, cognitive impairment, and emotional dysregulation. French and Raven's power bases (1959) and Milgram's obedience research address the social dimension — how external coercive authority shapes inner states.

**Emergence — what causes the fear response to external constraint to arise:**
The fear response to coercive external forces arises when three conditions coincide: the force is appraised as threatening, it is perceived as uncontrollable, and escape or resistance is perceived as impossible or very costly. These conditions reliably produce acute fear and, if sustained, learned helplessness and despair. The biblical terms in this section describe forces with precisely these qualities — overwhelming, uncontrollable, and irresistible (waves, burning, ruthless agents). The inner fear response they name is the predictable psychological consequence of the external reality they describe.

**Reduction and absence — what causes this fear to diminish:**
Perceived controllability or the presence of an effective ally (someone who can address the threatening force on one's behalf) are the primary reducers. Agency — the capacity to take any effective action — significantly reduces the fear response even to genuinely dangerous situations. Social support that buffers the experience of inescapable threat (co-regulation, shared suffering) attenuates the learned helplessness response. The biblical pattern of crying out to God in the face of overwhelming force maps onto the psychological mechanism of finding an agent capable of addressing the inescapable threat.

**Location — where this characteristic is seated:**
This section is unique in that the glosses primarily describe the external situation rather than an internal state. The fear that arises is seated in the same internal locations as Sections 1–2 (neurological and somatic), but the point of origin is relational and environmental — outside the person. The distinction between the threatening agent and the inner response it produces is worth carrying into Session B: the biblical vocabulary at this point is describing the world as it bears down on the inner being, not the inner being in isolation.

**External influences:**
*Circumstances* are the primary driver for this section — coercive constraint is by definition an external circumstance rather than an internal predisposition. *Social and political environment:* Societies with high levels of arbitrary coercive power (war, occupation, famine, persecution) create sustained conditions in which this dimension of fear is normative rather than exceptional. Much of the biblical literature is produced in and addressed to such contexts. *Hereditary and developmental:* Prior exposure to coercive constraint (childhood experiences of powerlessness, abuse, deprivation) shapes both the sensitivity of the threat-detection system and the capacity for resilience under later constraint.

**Coverage assessment:** Thinner than Sections 1–3. These glosses describe the threatening force; the science literature primarily addresses the inner response to such forces. A short honest section is preferred over padding.

## Outside-science inventory

The following glosses, or registers of glosses, fall outside human science's descriptive scope for this cluster:

- **fear (ptoēsis)** — used in contexts of eschatological fear (the terror of the Day of the Lord as a divine event); science addresses the human experience of overwhelming fear but not its eschatological framing.
- **terror (chag.ga)** — terror as a divine instrument of judgment; the theological category of God deploying fear as a covenantal response is outside science's scope.
- **distress (me.tsar)** — in covenantal distress contexts where the distress is the consequence of covenant violation; the relational-covenantal structure is not a scientific category.
- **to fear: revere (ya.re)** — the reverential/worship register of ya.re, where fear names the appropriate posture before the holy God, is a theological category. Science addresses the phenomenology of awe and numinous experience (Section 4 above), but the normativity of such fear — that it is right and fitting given who God is — is outside science's descriptive reach.

**Observation:** The outside-science content for M01 is proportionally smaller than for clusters whose core content is theological. Fear as a human experience is richly covered by science. The theological distinctive of this cluster is not that fear falls outside science, but that the *object* and *normativity* of certain fears (fear of God as right response to covenant relationship) are theological categories that science does not adjudicate.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

These notes are prepared to assist the analyst during Phase 8 of Session B when T7.3 prompts are reached. They are provisional — the biblical pass may surface findings that modify or extend them.

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Neuroscience (affective neuroscience) — specifically the amygdala-centred model of fear processing and the fight-flight-freeze response. Clinical psychology (anxiety research) is the major secondary field for the anticipatory dimension of the cluster.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The somatic density of the cluster's vocabulary (Section 2) aligns with somatic marker theory and psychophysiology — science illuminates why fear is so extensively embodied in the scriptural witness. The emergence dimensions across all sections illuminate the conditional structure of fear: fear is not a fixed state but a response to a specific configuration of threat, appraisal, and perceived coping capacity. The external influences material illuminates the social and developmental dimensions of fear that the scriptural text addresses (generational transmission of fear patterns, community fear contexts, the fear of the oppressed). The awe/reverential-fear dimension (Section 4) illuminates how certain fear-of-God terms produce the downstream effects science identifies (self-diminishment, openness, prosocial orientation).

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The most significant divergence is the normativity of fear. Science treats fear as adaptive or maladaptive depending on context — a functional, value-neutral assessment. Scripture treats certain fears as categorically right (fear of God as the beginning of wisdom) and others as categorically destructive (fear that drives out trust and love). Science has no category equivalent to commanded fear that is simultaneously a virtue and a rightly-ordered response. Reductive divergence is likely if the cluster's theology of fear is mapped directly onto clinical constructs of anxiety management. A second divergence: the external influences material identifies parenting, heredity, and circumstance as the primary shapers of fear — a horizontal, immanent account. The scriptural account adds a vertical dimension: God as the one who both instils appropriate fear and addresses inescapable threat.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The freeze response dimension (Porges, polyvagal theory) may be underrepresented in the source groupings — worth testing in verse evidence whether any terms carry a dissociative or shutdown register rather than the arousal register of Section 1. The intolerance of uncertainty construct (Dugas) may surface nuances in how the cluster's anxiety terms map onto the perplexity/at-a-loss register of Section 3. The hereditary and developmental dimensions of fear (Kagan's behavioural inhibition, ACEs research) may have no direct biblical parallel but are worth noting as the science account of what shapes fear-proneness from the outside.
