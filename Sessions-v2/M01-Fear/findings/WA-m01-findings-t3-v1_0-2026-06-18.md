# T3.1 · Perception

## T3.1.1 — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — and if so, which inner sense and how?

**Finding.** Perception is engaged by the **ordinary/danger-fear register** and, by compound co-operation, by reverent fear's "seeing/hearing" — but **not** by the abiding-state fears.

- **Ordinary fear (G5401 set, filed under c1; = c2's register analytically):** **43 affect+perception blends** — fear aroused by **what is seen/heard**: Mat 14:26 — *"when the disciples saw him walking on the sea, they were terrified"*; Mar 4:41 — *"they were filled with great fear… 'Who then is this…?'"* The inner sense is chiefly **sight/hearing** of a threat or wonder. This is the faculty that most distinguishes ordinary fear from reverent fear.
- **c4 (Terror)** — one perception blend: Luk 21:11 (G5400, "fearful sights") — terror at perceived eschatological signs.
- **c1 (reverent, refined), c2, c3, c5, c6, c7** — perception co-operates via **compound** "to see"/"to hear" co-terms (e.g. c2 *ra.ah* "see" 4, *sha.ma* "hear" 4; c1 *ra.ah* 2, *sha.ma* 7) — fear arises alongside seeing/hearing, but `faculty` is marked affect-only.
- **c8, c9, c10, c11** — **silent** on perception as faculty.

## T3.1.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?

**Finding (inferential, data-directioned).** Where engaged, fear **follows from / depends on** perception rather than enabling or impairing it:

- **Ordinary fear (G5401)** — **presupposes** perception: fear is the affective response *to* what is perceived (seeing Jesus on the water → terror). It does not enable or impair the sense; it is **triggered by** it. (Inferential reading of the affect+perception coupling.)
- **c4** — terror follows perceived signs (Luk 21:11) — same dependence.
- All others — no enable/deepen/bypass/impair relation evidenced; **silent**.

## T3.1.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The pattern is **diagnostic of the act/state split**: the **act-fears triggered by a present stimulus** (ordinary fear; eschatological terror) couple with perception; the **abiding-state fears** (dread c3, terror-as-state c4, anxiety c9) and the **disposition/trait** (reverent fear c1-refined, cowardice c11) do **not** require a present percept. Reverent fear's non-engagement (in its refined sense) is itself a finding — it is not a reaction to a perceived threat but a standing orientation. Recorded.

# T3.2 · Cognition

## T3.2.1 — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

**Finding.** Cognition is **faculty-marked only in c9 (anxiety)**, but **co-operates by compound** across the cluster:

- **c9 (Anxiety)** — **the cognitive fear:** affect+cognition (2): Psa 139:23 — *"Search me, O God, and know my heart! Try me and know my thoughts!"*; the "disquieting thoughts" sense throughout. Anxiety is the fear that **thinks** (worried rumination).
- **c1 (Reverent fear)** — co-operates with cognition via compound: *ya.da* "to know" 6, *cha.kham* "wise" 3 — felt as affect but **operating with knowing**.
- **c2 (Fear/afraid)** — strong cognitive co-operation: *ya.da* "to know" 7, *da.at* "knowledge" 6, *bi.nah* "understanding" 3.
- **c3, c5, c6, c7** — lighter cognitive co-terms (know/understand/remember).
- **c4, c8, c10, c11** — minimal/no cognitive co-operation (c8's "thoughts" is the trigger-content, not discernment). Recorded.

## T3.2.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?

**Finding (inferential, data-directioned).**

- **c9** — anxiety **engages and arguably impairs** cognition: the careworn heart is **weighed down** (Pro 12:25), the thoughts churn — cognition is captured by worry rather than enabled. (Inferential.)
- **c1** — reverent fear **operates with** sound cognition (knowing, wisdom) — neither impairs nor bypasses; the "fear of the LORD" sits alongside knowledge. (Inferential, supported by compound co-terms.)
- **c2** — ordinary fear can **bypass** deliberate cognition (the fast, immediate alarm) yet also co-occurs with knowledge co-terms — mixed; recorded as not cleanly resolved in the fields.
- Others — **silent**.

## T3.2.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** Cognition-engagement separates the **interior, ruminative fear (c9)** and the **knowing reverence (c1)** from the **acute, pre-cognitive fears** (c4 terror, c8 alarm). It reveals that fear is **not a single cognitive kind**: anxiety is thought-laden; terror/alarm are immediate and pre-reflective; reverent fear is knowing without being driven by threat-cognition. Recorded.

# T3.3 · Memory

## T3.3.1 — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — and if so, how?

**Finding.** Memory is **never faculty-marked**, but co-operates by compound — most notably in **reverent fear**:

- **c1 (Reverent fear)** — *za.khar* "to remember" 4: reverent fear **operates with remembering** (recalling God's acts/commands) — C1: "felt as affect but operating with knowing and remembering."
- **c2** *za.khar* 3; **c3, c4, c5, c6, c7** — single remember co-terms each.
- **c8, c9, c10, c11** — **silent** on memory.

## T3.3.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?

**Finding (inferential).** Where it appears (chiefly c1), reverent fear **operates alongside** memory (remembering God) rather than enabling or impairing it — the two co-function. No characteristic shows fear impairing or enabling memory in the fields. For c2–c7 the remember co-terms are contextual, not a faculty relation. **Largely silent / inferential** for c1.

## T3.3.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The faint memory-coupling, concentrated in **c1**, reveals reverent fear as a **recollective** disposition — it is sustained partly by remembering God's character and acts (a forming, not merely reactive, orientation). Its near-total absence elsewhere confirms the threat-fears as **present-focused**, not memory-dependent. Recorded; the strongest signal (c1) labelled inferential pending verse-literary support.

# T3.4 · Affect

## T3.4.1 — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **Yes — universally and constitutively. Affect is engaged in all eleven characteristics (973 of 975 records; the 2 non-affect are field gaps in c8/c10).** Fear *is* an affective phenomenon in every register:

- **c2, c3, c5, c6, c7, c11** — **affect only** (single-faculty): these are constitutively and purely affective.
- **c1, c4, c9** — affect with a blend (perception / perception / cognition) but **affect-dominant**.
- **c8, c10** — affect on all but one record (field gap).

## T3.4.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?

**Finding.** The framing inverts here: fear does not *engage* affect as an external faculty — **it is affect**. So the question reads: each characteristic is a **mode of the affective faculty itself**. None bypasses affect; each **is** a distinct affective state (reverence, fright, dread, terror, trembling-feeling, horror, dismay, alarm, anxiety, astonishment, timidity). Recorded as constitutive identity rather than engagement.

## T3.4.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The **uniform, constitutive affect-engagement is the cluster's defining faculty fact**: M01 is, at root, an **affective** family. What differentiates the eleven is **not** the faculty (always affect) but **object, valence, type, constitutional location, and the secondary blend** — which is precisely why the `faculty` field "does not help differentiate" (lexical-extract review §5.4) and the differentiation had to come from valence/object/type. This is a methodologically important confirmation. Recorded for all eleven.

# T3.5 · Creativity

## T3.5.1 — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **No — silent across all eleven characteristics.** The creative/imaginative faculty is never engaged by any fear characteristic in the lexicon (c1–c11 all silent). *Adjacent note (not engagement):* alarm (c8) is triggered by **dreams/visions** (Daniel), which involve imagination's content, but the faculty engaged is affect, not creativity. Recorded as cluster-wide silence.

## T3.5.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity in the person?

**Finding.** **Not evidenced for any characteristic — silent (all eleven).** No enable/deepen/bypass/impair relation to creativity is recorded.

## T3.5.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The uniform non-engagement reveals fear as **not a generative/originating faculty** but a **responsive affective** one — it reacts (to threat, to God, to the overwhelming), it does not originate. The single adjacency (alarm at dream-content, c8) is the limit case and concerns the *trigger*, not a creative function. Recorded for all eleven as silence.

# T3.6 · Volition

## T3.6.1 — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

**Finding.** Volition is **never faculty-marked**, with faint compound signals in **reverent fear** only:

- **c1 (Reverent fear)** — *ba.char* "to choose" 1, *thelō* "to will/desire" 1; and the **obedience fruit** (withholding the son, keeping commandments) implies a **volitional outworking** — reverent fear issues in chosen obedience. (Inferential; C1 noted "the obedience fruit implies a volitional outworking.")
- **c2** — the **"fear not" command** addresses the will (fear is to be refused) — an indirect volitional engagement.
- **c11 (Cowardice)** — by contrast, is the **failure** of the volition to act in faith (rebuked as "little faith"); its remedy is the Spirit of "self-control" (a volitional capacity).
- **c3, c4, c5, c6, c7, c8, c9, c10** — **silent** on volition.

## T3.6.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition — including capacity, interaction, and constraints?

**Finding (inferential).**

- **c1** — reverent fear **directs/enables** the will toward obedience (the volitional fruit).
- **c4 (Terror)** — **impairs/overrides** volition: it **freezes agency** ("still as a stone," Exo 15:16) — the limit case where fear suspends the will. (Inferential, strongly supported by the paralysis immediate-response.)
- **c11** — represents volition **failing** (cowardice = the will not acting in faith).
- **c2** — the "fear not" address implies fear can **constrain** the will (hence the command to resist it).
- Others — **silent**.

## T3.6.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** Volition-engagement maps onto the **moral axis**: the morally-weighted fears (reverent c1, cowardice c11) engage the will (toward obedience or in its failure), while the **overwhelming-state fears** (terror c4) **suspend** it. The neutral states (c3/c6/c9) engage volition not at all. This reveals fear's relation to choice as **register-dependent**: cultivated reverence directs the will; terror overrides it; ordinary fear is commanded to be resisted by it. Recorded; the strongest claims labelled inferential.

# T3.7 · Agency

## T3.7.1 — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **Faculty-silent across all eleven.** Agency is never the engaged faculty. *Adjacent (not faculty-engagement):* reverent fear (c1) **produces action** (obedience, worship — `type` action 123), and terror (c4) **removes** the capacity to act ("still as a stone"). These are effects on action, captured under `type`/`immediate_response`, not engagement of an agency faculty. Recorded as cluster-wide faculty silence with the action cross-reference.

## T3.7.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?

**Finding (inferential, from type/immediate_response not the faculty field).**

- **c1** — **enables** agency toward obedience (action-typed; acts of obedience flow from it).
- **c4 (Terror)** — **impairs/abolishes** agency (paralysis, Exo 15:16); **c7 (Dismay)** — **impairs** agency (knees give way, composure fails); **c8 (Alarm)** — **impairs** (limbs give way, Dan 5:6).
- **c2** — can **impair** agency (flight/avoidance) or, when resisted, leave it intact.
- **c3, c5, c6, c9, c10, c11** — **silent / not evidenced** on agency.

## T3.7.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** Though no fear engages an agency *faculty*, the **effect on agency is a real divider**: reverent fear **mobilises** action; the overwhelming/collapse fears (terror, dismay, alarm) **disable** it (freezing, knees failing). This reveals fear's relation to action as the **inverse of its relation to God**: rightly-ordered (reverent) fear empowers obedient action; disordered/overwhelming fear paralyses. Recorded as inferential (drawn from type/immediate_response, the faculty field being silent).

# T3.8 · Moral Evaluation

## T3.8.1 — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — assessing against a standard of right, wrong, good, true — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **Faculty-silent across all eleven**, but morally engaged **through the `valence` field and the verses** in two characteristics:

- **c1 (Reverent fear)** — morally engaged: it is the **commanded/righteous** fear (valence commanded 52 / righteous 110), the rightly-ordered response to God; its absence/misdirection is morally faulted (sinful/forbidden).
- **c11 (Cowardice)** — morally engaged as the **negative pole**: the **sinful** fear (valence sinful 4), named a fault and listed among the disqualified (Rev 21:8).
- **c2** — partial: the "forbidden" framing morally evaluates ordinary fear as a failure to trust.
- **c3, c4, c5, c6, c7, c8, c9, c10** — **morally neutral-dominant** (valence neutral); the moral-evaluation faculty is **not engaged**. Recorded.

## T3.8.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?

**Finding (inferential).** **c1** reverent fear is itself the **ground of right moral orientation** (the fear of the LORD as the posture from which obedience flows) — it **deepens** moral seriousness. **c11** cowardice represents a **failure** of moral nerve. The neutral-state fears neither engage nor impair moral evaluation. Largely **inferential** (drawn from valence + verse, not a faculty field); silence noted for the neutral characteristics.

## T3.8.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** Moral-evaluation engagement tracks **exactly** the T0.2 design/fallen/vice split: only the morally-weighted fears (reverent c1, ordinary-as-forbidden c2, cowardice c11) engage moral evaluation; the **state/expression fears are morally inert** (neutral). This confirms that the cluster's moral content is concentrated in its **Godward** and **culpable** poles, not in its bodily/state members. Recorded.

**Finding.** **Almost entirely silent — one compound trace, in reverent fear:**

- **c1 (Reverent fear)** — a single conscience co-term: *suneidesis* "conscience" (1) — reverent fear appearing alongside conscience. The faculty is not marked; the co-occurrence is the only signal.

## T3.9.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?

**Finding.** **Not evidenced.** The single c1 *suneidesis* co-term does not establish a direction (enable/impair). All eleven recorded as **silent** on a conscience relation. *(Inferential adjacency only: reverent fear before God is conceptually near conviction, but the lexicon does not carry it — routed to T7.2 if pursued.)*

## T3.9.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The near-total silence reveals that, in this lexicon, fear is **not primarily a conscience phenomenon** — it is affect (T3.4), not the inner witness of guilt. The lone c1 trace hints that **reverent** fear alone touches the conscience's territory, but the evidence is too thin to build on. Recorded as cluster-wide silence with the single noted trace.

# T3.10 · Conscientiousness

## T3.10.1 — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **Faculty-silent across all eleven.** No characteristic engages "conscientiousness" as such. *Adjacent (inferential):* **reverent fear (c1)** comes closest — it integrates **moral awareness** (commanded/righteous valence), **volition** (chosen obedience), and **action** (keeping commandments) — i.e. the *components* of conscientiousness co-occur in c1, though the integrated faculty is not named. Recorded as silence with the c1 adjacency noted inferential.

## T3.10.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?

**Finding (inferential).** **c1** — reverent fear plausibly **deepens** conscientiousness (it is the disposition from which faithful, obedient living flows). All other characteristics — **silent / not evidenced**. Labelled inferential (no faculty field).

## T3.10.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** Only **reverent fear** has the ingredients of conscientiousness; the rest do not. This reinforces the T0/T3.8 result: the cluster's **formative, character-building capacity is concentrated in reverent fear**, while the threat/state fears are reactive and non-formative. Recorded.

# T3.11 · Relational Capacity

## T3.11.1 — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another — and if so, how?

**Finding.** **Faculty-silent across all eleven** (relational capacity is never the engaged faculty). **However**, fear is **intensely relational at the object/interface level** (T4): every characteristic has an *object* (God, person, situation), and reverent fear in particular is constitutively Godward. The relational content lives in **T4 (Relational Interfaces)**, not in a T3 faculty.

- All eleven — **faculty-silent**; relational engagement cross-referenced to **T4**.

## T3.11.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?

**Finding (inferential).**

- **c1** — reverent fear **deepens** the Godward relation (it is the right posture of connection with God) and **orders** human relations (reverence of parents, Lev 19:3). Inferential.
- **c2** — ordinary fear of others can **impair** connection (fear of enemies/crowds creates relational distance), and the "fear not" reassurance **restores** it.
- **c11** — cowardice **impairs** faithful relational standing (failure before Christ).
- **c3, c4, c5, c6, c7, c8, c9, c10** — **silent / not evidenced** at the faculty level (object-relations handled in T4).

## T3.11.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**Finding.** The faculty-silence alongside the **rich object-relations** (T4) reveals an important distinction: fear is **relationally directed** (it always has an object) without engaging a distinct relational *faculty* — its relational character is a matter of **interface and object**, not of a constitutional connective capacity. This is why the programme captures fear's relationality under T4, not T3. Recorded for all eleven, with the c1/c2/c11 inferential directions noted.
