## 0. Scope and how the occurrence set was selected

This characteristic is anchored on the six reverence-bearing terms assigned to it in the characteristic list: **H3372H** "to fear: revere" (107), **H3373** "afraid / fearing, reverent" (64), **H4172A** "fear, reverence, terror" (11), **H4172B** (11), **H6345** "dread, fear, awe, religious awe" (1), **G6015** "fear, reverence, awe" (1) — **195 raw occurrences**.

**Integrity caveat (validation item):** H4172A and H4172B carry the **identical 11 verses** (Deu 4:34; 11:25; 26:8; 34:12; Gen 9:2; Isa 8:12, 8:13; Jer 32:21; Mal 1:6; 2:5; Psa 76:11) with identical lexical profiles. This is a sub-entry double-count. Deduplicated, the characteristic holds **~184 distinct occurrences**. Figures below are on the raw 195 unless noted; the duplication does not change any proportion materially.

The set is **almost entirely Old Testament Hebrew** (194 Hebrew / 1 Greek). This characteristic, as anchored, is an OT-weighted reading; the Greek reverence vocabulary sits mostly under other terms (e.g. reverent uses of G5401) not captured by these anchors — a boundary point for §5.

## 1. Observation — what the lexical fields show

**Moral framing is overwhelmingly positive.** `valence`: righteous 104, commanded 46, neutral 31, sinful 10, forbidden 4. So **150 of 195 (77%)** are framed as righteous or commanded. This is the field that most distinguishes the characteristic.

**The object, when named, is God.** `object_type`: God 95, person 21, abstract/thing/spiritual-being 6, none 73. Of the 122 occurrences with a named object, **95 (78%) are God**. The resolved `object` values bear this out: "Lord" 37, "God" 17, "name" 5, "sanctuary" 2. `divine_involvement` records God as the **object** of the state in 98 occurrences (agent 4, giver 2) — i.e. this is fear *of* God, not fear *caused by* God.

**It is reported of others and enjoined, rarely owned in the first person.** `experiencer`: other 52, other (addressed) 40, self 7, none 96. First-person reverent fear is rare in the data (7).

**It is as much a settled trait as an act.** `type`: action 123, **quality 48**, status 24. Nearly a quarter are "quality" — reverent fear as a characteristic of a person ("God-fearing"), not only an event.

**Its causes are theological.** Of the resolved `cause` values, the arousers are God's attributes and acts: "God's greatness, might, and awesomeness," "the awesome works the Lord will do," "the Lord's mighty hand," "great things God has done," "being fearfully and wonderfully made," "the great and awesome day of the Lord," and — notably — **"God's forgiveness."** A minority name non-divine causes (Esau's threat, the king of Babylon, nearness to Philistines, "other gods") — these align with the sinful/forbidden cases.

**Seat, where given, is the heart.** `location` is mostly absent (181); where present: heart 9, soul 3, plus blends. **`faculty` is "affect" on all 195** — uniform, so it does not differentiate within the characteristic.

**Grounding verses (verse text, as in the data):**
- *Commanded, object God* — Lev 19:14: "…but you shall fear your God: I am the Lord." (valence=commanded, object_type=God)
- *Reverent fear as a proven trait* — Gen 22:12: "…now I know that you fear God, seeing you have not withheld your son…" (type=quality; cause="not withholding his only son")
- *Cause = God's forgiveness* — Psa 130:4: "But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared." (cause="God's forgiveness")
- *God as awesome* — Exo 15:11: "…majestic in holiness, awesome in glorious deeds, doing wonders?" (sense=awesome, object_type=God)
- *Sinful — the absence condemned* — Exo 9:30: "…I know that you do not yet fear the Lord God." (valence=sinful)
- *Forbidden — reverent fear misdirected* — Judg 6:10: "…you shall not fear the gods of the Amorites…" (valence=forbidden, object_type=spiritual-being)

## 2. Interpretation (held to the data)

- **Reverent fear is defined less by its vocabulary than by its direction and framing.** The anchor verbs are the same ones used elsewhere for ordinary fright (H3372 carries both); what makes an occurrence *reverent* in the data is the combination object=God + valence=righteous/commanded. The characteristic is real, but its boundary is drawn by `object_type`/`valence`, not by the lemma alone.
- **It behaves as a disposition, not merely an emotion.** The high "quality" share (48) and the "God-fearing" senses suggest the data treats this as a standing orientation of the person toward God — closer to a character trait than a passing affect.
- **The same capacity can be misdirected.** The 10 sinful and 4 forbidden cases use the reverence-capable verb for fear pointed the wrong way — at other gods (Judg 6:10) or absent where it should be present (Exo 9:30). The data thus presents reverent fear as a faculty that is *right when directed at God and wrong when withheld or redirected* — a normative shape, carried by `valence`.
- **An unusual arouser stands out:** in Psa 130:4 the cause of fear is God's *forgiveness*, not his threat. The data does not generalise this, but it is a datum that reverent fear is not reducible to dread of punishment.

## 3. Reflection (significance and affected perspectives)

- **This is where reading communities diverge most.** A tradition that hears "fear of God" as filial reverence and one that hears it as dread of judgement will both find support in this set — reverence senses (awe, awesome, God-fearing) sit beside terror senses (terror 8, dread 2) under the same anchor terms. The `valence` field has, in effect, already taken a position (mostly righteous/commanded); that is useful, but it is an automated moral reading and should be weighed, not assumed, where a tradition would contest it.
- **The "quality" finding matters for the inner-being question.** If reverent fear is a settled orientation rather than an episodic feeling, it speaks to the *character* of the inner being before God, not just its reactions — which is closer to the programme's governing question than the acute-fear characteristics will be.
- **The OT weighting is a real limit on scope.** As anchored, this characteristic is an OT portrait. Any claim about reverent fear "in Scripture" would need the Greek reverence vocabulary folded in (see §5).

## 4. Where the data is thin / what would help

- `cause` is resolved on only a minority of the 195; the theological-arouser pattern is real but rests on perhaps two dozen resolved cases.
- `location` is almost always absent — the heart/soul seat is suggestive (12 cases) but not a pattern.
- The H4172A/B duplication should be resolved at source before counts are published.
