## Summary
Dismay is the **collapse of inner composure** — being **shattered / broken / unnerved** by what one faces. A large OT characteristic (74) whose grammar is telling: predominantly **Niphal passive** (37) — dismay is something one *is made* (broken), not chiefly something one does. It is the fear most paired with **shame** (compound "be ashamed" 9), governed by the standing **"fear not, neither be dismayed"** formula (valence forbidden 7), and frequently **inflicted by God** on enemies (agent 12). Its opposite is **rest/composure**.

## Reflection
Dismay names the **breaking of the inner being's strength** — composure shattered. Its passive grammar (Niphal) is the lexical signature: one is *unnerved*, broken, more than one *acts*. The strong shame-pairing suggests dismay and shame travel together (the shattering and the disgrace). As an instrument of divine judgement (agent 12) it joins dread/terror as a fear God inflicts; as a forbidden state ("be not dismayed") it joins ordinary fear as a collapse commanded away. It sits between trembling (the body shaken) and despair, and the precise boundary is a T6 question.
