## T1 — Definition (from the lexicon)

**Name.** Carried by H3372 "to fear: revere" (the reverence sub-sense H3372H, 107), H3373 "fearing, reverent, afraid" (64), H4172 "fear, reverence, terror" (A/B, 11 — see integrity note), H6345 "dread, fear, awe, religious awe" (1), and one Greek term G6015 "fear, reverence, awe" (1). The root range itself fuses three notions — *fear*, *reverence*, *awe* — within a single lemma. The name therefore signals at the definitional level that this is not a distinct word for "reverence" set apart from "fear": it is the fear vocabulary used in a reverent register.

**Kind (working description from data).** An **affect-seated** state (`faculty`=affect on all 195) that is **God-directed** (`object_type`=God on 95 of 122 named; `divine_involvement`=object on 98) and **morally framed as right or required** (`valence` righteous 104 / commanded 46 = 77%). It presents as much as a **settled trait as an act** (`type` quality 48 alongside action 123). Against the framework's reference dimension vocabulary, it reads closest to a **Relational Disposition** with **Moral-Character** weight and an affective core — but, as the framework directs, Kind is not reducible to a dimension label; the data's own description is: *a God-directed, morally-required disposition felt as affect and held as a standing orientation.*

**Boundary (from the lexicon's own contrast).** The data marks the structural opposite from within the same vocabulary: 4 occurrences `valence`=forbidden and 10 `valence`=sinful. These are reverent-fear *misdirected* (fear of other gods — Judg 6:10) or *withheld where owed* (Exo 9:30, "you do not yet fear the Lord God"). So the lexical boundary of reverent fear is not "no emotion" but **fear pointed at the wrong object, or absent toward the right one.**

## T5 — Relational Interfaces (rich — the strongest tier)

**Divine interface.** This is where the characteristic lives. `object`="Lord" 37, "God" 17, "name" 5; `object_type`=God 95; `divine_involvement`=**object 98** (agent only 4). Direction is **human → God** (the human fears God), not God → human. The resolved `cause` values name the arousers as God's attributes and acts — "God's greatness, might, and awesomeness," "the awesome works the Lord will do," "great things God has done," "the great and awesome day of the Lord," and notably **"God's forgiveness"** (Psa 130:4, "that you may be feared"). The interface is also **liturgical/covenantal** in its co-vocabulary (T7): name, glory, covenant, swearing, serving.

**Human interface.** `object_type`=person on 21. The same reverence verb is **commanded toward parents** — Lev 19:3, "Every one of you shall revere his mother and his father" (`valence`=commanded). It also covers **fear of a threatening person** — Gen 32:11, "I fear him [Esau]" (`valence`=neutral) — and the **dread of humans felt by creatures** — Gen 9:2, "The fear of you and the dread of you shall be upon every beast." So at the human interface the vocabulary spans honouring reverence (parents) and ordinary apprehension (an enemy).

**Spiritual-beings interface.** The framework flags this interface as a "confirmed gap" in the catalogue — but the lexicon **does** evidence it here, entirely negatively: `object_type`=spiritual-being on the "other gods" cases, all condemned — Judg 6:10 "you shall not fear the gods of the Amorites" (forbidden); 2Ki 17:7, feared other gods (sinful). The lexical witness is that reverent fear directed at other spiritual beings is uniformly forbidden.

## 8. Scope evidence and resolutions (v1.2)

**8.1 — Scoping decision (was 7.1): evidence on the table, decision with the researcher.**
A convergence test between the two candidate definitions shows they diverge: the **lemma-anchor** set (195) and the **field-defined** set — object=God AND valence∈{righteous,commanded} (123) — overlap on only **81**.
- Lemma anchor **over-includes 114** occurrences not reverent-of-God in the fields (neutral 31, righteous-but-object-not-God 55, commanded-but-object-not-God 14, sinful 10, forbidden 4).
- Lemma anchor **misses 42** reverent-of-God occurrences carried by other terms — including **all 10 Greek** (G5399 8, G5401 2) and the danger sub-entry H3372G used reverently (10).
- Strict field definition is too narrow: requiring object=God drops *commanded reverence of parents* (Lev 19:3) and righteous fear where the object isn't tagged God.

**Finding the evidence supports:** neither pure definition is adequate. `valence` (righteous/commanded) is the better discriminator because it generalises across all terms and both Testaments; `object` (God-dominant, not exclusive) qualifies rather than gates. Reverent fear is a *register marked by valence*, not a property of particular lemmas (the same root H3372 sits on both sides). **Decision deferred to the researcher.**
