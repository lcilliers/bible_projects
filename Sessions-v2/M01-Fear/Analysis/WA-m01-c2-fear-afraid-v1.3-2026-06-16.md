## Summary

Fear / being afraid is the **episodic, perceived, magnitude-bearing affect** that Scripture most often **forbids** when its object is a circumstance or person, and **commends** only when its object is God. It is felt and reacted to (dismay, trembling, flight), comes *upon* a person from outside, and its partnered opposite in the data is **courage / strength** ("be strong, do not fear"). It is the structural counterpart to Characteristic 1: where reverent fear is a commanded standing orientation toward God, ordinary fear is a forbidden spiking reaction to threat.

## Reflection (significance and affected perspectives)

- **The most-forbidden fear is the pastoral centre of gravity.** That ordinary fear's largest single moral framing is *forbidden* (107), and that its experiencer profile is dominated by *address* (137), means the data presents this characteristic chiefly as something **spoken against to a hearer** — the "do not be afraid" of reassurance. Different reading communities will weight this differently: a comfort/gospel reading hears grace ("fear not"); a reverence reading notices that the *same* vocabulary is *commanded* when its object is God. The data holds both, and the discriminator is the object, not the word.
- **The body is more present than in reverent fear.** Flesh as a seat (4), trembling as a co-state (14), and the "came/fell upon" idiom locate ordinary fear partly in the body — relevant to the spirit-soul-body question and to any later T6 work on the bodily deposit of chronic fear.
- **Courage as the structural opposite** (not calm) is a substantive lexical finding: the data does not pair fear with serenity but with **strength** — fear is overcome by being strengthened, not by being soothed.

I would hold off any application claim beyond the lexical: whether "fear not" functions as comfort, command, or both in a given verse is a verse-literary (T8.2) question the lexicon does not settle.
