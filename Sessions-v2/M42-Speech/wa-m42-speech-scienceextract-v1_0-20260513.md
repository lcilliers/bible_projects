## Science sections

### Section 1 — Vocal expression of emotion

**Primary field:** Affective science / psychoacoustics

**Glosses covered:**
cry (rin.nah), lamentation (a.niy.yah), moaning (he.geh), outcry (ze.a.qah), outcry (tse.va.chah), roaring (na.ham), roaring (she.a.gah), shout (hed), shout (he.dad), to cry (krazō), to cry aloud (tsa.vach), to cry out (za.aq), to groan (ste.na.zo), to groan (na.aq), to groan (na.ham), to roar (sha.ag)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Scherer's vocal emotion research (*Speech Communication*, 2003) identifies acoustic features of emotional vocalisations — pitch, intensity, rate, voice quality — that signal emotional states reliably across cultures. Juslin and Laukka's meta-analysis (*Psychological Bulletin*, 2003) confirms that vocal expression of emotion is decoded accurately above chance across cultures, establishing a cross-cultural vocal emotion system. The cry of distress (ze.aq, tsa.vach, krazō) functions as a social distress signal — it communicates need, demands response, and activates caregiving behaviour in observers. Lament as a cultural form (Rosenblatt's cross-cultural mourning research) addresses the communal function of structured vocal grief expression. The roaring vocabulary (sha.ag, na.ham) maps onto the vocal expression of intense negative affect — anger, grief, or both.

**Emergence — what causes these vocalisations to arise:**
Emotional vocalisations arise when internal affective states exceed the capacity for silent containment — they are the overflow of inner experience into bodily expression. The cry of distress specifically arises in situations of genuine need and threat where the person is unable to self-rescue — it is functionally a call for help. Groaning (stenazō, na.aq) arises from sustained distress that is not acute enough for outcry but too sustained for silence. Roaring arises from the most intense states — grief, rage, or the overwhelming weight of emotion.

**Reduction and absence — what causes emotional vocalisation to diminish:**
Emotional vocalisation diminishes through: the resolution of the underlying emotional state; social contexts that suppress emotional expression; the exhaustion of the expressive capacity through prolonged distress; and the internalisation of norms against emotional expression. Silence can represent either the resolution of distress (peace and rest — M33 Section 2) or its suppression (Section 3 below).

**Location — where emotional vocalisation is seated:**
These vocalisations are simultaneously inner-being expressions (the inner state that produces them) and outer-world events (the sound produced). For the programme's purposes, the relevant location is the inner state — the grief, distress, or rage that produces the cry. The cry itself is the evidence of the inner state's intensity. Physiologically, vocal distress signals are produced by autonomic arousal interacting with the vocal apparatus; they are not primarily deliberate but are released by the inner state.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Lament forms and their social acceptability are entirely culturally shaped — cultures vary dramatically in whether open vocal grief expression is normative or stigmatised. *Social:* The presence of a receptive community enables emotional vocalisation; the absence of one suppresses it (Psalm 137 — 'how shall we sing the LORD's song in a strange land?').

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Vocal emotion research provides direct coverage.

### Section 2 — Language, inner speech, and verbal thought

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / linguistics

**Glosses covered:**
to mutter (ha.gah), voice (qal), voice/sound: noise (fōnē), voice: sound (qal), answer (ma.a.neh), song (za.mir), song (zim.rat), song (ron)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Vygotsky's inner speech research (*Thought and Language*, 1934) established that language is not only a communicative tool but a cognitive one — inner speech (the internalisation of external language) is a primary medium of thought. Children's private speech (thinking aloud) gradually becomes internalised as inner speech in adulthood. Alderson-Day and Fernyhough's research on the varieties of inner speech (*Psychological Bulletin*, 2015) shows that inner speech varies in form — from condensed and abbreviated to fully dialogic, often involving imagined interlocutors. Muttering/meditation (ha.gah — used of the meditation of the righteous and of the growling of animals) spans the spectrum from inner verbal rehearsal to sub-vocal speech to full vocalisation. The voice (qal, fōnē) terms in this section describe sound as a physical medium of communication rather than as inner experience.

**Emergence — what causes inner speech to arise:**
Inner speech arises as the internalisation of social speech — it develops in childhood as external dialogue is progressively internalised. It is activated whenever the person engages in verbal thought, self-regulation, planning, or the rehearsal of upcoming social interactions. Meditation as a form of inner speech (ha.gah — to meditate, mutter, muse) involves the sustained verbal rehearsal of significant content — a cognitive practice that shapes attention and orientation over time.

**Location — where inner speech is seated:**
Neurologically, inner speech activates the same language networks as external speech but with reduced motor output. It is primarily cortical — a property of the language-using, self-reflecting mind. For the programme's framework, inner speech as meditation represents a significant inner-being practice — the deliberate use of verbal thought to orient and form the inner being.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Inner speech research is a developed field. The song vocabulary in this section is primarily social/musical rather than inner-speech, linking more naturally to M22 (Praise).

### Section 3 — Silence as inner state

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / contemplative psychology

**Glosses covered:**
be silent (sigaō), be silent (cha.shah), to slander (katalaleō), to slander (la.shan)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Psychology of silence (Ephratt — 'The Functions of Silence'; Jaworski — *The Power of Silence*) addresses the different functions of silence: communicative silence (saying something by not speaking), contemplative silence (inner stillness), coercive silence (being silenced by others), and pathological silence (emotional withdrawal). Contemplative psychology addresses silence as a practice that reduces cognitive noise and increases receptivity — supported by neuroscience (brain default mode network activity in silence and its role in integration and insight). Slander (katalaleō, la.shan) is the negative pole of the cluster — speech that injures rather than builds, which arises from the inner-being orientation of hostility and contempt (M06).

**Emergence — what causes silence to arise:**
Contemplative silence arises from deliberate practice — the intentional reduction of verbal output in order to increase inner attentiveness. Communicative silence arises from the assessment that speech would be harmful, premature, or unnecessary. Coerced silence arises from the exercise of power by others — being muzzled or suppressed. The slander terms in this section arise from a hostile inner orientation finding expression in destructive speech.

**Location — where silence is seated:**
Contemplative silence is an inner state of reduced verbal activity and increased receptivity — it is experienced as an inward orientation of stillness and openness. It is located in the whole person's orientation rather than in any single faculty.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Contemplative psychology of silence is a developing rather than established field.

### Section 4 — Prophetic speech and proclamation

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion

**Glosses covered:**
to prophesy (profēteuō), to prophesy (na.va), to sing (za.mar), to sing (ra.nan), to chant (qi.nen)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Psychology of religion addresses prophetic speech and glossolalia (Malony and Lovekin — *Glossolalia*, 1985; Spanos and Hewitt). The phenomenology of prophetic experience has been addressed by Aune (*Prophecy in Early Christianity*) and sociologically by Weber (*Economy and Society* — charismatic authority). The psychological state associated with prophetic speech — including altered states of consciousness in some traditions — is addressed through psychology of religious experience research (James, Hood).

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Prophetic speech as a theological category substantially exceeds psychology of religion's descriptive scope.

## Outside-science inventory

- **Prophetic speech as divine communication** — prophecy as the word of God spoken through a human agent is a theological category. Science addresses the psychological states of the prophet; the ontological claim (that the words are God's) is outside science.
- **The cultic and liturgical speech forms** (formal lamentation, chanting) have sociology of religion coverage but limited psychology coverage.
- **Slander (katalaleō, la.shan)** in its moral and covenantal register — speaking against God's people as a form of covenant violation — is a theological category beyond the psychology of harmful communication.

**Observation:** M42 sits systematically at the inner-being / outer-expression boundary. Most of its vocabulary describes speech acts rather than inner states. Session B will need to attend carefully to whether each verse's inner-being content is carried by the speech term itself (the inner state that produces it) or by surrounding vocabulary. The programme's relevance filter operates at term level — the speech terms are relevant only when they carry inner-being content directly.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Affective science and psychoacoustics (Section 1); cognitive psychology of inner speech (Section 2); psychology of religion (Section 4).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Vocal emotion research illuminates why the cluster's cry and lament vocabulary is so precise — different emotional states produce acoustically distinct vocalisations that carry inner-state information. Inner speech research illuminates meditation (ha.gah) as a cognitive inner-being practice — the deliberate use of verbal thought to shape inner orientation. The lament function as a social distress signal illuminates why the biblical lament form is simultaneously personal and communal.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Most of the cluster's content is at the inner-being / outer-expression boundary — the science addresses the expression side more readily than the inner state itself. The prophetic dimension is the deepest divergence.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The cluster's most significant Session B analytical challenge is the boundary question — which speech terms carry inner-being content and which are purely expressive acts. The ha.gah (meditate/mutter) vocabulary is the clearest inner-being term; the cry and outcry vocabulary indexes the inner state without itself being the state.
