## Science sections

### Section 1 — Planning, goal-setting, and purposive action

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
counsel (e.tsah), counsel (mo.e.tsah), counsel (tach.bu.lah), plan (boulē), plan (boulēma), plan (za.mam), purpose (prothesis), purpose (fronēma), beginning (archē), beginning (te.chil.lah), cause (div.rah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Goal-setting theory (Locke and Latham, *A Theory of Goal Setting and Task Performance*, 1990) is among the most extensively validated theories in organisational psychology — specific, challenging goals consistently outperform vague or easy goals in motivating performance. Gollwitzer's implementation intention research demonstrates that forming specific if-then plans (when X occurs, I will do Y) dramatically increases goal follow-through. Prospective cognition research (Szpunar and colleagues) examines future-oriented mental simulation — the cognitive capacity to model not-yet-existing states that underlies all planning. The Hebrew tach.bu.lah (counsel, steering — from the root for ropes used to steer a ship) carries a navigational metaphor for planning: skilled direction toward a goal.

**Emergence — what causes planning and purposive action to arise:**
Planning arises from: the identification of a valued goal that is not yet achieved; the cognitive capacity to represent the future state and the path to it; the motivation to invest effort in pursuit; and the executive function capacity to translate intention into sustained, sequenced action. Purpose (prothesis — setting before oneself; fronēma — mind-set, orientation) names the more comprehensive orientation of the whole person toward their telos, of which specific plans are expressions.

**Reduction and absence — what causes planning to fail:**
Planning fails through: the absence of clear values that would motivate goal pursuit; executive function deficits that prevent sustained deliberate action; the failure to form implementation intentions (wanting to do something without specifying when, where, and how); and — from the biblical account — planning that excludes God's sovereignty from its framework (James 4:13–15).

**Location — where planning is seated:**
Primarily prefrontal and cognitive — planning is the paradigmatic executive function, dependent on the prefrontal cortex's capacity for working memory, prospective representation, and inhibitory control. Purpose (fronēma) extends beyond this to the whole-person orientation — the values and identity that determine what goals are worth pursuing.

**External influences:**
*Mentorship and counsel:* The cluster's counsel vocabulary (e.tsah, tach.bu.lah) specifically names the social dimension of planning — the wisdom of the counsellor who helps another navigate toward their goal. The proverb "plans fail for lack of counsel" (Proverbs 15:22) reflects the research finding that social input improves decision quality. *Cultural:* Cultures with strong planning and future-orientation (low present-hedonism, high future-orientation — Zimbardo and Boyd's time perspective research) produce more systematic planners. *Developmental:* Goal-setting capacity develops through adolescence and early adulthood with the maturation of the prefrontal cortex.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Goal-setting and planning research are well-developed fields.

### Section 2 — Counsel, advice, and deliberative social reasoning

**Primary field:** Social psychology / organisational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
counsel/council (sumboulion), counsellor (paraklētos), counselor (sumboulos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Group decision-making research (Janis — groupthink, 1972; Surowiecki — wisdom of crowds, 2004) examines how collective deliberation can both enhance and impair judgment quality. Advice-taking research (Yaniv — egocentrism in advice use) documents the tendency to discount advice relative to one's own judgment, even when advisors have superior information. The counsellor relationship in organisational contexts has been extensively studied — mentoring, coaching, and advisory relationships all show consistent positive effects on decision quality and goal achievement.

**Emergence — what causes counsel-seeking to arise:**
Counsel-seeking arises from: the recognition that one's own judgment is insufficient for the decision at hand; the trust in the counsellor's wisdom and benevolence; and the humility to receive information that may challenge one's existing plans. Resistance to counsel (a consistent pattern in the biblical folly literature) arises from overconfidence and pride — the refusal to acknowledge the limits of one's own perspective.

**Reduction and absence — what causes counsel-seeking to diminish:**
Counsel-seeking diminishes with: overconfidence in one's own judgment; the absence of trustworthy advisors; and the pride that treats advice-seeking as weakness. The pathology of advice-ignoring (Yaniv's egocentrism) is pervasive and consistent.

**Location — where counsel is seated:**
Relational — it exists in the between-space of the counsellor-counselled relationship, not in either party alone. It has cognitive products (improved plans, better decisions) but is constituted by the relationship.

**External influences:**
*Access:* Access to wise counsellors is a significant social advantage — the poor and marginalised often lack access to the quality of advisory relationships available to the privileged. *Cultural:* Cultures with strong mentorship traditions and elder-respect transmit counsel more effectively.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Group decision-making and advice research provide frameworks; the gloss count is small.

### Section 3 — Anticipation, expectation, and future orientation

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology — prospective cognition

**Glosses covered:**
to await (qa.vah), to await (sa.var)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Prospective memory research (Einstein and McDaniel) examines the capacity to remember to do something in the future — the cognitive system that holds future intentions. Future-oriented cognition more broadly (Szpunar and colleagues) examines mental time travel into the future — the simulation of not-yet-existing states. Expectation research (expectancy theory — Vroom) examines how anticipated future outcomes motivate present behaviour. Qa.vah (to await, to hope, to twist together — as of strands into a rope) carries both the waiting and the attachment-to-the-expected dimensions of anticipation.

**Emergence — what causes active awaiting to arise:**
Active anticipation arises from: a valued expectation (something worth waiting for) combined with the belief that the expected outcome will arrive. Qa.vah's root (twisting/binding) suggests that genuine waiting involves an active binding of oneself to the expected outcome — a more engaged stance than passive waiting.

**Reduction and absence:** Anticipation ends when the expected event arrives or is abandoned. Prolonged waiting without arrival is a theme of the lament tradition — the tension of qa.vah held against unanswered waiting.

**Location:** Cognitive and motivational — anticipation is a forward-directed mental state that shapes present behaviour in light of an expected future.

**External influences:** The reliability of past expectation-fulfilment shapes the intensity of present anticipation. Communities that hold shared expectations (covenant community awaiting God's promise) sustain anticipation more durably than individuals in isolation.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Only two glosses; prospective cognition research provides the framework.

## Outside-science inventory

- **purpose (prothesis) in its predestinarian/divine-plan register** — where prothesis names God's eternal purpose (Romans 8:28; Ephesians 1:11), it is a theological category — the sovereign, pre-temporal plan of God for creation and redemption. Science addresses human purposive planning; it does not address divine predestination.
- **counsellor (paraklētos) as the Holy Spirit** — paraklētos as the Paraclete (John 14–16) is a christological and pneumatological category — the Spirit as advocate, helper, and comforter. The human counselling dimension of the term is science-addressable; the divine Paraclete is not.

**Observation:** M17 has modest outside-science content. Human planning, counsel, and anticipation are well covered by science. The theological distinctive is the divine Counsellor who guides, and the divine Plan (prothesis) within which all human planning is embedded.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Cognitive and motivational psychology (planning — Section 1); social and organisational psychology (counsel — Section 2); prospective cognition research (Section 3).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Implementation intention research illuminates why the biblical wisdom literature is specific in its planning counsel — the specificity of if-then planning dramatically increases follow-through. The advice-taking egocentrism research illuminates the biblical pattern of the fool who refuses counsel — Yaniv's finding that people systematically discount others' wisdom is the psychological mechanism underlying the Proverbs' consistent contrast between the wise who seek counsel and the fool who does not.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats planning as a human cognitive capacity optimised for goal achievement. The biblical material embeds human planning within divine sovereignty — the plans of the human heart versus the purpose of God (Proverbs 19:21) — a structure that science cannot account for within its immanent framework.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The group deliberation research (Janis on groupthink; Surowiecki on wisdom of crowds) may surface a dimension of the cluster's council vocabulary — the conditions under which collective counsel enhances or distorts judgment. This may be worth testing in the biblical material's portrayal of royal councils and prophetic counsel.
