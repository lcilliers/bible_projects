## Science sections

### Section 1 — Gratitude expression and social appreciation

**Primary field:** Positive psychology — gratitude research

**Glosses covered:**
thanksgiving (to.dah), to give thanks (ya.dah), blessing (be.ra.khah), praise (ainesis), praise (ainos), praise (epainos), to praise (aineō), to praise (epaineō), worthy (axios)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Emmons and McCullough's gratitude intervention research established that the regular practice of expressing gratitude — both internally and to others — produces sustained wellbeing benefits. Algoe's find-remind-and-bind theory of gratitude (*Social and Personality Psychology Compass*, 2012) proposes that gratitude functions to find good others in one's social environment, remind oneself of their goodness, and bind oneself to them in ongoing relationship. Praise as social appreciation is addressed in organisational psychology — recognition and appreciation are among the strongest motivators in workplace contexts (Buckingham and Clifton). Worthiness assessment (axios) maps onto the psychological literature on moral evaluation and merit attribution.

**Emergence — what causes thanksgiving and praise to arise:**
Gratitude and thanksgiving arise when a benefit is received and attributed to another's intentional benevolent action. The worthy (axios) attribution is central — praise is directed at one appraised as genuinely deserving of it. Communal thanksgiving arises through shared acknowledgement of received benefit and through liturgical practice that structures gratitude expression as a collective act.

**Reduction and absence — what causes thanksgiving to diminish:**
Entitlement (the perception that benefits are owed rather than given), habituation (benefits becoming expected and invisible), and social norms that suppress gratitude expression all reduce thanksgiving. The absence of a perceived benefactor — whether human or divine — removes the relational direction that thanksgiving requires.

**Location — where gratitude and praise are seated:**
Primarily relational and emotional. Gratitude is irreducibly other-directed — it cannot exist without a perceived benefactor. Praise extends this relational orientation into social expression — it is gratitude given voice in community. Physiologically, gratitude practice activates the parasympathetic system and is associated with reduced inflammatory markers. Cognitively, it involves the attribution of benefit to an agent who acted benevolently.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Cultures and communities with strong thanksgiving practices — liturgical, familial, civic — produce more grateful individuals. *Developmental:* Gratitude is modelled and taught; households and communities that practise expressed thanksgiving cultivate it in the next generation. *Circumstances:* The experience of genuine undeserved gift is the primary situational trigger; adversity followed by relief intensifies gratitude.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Gratitude research is one of positive psychology's most developed areas. The cluster's thanksgiving vocabulary maps well onto this literature.

### Section 2 — Awe, wonder, and elevation

**Primary field:** Psychology of awe / positive psychology

**Glosses covered:**
glory (doxa), glory (he.der), glory (ha.dar), glory (ka.vod), glorious (ka.vod), splendor (hod), adornment (ha.da.rah), to honor (ha.dar), good report (eufēmia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Keltner and Haidt's awe research (covered in M01, Section 4) applies here — glory as a category of overwhelming positive regard produces the same awe-signature: self-diminishment, time dilation, prosocial orientation. Haidt's elevation construct (*Daedalus*, 2000) specifically addresses the emotional response to witnessing great virtue or moral beauty — a warm, expansive feeling in the chest, motivation to become better, and prosocial action. Glory vocabulary — particularly ka.vod (weight, heaviness as glory) — carries the weight of impressiveness that awe research addresses.

**Emergence — what causes glory-perception and elevation to arise:**
Glory perception arises when something is encountered as genuinely impressive — possessing weight, beauty, majesty, or moral excellence that exceeds ordinary experience. Elevation arises specifically in response to moral beauty: witnessing another act with exceptional virtue, generosity, or courage. Both are triggered by encountering something whose worth exceeds the observer's ordinary categories.

**Reduction and absence:**
Familiarity and habituation reduce the perception of glory — the extraordinary becomes ordinary through repetition. Cynicism and moral disengagement reduce the capacity for elevation. Narcissism reduces both, by making the self the primary measure of worth.

**Location — where glory-perception is seated:**
Elevation has a distinctive somatic signature — Haidt describes it as a warm, open feeling in the chest, distinct from other positive emotions. Awe more broadly produces physiological responses (goosebumps, tingling, altered time perception). Both are primarily emotional and relational — they are inherently responsive, requiring an encounter with something external.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* What counts as glorious is culturally shaped — beauty standards, standards of excellence, and the perception of moral greatness vary by culture. *Developmental:* Exposure to genuine moral beauty and excellence in childhood cultivates the capacity for elevation. *Circumstances:* Encounters with unexpected greatness — moral, aesthetic, or natural — are the primary triggers.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. The glory vocabulary's primary referent is divine glory, which is substantially outside science's scope. Science contributes at the experiential edge.

### Section 3 — Music, song, and communal celebration

**Primary field:** Music psychology / social psychology

**Glosses covered:**
melody (zim.rah), melody (miz.mor), musical (na.im), to sing praise (psallō), to praise (humneō), to celebrate (eufrainō), to conduct (na.tsach), song (za.mir)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Dunbar's social bonding through music research (*Behavioural Sciences*, 2012) demonstrates that communal singing produces oxytocin release and social bonding comparable to physical synchrony and touch. Music psychology (Juslin and Sloboda, *Music and Emotion*, 2010) addresses the mechanisms by which music produces emotional responses. Communal singing in particular is associated with increased in-group cohesion, shared emotional experience, and collective identity. Cross-cultural research confirms that music is a universal human practice, present in all known cultures, and functions primarily in social and ritual contexts.

**Emergence — what causes communal song and celebration to arise:**
Communal celebration arises from shared positive experience — victory, deliverance, harvest, religious observance. Song arises as a natural expression of emotions that exceed ordinary verbal formulation — both joy and grief are expressed in song across cultures. Musical praise in religious contexts arises from the combination of awe (a response to God's greatness) and gratitude (a response to God's goodness).

**Reduction and absence:**
Communal song diminishes in contexts of grief, oppression, and social fragmentation (Psalm 137 — 'how shall we sing the LORD's song in a strange land?' maps onto the social psychology of song's dependency on community and safety). Individual singing is suppressed by self-consciousness; communal singing is suppressed by the loss of shared occasion.

**Location — where music and song are seated:**
Embodied, emotional, cognitive, and social simultaneously. Music engages the body (rhythm, breath, posture), the emotions (affective response to melody and harmony), the mind (text comprehension and memory), and the social dimension (synchrony with others). Of all the sections across the programme, music is the most comprehensively whole-person: it is difficult to assign it to any single domain without loss.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Musical forms, instruments, and occasions are entirely culturally shaped, though the practice of communal singing is universal. *Developmental:* Early musical experience — particularly parental singing and communal worship — shapes musical capacity and its emotional associations. *Circumstances:* Celebration arises from positive shared events; lament arises from shared loss. Both find musical expression in religious communities.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Music psychology is a well-developed field with direct relevance to this section's vocabulary.

### Section 4 — Holiness and consecration in praise

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion

**Glosses covered:**
holiness (hagiasmos), holiness (hagiotēs), holiness (hagiōsunē), holiness (hosiotēs), holiness (qo.desh), holy (hagios), holy (qa.dosh), holy (qad.dish), extolling (ro.mam), to boast: praise (ha.lal), to praise (ye.da), to praise (she.vach), to praise/bless (eulogeō), honor (ne.di.vah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Pargament's work on the sacred (*The Psychology of Religion and Coping*, 1997) addresses sanctification — the process by which ordinary objects, relationships, and activities are perceived as carrying sacred significance. Otto's *Das Heilige* (1917) — the numinous as mysterium tremendum et fascinans — is the foundational phenomenological account of the holiness experience. Hood's Mysticism Scale operationalises Otto's categories for empirical study. Holiness as an experiential category is accessible to psychology of religion; holiness as ontological status (God's actual separateness and moral purity) is outside its scope.

**Emergence — what causes the holiness-response to arise:**
The holiness-response arises through encounter with the perceived sacred — that which is set apart, pure, and overwhelming in its moral weight. It combines elements of awe (Section 2) and reverence (M21, Section 2) with a specific awareness of moral distance. Extolling and blessing God (eulogeō, ro.mam) arise from this holiness-perception — they are the praise responses to the holy.

**Location — where the holiness-response is seated:**
Both cognitive (the recognition of divine otherness) and emotional (the complex of awe and reverence). Otto's analysis emphasises the non-rational dimension of holiness experience — it resists full cognitive comprehension and has a quality of overwhelming givenness.

**External influences:**
*Liturgical and cultural:* The perception of holiness is almost entirely formed through liturgical practice, sacred narrative, and religious community. Without these, the category of the holy does not naturally arise in ordinary experience. *Developmental:* Childhood formation in religious community cultivates the capacity to perceive and respond to holiness.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin for direct psychology; moderate for psychology of religion. The theological core of this section (holiness as God's actual ontological attribute) is outside science entirely.

## Outside-science inventory

- **glory (ka.vod, doxa)** in its divine-attribute register — the manifest, weighty presence of God's own being — exceeds what psychology of awe can address. Science addresses the human experience of glory; the theological claim is that God's glory is real, not merely perceived.
- **holiness (qa.dosh, hagios)** in its ontological sense — being set apart by God, belonging to God — is a theological category.
- **to praise/bless (eulogeō)** where addressed to God — eulogeō applied to God is doxological speech, not a social-appreciation act.
- **praise (ha.lal)** in its fullest doxological register — ascribing infinite worth to an infinitely worthy God — exceeds the psychology of social appreciation.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Positive psychology (gratitude) for Section 1; psychology of awe and elevation for Section 2; music psychology for Section 3; psychology of religion for Section 4.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Gratitude research illuminates the relational, other-directed character of thanksgiving vocabulary. Music psychology illuminates why praise in Scripture is so consistently communal and embodied — the social bonding function of music is well-established. Elevation research illuminates why witnessing divine glory produces prosocial and self-transcendent responses in those who witness it.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The normative claim that God is actually worthy of infinite praise — that praise directed at God is rightly calibrated to its object — is outside science's descriptive reach. Science can explain why people praise and what effects praise has; it cannot adjudicate whether the object of praise deserves it. This is the cluster's central theological divergence.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The distinction between lament and praise in the Psalms (which science might classify under the same gratitude/awe umbrella) may surface as a sub-group distinction in Session B. The role of communal singing in creating and sustaining community identity may illuminate the social function of the cluster's praise vocabulary in Israel's worship life.
