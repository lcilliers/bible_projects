## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Travail images God as the one who apportions pangs in judgement (God distributes "pains in his anger", Job 21:17) and, in the prophets, as the one whose acts bring on a nation's birth-pang anguish (Jer 13:21). It pictures God as sovereign over the onset of inescapable anguish.
**T0.1.2** — Travail is related to God as **giver** in one verse (Job 21:17, God distributing pains); otherwise it is borne by persons and nations, not by God.
**T0.1.3** — God's apportioning rather than bearing of pangs frames travail as a creaturely anguish that God can ordain.
**T0.2.1** — Travail serves, figuratively, to mark anguish that is sudden, gripping, and productive of something (a birth) — it equips the language of unavoidable, purposive pain.
**T0.2.2** — As literal childbirth pain it belongs to the fallen order (Gen 3:16, the multiplied pangs — though that verse's `e.tsev` sits in A); as a figure it describes the fallen experience of sudden anguish.
**T0.2.3** — Future-oriented in its NT use: birth-pangs become the figure of the present age's anguish straining toward a new birth (Gal 4:19, until Christ is formed; the woman in birth-pangs, Rev 12:2) — present travail anticipates a coming birth.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties travail to the image of God. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Travail is strongly typological/figurative: the birth-pang carries covenantal and apocalyptic meaning (Isa 66:7, Zion's birth; Rev 12:2, the woman in labour) — the human experience of labour established as the pattern for anguish that issues in new birth, a present→consummation direction.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Travail / labour-pangs": grief and anguish figured as the pains of childbirth — sudden, gripping, inescapable, and productive. The name signals the birth metaphor.
**T1.1.2** — `che.vel` (birth-pang), `ōdinō` (to be in labour); the terms are the literal vocabulary of childbirth applied to anguish.
**T1.1.3** — The terms carry an implication of inevitability and onset — pangs that seize and cannot be deferred (Isa 13:8; Jer 13:21).
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly a **status** (9) — the gripped condition of being in pangs.
**T1.2.2** — Compound by metaphor: travail fuses physical pain, suddenness, and the expectation of a birth.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: the ease of safe deliverance / painless birth (Isa 66:7, Zion delivered "before she was in labour"). Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Travail excludes calm and control; pangs overtake.
**T1.3.3** — It is the figure of sudden seizing anguish, distinct from sustained ache (A) or pressure (B).
**T1.4.1** — Operates as the figure for a nation's terror (pangs seize them, Isa 13:8; Jer 49:24), for personal anguish, and for spiritual labour (Paul "in travail until Christ is formed", Gal 4:19).
**T1.4.2** — Operates literally (childbirth, Job 39:3) and figuratively (national and spiritual anguish).
**T1.4.3** — Travail can issue in a cry (the cry of a woman in labour, Jer 4:31 — though that verse's term sits in I), but it is not characteristically speech-based.
**T1.5.1** — The immediate response is being gripped/seized — pangs taking hold (Isa 13:8).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent: sudden onset and gripping.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained travail strains toward a birth — its over-time orientation is productive (Gal 4:19).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Travail is undergone, not received as a gift. Negative on reception.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1–T2.5** — No constitutional seat is recorded for travail at any level (spirit, soul, heart, mind, other soul-subset). It is figured bodily, not located in a named inner seat. Negative throughout.
**T2.6** — The body (the womb, the loins) is the implicit site of the metaphor, though a named inner organ is not the locus.
**T2.7** — As a bodily figure, travail runs body→whole-person: the bodily pang stands for total anguish.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the onset of overwhelming circumstance — received-from-outside (the enemy, the day of calamity) — and once divinely apportioned (Job 21:17).
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin varies: literal childbirth, national catastrophe (Jer 13:21), divine distribution (Job 21:17), spiritual labour (Gal 4:19).
**T2.10** — No constitutional movement across levels evidenced. Negative.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
No inner faculty is tagged on the travail terms — they are figurative bodily descriptors rather than direct faculty-engagements.
**T3.1–T3.11** — Not engaged at faculty level in the evidence. This is itself the finding: travail is a **bodily metaphor** for anguish, so it engages the inner faculties only by the felt-states it figures (A, B), not directly.
**Pattern** — Travail's non-engagement of any faculty marks it as a figure rather than a primitive inner-state; it lends the cluster its image of inescapable, productive anguish without being itself a discrete faculty-engaging characteristic.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God apportions pangs (Job 21:17) and brings on the birth-pang anguish of nations through his acts (Jer 13:21). Basis: judicial/providential.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Not directed Godward in these verses. Negative.
**T4.3 Giving** — Paul's travail is extended *for* others — "I am in travail until Christ is formed in you" (Gal 4:19), a redemptive labour on another's behalf. This is the one clear relational extension.
**T4.4 Receiving** — Not characteristically received person-to-person.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates over individuals (childbirth), nations (prophetic), and the church (Gal 4:19).
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — The apocalyptic woman in travail (Rev 12:2) sits in a cosmic-conflict scene, but travail itself is not an interface with spiritual beings. Largely negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Travail is transformative by figure: it strains toward a birth — Paul's travail aims at Christ being formed (Gal 4:19), a real formation.
**T5.2 Sequence** — The inherent sequence is pangs → birth; the metaphor's whole force is the productive movement from anguish to new life.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the gripping onset that issues in delivery — anguish as the necessary precursor to birth.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Travail is suffering figured as productive — pangs are the **context** of a coming birth; suffering here is purposive, oriented toward an outcome.
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Gal 4:19 makes travail the figure of formative spiritual labour — the apostolic anguish by which Christ is formed in others.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Strongly oriented: birth-pangs are a NT figure for the present age's anguish before the new birth (Rev 12:2; cf. the wider birth-pang motif) — travail anticipates consummation.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Sparse same-verse co-occurrence (with E, B, G once each); travail mostly stands as a figure attaching to a scene.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Inherently sequential (pangs→birth); attaches to scenes of sudden calamity.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by sudden overwhelming circumstance; constitutes the figure of productive anguish.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `che.vel` (pang) shares its consonantal form with the out-of-scope homonym for cord/region (set aside); `ōdinō`/`ōdin` is the NT labour family. (Cross-cluster — flagged; note the homonym separation.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbours are **A (inner pain)** and **B (distress)**; travail is distinguished as the *figure* of sudden, gripping, productive pain rather than a felt-state in its own right — a distinction of register (metaphor vs primitive).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `che.vel` (birth-pang), `ōdinō`/`ōdin` (to be in labour / labour); roots in the literal vocabulary of childbirth.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: noun (pang) and verb (to be in labour) — the figure works as both the pain and the act.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: pang ↔ labour ↔ birth-pain ↔ agony — the childbirth band applied to anguish.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the pang (`che.vel`) from the act of labouring (`ōdinō`).
**T7.1.5–.7** — No internal opposite, person-type, or supplication term. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `che.vel`-pang continues into NT `ōdinō`; the birth-pang figure is stable and gains an eschatological charge in the NT.
**T7.1.10** — The arc runs from prophetic national birth-pangs (Isaiah, Jeremiah) to apostolic spiritual travail (Gal 4:19) and apocalyptic birth (Rev 12:2) — anguish always oriented toward a coming birth.
**T7.2.1** — In Gal 4:19 the travail term carries Paul's pastoral anguish — the labour by which Christ is to be formed in the Galatians.
**T7.2.2** — Forms: prophecy (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Hosea), wisdom (Job), epistle (Galatians), and apocalyptic (Revelation).
**T7.2.4** — Settings: covenant judgement (the prophetic birth-pangs of the nations), and eschatological birth (Rev 12).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Galatians 4:19** — "my little children, for whom I am again in the anguish of childbirth until Christ is formed in you" most fully expresses travail as productive, redemptive anguish borne for another.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals travail as *vicarious and formative* — anguish willingly borne so that new life (Christ formed) may come in another.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: physiology of grief and the clinical reality of labour (psychosomatic research; the labour analogy).
**T7.3.2** — The science notes that grief and labour share genuine physiological features (involuntary contractions, waves of pain, exhaustion), making the biblical birth-pang figure "more than literary" — it rests on a real bodily analogue.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science treats labour as a physiological process; the biblical material loads it with covenantal and eschatological meaning (new birth, the age to come) that lies outside the physiological frame.
**T7.3.4** — The shared physiology supports treating travail as the cluster's bodily image of *productive* anguish — distinct from the depleting toil-pain of A — a distinction to test in synthesis.
