## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Distress images God as the one who both sends adversity in judgement and is the refuge to whom the distressed cry: the Psalm 107 refrain has the people cry to the LORD in their trouble and be delivered (Psa 107:6,13,19,28), while elsewhere God is the agent who brings the distress (2Ch 15:6; Deu 28:57; Deu 31:17). God is thus pictured as sovereign over the straits — both their author and their exit.
**T0.1.2** — Distress is related to God as **agent** in 12 verses (e.g. Deu 28:57; Deu 31:17; 2Ch 15:6; Psa 107:6,13,19,28); it is not predicated of God as a state he bears. God stands over distress, not within it.
**T0.1.3** — That God is never said to be in distress himself frames the characteristic as a creaturely condition under his governance, not a divine attribute.
**T0.2.1** — Where a purpose appears, distress drives the person to cry out to God and be heard (Psa 107; Jon 2:2, distress from the belly of Sheol answered). Its served purpose is to turn the person Godward.
**T0.2.2** — Distress is presented as a feature of the fallen condition — "man is born to trouble" (Job 5:7) — not of original design.
**T0.2.3** — The evidence orients distress toward relief rather than fullness: the recurring movement is from straits to deliverance (Psa 107), with no verse pointing distress itself toward an eschatological fullness. Negative on future-fullness orientation.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties distress to the image of God; it is a creaturely experience of pressure, not an image-bearing attribute. Negative finding.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Distress is not deployed typologically in its own right; it functions as the circumstance from which deliverance is sought. No christological or covenantal typology rests on the distress terms themselves.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Distress / constriction": grief experienced as pressure and narrowing — the inner being hemmed in. The name signals straitness, not the felt ache (A) but the squeeze that produces it.
**T1.1.2** — Hebrew `tsa.rah`/`tsar` carry the root sense of *narrowness, straits*; `me.tsu.qah`/`ma.tsoq` of *constraint*; Greek `thlibō` is *to press*, `stenochōreō` *to crush into a narrow place*. The vocabulary is spatial-bodily before it is emotional.
**T1.1.3** — The terms carry a constitutional implication (the soul pressed) and a directional one (pressure from without, inward), orienting the enquiry toward external cause.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly a **status/condition** (status 109, quality 23, action 16) — a state of being-pressed borne by the person.
**T1.2.2** — Simple in structure, though it characteristically arises with an external besieging agent named in the verse.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: relief / enlargement ("he set me in a broad place", the antonymic spatial image). Cross-cluster (peace/rest), named only.
**T1.3.2** — Distress excludes ease and security; it is the felt closing-in of options.
**T1.3.3** — It ends where the external pressure lifts (deliverance) or turns inward into felt ache (A) — the boundary with A is constitutional (soul-pressure vs heart-ache).
**T1.4.1** — Operates as a borne condition of constriction, occasionally as an act of pressing (`thlibō`), and as a quality (`tsar` "distressed").
**T1.4.2** — Operates chiefly at the soul level, with bodily registration (flesh, inward-parts) in extremity (Deu 28:53–57, siege).
**T1.4.3** — Distress finds voice in the cry for help (Psa 107; Psa 120:1, "in my distress I called"), so it has a communicative mode — the cry out of the straits.
**T1.5.1** — The most immediate response is the cry to God for deliverance (Psa 107:6; Psa 120:1; Jon 2:2).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent across the evidence: distress repeatedly issues in calling out.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained distress, where unrelieved, wears the person down and can harden into bitterness (the F-boundary); where relieved, it issues in deliverance and testimony (Psa 107).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Distress is borne under pressure, not received as a gift; reception-framing does not apply. Negative.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No occurrence locates distress at the spirit level. Negative.
**T2.2** — Located at the **soul** level in 12 occurrences (e.g. Gen 42:21, "the distress of his soul"; 1Sa 22:2; 2Sa 4:9) — distress is predominantly a soul-phenomenon, the innermost self pressed.
**T2.3** — Located in the **heart** in 4 occurrences (e.g. Psa 25:17, "the troubles of my heart"; 2Cor 2:4), engaging the heart's feeling-under-pressure.
**T2.4** — No mind-location. Negative — distress does not engage the mind in the evidence.
**T2.5** — Beyond heart and soul, distress is seated in the **inward-parts** twice (Psa 55:10; 2Cor 6:12), an intensifying interior locus.
**T2.6** — Linked to the **flesh** in 4 occurrences, all in extremity (the siege of Deu 28:53,55; Jer 19:9; 2Cor 7:5) — distress pressing into the body.
**T2.7** — Where the flesh-link appears, direction runs soul→body: the inward straits register as bodily affliction; the siege verses make the bodily consequence the measure of the distress.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is overwhelmingly **received-from-outside** — an external besieging adversity (enemy, evil, calamity) — with God named as the bestowing agent in 12 verses.
**T2.9.2** — Origin is therefore double: the proximate external pressure and the ultimate divine permission (Psa 107).
**T2.9.3** — Origin shifts by context: enemy-inflicted (Deu 28:57; 1Sa 13:6), divinely-sent (2Ch 15:6), or the bare human condition (Job 5:7).
**T2.10** — Movement runs from soul-pressure into bodily affliction in extremity (Deu 28); no spirit-level movement evidenced.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Distress is **pure affect** (129/129).
**T3.1 Perception / T3.2 Cognition / T3.3 Memory** — Not engaged. Negative findings.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged wholly: distress is the affective experience of being pressed; it intensifies and dominates affect (e.g. Psa 25:17).
**T3.5 Creativity / T3.6 Volition / T3.7 Agency** — Not engaged; distress is undergone, not initiated. Negative.
**T3.8 Moral evaluation** — Not at faculty level; the rare sinful framing (Job 15:35) sits in valence, not in an engaged faculty.
**T3.9 Conscience / T3.11 Relational** — Not engaged at faculty level, though distress is frequently about a threatening situation (object situation ×32).
**Pattern** — Distress engages affect alone; it neither enables nor impairs other faculties, marking it (with A) as a pure-affect characteristic distinguished from A by its spatial-constrictive register.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — Yes: God sends distress in judgement (Deu 28:57; Deu 31:17) and delivers from it (Psa 107 throughout); the basis is covenantal and responsive — distress follows covenant breach and lifts on the cry of repentance.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Strongly present: distress is the classic occasion of the cry to God (Psa 120:1; Jon 2:2; Psa 107:6). The inner posture required is the turn from self-reliance to calling out.
**T4.3 Giving** — Not extended person-to-person. Negative.
**T4.4 Receiving** — Distress is received from circumstances and adversaries (1Sa 13:6; Deu 28:57), involuntarily.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates across covenantal and communal scope: national distress (Neh 9:27), personal distress (Psa 25:17), and the distress of enemies (Obd 12,14) all appear.
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates distress to angelic or adversarial beings directly. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Distress changes the person's orientation more than their condition: it drives the self Godward (Psa 107). Where the cry is answered, the change is to gratitude and testimony.
**T5.2 Sequence** — A clear before→during→after recurs in Psalm 107: adversity → cry in distress → deliverance → thanks.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the pressure of circumstance breaking self-sufficiency (Lazarus's demand-resource gap, T7.3) and forcing dependence.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Central: distress is the very experience of affliction — both a **product of** adversity and the **context** in which the person cries out. Suffering reveals and intensifies it (Psa 107; Lam).
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Distress participates in formation as the recurring school of dependence (Psa 107's fourfold cycle); 1Ti 5:10 commends the one who has relieved the afflicted, tying distress into the sanctified life of others.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — No verse orients distress itself toward eschatological fullness; its trajectory is toward deliverance within history. Negative on eschatological orientation.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Same-verse with A (×2, 2Cor 2:4; Psa 116:3), and singly with F, J, E, I, D, H — distress sits beside the felt ache (A) and the voiced complaint (J), and can tip into bitterness (F).
**T6.2 Sequential** — Distress precedes the cry and the deliverance (Psa 107); it is the antecedent state in the deliverance sequence.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by external adversity and divine permission; in extremity it produces the bodily affliction and the cry; it can produce bitterness if unrelieved.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `tsar` overlaps with the homonym for "adversary/enemy" (the one who distresses), tying distress lexically to its external cause; `thlibō`/`thlipsis` is shared NT affliction vocabulary. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **A (inner pain)**. The distinction is constitutional and qualitative: A is the heart's felt ache; distress is the soul's experience of external pressure. The difference is of kind and seat, not degree.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — Primary terms `tsa.rah`/`tsar` (narrowness, straits), `me.tsu.qah`/`ma.tsoq` (constraint), Greek `thlibō` (press), `stenochōreō` (crush into a narrow space); the root meanings are spatial — being hemmed in.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: nouns (the condition of distress), an adjective (`tsar`, "distressed"), verbs (`thlibō`, to press) — distress is mostly a borne noun-state.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: distress ↔ trouble ↔ anguish ↔ affliction ↔ adversity — a band of external-pressure experience.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the condition (`tsa.rah`) from the pressing act (`thlibō`).
**T7.1.5** — The opposite/absence is the spatial image of enlargement ("a broad place"), present antonymically.
**T7.1.6–.7** — No person-type term and no supplication term internal to the vocabulary (the cry is expressed with other verbs). Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `tsar`/`tsa.rah` continues into NT `thlibō`/`thlipsis`/`stenochōreō`; the constrictive metaphor is stable across the Testaments.
**T7.1.10** — The arc presents distress consistently as external pressure on the soul, from the patriarchal narratives to Paul's afflictions (2Cor 4:8, "afflicted in every way, but not crushed").
**T7.2.1** — In its anchor verses the distress term names the besetting condition that motivates the cry (Psa 107:6).
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Genesis, Samuel), covenant law (Deu 28), wisdom (Job, Proverbs), psalm/lament (Psa 107; 120), prophecy (Jeremiah), and epistle (2Cor).
**T7.2.4** — Settings are covenantal (Deu 28), liturgical/lament (Psa 107), and communal (siege, exile).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Psalm 107** — its fourfold "they cried to the LORD in their trouble/distress, and he delivered them" most fully expresses distress as the straits answered by God.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals distress as the structured occasion of deliverance — not a dead-end but the recurring hinge of the cry-and-rescue pattern.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: clinical stress and coping research (Lazarus and Folkman), trauma research (van der Kolk, Herman).
**T7.3.2** — The demand-resource model illuminates distress precisely: it arises when adversity exceeds perceived coping resources — exactly the soul "hemmed in" of `tsar`; the constriction vocabulary maps onto the somatic experience of being pressed.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science frames distress as a coping problem to be resourced; the biblical material frames it as the occasion to cry to God, where the decisive "resource" is divine deliverance rather than internal coping capacity.
**T7.3.4** — The science's social-support finding invites testing whether the distress verses present communal lament as a buffer — coherent with the communal-distress texts (Neh 9:27) and worth carrying into the cross-cluster pass.
