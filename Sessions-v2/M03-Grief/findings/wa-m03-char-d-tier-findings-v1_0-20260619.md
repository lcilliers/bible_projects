## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Weeping images God as the one who keeps and wipes away tears: he is the addressee before whom the people weep (Num 11:18; Judg 20:23,26), and the eschatological promise is that he will wipe every tear (Rev 7:17; Rev 21:4). The divine character reflected is attentiveness to tears.
**T0.1.2** — Weeping is related to God as **addressee** in 5 verses (Deu 1:45; Judg 20:23,26; Num 11:18,20) and once each as object, possessor, and agent; it is not a state God bears, but one offered to and answered by him.
**T0.1.3** — God's role as the receiver and eventual wiper of tears frames weeping as the creaturely overflow that he attends to.
**T0.2.1** — Weeping's purpose is expressive and appellative — it pours out grief and appeals to God or others (Psa 6:6; the petitionary weeping of Num 11). Rightly directed it is sowing that reaps joy (Psa 126:5–6).
**T0.2.2** — Weeping belongs to the fallen condition (it answers loss and pain) and is promised to cease (Rev 21:4), confirming it is not part of the consummated design.
**T0.2.3** — Strongly oriented to future fullness: "those who sow in tears shall reap with shouts of joy" (Psa 126:5) and Rev 21:4's wiping of tears — present weeping anticipates its reversal.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties weeping to the image of God as an attribute. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Weeping carries eschatological typology in two directions: the "weeping and gnashing of teeth" of judgement (Mat 13:42,50; Luk 13:28) points to final exclusion, while Psa 126 and Rev 21:4 point present tears toward eschatological joy — present→future.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Weeping / tears": grief overflowing as shed tears and crying. The name signals the body's water of grief — the most immediate visible expression.
**T1.1.2** — `ba.khah` (to weep), `be.khi` (weeping), `dim.ah` (tears); Greek `klaiō` (to weep), `klauthmos` (weeping). The terms name the act and product of crying.
**T1.1.3** — Weeping is directional — wept *before* God or *over* a loss (relational "before/over") — orienting toward both the object grieved and the one appealed to.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly an **action** (action 112, status 57) — weeping is something done, with the residue of tears as a status.
**T1.2.2** — Simple as an act, but characteristically bound up with mourning (C) into a single expressive episode.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: laughter/joy (Ecc 3:4, "a time to weep and a time to laugh"). Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Weeping excludes composure; Rev 21:4 sets its wiping-away as the mark of consummated joy.
**T1.3.3** — It ends where tears are wiped (comfort) or dry; its boundary with mourning (C) is that weeping is the tears, mourning the observance around them.
**T1.4.1** — Operates as spontaneous overflow (Gen 43:30), as loud public weeping (1Sa 11:4), and as appeal before God (Num 11).
**T1.4.2** — Operates differently by direction: weeping over a loss, weeping before God in petition, and the judgement-weeping of the lost.
**T1.4.3** — Weeping is often vocal — "weeping aloud", "lifted up their voice and wept" (Gen 21:16; Judg 21:2) — a loud communicative mode.
**T1.5.1** — Weeping is itself the immediate response to grief; where a downstream response appears, it is appeal or collapse (refusing food, 1Sa 1:7–10).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent as the first overflow of grief across the evidence.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained weeping wears the body (Psa 6:6, "I drench my couch with my tears"); rightly held it is sowing toward joy (Psa 126:5–6).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Weeping is overflow, not received; reception-framing does not apply. Limited.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative.
**T2.2** — Soul-level in 9 occurrences — weeping reaching the innermost self (the soul that "weeps in secret", Jer 13:17).
**T2.3** — Located in the **heart** in 10 occurrences — weeping as the heart's overflow.
**T2.4 / T2.5** — No mind-location; one inward-parts locus. Negative on mind.
**T2.6** — Weeping is itself the body's act (the eyes, tears) — the most inherently somatic characteristic, though a specific organ is rarely named beyond the eyes/tears.
**T2.7** — Direction runs soul→body: inner grief overflows as bodily tears — weeping is the paradigm somatic expression of the soul (cf. T7.3, van der Kolk's "the body keeps the score").
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the loss or pain that triggers it — received-from-outside (a death, a threat, a separation).
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin varies: bereavement (Gen 50:1), national threat (1Sa 11:4), compassion (Gen 43:30), repentant grief (2Ki 22:19).
**T2.10** — Movement runs cleanly soul→body; weeping is where inner grief becomes visible water.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Weeping is **affect** (148) overflowing somatically.
**T3.1 Perception / T3.2 Cognition / T3.3 Memory** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged wholly: weeping is affect in its most embodied overflow.
**T3.5 Creativity / T3.6 Volition / T3.7 Agency** — Not engaged; weeping is largely involuntary overflow, though it can be summoned (commanded weeping, Joe 2:17) — a marginal volitional edge.
**T3.8 Moral evaluation / T3.9 Conscience** — Not at faculty level, though weeping carries moral framing (sinful weeping of rebellion, Num 11:20; righteous weeping of repentance, 2Ki 22:19).
**T3.11 Relational** — Strongly relational in object (wept over/for persons ×34) but not the engaged faculty per se.
**Pattern** — Weeping is affect at its most somatic and involuntary; it neither enables nor impairs other faculties, and is distinguished from mourning (C) by being the bodily overflow rather than the observance.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God receives the people's weeping and answers (Num 11), and finally wipes every tear (Rev 7:17; 21:4). Basis: responsive and compassionate.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Strongly present: weeping is offered to God in petition and repentance (Num 11:18; 2Ki 22:19; Joe 2:17, "let the priests weep"). The posture is dependence and contrition.
**T4.3 Giving** — Weeping is shared with others — weeping *with* those who weep is implied in communal weeping (Gen 50:1–3; Joh 11:33).
**T4.4 Receiving** — The weeping of others is received as appeal or grief to be answered (Joseph wept as his brothers spoke, Gen 50:17).
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Weeping crosses bonds: personal, familial, national, and even over enemies (Isa 16:9, weeping over Moab).
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates weeping to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Weeping changes orientation: it is the turning of repentance (2Ki 22:19; 2Ch 34:27) and the appeal that moves toward relief (Psa 126).
**T5.2 Sequence** — A before→after is explicit in Psa 126:5–6: sowing in tears → reaping in joy; the tears are the seed of the later joy.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the overflow and release of grief, and the appeal it constitutes before God and others.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Weeping is the immediate **response to** suffering and loss; suffering produces it directly (bereavement, threat, pain).
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Weeping participates in formation as the bodily form of repentance (Josiah's tender, weeping heart commended, 2Ki 22:19); Psa 126 frames tears as formative sowing.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Strongly oriented: Psa 126 and Rev 7:17 / 21:4 — present tears anticipate the joy and the wiping-away to come; conversely, judgement-weeping (Mat 13:42) anticipates final exclusion.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Strong same-verse pairing with **C (mourning)** — 19 shared verses; lesser with F (bitterly, ×3), E, I, B — weeping is the tears within mourning and shades into bitter weeping (F).
**T6.2 Sequential** — Weeping typically accompanies the moment of loss and surrounds mourning; in Psa 126 it precedes joy.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by grief, loss, and compassion; constitutes the bodily core of the mourning episode.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `ba.khah`/`be.khi` share the weeping root; the "bitterly" intensifier (×6) ties weeping to bitterness (F); `klaiō`/`klauthmos` is the NT weeping family. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **C (mourning)**. The distinction is of kind: weeping is the shedding of tears (somatic overflow); mourning is the observance and condition that contains it.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `ba.khah` (to weep), `be.khi` (weeping), `dim.ah` (tears); Greek `klaiō`, `klauthmos`; roots denote shedding tears and crying aloud.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: verbs (to weep), nouns (weeping, tears) — weeping is act-led with a noun residue (tears).
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: weep ↔ wept ↔ weeping ↔ tears ↔ crying aloud — the band of shed-tear expression.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the act (`ba.khah`) from the product (`dim.ah`, tears).
**T7.1.5** — Opposite: laughter/joy vocabulary (Ecc 3:4), present antonymically.
**T7.1.6–.7** — No distinct person-type or supplication term internal to the weeping vocabulary. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `ba.khah` continues into NT `klaiō`; weeping is one of the most stable grief expressions across the Testaments, and the NT adds the judgement-weeping motif (Mat 13).
**T7.1.10** — The arc runs from patriarchal weeping to the wiped tears of Revelation — the most frequent and most embodied grief term, framed first to last as that which God finally ends.
**T7.2.1** — In Psa 126:5 the weeping term anchors the contrast that drives the verse (tears now, joy later).
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Genesis, Samuel), psalm (6; 126), prophecy (Jeremiah; Joel), gospel (Luke, John), and apocalyptic (Revelation).
**T7.2.4** — Settings are domestic/funerary (Gen 50), liturgical-petitionary (Joe 2; Psa 126), and eschatological (Rev 7; 21).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Psalm 126:5–6** — "those who sow in tears shall reap with shouts of joy" most fully expresses weeping as grief that is also seed for coming joy.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals weeping as productive — not merely expressive but sown, with a promised harvest of joy.
**T7.3.1** — Frameworks: clinical grief research (Bowlby; Stroebe) and physiology/psychosomatics of grief (van der Kolk; the autonomic basis of weeping).
**T7.3.2** — The somatic-grief research illuminates weeping as a genuine bodily event — autonomic, involuntary, storing and releasing grief — matching the cluster's treatment of weeping as the body's overflow.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: science treats weeping as expression/release toward regulation; the biblical material also makes weeping an appeal to God and a seed of promised joy (Psa 126) — meanings beyond physiological release.
**T7.3.4** — Van der Kolk's "the body keeps the score" invites testing whether unwept grief reappears elsewhere in the cluster (the somatic characteristics E, I) — a thread for the cross-cluster pass.
