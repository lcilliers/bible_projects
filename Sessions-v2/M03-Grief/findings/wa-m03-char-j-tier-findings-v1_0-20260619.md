## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Voiced complaint images God as the one who receives and attends to the poured-out grievance: the psalmist pours his complaint *before* the LORD (Psa 142:2) and asks God to hear his complaint (Psa 64:1). The divine character reflected is a God who invites and hears the reasoned grievance of the afflicted.
**T0.1.2** — Complaint is related to God as the **object/addressee** who hears it (Psa 64:1); it is not borne by God. Its theological weight is that grievance may be brought to God.
**T0.1.3** — God's hearing rather than complaining frames complaint as the creaturely grievance he legitimately receives.
**T0.2.1** — Complaint serves to give grief a voice and a case — to "give free utterance to" the soul's bitterness (Job 10:1) and to lay the grievance before God (Psa 142:2). It equips the sufferer to bring sorrow into articulate, God-directed speech.
**T0.2.2** — Complaint answers the fallen experience of affliction; yet it is also a sanctioned mode of address to God (the lament psalms), not merely a defect.
**T0.2.3** — No explicit orientation to future fullness; complaint seeks present hearing and relief. The one positive note (Psa 104:34, "may my meditation/`si.ach` be pleasing") points to complaint's kin, godly musing. Largely negative on eschatological orientation.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties complaint to the image of God. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Complaint is not deployed typologically; it functions within the lament tradition as the form of God-directed grievance.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Voiced complaint / grievance": grief made articulate — poured out as a reasoned case, characteristically before God. The name signals speech and grievance, not mute feeling.
**T1.1.2** — `si.ach` — the term ranges across *complaint*, *musing*, *meditation*, *talk* — the inner discourse that can be poured out as grievance.
**T1.1.3** — The term carries a cognitive-communicative implication: complaint reasons and voices, orienting the enquiry toward articulate inner speech directed outward (often Godward).
**T1.2.1** — Wholly a **status** (14/14) — complaint is the condition of the burdened, voicing soul.
**T1.2.2** — Compound: complaint fuses felt grief (affect) with reasoned articulation (cognition) — uniquely in the cluster.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: silent contentment / settled praise (the antithesis of grievance). Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Complaint excludes mute resignation; it insists on voicing the grievance.
**T1.3.3** — It is grief that has become articulate case-making, distinct from the inarticulate groan (E) and the mute ache (A).
**T1.4.1** — Operates as grievance poured out before God (Psa 142:2; Psa 64:1), as the bitter free-utterance of the suffering soul (Job 10:1; Job 23:2), and as anxious musing (1Sa 1:16, Hannah's "great anxiety and vexation"); the same root also covers godly meditation (Psa 104:34) and even derisive musing (1Ki 18:27).
**T1.4.2** — Operates Godward (lament before the LORD) and inwardly (musing/meditation) — the term spans grievance and reflection.
**T1.4.3** — Complaint **is** the speech-based mode of grief — its defining mode is articulate utterance (Job 10:1, "I will give free utterance to my complaint").
**T1.5.1** — The immediate movement is to pour it out — to voice the grievance rather than contain it (Psa 142:2).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent: complaint presses toward utterance.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained complaint, voiced to God, moves toward relief or resolution (Hannah's complaint, once spoken, ends her sadness, 1Sa 1:18); unaddressed, it cycles in the suffering soul (Job).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Complaint is voiced, not received as a gift. Negative on reception.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative. **T2.2** — Soul-level once (the soul's complaint, Job 10:1's "bitterness of my soul"). **T2.3 / T2.4 / T2.5** — No heart, mind, or other soul-subset seat recorded. Negative — notably, despite its cognitive dimension, complaint is not seated in a "mind" in the evidence.
**T2.6** — No body-part link. Negative — complaint is articulate, not somatic.
**T2.7** — No body-direction. Negative.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the affliction or anxiety that presses the soul to speak — received-from-outside (suffering, vexation), then voiced from within.
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin varies: personal distress (Hannah, 1Sa 1:16), bodily/spiritual suffering (Job), affliction and faintness (Psa 102:1).
**T2.10** — No constitutional movement across levels; complaint moves from inner burden to outward speech (an inward→expressive movement rather than a cross-level one).

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Complaint uniquely engages **both affect (14) and cognition (14)** — every occurrence carries both.
**T3.1 Perception / T3.3 Memory / T3.5 Creativity / T3.7 Agency / T3.11 Relational** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative.
**T3.2 Cognition** — Engaged fully and distinctively: complaint reasons, frames a grievance, and argues a case (Job 23:2–4, ordering his case before God) — the only M03 characteristic that engages cognition throughout.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged fully: complaint is felt grief and bitterness given articulate form.
**T3.6 Volition** — Engaged at the edge: the choice to "give free utterance" (Job 10:1) is volitional, though the tagged faculties are affect and cognition.
**T3.8 Moral evaluation / T3.9 Conscience** — Not engaged at faculty level (Pro 23:29's "complaining" carries a moral context but engages affect/cognition).
**Pattern** — Complaint is the cluster's one affect-plus-cognition characteristic: grief that thinks and speaks its grievance. This makes it the bridge between the felt-states and the reasoning/speaking faculties — the point at which grief becomes a case brought before God.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God receives and hears complaint (Psa 64:1); the relationship is one in which grievance is invited.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Centrally present: complaint is the classic God-directed grief-speech — "I pour out my complaint before him" (Psa 142:2); Hannah's bitter complaint to the LORD (1Sa 1:16); Job's case laid before God (Job 23:2). The inner posture is honest, often raw, dependence.
**T4.3 Giving / T4.4 Receiving** — Not extended or received person-to-person as a benefit; complaint is directed to God or vented inwardly.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates within the covenant relationship (the afflicted to their God) and inwardly (musing); scope is the suffering believer before God.
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates complaint to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Complaint can be transformative when voiced to God: Hannah's complaint, once poured out and heard, changes her countenance — "her face was no longer sad" (1Sa 1:18). Voicing the grievance Godward reorders the inner state.
**T5.2 Sequence** — Hannah's narrative gives a clear before→after: bitter, complaining soul → poured-out prayer → settled, no longer sad.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is articulation before God — bringing the grievance into ordered, addressed speech, which itself relieves (the lament-psalm pattern).
**T5.4 Suffering** — Complaint is the **response to** sustained suffering — the vocalisation of distress that has not resolved (Job; Psa 102:1, the afflicted who faints and pours out his complaint).
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Complaint participates in formation as the sanctioned lament that keeps the sufferer in honest relationship with God rather than in silence or despair (the lament psalms model this).
**T5.6 Eschatological** — No eschatological orientation in the evidence; complaint seeks present hearing. Negative.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Sparse same-verse co-occurrence (with F, E, B once each); complaint largely stands as the articulate voicing alongside felt distress.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Complaint follows the pressing of distress (B) and the bitterness of soul (F), giving them voice; in Hannah it precedes relief.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by affliction and anxiety; constitutes the articulate, God-directed expression of grief.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `si.ach` spans complaint, musing, and meditation — bridging grief-grievance with reflective/meditative vocabulary (Psa 104:34's godly `si.ach`). (Cross-cluster — flagged; the complaint/meditation range is notable.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbours are **E (sighing/groaning)** and **F (bitterness)**. The distinction from E is articulacy (complaint reasons and speaks; groaning is wordless); from F, direction and form (complaint is voiced grievance, often Godward and relievable; bitterness is a settled embittered stance). The distinction is of kind — complaint is the cognitive-articulate member of the cluster.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `si.ach` — to muse, meditate, talk, complain; the root denotes inner discourse that can be poured out, ranging from grievance to godly meditation.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: noun (complaint/​musing) — complaint is a borne, voiced condition.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: complaint ↔ grievance ↔ anxiety ↔ musing ↔ meditation — uniquely spanning grief-speech and reflective thought.
**T7.1.4** — The same term distinguishes the bitter grievance (Job 10:1) from the pleasing meditation (Psa 104:34) — a single root holding both the lament and its godly counterpart.
**T7.1.5** — Opposite: silent resignation, or settled praise; present by contrast.
**T7.1.6–.7** — No distinct person-type or supplication term, though complaint itself functions as a seeking-speech toward God. Limited.
**T7.1.8** — OT-only in this set; `si.ach` has no single NT lexical successor in the cluster, though the lament/petition it embodies continues in NT prayer.
**T7.1.10** — The arc presents complaint as the OT lament voice — the afflicted soul's grievance poured out before God, holding together grief and reasoned speech.
**T7.2.1** — In Psa 142:2 the complaint term is the direct object of "I pour out" — the grievance laid before God.
**T7.2.2** — Forms: narrative (1 Samuel; 1–2 Kings), wisdom (Job; Proverbs), and psalm/lament (Psalms).
**T7.2.4** — Settings: individual lament before God (Psalms; Job), and personal distress brought to prayer (Hannah).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Psalm 142:2** — "I pour out my complaint before him; I tell my trouble before him" most fully expresses complaint as grief made articulate and brought to God.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals complaint as a *legitimate, God-directed* pouring-out — grief that is neither suppressed nor merely vented but addressed to God as the one who hears.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: clinical stress research — complaint as the vocalisation of sustained, unresolved distress (Lazarus and Folkman's coping; the lament-as-coping literature).
**T7.3.2** — The science frames complaint as the vocalisation of distress that has not resolved — matching `si.ach` as the cry that the trouble persists; voicing distress to a witness reliably reduces it, illuminating why poured-out complaint relieves (Hannah, 1Sa 1:18).
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science treats complaint as venting toward regulation; the biblical material makes it a covenantal address to God who hears — the "witness" is not merely present but is the LORD, which reframes the relief as relational, not merely cathartic.
**T7.3.4** — The witnessed-distress finding supports the lament tradition's value and invites testing complaint's God-directedness (with D's and E's God-directed forms) as a "grief addressed to God" axis in the cross-cluster pass.
