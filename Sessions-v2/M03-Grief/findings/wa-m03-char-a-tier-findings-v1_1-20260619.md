## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Inner pain images God as the righteous author and sovereign over human sorrow: where God is the subject he multiplies pain in judgement (Gen 3:16–17), gives "anguish of soul" (Deu 28:65), adds it (Jer 45:3), and finally abolishes it (Rev 21:4). The verses picture a God who governs grief — imposing, permitting, and ending it — rather than one driven by it. The single place the boundary is crossed is the Servant who bears sorrows (Isa 53:3–4).
**T0.1.2** — Inner pain is related to God as **agent** in 10 verses (Gen 3:16,17; Deu 28:65; Isa 14:3; Isa 50:11; Jer 45:3; 2Cor 7:9,11; Eph 4:30); it is not predicated of God as his own felt state. The qualified exception is Isa 53:3–4, where `makh.ov` ("sorrows") is borne by the Servant — read christologically, sorrow is taken up into the divine–human.
**T0.1.3** — That God inflicts and removes but is not said to bear this pain frames inner pain as a creaturely condition under divine governance rather than a shared divine attribute — with Isa 53 the deliberate exception in the Servant.
**T0.2.1** — Where a purpose is stated, godly grief "equips" repentance unto salvation (2Cor 7:9–11); elsewhere inner pain's purpose is not stated at verse level. Its served end, where named, is to turn the person toward repentance.
**T0.2.2** — Inner pain is presented as a feature of the fallen condition: it enters with the fall (Gen 3:16–17) and is abolished in the consummation (Rev 21:4), so it is not part of the original or final design.
**T0.2.3** — Strongly oriented to future fullness by way of its cessation: Rev 21:4 promises that pain shall be no more — present inner pain is oriented toward its own abolition.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties the felt-pain state to the image of God as an attribute. Negative on image-bearing; the capacity to grieve over loss and sin reflects a being made for attachment and moral response, but this is inference, not verse-stated.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Inner pain carries christological typology: Isa 53:3–4 deploys `makh.ov` of the Suffering Servant, and Lam 1:12 ("any sorrow like my sorrow") functions in the typology of Zion's suffering. The direction is divine→human / pattern-establishing — the human lament points toward the Servant who bears the sorrows of others.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Inner pain / sorrow": the felt interior state of grief — the ache itself, not its expression (weeping, mourning) or its cause. The name signals an interior, borne suffering.
**T1.1.2** — Hebrew `makh.ov` (pain), `ya.gon` (grief/sorrow), `ka.av`/`ke.ev` (pain), `e.tsev` (pain/toil); Greek `lupeō`/`lupē` (grief), `perilupos` (deeply grieved), `odunē` (anguish), `ponos` (pain) — pain, sorrow, and anguish as borne states.
**T1.1.3** — The terms are constitutional (heart/soul-seated) and largely non-directional — inner pain is borne within, not aimed at an object the way a relational characteristic is.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly a **status/condition** (status 63, action 34, quality 7) — a state borne by the person, with a minority of act-forms (to grieve, `lupeō`) and quality-forms (`perilupos`, "sorrowful").
**T1.2.2** — Simple at first encounter — a single felt state — though it co-occurs with audible expression (mourning, sighing) and with external pressure (distress).
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: joy / gladness (M04) — the felt-state contrary of inner pain. Cross-cluster, named only.
**T1.3.2** — Inner pain excludes comfort and consolation; Rev 21:4 sets the wiping-away of pain as the mark of consummated joy.
**T1.3.3** — It ends where the felt ache becomes outward expression (mourning, weeping, sighing) or external pressure (distress); its boundary is seat (heart/soul) and the fact that it is felt rather than enacted.
**T1.4.1** — Operates as a borne inner state (the standing condition of sorrow), occasionally as an act of grieving (`lupeō`), and as a quality (`perilupos`, the sorrowful person).
**T1.4.2** — Operates at the heart and soul levels, not at spirit or mind; no directional dependence is evidenced — it is consistently an interior state.
**T1.4.3** — Inner pain is not itself speech-based (it is the felt ache, which other characteristics voice). Negative finding on a communicative mode.
**T1.5.1** — The immediate inner response is low-signal and verse-specific; no single dominant reaction attends the onset of inner pain across the evidence.
**T1.5.2** — Variable across the evidence rather than uniform.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained inner pain tends to consume — sorrow increasing with knowledge (Ecc 1:18), days full of grief (Ecc 2:23) — yet in the godly case it turns to repentance and is not regretted (2Cor 7:9–11).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Inner pain is borne, not received as a gift; reception-framing largely does not apply. The one reception-adjacent case is godly grief willingly entered (2Cor 7).

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No occurrence locates inner pain at the spirit level. Negative.
**T2.2** — Located at the **soul** level in 6 occurrences — the felt ache reaching the innermost self.
**T2.3** — Located in the **heart** in 11 occurrences — the **dominant** seat; inner pain is pre-eminently a heart-state, engaging the heart's feeling function.
**T2.4** — No mind-location. Negative — inner pain does not engage the mind as a seat (Ecc 1:18's cognitive *cause* is not a mind-seat).
**T2.5** — No soul-subset location beyond heart and soul. Negative.
**T2.6** — Linked to the **flesh** once (Job 33:19, pain in the body); a minimal, functional body-link.
**T2.7** — Where the flesh-link appears, direction runs soul→body: the inner pain registering bodily. Single data point, held lightly.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is predominantly received-from-outside (external adversity, divine action), with `origin` explicitly received-from-outside in 3 occurrences and God named as bestowing agent in 10.
**T2.9.2** — Origin is multiple: external infliction and divine bestowal both appear.
**T2.9.3** — Origin shifts by context: divinely imposed (Gen 3; Deu 28), relationally inflicted (2Cor 2:2), and self-incurred (1Ti 6:10, those who pierce themselves with many griefs).
**T2.10** — Movement is limited to heart–soul co-seating; no spirit-level movement is evidenced.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Inner pain is **pure affect** (104/104).
**T3.1 Perception / T3.2 Cognition / T3.3 Memory** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative findings (the cognitive *cause* in Ecc 1:18 is not the faculty inner pain engages).
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged wholly: inner pain *is* the affective faculty in its pain mode; it intensifies and dominates affect.
**T3.5 Creativity / T3.6 Volition / T3.7 Agency** — Not engaged; inner pain is borne, not chosen or initiated (the repentance of 2Cor 7 is a downstream volitional act, not inner pain's faculty).
**T3.8 Moral evaluation / T3.9 Conscience** — Not at faculty level; the moral framing sits in valence (sinful/righteous grief), not in an engaged faculty — though godly grief (2Cor 7) is conviction-adjacent.
**T3.11 Relational** — Not at faculty level, though inner pain is frequently *about* persons (object person ×10).
**Pattern** — Inner pain engages a single faculty, affect, purely; it neither enables nor impairs the other faculties in the evidence. This marks it as the cluster's clearest pure-affect state — the felt ache that the expressive and somatic characteristics give form to.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God extends or imposes inner pain as agent (Gen 3:16–17; Deu 28:65; Jer 45:3; struck down, Psa 69:26) and removes it (Rev 21:4). Basis: judgement and discipline — conditional and covenantal.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Limited: inner pain is felt rather than directed at God; the godward case is godly grief turning to repentance (2Cor 7:9–11), whose inner posture is openness to conviction.
**T4.3 Giving** — Inner pain is not extended person-to-person (contrast compassion). Negative.
**T4.4 Receiving** — Inner pain is received in the inflicted sense: from circumstances and persons (Eze 28:24, the contempt of neighbours; 2Cor 2:2, pain caused relationally) — involuntarily.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates both within bonds (pain *over*/​*for* persons, object person ×10) and across distance; relational scope is wide.
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates inner pain to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Inner pain produces transformation in one clear case: godly grief → repentance → salvation (2Cor 7:9–11), changing both condition and orientation; the change is explicitly "without regret" (irreversible-for-good). Elsewhere transformation is not stated.
**T5.2 Sequence** — 2Cor 7:9–11 gives a before→during→after: grief → repentance → salvation without regret.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Encounter and conviction — godly grief as the mechanism that moves the person toward repentance.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Central: inner pain is most often the **product of** suffering (it *is* the felt suffering) and a **response to** loss — oppression (Exo 3:7), divine wounding (Job 5:18; Psa 69:26), the sorrows of Zion (Lam 1:12,18). Suffering produces and deepens it; in 2Cor 7 it also reveals repentance.
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Godly grief participates in the sanctification arc, working repentance unto salvation (2Cor 7:10).
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Strongly oriented: Rev 21:4 orients inner pain toward eschatological abolition — present pain anticipates a future where it is wiped away.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Same-verse with C (mourning, ×4) and E (sighing, ×4), and with B (distress, ×2) and I (debilitation, ×2). The pattern: inner pain is the felt state that the expressive characteristics (mourning, sighing) make audible and that debilitation carries into the body.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Limited same-verse sequence; the clearest is inner pain → repentance (2Cor 7).
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by external adversity (the cause field) and by God (agent ×10); inner pain produces repentance in the godly case (2Cor 7).
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `e.tsev`/`its.tsa.von` carry "painful toil" (Gen 3:16–17), bridging inner pain with labour/toil vocabulary; `lupeō` is shared NT grief vocabulary. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **B (distress)**. The distinction is constitutional and qualitative: inner pain is the heart's felt ache; distress is the soul's experience of external pressure (constriction). The difference is of kind and seat, not degree.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — Primary terms `makh.ov`, `ya.gon`, `ka.av`/`ke.ev`, `e.tsev` (Hebrew); `lupeō`/`lupē`, `odunē`, `ponos` (Greek) — pain/sorrow/anguish as felt suffering (root glosses per the lexicon; deeper etymology not asserted beyond it).
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: nouns (the borne state), verbs (`lupeō`, `ka.av` — the act of grieving), adjective (`perilupos` — the quality) — inner pain operates as both borne state and act.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: pain ↔ sorrow ↔ anguish ↔ grief — a felt-suffering band.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the condition (`makh.ov`) from the act (`lupeō`) from the quality (`perilupos`).
**T7.1.5** — Opposite/absence term: comfort/joy vocabulary (cross-cluster); within inner pain itself, none.
**T7.1.6** — No distinct person-type term ("the sorrowful one"); `perilupos` is a quality, not a person-type. Negative.
**T7.1.7** — No supplication or seeking term (inner pain is not sought). Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `makh.ov`/`ya.gon` are answered by NT `lupeō`/`odunē`; the felt-pain experience is continuous across the Testaments, with the NT adding the godly-grief framing (2Cor 7).
**T7.1.9** — No clearly NT-coined term for inner pain. Negative.
**T7.1.10** — The vocabulary arc runs pain/sorrow/anguish across both Testaments, climaxing in the Servant's `makh.ov` (Isa 53) and in godly grief (2Cor 7).
**T7.2.1** — In Isa 53:3 `makh.ov` functions predicatively, defining the Servant as "a man of sorrows."
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Genesis), covenant law (Deu 28), wisdom (Ecclesiastes), prophecy (Isaiah, Jeremiah), lament (Lamentations), epistle (2 Corinthians), and apocalyptic (Revelation).
**T7.2.3** — Logical structure example: 2Cor 7:9–11 — godly grief → repentance → salvation, "without regret."
**T7.2.4** — Settings are covenantal (Deu 28), eschatological (Rev 21), and christological (Isa 53).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Isaiah 53:3** — "a man of sorrows" most fully and directly expresses inner pain and carries the cluster's deepest theological weight.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals that inner pain is taken up and borne **vicariously** by the Servant — the deepest theological turn the characteristic makes.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: clinical psychology of pain and suffering — Eisenberger (social-pain neuroscience), Cassell (the nature of suffering), Frankl (meaning).
**T7.3.2** — Eisenberger's finding that social/psychological pain shares neural circuits with physical pain gives the biblical pain-of-grief vocabulary a literal, not merely metaphorical, basis; Cassell's account of suffering as a threat to the whole person aligns with inner pain reaching beyond bodily hurt.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science is primarily therapeutic (move through pain toward restored function); the biblical material includes pain sustained unresolved (Lamentations) and given vicarious, redemptive meaning (Isa 53; 2Cor 7) that the therapeutic frame does not capture.
**T7.3.4** — The science's meaning-making pathway invites testing whether inner pain presents meaning as the turning point — and 2Cor 7's godly grief supports exactly that, a coherent and evidence-grounded extension.
