## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Groaning images God as the one who hears and is moved by it: the Exodus groaning "rose up to God", who heard it and remembered his covenant (Exo 2:23–24; Exo 6:5); the LORD was moved to pity by the groaning of the oppressed (Judg 2:18). The divine character reflected is responsiveness to the inarticulate cry.
**T0.1.2** — Groaning is related to God as the **object/recipient** who hears it (Judg 2:18); it is not borne by God. Its theological weight lies in being heard.
**T0.1.3** — That God hears rather than groans frames groaning as the creaturely cry he attends to even when it has no words.
**T0.2.1** — Groaning's purpose is to voice affliction below the level of speech — the involuntary sound that nonetheless reaches God (Exo 2:23–24).
**T0.2.2** — Groaning belongs to the fallen condition (it answers oppression and pain), with no claim to original design.
**T0.2.3** — Limited future-fullness orientation in the OT terms; the cluster's NT groaning (Rom 8, by extension) points toward redemption, but within this characteristic's verses the orientation is toward being heard now. Largely negative on eschatological orientation for these lemmas.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties groaning to the image of God. Negative.
**T0.4.1–.2** — The Exodus groaning functions typologically of covenant-remembrance — the cry that triggers God's redemptive action — a present-cry→divine-response pattern, but no developed christological typology rests on these lemmas.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Sighing / groaning": grief as involuntary breath-voice — the moan or sigh below articulate speech. The name signals the inarticulate sound of inner pain.
**T1.1.2** — `a.nach` (to sigh/groan), `a.na.chah` (sighing); `ne.a.qah`/`a.na.qah` (groaning); the terms denote the breathed sound of distress.
**T1.1.3** — Groaning is expressive and (in the Exodus texts) appellative — it rises toward God even without words.
**T1.2.1** — A mix of **status** (the condition of sighing, 19) and **action** (the act of groaning, 12).
**T1.2.2** — Simple — a single involuntary sound, though it accompanies affliction and weeping.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: rest/relief and glad song. Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Groaning excludes ease; it is the sound of the unrelieved.
**T1.3.3** — It ends where relief or rescue comes (the Exodus groaning ends in deliverance); its boundary with weeping (D) is that groaning is breath-voice, weeping is tears.
**T1.4.1** — Operates as involuntary sighing (Lam 1:22, "my groans are many"), and as the collective groaning of the oppressed (Exo 2:23).
**T1.4.2** — Operates both individually (Psa 6:6, weary with groaning) and corporately (the enslaved nation).
**T1.4.3** — Groaning is itself a quasi-communicative mode — sound without words that nonetheless communicates and is heard (Exo 2:24).
**T1.5.1** — Groaning is itself the immediate response to sustained affliction; it is the first sound of the unrelieved.
**T1.5.2** — Consistent as the breath-response to oppression and sorrow.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained groaning marks chronic, unrelieved affliction (Psa 6:6); its over-time effect is exhaustion.
**T1.7.1–.3** — Groaning is involuntary expression, not received. Limited.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative.
**T2.2** — Soul-level once. **T2.3** — Heart in 3 occurrences — the heart's sound under affliction (cf. Psa 38:8, the tumult/groaning of the heart). **T2.4 / T2.5** — No mind or other soul-subset. Negative.
**T2.6** — Linked to the flesh once; groaning is intrinsically respiratory/bodily (the breath), though a specific organ is rarely named.
**T2.7** — Direction runs soul→body: inner affliction registers as breathed sound — groaning is the autonomic voice of grief (cf. T7.3, vagal mediation).
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the affliction that compresses the person — received-from-outside (oppression, sorrow).
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin is consistently external pressure (Egyptian bondage, enemy oppression, accumulated sorrow).
**T2.10** — Movement runs soul→body (the breath); the Exodus texts add a movement outward-and-upward (groaning rising to God).

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Sighing/groaning is **affect** (23).
**T3.1–T3.3, T3.5–T3.11** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative findings throughout — groaning is a pure affective-somatic sound, engaging no cognitive, volitional, moral, or relational faculty in the evidence.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged wholly: groaning is the breathed form of grief-affect.
**Pattern** — Groaning is affect at its most involuntary and least articulate; it engages a single faculty and is the audible underside of inner pain (A), with which it most co-occurs.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God hears and responds to groaning, remembering his covenant and acting (Exo 2:24; Judg 2:18). Basis: covenant-remembrance, responsive to the cry.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Groaning rises to God even without words (Exo 2:23) — the wordless human-to-God cry; the posture is helpless affliction.
**T4.3 Giving / T4.4 Receiving** — Not extended or received person-to-person. Negative (the related `sustenazō`, to groan *with*, lies at the cluster's communal edge but is sparse here).
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates corporately (the nation's groaning) and individually; scope is the afflicted.
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates groaning to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Groaning itself is not transformative; it is the sound of the unrelieved that triggers divine action (Exo) rather than changing the groaner.
**T5.2 Sequence** — In the Exodus narrative: bondage → groaning → God hears → deliverance — groaning is the pivot that moves God.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the involuntary breath-release of compressed affliction.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Groaning is the immediate, involuntary **product of** sustained suffering and oppression (Exo 2:23; Lam 1:22).
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Eze 9:4 marks those who "sigh and groan" over abominations for preservation — groaning here is the mark of the righteous remnant, tying it into a formative/discriminating role.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Within these lemmas, limited; the redemptive-groaning trajectory belongs to the NT terms beyond this set. Largely negative.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Most same-verse co-occurrence with **A (inner pain)** (×4) — groaning is the audible form of the felt ache; lesser with D, B, C, H, I, J.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Groaning accompanies affliction and can precede divine deliverance (Exodus).
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by oppression and sorrow; in Eze 9:4 it constitutes the mark of the grieving righteous.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `a.nach`/`a.na.chah` share the sighing root; `ne.a.qah`/`a.na.qah` the groaning root. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbours are **D (weeping)** and **A (inner pain)**. The distinction is of channel: groaning is breath-voice; weeping is tears; A is the unexpressed felt state. Distinction of kind/medium.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `a.nach` (to sigh/groan), `a.na.chah` (sighing), `ne.a.qah`/`a.na.qah` (groaning); roots denote the breathed sound of distress.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: verb (to sigh/groan) and noun (sighing, groaning) — sound as act and condition.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: sigh ↔ groan ↔ moan ↔ groaning — the breath-voice band.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the act (`a.nach`) from the condition (`a.na.chah`).
**T7.1.5–.7** — No internal opposite, person-type, or supplication term. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `a.nach`/`ne.a.qah` are joined in the NT by `stenagmos`/`sustenazō`; the wordless-groan motif carries into the NT's groaning-for-redemption (beyond this set).
**T7.1.10** — The arc presents groaning as the inarticulate cry that God hears — from the Exodus bondage to the marked remnant of Ezekiel.
**T7.2.1** — In Exo 2:24 the groaning term is the grammatical object of God's hearing — the cry that God heard.
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Exodus, Judges), psalm (6; 38), lament (Lamentations), and prophecy (Ezekiel).
**T7.2.4** — Settings are covenantal (Exodus deliverance), lament (Lamentations), and prophetic-discriminating (Eze 9).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Exodus 2:23–24** — the people's groaning rises to God, who hears it and remembers his covenant; this most fully expresses groaning as the wordless cry that moves God to act.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals groaning as covenant-effective — the inarticulate sound that nonetheless triggers divine remembrance and redemption.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: physiology/psychosomatics of grief — the autonomic, vagally-mediated basis of sighing and groaning (Porges; psychosomatic research).
**T7.3.2** — The science illuminates groaning as a genuine involuntary autonomic response that cannot be fully suppressed — matching the biblical treatment of groaning as the cry that rises without deliberate composition.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science locates groaning in autonomic regulation; the biblical material makes the same wordless sound covenant-effective — heard and answered by God (Exodus).
**T7.3.4** — The somatic-involuntariness finding supports treating groaning (with weeping) as the body's automatic grief channel — a thread for the cross-cluster somatic synthesis.
