## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Torment images God indirectly as the righteous judge whose final judgement includes the torment of the impenitent and the devil (Rev 14:10; Rev 20:10) and from whom the demons recoil, fearing torment "before the time" (Mat 8:29). God is not the bearer of torment but the one whose judgement defines its ultimate reference.
**T0.1.2** — Torment is **not** related to God as a state he bears; no divine role is recorded on these verses. It is borne by sufferers, the lost (Luk 16:23), and finally the adversary (Rev 20:10).
**T0.1.3** — The absence of any divine bearing frames torment as an extremity of creaturely (and adversarial) suffering, not a divine attribute.
**T0.2.1** — Torment serves, in the evidence, chiefly as the marker of judgement and of acute affliction (the rich man's torment, Luk 16:23–28); it equips nothing positive in the person — it is suffering at the extreme.
**T0.2.2** — Torment belongs wholly to the fallen/judgement order; it has no place in original design and is the very content of final judgement (Rev 20:10).
**T0.2.3** — Strongly future-oriented, but toward judgement rather than fullness: torment is the eschatological end of the impenitent and the devil (Rev 14:10; 20:10).
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties torment to the image of God. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Torment is eschatological/apocalyptic in reference: Revelation deploys it for final judgement (Rev 14:10; 20:10), a present-foretaste→final-reality direction.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Torment / acute affliction": suffering at the torture level — imposed, overwhelming, often eschatological. The name signals extremity.
**T1.1.2** — `basanizō` (to torture, torment), `basanos` (torment) — Greek terms from the language of testing/torture, applied to acute affliction.
**T1.1.3** — The terms carry an implication of imposition — torment is inflicted, whether by circumstance, judgement, or adversary.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly an **action** (12) over status (3) — torment is something done to the sufferer.
**T1.2.2** — Simple — a single extremity of affliction.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: rest / relief from suffering (Rev 14:11 sets "no rest" against it). Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Torment excludes rest and ease utterly.
**T1.3.3** — It is the severe pole of suffering, beyond ordinary pain (A) or pressure (B).
**T1.4.1** — Operates physically (a boat tormented by the waves, Mat 14:24; Mar 6:48), bodily-medically (those severely afflicted/tormented, Mat 4:24; Mat 8:6), psychologically (Lot's righteous soul tormented, 2Pe 2:8), and eschatologically (Luk 16:23; Rev 20:10).
**T1.4.2** — Operates differently by register — physical buffeting, bodily disease, inner moral torment, and final judgement.
**T1.4.3** — Torment can issue in a cry (the demons' "have you come to torment us?", Mat 8:29), but it is not characteristically speech-based.
**T1.5.1** — The immediate response is a cry of protest or dread (Mat 8:29; Mar 5:7; Luk 8:28).
**T1.5.2** — Variable across the registers (physical, moral, eschatological).
**T1.6.1** — Sustained torment is, in its eschatological form, unending ("the smoke of their torment goes up for ever", Rev 14:11).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Torment is inflicted, not received as a gift. Negative on reception-framing.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative. **T2.2** — Soul-level once (Lot's righteous soul tormented, 2Pe 2:8). **T2.3 / T2.4 / T2.5** — No heart, mind, or other soul-subset location. Negative.
**T2.6** — Torment is often bodily in reference (disease, buffeting) but rarely tied to a named organ; the body is the site, not a specific part.
**T2.7** — Where bodily, torment is imposed on the body from without (waves, disease) — an outside→body direction rather than soul→body.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is **received-from-outside** — inflicted by circumstance, disease, judgement, or adversary; never generated within.
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin varies: natural (storm), pathological (disease), moral (Lot's vexed soul), and judicial (final judgement).
**T2.10** — No constitutional movement across levels evidenced. Negative.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Torment engages **affect** (4) with a **cognitive** component (3 — the tormenting awareness, e.g. Lot's soul tormented by what he saw, 2Pe 2:8).
**T3.1 Perception** — Engaged at the edge in 2Pe 2:8 (tormented by *seeing and hearing* lawless deeds), but not characteristically. **T3.2 Cognition** — Engaged where torment is the anguish of awareness (2Pe 2:8). **T3.3, T3.5, T3.7, T3.11** — Not engaged. Negative.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged: torment is acute affective suffering.
**T3.6 Volition / T3.8 Moral evaluation / T3.9 Conscience** — Not engaged at faculty level (the moral framing of judgement-torment sits in valence/context).
**Pattern** — Torment is affect at its extreme, occasionally joined by the cognition of unbearable awareness; it is distinguished from ordinary pain (A) by intensity and imposition.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — Indirect: God's final judgement is the reference of eschatological torment (Rev 14:10; 20:10), but no verse shows God extending torment as a relational gift. Basis: judicial.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Not present; torment is not directed Godward. Negative.
**T4.3 Giving / T4.4 Receiving** — Torment is inflicted (received from circumstance, disease, or judgement), not given person-to-person as a benefit.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates across the living (disease, distress) and the dead/judged (Luk 16; Rev).
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — **Yes — uniquely.** Torment is the site of an adversarial interface: demons dread it and protest being tormented "before the time" (Mat 8:29; Mar 5:7; Luk 8:28; cf. the locusts tormenting men, Rev 9:5; the devil's final torment, Rev 20:10). This is the only M03 characteristic with a developed spiritual-beings interface.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Torment is not formative in the persons who bear it; it is endured or judicial. Negative on positive transformation.
**T5.2 Sequence** — In the eschatological texts, impenitence → final torment (Rev 14:9–11).
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is infliction — by force (waves), disease, awareness (2Pe 2:8), or judgement.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Torment **is** suffering at its extreme — the torture-level of affliction; it is suffering itself, not a response to it.
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — No formative/sanctifying role in the evidence. Negative.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Strongly oriented: torment is a defining content of final judgement (Rev 14:10; 20:10) — the cluster's most eschatologically loaded characteristic.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Sparse same-verse co-occurrence (with H once); torment largely stands alone as an extremity.
**T6.2 Sequential** — In judgement texts, follows impenitence; in narrative, follows the encounter with disease or the demonic.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by storm, disease, awareness, or judgement; constitutes the severe pole of suffering.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `basanizō`/`basanos` are a distinct NT family (from the testing-stone metaphor); little vocabulary overlap with the OT grief terms. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **A (inner pain)** at the severe end; the distinction is intensity and imposition — torment is acute, inflicted, often eschatological or adversarial, where A is the ordinary felt ache.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `basanizō` (to torment/torture), `basanos` (torment); the root lies in the touchstone/testing metaphor, sharpened to torture-level affliction.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: verb (to torment) and noun (torment) — predominantly verbal/inflicted.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: torment ↔ torture ↔ severe suffering ↔ being buffeted.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the act (`basanizō`) from the state/place (`basanos`, "the place of torment", Luk 16:28).
**T7.1.5** — Opposite: rest (Rev 14:11, "no rest"), present antonymically.
**T7.1.6–.7** — No person-type or supplication term. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — NT-only; there is no OT lexical antecedent in this set — torment's intensification is a NT development (esp. its eschatological use).
**T7.1.9** — The eschatological-torment usage (Revelation) is effectively a NT-period deployment of the term for final judgement.
**T7.1.10** — The arc is NT and bimodal: present acute affliction (storm, disease, vexed soul) and final judgement (Revelation).
**T7.2.1** — In Luk 16:23 the term names the rich man's condition in Hades — "in torment" defines his state.
**T7.2.2** — Forms: gospel narrative (Matthew, Mark, Luke), epistle (2 Peter), and apocalyptic (Revelation) — the apocalyptic carrying the eschatological weight.
**T7.2.4** — Settings: the demonic encounter, the deathbed/afterlife (Luk 16), and final judgement (Revelation).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Luke 16:23–28** — the rich man "in torment" in Hades most fully expresses torment as acute, conscious, irremediable affliction with an eschatological frame.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals torment as a *conscious, communicative* extremity — the tormented man still sees, remembers, and pleads, making torment a state of unrelieved awareness, not oblivion.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: clinical psychology of suffering (Cassell, *The Nature of Suffering*).
**T7.3.2** — Cassell's account of suffering as a threat to the whole person illuminates torment as the extremity where the person is overwhelmed, not merely pained — matching the term's reach beyond physical hurt (2Pe 2:8, the tormented righteous soul).
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science has no category for eschatological or adversarial torment; the biblical material's judicial and demonic uses (Rev; Mat 8:29) lie wholly outside the clinical frame.
**T7.3.4** — The science surfaces nothing the evidence misses; rather, the biblical torment vocabulary exceeds the science's scope at the eschatological/adversarial end — a divergence to carry into synthesis.
