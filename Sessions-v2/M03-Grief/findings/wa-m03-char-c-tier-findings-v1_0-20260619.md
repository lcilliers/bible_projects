## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Mourning images God as the one who reverses it: he "turns mourning into dancing" (Psa 30:11), and the gospel promise is that mourners are comforted (Mat 5:4). Where God acts, he is the comforter and reverser of mourning; where he calls for it, he is the judge summoning lament (Isa 22:12; Amo 8:10).
**T0.1.2** — Mourning is related to God as **agent** in 3 verses (Amo 8:10; Isa 22:12; Psa 30:11) — he both commands mourning in judgement and ends it in mercy. It is not borne by God as his own state in this vocabulary.
**T0.1.3** — God's not mourning, but reversing mourning, frames the characteristic as the creaturely response to loss that God answers.
**T0.2.1** — Mourning's served purpose is to honour the lost and to mark the gravity of judgement (the funeral rites of 2Sa 3:31; the prophetic summons of Joe 2:12); rightly directed, it opens onto comfort (Mat 5:4).
**T0.2.2** — Mourning belongs to the fallen condition (it answers death and loss), yet Scripture also **commands** it (Joe 2:12; Jam 4:9), making it a rightful response within the present order rather than a defect.
**T0.2.3** — Mourning is oriented toward future fullness: Mat 5:4 promises the mourners' comfort, and Rev 21:4 promises mourning (`penthos`) shall be no more — its present operation anticipates its own consolation.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties mourning to the image of God as an attribute; it is the creaturely answer to loss. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Mourning carries covenantal-eschatological typology: the commanded lament of the prophets points beyond the immediate loss to covenant judgement, and Mat 5:4 / Rev 21:4 point present mourning toward eschatological comfort — a present→future (human→consummation) direction.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Mourning / lament": grief enacted and observed — the recognised condition and acts of bereavement. The name signals action and observance, not the inner ache alone.
**T1.1.2** — `a.val` (to mourn), `e.vel` (mourning), `sa.phad` (to wail, beat the breast), `mis.ped` (lamentation); Greek `pen.the.o`/`penthos` (to mourn/mourning), `koptō` (to beat oneself in grief). The terms name visible, often ritual, acts.
**T1.1.3** — The vocabulary is relational and directional — mourning is *over* or *for* a person or loss (object person ×28), orienting the enquiry toward the bond that is broken.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly an **action** (action 78, status 44, quality 8) — mourning is something done, enacted in rite and gesture.
**T1.2.2** — Compound in practice: mourning gathers weeping (D), lament-speech, and bodily gesture (sackcloth, beating the breast) into a recognised observance.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: dancing/feasting/joy (Psa 30:11 explicitly opposes mourning to dancing). Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Mourning excludes celebration; the prophets make the two mutually exclusive (Amo 8:10, feasts turned to mourning).
**T1.3.3** — It ends where the loss is comforted or the rite completed; its boundary with weeping (D) is that mourning is the observance, weeping the tears within it.
**T1.4.1** — Operates as enacted rite (funeral, sackcloth), as a summoned communal act (Joe 2), and as a settled condition of bereavement (Gen 37:35, "I shall go down to Sheol mourning").
**T1.4.2** — Operates differently by direction: self-mourning (one's own loss), mourning *for* another (the dead), and commanded corporate mourning (covenant judgement).
**T1.4.3** — Mourning is strongly speech-based — it is carried in lament and dirge (`mis.ped`, the formal lamentation; the dirge over the man of God, 1Ki 13:30).
**T1.5.1** — The immediate response is bodily and ritual: tearing garments, sackcloth, weeping, refusing comfort (Gen 37:34–35; 2Sa 3:31).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent in its ritual register across the evidence, though intensity varies with the loss.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained mourning settles into a prolonged condition (Jacob's refusal of comfort, Gen 37:35); rightly held, it opens onto comfort (Mat 5:4).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Mourning is enacted, not received as a gift; the relevant condition is permission to grieve (culturally given), not reception. Limited.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative.
**T2.2** — Soul-level in 2 occurrences — mourning reaching the innermost self.
**T2.3** — Located in the **heart** in 7 occurrences — the seat where mourning is felt before it is enacted; the heart's feeling function under bereavement.
**T2.4 / T2.5** — No mind-location and no other soul-subset. Negative.
**T2.6** — Linked to the **flesh** once; mourning is otherwise expressed through the body's gestures (sackcloth, ashes) rather than seated in a named organ.
**T2.7** — Direction runs soul→body: inner grief is expressed outward in bodily rite — mourning is the paradigm of the soul made visible in the body.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the loss itself — received-from-outside (a death, a defeat, a judgement) — not generated within.
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin is consistently external loss, varying only in scale (personal bereavement to national catastrophe).
**T2.10** — Movement runs from inner grief outward into enacted, communal observance — the clearest soul→body→community movement in the cluster.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Mourning is **affect** (130) expressed in action.
**T3.1 Perception / T3.2 Cognition / T3.3 Memory** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative. (Mourning answers a remembered loss, but the engaged faculty is affect, not memory.)
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged wholly: mourning is grief-affect given enacted form.
**T3.5 Creativity** — Not engaged, though the dirge/lament is a composed form; the faculty tagged is affect, not creativity. Negative.
**T3.6 Volition / T3.7 Agency** — Engaged indirectly: mourning is enacted (acts done), and can be commanded or refused (Eze 24:16), implying a volitional dimension — but the lexical faculty is affect; volition appears in the commanded/forbidden framing (T0.2.2).
**T3.8 Moral evaluation** — Not at faculty level, though mourning is uniquely morally regulated (see valence). 
**T3.9 Conscience / T3.11 Relational** — Relational dimension is strong in object (mourning *for* persons ×28) but not engaged as the relational faculty per se.
**Pattern** — Mourning is affect enacted; among the felt-states it is the most behaviourally and morally governed, which distinguishes it from the purely interior A.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God turns mourning to dancing (Psa 30:11) and promises comfort to mourners (Mat 5:4); he also summons mourning in judgement (Isa 22:12; Joe 2:12). Basis: responsive and covenantal.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Mourning is directed Godward in repentant lament (Joe 2:12, "return to me... with mourning"); the posture is contrition.
**T4.3 Giving** — Mourning is extended to others as shared lament — mourners join the bereaved (Gen 50:10; Joh 11:31, the Jews who came to mourn with Mary). 
**T4.4 Receiving** — The bereaved receive the community's mourning as comfort or refuse it (Jacob "refused to be comforted", Gen 37:35).
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Mourning operates within bonds (family, nation) and is bounded by covenant: Ezekiel is forbidden to mourn his wife as a sign (Eze 24:16–17), showing mourning can be covenantally suspended.
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates mourning to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Mourning changes condition and orientation: rightly held it moves toward comfort (Mat 5:4) and can issue in repentance (Joe 2; 2Cor 7:7's report of the Corinthians' mourning).
**T5.2 Sequence** — A before→during→after is visible in the prophetic summons: sin → called mourning/repentance → restoration.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the enacted marking of loss (rite, lament), which both expresses and processes the grief.
**T5.4 Suffering** — Mourning is the **response to** suffering and loss; it is the appointed form for bearing affliction (Ecc 7:2–4, the house of mourning). Suffering produces it; the rite gives it shape.
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Mourning participates in formation: "it is better to go to the house of mourning" (Ecc 7:2) is presented as instructive for the living heart; godly mourning works repentance (2Cor 7:7,10).
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Strongly oriented: Mat 5:4 and Rev 21:4 promise the end of mourning — present lament anticipates eschatological comfort.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Strong same-verse pairing with **D (weeping)** — 19 shared verses — mourning and tears are a near-fused expressive pair; lesser co-occurrence with A and E.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Mourning typically accompanies or follows the loss and surrounds weeping; in repentance sequences it precedes restoration.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by loss; constitutively gathers weeping, lament-speech, and bodily rite.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `sa.phad` and `mis.ped` share the lamentation root; `pen.the.o`/`penthos` is the NT mourning family. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **D (weeping)**. The distinction is of form: mourning is the observance (rite, lament, condition), weeping the shedding of tears within it — a distinction of kind/scope, not degree.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `a.val` (to mourn), `sa.phad` (to wail/beat the breast), `e.vel`/`mis.ped` (mourning, lamentation); Greek `pen.the.o`/`penthos`, `koptō` (to beat oneself); roots denote enacted lament.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: verbs (the act of mourning), nouns (the condition/lamentation) — mourning is action-led.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: mourn ↔ lament ↔ wail ↔ bewail — the enacted register of grief.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the act (`a.val`, `sa.phad`) from the formal lament-piece (`mis.ped`, the dirge).
**T7.1.5** — Opposite: joy/dancing/feasting vocabulary (Psa 30:11), present antonymically.
**T7.1.6** — A person-type term is present: the **mourner** (`a.vel`, the one who mourns).
**T7.1.7** — No supplication term internal to the vocabulary. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `a.val`/`sa.phad` continue into NT `pen.the.o`; the Beatitude (Mat 5:4) takes up the mourning vocabulary and gives it gospel reversal.
**T7.1.10** — The arc runs from patriarchal and prophetic lament to the Beatitude's blessed mourners and Revelation's end of mourning — loss answered, then abolished.
**T7.2.1** — In Mat 5:4 the mourning term carries the blessing's protasis — the mourners are the subject of promised comfort.
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Genesis, Samuel), prophecy (Amos, Joel, Ezekiel), wisdom (Ecclesiastes), gospel (Matthew), and epistle (2 Corinthians, James).
**T7.2.4** — Settings are funerary/liturgical (2Sa 3:31), covenantal-prophetic (Joe 2; Eze 24), and eschatological (Rev 21:4).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Matthew 5:4** — "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted" most fully expresses mourning as the grief that God answers with comfort, gathering the OT lament tradition into gospel promise.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals mourning as blessable — not merely endured but pronounced blessed because of the comfort it opens onto.
**T7.3.1** — Frameworks: clinical grief/bereavement research (Bowlby's attachment, Kübler-Ross, Stroebe's dual-process) and social/cultural psychology of communal mourning (Durkheim, Rosenblatt).
**T7.3.2** — Bowlby's finding that grief is the measure of attachment illuminates why the mourning vocabulary is so extensive and intense; Durkheim's account of communal ritual illuminates the corporate lament texts (Joel; Lamentations).
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science frames mourning therapeutically (a process toward restored function); the biblical material includes commanded and covenantal mourning, and sustained lament addressed to God that does not force resolution — a register the dual-process model under-captures.
**T7.3.4** — The cross-cultural finding that permission-to-grieve shapes outcomes invites testing the biblical mourning rites as a structured permission — coherent with the prophetic summons to mourn, and a thread for the cross-cluster pass.
