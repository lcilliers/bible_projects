## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Debilitating grief images God most pointedly at Isa 53:10, where "it was the will of the LORD to crush him; he has put him to grief" — God is the agent who lays grievous sickness on the Servant. The divine character reflected is sovereign purpose working even through grief that crushes.
**T0.1.2** — Debilitating grief is related to God as **agent** once (Isa 53:10); it is not borne by God himself, except as the Servant (read christologically) bears the grief God lays on him.
**T0.1.3** — That God lays grief but is not (in this vocabulary) himself made sick frames the characteristic as a creaturely weakening that God can ordain to purpose (Isa 53).
**T0.2.1** — Where a purpose appears, it is the Servant's grief working redemption (Isa 53:10, the grief that makes him an offering); otherwise debilitating grief equips nothing — it depletes.
**T0.2.2** — It belongs to the fallen condition — grief that wounds incurably (Jer 30:12, "your hurt is incurable") — except where God ordains it to purpose (Isa 53).
**T0.2.3** — No general orientation to future fullness; its trajectory is toward sickness/wasting, save the Servant's grief which is oriented to a redemptive outcome (Isa 53:10–11). Limited.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties debilitating grief to the image of God. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Isa 53:10 is christological/typological: the LORD putting the Servant to grief establishes the divine→human pattern in which grief becomes redemptive offering.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Debilitating grief": grief that weakens, sickens, and faints the person — where sorrow crosses into bodily failure. The name signals the toll grief takes on strength.
**T1.1.2** — `cha.lah` (to be/​become weak, sick, grieved), `dav.va` (faint), `ne.dud` (tossing); the terms denote grief shading into sickness and exhaustion.
**T1.1.3** — The terms carry a somatic implication — grief registering as the body's weakening.
**T1.2.1** — Predominantly an **action/process** (12) — the becoming-weak of grief — with some quality (faint, 3).
**T1.2.2** — Compound at the edge: it fuses felt grief with bodily debilitation.
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: strength / health / a sustained heart. Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — It excludes vigour; grief here saps the body's capacity.
**T1.3.3** — It is the point where felt grief (A) or its causes cross into bodily failure — the boundary with A is the somatic collapse.
**T1.4.1** — Operates as grievous wounding (Jer 30:12, an incurable hurt), heart-sickness ("hope deferred makes the heart sick", the `cha.lah` idiom), faintness (`dav.va`), and restless tossing (Job 7:4).
**T1.4.2** — Operates individually (sickened grief) and nationally (Nah 3:19, an incurable wound).
**T1.4.3** — Debilitating grief can issue in a cry of distress (Jer 4:31) but is not characteristically speech-based; it is more often the silent sinking of strength.
**T1.5.1** — The immediate response is faintness/weakening — the body giving way (`dav.va`; Job 7:4, the restless wasting of the night).
**T1.5.2** — Consistent: grief presenting as bodily decline.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained debilitating grief becomes chronic sickness and incurable wounding (Jer 30:12; Nah 3:19).
**T1.7.1–.3** — It is undergone, not received as a gift. Negative.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative. **T2.2** — Soul-level in 2 occurrences. **T2.3** — Heart in 3 occurrences — the heart made sick by grief (the `cha.lah` heart-sickness idiom). **T2.4 / T2.5** — No mind or other soul-subset. Negative.
**T2.6** — The body/flesh is the implicit site (faintness, wasting, sickness), though debilitation is described as a whole-body weakening more than a single organ.
**T2.7** — Direction runs soul→body: inner grief saps bodily strength — the clearest cluster case (with E) of grief feeding back into the body.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is the grief or wound received-from-outside (betrayal, threat, loss), and once divinely laid (Isa 53:10).
**T2.9.2–.3** — Origin varies: relational (1Sa 22:8, none grieved for the speaker), threat (Jer 4:31), accumulated groaning (Lam 1:22), divine purpose (Isa 53:10).
**T2.10** — Movement runs soul→body (grief into bodily failure); no spirit-level movement. This is the characteristic that most clearly shows grief crossing the soul-body boundary downward.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Debilitating grief is **affect** (12) with a **perceptual** component (3 — the faint/sick perception of one's own weakening).
**T3.1 Perception** — Engaged at the edge: the faintness terms carry a perceptual sense of the body's failing (`dav.va`). **T3.2, T3.3, T3.5, T3.7, T3.11** — Not engaged. Negative.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged: it is grief-affect crossing into bodily depletion.
**T3.6 Volition / T3.8 Moral evaluation / T3.9 Conscience** — Not engaged at faculty level (Isa 1:5's "whole heart faint" carries a moral context, but the engaged faculty is affect/perception).
**Pattern** — Debilitating grief engages affect with a perceptual-somatic edge; it is the characteristic where grief most clearly becomes a bodily event, distinguishing it from the purely interior A.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God lays grief on the Servant to purpose (Isa 53:10); basis: redemptive, sovereign.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Not characteristically directed Godward in these verses. Negative.
**T4.3 Giving / T4.4 Receiving** — Not extended or received as a benefit; debilitating grief is incurred from loss, threat, or (for the Servant) divine appointment.
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates over individuals (sick heart) and nations (incurable wound, Nah 3:19; Jer 30:12).
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates it to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Debilitating grief mostly depletes rather than transforms; the singular exception is the Servant's grief, ordained to redemptive transformation (Isa 53:10–11).
**T5.2 Sequence** — In Isa 53 the sequence is divine will → the Servant put to grief → offering → fruit ("he shall see his offspring").
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is the sapping of bodily strength under grief (the somatic toll, T7.3).
**T5.4 Suffering** — Debilitating grief is the **product of** suffering at the point where it overwhelms the body — incurable wounds, faintness, wasting (Jer 30:12; Job 7:4).
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — No general formative role; the Servant's grief (Isa 53) is uniquely redemptive-formative for others.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — Limited; the Servant's grief is oriented to a redemptive outcome (Isa 53:10–11), but the OT wasting-grief verses carry no eschatological orientation. Largely negative.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Same-verse most with **A (inner pain)** (×2), and with B, D, E — debilitation is where the felt ache (A) and the cry (E) cross into bodily failure.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Tends to follow sustained grief (the chronic, incurable end of A/B); in Isa 53 it follows divine appointment.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by sustained grief, wounding, and (for the Servant) divine will; constitutes the somatic collapse of grief.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — `cha.lah` ranges across "be weak / be sick / be grieved", bridging grief and illness vocabulary. (Cross-cluster — flagged.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbour is **A (inner pain)**. The distinction is the somatic outcome: A is the felt ache; debilitation is that ache (or its cause) crossing into bodily weakening and sickness — a distinction of constitutional consequence (soul→body).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `cha.lah` (to be weak / sick / grieved), `dav.va` (faint), `ne.dud` (tossing); roots in sickness, faintness, and restlessness.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: verb (to become weak/grieved), adjective/quality (faint) — debilitation is process-led.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: grievous ↔ sick ↔ weak ↔ faint ↔ wasting — the band where grief meets bodily failure.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the process (`cha.lah`, becoming weak/sick) from the resulting state (faint).
**T7.1.5–.7** — No internal opposite, person-type, or supplication term. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT-only in this set; `cha.lah`'s grief-sense is a Hebrew development of a wider weakness/sickness verb.
**T7.1.10** — The arc runs from the incurable wounds of the prophets (Jer 30:12; Nah 3:19) to the Servant's redemptive grief (Isa 53:10) — grief that wastes, and grief that God ordains to heal.
**T7.2.1** — In Isa 53:10 the grief term carries the clause of divine purpose — the LORD's will to put the Servant to grief.
**T7.2.2** — Forms: narrative (1 Samuel), wisdom (Job; Ecclesiastes), prophecy (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Nahum, Amos), and lament (Lamentations).
**T7.2.4** — Settings: personal lament, prophetic woe (incurable wounds), and the Servant Song (Isa 53).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Isaiah 53:10** — "it was the will of the LORD to crush him; he has put him to grief" most fully expresses debilitating grief as divinely ordained and redemptively purposed.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals that grief which crushes can be the appointed means of redemption — the Servant's debilitating grief made an offering for many.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: physiology/psychosomatics of grief (Engel's biopsychosocial model; the bereavement-physiology research — immune suppression, broken-heart syndrome, fatigue).
**T7.3.2** — The science documents that grief produces measurable bodily debilitation — immune suppression, cardiovascular effects (Takotsubo "broken heart"), fatigue — giving the biblical wasting/sickness vocabulary a literal physiological basis.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science treats grief-debilitation as a health outcome to be mitigated; the biblical material includes grief-debilitation ordained to redemptive purpose (Isa 53), a meaning outside the clinical frame.
**T7.3.4** — The bereavement-physiology research supports treating debilitation as the genuine somatic end of grief (with E) — and the broken-heart finding invites testing the heart-sickness idiom in synthesis.
