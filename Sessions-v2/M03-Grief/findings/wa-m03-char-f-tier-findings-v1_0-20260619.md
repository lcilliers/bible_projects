## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design
**T0.1.1** — Bitterness images God most strikingly as one who can **administer** it: Naomi says "the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me" and "the hand of the LORD has gone out against me" (Rut 1:13,20), and Job says "God... has made my soul bitter" (Job 27:2) and "filled me with bitterness" (Job 9:18). The divine character reflected is sovereign even over the soul's embittering — bitterness can be a dispensation of God's hand, not only a human sin.
**T0.1.2** — Bitterness is related to God as **agent** (Rut 1:13; Job 27:2) and **giver** (Job 9:18) in 3 verses; it is not borne by God, but God is named as its source in the sufferer's complaint.
**T0.1.3** — That God administers but does not bear bitterness frames it as a creaturely soul-state that can fall under divine providence in suffering.
**T0.2.1** — Where a purpose appears, bitterness is the soul's honest naming of grievous loss (Naomi renaming herself Mara, Rut 1:20) — it serves as truthful lament; it is never commended as a settled disposition.
**T0.2.2** — Bitterness belongs to the fallen condition — a response to loss and perceived injustice — and the NT explicitly forbids it as a settled stance (Col 3:19, husbands not to be harsh/bitter).
**T0.2.3** — No verse orients bitterness toward future fullness; its trajectory is either honest lament resolved or a forbidden hardening. Negative on eschatological orientation.
**T0.3.1–.3** — No verse ties bitterness to the image of God. Negative on image-bearing.
**T0.4.1–.2** — Bitterness is not deployed typologically in its own right; it functions within lament (Naomi, Job) and as a moral danger to be avoided.

## T1 — Definition
**T1.1.1** — "Bitterness of soul": grief turned to an embittered condition, carrying a moral edge. The name signals the taste-metaphor of a soul made bitter.
**T1.1.2** — `mar` (bitter), `ma.rar` (to be/​make bitter), `mar.rah`/`me.mer`/`mam.ror` (bitterness); Greek `pikrainō` (to embitter), `pikrōs` (bitterly). The root is the taste of bitterness, applied to the soul.
**T1.1.3** — The terms carry a moral-evaluative implication (a sustained stance toward what was wrong or lost), orienting the enquiry toward appraisal as well as feeling.
**T1.2.1** — A mix of **quality** (the bitter soul, 14) and **action** (to embitter, 13) — bitterness is both a condition and an act.
**T1.2.2** — Compound at the edges: bitterness combines felt grief with an evaluative stance toward its cause (the soul that judges its loss unjust).
**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite: sweetness / contentment (the taste antonym), and a settled, forgiving heart. Cross-cluster, named.
**T1.3.2** — Bitterness excludes peace with one's circumstances; the NT sets it against forbearance (Col 3:19).
**T1.3.3** — It ends where lament is honest and resolved; it hardens where the evaluative stance becomes settled — the boundary with anger (M02) lies here (the held-open `ma.rar` anger-bleed).
**T1.4.1** — Operates as the soul's embittered condition (1Sa 1:10, Hannah "bitter of soul"), as an act of embittering (Exo 1:14, lives made bitter), and as bitter weeping (Mat 26:75, Peter "wept bitterly").
**T1.4.2** — Operates differently by direction: bitterness borne (the grieving soul), bitterness inflicted (oppressors embittering lives, Exo 1:14), and bitterness named against God (Job, Naomi).
**T1.4.3** — Bitterness finds voice in bitter complaint and bitter weeping — it has a vocal, expressive mode (Mat 26:75; Job's bitter speech, Job 7:11, "I will speak in the bitterness of my soul").
**T1.5.1** — The immediate expression is bitter speech or bitter weeping (Job 7:11; Mat 26:75).
**T1.5.2** — Variable: sometimes voiced complaint, sometimes silent embittered condition.
**T1.6.1** — Sustained bitterness hardens into a fixed retrospective stance (Linden's embitterment, T7.3) — the NT's reason for forbidding it (Col 3:19).
**T1.7.1–.3** — Bitterness is incurred or administered, not received as a gift. Limited.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries
**T2.1** — No spirit-level location. Negative.
**T2.2** — Located at the **soul** level in 10 occurrences — the clearest seat-signal of any small characteristic; "bitterness of soul" (1Sa 1:10; Job 7:11) is the idiom. Bitterness is pre-eminently a soul-state.
**T2.3** — Located in the **heart** in 3 occurrences (e.g. Pro 14:10, "the heart knows its own bitterness").
**T2.4 / T2.5** — No mind or other soul-subset location. Negative on mind (though the moral-evaluative faculty, T3.8, gives bitterness a cognitive colouring without a mind-seat).
**T2.6** — Linked to the flesh once; bitterness is otherwise an interior soul-state.
**T2.7** — Where bodily, direction runs soul→body (the bitter soul registering bodily); limited data.
**T2.9.1** — Origin is double and distinctive: bitterness is **received-from-outside** (the loss/injustice that embitters) and, uniquely, **bestowed-by-God** in the sufferer's account (Rut 1:13; Job 9:18) — the only characteristic with a clear God-as-giver origin.
**T2.9.2** — Origin is therefore multiple: circumstance, oppressor, and (as named in lament) God's own hand.
**T2.9.3** — Origin shifts: inflicted by oppressors (Exo 1:14), incurred by loss (2Ki 4:27), attributed to God (Job; Naomi).
**T2.10** — No constitutional movement across levels evidenced beyond soul/heart co-location.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties
Bitterness is the cluster's one characteristic with a **moral-evaluative** core (moral_evaluation 12, affect 5).
**T3.1 Perception / T3.3 Memory / T3.5 Creativity / T3.7 Agency / T3.11 Relational** — Not engaged at faculty level. Negative.
**T3.2 Cognition** — Engaged at the edge: bitterness involves an evaluative appraisal of the past (the soul judging its loss), bordering cognition, though the tagged faculty is moral-evaluation.
**T3.4 Affect** — Engaged: bitterness is felt grief with a bitter quality.
**T3.6 Volition** — Engaged negatively: the NT command not to be embittered (Col 3:19) implies bitterness is, in part, a stance that can be refused — a volitional dimension.
**T3.8 Moral evaluation** — Engaged centrally and uniquely: bitterness carries a sustained evaluative stance (this was unjust / should not have been) — the defining faculty of this characteristic.
**T3.9 Conscience** — Not directly engaged. Negative.
**Pattern** — Bitterness is distinguished from every other M03 characteristic by engaging moral-evaluation, not affect alone; it is grief that has taken up a verdict about its cause, which is why it can be both a legitimate lament and a forbidden hardening.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces
**T4.1 God→human** — God is named (in lament) as the one who has dealt bitterly (Rut 1:13; Job 27:2) — bitterness can be experienced as coming from God's hand in affliction. Basis: providential, within suffering.
**T4.2 Human→God** — Bitterness is voiced *to* and *against* God in lament (Job 7:11; 10:1) — the embittered soul brings its complaint Godward; the posture is raw, accusing honesty.
**T4.3 Giving** — Bitterness is inflicted by oppressors on others (Exo 1:14, the Egyptians made their lives bitter) — a negative "giving".
**T4.4 Receiving** — The oppressed receive embittered lives from their oppressors (Exo 1:14); the bereaved receive bitterness from loss (2Ki 4:27).
**T4.5 Boundaries** — Operates within bonds (a wife embittered, the NT warning to husbands, Col 3:19) and across enmity (oppressor to oppressed).
**T4.6 Spiritual beings** — No occurrence relates bitterness to angelic or adversarial beings. Negative.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension
**T5.1 Transformation** — Bitterness can deform (a hardened retrospective stance) or, as honest lament, be carried before God toward resolution (Hannah's bitter prayer answered, 1Sa 1:10–20).
**T5.2 Sequence** — In Hannah's narrative: bitter soul → poured-out prayer → no longer sad — bitterness named to God moves toward relief.
**T5.3 Mechanism** — Mechanism is appraisal: bitterness forms when loss is judged unjust or inflicted; it resolves through acknowledgement and reframing (T7.3, the justice dimension).
**T5.4 Suffering** — Bitterness is a **product of** suffering, specifically suffering perceived as unjust or inflicted (Exo 1:14; Job); it is the embittering of the soul under affliction.
**T5.5 Formation/sanctification** — Negatively formative: the NT forbids settled bitterness (Col 3:19), making its refusal part of sanctified relating; Hannah shows the godly path of carrying bitterness to God rather than nursing it.
**T5.6 Eschatological** — No eschatological orientation in the evidence. Negative.

## T6 — Structural Relationships
**T6.1 Co-occurrence** — Same-verse with **D (weeping)** most (×3) — bitter weeping (Mat 26:75); lesser with B, J, C.
**T6.2 Sequential** — Bitterness can follow distress unrelieved (the B→F hardening) and precede bitter weeping or complaint.
**T6.3 Causal/constitutive** — Produced by unjust/inflicted suffering; can itself produce bitter speech and bitter weeping.
**T6.4 Vocabulary/root sharing** — The `mar`/`ma.rar` root spans bitterness and the anger-bleed (`ma.rar` as "provoke/rage"), tying F to anger (M02); `pikrainō`/`pikria` is the NT bitterness family (cf. "root of bitterness", Heb 12:15, outside this lemma set). (Cross-cluster — flagged; the M02 boundary is the key open question.)
**T6.5 Distinctions** — Nearest neighbours are **A (inner pain)** and, by the anger-bleed, **M02 (anger)**. The distinction from A is the evaluative verdict bitterness carries (A is felt ache without the judgement); the distinction from anger is direction (bitterness is retrospective and self-corroding; anger is outward and active).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation
**T7.1.1** — `mar` (bitter), `ma.rar` (to be/​make bitter), `mar.rah`/`me.mer`/`mam.ror` (bitterness); Greek `pikrainō` (to embitter), `pikrōs` (bitterly); the root is the taste of bitterness transferred to the soul.
**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range: adjective (`mar`, bitter), verb (`ma.rar`/`pikrainō`, to embitter), noun (bitterness), adverb (`pikrōs`, bitterly) — a full grammatical spread.
**T7.1.3** — Semantic range: bitter ↔ bitterness ↔ bitterly ↔ embittered — the band of soul-bitterness.
**T7.1.4** — The vocabulary distinguishes the quality (`mar`, bitter) from the act (`ma.rar`, to embitter) from the manner (`pikrōs`, bitterly).
**T7.1.5** — Opposite: sweetness (the taste antonym), present implicitly.
**T7.1.6** — A person-type term is implicit in Naomi's renaming herself **Mara** ("bitter") — the bitter one.
**T7.1.7** — No supplication term. Negative.
**T7.1.8** — OT `mar`/`ma.rar` continue into NT `pikrainō`/`pikria`; the taste-metaphor of the embittered soul is stable, and the NT adds an explicit prohibition (Col 3:19; Eph 4:31 "let all bitterness... be put away", outside this set).
**T7.1.10** — The arc runs from the bitter soul of OT lament (Naomi, Hannah, Job) to the NT's forbidding of settled bitterness — grief's bitterness named honestly in the OT, guarded against in the NT.
**T7.2.1** — In Rut 1:20 the bitterness term carries Naomi's self-renaming — the predicate that reinterprets her identity through loss.
**T7.2.2** — Forms span narrative (Ruth; Exodus), wisdom (Proverbs; Job), gospel (Matthew), and epistle (Colossians).
**T7.2.4** — Settings are domestic-lament (Ruth; Hannah), the legal-dispute lament (Job), and ethical instruction (Colossians).
**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor: **Ruth 1:20** — "Call me Mara, for the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me" most fully expresses bitterness as a soul-state named before God, attributed to his hand, in honest grief.
**T7.2.6** — The anchor uniquely reveals bitterness as a name the grieving soul can take upon itself before God — lament that reinterprets identity through loss without yet being condemned.
**T7.3.1** — Framework: clinical stress/embitterment research — Linden's embitterment-disorder concept; the justice-appraisal literature.
**T7.3.2** — Linden's finding that bitterness is chronic, retrospectively oriented, and justice-focused (distinct from acute grief) illuminates the cluster's bitterness vocabulary precisely — bitterness sits at the intersection of emotional suffering and cognitive appraisal, matching its moral-evaluative faculty tag.
**T7.3.3** — Divergence: the science treats bitterness as a disorder to be resolved by addressing the justice dimension; the biblical material both legitimises bitterness as honest lament *before God* (Naomi, Job) and forbids it as a settled stance *toward others* (Col 3:19) — a moral distinction the clinical frame does not draw.
**T7.3.4** — The science's grief-vs-bitterness distinction (loss-focused vs justice-focused) directly supports keeping F distinct from A/C/D, and invites testing the `ma.rar` anger-bleed against M02 in the cross-cluster pass.
