## Science sections

### Section 1 — Envy and social comparison

**Primary field:** Social psychology — envy research

**Glosses covered:**
envy (phthonos), to envy (phthoneō), to make envious (parazēloō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Parrott and Smith's foundational distinction (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 1993) differentiates benign envy (wanting what another has, motivating self-improvement) from malicious envy (wanting the other to lose what they have, regardless of personal gain). Smith and Kim (*Psychological Bulletin*, 2007) established that envy is distinct from jealousy: envy is dyadic (self and rival who has what I lack), jealousy is triadic (self, valued partner, threatening rival). Social comparison theory (Festinger) is the cognitive foundation — envy arises from upward comparisons with similar others in domains of personal relevance. Van de Ven's research distinguishes cultures in which benign envy is normative (some European cultures) from those in which malicious envy dominates.

**Emergence — what causes envy to arise:**
Envy arises when: (1) another person possesses a desired good; (2) the comparison is with a similar other (proximity makes comparison relevant); (3) the good is in a domain that is personally important to the envier's self-image or identity. Envy is intensified when the rival's advantage feels undeserved or unjust. The social media context has dramatically amplified the conditions for envy by making comparison with similar others continuous and unavoidable.

**Reduction and absence — what causes envy to diminish:**
Benign envy motivates productive self-improvement and dissipates when the gap is closed. Malicious envy is harder to resolve — it reduces only through the rival's loss or through the radical revaluation of the comparison domain (ceasing to care about what the rival has). Gratitude is among the most effective psychological countermeasures — it redirects attention from what one lacks to what one has. Secure identity (not needing to be better than others to feel adequate) is the primary protective factor.

**Location — where envy is seated:**
Social psychology locates envy in the interface between self-evaluation and social comparison — it is primarily cognitive (comparative evaluation) and emotional (the painful awareness of inferiority). It has a distinctively painful quality (phthonos — the pain of another's good) that makes it unique among the appetitive emotions. There is no somatic location in the way that fear has the amygdala and shame has the face — envy's primary experience is cognitive-affective. In body-language and metaphor, envy is often associated with the eye (the evil eye in many traditions) — watching the rival's advantage with hostile attention.

**External influences:**
*Social:* The availability and proximity of comparison targets is the primary external driver. Equal or near-equal societies produce more envy than highly stratified ones (because proximity of comparison is greater). *Cultural:* Some cultures have strong social norms against envy expression; others treat it more openly. *Developmental:* Sibling dynamics are among the earliest developmental contexts for envy. Parental treatment of sibling comparisons shapes envy reactivity. *Hereditary:* Moderate heritability, through the personality traits that predispose to upward social comparison and identity threat.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Envy research is a focused and well-developed field.

### Section 2 — Desire, lust, and appetitive drives

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
desire (epithumia), desire (o), desire (hav.vah), desire (chem.dah), desire (che.sheq), desire (mach.mad), desire (ta.a.vah), desire (te.shu.qah), lust (orexis), lust (a.gav), lust (a.ga.vah), desirable thing (cha.mu.dah), precious thing (cha.mu.dah), to desire (himeirō), to desire (katastrēniaō), to desire (a.vah), to desire (cha.mad), to desire (cha.shaq)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Berridge and Robinson's distinction between 'wanting' and 'liking' (*Brain Research Reviews*, 1998) is foundational — the neural systems for wanting (dopaminergic, incentive salience) and liking (opioid, hedonic pleasure) are dissociable. Addictive desire is a disorder of wanting in the absence of liking — the person compulsively seeks the object without experiencing pleasure from it. Baumeister's desire research (*Psychological Science*, 2001) found that sexual desire is the most frequently experienced strong desire, followed by eating and sleeping desires. The epithumia vocabulary in the New Testament spans desire generally and sexual/appetitive desire specifically — the science coverage for both is available. Te.shu.qah (desire, longing, turning — in Gen 3:16 and Song 7:11) has received attention for its semantic range between longing and compulsive desire.

**Emergence — what causes desire to arise:**
Desire arises from the interaction between internal drive states (hunger, sexual motivation) and external cues that activate the wanting system. The dopaminergic wanting system responds strongly to cues associated with prior reward — desire is often triggered by association before the object is encountered. Desire escalates through repeated engagement and habituates to lower levels of stimulation over time (tolerance), driving the pursuit of more intense stimulation. The strength of desire is shaped by deprivation, proximity to the desired object, and the subjective value assigned to it.

**Reduction and absence — what causes desire to diminish:**
Satiation temporarily reduces desire; habituation reduces the pleasure from satisfaction over time without proportionally reducing the wanting (the hedonic treadmill). Self-regulation (willpower — Baumeister's ego depletion model) can suppress desire expression but depletes regulatory resources. Reappraisal of the desired object (reducing its value in the cognitive appraisal) is more sustainable than suppression. Cultivated desires for alternative goods (redirecting the wanting system toward different objects) is the positive psychology approach.

**Location — where desire is seated:**
Desire is seated in the brain's reward system — primarily the nucleus accumbens and ventral tegmental area for the wanting dimension, with hedonic networks (opioid systems) for liking. The experience of desire is felt as a pull — both toward the object and as an internal tension in the body (hunger as stomach sensation, sexual desire as bodily arousal). Desire is therefore simultaneously neurological (reward circuitry), somatic (bodily sensations of wanting), cognitive (attention oriented toward the object), and motivational (readiness to act to obtain it).

**External influences:**
*Hereditary:* Sexual motivation, temperament, and the sensitivity of reward circuitry are partially heritable. *Developmental:* Early experiences of satisfaction and deprivation shape the intensity and direction of desire. Attachment experiences shape desire for relational intimacy. *Cultural:* Cultures shape which desires are expressed, which are suppressed, and which objects are desirable. Advertising and media exposure powerfully shape the direction of desire. *Circumstances:* Deprivation intensifies desire; abundance and satiation temporarily reduce it.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Desire and motivation research are well-developed. The cluster's Hebrew desire vocabulary is exceptionally rich, which the science covers well at the general level even if specific glosses require verse-by-verse disambiguation.

### Section 3 — Greed, covetousness, and acquisitive excess

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / behavioural economics

**Glosses covered:**
greediness (pleonexia), greedy (pleonektēs), love of money (filarguria), money-loving (filarguros), self-indulgence (akrasia)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Kasser's materialism research (*The High Price of Materialism*, 2002) demonstrates that strong financial and material goals are negatively correlated with wellbeing — materialistic people report lower life satisfaction, more anxiety, and poorer relationships. The paradox of wealth (Easterlin paradox — rising national income does not produce rising life satisfaction beyond a threshold) is well-established. Thaler and Sunstein's behavioural economics addresses akrasia (self-indulgence — acting against one's better judgment) as weakness of will — the gap between intended and actual behaviour. Pleonexia (always wanting more, never satisfied) maps precisely onto research on aspirational consumption — the perpetual upward revision of material goals.

**Emergence — what causes greed and covetousness to arise:**
Greed arises from the combination of strong acquisitive desire, the belief that more possessions will produce satisfaction, and the absence of an effective natural stop point (unlike hunger, which is satisfied by eating, material desire does not have an equivalent satiation mechanism). Social comparison amplifies greed — wanting more than others, fearing having less. The love of money specifically arises when money becomes an end in itself rather than a means, which occurs when money is perceived as providing security, status, and control that are not available through other means.

**Reduction and absence — what causes greed to diminish:**
Greed is reduced by the genuine experience that more does not produce more satisfaction (empirical disconfirmation of the acquisitive belief). Gratitude — attention to what one already has — is the primary psychological countermeasure. Meaningful non-material goals (relationships, contribution, growth) reduce the motivational weight assigned to material acquisition. Communal norms of sufficiency and sharing constrain acquisitive behaviour through social accountability.

**Location — where greed is seated:**
Primarily cognitive (the belief that more is always better) and motivational (the acquisitive drive). Akrasia (self-indulgence) adds a dimension of motivational conflict — the person is not simply greedy but acts against their own values, suggesting a split between the evaluative self and the desiring self. This maps onto the programme's framework in a potentially significant way: greed and self-indulgence are not simply strong desires but desires that have escaped the governance of the person's values — a disorder of the inner governance structure.

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Consumer capitalism creates cultural conditions that systematically cultivate acquisitive desire. Cultures with strong communal sufficiency norms produce lower rates of acquisitive greed. *Developmental:* Family modelling of materialism strongly predicts adult materialism. *Circumstances:* Scarcity in childhood often predicts adult acquisitiveness as a survival-oriented overlearned response.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Materialism and behavioural economics research provide strong coverage.

### Section 4 — Sexual desire and fornication

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology — sexuality research

**Glosses covered:**
debauchery (aselgeia), debauchery (asōtia), fornication (ze.nu.nim), fornication (ze.nut), fornication (taz.nut), pleasure-loving (filēdonos), pleasure (hēdonē)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Sexual psychology (Bancroft, Masters and Johnson) addresses sexual motivation and behaviour. Impulse control research addresses the failure of self-regulatory capacities in sexual behaviour — sexual compulsivity and out-of-control sexual behaviour are established clinical presentations even if not yet a formal DSM category. Debauchery (aselgeia — licentiousness, the absence of shame or restraint) maps onto the psychology of disinhibition — the removal of ordinary social and self-regulatory constraints on behaviour.

**Emergence — what causes debauchery to arise:**
Disinhibited sexual behaviour arises through the combination of strong sexual motivation with reduced self-regulatory capacity. Substance intoxication, social contexts that normalise licentiousness, peer pressure, and identity contexts that celebrate transgression all reduce inhibition. The pleasure-loving (filēdonos) term describes a character orientation in which pleasure is the organising principle of life — a settled motivational structure rather than situational disinhibition.

**Reduction and absence:**
Self-regulatory capacity (Section 5 below), social accountability, and the internalisation of values that govern sexual behaviour are the primary reducers. Cultural norms that define sexual boundaries and assign shame to their violation constrain debauchery through social mechanisms.

**Location — where this is seated:**
Both neurological (reward and inhibition systems) and social (the absence of external constraints). The pleasure-loving character orientation is primarily motivational and cognitive — it is an organisation of the self around the principle of pleasure-seeking.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. The clinical literature on sexual behaviour and impulse control is relevant; the moral dimensions (fornication as covenant violation) are outside science.

### Section 5 — Fleshly and embodied drives

**Primary field:** Neuroscience / motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
fleshly (sarkikos), fleshly (sarkinos), to desire (katastrēniaō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Dual-process theories of motivation (Evans and Stanovich; Kahneman) distinguish automatic, habitual, and appetitive processes (System 1 — fast, emotional, embodied) from deliberate, reasoning, and value-governed processes (System 2 — slow, cognitive, deliberate). The Pauline flesh/spirit distinction has been compared to this dual-process framework, though the comparison is imperfect — Paul's sarx (flesh) is not simply the emotional/automatic system but a power that corrupts desire itself. Neuroscience of embodied drives addresses the neurological basis of appetitive behaviour — the role of the hypothalamus, limbic system, and reward circuitry.

**Emergence — what causes fleshly drives to arise:**
At the neurological level, embodied appetitive drives arise from subcortical systems (hypothalamus, limbic) that operate below conscious control and in advance of deliberate decision-making. The Pauline sarkikos/sarkinos terminology implies something stronger than merely automatic processing — it implies that the flesh represents a motivational orientation that is resistant to the governance of the spirit, not merely fast processing that the deliberate system can override.

**Location — where fleshly drives are seated:**
Science locates appetitive drives in subcortical neurological systems — below the cortical regions associated with deliberate self-governance. The Pauline framework locates the flesh in a broader theological anthropology where it represents the whole person organised around self-serving desire rather than God-oriented surrender. These are related but not identical accounts.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin for direct science; moderate for the dual-process analogy. The flesh/spirit distinction is primarily a theological category with partial psychological analogues.

## Outside-science inventory

- **pleasure-loving (filēdonos) in its moral-character register** — as a settled vice and spiritual danger — is a normative theological category beyond the descriptive psychology of pleasure-orientation.
- **fornication vocabulary (ze.nu.nim, ze.nut) in its covenantal-purity register** — Israel's unfaithfulness to God as spiritual fornication (Hosea) — is a theological metaphor that exceeds the psychology of sexual behaviour.
- **sarx (flesh) in Paul's theological register** — as a power that opposes the Spirit and corrupts desire from within — is a theological category with psychological analogues but no direct science equivalent.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Social psychology (envy research) for Section 1; motivational neuroscience and clinical psychology (desire and addiction) for Section 2; behavioural economics (greed/materialism) for Section 3.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The wanting/liking distinction illuminates why the cluster's desire vocabulary includes terms that describe compulsive pursuit alongside terms that describe pleasure — these are dissociable systems. The social comparison mechanism illuminates envy precisely — upward comparison with similar others in self-relevant domains. Materialism research illuminates why pleonexia (wanting more) is a stable character orientation that produces negative wellbeing outcomes even when material goals are achieved.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** Science treats desire as motivationally neutral — strong desire is adaptive or maladaptive depending on the object and context. Scripture treats certain desires as disordered not merely because of their consequences but because of their orientation — desire that displaces God from the centre of the person's motivational structure is disordered in principle. This normative dimension is outside science.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The distinction between desire-as-longing (te.shu.qah in Song of Songs) and desire-as-compulsion (ta.a.vah in Numbers 11) may surface in Session B. The science of desire does not pre-determine this — the verse evidence must establish whether these terms carry different inner-being registers.
