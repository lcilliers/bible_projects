## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Yes, **God as agent**: "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:3–4). Also **giver** (the comfort God supplies, 2Cor 1:4) and **object** (the appeal made *to* the Lord, "Three times I pleaded with the Lord", 2Cor 12:8).

**T0.1.2** — Borne by God as "the God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3) — comfort is named a divine title. God comforts and the person, having been comforted, comforts others (2Cor 1:4) — establishing comfort as a divine attribute the person relays rather than originates.

**T0.2.1** — Yes — comfort relieves the afflicted and builds up the community: "the one who prophesies speaks to people for their upbuilding and encouragement and consolation" (1Cor 14:3); David sends "to console" the grieving (1Ch 19:2); brothers come "to comfort" the mourner (1Ch 7:22).

**T0.2.2** — **Both**, weighted to the fallen condition: comfort answers a world of affliction, grief, and weakness ("comforts us in all our affliction", 2Cor 1:4) — its very operation presupposes distress to be relieved; yet the upbuilding of persons toward maturity belongs to created/redeemed design (1Cor 14:3).

**T0.2.3** — Yes — comfort is bound to **hope and salvation**: it co-occurs with hope (elpis), perseverance (hupomonē), and salvation (sōtēria); the consolation of the Scriptures sustains the person toward future hope. It orients beyond present affliction.

**T0.3.1** — The likeness of **coming alongside the afflicted** — God who comforts "in all our affliction" reproduced in the person who consoles the grieving (1Ch 7:22) and builds up the weak (1Cor 14:3).

**T0.3.2** — **Shared**: God comforts, and the comforted "comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God" (2Cor 1:4) — the same comfort, passed on.

**T0.3.3** — Presence images God's consoling care; its corruption is the false comfort — building "the tombs of the prophets" (Luk 11:47) or emboldening a weak conscience to sin (1Cor 8:10). **Partly inferential** as image-doctrine.

**T0.4.1** — Yes — the comfort relayed from God to others (2Cor 1:4) is **typological of divine consolation**, the divine instance grounding the human ministry of comfort. Direction: divine establishing the pattern.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Comfort (consolation, encouragement). The name signals **coming alongside another in distress** — to console the grieving and to strengthen, urge, and build up.

**T1.1.2** — Hebrew **nacham** (to comfort, lit. "be sorry"), **tanchum** (consolation), **lavav** (encourage); Greek **parakaleō / paraklēsis** (the *para-kaleō* "call alongside" — comfort *and* exhort/appeal), **oikodomeō / oikodomē** (build up, edify). The Greek hinge term shows comfort and summons are one movement.

**T1.1.3** — The name carries a **come-alongside** directional implication — *para-* "beside": the comforter draws near the afflicted to relieve and strengthen (1Ch 7:22; 2Cor 1:4).

**T1.2.1** — Chiefly an **act** — the most enacted characteristic in the cluster: *action* (188) far above *status* (48). Comfort is something *done* to another — comforting, urging, building up — not borne as a state.

**T1.2.2** — **Compound**, spanning two registers under one hinge: **consolation** (soothing the grieving — nacham, tanchum) and **exhortation/edification** (urging, appealing, building up — parakaleō, oikodomeō). The compound holds soothing and summoning together.

**T1.3.1** — Its structural opposite is **affliction left unrelieved / discouragement** — the state comfort answers ("a comforter is far from me, one to revive my spirit", Lam 1:16 register; "Reproaches have broken my heart … I looked … for comforters, but I found none", Psa 69:20).

**T1.3.2** — At its edge it excludes **false or harmful upbuilding** — building that does not help ("'All things are lawful,' but not all things build up", 1Cor 10:23) and emboldening into sin ("will he not be encouraged … to eat?", 1Cor 8:10).

**T1.3.3** — Comfort is **not flattery and not mere relief of feeling**: it includes summons and correction (parakaleō "I appeal to you … that all of you agree", 1Cor 1:10; "I exhort the elders", 1Pe 5:1). It ends where soothing becomes the false comfort that builds tombs (Luk 11:47).

**T1.4.1** — It operates as an **enacted coming-alongside** — comforting, consoling, urging, building up — and as **speech**: "I appeal to you, brothers" (1Cor 1:10); "I urge you" (1Cor 16:15). Grammatically the band is verb-heavy (the action register).

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies sharply by context: toward the grieving it is **consolation** (1Ch 7:22); toward the wayward or weak it is **exhortation/appeal** (1Cor 1:10; 1Pe 5:1); toward the church it is **edification** ("build up the church", 1Cor 14:12).

**T1.4.3** — Yes, pervasively — comfort is **fundamentally communicative**: it operates through appeal, exhortation, encouragement, and prophecy "for … upbuilding and encouragement and consolation" (1Cor 14:3). It is a speech-borne characteristic more than any other in the cluster.

**T1.5.1** — The immediate response comfort produces is **relief and renewed courage** — the afflicted strengthened, the discouraged "encouraged" (1Cor 14:31), the grieving consoled (1Ch 7:22).

**T1.5.2** — Varied by need — consolation for the griever, courage for the fearful, upbuilding for the weak — but consistent in its direction toward relief and strengthening.

**T1.6.1** — Over time comfort **builds up and establishes** — "build up the church" (1Cor 14:12), "comfort your hearts and establish them in every good work" (2Th 2:17) — producing settled strength and maturity.

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is momentary relief/courage; the sustained effect is **establishment** — hearts comforted *and established* (2Th 2:17), the church built up over time (1Cor 14).

**T1.7.1** — It takes hold rightly in the context of **affliction met by love** — comfort "in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:4), upbuilding done "in love" (the 1Cor edification ethic) and "for upbuilding" rather than self-display (1Cor 14).

**T1.7.2** — It is blocked or distorted by **self-serving or harmful building** (1Cor 8:10; 1Cor 10:23) and by the absence of any comforter ("I found none", Psa 69:20). **No adversarial spirit** is shown distorting it — **Silent**.

**T1.7.3** — The person who is offered comfort but does not take it up is the **inconsolable** — "my soul refuses to be comforted" (Psa 77:2); "she refused to be comforted" (the Rachel/Jacob register) — comfort met but not received.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — **Heart** (8 — "comfort your hearts and establish them", 2Th 2:17; "encourage your hearts", Col 4:8), **soul** (4 — "strengthening the souls of the disciples", Act 14:22), **mind** (1 — "I appeal to you … that you agree", 1Cor 1:10), **conscience** (1 — the weak conscience "encouraged"/built up, 1Cor 8:10), **inward-parts** (1 — "the hearts of the saints have been refreshed", Phm 7).

**T2.1.2** — Comfort engages the **heart and soul** (the seat of courage and grief) and, through its exhortation register, the **mind**. Its seat is the affective-volitional centre; the body link is faint (refreshed "hearts", Phm 7) — **functional/expressive**.

**T2.10.1** — Yes — comfort moves **from God onto the person and then through the person to others**: "comforts us … so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God" (2Cor 1:4) — a clear received→relayed movement (received-from-outside, 5 occurrences).

**T2.7.1** — The body link runs **inward (refreshment) and is expressive** — "the hearts of the saints have been refreshed" (Phm 7); largely interior, not an emphatic somatic seat.

**T2.9.1** — **Bestowed by God** ("the God of all comfort", 2Cor 1:3) and **mediated by persons** (David's messengers "to console", 1Ch 19:2; prophecy that consoles, 1Cor 14:3). Origin is God, channelled through the comforter.

**T2.9.2** — **Multiple and relayed** — God the source, the comforted person the channel (2Cor 1:4); the origin is consistently external-to-the-afflicted (received from God or another).

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1 / T3.1.2 / T3.1.3 · Perception** — **Largely Silent** (2 minor occurrences). Comfort is not primarily a perceptive faculty; the comforter perceives the affliction, but the characteristic itself operates as relief and exhortation, not perception.

**T3.2.1 · Cognition (engage?)** — Yes (16 occurrences): the exhortation/appeal register engages the mind — "I appeal to you … that all of you agree, and that there be no divisions … in the same mind" (1Cor 1:10); prophecy that instructs "so that all may learn" (1Cor 14:31).

**T3.2.2 · Cognition (effect)** — It persuades and instructs — comfort-as-exhortation moves the mind toward agreement, learning, and resolve (1Cor 1:10; 1Cor 14:31).

**T3.2.3 · Cognition (pattern)** — Cognition is a real strand (the exhortation pole), distinguishing comfort's *parakaleō*-as-appeal from the purely affective consolation pole.

**T3.3.1 / T3.3.2 / T3.3.3 · Memory** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages comfort as a memorial faculty.

**T3.4.1 · Affect (engage?)** — Yes, dominantly (affect 230): comfort meets grief and fear with relief — "came to comfort him" the mourner (1Ch 7:22); "comfort your hearts" (2Th 2:17).

**T3.4.2 · Affect (effect)** — It relieves grief and fear and restores courage — the discouraged "encouraged" (1Cor 14:31), the grieving consoled, the refused comfort leaving the soul in distress (Psa 77:2).

**T3.4.3 · Affect (pattern)** — Affect dominance with a high *self*-experiencer share marks comfort as the **most received affection** — it is felt as relief *in* the afflicted, not only extended.

**T3.5.1 / T3.5.2 / T3.5.3 · Creativity** — **Silent.** No engagement with imagination or origination.

**T3.6.1 · Volition (engage?)** — Yes — comfort-as-exhortation appeals to the will: "I urge you, brothers" (1Cor 16:15); "I exhort the elders" (1Pe 5:1); "encouraging them to continue in the faith" (Act 14:22).

**T3.6.2 · Volition (effect)** — It moves the will to persevere, agree, and remain faithful (Act 14:22 "continue in the faith"; 1Cor 1:10 "agree").

**T3.6.3 · Volition (pattern)** — The commanded/exhortation register (35 commanded) shows comfort engages the will as much as the feeling — it summons as well as soothes.

**T3.7.1 · Agency (engage?)** — Yes, strongly — comfort acts: consoling, building up, exhorting are deeds done to another ("build up the church", 1Cor 14:12; "sent comforters", 1Ch 19:3).

**T3.7.2 · Agency (effect)** — It strengthens and establishes the other — "strengthening the souls of the disciples" (Act 14:22), "the other person is … built up" (1Cor 14:17).

**T3.7.3 · Agency (pattern)** — The action-dominant typing (188) confirms comfort is **the cluster's most agentive characteristic** — its very being is enacted toward another.

**T3.8.1 · Moral evaluation (engage?)** — Partially — comfort/edification is weighed by whether it truly *helps* and *builds up*: "not all things are helpful … not all things build up" (1Cor 10:23); the false comfort of building tombs is condemned (Luk 11:47).

**T3.8.2 · Moral evaluation (effect)** — Right comfort builds up; wrong "comfort" emboldens to sin (1Cor 8:10) or honours the killers of the prophets (Luk 11:47) — comfort is morally assessed by its effect.

**T3.8.3 · Moral evaluation (pattern)** — Mostly neutral-valenced (87) because the appeal/urge speech-acts are morally neutral in themselves; the few sinful cases mark the boundary where "building up" turns harmful.

**T3.9.1 · Conscience (engage?)** — Yes, in one decisive negative case: a weak conscience wrongly "encouraged"/built up to act against itself — "will he not be encouraged, if his conscience is weak, to eat?" (1Cor 8:10).

**T3.9.2 · Conscience (effect)** — Misapplied edification can **wound** the conscience (1Cor 8:10–12); rightly applied, comfort establishes the heart "in every good work" (2Th 2:17).

**T3.9.3 · Conscience (pattern)** — Conscience engagement is minor but pointed — comfort must not embolden against conscience, marking an ethical limit on edification.

**T3.10.1 · Conscientiousness (engage?)** — Yes — comfort integrates perceiving the need, willing the other's good, and acting (sending comforters, 1Ch 19:2; exhorting to continue, Act 14:22).

**T3.10.2 · Conscientiousness (effect)** — It yields steady ministry to the afflicted and the building up of the community over time (1Cor 14).

**T3.10.3 · Conscientiousness (pattern)** — Comfort reads as an **integrated, other-directed ministry** — awareness, will, and action converging on the strengthening of another.

**T3.11.1 · Relational capacity (engage?)** — Yes, constitutively — comfort *is* relational ministry: coming alongside (para-), consoling, building up "one another" (the mutual-edification ethic).

**T3.11.2 · Relational capacity (effect)** — It repairs and strengthens bonds — refreshing "the hearts of the saints" (Phm 7), knitting the church together (1Cor 14), restoring the grieving.

**T3.11.3 · Relational capacity (pattern)** — Strongly relational, with the highest *self*-experiencer share — comfort both **binds the comforter to the afflicted** and is **received within** the afflicted, a two-way ministry.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1 · God→human** — Yes — "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:3–4). God comforts the person directly.

**T4.1.2 · basis** — **Responsive to affliction and grounded in mercy** — comfort flows from "the Father of mercies" to those "in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:3–4). Given to the distressed.

**T4.1.3 · what it shows of God** — That God is **the consoler of the afflicted** — his mercy expressed as comfort that meets every affliction (2Cor 1:4).

**T4.2.1 · human→God** — Yes — the appeal/plea to God: "Three times I pleaded [parakaleō] with the Lord about this, that it should leave me" (2Cor 12:8) — the person's entreaty toward God.

**T4.2.2 · inner posture required** — **Dependence in affliction** — the plea of the afflicted casting the need on the Lord (2Cor 12:8).

**T4.2.3 · what it shows** — That the person turns to God **as the source of comfort and relief** — the one entreated in distress.

**T4.3.1 · human→human giving** — Yes, dominantly — comfort extended person to person: "sent messengers to console" (1Ch 19:2); "I appeal to you, brothers" (1Cor 1:10); "build up the church" (1Cor 14:12); "exhort the elders" (1Pe 5:1).

**T4.3.2 · giver's inner conditions** — Love and the desire for the other's upbuilding (the 1Cor 14 ethic of edification in love), and having oneself been comforted by God (2Cor 1:4).

**T4.3.3 · what the giver must have received/become** — They must have **received God's comfort** — "the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God" (2Cor 1:4): received comfort is the source of given comfort.

**T4.4.1 · human→human receiving** — Yes, prominently — comfort is **received**: the comforted, the built-up, the encouraged ("the other person is … built up", 1Cor 14:17; "the hearts of the saints have been refreshed", Phm 7). Comfort has the cluster's highest *self*-experiencer share.

**T4.4.2 · conditions accompanying / blocking uptake** — Uptake accompanies a heart open to consolation; it is blocked by a soul that "refuses to be comforted" (Psa 77:2) or grief too deep for present comfort (Psa 69:20).

**T4.4.3 · state of one who meets it but does not take it up** — The **inconsolable**: "my soul refuses to be comforted" (Psa 77:2) — comfort offered and refused, the soul remaining in distress.

**T4.5.1 · within bonds vs across distance** — Operates **within bonds** (brothers comforting the mourner, 1Ch 7:22; the church's mutual upbuilding) and across distance (messengers sent to console, 1Ch 19:2).

**T4.5.2 · within covenant only, or crossing** — Primarily **within the community** (the upbuilding of the church, 1Cor 14) but extended in acts of consolation to others (1Ch 19:2 to Hanun).

**T4.5.3 · relational scope** — The afflicted, the grieving, the weak, the church — comfort's scope is wherever there is distress to relieve; no class is excluded.

**T4.6.1 / T4.6.2 / T4.6.3 · Spiritual beings** — **Silent.** No occurrence shows comfort operating toward, attacked by, or mediated through angelic or adversarial beings (the co-occurring "enemy" is human).

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1 · Transformation** — Yes — comfort changes the afflicted's **condition** (the grieving consoled, the weak built up) and **orientation** (the discouraged "encouraged … to continue in the faith", Act 14:22) — both state and stance toward the affliction.

**T5.1.2 · reversible / irreversible** — **Silent** on reversibility; the refused comfort (Psa 77:2) shows the change is not automatic.

**T5.2.1 · Sequence of inner states** — Yes — **affliction → comfort received → comfort relayed**: "who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction" (2Cor 1:4) — a clear before/after with onward movement.

**T5.3.1 · Mechanism of change** — By **coming-alongside and speech** — consolation, exhortation, and edification delivered to the afflicted (1Cor 14:3); and by **received divine comfort** (2Cor 1:4).

**T5.3.2 · mechanism differ across contexts** — Yes: grief is met by consolation (1Ch 7:22); weakness by upbuilding (1Cor 14); waywardness by exhortation/appeal (1Cor 1:10); the church by mutual edification.

**T5.4.1 · Suffering / affliction** — Yes, constitutively — comfort is **the response to affliction**: "comforts us in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:4); it co-occurs with affliction/pressure (thlipsis) and perseverance (hupomonē). Affliction is its native context.

**T5.4.2 · what suffering does to it** — Suffering is the **occasion that calls comfort forth**, and the comfort received in suffering **equips the sufferer to comfort others** (2Cor 1:4) — suffering becomes a school of consolation.

**T5.5.1 · Formation and sanctification** — Yes — comfort builds up the person and the church toward maturity ("build up the church", 1Cor 14:12; "establish them in every good work", 2Th 2:17) — edification is formative over the whole arc.

**T5.6.1 · Eschatological trajectory** — Yes — comfort is bound to **hope and perseverance**: it co-occurs with hope (elpis), perseverance (hupomonē), and salvation (sōtēria); the consolation that sustains the afflicted points them toward the hope held out. Present comfort anticipates final relief.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1 · Co-occurrence** — Comfort co-occurs most with **affliction/pressure** (thlipsis), **hope** (elpis), **perseverance** (hupomonē), **love** (agapē), **joy/rejoicing** (chairō), **the assembly** (ekklēsia — the object of edification), **prophecy** (profēteuō — the means of upbuilding), **salvation** (sōtēria), **peace, grace**, and **faithfulness** (pistos).

**T6.1.2 · what co-occurrence shows** — Comfort sits in an **affliction-and-hope complex** — it is the ministry that meets affliction (thlipsis) with consolation and turns the afflicted toward hope (elpis) and perseverance (hupomonē), within the upbuilding of the assembly.

**T6.2.1 · Sequential relationship** — Yes — comfort **follows affliction** and **precedes hope/perseverance**: the consolation of the Scriptures yields hope; comfort received yields comfort given (2Cor 1:4). It is the hinge between distress and renewed endurance.

**T6.2.2 · what the sequence shows** — The relationship is **causal/developmental**: affliction occasions comfort; comfort produces hope, perseverance, and the capacity to comfort others.

**T6.3.1 · does it produce another?** — Yes — comfort produces **hope and perseverance** (the elpis/hupomonē it sustains) and the **mutual love** of the built-up community.

**T6.3.2 · is it produced by another?** — Produced by **compassion/mercy** ("the Father of mercies and God of all comfort", 2Cor 1:3 — mercy issuing in comfort) and by **love** (the edifying love of 1Cor).

**T6.3.3 · constituent of / constituted by another?** — Comfort is the **enacted face of mercy** (B issuing in D, 2Cor 1:3) and a **constituent of the church's upbuilding** (edification as the corporate good).

**T6.4.1 · shared vocabulary** — The hinge **parakaleō** ("appeal/exhort/comfort") shares the speech-appeal register with the cluster's commands; **oikodomeō** ("build up") is shared with the corporate edification field; comfort co-occurs lexically with love (agapē).

**T6.4.2 · shared root architecture** — The **para-kaleō / paraklēsis** root generates the band's exhortation-consolation core; **oikodom-** generates the edification terms — two roots, one come-alongside characteristic.

**T6.4.3 · what the sharing shows** — The parakaleō sharing shows comfort, exhortation, and appeal are **one act in the NT** — to come alongside is at once to console and to summon.

**T6.5.1 · nearest neighbour and distinction** — The nearest neighbour is **compassion (B)**. The distinction: compassion is the **felt stirring** toward the afflicted (affect, viscerally seated); comfort is the **enacted coming-alongside** that relieves and strengthens (action-dominant, speech-borne). Compassion feels; comfort acts and speaks.

**T6.5.2 · precise boundary at overlap** — Where they overlap (2Cor 1:3 "Father of mercies and God of all comfort"), the boundary is **stage**: compassion is the mercy felt; comfort is the consolation delivered.

**T6.5.3 · distinction of degree, kind, direction, or level?** — One of **stage and mode** (felt mercy vs enacted/spoken relief); against love (A) the distinction is **function** — comfort is love deployed toward the afflicted.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1 · primary terms and root meanings** — Hebrew **nacham** (comfort, "be sorry/relent"), **tanchum** (consolation), **lavav** (encourage); Greek **parakaleō / paraklēsis** (*para-kaleō*, "call alongside" — comfort/exhort/appeal), **oikodomeō / oikodomē** (build up). Roots converge on **coming alongside to relieve and strengthen**.

**T7.1.2 · grammatical range** — Verb (nacham, parakaleō, oikodomeō), noun (tanchum, paraklēsis, oikodomē). The strongly verbal weighting confirms comfort operates as **enacted ministry** more than borne quality.

**T7.1.3 · semantic range** — From consoling the grieving (1Ch 7:22) through encouraging the fearful (Act 14:22), appealing/urging (1Cor 1:10; 1Cor 16:15), to building up the church (1Cor 14:12) and pleading with God (2Cor 12:8). One of the widest action-ranges in the cluster — soothing to summoning.

**T7.1.4 · terms distinguishing aspects** — Yes — **nacham/tanchum** (console — the soothing pole) are distinguished from **parakaleō-as-exhort** (urge/appeal — the summoning pole) and **oikodomeō** (build up — the strengthening pole). The disposition-vs-act distinction is carried by the noun (paraklēsis) vs verb (parakaleō).

**T7.1.5 · term for the opposite/absence** — No single antonym, but the opposite is named contextually — the **comfortless** state ("a comforter is far from me", Lam 1:16; "comforters, but I found none", Psa 69:20).

**T7.1.6 · person-type term** — Yes — the **comforter** ("comforters", 1Ch 19:3; the *paraklētos* register beyond this band) names the one who comforts.

**T7.1.7 · supplication / seeking term** — Yes — **parakaleō** itself functions as the plea ("I pleaded with the Lord", 2Cor 12:8; "I appeal to you", 1Cor 1:10) — the same term seeks comfort and gives exhortation.

**T7.1.8 · OT↔NT continuity / development** — OT **nacham** (console, with the "be sorry/relent" sense) continues into NT **parakaleō**, which **develops** the field to fuse consolation with exhortation and edification (and stands behind the Spirit as *paraklētos*). Continuity of consolation; development into a fuller come-alongside ministry.

**T7.1.9 · NT-coined / developed term** — **parakaleō / paraklēsis** is decisively **developed** in the NT to carry comfort, exhortation, and appeal together (and grounds the title *Paraclete*) — a development whose breadth has no single OT equivalent.

**T7.1.10 · full vocabulary arc** — The arc spans **consoling the grieving (nacham) → encouraging (lavav) → appealing/exhorting (parakaleō) → building up (oikodomeō) → consolation as a divine title (the God of all comfort)** — the full range of coming alongside to relieve and strengthen.

**T7.2.1 · function of the primary term in its primary verse** — In **2Cor 1:3–4** paraklēsis/parakaleō is the **defining divine title and the engine of the relay** — God is "the God of all comfort, who comforts us … so that we may be able to comfort" — the term governs the whole received→given argument.

**T7.2.2 · literary forms** — **Narrative** (1Ch 7; 19), **psalm/lament** (Psa 69; 77), **prophecy** (Isaiah's consolation register), **epistle** (2Cor 1; 1Cor 14; 1Pe 5). Responsible reading honours the lament's unrelieved grief distinctly from the epistle's confident consolation.

**T7.2.3 · logical structure** — 2Cor 1:3–4: premise — God comforts us in all affliction; conclusion — therefore we can comfort others with the comfort we received. 1Cor 14: premise — prophecy builds up; conclusion — strive to excel in building up the church. Comfort is consistently the **means by which affliction is turned toward strength**.

**T7.2.4 · contextual setting** — **Communal and pastoral** (the upbuilding of the church, 1Cor 14; exhortation of elders, 1Pe 5), **consolatory** (grief and lament, 1Ch 7; Psa 77), and **eschatological-hopeful** (comfort tied to hope and salvation). The communal-pastoral setting is decisive.

**T7.2.5 · primary anchor verse** — **2Cor 1:3–4** — "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God" — most fully expresses comfort's essential character: divine source, affliction-meeting, and relayed to others.

**T7.2.6 · what the anchor shows that no other shows** — 2Cor 1:3–4 alone displays the **received→given relay** in full — comfort is not retained but passed on, the comforted becoming comforters — the defining circuit of the characteristic.

**T7.3.1 · most useful human-science framework** — **Clinical psychology** — compassion-focused therapy and consolation psychology (Gilbert), and **positive psychology** of encouragement (self-efficacy through social support).

**T7.3.2 · where the framework illuminates** — Compassion-focused therapy's attention to the **capacity to receive as well as offer comfort** illuminates the band's high *self*-experiencer share (comfort is received — Psa 77:2; 1Cor 14:17); encouragement psychology illuminates the upbuilding/edification stream (strengthening through supportive speech, Act 14:22; 1Cor 14:3).

**T7.3.3 · where evidence and framework diverge** — Science frames comfort as interpersonal support and therapeutic technique; the corpus grounds comfort in **God as "the God of all comfort"** (2Cor 1:3) and makes it a relayed divine gift, and fuses it with **exhortation** (parakaleō) in a way that exceeds therapeutic consolation — comfort that also summons and builds toward maturity.

**T7.3.4 · aspect surfaced by the framework** — The receiving-comfort literature surfaces the **inconsolable** as a clinical reality ("my soul refuses to be comforted", Psa 77:2) and invites a verse test of what the corpus shows about comfort *refused* or *delayed* — a dimension the band touches but does not fully develop. **Flagged for further verse work.**
