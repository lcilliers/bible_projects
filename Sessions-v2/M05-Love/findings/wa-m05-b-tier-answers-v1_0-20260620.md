## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Yes, predominantly **God as agent**: "for his mercy is very great" (1Ch 21:13); "grant them compassion in the sight of those who carried them captive" (1Ki 8:50); "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3). Also **possessor** ("his great mercy", 1Pe 1:3) and **giver** ("mercy … from God the Father", 1Ti 1:2).

**T0.1.2** — Borne by God far more than by the creature in this band (agent-dominant, 60 of the divine-tagged occurrences). The pattern marks compassion as **primarily a revealed divine attribute** that the human bears derivatively — "the Lord your God is gracious and merciful" (2Ch 30:9) is the ground from which human mercy is called.

**T0.2.1** — Yes — compassion moves God to forgive and restore ("forgive your people … and grant them compassion", 1Ki 8:50) and moves the person to spare, relieve, and give alms ("gave alms generously to the people", Act 10:2). Its effect is the preservation of the vulnerable.

**T0.2.2** — **Both**, with a bounded edge: as design, compassion mirrors God's own mercy toward the needy; within the fallen condition it is both rightly exercised (toward the weak) and rightly *withheld* (in judicial/herem contexts — "do not spare them", 1Sa 15:3). The bounding belongs to the order of justice within the fallen world.

**T0.2.3** — Yes — God's mercy is the ground of future hope: "his mercy … has caused us to be born again to a living hope" (1Pe 1:3); future restoration turns on finding compassion (2Ch 30:9). It orients toward a mercy yet to be fully received.

**T0.3.1** — The likeness of **mercy toward the weak and guilty** — God who pities the needy reproduced in the person of "compassionate hearts" (Col 3:12) and the "tender heart" (1Pe 3:8).

**T0.3.2** — **Shared**, but asymmetrically — compassion is chiefly God's (agent-dominant) and derivatively the person's; the human instance images the divine ("the Father of mercies … who comforts us … so that we may be able to comfort", 2Cor 1:3–4).

**T0.3.3** — Presence images God's mercy (Col 3:12 "God's chosen ones … compassionate hearts"); its hardening is the mark of the unmerciful — "because he had no pity" (2Sa 12:6); "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" (Pro 12:10). **Partly inferential** as an image-doctrine.

**T0.4.1** — Yes — **covenantal/christological, divine establishing the pattern**: God's mercy in the womb-yearning over Ephraim ("my heart yearns for him; I will surely have mercy on him", Jer 31:20) and "the tender mercy of our God, whereby the sunrise shall visit us" (Luk 1:78) point typologically to the mercy realised in Christ's visitation.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Compassion (with mercy, pity). The name signals the **visceral, other-regarding stirring** toward another's plight that moves to spare and relieve — felt, in the Hebrew, in the womb (*rachamim*).

**T1.1.2** — Hebrew **racham / rachamim** (compassion, from *rechem*, the womb), **chus** (to pity), **chamal** (to spare); Greek **eleos / eleeō** (mercy), **oiktirmos** (compassion), the **splanchna** family (moved in the bowels). Definitionally the terms locate compassion in the **viscera** and direct it toward the suffering or guilty.

**T1.1.3** — The name carries a **downward, other-directed** implication — mercy moves from the stronger toward the weaker, the able toward the needy, the wronged toward the guilty ("have mercy on me", Mat 15:22).

**T1.2.1** — Chiefly an **act** (action 143), with a substantial **disposition/quality** register (status 89, quality 23): mercy is shown and pity is enacted ("spared", "had mercy"), yet also borne as a "compassionate heart" (Col 3:12).

**T1.2.2** — **Compound**: it combines a felt stirring (affect — the yearning heart, 1Ki 3:26), a perceiving of the plight (perception — "the eye … pities"), and an enacted relief (sparing, almsgiving). The Hebrew womb-image fuses feeling and origin.

**T1.3.1** — Its structural opposite is **cruelty / mercilessness** — "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" (Pro 12:10); "because he had no pity" (2Sa 12:6) — the hardened refusal to be moved.

**T1.3.2** — It excludes **indifference and hardness** toward suffering; at its edge it resists the calculating self-interest that "was unwilling to take one of his own flock" (2Sa 12:4).

**T1.3.3** — Compassion is **not the suspension of justice**: it is rightly *bounded* — "your eye shall not pity" in lex talionis (Deu 19:21) and "do not spare" Amalek (1Sa 15:3); misplaced sparing is sin (Saul/Agag, 1Sa 15:9). It ends where pity would overthrow justice or covenant fidelity.

**T1.4.1** — It operates as a **felt-then-enacted movement**: the heart "yearns" (1Ki 3:26) and the hand spares/gives. Grammatically the band is action-heavy (verbs of having mercy, sparing) with a noun register (mercies, compassion).

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by **object**: toward the needy it is relief and almsgiving (Act 10:2); toward the guilty it is forgiveness ("grant them compassion", 1Ki 8:50); toward the condemned in judicial/herem settings it is *withheld* (1Sa 15:3). It varies by context more than any other band's characteristic.

**T1.4.3** — Yes — compassion is **invoked and commanded in speech**: the cry "Have mercy on me, O Lord" (Mat 15:22), the petition "grant them compassion" (1Ki 8:50), and the command to "put on … compassionate hearts" (Col 3:12).

**T1.5.1** — The immediate response is **relief, sparing, or forgiveness**: "out of pity for him, the master … released him and forgave him the debt" (Mat 18:27); the yearning heart that gives up the child to save it (1Ki 3:26).

**T1.5.2** — Consistent in direction (toward relieving the plight) but **varied in act** — sparing, forgiving, giving alms, yielding the contested good.

**T1.6.1** — Over time compassion **establishes a merciful disposition** — the "compassionate heart" and "tender heart" (Col 3:12; 1Pe 3:8) — and, received from God, a "living hope" (1Pe 1:3).

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is a single act of relief; the sustained effect is a **formed tenderness** of heart (Col 3:12) and a settled hope grounded in God's mercy (1Pe 1:3).

**T1.7.1** — It takes hold under a **perceived, undeserved plight and felt connection** — the yearning over one's own (1Ki 3:26 "her heart yearned for her son"), the seeing of need (Psa 72:13 "he has pity on the weak and the needy").

**T1.7.2** — It is blocked by **hardness, self-interest, and (rightly) by the demands of justice**: "no pity" (2Sa 12:6); unwillingness to give from one's own (2Sa 12:4); and the judicial bar on pity (Deu 19:21). **No adversarial spirit** is shown distorting it — that branch is **Silent**.

**T1.7.3** — The person in whom compassion is present but unrealised is shown in **misplaced sparing** — Saul spares Agag and the best of the flock against the command (1Sa 15:9, 15:15): compassion present but disordered into disobedience.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — **Inward-parts / bowels** (the seat of the Hebrew *rachamim* and Greek *splanchna*): "my heart yearns for him; I will surely have mercy" (Jer 31:20); "the tender mercy [splanchna] of our God" (Luk 1:78); "her heart yearned for her son" (1Ki 3:26). **Soul** (9 — Psa 72:13), **heart** (1), and **conscience** (1). The eye is engaged as the organ of pity ("your eye shall not pity", Deu 19:21).

**T2.1.2** — Compassion is the **most somatically-seated affection in the cluster** — felt in the womb/bowels before it is deliberated. The pattern (visceral seat + perceiving eye + enacted relief) shows a movement from body to act, indicating a pre-cognitive, embodied origin.

**T2.10.1** — Yes — it moves **from God onto the person** (mercy "from God the Father", 1Ti 1:2; received-from-outside, 3 occurrences) and **from within the viscera outward** to the act of relief (the yearning heart → giving up the child, 1Ki 3:26).

**T2.7.1** — The body link runs **body→soul and soul→act (emphatic)**: the womb/bowels are the *seat* from which mercy arises (Jer 31:20), making the link emphatic and originating, not merely expressive.

**T2.9.1** — **Bestowed by God** ("his great mercy", 1Pe 1:3) and **generated within** in the visceral stirring (1Ki 3:26). No generational or adversarial origin appears.

**T2.9.2** — **Multiple** — divine source (God the Father of mercies, 2Cor 1:3) and the inward stirring (the yearning heart) — and context-dependent (relief vs withholding).

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1 · Perception (engage?)** — Yes (30 occurrences tagged perception): compassion is bound to **seeing** — "your eye shall not pity" (Deu 19:21); pity follows the registering of the plight ("he has pity on the weak and the needy", Psa 72:13).

**T3.1.2 · Perception (effect)** — Seeing the sufferer moves the viscera; the eye is the gateway to mercy, and closing it ("your eye shall not pity") is how mercy is barred.

**T3.1.3 · Perception (pattern)** — The recurrent eye-idiom marks compassion as **perceptually triggered** — it begins in the sight of need, distinguishing it from the other affections.

**T3.2.1 / T3.2.2 / T3.2.3 · Cognition** — **Silent / minimal.** No occurrence is tagged to the cognitive faculty; compassion in this band is visceral and perceptual, not a knowing or reasoning — its non-engagement with cognition marks it as pre-deliberative.

**T3.3.1 · Memory (engage?)** — Yes, lightly: compassion is bound to *remembering* — God "remembers" his mercy (the zakhar co-occurrence, 7×), and almsgiving is "remembered before God" (Act 10:31). 

**T3.3.2 · Memory (effect)** — God's recollection of mercy moves him to relent; the person's mercy is held in divine memory.

**T3.3.3 · Memory (pattern)** — Memory engagement is minor but real — mercy is tied to covenant remembrance, indicating its rootedness in remembered relationship.

**T3.4.1 · Affect (engage?)** — Yes, dominantly (affect 255): compassion is the felt stirring — "her heart yearned" (1Ki 3:26); "my heart yearns for him" (Jer 31:20).

**T3.4.2 · Affect (effect)** — It produces the yearning, the being-moved ("out of pity … released him", Mat 18:27) that issues in relief.

**T3.4.3 · Affect (pattern)** — Affect dominance with a visceral seat marks compassion as **feeling localised in the body** — the most embodied of the cluster's affections.

**T3.5.1 / T3.5.2 / T3.5.3 · Creativity** — **Silent.** No engagement with imagination or origination.

**T3.6.1 · Volition (engage?)** — Yes — compassion is **commanded** ("put on … compassionate hearts", Col 3:12) and is exercised as a choice to spare or relieve (or, wrongly, to withhold against command, 1Sa 15:9).

**T3.6.2 · Volition (effect)** — It directs the will toward relief; its disorder is a will that spares what should not be spared (Saul, 1Sa 15:9) or withholds what should be given.

**T3.6.3 · Volition (pattern)** — The bounded valence (forbidden/sinful sparing) shows the will must govern compassion's exercise — mercy is right or wrong by whether the will keeps it within justice.

**T3.7.1 · Agency (engage?)** — Yes — compassion acts: it spares, releases, forgives, gives alms ("gave alms generously", Act 10:2; "released him and forgave him the debt", Mat 18:27).

**T3.7.2 · Agency (effect)** — It moves the powerful to relieve the powerless — the master forgiving the servant (Mat 18:27); the king pitying the needy (Psa 72:13).

**T3.7.3 · Agency (pattern)** — Action-heavy typing confirms compassion is **enacted mercy** — its agency (relief, sparing, almsgiving) is integral.

**T3.8.1 · Moral evaluation (engage?)** — Yes — compassion is morally weighed: right toward the needy, wrong when it spares the condemned ("the mercy of the wicked is cruel", Pro 12:10; misplaced sparing, 1Sa 15:9).

**T3.8.2 · Moral evaluation (effect)** — It is assessed against justice — mercy that obstructs justice is condemned (Deu 19:21; 1Sa 15), mercy toward the needy is righteousness.

**T3.8.3 · Moral evaluation (pattern)** — The forbidden/sinful register (7 + 7) is unique in marking compassion as **bounded by justice** — it is the one affection the corpus shows being rightly withheld.

**T3.9.1 · Conscience (engage?)** — Lightly (1 occurrence): the merciless act troubles the moral witness — "because he had no pity" pronounces guilt (2Sa 12:6, in Nathan's conviction of David).

**T3.9.2 · Conscience (effect)** — The absence of pity is convicted as sin; mercy clears, mercilessness condemns.

**T3.9.3 · Conscience (pattern)** — Conscience engagement is minimal; compassion touches moral evaluation more than the convicting conscience.

**T3.10.1 · Conscientiousness (engage?)** — Yes — compassion integrates perceiving the need, being moved, and acting (Psa 72:13; Mat 18:27) — awareness, affect, and deed together.

**T3.10.2 · Conscientiousness (effect)** — It yields consistent relief of the vulnerable; its failure yields hardness.

**T3.10.3 · Conscientiousness (pattern)** — Compassion reads as an **integrated response** moving from sight to feeling to action.

**T3.11.1 · Relational capacity (engage?)** — Yes — compassion is the equipment for connection with the suffering other (the yearning over one's own, 1Ki 3:26; mercy toward captives, 2Ch 30:9).

**T3.11.2 · Relational capacity (effect)** — It binds the merciful to the pitied — forgiving the debtor, relieving the needy — repairing or preserving relationship.

**T3.11.3 · Relational capacity (pattern)** — Strongly other-directed (other 105 + addressed 48 vs self 40), compassion is **fundamentally relational and asymmetric** — directed from the able toward the needy.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1 · God→human** — Yes, dominantly: "his mercy is very great" (1Ch 21:13); "grant them compassion" (1Ki 8:50); "the Father of mercies" (2Cor 1:3). God's compassion moves toward the person.

**T4.1.2 · basis** — **Covenantal and responsive** — "if you return to the Lord … find compassion" (2Ch 30:9); grounded in God's character, "gracious and merciful, slow to anger" (the channun/rachum formula). Extended to the guilty who return.

**T4.1.3 · what it shows of God** — That God is **disposed to relent and relieve** — his greatness expressed as mercy toward the distressed and forgiveness toward the penitent (1Ki 8:50).

**T4.2.1 · human→God** — Yes — the cry for mercy is the person's movement toward God: "Have mercy on me, O Lord, Son of David" (Mat 15:22); "let me fall into the hand of the Lord, for his mercy is very great" (1Ch 21:13).

**T4.2.2 · inner posture required** — **Distress and dependence** — the appeal of the helpless casting themselves on God's mercy (1Ch 21:13; Mat 15:22).

**T4.2.3 · what it shows** — That the person relates to God **as the needy to the merciful** — the posture of the supplicant who has only God's compassion to appeal to.

**T4.3.1 · human→human giving** — Yes — mercy extended person to person: sparing, forgiving the debt (Mat 18:27), giving alms (Act 10:2), the king's pity on the needy (Psa 72:13).

**T4.3.2 · giver's inner conditions** — A **compassionate, tender heart** (Col 3:12; 1Pe 3:8) and freedom from the hardness that "had no pity" (2Sa 12:6).

**T4.3.3 · what the giver must have received/become** — They must have **received God's mercy** — "the Father of mercies … who comforts us … so that we may be able to comfort" (2Cor 1:3–4): received mercy is the spring of given mercy.

**T4.4.1 · human→human receiving** — Yes — the pitied receive relief (the forgiven debtor, Mat 18:27; the captives who "find compassion", 2Ch 30:9).

**T4.4.2 · conditions accompanying / blocking uptake** — Uptake accompanies the plight acknowledged; it is blocked where the merciful is hardened (2Sa 12:6) or where justice forbids (Deu 19:21).

**T4.4.3 · state of one who meets it but does not take it up** — **Silent / partial:** the forgiven-but-unmerciful servant (Mat 18, who received mercy yet showed none) is the nearest case — mercy received but not passed on.

**T4.5.1 · within bonds vs across distance** — Both: most natural within bonds (the yearning over one's own son, 1Ki 3:26) yet extended across distance to captives (2Ch 30:9) and the needy stranger (Psa 72:13).

**T4.5.2 · within covenant only, or crossing** — It **crosses covenantal boundaries** in relief of the needy (Psa 72:13) and is **bounded by covenant** in the herem/judicial cases (1Sa 15:3) — compassion both reaches beyond and is checked by covenant.

**T4.5.3 · relational scope** — Broad (the weak, needy, guilty, captive) but **uniquely bounded** — the condemned in judicial/herem contexts are explicitly excluded from pity (Deu 19:21; 1Sa 15:3).

**T4.6.1 / T4.6.2 / T4.6.3 · Spiritual beings** — **Silent.** No occurrence shows compassion operating toward, attacked by, or mediated through angelic or adversarial beings.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1 · Transformation** — Yes — compassion changes the sufferer's **condition** (the debtor released, Mat 18:27; the captives restored, 2Ch 30:9) and, received from God, the person's standing ("born again to a living hope", 1Pe 1:3).

**T5.1.2 · reversible / irreversible** — **Silent** on reversibility, though the unmerciful servant's later judgment (Mat 18) implies received mercy can be forfeited.

**T5.2.1 · Sequence of inner states** — Yes — **plight perceived → heart moved/yearns → relief enacted**: "her heart yearned for her son" → she yields him to save him (1Ki 3:26); "out of pity … released him" (Mat 18:27).

**T5.3.1 · Mechanism of change** — By **encounter with mercy** (received relief, forgiveness) and by **the visceral movement** that perception of suffering triggers (1Ki 3:26).

**T5.3.2 · mechanism differ across contexts** — Yes: divine mercy works by sovereign relenting (1Ki 8:50); human mercy by the moved heart acting (Mat 18:27); its withholding by the demand of justice (1Sa 15:3).

**T5.4.1 · Suffering / affliction** — Yes, constitutively — compassion is the **response to suffering**: it arises at the sight of the afflicted (Psa 72:13) and meets distress with relief (1Ch 21:13 "I am in great distress … his mercy is very great").

**T5.4.2 · what suffering does to it** — Suffering is the **occasion that evokes** compassion — the plight of the weak draws it out; in the merciful, the experience of having been pitied deepens the capacity to pity (2Cor 1:3–4).

**T5.5.1 · Formation and sanctification** — Yes — compassion is "put on" as a garment of the chosen (Col 3:12) and listed among the marks of the sanctified ("a tender heart", 1Pe 3:8) — it forms the merciful character over time.

**T5.6.1 · Eschatological trajectory** — Yes — God's mercy grounds the future: "according to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope" (1Pe 1:3); "the tender mercy of our God, whereby the sunrise shall visit us from on high" (Luk 1:78). Present mercy anticipates final visitation.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1 · Co-occurrence** — Compassion co-occurs most with **kindness/steadfast love** (chesed, 19×), **graciousness** (channun/chanan — the "gracious and merciful" formula, 21×), the within-band **pity/sparing** terms (chus, chamal), **remembrance** (zakhar), **the soul/life** (nephesh, co-seated), **peace** (eirēnē), and, by contrast, with **transgression/abomination** (pesha, toevah — the wrong that mercy meets).

**T6.1.2 · what co-occurrence shows** — Compassion stands in a **covenant-mercy complex** — fused with grace and steadfast love (the classic "gracious and merciful, abounding in steadfast love" triad) — and is characteristically directed at sin and need (transgression, the soul in distress).

**T6.2.1 · Sequential relationship** — Yes — compassion **follows the perceiving of need/transgression** and **precedes forgiveness/relief**: "forgive your people … and grant them compassion" (1Ki 8:50) — mercy as the hinge between sin seen and relief given.

**T6.2.2 · what the sequence shows** — The relationship is **causal**: perceived plight causes the yearning; the yearning causes the act of relief.

**T6.3.1 · does it produce another?** — Yes — compassion produces **comfort** (D): "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3) — mercy issuing in consolation; and **forgiveness** (1Ki 8:50).

**T6.3.2 · is it produced by another?** — It is bound up with **grace** and **steadfast love** as their near-equivalent (the channun/chesed pairing); it flows from God's covenant character.

**T6.3.3 · constituent of / constituted by another?** — Compassion is a **constituent of God's covenant goodness** (gracious, merciful, steadfast-loving) and, with kindness and humility, a constituent of the "compassionate heart" put on by the chosen (Col 3:12).

**T6.4.1 · shared vocabulary** — It shares the **chesed** field with kindness (C) at the lexical edge (the "gracious and merciful, abounding in steadfast love" formula joins them), and the **splanchna** (bowels) imagery overlaps with love's embodied register.

**T6.4.2 · shared root architecture** — The **racham** root (rachamim, rachum, racham) generates the band internally; *rechem* (womb) is its root-image, shared with no other characteristic — a distinct visceral architecture.

**T6.4.3 · what the sharing shows** — The chesed-overlap shows compassion and kindness as **adjacent faces of covenant mercy** — compassion the felt stirring, chesed the kept commitment.

**T6.5.1 · nearest neighbour and distinction** — The nearest neighbour is **kindness/chesed (C)**. The distinction: compassion is the **affective, often momentary stirring toward a plight** (felt in the viscera, action-led); chesed is the **borne, durable loyalty** (status/quality, possessor-dominant). Compassion responds; chesed keeps.

**T6.5.2 · precise boundary at overlap** — Where they overlap (the mercy formulae), the boundary is **duration and typing**: compassion is enacted relief at a moment of need; chesed is steadfast love that endures "forever".

**T6.5.3 · distinction of degree, kind, direction, or level?** — One of **kind and duration** (momentary affective relief vs durable borne loyalty); against comfort (D) the distinction is **stage** (compassion feels the plight; comfort comes alongside to console).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1 · primary terms and root meanings** — Hebrew **racham/rachamim** (compassion, from *rechem* the womb), **chus** (pity), **chamal** (spare); Greek **eleos/eleeō** (mercy), **oiktirmos** (compassion), **splanchnizō** (be moved in the bowels). Roots converge on a **visceral movement** (womb, bowels) toward the needy.

**T7.1.2 · grammatical range** — Verbs (racham, chus, chamal, eleeō, splanchnizō), nouns (rachamim, eleos, oiktirmos), adjectives (rachum, eleēmōn, eusplanchnos — "good-gutted"). The range shows compassion as **act, attribute, and disposition**.

**T7.1.3 · semantic range** — From visceral yearning (Jer 31:20) through pity, sparing, and forgiveness (1Ki 8:50) to enacted almsgiving (Act 10:2) and bounded withholding (Deu 19:21). A range from felt stirring to material relief to its just limitation.

**T7.1.4 · terms distinguishing aspects** — Yes — the **act** terms (eleeō, splanchnizō — to have mercy/be moved) are distinguished from the **disposition** adjectives (rachum, eusplanchnos — merciful, tender-hearted), and **eleēmosunē** (alms) names the *enacted/given* form distinct from the felt stirring.

**T7.1.5 · term for the opposite/absence** — Yes — the antonym terms **aneleēmōn / anileōs** ("merciless") name the absence; "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" (Pro 12:10) names it in context.

**T7.1.6 · person-type term** — Yes — **rachum / eleēmōn** (merciful one): "Blessed are the merciful" (the Beatitude register); "the Lord your God is gracious and merciful" (2Ch 30:9).

**T7.1.7 · supplication / seeking term** — Yes — the **cry for mercy** is a recurrent speech form: "Have mercy on me, O Lord" (Mat 15:22) — *eleeō* in the imperative as the supplication for compassion.

**T7.1.8 · OT↔NT continuity / development** — The OT **racham/chesed** mercy (covenant, visceral, "gracious and merciful") continues into the NT **eleos/oiktirmos/splanchna**, with the womb/bowels imagery preserved (splanchna). Continuity of the visceral covenant-mercy field; development toward mercy realised in Christ's "tender mercy" (Luk 1:78).

**T7.1.9 · NT-coined term** — **None** clearly coined; the splanchna-verbs intensify existing imagery rather than coin a new concept. **Silent.**

**T7.1.10 · full vocabulary arc** — The arc spans **womb-yearning (rachamim) → pity/sparing (chus, chamal) → mercy/forgiveness (eleos) → tender-heartedness (eusplanchnos) → almsgiving (eleēmosunē) → its merciless opposite (aneleēmōn)** — the complete range of being-moved toward the needy and its failure.

**T7.2.1 · function of the primary term in its primary verse** — In **Luk 1:78** ("because of the tender mercy of our God") oiktirmos/splanchna is the **cause** of God's visitation — the term grounds the whole salvation-clause. In **2Cor 1:3** oiktirmos names God by his mercies.

**T7.2.2 · literary forms** — **Narrative** (1Ki 3; 1Sa 15; Mat 18 parable), **law** (Deu pity-prohibitions), **psalm** (Psa 72), **prophecy** (Jer 31), **epistle/canticle** (2Cor 1; Luk 1). Responsible reading honours the judicial law's bounding of pity distinctly from the psalm's praise of it.

**T7.2.3 · logical structure** — 2Cor 1:3–4: premise — God is the Father of mercies who comforts us; conclusion — therefore we can comfort others "with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted." 1Ki 8:50: petition — forgive and grant compassion — grounded in God's merciful character.

**T7.2.4 · contextual setting** — **Covenantal** (2Ch 30:9 return-and-find-compassion), **judicial** (Deu pity-prohibitions; 1Sa 15 herem), **liturgical/petitionary** (1Ch 21:13; Mat 15:22), and **eschatological** (Luk 1:78). The judicial setting shows compassion's unique bounding.

**T7.2.5 · primary anchor verse** — **Luk 1:78** — "because of the tender mercy of our God, whereby the sunrise shall visit us from on high" — most fully expresses compassion's essential character: visceral (splanchna), divine, and salvific. **Jer 31:20** is the OT anchor (the womb-yearning).

**T7.2.6 · what the anchor shows that no other shows** — Luk 1:78 alone **joins the visceral seat (splanchna) to the dawn of salvation** — compassion as the very motive of God's redemptive visitation, the bowels-mercy made the engine of redemption.

**T7.3.1 · most useful human-science framework** — **Social neuroscience / clinical psychology** — empathy and compassion research (Batson's empathy-altruism; Singer's shared-pain neuroimaging; Neff's self-compassion).

**T7.3.2 · where the framework illuminates** — The shared-pain research (witnessing another's pain activates one's own pain circuits) is the **literal basis of compassion as co-suffering** — illuminating the splanchna/rachamim somatic vocabulary; and Batson's empathy-altruism (empathic concern reliably motivates other-directed action) illuminates compassion's action-heavy typing. The womb-etymology aligns with a "primal, pre-cognitive" movement of the viscera — matching the corpus's pre-deliberative (no cognition tag) profile.

**T7.3.3 · where evidence and framework diverge** — Science explains compassion by in-group similarity and is undercut by dehumanisation and out-group distance; the corpus extends mercy to the **guilty, the captive, and the enemy who returns** (2Ch 30:9; 1Ki 8:50) and grounds it in God's character — and, conversely, the corpus *bounds* pity by justice (Deu 19:21; 1Sa 15) in a way the empathy account does not address.

**T7.3.4 · aspect surfaced by the framework** — **Compassion fatigue** (empathic arousal overwhelming rather than motivating) surfaces a question the corpus's bounded-pity material may answer differently — whether the *withholding* of pity in judicial contexts is a moral boundary rather than a depletion; and whether the cluster includes resources for sustaining compassion (cross-link to comfort, D). **Flagged for further verse work.**
