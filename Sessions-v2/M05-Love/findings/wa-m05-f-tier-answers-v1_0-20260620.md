## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — At the lexical-tag level, **no occurrence tags divine involvement** — friendship is the cluster's one wholly horizontal characteristic. At the verse level, two exceptions relate it to God: **God as the one befriended** — "Abraham believed God … and he was called a friend of God" (Jam 2:23); and **Christ befriending** — "No longer do I call you servants … but I have called you friends" (Joh 15:15). *(Observation: the tag is empty; interpretation: these two verses extend friendship to the God-relation.)*

**T0.1.2** — Friendship is **not predicated of God as a borne attribute** (God is not said to *be* friendship as he is said to be love or "the God of all comfort"); it appears as a **relation entered** — God befriends Abraham (Jam 2:23) and Christ befriends the disciples (Joh 15:14–15). The pattern indicates friendship is a creaturely-relational reality into which God graciously enters, rather than a divine attribute mirrored in the creature.

**T0.2.1** — Yes — friendship serves **loyal presence, disclosure, and rejoicing-together**: "David's friend" stands by him in crisis (2Sa 15:37); the friend "rejoices greatly" with the other (Joh 3:29); friends are called together to "Rejoice with me" (Luk 15:6). It binds persons in shared life.

**T0.2.2** — **Created design** as the good of human bonding, operating in the fallen world (friends in danger, betrayal, Luk 21:16) and capable of disorder ("friendship with the world is enmity with God", Jam 4:4). The bond itself is a creaturely good; its object can corrupt it.

**T0.2.3** — Yes, lightly — friendship is bound to a future welcome: "make friends for yourselves … so that when it fails they may receive you into the eternal dwellings" (Luk 16:9); and Christ's friends share in his disclosed purposes (Joh 15:15). It orients toward enduring fellowship.

**T0.3.1** — The likeness instantiated is **intimate disclosure and loyal love between persons** — Christ's "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" (Joh 15:15) images a fellowship of shared knowledge and self-giving ("lay down his life for his friends", Joh 15:13).

**T0.3.2** — **An exclusively creaturely-relational good into which God enters** rather than a quality shared symmetrically — God is "friend *of*" Abraham (Jam 2:23) within a relation, not the bearer of a friendship-attribute. *(Interpretation, labelled — flows from the empty divine-bearer tag.)*

**T0.3.3** — Friendship's presence marks the person as bonded and known (Joh 15:15); its misdirection ("friend of the world", Jam 4:4) marks alienation from God. No image-doctrine is stated; **largely inferential**.

**T0.4.1** — Yes — **christological, divine establishing the pattern**: Christ's befriending of the disciples (Joh 15:14–15) and Abraham's friendship with God (Jam 2:23) set the pattern of divine-human friendship the human bond points toward.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Friendship. The name signals the **chosen peer-bond of mutual affection, loyalty, and shared life** — the standing relation between those who love one another as equals.

**T1.1.2** — Greek **filos** (friend), **filia** (friendship), **filofrōn** (friendly/of a friendly mind); Hebrew **re.eh** ("the king's friend", a court companion-office, 1Ki 4:5). Definitionally the *phil-* terms name the **affectionate bond** (shared root with love and brotherly-love).

**T1.1.3** — The name carries a **mutual, horizontal** implication — friendship runs between peers ("became friends with each other", Luk 23:12), marked by reciprocity and standing-with rather than the asymmetry of mercy or comfort.

**T1.2.1** — A **borne relational quality** — *quality* (28) far above *status* (4) and with no action tagging. Friendship is a standing bond one *has*, not a discrete act. **Scope-flag note:** this "quality" is the quality of a *relation between* persons, which is part of why F sits at the cluster's edge.

**T1.2.2** — **Compound**: friendship combines affection (the felt warmth, affect-tagged), loyalty (standing by — "David's friend", 2Sa 15:37), disclosure (shared knowledge — Joh 15:15), and shared rejoicing (Joh 3:29; Luk 15:6). It is a bond of feeling, fidelity, and mutual openness.

**T1.3.1** — Its structural opposite is **enmity** — "Herod and Pilate became friends … for before this they had been at enmity" (Luk 23:12); "friendship with the world is enmity with God" (Jam 4:4). Friendship and enmity are the named contraries.

**T1.3.2** — At its edge friendship excludes and resists **betrayal and divided loyalty**: friends may betray ("delivered up even by … friends", Luk 21:16), and friendship of the wrong object is forbidden ("a friend of the world makes himself an enemy of God", Jam 4:4).

**T1.3.3** — Friendship is **not mere acquaintance and not the asymmetry of patronage**: it is mutual and disclosing — "No longer do I call you servants … but … friends" (Joh 15:15) marks the move from hierarchy to friendship. It ends where the bond is one-sided or its object displaces God (Jam 4:4).

**T1.4.1** — It operates as a **standing bond expressed in presence, request, disclosure, and shared joy** — standing by (2Sa 15:37), asking a friend's help ("Friend, lend me three loaves", Luk 11:5), being entrusted with knowledge (Joh 15:15), rejoicing together (Luk 15:6). Grammatically it is borne (noun/adjective), not a verb of doing.

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by **object and depth**: court loyalty ("the king's friend", 1Ki 4:5), intimate disclosure (Joh 15:15), mutual aid (Luk 11:5–8), and disordered alliance ("friendship with the world", Jam 4:4).

**T1.4.3** — Yes — friendship operates in **speech**: it is the form of address ("Friend, move up higher", Luk 14:10; "my friends, do not fear", Luk 12:4) and of mutual greeting ("The friends greet you. Greet the friends, each by name", 3Jo 15). One occurrence is commanded — the "friendly/humble mind" (filofrōn, 1Pe 3:8).

**T1.5.1** — The immediate response friendship shows is **loyal standing-with and shared rejoicing** — the friend who stands and "rejoices greatly" (Joh 3:29); friends called to "Rejoice with me" (Luk 15:6).

**T1.5.2** — Varied — loyalty in crisis (2Sa 15:37), aid in need (Luk 11:5), joy in finding (Luk 15:6), and, in betrayal, abandonment (Luk 21:16) — but consistent in expressing the bond's standing.

**T1.6.1** — Over time friendship **establishes loyal, disclosing fellowship** — the standing relation of those entrusted with one another ("I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you", Joh 15:15).

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is an act of loyalty or shared joy; the sustained effect is the **durable bond** itself — friends who stand by over time (2Sa 15:37) and are received "into the eternal dwellings" (Luk 16:9).

**T1.7.1** — It takes hold rightly in **mutual love, loyalty, and obedience to Christ** — "You are my friends if you do what I command you" (Joh 15:14) — and in a "friendly/humble mind" toward one another (1Pe 3:8).

**T1.7.2** — It is blocked or distorted by **enmity, betrayal, and misdirected alliance** — enmity (Luk 23:12), betrayal by friends (Luk 21:16), and friendship with the world that becomes "enmity with God" (Jam 4:4). **No adversarial spirit** is shown distorting it — **Silent**.

**T1.7.3** — The person in whom friendship is present but does not take hold is the **false or betraying friend** — those who "deliver up" their friends (Luk 21:16), and the elder brother who claims faithful service yet was never given "a young goat … to celebrate with my friends" (Luk 15:29), missing the fellowship offered.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — **Soul** (1 — the only constitutional tag in the band): friendship reaches the soul in the deepest bonds, but the corpus rarely localises it constitutionally. **Largely unlocated** — consistent with the scope flag (a bond *between* persons is not easily seated *within* one).

**T2.1.2** — The near-absence of constitutional location is itself a finding: friendship is **the least interiorly-seated characteristic** in the cluster, engaging the affections (affect-tagged) but rarely named to heart, mind, or spirit. This supports the question whether F is an inner-being characteristic or a relational category.

**T2.10.1** — Yes, lightly — friendship is **received from outside** (received-from-outside, 3 occurrences) — entered through another's befriending: Christ "called you friends" (Joh 15:15); Herod and Pilate "became friends" (Luk 23:12). It comes upon the person through relation.

**T2.7.1** — **Silent.** No body link is evidenced.

**T2.9.1** — **Generated between persons and, in the key cases, initiated by another** — Christ initiates ("I have called you friends", Joh 15:15); God befriends Abraham (Jam 2:23). Origin is relational, often initiated by the greater party.

**T2.9.2** — **Single in kind** (relational origin) but varied in initiator — sometimes mutual ("became friends", Luk 23:12), sometimes graciously initiated (Joh 15:15).

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1 / T3.1.2 / T3.1.3 · Perception** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages perception.

**T3.2.1 · Cognition (engage?)** — Yes, lightly — friendship involves **shared knowledge/disclosure**: "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" (Joh 15:15). The friend is the one let into knowledge.

**T3.2.2 · Cognition (effect)** — It opens mutual understanding — the friend, unlike the servant, "knows what his master is doing" (Joh 15:15).

**T3.2.3 · Cognition (pattern)** — Cognition appears only at the bond's highest register (disclosure); it is not a general feature, marking friendship as chiefly affective with an apex of shared knowing.

**T3.3.1 / T3.3.2 / T3.3.3 · Memory** — **Silent.** No memorial engagement evidenced.

**T3.4.1 · Affect (engage?)** — Yes, dominantly (affect 28 of 32): friendship is the felt bond — warmth, loyalty, and shared joy ("rejoices greatly", Joh 3:29).

**T3.4.2 · Affect (effect)** — It produces the warmth of belonging and the joy of shared life (Luk 15:6) — and, in loss, the pain of betrayal (Luk 21:16).

**T3.4.3 · Affect (pattern)** — Affect dominance with almost no other faculty marks friendship as **a felt relational bond** more than a structured inner faculty — its whole register is affection-and-loyalty.

**T3.5.1 / T3.5.2 / T3.5.3 · Creativity** — **Silent.** No engagement with imagination or origination.

**T3.6.1 · Volition (engage?)** — Yes, lightly — friendship is **chosen and conditioned on will**: "You are my friends if you do what I command you" (Joh 15:14); one may "make friends" (Luk 16:9) or wrongly choose "friendship with the world" (Jam 4:4).

**T3.6.2 · Volition (effect)** — It directs the will toward loyalty and right alliance (Joh 15:14) or, disordered, toward the world (Jam 4:4).

**T3.6.3 · Volition (pattern)** — Volition appears at the points of *choosing* the bond and its object — friendship is entered and aimed by the will, though it is felt as affection.

**T3.7.1 · Agency (engage?)** — Lightly — friends act for one another: standing by (2Sa 15:37), aiding (Luk 11:5–8), interceding ("friends … urging him not to venture", Act 19:31), caring ("gave him leave to go to his friends and be cared for", Act 27:3).

**T3.7.2 · Agency (effect)** — The bond moves friends to mutual aid and protection.

**T3.7.3 · Agency (pattern)** — Agency is the bond's outworking, not its essence — friendship *is* a standing relation that *issues in* acts of loyalty.

**T3.8.1 · Moral evaluation (engage?)** — Yes, at the object: friendship is morally weighed by *whom* one befriends — "friendship with the world is enmity with God" (Jam 4:4, sinful); "a friend of God" (Jam 2:23, righteous); "a friend of tax collectors and sinners" (Luk 7:34, a charge Jesus bears in righteousness).

**T3.8.2 · Moral evaluation (effect)** — The bond is good or evil by its object — godly friendship is righteousness (Jam 2:23); worldly friendship is enmity with God (Jam 4:4).

**T3.8.3 · Moral evaluation (pattern)** — Mostly neutral-valenced (20 of 32) because friendship is a relational fact morally indifferent in itself; like love, its moral value **turns on its object** at the edges (the sinful and righteous cases).

**T3.9.1 / T3.9.2 / T3.9.3 · Conscience** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages the conscience.

**T3.10.1 · Conscientiousness (engage?)** — Lightly — friendship integrates loyal awareness and faithful action (standing by in danger, 2Sa 15:37; the "friendly mind", 1Pe 3:8) — but it is not a developed conscientiousness in the corpus.

**T3.10.2 · Conscientiousness (effect)** — Where present, it yields dependable loyalty between persons.

**T3.10.3 · Conscientiousness (pattern)** — Minimal — friendship's reliability is a feature of the *bond*, not a structured inner conscientiousness.

**T3.11.1 · Relational capacity (engage?)** — Yes, constitutively and pre-eminently — friendship **is** relational capacity in its purest form: the equipment for mutual, disclosing, loyal connection between persons (Joh 15:15).

**T3.11.2 · Relational capacity (effect)** — It binds persons as peers — standing by, sharing knowledge and joy, aiding in need — the most directly relational of all the cluster's characteristics.

**T3.11.3 · Relational capacity (pattern)** — Friendship is so wholly relational that it raises the **scope question** — it engages the relational capacity not as one faculty among others within a person but as the *bond itself between* persons.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1 · God→human** — Yes, lightly — Christ befriends and discloses to the disciples: "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" (Joh 15:15).

**T4.1.2 · basis** — **Gracious initiative conditioned on faithful response** — "You are my friends if you do what I command you" (Joh 15:14): Christ initiates the friendship and it holds in obedience.

**T4.1.3 · what it shows of God** — That God in Christ **stoops to befriend** — moving the disciples from servants to friends and admitting them to his counsel (Joh 15:15), a friendship of disclosure and self-gift ("lay down his life for his friends", Joh 15:13).

**T4.2.1 · human→God** — Yes — Abraham's friendship with God: "Abraham believed God … and he was called a friend of God" (Jam 2:23) — friendship grounded in faith.

**T4.2.2 · inner posture required** — **Faith and obedience** — Abraham "believed God" (Jam 2:23); the disciples are friends "if you do what I command" (Joh 15:14).

**T4.2.3 · what it shows** — That the person may stand with God **as friend** — admitted to intimacy and counsel — the highest dignity of the bond, grounded in faith.

**T4.3.1 · human→human giving** — Yes, dominantly — friendship extended between persons: standing by (2Sa 15:37), aiding (Luk 11:5), interceding (Act 19:31), caring (Act 27:3), greeting (3Jo 15).

**T4.3.2 · giver's inner conditions** — A "friendly/humble mind" (filofrōn, 1Pe 3:8), loyalty, and shared love; and, supremely, willingness to "lay down his life for his friends" (Joh 15:13).

**T4.3.3 · what the giver must have received/become** — They must have **entered the mutual bond** — friendship presupposes reciprocity; Christ's friends first received his befriending and disclosure (Joh 15:15).

**T4.4.1 · human→human receiving** — Yes — friendship is received in being befriended ("I have called you friends", Joh 15:15) and welcomed ("Friend, move up higher", Luk 14:10; "received … into the eternal dwellings", Luk 16:9).

**T4.4.2 · conditions accompanying / blocking uptake** — Uptake accompanies mutual loyalty; it is blocked by enmity (Luk 23:12 before reconciliation), betrayal (Luk 21:16), or a heart set on the world (Jam 4:4).

**T4.4.3 · state of one who meets it but does not take it up** — The **elder brother**, who served faithfully yet never celebrated "with my friends" (Luk 15:29) — fellowship available but unentered; and the betrayer who turns friend to enemy (Luk 21:16).

**T4.5.1 · within bonds vs across distance** — Friendship **is** the existing bond, yet it can **cross distance and difference** — Jesus the "friend of tax collectors and sinners" (Luk 7:34) befriends across the moral boundary; former enemies "became friends" (Luk 23:12).

**T4.5.2 · within covenant only, or crossing** — It **crosses** — Jesus befriends sinners (Luk 7:34); friendship is extended to outsiders and former enemies (Luk 23:12) — though friendship "with the world" as a system is forbidden (Jam 4:4).

**T4.5.3 · relational scope** — Peers, kin, the powerful ("the king's friend", 1Ki 4:5; Asiarchs, Act 19:31), sinners (Luk 7:34), and the disciples whom Christ befriends; the one excluded bond is friendship *with the world* set against God (Jam 4:4).

**T4.6.1 / T4.6.2 / T4.6.3 · Spiritual beings** — **Silent.** No occurrence shows friendship operating toward, attacked by, or mediated through angelic or adversarial beings.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1 · Transformation** — Yes, lightly — friendship transforms **status**: from servant to friend (Joh 15:15), from enemies to friends (Luk 23:12) — a change in the relation between persons.

**T5.1.2 · reversible / irreversible** — **Shown reversible** — friends may become betrayers (Luk 21:16) and enemies may become friends (Luk 23:12); the bond can be entered and lost.

**T5.2.1 · Sequence of inner states** — Yes, lightly — **enmity → reconciliation → friendship** (Luk 23:12), and **servant → friend** (Joh 15:15) — relational sequences more than inner-state sequences.

**T5.3.1 · Mechanism of change** — By **befriending and disclosure** (Joh 15:15) and by **reconciliation** (Luk 23:12); friendship is formed through shared life, loyalty, and admitted intimacy.

**T5.3.2 · mechanism differ across contexts** — Yes: divine friendship by Christ's gracious disclosure (Joh 15:15); human friendship by reciprocal loyalty and shared experience (the aid and rejoicing texts); disordered friendship by alliance with the world (Jam 4:4).

**T5.4.1 · Suffering / affliction** — Yes — friendship operates **in danger and loss**: friends stand by in crisis (2Sa 15:37), are sources of care in trouble (Act 27:3), yet may betray under pressure (Luk 21:16); the greatest friendship is proven in death ("lay down his life for his friends", Joh 15:13).

**T5.4.2 · what suffering does to it** — Affliction **tests and proves** friendship — it is the arena in which loyalty is shown (2Sa 15:37; Joh 15:13) or fails (Luk 21:16).

**T5.5.1 · Formation and sanctification** — Lightly — friendship with Christ shapes the disciple toward obedience and shared purpose (Joh 15:14–15); the "friendly mind" is a commanded disposition (1Pe 3:8). Its formative role is real but thinner than the cluster's other characteristics. **Scope-flag note:** F's formation is largely the formation *of a relationship*, not of an inner virtue.

**T5.6.1 · Eschatological trajectory** — Yes, lightly — friendship is bound to a future welcome: "make friends for yourselves … so that … they may receive you into the eternal dwellings" (Luk 16:9). Present friendship anticipates eternal fellowship.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1 · Co-occurrence** — Friendship co-occurs with **love** (agapē — supremely Joh 15:13, love laid down "for his friends"), **rejoicing-with** (sunchairō — "Rejoice with me", Luk 15:6, 9), **brotherly-love** (filadelfos) and **compassion/sympathy** (eusplanchnos, sumpathēs — the 1Pe 3:8 virtue-list), **like-mindedness** (homofrōn), **calling** (kaleō), and, by contrast, with **the sinful** (hamartōlos — "friend of … sinners", Luk 7:34).

**T6.1.2 · what co-occurrence shows** — Friendship sits within the **affectionate-bonding family** (love, brotherly-love, like-mindedness) and is the relational frame within which love is exercised ("for his friends", Joh 15:13) and joy is shared (Luk 15:6).

**T6.2.1 · Sequential relationship** — Yes — friendship is the **bond within which love is enacted**: "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends" (Joh 15:13) — love is shown *to* friends; friendship is the standing context, love the act within it.

**T6.2.2 · what the sequence shows** — The relationship is **constitutive/contextual**: friendship is the relation; love is the movement that fills and proves it.

**T6.3.1 · does it produce another?** — Yes — friendship produces **shared joy** (sunchairō, Luk 15:6) and is the occasion of **sacrificial love** (Joh 15:13).

**T6.3.2 · is it produced by another?** — It is produced by **love and like-mindedness** — the affectionate, like-minded disposition that forms the bond (1Pe 3:8 virtue-list); Christ's love produces his friendship with the disciples (Joh 15).

**T6.3.3 · constituent of / constituted by another?** — Friendship is **constituted by** love, loyalty, and shared joy, and is itself the **relational frame** of which love (A) is the animating act. It is a *category* hosting the cluster's affections more than a discrete inner faculty — the scope flag again.

**T6.4.1 · shared vocabulary** — Friendship shares the **phil- root** with love (fileō), brotherly-love (filadelfia), benevolence (filanthrōpia), and good-loving (filagathos); filos, filia, and filofrōn are the friendship members of that family.

**T6.4.2 · shared root architecture** — The **phil-** root is the shared architecture generating love, friendship, brotherly-love, and benevolence — friendship is one branch of the single affectional root.

**T6.4.3 · what the sharing shows** — The phil-root sharing shows friendship is **a species of love's bonding** — the standing peer-relation within the wider family of affection.

**T6.5.1 · nearest neighbour and distinction** — The nearest neighbour is **love (A)**. The distinction: love is the **affection/act** (the movement of self-giving toward an object); friendship is the **standing mutual bond** (the relation between peers within which love lives). Love is what is given; friendship is the relationship that hosts it — "lay down his life [love] for his friends [friendship]" (Joh 15:13) holds both in one verse.

**T6.5.2 · precise boundary at overlap** — Where they overlap (Joh 15:13–15), the boundary is **act vs relation**: love names the self-giving; friendship names the mutual, disclosing bond between the parties.

**T6.5.3 · distinction of degree, kind, direction, or level?** — One of **kind** (act/affection vs standing relation) — and, at the deepest level, a **category** distinction that grounds the scope flag (F as relational category vs A as inner-being affection).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1 · primary terms and root meanings** — Greek **filos** (friend), **filia** (friendship), **filofrōn** (friendly-minded) — all from the *phil-* root (affection); Hebrew **re.eh** ("the king's friend", a court office). Roots converge on **affectionate peer-bond**.

**T7.1.2 · grammatical range** — Noun (filos, filia), adjective (filofrōn "friendly"). The absence of an action-verb and the dominance of nouns confirm friendship operates as a **borne relation**, not an act.

**T7.1.3 · semantic range** — From court office ("the king's friend", 1Ki 4:5) through ordinary companionship (Luk 11:5; Act 10:24), intimate disclosure (Joh 15:15), and friendship with God (Jam 2:23) to disordered alliance ("friendship with the world", Jam 4:4). A range from social role to divine intimacy to forbidden alliance.

**T7.1.4 · terms distinguishing aspects** — Lightly — **filos** (the friend, person) is distinguished from **filia** (the friendship, relation) and **filofrōn** (the friendly disposition); the person/relation/disposition aspects are lexicalised within the small band.

**T7.1.5 · term for the opposite/absence** — The opposite is **enmity / enemy** (echthros, named in contrast — "enmity", Luk 23:12; "enemy of God", Jam 4:4); no dedicated antonym within the band, but enmity is its clear contrary.

**T7.1.6 · person-type term** — Yes — **filos** *is* the person-type ("the friend"), and Hebrew re.eh names the office-holder ("the king's friend", 1Ki 4:5).

**T7.1.7 · supplication / seeking term** — **None** dedicated; though one may "make friends" (Luk 16:9) and ask a friend's help ("Friend, lend me", Luk 11:5), there is no term for *seeking friendship* as such. **Silent.**

**T7.1.8 · OT↔NT continuity / development** — The OT friendship evidence in this band is the narrow court-office **re.eh** ("the king's friend"); the NT **filos/filia** carries the fuller relational and theological weight (friend of God, Jam 2:23; Christ's friends, Joh 15). The richer Hebrew *companion* term (re.a of Proverbs) is not in this band — a **data-coverage note**. Development runs toward the NT's elevated friendship.

**T7.1.9 · NT-coined term** — **None** coined, though Joh 15 **elevates** filos to name the disciples' relation to Christ — a theological elevation rather than a coinage. **Silent on coinage.**

**T7.1.10 · full vocabulary arc** — The arc spans **the king's friend (re.eh) → companion/friend (filos) → the friendship-bond (filia) → the friendly disposition (filofrōn) → friend of God / friends of Christ (Jam 2:23; Joh 15)** — from social office to divine intimacy.

**T7.2.1 · function of the primary term in its primary verse** — In **Joh 15:13–15** filos is the **term of the disciples' new standing** — Christ's "friends" rather than "servants", the relation that carries his self-disclosure and his self-giving love; the term bears the whole elevation of the passage.

**T7.2.2 · literary forms** — **Narrative** (1Ki 4; 2Sa 15; the Gospel scenes), **parable** (Luk 11:5–8 the friend at midnight; Luk 15 the lost-and-found celebrations), **discourse** (Joh 15), **epistle** (Jam 2; 4; 1Pe 3; 3Jo). Responsible reading honours the parable's illustrative aim distinctly from the discourse's theological claim.

**T7.2.3 · logical structure** — Joh 15:14–15: premise — friends, unlike servants, are admitted to the master's knowledge; conclusion — "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you." Jam 4:4: premise — friendship with the world is enmity with God; conclusion — therefore the would-be friend of the world makes himself God's enemy. Friendship is consistently defined by **whose** it is.

**T7.2.4 · contextual setting** — **Communal/social** (companionship, court, hospitality), **covenantal-theological** (friend of God, Jam 2:23; Christ's friends, Joh 15), and **antithetical** (friendship with the world vs God, Jam 4:4). The theological settings give the bond its weight.

**T7.2.5 · primary anchor verse** — **Joh 15:13** — "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends" — most fully expresses friendship's essential character by binding it to supreme love; with Joh 15:15 ("I have called you friends") as the relational anchor and Jam 2:23 ("friend of God") as the theological one.

**T7.2.6 · what the anchor shows that no other shows** — Joh 15:13–15 alone **raises friendship to the highest dignity** — the bond for which life is laid down and into which Christ admits his own, disclosing the Father's counsel — showing friendship at its summit is divine-human intimacy, not mere human companionship.

**T7.3.1 · most useful human-science framework** — **Social psychology / positive psychology** — friendship and belonging research (Aristotle's utility/pleasure/virtue friendships; Baumeister and Leary's "need to belong"; Putnam's social capital; Eisenberger's social-pain findings).

**T7.3.2 · where the framework illuminates** — The "need to belong" research illuminates friendship as a **fundamental human motivation** whose frustration produces distress — matching the corpus's portrayal of friends as sources of care and standing-with (Act 27:3; 2Sa 15:37); Aristotle's virtue-friendship illuminates the highest biblical register (Joh 15, friendship grounded in shared good and disclosure rather than utility).

**T7.3.3 · where evidence and framework diverge** — Science explains friendship by proximity, similarity, and reciprocal benefit; the corpus presents friendship **with God** (Jam 2:23) and **Christ's befriending of sinners and servants** (Luk 7:34; Joh 15:15) — bonds grounded in grace and faith rather than affinity or exchange — and **forbids** "friendship with the world" (Jam 4:4) on theological, not social, grounds.

**T7.3.4 · aspect surfaced by the framework** — Belonging research frames friendship as an inner human *need*, which sharpens the **scope question** the band already carries: is F an inner-being characteristic (a constitutional capacity/need within the person) or a relational category (the bond between persons)? The science's framing of belonging as an inner motivation may favour retaining F, but on the *need/capacity* rather than the *bond*. **Flagged for researcher review — the standing scope flag.**
