## §1 — Definition & scope of the characteristic

Gentleness is the cluster's **restraint** disposition: a settled mildness of temper — strength held deliberately in check — that governs *how* a person bears wrong, exercises authority, and meets others. Where bands A–D are affections that reach *toward* another (love, pity, kindness, comfort), E governs the **manner and limits** of that reach: power withheld, provocation absorbed, correction softened.

The band gathers two language streams. Hebrew **anavah / anvah** (H6038 / H6037) — rendered "gentleness, meekness, humility" — carries the posture of lowliness, frequently paired with the fear of the Lord and set before honour. Greek **praus / prautēs / praotēs** (G4239 / G4240 / G4236) — "meek, gentleness" — and **epieikēs / epieikeia** (G1933 / G1932) — "gentle, reasonable, forbearing, yielding" — carry the relational manner enjoined in New-Testament instruction. **anexikakos** (G0420, "enduring evil, not resentful") sits at the forbearance edge of the band.

## §5 — Observation (grounded in the 30 occurrences above)

- **Faculty is mind-governed, never affective.** Where a faculty is tagged (11/30, all on the Greek *praus* / *epieikēs* terms) it is uniformly **cognition** — no occurrence in the band is tagged *affect*. This sets E apart from the affect-dominated A–D bands: gentleness reads as a measured, deliberate restraint, not a felt affection.
- **It is a borne quality, not an act.** Type is *status* (20) and *quality* (9), with only one *action*. The characteristic is a disposition one *has*, not a thing one *does*.
- **Wholly positive, and heavily enjoined.** Valence is *righteous* (18) + *commanded* (11) + one *neutral*, with **zero** sinful or forbidden. Eleven of thirty are *commanded* — the New-Testament paraenesis enjoins it directly (Gal 6:1; Eph 4:2; Col 3:12; 1Ti 6:11; Tit 3:2; Jam 1:21; 1Pe 3:15).
- **It is shown toward others.** Where experiencer is tagged, *other* (8) + *other-addressed* (3) outweigh *self* (2). The evidence ties it to specific relational functions: restoring the fallen "in a spirit of gentleness" (Gal 6:1), correcting opponents "with gentleness" toward "repentance" (2Ti 2:25), the "gentle and quiet spirit" of 1Pe 3:4.
- **When seated, it sits in heart/soul.** Location is rare (4/30) but consistent with the inner being: heart (Pro 18:12; 1Pe 3:4 "the hidden person of the heart"), inward-parts (Col 3:12 "put on"), soul (Jam 1:21 "save your souls").
- **It is a divine attribute and a Christ-pattern.** Divine involvement appears as *possessor* — "your gentleness made me great" (2Sa 22:36 / Psa 18:35) — and as *object* — "the meekness and gentleness of Christ" (2Cor 10:1); the Lord's servant correcting "with gentleness" (2Ti 2:25).
- **Distribution.** The Hebrew *anavah* stream concentrates in OT wisdom and Psalms, where its sense leans **humility** (Pro 15:33; 18:12; 22:4 "humility comes before honour"; Zep 2:3 "seek humility"). The Greek stream concentrates in NT epistles as a **commanded virtue**, including the fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:23) and the Beatitude "the meek shall inherit" (Mat 5:5). Contextual sense spreads across gentleness / meekness / gentle / humility, with *epieikēs* also surfacing as "reasonableness" (Php 4:5) and "courtesy" (Tit 3:2), and *anexikakos* as "enduring evil" (2Ti 2:24).

## §6 — Interpretation (labelled; inferential where marked)

- **E is the governor of the cluster's affections.** A–D supply the outward movement of love; E supplies its temper — the withheld rod (1Cor 4:21), the borne wrong (2Ti 2:24), the softened correction (Gal 6:1; 2Ti 2:25). Read with the cognition-faculty tag, this is a deliberate, mind-governed control of strength rather than a spontaneous feeling.
- **A possible OT→NT centre-of-gravity shift — INFERENTIAL (GR-PROG-009).** The Hebrew *anavah* leans *humility* (lowliness before God and before honour); the Greek *praotēs / epieikēs* leans *gentleness toward people* (manner in relationship and correction). The field is one; its gravitational centre appears to move from posture-before-God to manner-toward-others. Labelled inferential pending a dedicated cross-testament read; not upgraded here.
- **An enjoined, imitable virtue.** The heavy *commanded* load plus the explicit Christ-pattern (2Cor 10:1) frames gentleness as something put on and learned, not a temperament one is simply born with.

## §7 — Reflection (cultural reach and tentative application — not prescriptive)

- **"Meekness" is culturally mis-heard as weakness.** The evidence reads against that: gentleness is God's own attribute that "made me great" (Psa 18:35), the manner in which authority is exercised (1Cor 4:21; 1Ti 3:3), and a strength under restraint. Any reader-facing output should expect and address this misreading.
- **Translation fragments the field.** The same *praus* is "meek" (Mat 5:5) and "gentle" (1Pe 3:4); *epieikēs* is "reasonable" (Php 4:5) and "courteous" (Tit 3:2). A reader who meets only one English word sees only part of the characteristic — relevant for cross-cultural and multi-translation audiences.
- **Situated, not generalised, application.** The verses attach gentleness to concrete functions — restoring the fallen, correcting opponents, marital conduct (1Pe 3:4), eldership qualification (1Ti 3:3). I record these situated uses without generalising them into a universal prescription; application is noted, not concluded.
