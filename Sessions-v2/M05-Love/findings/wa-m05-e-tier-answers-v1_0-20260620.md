## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — *Is the characteristic predicated of God / related to God, and in what relation?*
Yes, in two relations. **God as the one who bears it (possessor):** "You have given me the shield of your salvation … and your gentleness made me great" (2Sa 22:36; identically Psa 18:35). **Christ as the one whose gentleness is the named standard (object/pattern):** "I, Paul, myself entreat you, by the meekness and gentleness of Christ" (2Cor 10:1). Elsewhere gentleness is human and not predicated of God (e.g. Gal 5:23; Tit 3:2).

**T0.1.2** — *Is it ever borne by God himself or only by the creature, and what does the pattern indicate?*
It is borne by God: his "gentleness made me great" (Psa 18:35) attributes gentleness to God as the source of the petitioner's exaltation, and Christ's "meekness and gentleness" (2Cor 10:1) is the explicit pattern. It is overwhelmingly creaturely in the corpus (commanded, pursued, put on), but the divine instances establish it as a genuine point of the divine likeness, not an exclusively human trait.

**T0.2.1** — *Does the evidence state a purpose, role, or effect the characteristic serves in the person?*
Yes. It leads to exaltation and inheritance — "the meek … shall inherit the earth" (Mat 5:5); "humility comes before honor" (Pro 18:12; 22:4 "the reward for humility and fear of the Lord is riches and honor and life"); and it is salvific in its reception — "receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save your souls" (Jam 1:21).

**T0.2.2** — *Does its role belong to created design, the fallen condition, both, or not determinable?*
The evidence presents it as a **restored and commanded good** — fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:23), a garment to "put on" (Col 3:12) — operating within the fallen condition (restoring the "caught in transgression", Gal 6:1; enduring "the unjust", 1Pe 2:18). Whether it belongs to unfallen created design is not directly stated; **labelled inferential** that a virtue this consistently enjoined and Spirit-given reflects created design marred and restored.

**T0.2.3** — *Is there orientation toward a future fullness?*
Yes — "Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth" (Mat 5:5) orients present gentleness toward a future possession; "The Lord is at hand" frames present reasonableness (Php 4:5); and humility is the refuge sought against "the day of the anger of the Lord" (Zep 2:3).

**T0.3.1** — *What aspect of the divine likeness does it instantiate?*
The likeness of **strength exercised in restraint** — God's gentleness that makes the lowly great (Psa 18:35) and Christ's meekness that corrects without crushing (2Cor 10:1; 2Ti 2:25), reproduced in the person who restores gently (Gal 6:1) and corrects opponents with gentleness (2Ti 2:25).

**T0.3.2** — *Shared between God and the person, or an exclusively creaturely analogue?*
**Shared.** The same quality is named of God (Psa 18:35), of Christ (2Cor 10:1), and commanded of the person (Col 3:12; 1Ti 6:11) — a genuine common term, not merely an analogue.

**T0.3.3** — *What does its presence or absence indicate about the divine image in the person?*
The evidence pairs gentleness with the renewed self ("God's chosen ones, holy and beloved", Col 3:12; the Spirit's fruit, Gal 5:23) and its opposite with the unrenewed ("a man's heart is haughty", Pro 18:12; "violent … quarrelsome", 1Ti 3:3). **Labelled inferential**: presence tracks the renewed image, absence the haughty/contentious condition; the corpus shows the correlation without stating an image-doctrine.

**T0.4.1** — *Is the characteristic used typologically; if so which reality and in which direction?*
Yes, **christologically, divine-instance-establishing-the-pattern**. "Behold, your king is coming to you, humble, and mounted on a donkey" (Mat 21:5) reads the meek king typologically; "the meekness and gentleness of Christ" (2Cor 10:1) makes Christ's gentleness the pattern the human instance points back to.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — *What is it called, and what does the name signal?*
Gentleness (also meekness, humility, forbearance). The name signals a **settled mildness of temper — strength held in restraint** — governing how a person bears wrong and meets others, as the verses consistently show (gentle "to everyone", Php 4:5; "not violent but gentle", 1Ti 3:3).

**T1.1.2** — *What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms show definitionally?*
The lexical glosses: praus / prautēs / praotēs — "gentle, meek, the positive moral quality of dealing with people in a kind way"; epieikēs / epieikeia — "gentle, considerate … with an implication of tolerance and graciousness"; anavah / anvah — "humility, gentleness, meekness"; anexikakos — "enduring, or patient under evils and injuries, not resentful". Together they show a quality defined by **its manner toward others and its bearing under injury**.

**T1.1.3** — *What directional / relational / constitutional implication does the name carry?*
Relational and **other-directed**: it is gentleness *toward* people ("toward all people", Tit 3:2; "to everyone", Php 4:5; "correcting his opponents", 2Ti 2:25). Constitutionally it is a borne quality, not an act (see T1.2.1).

**T1.2.1** — *What kind of inner-being phenomenon is it?*
A **disposition / borne quality**. Across the corpus the term is overwhelmingly a *status* or *quality* (29 of 30 occurrences) and only once an *action* — it is something one *is* and *has* ("a gentle and quiet spirit", 1Pe 3:4; "put on … meekness", Col 3:12), not chiefly something one does.

**T1.2.2** — *Is it simple or compound; if compound, which elements?*
**Compound at its edges.** Its core is restraint-toward-others, but the vocabulary folds in humility (anavah, "humility comes before honor", Pro 18:12), forbearance/non-resentment (anexikakos, "patiently enduring evil", 2Ti 2:24), and reasonableness/tolerance (epieikēs, "Let your reasonableness be known", Php 4:5). It combines mildness, lowliness, and forbearance.

**T1.3.1** — *What is its structural opposite?*
**Harshness / violence / contentiousness.** The verses name the opposite directly: "not violent but gentle, not quarrelsome" (1Ti 3:3); "not be quarrelsome but kind … patiently enduring evil" (2Ti 2:24); "avoid quarreling … be gentle" (Tit 3:2). Pride is its opposite on the humility axis — "a man's heart is haughty" set against "humility" (Pro 18:12).

**T1.3.2** — *What does it exclude or resist at its edge?*
It excludes quarrelsomeness, violence, evil-speaking, and self-assertion: "speak evil of no one, … avoid quarreling" (Tit 3:2); and it resists resentment under injury — "patiently enduring evil" (2Ti 2:24).

**T1.3.3** — *Where does it end and another thing begin — what is it not?*
It is **not weakness or timidity**: the gentle Paul is "bold toward you when I am away" (2Cor 10:1); the meek king is still the conquering king who rides "in your majesty … victoriously" (Psa 45:4). It ends where mildness becomes mere passivity; gentleness retains strength and acts (restores, corrects, defends).

**T1.4.1** — *In what mode(s) does it operate, including grammatical/stem form and manner?*
It operates as a **manner qualifying an action** — most often adverbially, "with/in gentleness": coming "in a spirit of gentleness" (1Cor 4:21), restoring "in a spirit of gentleness" (Gal 6:1), correcting "with gentleness" (2Ti 2:25), making a defense "with gentleness and respect" (1Pe 3:15). Grammatically the band is dominated by nouns and adjectives (a borne quality) rather than finite verbs.

**T1.4.2** — *Does the mode vary by context, direction, or level?*
Yes. Toward inferiors/the fallen it is restorative (Gal 6:1); toward opponents it is corrective (2Ti 2:25); toward all it is courteous (Tit 3:2; Php 4:5); under unjust masters it is submissive endurance (1Pe 2:18); before God it is humble seeking (Zep 2:3).

**T1.4.3** — *Does it operate through a communicative or speech-based mode?*
Yes, markedly. Gentleness governs **how one speaks**: the defense "with gentleness and respect" (1Pe 3:15), correcting opponents "with gentleness" (2Ti 2:25), "speak evil of no one … show perfect courtesy" (Tit 3:2), and the entreaty made "by the meekness … of Christ" (2Cor 10:1) and "in your kindness to hear us" (Act 24:4).

**T1.5.1** — *What first / most immediate inner-being response follows the characteristic?*
The corpus tags few immediate responses; where present they are paradoxical — **boldness** ("humble when face to face … but bold toward you when I am away", 2Cor 10:1) and the **lowly royal entry** ("humble, and mounted on a donkey", Mat 21:5). Gentleness does not produce timidity but a composed boldness.

**T1.5.2** — *Is that immediate response consistent or varied?*
Sparse and varied; the evidence is thin on an immediate response, since gentleness is presented as a standing quality rather than a triggering event. **Largely silent** beyond the two instances above.

**T1.6.1** — *What does it produce in the inner being over time?*
**Honour, inheritance, and preservation.** "Humility comes before honor" (Pro 18:12; Pro 22:4 honour, riches, and life); "the meek … shall inherit the earth" (Mat 5:5); and received with meekness, the word "is able to save your souls" (Jam 1:21).

**T1.6.3** — *How does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?*
The immediate register (composed boldness, lowly bearing) is a present manner; the sustained effect is a standing reversal — the lowly are made great (Psa 18:35), the meek inherit (Mat 5:5), the humble are honoured (Pro 18:12). The manner is momentary; the outcome is exaltation over time.

**T1.7.1** — *Under what inner conditions does it take hold or operate rightly?*
Under **wisdom and the fear of the Lord**: "By his good conduct let him show his works in the meekness of wisdom" (Jam 3:13); "the wisdom from above is … gentle" (Jam 3:17); "the fear of the Lord is instruction in wisdom, and humility comes before honor" (Pro 15:33); and in the "spiritual" person (Gal 6:1).

**T1.7.2** — *Under what conditions is it blocked, distorted, or resisted (incl. another spirit)?*
By **haughtiness, quarrelsomeness, and violence**: "a man's heart is haughty" (Pro 18:12) blocks humility; the "drunkard … violent … quarrelsome" (1Ti 3:3) is its negation. **No adversarial or angelic spirit** is shown distorting it in the corpus — that branch is **Silent**.

**T1.7.3** — *What is the inner state of one in whom it is present but does not take hold?*
**Silent.** The corpus contrasts the gentle with the haughty/violent person but does not depict a person in whom gentleness is offered yet fails to take hold.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — *At which constitutional level(s) is it located, and how is each engaged?*
**Heart:** "the hidden person of the heart … a gentle and quiet spirit" (1Pe 3:4); "a man's heart is haughty, but humility …" (Pro 18:12). **Soul:** received with meekness, the word is "able to save your souls" (Jam 1:21). **Inward person / the renewed self:** "Put on … meekness" (Col 3:12). It also engages the **mind** functionally (see T3.2).

**T2.1.2** — *What does the pattern of engaged and absent levels indicate?*
Gentleness seats in the **heart and soul** (the hidden, governing centre — 1Pe 3:4 "the hidden person of the heart") and is mind-governed (T3.2), but it **never engages perception, memory, or creativity** (T3.1/3.3/3.5 silent). The seat is deep (heart/soul) yet the faculty engaged is cognitive rather than affective — a settled, governed quality rather than a felt surge.

**T2.10.1** — *Does it move across levels, or onto the person from an external source?*
Yes — **from the Spirit onto the person**: gentleness is "fruit of the Spirit" (Gal 5:23), and God's gentleness is what "made me great" (Psa 18:35), implying a movement from God/Spirit to the inner person. No spirit→soul→body sequence is otherwise detailed.

**T2.7.1** — *Where a body link exists, in which direction does it run?*
The one clear body link runs **soul→body (expressive)**: the inner "gentle and quiet spirit" is set against outward "adorning" (1Pe 3:4), the hidden disposition expressed in conduct rather than ornament. Otherwise the body link is faint; largely **functional/expressive**, not emphatic.

**T2.9.1** — *Where does the evidence say it originates?*
**Bestowed by God / the Spirit:** "fruit of the Spirit … gentleness" (Gal 5:23); "your gentleness made me great" (Psa 18:35). Also **cultivated by the person** as something pursued and put on: "Pursue … gentleness" (1Ti 6:11); "Put on … meekness" (Col 3:12). Origin is thus both divine gift and human cultivation.

**T2.9.2** — *Is the origin single or multiple, and does it change with context?*
**Multiple** — divine bestowal (Gal 5:23) and human pursuit/donning (1Ti 6:11; Col 3:12) co-exist; the gift is received and then actively put on. No generational or adversarial origin appears.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1 / T3.1.2 / T3.1.3 · Perception** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages the inner senses or discernment as perception; gentleness is not presented as a mode of perceiving. The consistent non-engagement marks it as a temper, not a sense.

**T3.2.1 · Cognition (engage?)** — Yes. Gentleness is the only faculty-tagged dimension in this band, and it is tagged **cognition**, never affect. It is bound to wisdom and understanding: "Who is wise and understanding among you? … the meekness of wisdom" (Jam 3:13); "the wisdom from above is … gentle, open to reason" (Jam 3:17); "reasonableness" (epieikēs, Php 4:5).

**T3.2.2 · Cognition (effect)** — It renders thought **reasonable and teachable**: "open to reason" (Jam 3:17), "able to teach" (2Ti 2:24), and reasonableness "known to everyone" (Php 4:5). Gentleness disposes the mind to receive ("receive with meekness the implanted word", Jam 1:21).

**T3.2.3 · Cognition (pattern)** — The exclusive cognition-tagging across the band indicates gentleness is a **mind-governed restraint** — a deliberate, considered temper rather than a spontaneous feeling. This is its defining faculty signature.

**T3.3.1 / T3.3.2 / T3.3.3 · Memory** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages the holding or retrieving of inner-being reality across time; gentleness is not presented as a memorial faculty.

**T3.4.1 · Affect (engage?)** — **No / Silent.** Strikingly, **no** gentleness occurrence is tagged to the affective faculty. Unlike love, compassion, kindness, and comfort (all affect-led), gentleness does not present as a felt emotion.

**T3.4.2 · Affect (effect)** — **Silent** (no affective engagement to report).

**T3.4.3 · Affect (pattern)** — The complete absence of affect tagging across all 30 occurrences is itself a finding: gentleness is constitutionally a **governed quality of the will-and-mind**, distinguished from the cluster's affections precisely by not being a feeling.

**T3.5.1 / T3.5.2 / T3.5.3 · Creativity** — **Silent.** No engagement with imagination or the capacity to originate.

**T3.6.1 · Volition (engage?)** — Yes. Gentleness is **commanded and pursued**, which engages the will: "Pursue … gentleness" (1Ti 6:11); "Put on … meekness" (Col 3:12); "restore him in a spirit of gentleness" (Gal 6:1, a chosen manner).

**T3.6.2 · Volition (effect)** — It directs the will toward **restraint and restoration** rather than retaliation — choosing to correct gently (2Ti 2:25), to bear with others (Eph 4:2 "bearing with one another"), and to endure evil without resentment (2Ti 2:24).

**T3.6.3 · Volition (pattern)** — The heavy *commanded* load (the imperatives to pursue, put on, receive) shows gentleness as a **virtue of the will that is cultivated**, not a temperament merely possessed.

**T3.7.1 · Agency (engage?)** — Partially. Gentleness qualifies acts of agency — restoring (Gal 6:1), correcting (2Ti 2:25), making a defense (1Pe 3:15), showing courtesy (Tit 3:2) — it governs *how* the person acts.

**T3.7.2 · Agency (effect)** — It moderates the exercise of power: the one with authority comes "with love in a spirit of gentleness" rather than "with a rod" (1Cor 4:21); the strong "restore" the fallen gently (Gal 6:1).

**T3.7.3 · Agency (pattern)** — Where agency appears, gentleness is its **governor**, not its engine — it shapes the manner of action across contexts (correction, defense, rule).

**T3.8.1 · Moral evaluation (engage?)** — Yes. Gentleness is assessed against the standard of right: it is "the positive moral quality of dealing with people" (lexical), wholly righteous or commanded across the corpus, and "against such things there is no law" (Gal 5:23).

**T3.8.2 · Moral evaluation (effect)** — It is itself a measure of righteous conduct, paired with righteousness and goodness: "the cause of truth and meekness and righteousness" (Psa 45:4); "full of mercy and good fruits, impartial and sincere" (Jam 3:17).

**T3.8.3 · Moral evaluation (pattern)** — The uniformly positive valence (zero sinful/forbidden across 30 occurrences) marks gentleness as a **stable moral good** in the corpus, never disordered — unlike love, whose valence flips on its object.

**T3.9.1 / T3.9.2 / T3.9.3 · Conscience** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages the conscience (the acute witness of sin, guilt, conviction); gentleness touches moral evaluation (T3.8) but not the convicting conscience in this corpus.

**T3.10.1 · Conscientiousness (engage?)** — Yes — gentleness integrates moral awareness, will, and action: the "spiritual" person *chooses* to *restore* the fallen *gently* (Gal 6:1), and the servant is "kind to everyone, able to teach, patiently enduring evil" (2Ti 2:24) — awareness, volition, and conduct together.

**T3.10.2 · Conscientiousness (effect)** — It yields conduct that is consistent and dependable in manner — "show perfect courtesy toward all people" (Tit 3:2), "let your reasonableness be known to everyone" (Php 4:5).

**T3.10.3 · Conscientiousness (pattern)** — Gentleness reads as a **character-level integration** (a settled disposition expressed in steady conduct), consistent with its status/quality typing.

**T3.11.1 · Relational capacity (engage?)** — Strongly. Gentleness is constitutionally other-directed equipment for connection: restoring (Gal 6:1), bearing with one another (Eph 4:2), correcting opponents toward repentance (2Ti 2:25), courtesy "toward all people" (Tit 3:2).

**T3.11.2 · Relational capacity (effect)** — It **preserves and repairs** relationship — turning correction into a path to "repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth" (2Ti 2:25) and bearing "with one another in love" (Eph 4:2).

**T3.11.3 · Relational capacity (pattern)** — The dominant other-experiencer pattern (gentleness shown toward others, rarely toward self) indicates it is **fundamentally relational** — its whole operation is in the meeting of persons.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1 · God→human (engage?)** — Yes: "your gentleness made me great" (2Sa 22:36; Psa 18:35) — God's gentleness operates toward the person, exalting the lowly.

**T4.1.2 · God→human (basis)** — Presented as **responsive grace within covenant relationship** — bound to "the shield of your salvation" (Psa 18:35) and to the fear of the Lord that precedes honour (Pro 15:33). Not stated as strictly conditional or unconditional; **partly inferential**.

**T4.1.3 · God→human (what it shows of God)** — That God's disposition toward the person is **condescending and exalting** — his greatness expressed as gentleness that lifts the small (Psa 18:35), the pattern Christ embodies (2Cor 10:1).

**T4.2.1 · human→God (engage?)** — Yes: "Seek the Lord, all you humble of the land … seek humility" (Zep 2:3); the humility joined to "the fear of the Lord" (Pro 15:33; 22:4) is the person's posture toward God.

**T4.2.2 · human→God (inner posture required)** — **Lowliness and submission** — seeking the Lord and doing "his just commands" in humility (Zep 2:3); a non-haughty heart (Pro 18:12).

**T4.2.3 · human→God (what it shows)** — That right relationship with God is entered **from below** — the humble are those hidden "on the day of the anger of the Lord" (Zep 2:3) and rewarded with "riches and honor and life" (Pro 22:4).

**T4.3.1 · human→human giving (engage?)** — Yes, dominantly: gentleness is extended person-to-person — restoring (Gal 6:1), correcting (2Ti 2:25), serving even harsh masters (1Pe 2:18), courtesy to all (Tit 3:2), defense to enquirers (1Pe 3:15).

**T4.3.2 · giver's inner conditions** — The giver is **"spiritual"** (Gal 6:1), **wise** ("the meekness of wisdom", Jam 3:13), and **self-watchful** ("Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted", Gal 6:1).

**T4.3.3 · what the giver must have received/become** — They must have **received the word with meekness** (Jam 1:21) and **put on** the renewed self ("as God's chosen ones … put on … meekness", Col 3:12) and the Spirit's fruit (Gal 5:23) before extending it.

**T4.4.1 · human→human receiving (engage?)** — Yes: "receive with meekness the implanted word" (Jam 1:21) shows gentleness as the posture of *uptake*; and being corrected "with gentleness" aims at the recipient's "repentance" (2Ti 2:25).

**T4.4.2 · conditions accompanying / blocking uptake** — Uptake is accompanied by meekness and aims at repentance (Jam 1:21; 2Ti 2:25); it is blocked by the haughty heart (Pro 18:12) and the quarrelsome temper (1Ti 3:3). 

**T4.4.3 · state of one who meets it but does not take it up** — **Silent** (not depicted; cf. T1.7.3).

**T4.5.1 · within bonds vs across distance** — It operates **across distance and difference**, not only within bonds: "gentle … toward all people" (Tit 3:2), reasonableness "known to everyone" (Php 4:5), and even toward "the unjust" master (1Pe 2:18) and "opponents" (2Ti 2:25).

**T4.5.2 · within covenant only, or crossing covenantal boundaries** — It **crosses boundaries** — extended "to everyone" / "all people" (Php 4:5; Tit 3:2) and to opponents (2Ti 2:25), not confined to the covenant community.

**T4.5.3 · relational scope (who included / excluded)** — **Universal in scope:** everyone, all people, the unjust, opponents. No class is excluded; gentleness is owed even where it is not reciprocated (1Pe 2:18).

**T4.6.1 / T4.6.2 / T4.6.3 · Spiritual beings interface** — **Silent.** No occurrence shows gentleness operating toward, attacked by, or mediated through angelic or adversarial beings.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1 · Transformation (engage / which)** — Yes — it changes both **condition and orientation**: received with meekness, the word "is able to save your souls" (Jam 1:21, condition); restoring the transgressor "in a spirit of gentleness" reorients the fallen toward repentance (Gal 6:1; 2Ti 2:25, orientation).

**T5.1.2 · reversible or irreversible** — **Silent** on reversibility as such; the salvific reception (Jam 1:21) implies a decisive change but the corpus does not address reversibility directly.

**T5.2.1 · Sequence of inner states** — A sequence appears in correction: gentle correction → "God may perhaps grant them repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth" (2Ti 2:25) — gentleness (before) producing repentance and knowledge (after). Otherwise sequences are sparse.

**T5.3.1 · Mechanism of change** — By **gradual formation and reception** rather than sudden event: "Pursue … gentleness" (1Ti 6:11), "Put on … meekness" (Col 3:12), "receive with meekness the implanted word" (Jam 1:21) — cultivation and uptake.

**T5.3.2 · mechanism differ across contexts** — Yes: in the person it forms by pursuit/donning (1Ti 6:11; Col 3:12); toward others it works by gentle correction that may yield repentance (2Ti 2:25); under injustice it works by patient endurance (1Pe 2:18; 2Ti 2:24).

**T5.4.1 · Suffering / affliction (relation)** — Yes, as a **response to and context for** affliction: servants "subject to … masters with all respect, not only to the good and gentle but also to the unjust" (1Pe 2:18); the Lord's servant "patiently enduring evil" (anexikakos, 2Ti 2:24).

**T5.4.2 · what suffering does to the characteristic** — Suffering is the **arena that proves** gentleness — it is borne under the unjust master and under evil without resentment (1Pe 2:18; 2Ti 2:24); the evidence shows it *enduring* rather than being diminished.

**T5.5.1 · Formation and sanctification** — Yes, centrally: gentleness is **fruit of the Spirit** (Gal 5:23), a garment of "God's chosen ones" to "put on" (Col 3:12), and an object of pursuit alongside the virtues of the sanctified life (1Ti 6:11) — it participates in the long arc of character formation.

**T5.6.1 · Eschatological trajectory** — Yes: "the meek … shall inherit the earth" (Mat 5:5) anticipates a future possession; "The Lord is at hand" frames present reasonableness (Php 4:5); and humility is the present refuge against "the day of the anger of the Lord" (Zep 2:3). Present gentleness anticipates future inheritance and vindication.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1 · Co-occurrence (which / how often)** — In the verse evidence gentleness co-occurs most with **humility** (tapeinos / tapeinofrosunē — Col 3:12; Eph 4:2), **patience** (makrothumia — Col 3:12; Eph 4:2; with anexikakos 2Ti 2:24), **wisdom** (sofia — Jam 3:13, 3:17), **righteousness** (Psa 45:4; 1Ti 6:11), **the fear of the Lord** (Pro 15:33; 22:4), **love** (Eph 4:2; 1Cor 4:21), and **comfort/encouragement** (parakaleō — entreaty, 2Cor 10:1; Act 24:4), with **glory/honour** and **goodness** also adjacent.

**T6.1.2 · what co-occurrence shows** — Gentleness sits in a **cluster of restraint-and-relationship virtues** (humility, patience, forbearance) and is **generated by wisdom and the fear of the Lord** — its company places it among the dispositions that govern conduct toward others under God.

**T6.2.1 · Sequential relationship** — Yes: **the fear of the Lord / wisdom precedes gentleness-humility** — "the fear of the Lord is instruction in wisdom, and humility comes before honor" (Pro 15:33); "the wisdom from above is … gentle" (Jam 3:17). Gentleness in turn precedes honour and inheritance (Pro 18:12; Mat 5:5).

**T6.2.2 · what the sequence shows** — The relationship reads as **causal/constitutive in two directions**: wisdom and the fear of the Lord *produce* gentleness; gentleness *leads to* honour and inheritance — a developmental chain.

**T6.3.1 · does it produce another?** — Yes — gentleness in correction *produces* repentance and knowledge in another: "correcting his opponents with gentleness. God may perhaps grant them repentance leading to a knowledge of the truth" (2Ti 2:25).

**T6.3.2 · is it produced by another?** — Yes — by **wisdom** ("the meekness of wisdom", Jam 3:13; wisdom "is … gentle", Jam 3:17) and by the **Spirit** (Gal 5:23).

**T6.3.3 · constituent of / constituted by another?** — Gentleness is a **constituent of wisdom-from-above** ("the wisdom from above is first pure, then peaceable, gentle …", Jam 3:17) and a **constituent of the Spirit's single fruit** (Gal 5:22–23); humility (anavah) is in turn a near-constituent of gentleness in the Hebrew terms.

**T6.4.1 · shared vocabulary** — The Hebrew **anavah / anvah** is glossed both "humility" and "gentleness", sharing vocabulary with the **humility** characteristic (M09 territory). The Greek gentleness terms are distinct to this band.

**T6.4.2 · shared root architecture** — Within the band three roots generate the field: praus/prautēs/praotēs (one root), epieikēs/epieikeia (one root), anavah/anvah (one root). The anavah root reaches **across into humility**; the praus root does not.

**T6.4.3 · what the sharing shows** — The anavah overlap shows gentleness and **humility** are conceptually fused in Hebrew (one term, two glosses) and only differentiated in the Greek, where praotēs (manner toward others) separates from tapeinofrosunē (lowliness of self). **Labelled inferential** as a development across the Testaments.

**T6.5.1 · nearest neighbour and the distinction** — The nearest neighbour is **humility**. The distinction: humility is the person's posture **before God and concerning self** (lowliness — "humility comes before honor", Pro 18:12), whereas gentleness is the manner **toward others** (restraint in dealing — "gentle … toward all people", Tit 3:2). Patience/forbearance is the second neighbour (endurance over time).

**T6.5.2 · precise boundary at apparent overlap** — Where they overlap (anavah carries both), the boundary is **direction**: the same disposition seen *toward God / self* is humility; seen *toward others* is gentleness. Eph 4:2 and Col 3:12 list them together precisely because they are adjacent but distinct.

**T6.5.3 · distinction of degree, kind, direction, or level?** — Primarily **of direction** (toward self/God vs toward others), secondarily of **kind** vis-à-vis patience (manner vs endurance).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1 · primary terms and root meanings** — Greek **praus / prautēs / praotēs** (the moral quality of dealing kindly with people), **epieikēs / epieikeia** (gentle, considerate, tolerant, gracious), **anexikakos** (patient under evil, not resentful); Hebrew **anavah / anvah** (humility, gentleness, meekness). The roots converge on **mildness-with-strength and lowliness**.

**T7.1.2 · grammatical range** — Nouns (prautēs, epieikeia, anavah), adjectives (praus, epieikēs, anexikakos), with adverbial use ("in/with gentleness"). The dominance of **nouns and adjectives** over finite verbs shows the characteristic operates as a **borne quality**, not chiefly an act.

**T7.1.3 · semantic range** — From "meek / humble" (Mat 5:5; Mat 21:5) through "gentle / considerate" (1Ti 3:3; Tit 3:2) to "reasonable / tolerant" (Php 4:5; Jam 3:17 "open to reason") and "courteous" (Tit 3:2) and "patient under evil / not resentful" (2Ti 2:24). A breadth running lowliness → mildness → reasonableness → forbearance.

**T7.1.4 · terms distinguishing aspects (disposition vs act, etc.)** — Yes: the band is almost entirely **disposition/quality** terms (praus, prautēs, epieikēs as borne traits) with the lone forbearance term anexikakos naming endurance *under* injury. It does **not** carry a distinct "received vs given" pair.

**T7.1.5 · term for the structural opposite / absence** — Not a single antonym term, but the opposite is named lexically in context — "violent … quarrelsome" (1Ti 3:3), "quarrelsome" (2Ti 2:24) — and on the humility axis, "haughty" (Pro 18:12). **No dedicated antonym lexeme** in the band.

**T7.1.6 · person-type term** — Yes — **praus** functions substantivally for the person who has it: "Blessed are the **meek**" (Mat 5:5); "the **humble** of the land" (anavah, Zep 2:3).

**T7.1.7 · supplication / seeking term** — **None** specific to gentleness; though humility is the posture of seeking the Lord ("seek … humility", Zep 2:3), there is no term for *seeking gentleness from another*. **Silent.**

**T7.1.8 · OT↔NT continuity / development** — The OT term **anavah** centres on **humility (lowliness before God)** in wisdom and psalm contexts (Pro 15:33; Zep 2:3; Psa 45:4), while the NT **praotēs / epieikēs** centre on **gentleness toward people** in epistolary ethics (Gal 5:23; Tit 3:2). One field; the centre of gravity moves from posture-before-God to manner-toward-others. **Labelled inferential.**

**T7.1.9 · NT-coined term** — **None evidenced** in the band; no coinage is signalled. **Silent.**

**T7.1.10 · full vocabulary arc** — The complete arc spans **lowliness (anavah/humility) → meekness (praus) → gentleness/consideration (epieikēs) → reasonableness/tolerance (Php 4:5; Jam 3:17) → forbearance under injury (anexikakos)** — a single disposition of strength-in-restraint viewed from self-before-God to manner-toward-others to bearing-under-wrong.

**T7.2.1 · function of the primary term in its primary verse** — In **Mat 5:5** ("Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth") praus is the **subject-class** of a beatitude — the meek are the ones pronounced blessed and promised inheritance; the term carries the whole weight of the blessing.

**T7.2.2 · literary forms and what they require** — The evidence spans **beatitude** (Mat 5:5), **wisdom proverb** (Pro 15:33; 18:12; 22:4), **psalm** (Psa 18:35; 45:4), **prophetic summons** (Zep 2:3), and **epistolary paraenesis** (Gal 5:23; Col 3:12; Tit 3:2; 1Pe). Responsible reading requires honouring each form — proverb as general pattern, beatitude as eschatological pronouncement, paraenesis as community command.

**T7.2.3 · logical structure of key arguments** — E.g. Jam 3:13–17: premise — true wisdom shows itself in conduct; conclusion — therefore "show his works in the meekness of wisdom", because "the wisdom from above is … gentle". And Pro 18:12: premise — pride precedes destruction; conclusion — humility precedes honour. Gentleness is consistently the **middle term between a right inner condition and an outcome of honour/inheritance**.

**T7.2.4 · contextual setting** — **Covenantal-ethical and communal** — virtue lists for the renewed community (Col 3:12; Eph 4:2; Gal 5:23), conduct toward outsiders (Tit 3:2; 1Pe 3:15), and **eschatological** framing (Mat 5:5; Zep 2:3). The setting shows gentleness as ordered community life under God and toward the world.

**T7.2.5 · primary anchor verse** — **2Cor 10:1** — "by the meekness and gentleness of Christ" — most fully and directly expresses the characteristic's essential character, grounding it in Christ and holding together its paradox (humble yet bold). **Mat 5:5** is the eschatological anchor.

**T7.2.6 · what the anchor shows that no other verse shows** — 2Cor 10:1 alone **grounds gentleness in Christ as its source-pattern** and displays its paradox in one breath — "the meekness and gentleness of Christ — I who am humble when face to face … but bold toward you when I am away" — showing gentleness is not weakness but governed strength.

**T7.3.1 · most useful human-science framework** — **Behavioural science / positive psychology and trait theory**: gentleness maps onto the Big Five **agreeableness** dimension (low aggressiveness combined with warmth and consideration) and is treated as a settled, self-regulated character trait.

**T7.3.2 · where the framework illuminates** — Trait psychology illuminates the band's data that gentleness is a **stable disposition rather than a situational response** — "a settled, self-regulated quality … not merely the absence of aggression but the presence of controlled, other-directed care" — matching the corpus's status/quality typing and its mind-governed (cognition) signature.

**T7.3.3 · where evidence and framework diverge** — The science treats gentleness as a temperament/trait shaped by heredity and parenting; the corpus presents it as **fruit of the Spirit** (Gal 5:23) and **patterned on Christ** (2Cor 10:1), commanded and "put on" — a grounding (divine gift, christological pattern, eschatological reward in Mat 5:5) the trait account cannot generate.

**T7.3.4 · aspect surfaced by the framework calling for further verse work** — The agreeableness/self-regulation literature frames gentleness as **emotion regulation**; yet the corpus tags gentleness to **cognition, never affect**, raising the question whether biblical gentleness is better read as a *governed-mind* virtue than an *emotion-regulation* one. **Flagged for further verse investigation**, especially a faculty read on the Hebrew anavah terms (untagged here).
