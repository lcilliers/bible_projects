## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1** — Yes, in every relation the catalogue names. **God as agent:** "if God so loved us, we also ought to love one another" (1Jn 4:11). **God as giver/source:** "love is from God, and whoever loves has been born of God" (1Jn 4:7). **God as object:** "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart" (Mat 22:37). **God as possessor:** "the love of God and the fellowship of the Holy Spirit be with you all" (2Cor 13:14).

**T0.1.2** — Borne by God himself, pre-eminently and originally: "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son" (1Jn 4:10). The pattern — God loves first, the creature loves in response and derivation — establishes love as the deepest point of the divine likeness: the human capacity to love is a created participation in what God himself is ("God is love", the corpus's theological centre).

**T0.2.1** — Yes. Love is the sum and aim of the commandments — "You shall love the Lord your God … and your neighbor as yourself" (Luk 10:27) — and the criterion of true life: "If anyone has no love for the Lord, let him be accursed" (1Cor 16:22). Without it all else is void: "but have not love, I am a noisy gong" (1Cor 13:1).

**T0.2.2** — Both. As created design it is the orientation toward God and neighbour the person is *made* for (Great Commandment); within the fallen condition it appears disordered (love of the world, 1Jn 2:15; love of self/gain, 2Ti 4:10; 2Pe 2:15). The same capacity serves design when rightly aimed and sin when misaimed.

**T0.2.3** — Yes — love is what abides into the fullness: "So now faith, hope, and love abide, these three; but the greatest of these is love" (1Cor 13:13), and "Love never ends" (1Cor 13:8). It orients the person toward an enduring future state.

**T0.3.1** — The likeness of **self-giving toward another** — God who "so loved us … and sent his Son" (1Jn 4:10) reproduced in the person who lays down life "for his friends" (Joh 15:13) and loves "one another" (1Jn 4:11).

**T0.3.2** — **Shared.** The same term names God's love and the person's: "he loved us" / "we also ought to love" (1Jn 4:11) — a genuine common reality, the creature's love derived from and modelled on God's.

**T0.3.3** — Presence indicates the renewed image and knowledge of God — "whoever loves has been born of God and knows God" (1Jn 4:7); absence indicates its lack — "Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love" (the corpus's logic; cf. 1Jn 4:7–8). Love is presented as the diagnostic of the image's condition.

**T0.4.1** — Yes — **christologically and covenantally, divine-instance-establishing-the-pattern**: God's love in sending the Son (1Jn 4:10) is the archetype the human love points back to and imitates ("as I have loved you"; cf. Joh 15:13). Direction: the divine instance grounds the human.

## T1 — Definition

**T1.1.1** — Love. The name signals the **core bonding affection** — the inward movement that attaches the self to another and commits it toward the good (or possession) of what is loved; the cluster's headline and moral pivot.

**T1.1.2** — Greek **agapaō / agapē** name volitional, covenantal love ("God so loved", 1Jn 4:11); **fileō / philia** affectionate love ("love one another with brotherly affection"); the phil-compounds name kinship love (filadelfia, filostorgos). Hebrew **ahav / ahavah** name love broadly, from covenant ("Abraham your friend", 2Ch 20:7) to disordered desire (1Ki 11:2). Definitionally the field runs from chosen commitment to felt affection to embodied attachment.

**T1.1.3** — The name carries a **directional** implication: love is always *of* something — it must have an object, and that object determines its worth (T1.3.2). Relationally it is other-binding; constitutionally it engages the whole inner person (T2.1).

**T1.2.1** — Both an **act and a disposition/condition**: the corpus tags love almost evenly as *action* (149 — "love one another", 1Jn 4:11) and *status/quality* (151 — "Love is patient and kind", 1Cor 13:4). It is a thing done and a thing borne.

**T1.2.2** — **Compound.** Love combines affection (the felt warmth, "brotherly affection", 2Pe 1:7), volition/commitment ("love your enemies"; the commanded register, 70 occurrences), valuation/moral-evaluation (the choosing of an object), and embodied expression (kiss, embrace). 1Cor 13:4–7 unfolds it into patience, kindness, humility, endurance — a compound of constituent qualities.

**T1.3.1** — Its structural opposite is **hatred**. The corpus pairs them directly: love co-occurs with sin.ah ("hating") as its contrary — "Amnon hated her with very great hatred … greater than the love" (2Sa 13:15); "whoever hates his life in this world will keep it" set against "whoever loves his life loses it" (Joh 12:25).

**T1.3.2** — At its edge love **excludes and resists rival attachment** — it cannot be given to God and the world at once: "Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him" (1Jn 2:15); "you cannot serve God and money" (the same logic, 2Ti 4:10 "in love with this present world").

**T1.3.3** — Love is **not mere feeling and not self-seeking**: "Love … does not envy or boast; it is not arrogant … it does not insist on its own way" (1Cor 13:4–5). It ends where affection turns acquisitive (the disordered loves of gain, glory, self) — there it has become its own counterfeit.

**T1.4.1** — It operates as **both verb and state**: as enacted love ("love one another earnestly from a pure heart", 1Pe 1:22) and as predicated quality ("Love is patient", 1Cor 13:4). Its manner is self-giving ("gave himself"; "lay down his life", Joh 15:13).

**T1.4.2** — Yes — mode varies by **object and direction**: toward God it is whole-person devotion (Mat 22:37); toward neighbour, active good-doing (Luk 10:27); toward the brethren, affectionate loyalty (1Pe 1:22); toward the wrong object, disordered craving (1Jn 2:15). It also varies by constitutional depth (heart/soul/mind together, Mat 22:37).

**T1.4.3** — Yes — love operates in a **commanded and addressed** mode pervasively: "A new commandment … that you love one another"; "You shall love" (Mat 22:37; Luk 10:27); and it is expressed in speech-acts of greeting and blessing ("Greet one another with a holy kiss", 1Cor 16:20).

**T1.5.1** — The immediate response love shows is **action toward the beloved** — sending, giving, laying down life ("he loved us and sent his Son", 1Jn 4:10; "lay down his life for his friends", Joh 15:13) — and bodily expression (embrace, kiss: "they embraced Paul and kissed him", Act 20:37).

**T1.5.2** — Consistent in direction (toward the object) but **varied in expression** — from sacrificial self-giving to affectionate greeting to, in the disordered cases, grasping after the object (best seats, gain, world).

**T1.6.1** — Over time love **establishes knowledge of God and belonging** — "whoever loves has been born of God and knows God" (1Jn 4:7) — and binds persons together: "knit together in love" (Col 2:2). It produces the enduring inner reality that "abides" (1Cor 13:13).

**T1.6.3** — The immediate response is an act of self-giving; the sustained effect is a **settled bond and knowledge** — love that "abides" and "never ends" (1Cor 13:8, 13), a formed orientation rather than a single act.

**T1.7.1** — Love takes hold rightly from **a pure heart, good conscience, and sincere faith** — "the aim of our charge is love that issues from a pure heart and a good conscience and a sincere faith" (1Ti 1:5) — and as response to being first loved (1Jn 4:11; 4:19 "we love because he first loved us").

**T1.7.2** — It is blocked or distorted by **rival attachment and hypocrisy** — love of the world drives out love of the Father (1Jn 2:15); love must be "genuine"/unhypocritical (anupokritos co-occurs, Rom 12:9 "Let love be genuine"). **No adversarial spirit** is shown distorting it directly — that branch is **Silent**.

**T1.7.3** — The person in whom love is present but does not take hold is the one who professes it falsely or partially — whose love is "in word" only, or misdirected; the corpus shows misdirected love (toward world/self) rather than a developed portrait of failed uptake. **Largely Silent.**

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

**T2.1.1** — **Heart** (24 — "love … from a pure heart", 1Pe 1:22; "rooted and grounded in love", Eph 3:17), **soul** (15 — "loved him as his own soul", 1Sa 18:3), **mind** (3), and uniquely **all three together**: "love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind" (Mat 22:37; Mar 12:30; Luk 10:27). Also **conscience** ("a good conscience", 1Ti 1:5) and **flesh** (Gal 5:13).

**T2.1.2** — The pattern shows love is the affection that **engages the whole inner being** — no other M05 characteristic is shown engaging heart, soul, and mind at once. Its seat is the deepest and broadest in the cluster; the body link (embrace, kiss) is **expressive**, not constitutive.

**T2.10.1** — Yes — love moves **onto the person from God as external source**: "love is from God" (1Jn 4:7); "we love because he first loved us" (the received→given movement, 1Jn 4:19); *origin* = received-from-outside (8 occurrences). It then moves outward to neighbour ("love one another", 1Jn 4:11).

**T2.7.1** — The body link runs **soul→body (expressive)**: the inner bond is expressed in embrace and kiss — "they embraced Paul and kissed him" (Act 20:37); "Greet one another with a holy kiss" (1Cor 16:20). Direction: the inner affection manifests bodily.

**T2.9.1** — **Bestowed by God** ("love is from God", 1Jn 4:7) and **received from another** in being first loved ("he first loved us", 1Jn 4:19); also generated within in the disordered cases (love of self/world). Multiple origins.

**T2.9.2** — **Multiple and context-dependent**: divine in its right form (1Jn 4:7), self-generated in its disordered form (love of the world); the origin shifts with the object.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

**T3.1.1 · Perception (engage?)** — Yes, partially (11 occurrences tagged perception). Love is bound to perceiving and knowing the beloved: "if anyone loves God, he is known by God" (1Cor 8:3); "what no eye has seen … God has prepared for those who love him" (1Cor 2:9).

**T3.1.2 · Perception (effect)** — It directs attentive regard toward its object — love "knows" and is "known" (1Cor 8:3). 

**T3.1.3 · Perception (pattern)** — Perception is engaged but secondary to affect; love perceives in order to attach, indicating its perceptive dimension serves the bond rather than standing alone.

**T3.2.1 · Cognition (engage?)** — Yes (13 occurrences): love is commanded as a knowing devotion — "with all your mind" (Mat 22:37) — and is "genuine" by discernment (Rom 12:9). 

**T3.2.2 · Cognition (effect)** — It engages the mind in devotion and in discerning the true object; love and knowledge of God are joined ("whoever loves … knows God", 1Jn 4:7).

**T3.2.3 · Cognition (pattern)** — Cognition is a real but minor strand (13 of 300) — love is chiefly affective, with the mind enlisted, not the seat.

**T3.3.1 / T3.3.2 / T3.3.3 · Memory** — **Silent.** No occurrence engages love as a memorial faculty.

**T3.4.1 · Affect (engage?)** — Yes, dominantly — affect is the band's primary faculty (263 of 300). Love is the felt bond: warmth, longing, and attachment ("loved him as his own soul", 1Sa 18:3).

**T3.4.2 · Affect (effect)** — It produces the whole register of bonded feeling — devotion, tenderness ("a tender heart", 1Pe 3:8), and, disordered, craving (love of gain/glory).

**T3.4.3 · Affect (pattern)** — The overwhelming affect dominance marks love as **the cluster's defining affection** — the felt bond from which the other characteristics' affections can be seen as specifications.

**T3.5.1 / T3.5.2 / T3.5.3 · Creativity** — **Silent.** No engagement with imagination or origination.

**T3.6.1 · Volition (engage?)** — Yes — love is **commanded** (70 occurrences), which presupposes the will: "You shall love" (Mat 22:37); "love one another" (1Jn 4:11). Commanded love is chosen, not merely felt.

**T3.6.2 · Volition (effect)** — It directs the will toward the good of another, even sacrificially ("lay down his life for his friends", Joh 15:13) and toward the right object against rival pulls (1Jn 2:15).

**T3.6.3 · Volition (pattern)** — That love can be *commanded* shows it is not purely affective but **volitional at its core** — a directing of the self that the person is answerable for.

**T3.7.1 · Agency (engage?)** — Yes — love acts: it "is patient and kind", "bears all things … endures all things" (1Cor 13:4–7); it gives and lays down life (Joh 15:13).

**T3.7.2 · Agency (effect)** — It moves the person to self-giving action and service ("through love serve one another", Gal 5:13).

**T3.7.3 · Agency (pattern)** — The near-even action typing (149) shows love is as much **enacted** as felt — its agency is integral, not incidental.

**T3.8.1 · Moral evaluation (engage?)** — Yes (13 occurrences tagged moral_evaluation): love is the standard by which conduct is measured — "the fulfilling of the law" — and its objects are morally weighed (love of God good, love of world evil, 1Jn 2:15).

**T3.8.2 · Moral evaluation (effect)** — It is itself the criterion: without love, gifts are worthless (1Cor 13:1–3); with the wrong object, love is sin (1Jn 2:15).

**T3.8.3 · Moral evaluation (pattern)** — Uniquely in the cluster, love's moral value **flips on its object** (24 sinful + 1 forbidden) — the moral-evaluation faculty is decisively engaged because love must be rightly aimed to be good.

**T3.9.1 · Conscience (engage?)** — Yes, lightly (2 occurrences): love issues "from a pure heart and a good conscience" (1Ti 1:5).

**T3.9.2 · Conscience (effect)** — Genuine love is bound to a clear conscience; the two are named together as its source (1Ti 1:5).

**T3.9.3 · Conscience (pattern)** — Conscience is a minor but real seat — love rightly held is conscience-clean, indicating moral integrity as a condition of true love.

**T3.10.1 · Conscientiousness (engage?)** — Yes — love integrates feeling, will, and action: it is felt, commanded, and enacted in one ("love one another earnestly from a pure heart", 1Pe 1:22).

**T3.10.2 · Conscientiousness (effect)** — It yields steady other-directed conduct — patience, kindness, endurance (1Cor 13:4–7).

**T3.10.3 · Conscientiousness (pattern)** — Love reads as a **whole-person integration** — the most comprehensive of the cluster's characteristics, engaging affect, will, mind, and conscience together.

**T3.11.1 · Relational capacity (engage?)** — Yes, constitutively — love *is* the equipment for connection: "love one another" (1Jn 4:11); "knit together in love" (Col 2:2).

**T3.11.2 · Relational capacity (effect)** — It binds persons — to God (Mat 22:37), to one another (1Pe 1:22), to the brethren (filadelfia) — and is the bond's very substance.

**T3.11.3 · Relational capacity (pattern)** — The dominant other-experiencer pattern confirms love as **fundamentally relational**: its whole being is in the binding of persons.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

**T4.1.1 · God→human** — Yes, foundationally: "he loved us and sent his Son" (1Jn 4:10); "God so loved us" (1Jn 4:11). God's love moves first toward the person.

**T4.1.2 · basis** — **Unconditional and prior** — "not that we have loved God but that he loved us" (1Jn 4:10); love precedes the creature's response ("he first loved us", 1Jn 4:19). Covenantal in form (Abraham "your friend", 2Ch 20:7).

**T4.1.3 · what it shows of God** — That God's disposition is **self-giving toward the person** to the point of sending the Son — love is God's defining orientation toward humanity.

**T4.2.1 · human→God** — Yes — the first commandment: "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind" (Mat 22:37); "if anyone loves God, he is known by God" (1Cor 8:3).

**T4.2.2 · inner posture required** — **Whole-person devotion** — heart, soul, mind, strength together (Mar 12:30) — undivided from rival loves (1Jn 2:15).

**T4.2.3 · what it shows** — That the person's relationship with God is **total self-orientation toward him** — love is the right name for the creature's whole movement toward the Creator.

**T4.3.1 · human→human giving** — Yes, dominantly — "love one another" (1Jn 4:11); "lay down his life for his friends" (Joh 15:13); affectionate greeting (1Cor 16:20).

**T4.3.2 · giver's inner conditions** — A **pure heart, good conscience, sincere faith** (1Ti 1:5), and love that is "genuine"/unhypocritical (Rom 12:9; 1Pe 1:22 "sincere brotherly love").

**T4.3.3 · what the giver must have received/become** — They must first have **been loved by God** — "we love because he first loved us" (1Jn 4:19); "if God so loved us, we also ought to love one another" (1Jn 4:11). Received love is the precondition of given love.

**T4.4.1 · human→human receiving** — Yes — love is taken up in being loved and in the command's reception ("beloved by the Lord", 2Th 2:13; "brothers beloved", the addressed register).

**T4.4.2 · conditions accompanying / blocking uptake** — Uptake accompanies a pure heart and faith (1Ti 1:5); it is blocked by rival love (1Jn 2:15) and hypocrisy.

**T4.4.3 · state of one who meets it but does not take it up** — **Silent / partial:** the corpus shows the one who loves the world rather than the Father (1Jn 2:15) but does not develop a portrait of love offered and refused.

**T4.5.1 · within bonds vs across distance** — Both, and the command stretches it **across distance**: beyond the brethren to the neighbour (Luk 10:27) and, in the cluster's wider teaching, the enemy — love that does not depend on existing bond.

**T4.5.2 · within covenant only, or crossing** — It **crosses covenantal boundaries** — neighbour-love (Luk 10:27) reaches beyond the covenant community; love is owed where no reciprocity is expected.

**T4.5.3 · relational scope** — Widest in the cluster: God, neighbour, brethren, the stranger — explicitly inclusive. The only ones "excluded" are the wrong *objects* (world, money, self), not persons.

**T4.6.1 / T4.6.2 / T4.6.3 · Spiritual beings** — **Silent.** No occurrence shows love operating toward, attacked by, or mediated through angelic or adversarial beings (the disordered-love material concerns objects, not spirits).

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

**T5.1.1 · Transformation** — Yes — love changes both **condition** ("born of God", 1Jn 4:7) and **orientation** (reorienting the self from world to God, 1Jn 2:15; from self to neighbour, Gal 5:13).

**T5.1.2 · reversible / irreversible** — **Silent** on reversibility directly, though "love never ends" (1Cor 13:8) implies permanence of the formed reality.

**T5.2.1 · Sequence of inner states** — Yes — the received→given sequence: **being first loved → loving in return → loving one another** ("we love because he first loved us", 1Jn 4:19; "if God so loved us, we also ought to love one another", 1Jn 4:11).

**T5.3.1 · Mechanism of change** — By **encounter with prior love** ("he first loved us", 1Jn 4:19) and by **command/cultivation** ("love one another earnestly", 1Pe 1:22) — both sudden grounding and sustained practice.

**T5.3.2 · mechanism differ across contexts** — Yes: divine love grounds by gift (1Jn 4:10); human love grows by command and practice (1Pe 1:22); disordered love forms by misdirected craving (1Jn 2:15).

**T5.4.1 · Suffering / affliction** — Yes — love operates as **costly self-giving under affliction**: "lay down his life for his friends" (Joh 15:13); Paul writes "out of much affliction and anguish of heart … to let you know the abundant love that I have for you" (2Cor 2:4).

**T5.4.2 · what suffering does to it** — Suffering **proves and deepens** love — it is shown bearing affliction for the beloved's sake (2Cor 2:4; the cross as love's measure, 1Jn 4:10).

**T5.5.1 · Formation and sanctification** — Yes, centrally — love is the goal of the sanctified life ("the aim of our charge is love", 1Ti 1:5), grows "earnestly" (1Pe 1:22), and is the crown of the virtue-chain (2Pe 1:7 "brotherly affection with love"). It shapes the person over the whole arc.

**T5.6.1 · Eschatological trajectory** — Yes — love is what **endures into the consummation**: "Love never ends" (1Cor 13:8); "now faith, hope, and love abide … but the greatest of these is love" (1Cor 13:13). Present love anticipates the abiding state.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

**T6.1.1 · Co-occurrence** — Love co-occurs most with **the heart and soul** (co-seated, kardia / psuchē / nephesh), **holiness** (hagios), **truth** (alētheia), **faith** (pistis), **hope** (elpis), **grace** (charis), **peace** (eirēnē), **patience** (makrothumia), **goodness** (agathos), and **genuineness/unhypocrisy** (anupokritos) — and with its antonym **hate** (sin.ah).

**T6.1.2 · what co-occurrence shows** — Love sits at the **centre of the inner-being landscape**, bound to the constitutional seats (heart/soul) and to the theological virtues (faith, hope), and defined against hatred. Its company is the widest of any cluster characteristic.

**T6.2.1 · Sequential relationship** — Yes — love **follows being loved** ("we love because he first loved us", 1Jn 4:19) and **heads the virtue-chain** ("supplement … brotherly affection with love", 2Pe 1:7). It is consistently the culminating term.

**T6.2.2 · what the sequence shows** — The relationship is **causal and developmental**: divine love causes human love; love completes the chain of virtues ("the greatest of these", 1Cor 13:13).

**T6.3.1 · does it produce another?** — Yes — love produces the cluster's other characteristics: "Love is patient and kind" (1Cor 13:4) names kindness (C) and patience as love's own operations; it issues in service (Gal 5:13).

**T6.3.2 · is it produced by another?** — Produced by **faith** ("faith working through love") and grounded in **God's prior love** (1Jn 4:19); the corpus presents love as both root and fruit.

**T6.3.3 · constituent of / constituted by another?** — Love is **constituted by** patience, kindness, humility, endurance (1Cor 13:4–7 unfolds it into these) and is itself a **constituent of** the Spirit's fruit (Gal 5:22, heading the list). It is the comprehensive characteristic of which others are facets.

**T6.4.1 · shared vocabulary** — Love shares vocabulary across the cluster: **fileō/philia** with friendship (F); **filadelfia/storgos** with kinship; and its operations (patient, **kind** — chrēstos) reach into kindness (C) and gentleness (E).

**T6.4.2 · shared root architecture** — The **phil-** root generates terms across love (fileō), friendship (filos, filia), kinship (filadelfia), and benevolence (filanthrōpia) — a single root spanning several characteristics.

**T6.4.3 · what the sharing shows** — The phil-root sharing shows love is the **generative centre** of the affectional field — friendship, kinship, and benevolence are species of love's bonding.

**T6.5.1 · nearest neighbour and distinction** — The nearest neighbour is **friendship (F)** (the phil-bond). The distinction: love is the **affection itself**, given to any object (God, neighbour, even wrongly the world); friendship is the **standing peer-bond** within which mutual love lives. Love is the movement; friendship is the relationship.

**T6.5.2 · precise boundary at overlap** — Where they overlap (Joh 15:13, love laid down "for his friends"), the boundary is: friendship names *who* (the bonded peers); love names *what is given* (the self-giving). 

**T6.5.3 · distinction of degree, kind, direction, or level?** — One of **kind**: love is an affection/act; friendship is a relational status. Against kindness (C) the distinction is **direction-of-aim** (love fastens on an object; kindness is borne loyalty).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

**T7.1.1 · primary terms and root meanings** — Greek **agapaō/agapē** (volitional, esteeming love), **fileō/philia** (affectionate love, phil-root), the phil-compounds (kinship); Hebrew **ahav/ahavah** (love, broad). Roots converge on **attachment/bonding** — chosen and felt.

**T7.1.2 · grammatical range** — Verb (agapaō, fileō, ahav), noun (agapē, philia, ahavah), adjective/participle (beloved — agapētos; lovely — prosfilēs). The full range (act, state, quality) shows love operates **as deed, condition, and attribute** alike.

**T7.1.3 · semantic range** — From covenant devotion (Mat 22:37) through sacrificial self-giving (Joh 15:13), affectionate kinship (2Pe 1:7), and embodied tenderness (embrace, kiss) to disordered craving (1Jn 2:15). One of the widest semantic ranges in the programme.

**T7.1.4 · terms distinguishing aspects** — Yes — **agapē** (volitional/given) is partly distinguished from **philia** (affectionate/felt), and the **beloved** terms (agapētos, yedidut) name the *received/object* aspect against the active verbs. The disposition-vs-act distinction is carried by the noun/verb pair.

**T7.1.5 · term for the opposite/absence** — Not within band A, but the opposite **hatred** (sin.ah / miseō) is its lexical contrary and co-occurs (2Sa 13:15; Joh 12:25). 

**T7.1.6 · person-type term** — Yes — **the beloved** (agapētoi — "Beloved, let us love one another", 1Jn 4:7) and **friend/lover** (ahev — "Abraham your friend", 2Ch 20:7) name the one characterised by love.

**T7.1.7 · supplication / seeking term** — **None** specific to love (one does not "seek love from" another by a dedicated term). **Silent.**

**T7.1.8 · OT↔NT continuity / development** — Hebrew **ahavah** (broad love, covenantal and affectional) develops into the NT's differentiated **agapē** (elevated to name God's self-giving love and the Christian command) alongside **philia**. Continuity of the bonding field; development toward agapē as the theologically loaded term.

**T7.1.9 · NT-coined / newly elevated term** — **agapē**: though attested earlier, the NT decisively **elevates** it to name God's love and the supreme command (1Cor 13; 1Jn 4) — a near-coinage in significance. The elevation shows love re-centred on God's self-giving as the pattern.

**T7.1.10 · full vocabulary arc** — The arc spans **covenant devotion → self-giving agapē → affectionate philia → kinship phil-compounds → embodied tokens (kiss, embrace) → disordered craving** — the complete range of bonding from the divine pattern to its human counterfeits.

**T7.2.1 · function of the primary term in its primary verse** — In **Mat 22:37** agapaō is the **governing imperative** ("You shall love the Lord your God …") on which "all the Law and the Prophets depend" — the term carries the whole weight of the commandment.

**T7.2.2 · literary forms** — **Law/gospel command** (Mat 22:37), **epistolary exposition** (1Cor 13; 1Jn 4), **narrative** (1Sa 18 Jonathan–David; the kiss texts), **wisdom** (Pro), **prophecy** (Hos). Responsible reading honours each — the hymn of 1Cor 13 as praise-definition, the command as covenant obligation.

**T7.2.3 · logical structure** — 1Jn 4:10–11: premise — God loved us first and sent his Son; conclusion — "we also ought to love one another." 1Cor 13:1–3: premise — without love all gifts are nothing; conclusion — love is supreme. Love is consistently the **ground and the goal** of the argument.

**T7.2.4 · contextual setting** — **Covenantal** (Great Commandment), **liturgical/communal** (holy kiss, brotherly love), **eschatological** (love abides, 1Cor 13:13). The settings show love as the substance of covenant life oriented to the end.

**T7.2.5 · primary anchor verse** — **1Jn 4:10** — "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" — most fully expresses love's essential character: divine, prior, self-giving, defining. **1Cor 13** is the descriptive anchor; **Mat 22:37** the imperative anchor.

**T7.2.6 · what the anchor shows that no other shows** — 1Jn 4:10 alone **defines love by God's initiating self-gift** rather than by human emotion — locating love's origin and pattern in God's sending of the Son, against which all human love is response.

**T7.3.1 · most useful human-science framework** — **Developmental psychology / attachment theory** (Bowlby, Ainsworth; Sternberg's triangular theory; Hatfield's passionate vs companionate love).

**T7.3.2 · where the framework illuminates** — Attachment science illuminates love as a **bonding system** with an embodied basis (oxytocin released through touch, eye-contact) — matching the cluster's embodied vocabulary (embrace, kiss); and Hatfield's passionate/companionate distinction maps onto the **agapē/philia** registers. Secure attachment as ground for outward love illuminates why the corpus grounds neighbour-love in being first loved by God (1Jn 4:19).

**T7.3.3 · where evidence and framework diverge** — Science explains love by evolved bonding, reciprocity, and in-group preference; the corpus **commands love to enemies, strangers, and those from whom no reciprocal benefit is expected** (neighbour-love, Luk 10:27), and grounds it in God's self-giving (1Jn 4:10) — a scope and ground the science framework cannot generate.

**T7.3.4 · aspect surfaced by the framework** — The passionate/companionate and agapē/philia distinction invites a **closer verse test of whether the corpus's agapaō and fileō are functionally distinguished** — the band's data shows overlap rather than a clean split (both affect-tagged), **flagging further verse investigation** of the two registers.
