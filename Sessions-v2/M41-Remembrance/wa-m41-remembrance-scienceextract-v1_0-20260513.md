## Science sections

### Section 1 — Memory, recall, and remembering

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology — memory research

**Glosses covered:**
remembrance (anamnēsis), remembrance (mneia), remembrance (mnēmē), remembrance (hupomnēsis), to remember (mimnēskō), to remember (mnaomai), to remember (mnēmoneuō), to remember (za.khar), to remind (anamimnēskō), to remind (hupomimnēskō), to forget (epilanthanō)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Tulving's episodic and semantic memory distinction (*Elements of Episodic Memory*, 1983) is foundational — episodic memory is autobiographical, contextually located, and personally experienced; semantic memory is factual, context-free, and impersonal. Remembrance (anamnēsis, za.khar) in the biblical material typically involves episodic memory — remembering specific events in history with their personal and covenantal significance. Schacter's *Seven Sins of Memory* (2001) describes the failure modes of human memory — transience, absent-mindedness, blocking, misattribution, suggestibility, bias, and persistence — which provide a framework for understanding the biblical concern with faithful remembering. Bartlett's constructive memory research (*Remembering*, 1932) established that memory is not reproductive (a recording played back) but reconstructive — each remembering is an active construction influenced by current knowledge, expectations, and emotional state.

**Emergence — what causes remembering to arise:**
Remembering arises through retrieval cues — contextual, emotional, or semantic triggers that activate stored memory traces. Emotional events are remembered more vividly and durably (flashbulb memory — Brown and Kulik) — which illuminates why the biblical material associates remembering with significant events of deliverance, covenant, and judgment. Reminding (hupomimnēskō — bringing to remembrance) is an external trigger that serves the retrieval function — a relational act of activating another person's memory.

**Reduction and absence — what causes remembering to diminish:**
Forgetting is the default trajectory of most memories (Ebbinghaus forgetting curve) — without rehearsal, retrieval, or emotional significance, memories fade. Active forgetting (epilanthanō — to forget) can be either passive (the natural decay of an unrehearsed memory) or active (the motivated suppression of a painful memory — though motivated forgetting is less reliable than people assume). Traumatic memories are paradoxically both more vivid (intrusive re-experiencing) and more distorted (narrative memory of trauma is often incomplete and fragmented).

**Location — where memory is seated:**
Memory is distributed across multiple brain systems — episodic memory involves the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex; emotional memory involves the amygdala; procedural memory involves the basal ganglia and cerebellum. Memory is not localised to a single 'memory centre' but is a property of distributed neural networks. Experientially, memory is felt as a return — the past brought into the present, with varying degrees of vividness, emotional charge, and narrative coherence.

**External influences:**
*Social:* Collective memory (Halbwachs) — the shared memory of a community — shapes what individuals remember and how they remember it. The Passover, the Lord's Supper, and communal liturgical acts are social memory practices that sustain collective remembrance across generations. *Developmental:* Childhood amnesia means that explicit episodic memories begin around age 3–4; prior implicit memories still shape emotional and relational patterns. *Circumstances:* Stress both enhances and impairs memory — acute stress enhances memory for central details; chronic stress impairs hippocampal function and can disrupt memory consolidation.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Cognitive memory research is one of psychology's most developed fields and directly relevant to this section.

### Section 2 — Hearing, listening, and auditory attention

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology — attention and perception

**Glosses covered:**
hearing (akoē), hearing (mish.ma), attentive (qash.shav), attentive (qash.shuv), attentiveness (qe.shev), to hear: hear (sha.ma), to hear: judge (sha.ma), to hear: obey (sha.ma), to hear: proclaim (sha.ma), to hear: understand (sha.ma), to hear: welcome (sha.ma), to listen (a.zan), to listen (qa.shav)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Attentional psychology (Kahneman — attention as a limited resource; Treisman — selective attention) addresses the mechanisms by which certain stimuli are selected for processing while others are filtered out. Active listening research (Rogers — client-centred therapy; Weger — active listening in communication) addresses listening as a relational skill — attending to, understanding, and responding to another person's message. The sha.ma root is analytically significant: it spans hearing, understanding, obeying, and proclaiming — a unified Hebrew concept that resists the modern fragmentation of perception, cognition, and action. Attentiveness (qash.shav, qe.shev) describes the quality of engaged, directed attention that is prerequisite for both hearing and obedience.

**Emergence — what causes attentive listening to arise:**
Attentive listening arises from the combination of: perceived importance of the message; relational commitment to the speaker; and adequate attentional resources. Motivation significantly shapes what is heard — people hear what they are disposed to hear (confirmation bias in perception), and the dispositional orientation of the heart toward or away from the speaker shapes the quality of listening.

**Reduction and absence — what causes hearing to diminish:**
Hearing diminishes through distraction (competing attentional demands), through cognitive load (insufficient processing capacity), and through motivated inattention (not wanting to hear). The obedience dimension of sha.ma — to hear is to obey — means that not doing is a form of not truly hearing. The deaf-ear vocabulary in Scripture ('ears to hear but hearing not') names this failure of auditory attention as a moral and spiritual failure, not merely a perceptual one.

**Location — where hearing is seated:**
Neurologically, auditory processing is located in the temporal cortex; attentional selection is mediated by frontal and parietal networks. Experientially, hearing is simultaneously sensory (the physical reception of sound), cognitive (the interpretation of meaning), and dispositional (the orientation of the whole person toward what is heard). The sha.ma vocabulary places this faculty at the centre of the person's response to God's address.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Attentive listening is cultivated through practice and modelling — communities that value and practise deep listening produce more attentive persons. *Social:* The quality of the speaking context (whether the speaker is trusted, whether the message is safe to hear) shapes attentive listening capacity.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Attention research and active listening psychology are well-developed. The obedience dimension of sha.ma extends beyond auditory psychology into M30's territory.

### Section 3 — Seeking, searching, and information acquisition

**Primary field:** Cognitive psychology / motivational psychology

**Glosses covered:**
to seek (zēteō), to seek (ba.qash), to seek (da.rash), to seek (sha.char)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Loewenstein's information-gap theory of curiosity (*Psychological Bulletin*, 1994) proposes that curiosity arises from the awareness of a gap between what one knows and what one wants to know. Information-seeking behaviour research (Wilson) addresses the motivated pursuit of information across contexts. Religious seeking (Batson — quest orientation) addresses seeking as a form of open, questioning engagement with ultimate questions — seeking without a predetermined destination. The four seeking verbs in this section span from intense searching (da.rash — to seek earnestly, to enquire) to the dawn-seeking (sha.char — to seek at dawn, to seek early and diligently).

**Emergence — what causes seeking to arise:**
Seeking arises from the awareness of something not yet possessed or known — a gap that generates motivational tension toward its resolution. Religious seeking arises from unresolved ultimate questions. The intensity of seeking (da.rash — to seek earnestly) reflects the degree to which the sought object is valued and the gap is felt as significant.

**Location — where seeking is seated:**
Primarily motivational and cognitive — seeking is an oriented state of the whole person moving toward a not-yet-attained object. It has an emotional dimension (the restlessness of the one who seeks; the anticipation of finding) and a behavioural dimension (directed searching activity).

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Curiosity and information-seeking research provide coverage; the theological seeking of God (da.rash as seeking God's face) extends beyond what information-seeking research addresses.

### Section 4 — News, report, and social communication of information

**Primary field:** Social psychology — communication research

**Glosses covered:**
news (fēmē), report (hash.ma.ut), report (she.ma), report (sho.ma), tidings (she.mu.ah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Allport and Postman's rumour research (*The Psychology of Rumor*, 1947) addresses how information is transmitted and transformed in social communication. Information diffusion research (social network analysis) addresses how news spreads through communities. The terms in this section describe reported information — heard and transmitted news, tidings, and reports — which occupy the social communication dimension of the sha.ma root.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. Communication research provides limited coverage. These terms primarily describe the social vehicle of information rather than inner-being states.

## Outside-science inventory

- **to remember (za.khar) in its covenantal register** — God remembering his covenant as a divine act of faithfulness (God 'remembering' Noah, Abraham, Rachel) — is a theological category. Science addresses human memory; divine remembering is an anthropomorphic theological category that does not imply divine forgetfulness but divine covenantal faithfulness made active.
- **Remembrance as liturgical/sacramental practice** — anamnēsis in the Eucharist as the church's participation in Christ's redemptive act through memorial — is a theological category that exceeds the psychology of social memory practices.
- **Memory as a category of divine faithfulness** — God's faithfulness as the sustained 'remembering' of his covenant across generations — is a theological category.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Cognitive memory research (Tulving, Schacter) for Section 1; attention and active listening psychology for Section 2; curiosity and information-seeking for Section 3.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Constructive memory research illuminates why remembering in Scripture is always a present act — not passive reproduction of the past but an active, interpretative engagement with it. Social memory research illuminates why Israel's liturgical practices (Passover, Sabbath, the Lord's Supper) are cognitively sophisticated memory-sustaining mechanisms. The sha.ma unified concept (hearing-understanding-obeying) illuminates why the programme's framework may need a unified inner-being category rather than three separate ones.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** God's remembering is not cognitive retrieval but covenantal faithfulness made active — an entirely different category from human memory, despite the shared vocabulary. Science provides the source domain of the metaphor; the theological content exceeds it.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The sha.ma root's span (hearing through obedience) will be a key Session B analytical question — whether the cluster's hearing vocabulary primarily describes perceptual hearing, cognitive understanding, or dispositional obedience in each verse context.
