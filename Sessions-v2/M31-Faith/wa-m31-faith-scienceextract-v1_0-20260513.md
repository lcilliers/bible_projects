## Science sections

### Section 1 — Religious belief, faith, and trust

**Primary field:** Psychology of religion

**Glosses covered:**
faith (pistis), faith: faithfulness (pistis), to trust (in) (pisteuō), devout (eulabēs), religion (thrēskeia), to disbelieve (apisteō), unbelief (apistia), unbelieving (apistos), little faith (oligopistia), of little faith (oligopistos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Fowler's stages of faith (*Stages of Faith*, 1981) proposes a developmental framework moving from childhood undifferentiated faith through conventional and individualising stages to universalising faith. Pargament's psychology of religion and coping addresses faith as a resource that expands perceived coping capacity through relationship with a perceived God. Exline's research on spiritual struggle (*Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion*) addresses doubt and unbelief — the empirical finding that spiritual struggle (wrestling with God, doubt, anger at God) is common, often painful, and does not automatically resolve toward either faith or unbelief. The little faith (oligopistia — quantitatively reduced faith) vocabulary has a parallel in research on faith variability — individual differences in religious commitment and the situational fluctuation of faith.

**Emergence — what causes faith to arise:**
Psychology of religion identifies multiple pathways to religious belief: socialisation (formation in a believing community), personal religious experience (William James — the 'twice-born' conversion type), gradual religious development, and existential crisis that drives religious seeking. Granqvist's attachment theory of religion suggests that faith in God as an attachment figure arises through similar psychological processes as human attachment — it is facilitated by secure parenting and disrupted by insecure or absent parenting.

**Reduction and absence — what causes faith to diminish:**
Faith diminishes through: (1) negative religious experiences (unanswered prayer, perceived divine abandonment); (2) intellectual challenges to belief; (3) social context loss (leaving believing community); (4) gradual drift through reduced religious practice. Exline's spiritual struggle research shows that unresolved spiritual struggle is among the strongest predictors of religious departure. Little faith (oligopistia) suggests a quantitative reduction rather than complete absence — partial trust, tentative belief, insufficient to sustain in crisis.

**Location — where faith is seated:**
Pistis (faith, trust, faithfulness) is simultaneously cognitive (belief that something is true), affective (trust as a felt state of reliance), and behavioural (faithfulness as sustained orientation in action). Psychology of religion locates religious faith as a whole-person orientation rather than merely a cognitive belief — it involves the whole person's directedness toward God. This aligns with the programme's interest in faith as an inner-being characteristic that organises the whole person.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Religious formation in childhood (parental faith, community practice, religious education) is the strongest predictor of adult religious belief and practice. The 'none' category (religiously unaffiliated) is disproportionately concentrated in those without childhood religious formation. *Social:* Religious community membership is the strongest predictor of sustained adult faith — faith is a social practice, not merely a private belief. *Attachment:* Secure attachment correlates with God attachment — people with secure human attachment patterns tend toward secure attachment to God (Granqvist). *Circumstances:* Life crises can either drive people toward faith (crisis as religious seeking trigger) or away from it (perceived divine failure to protect).

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Psychology of religion provides genuine coverage for the experiential and social dimensions of faith; the theological core (pistis as the instrument of justification; faith as the gift of God) is outside science.

### Section 2 — Calling, vocation, and identity

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / developmental psychology

**Glosses covered:**
called (klētos), calling (klēsis), be permitted (exesti exon)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Dik and Duffy's vocation research (*The Counseling Psychologist*, 2009) addresses calling as a sense of being summoned to a particular work or life direction — distinguished from career (what one does for money) and occupation (what one does for a living). Research on purpose (Frankl — logotherapy; Damon — *The Path to Purpose*) addresses the developmental formation of a sense of life direction. Identity research (Erikson's identity development; Marcia's identity statuses) addresses the formation of a stable sense of who one is and what one is for.

**Emergence — what causes a sense of calling to arise:**
A sense of calling arises through the convergence of: perceived inner gifts and strengths; the experience of meaningful work that produces positive contribution; external affirmation from community; and in some accounts, a perceived transcendent summons. Dik and Duffy's research identifies transcendent summons as a defining feature of calling (as distinct from mere strong interest) — the sense of being called by something beyond oneself.

**Location — where calling is seated:**
Primarily cognitive and motivational — a sense of direction and purpose that organises the person's energies toward a meaningful end. It has an identity dimension — calling becomes part of who the person understands themselves to be.

**External influences:**
*Developmental:* Calling typically crystallises in adolescence and early adulthood through exploration, experience, and community confirmation. *Social:* Communities that identify and affirm gifts in their members facilitate the development of a sense of calling.

**Coverage assessment:** Thin. The theological register (divine calling, election) substantially exceeds the vocational psychology coverage.

## Outside-science inventory

- **faith (pistis) in its Pauline theological register** — faith as the instrument of justification, the relational trust through which the believer receives the righteousness of Christ — is a theological category. Psychology addresses the phenomenology and social dynamics of religious belief; it does not evaluate the truth of what is believed or the mechanism of how faith relates to salvation.
- **calling (klēsis) as divine election** — the theological category of God's effectual call that brings the elect to faith — is outside science entirely.
- **unbelief (apistia) as a moral/spiritual condition** — not merely cognitive doubt but the settled refusal of the covenant God — is a theological category.
- **devout (eulabēs)** in its worship-oriented register — the whole-person orientation of reverential, attentive religious devotion.

**Observation:** M31 is the cluster where the programme's analytical work is most likely to surface content that science can only partially address. The cluster's core subject — pistis as trust in the God who justifies the ungodly — is a theological reality that psychology can describe only from the outside.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Psychology of religion (faith development, religious belief and doubt) for Section 1; vocational psychology for Section 2.

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** Fowler's faith development stages illuminate why the cluster may show different faith registers — immature, tested, mature — across different verse contexts. Attachment theory of religion illuminates why faith and trust are so structurally similar in the cluster — pistis is both cognitive belief and relational trust, which are not separate in the psychology of attachment. Spiritual struggle research illuminates the unbelief vocabulary — unbelief in the biblical material is not simply the absence of belief but often the presence of active resistance or tested doubt.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The theological claim that faith is a gift (Eph 2:8) — that the capacity to believe is itself divinely produced — runs counter to the psychology of religion's account of faith as a human developmental and social achievement. The mechanism and source of faith are contested at this theological-psychological boundary.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps:** The oligopistia (little faith) terminology may surface in contexts where the distinction between present weak faith and absent faith is analytically important — a dimension the science of faith variability can partially illuminate.
