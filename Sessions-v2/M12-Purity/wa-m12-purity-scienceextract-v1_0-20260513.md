## Science sections

### Section 1 — Moral purity and disgust sensitivity

**Primary field:** Moral psychology — purity research

**Glosses covered:**
pure (hagnos), pure (adolos), pure (eilikrinēs), pure (ta.hor), pureness (te.hor), clean (katharos), cleansing (katharismos), clearness (to.har), purity (hagneia), purity (hagnotēs), purity (to.har), genuine (anupokritos), to clean (katharizō), to purify (hagnizō), unblemished (ta.mim), soundness (me.tom), be pure (ta.her), purification (hagnismos), purifying (to.ho.rah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Haidt and Rozin's purity research establishes disgust as the emotion that underlies purity intuitions. The disgust system began as a pathogen-avoidance mechanism (Faulkner and colleagues) and was recruited for social and moral regulation — making certain acts and states feel contaminating even when no biological pathogen is involved. Purity intuitions are cross-culturally present but vary enormously in their specific content. Zhong and Liljenquist's Lady Macbeth effect research demonstrates that moral impurity produces literal physical discomfort that can be partially relieved by physical cleansing — showing that the metaphorical connection between moral and physical purity has a genuine psychological basis. The purity foundation in moral foundations theory (Haidt) generates moral intuitions about sanctity violation, bodily purity, and degradation.

**Emergence — what causes purity concerns to arise:**
Purity concerns arise from: contact with (or imagined contact with) disgust elicitors; the perception that moral or bodily boundaries associated with purity have been violated; and — at the moral-psychological level — the intuition that certain acts degrade or pollute the person regardless of their interpersonal harm consequences. The cluster's purity vocabulary covers both the physical (cleanliness, absence of blemish) and the moral (genuineness, integrity, absence of deception) dimensions of purity — a dual register that the disgust literature only partially captures.

**Reduction and absence — what causes purity concerns to diminish:**
Purity concerns diminish through: purification rituals (both physical and symbolic); habituation to previously disgusting stimuli; and — for moral purity — the processes of forgiveness and restoration that remove the sense of defilement. Disgust habituation through exposure is the primary clinical mechanism for disgust-based phobias and OCD. Absence of purity sensitivity is associated with psychopathy (no disgust response in moral contexts) and with certain forms of sociopathy.

**Location — where purity is seated:**
Viscerally somatic in its disgust substrate (gut-level); extended into cognitive and social domains through moral framing. Purity is experienced as both a bodily state (clean or unclean) and a moral-relational state (fit or unfit for certain relationships and spaces). The physical and moral dimensions are psychologically linked — moral impurity is felt bodily, and physical cleansing partially addresses moral impurity (Zhong and Liljenquist).

**External influences:**
*Cultural:* Purity norms are among the most culturally variable of all moral intuitions. What counts as polluting, what purification rituals are available, and who is defined as impure are all culturally determined on a universally present disgust substrate. *Religious formation:* Religious traditions that maintain active purity codes (dietary laws, sexual purity norms, ritual purification requirements) cultivate heightened purity sensitivity in their adherents. *Developmental:* Disgust sensitivity develops in childhood through socialisation. Scrupulosity (a clinically significant form of purity anxiety) develops in religiously sensitive individuals. *Hereditary:* Disgust sensitivity has heritable components.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Moral purity and disgust research are well-developed. The science account addresses the experiential and functional dimensions of purity; the theological account addresses the ontological dimension (what purity means before God).

### Section 2 — Integrity, sincerity, and authenticity

**Primary field:** Positive psychology / virtue psychology

**Glosses covered:**
integrity (tom), sound (haplous), sincerity (eilikrineia), genuine (anupokritos), innocent (akakos), innocent (anaitios), innocent (na.qi), sinless (anamartētos), uncondemned (akatakritos)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Authenticity research (Rogers — the fully functioning person; Kernis and Goldman — multi-component authenticity model) addresses the alignment between inner states and outward expression. Authenticity involves: self-awareness (knowing one's own values and motivations), unbiased processing (acknowledging negative as well as positive self-information), authentic action (behaving consistently with one's values), and relational authenticity (appropriate self-disclosure in relationships). Integrity psychology (Palanski and Yammarino) addresses consistency between words, values, and actions — particularly relevant to the cluster's single-mindedness vocabulary (haplous — simple, undivided, the eye that is clear). Innocence as a psychological state involves freedom from hidden agenda, guile, or malicious intent.

**Emergence — what causes integrity and authenticity to arise:**
Integrity and authenticity develop through: the internalisation of genuine values (rather than merely performing compliance with external standards); secure enough relationship to allow genuine self-expression rather than managed impression; and the character formation that aligns inner motivation with outward action over time. Authenticity is actively constructed rather than passively discovered — it requires ongoing attention to the alignment between inner and outer.

**Reduction and absence — what causes integrity to diminish:**
Integrity is undermined by: the pressures of impression management (performing a self that differs from one's actual values); conformity pressures that require suppression of genuine convictions; self-deception (the inability to acknowledge one's own actual motivations); and moral disengagement that allows incongruence between espoused and enacted values without registering as hypocrisy. The cluster's innocence vocabulary (absence of guilt or guile) is diminished by exposure to contexts that socialise deceit as adaptive.

**Location — where integrity is seated:**
Characterological — integrity is a whole-person orientation toward consistency between inner conviction and outer expression. It is not primarily an emotion or a mood but a stable disposition of character that is expressed across situations. It has cognitive (self-knowledge), volitional (commitment to consistent action), and relational (transparent engagement) dimensions.

**External influences:**
*Community of accountability:* Integrity is supported by communities that value transparency and create safe spaces for genuine self-expression. *Parenting:* Modelling of integrity in significant adults is among the most powerful formative influences. *Cultural:* Cultures that reward impression management and self-promotion inhibit authenticity; cultures that value integrity and punish hypocrisy support it.

**Coverage assessment:** Good. Authenticity and integrity psychology are well-developed fields.

### Section 3 — Self-regulation and moral self-concept

**Primary field:** Clinical psychology / developmental psychology

**Glosses covered:**
sacred (hosios), incorruptibility (aftharsia), to stain (miainō), uncleanness (tum.ah), unholy (anosios), perfection (mikh.lah), perfection (tikh.lah)

**Landmark findings and key researchers:**
Moral identity research (Aquino and Reed, *The Self-Importance of Moral Identity*, 2002) examines the degree to which moral traits (honest, caring, compassionate) are central to a person's self-concept. High moral identity centrality predicts prosocial behaviour, resistance to moral disengagement, and stronger ethical decision-making. Self-regulation research (Baumeister) addresses the capacity to maintain moral standards against competing impulses — the self-control dimension of avoiding stain and maintaining purity of conduct. Perfection as a psychological construct has been studied in perfectionism research (Hewitt and Flett) — distinguishing adaptive perfectionism (high standards with self-compassion) from maladaptive perfectionism (high standards with harsh self-criticism).

**Emergence — what causes strong moral self-concept to develop:**
High moral identity centrality develops through: significant moral experiences that crystallise moral commitment; community membership in groups with strong moral identity; role models who embody moral integrity; and deliberate moral formation through practice and reflection. Sacred (hosios) names the quality of one who is devoted entirely to what is right before God — a moral self-concept grounded in the divine claim on the person.

**Reduction and absence — what diminishes moral self-concept:**
Moral self-concept is weakened by: moral failure without adequate repair (shame rather than guilt responses that erode self-concept rather than motivating repair); communities that normalise moral compromise; and progressive moral disengagement that changes what the person considers part of their moral identity.

**Location:** Cognitive and characterological — moral self-concept is a cognitive schema about who one is in moral terms, with motivational consequences for behaviour.

**External influences:** Community membership, moral mentorship, and deliberate character formation are the primary environmental shapers. Early moral education significantly shapes which moral traits become self-defining.

**Coverage assessment:** Moderate. Moral identity research is growing; the perfection vocabulary and the sacred/holy vocabulary have significant outside-science content.

## Outside-science inventory

- **holy: saint (qa.dosh)** — holiness as divine attribute (God is holy — utterly set apart, morally perfect, overwhelming in his otherness) and as the covenantal status conferred on Israel (you shall be holy, for I am holy) is a theological category. Science addresses purity intuitions and the moral self-concept of those who consider themselves devout, but not holiness as ontological separation and divine character.
- **to consecrate (qa.dash) in all senses** — consecration is the act of setting apart for the exclusive use of God. This is a theological-liturgical act that creates a changed relationship between an object, person, or space and the divine. Science has no category for consecration as an ontological change in status before God.
- **sacred (hosios) in its cultic register** — where hosios names the person or thing designated for sacred use, it is a cultic category that science's moral self-concept research does not capture.
- **incorruptibility (aftharsia) as eschatological category** — the incorruptibility of the resurrection body is an eschatological theological claim. Science addresses biological processes of decay; it does not address eschatological transformation.

**Observation:** M12 has the largest outside-science content of all clusters so far — holiness, consecration, and eschatological incorruptibility are substantially theological. The science account addresses purity intuitions, authenticity, integrity, and moral self-concept; the theological account addresses what purity means ontologically in relation to the holy God and what consecration does to the person or thing set apart. The gap between the science account and the theological account is wider here than in most clusters.

## T7.3 prompt reference notes

**T7.3.1 — Primary field:** Moral psychology (purity and disgust — Section 1); positive and virtue psychology (integrity and authenticity — Section 2); clinical and developmental psychology (moral self-concept — Section 3).

**T7.3.2 — Where science illuminates:** The Lady Macbeth effect (physical cleansing partially addresses moral impurity) illuminates the embodied character of the cluster's purification vocabulary — physical purification rituals have genuine psychological effects. The authenticity research illuminates the cluster's integrity vocabulary (haplous — single-eyed, undivided) as describing a state of inner-outer alignment. Moral identity research illuminates how the cluster's holiness vocabulary operates motivationally — high moral identity centrality predicts the kind of consistent moral conduct that the cluster's purity and integrity terms describe.

**T7.3.3 — Divergence:** The deepest divergence is ontological. Science addresses purity as a psychological state and social category; the biblical material addresses holiness as an ontological condition — being genuinely set apart, genuinely transformed, genuinely different in status before God. No amount of psychological purification research reaches the biblical claim that God is holy and that his people are called to share in that holiness through his transforming work. This is the widest science-theology gap in the programme to date.

**T7.3.4 — Gaps surfaced by science:** The perfectionism research (adaptive vs maladaptive) may surface a distinction in the cluster's perfection vocabulary that the biblical pass should test — does the cluster include a perfectionism that is gracious and motivated by love, or only a perfectionistic standard that crushes? The scrupulosity research (clinically significant purity anxiety in religious contexts) may surface a dimension of the cluster's purity vocabulary that is worth noting as a divergence from healthy purity-consciousness.
