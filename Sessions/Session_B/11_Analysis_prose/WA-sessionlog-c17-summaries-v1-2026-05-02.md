## 3. Production Method

### Format and toolchain
- All outputs produced as .docx using the docx npm library (v9.6.1)
- Page size: US Letter (12240 × 15840 DXA), 1-inch margins
- Font: Arial throughout; heading hierarchy (H1 title, H2 section heads in navy)
- Footer on each document: filename, programme reference, source file
- Validated with the programme's office validation script before release
- All files dual-written to /home/claude/ then copied to /mnt/user-data/outputs/

### Document structure per summary
Each summary follows a consistent pattern:
- Title (word name) and subtitle (one-line characterisation drawn from the data)
- Horizontal rule
- Opening paragraph establishing the word's core nature and vocabulary
- Six to eight thematic sections covering: what the characteristic is, how it operates, key findings, structural opposites and boundaries, immediate and sustained effects, and a closing integrative observation
- Footer with file reference

### Governing discipline
- Every finding grounded strictly in the source prose extract for that registry
- No external commentary, theological tradition, or interpretive framework imported
- Research gaps present in the source material noted where directly relevant to the reader's understanding, but not foregrounded
- Technical language (Hebrew/Greek terms, programme vocabulary, registry numbers) translated into accessible prose throughout
- Where the source material distinguished between research findings and open questions deferred to Session D, the summaries reported only confirmed findings

## 5. Structural Observations Across the Eleven Summaries

These observations are drawn from the pattern across the full set. They are not conclusions — they are observations that may be useful for future production sessions or for the researcher's own synthesis work.

**Convergence of vocabulary roots.** Several of the C17 words share root families or etymological connections that the summaries surfaced individually. Compassion and mercy share the racham/womb root. Grace and the supplication vocabulary share the chen/chanan root on both the giving and receiving sides. Kindness (hesed) and love (chesed/ahavah) share substantial vocabulary. These connections are evidenced in individual source files but their collective pattern across the cluster is visible when the summaries are read together.

**The direction of flow.** Across all ten C17 words, the research consistently found that the characteristic originates with God before it operates in the human person. Grace is received before it is expressed. Mercy is modelled on divine mercy before it is extended to others. Love is received (1 John 4:19) before it generates love given. Forgiveness flows from divine character. Covenant is initiated by God. Fellowship at its deepest is participation in divine nature. This direction — from God outward through the person into relationship and community — is a consistent structural finding across the cluster.

**The counterfeiting problem.** Several summaries surfaced the theme that C17 characteristics can be counterfeited. Peace is the clearest case — the research identified false shalom as a distinct phenomenon with its own vocabulary. Fellowship can be misdirected (Hosea 4:17). Kindness has its counterfeit in chrēstologia (flattery that mimics kindness while serving self). Forgiveness can be verbal without being heart-level. Grace can be received without being registered as gift (acharistos). The consistent finding: these characteristics have both authentic and counterfeit forms, and the difference lies not in outward behaviour but in inner orientation and ground.

**Immediate responses are often counter-intuitive.** The research found that the immediate inner response to several of these characteristics is not what a reader might expect. Grace produces fear, mourning, and prostration before it produces joy. Mercy at its deepest produces shame and silence rather than relief. Contrition, which looks like a disqualifying condition, is specifically what draws divine nearness. Forgiveness produces love and reverence rather than simply relief. These counter-intuitive patterns are among the most distinctive findings across the cluster and may be worth drawing together for a future synthesis piece.

**The formation arc.** Each word in the cluster has a formation dimension — a trajectory from initial encounter to settled character. The chasid (kindness), the companion of the God-fearing (fellowship), the contrite person revived (contrition), the agapētos who loves because they were first loved (love), the person constituted as God's people by received mercy (mercy) — each names a person-type produced by sustained engagement with the characteristic. The C17 cluster as a whole describes not only what these qualities are but what kind of person they produce over time.
