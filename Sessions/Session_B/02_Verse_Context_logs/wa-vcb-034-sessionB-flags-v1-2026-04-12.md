## SBF-034-001 | G3077 (lupē) — grief | mti_id=5191 | Registry 214

**Source:** Classification of 2Cor 7:10 during VCB-034 processing.

**Observation:**
2Cor 7:10 introduces a qualitative distinction *within* grief as an inner-being state: *"For godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret, whereas worldly grief produces death."*

Grief (*lupē*) is not a uniform inner characteristic. Its orientation — whether directed toward God or not — determines its character and its outcome. Godly grief moves toward repentance and life; worldly grief moves toward death. This is not a difference in intensity or expression but a constitutive difference in the inner quality of the grief itself.

**For Session B Analysis:**
- What distinguishes godly from worldly grief at the inner-being level? Is the difference in the object (God vs self/world), the posture (surrender vs resistance), or the trajectory (outward/upward vs inward/downward)?
- Does this distinction map onto other grief vocabulary in this registry or across registries?
- What does this verse contribute to understanding the valence or directionality of inner emotional states?

## SBF-034-002 | H2470H (cha.lah) — be weak: ill | mti_id=1938 | Registry 214

**Source:** Classification of Neh 2:2 during VCB-034 processing.

**Observation:**
Neh 2:2: *"And the king said to me, 'Why is your face sad, seeing you are not sick? This is nothing but sadness of the heart.' Then I was very much afraid."*

The verse draws an explicit biblical distinction between physical illness (*chalah*) and *sadness of the heart* (יָגוֹן לֵבָב, *yagon levav*). The king uses the absence of physical illness as a diagnostic contrast: what Nehemiah has is not bodily sickness — it is an inner condition. The heart carries sorrow that the body makes visible.

**For Session B Analysis:**
- This verse provides a scriptural framework for distinguishing somatic states from inner-being states using the heart as locus.
- How does this distinction inform the programme's understanding of the relationship between physical suffering vocabulary and inner-being suffering?
- The term *chalah* (physical illness) is used here *negatively* — to name what Nehemiah does *not* have — as a way of identifying what he *does* have (inner grief). This is a methodologically interesting case: the term defines the inner-being category by contrast.
