## Session B Flag 1 — Pharaoh hardening series: divine vs. human agency in the volitional heart

**Group:** 581-005 (Heart as centre of the will)
**Relevant verse_record_ids:** 66606, 66607, 66608, 66609, 66610, 66611, 66612, 66632, 66421, 66633, 66634, 66635, 66636, 66637, 66638, 66639, 66640, 66641, 66644, 66645, 66646

**Finding:** The Exodus hardening series shows three distinct sub-patterns in how lev functions as the volitional organ:
1. **God hardens** (divine agent acting on the heart from outside): Exo 4:21; 7:3; 9:12; 10:1,20,27; 11:10; 14:4,8,17
2. **Pharaoh hardens his own heart** (self-hardening, active resistance): Exo 8:15,32; 9:34
3. **Passive construction** (hardening occurs, agency ambiguous): Exo 7:13,14,22; 8:19; 9:7,35

**Session B significance:** lev as volitional organ is shown as simultaneously susceptible to divine action and to the person's own self-direction. God can act upon the will from without; the person can harden the will from within. The same inner faculty is addressed by both agencies. This bears directly on theological anthropology: the human will is neither autonomous nor externally overridden in a simple sense. Session B should note this as a structural finding about the nature of the volitional heart in Hebrew thought.

## Session B Flag 2 — Divine lev: God described with a heart

**Groups:** 581-001, 581-004, 581-005, 581-006
**Relevant verse_record_ids:** 66659 (Gen 6:6), 66660 (Gen 8:21), 66595 (2Sa 7:21), 66662 (Hos 11:8), 66860 (Psa 33:11), 66696 (Isa 63:4), 66565 (1Ki 9:3)

**Finding:** lev is applied to God in affective, cognitive, volitional, and purposive dimensions:
- Divine grief (Gen 6:6)
- Divine internal reasoning/speech (Gen 8:21)
- Divine purposive planning (Psa 33:11; Isa 63:4)
- Divine affective compassion (Hos 11:8)
- Divine committed attention (1Ki 9:3)
- Divine purposive promise (2Sa 7:21)

**Session B significance:** The term that names the human inner person is used analogically for the divine inner life. Session B should address: (a) the analogical/anthropomorphic character of these uses; (b) their significance for theological anthropology — the human heart as created in correspondence to the divine heart; (c) whether the divine and human uses of lev share the same semantic range or whether there are uses specific to one or the other.

## Session B Flag 3 — "Speak to the heart" formula: lev as target of redemptive address

**Group:** 581-008 (Heart as organ of relational address and concern)
**Relevant verse_record_ids:** 66592, 66654, 66657, 66665, 66677, 66758, 66879

**Finding:** The idiom "speak to/on the heart" (al-lev / el-lev) consistently marks speech directed at the inner person in contexts of relational repair, comfort, restoration, or return:
- Isa 40:2 — God's comfort-speech to Jerusalem after judgment
- Hos 2:14 — God's wooing-speech to Israel in the wilderness
- Gen 50:21 — Joseph's forgiveness-speech to his brothers
- Gen 34:3 — Shechem's speech to Dinah (context: deceptive wooing — the formula appears in a violated relational context, showing the formula's use even in manipulation)
- Rut 2:13 — Boaz's speech to Ruth: comfort and inclusion
- 2Sa 19:7 — Joab urging David to speak to his troops: relational repair
- Judg 19:3 — husband seeking to restore estranged wife

**Session B significance:** The formula identifies lev as the inner organ that is addressed when words are meant to reach the person, not merely inform them. It marks a qualitative mode of address — one that aims at healing, reconciliation, or inner persuasion rather than external compliance. This is distinct from informational speech. Session B should note the formula as a marker of lev's role as the organ through which persons are relationally restored.
