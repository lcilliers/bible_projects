**Prompt 2: What does the pattern of co-occurrence reveal about this characteristic's place in the inner-being landscape?**

The co-occurrence pattern reveals several structurally significant things:

1. **The strength-contrition relationship**: 7 shared verses with R187 (strength) is the single highest co-occurrence. This is analytically unexpected. The most plausible explanation: many of the contrition-vocabulary verses appear in lament and petition contexts where the psalmist appeals to God's strength as the counterpart to their own weakness/crushing. The verse evidence frequently pairs the crushed/contrite condition with an appeal to divine strength. This is a contextual co-occurrence (divine strength appears in the same verse as the contrite person's appeal) rather than a conceptual pairing of strength and contrition as inner-being states.

2. **The despair adjacency**: R044 (despair, 4 shared verses) is the closest expected conceptual companion in the cluster. The crushing vocabulary of contrition and the vocabulary of despair naturally co-occur in anguish and lament contexts. This co-occurrence confirms the boundary question at T1.3: contrition and despair are adjacent but distinct — they share verse contexts but are assigned to different registries.

3. **The guilt co-occurrence**: R073 (guilt, 3 shared verses) is structurally expected — penitential contrition arises from awareness of guilt. The co-occurrence confirms the moral-self-knowledge dimension of contrition. However, the 3-verse shared count is lower than might be expected if contrition were primarily a guilt-response. This suggests the vocabulary of contrition and guilt only partially overlaps in the verse evidence.

4. **The unexpected absence**: **Repentance (R135)** and **brokenness (R018)** — the two registries named in the registry description as directly related — are not in the top 25 co-occurrence list. This is a significant gap: the characteristic described as the precondition for repentance and the interior form of brokenness has minimal direct verse-level co-occurrence with either. This may reflect the fact that the registries draw on different vocabulary families even when describing related inner-being phenomena.

*New finding: the absence of repentance (R135) and brokenness (R018) from the top co-occurrence list, despite being named in the registry description as directly related, is analytically significant. It suggests the vocabulary-level evidence for the connection between contrition and repentance is weaker than the conceptual-level claim. This is a gap that should be captured as an SD pointer.*


### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic consistently preceding, following, or accompanying another characteristic in a sequence?**

Yes — sequential relationships are well-evidenced:

1. **Contrition precedes repentance** (conceptual, not verse-level): The registry description establishes contrition as the inner precondition for genuine repentance. The sequence is contrition → repentance. This is a conceptual sequential claim supported by the registry's analytical framework but not by a verse that names both in sequence.

2. **Contrition follows confrontation/chastisement**: The crushing encounter (prophetic confrontation, divine chastisement, suffering) precedes the contrite posture. Sequence: confrontation/suffering → contrition.

3. **Contrition precedes divine revival**: The contrite posture is the condition from which divine reviving comes (Isa 57:15; Psa 34:18). Sequence: contrition → divine revival.

4. **Contrition is accompanied by fear/trembling** (Isa 66:2): The three-fold posture (humble + contrite + trembling-at-word) presents contrition and word-trembling as simultaneous companions.

5. **Contrition is accompanied by mourning/sorrow** (Isa 53:5 shared with R151 sorrow): In the substitutionary context, crushing and sorrow co-occur at the same verse.

**Prompt 2: What does the sequence reveal — is the relationship causal, developmental, or correlational?**

The relationships are of different types:

- **Causal** (confrontation/suffering → contrition): External encounters produce the contrite condition. This is a causal relationship in one direction.
- **Enabling/conditional** (contrition → divine revival): Contrition does not mechanically cause divine revival — divine response is not automatic — but contrition is the condition that enables divine revival to reach the inner being. This is an enabling rather than mechanical causal relationship.
- **Sequential-precondition** (contrition → repentance): Contrition is the inner precondition that makes genuine repentance possible. Without it, repentance is "merely a change of strategy" (registry description). This is a precondition relationship.
- **Correlational** (contrition + fear/trembling + humility): The three-fold posture of Isa 66:2 is simultaneous correlation — these characteristics are aspects of the same inner-being posture rather than in a sequential relationship.


### T6.3 — Causal and Constitutive Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce another characteristic in the verse evidence — and if so, which one, and by what mechanism?**

Contrition produces:

1. **Readiness for divine revival** — the contrite inner state is the condition that draws divine reviving (Isa 57:15). Contrition produces readiness or receptivity for the divine action that revives.

2. **Longing for joy** — Psa 51:8 shows the contrite person actively longing for joy and gladness. Contrition produces the longing for restoration.

3. **Lament and petition** — the contrite condition generates the speech-act of lament and petition toward God (Psa 51:8; Psa 143:3). Contrition produces the inner-being pressure toward honest speech before God.

The characteristic contrition most obviously leads toward (repentance, R135) is not directly evidenced as produced in a verse — it is a conceptual-analytical claim in the registry description rather than a verse-level finding.

**Prompt 2: Is this characteristic produced by another — and if so, which one?**

Contrition is produced by:

1. **Suffering/anguish** (causal, experiential-anguish mode): External suffering and anguish produce the crushed inner state.

2. **Divine chastisement** (causal): God's direct action produces the crushing (Psa 44:19; Psa 51:8).

3. **Confrontation with moral condition before a holy God** (causal, penitential-relational mode): The encounter of the inner being with its own moral reality in the presence of divine holiness produces contrition.

4. **Guilt** (enabling): The inner recognition of guilt is the moral-cognitive content that fuels the contrite condition. The co-occurrence with R073 (guilt, 3 verses) supports this.

5. **Fear of God/trembling at God's word** (correlational companion, possibly enabling): Isa 66:2 presents fear/trembling-at-word as accompanying contrition. Whether fear produces contrition or they are simultaneously present is not resolved by the verse evidence.

**Prompt 3: Is this characteristic a constituent element of another, or does another characteristic constitute part of this one?**

The registry description places contrition in the description of brokenness (R018) and repentance (R135) as related registries — suggesting contrition may be a constituent element of both. The description states: "Covered by repentance (#135), brokenness (#18)" — implying these registries overlap with contrition's content. However, the precise constitutive relationship is not determined from the current data.

DIM-30-SD001 suggests contrition may be the "penitential sub-form of Dependence/Creatureliness" — which would make it a constituent element within the broader Dependence/Creatureliness dimension.

*Partial: constitutive relationships are implied by the registry description and DIM-30-SD001 but not verse-level established.*


### T6.4 — Vocabulary and Root Sharing

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic share vocabulary terms with other characteristics in the programme?**

The registry data reports `shared_term_count: 0` and `term_sharing_ratio: 0.0`. This means none of R030's OWNER terms are owned by any other active registry — they are exclusively owned by R030. However, the XREF section shows three terms owned by R105 (lust) appearing in contrition's verse corpus. The XREF terms (H7533 ra.tsats "to crush"; H4835 me.ru.tsah "oppression"; H7518 rats "piece") are owned by R105 but appear in R030 verses. This is a crushing-vocabulary overlap that runs in one direction.

Additionally, the shared anchor verses create implicit vocabulary connections: Isa 53:5 is shared with R151 (sorrow) and R062 (fellowship); Isa 57:15 is shared with R123 (pride), R204 (name), and R008 (appetite); Isa 66:2 is shared with R061 (fear); Jer 46:5 is shared with R061 (fear).

**Prompt 2: Does vocabulary sharing extend to root-level architecture — a shared root that generates terms across two or more characteristics?**

At the root level, the dkh root family (H1792–H1795) is self-contained within R030 — no other active registry appears to own terms from this family. The nkh root (H5222, H5223) is shared with non-programme vocabulary (mak.kah — wound; na.khah — to smite) but these are not inner-being registries in the programme as far as identifiable.

The ktt root (H3795 ka.tit "beaten"; H3807 ka.tat "to crush") shows possible overlap with crushing vocabulary in adjacent registries but the programme's term assignment shows these as owned by R030.

The most significant root-level connection: the XREF term H7533 ra.tsats ("to crush", owned by R105 lust) shares root architecture with the crushing vocabulary of R030. The dkh and ra.tsats roots are semantically parallel (both crushing verbs) without being etymologically identical. Their co-presence in the same verse corpus is a semantic rather than etymological connection.

*New finding: the root-level analysis reveals that R030's crushing vocabulary is largely self-contained — its terms are not widely shared across the programme. The crushing semantic field is concentrated in R030 with only the R105 (lust) XREF connection crossing registry boundaries at the vocabulary level. This concentration may itself be an observation: the biblical vocabulary for inner crushing/contrition is a relatively self-contained lexical family.*

**Prompt 3: What does vocabulary sharing reveal about the conceptual relationship between characteristics?**

The minimal vocabulary sharing (zero shared OWNER terms) reveals that contrition occupies a relatively distinct lexical territory in the programme. It is not a characteristic whose vocabulary bleeds into adjacent registries but one with its own concentrated vocabulary cluster. The conceptual relationships (with repentance, brokenness, fear, pride) are therefore primarily established through verse-level co-occurrence and shared anchors rather than through shared vocabulary.

This is analytically significant: contrition's relationship to its adjacent characteristics is contextual (same-verse co-occurrence) rather than lexical (shared terms). The boundaries between contrition and its neighbours (repentance, brokenness, shame) are therefore primarily conceptual-analytical boundaries rather than lexical ones — they must be drawn by analysis rather than being built into the vocabulary.


### T6.5 — Distinctions

**Prompt 1: Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what precisely distinguishes them?**

The most conceptually adjacent characteristics are:

1. **Brokenness (R018)**: The registry description notes brokenness as directly related. The precise distinction is not verse-level established in R030's data. Conceptually: brokenness may be the broader category (inner breaking from any cause) and contrition the specific penitential sub-form (breaking arising from moral self-knowledge before God). But this boundary requires cross-registry examination.

2. **Repentance (R135)**: Contrition is the inner precondition; repentance is the directional act. Contrition is condition; repentance is movement. This is the clearest analytical distinction but is not verse-level evidenced in R030 — it is a conceptual claim in the registry description.

3. **Sorrow (R151)**: Sorrow is the broader affective category of grief; contrition is the morally specific grief arising from actual harm done. The Isa 53:5 shared anchor shows them co-occurring; the distinction is the moral-content specificity of contrition versus the broader affective character of sorrow.

*Partial: conceptual distinctions are identifiable; verse-level boundary drawing is not completed in the current data.*

**Prompt 2: Where the evidence shows apparent overlap, what is the precise boundary?**

The contrition/shame boundary is the most evidenced distinction in this registry (registry description and Q&A v1 Q&A-046). Shame is socially-oriented (concern for standing in others' eyes); contrition is God-oriented (grief over actual harm caused). The contrition/sorrow boundary (R151) is partially evidenced through the 1 shared anchor (Isa 53:5). The contrition/repentance boundary (R135) is evidenced by the description placing contrition as precondition. Other boundaries not drawn. OBS-030-001, OBS-030-003.

**Prompt 3: Is the distinction between this characteristic and its nearest neighbour one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?**

For the contrition-brokenness distinction: **kind** — if contrition is the penitential sub-form of brokenness, the distinction is qualitative (the presence/absence of moral self-knowledge before God as the constitutive element).

For the contrition-repentance distinction: **kind and direction** — contrition is a condition; repentance is a directional act. They are different kinds of inner-being phenomena, not merely degrees.

For the contrition-sorrow distinction: **kind** — moral specificity versus general affective grief.

*Partial: distinctions can be named conceptually but require verse-level evidence from adjacent registries to confirm.*

**Prompt 2: What does the shared anchor reveal about the relationship between the two characteristics?**

Each shared anchor reveals a distinct relational claim:

1. **Isa 53:5 shared with sorrow (R151) and fellowship (R062)**: The substitutionary crushing simultaneously engages sorrow (the Servant's crushing involves grief-vocabulary — cf. OBS-030-032) and fellowship (the relational dynamic of substitutionary peace-making). The three-way anchor (contrition + sorrow + fellowship at Isa 53:5) suggests this verse is a convergence point for the inner-being consequences of substitutionary suffering.

2. **Isa 57:15 shared with pride (R123), name (R204), appetite (R008)**: This verse is structurally rich — the high and holy God (name, authority dimension) dwelling with the contrite (contrition) rather than the proud (pride antithesis) to revive (appetite/desire dimension?). The four-registry convergence at this verse marks it as a key Session D synthesis anchor: a single verse that names multiple inner-being realities in structural relationship.

3. **Isa 66:2 shared with fear (R061)**: The three-fold posture (humble + contrite + trembling-at-word) directly links contrition and fear (trembling) as co-present inner-being states. The shared anchor confirms the textual basis for the conceptual connection.

4. **Jer 46:5 shared with fear (R061)**: The military-terror scene links crushing-vocabulary (inner panic/dismay under defeat) with fear-vocabulary. This is the anguish-mode connection between contrition and fear.
