# Chapter 1 — Meaning

**Registry 030 — Contrition** · *Word Characteristic Summary*

---

## What it is

> Contrition is the genuine, deep sorrow of someone who has done wrong and knows it — not the surface shame of being caught but the interior grief of having actually caused harm or broken trust. The biblical vocabulary gives this its strongest expression in the psalms of repentance: the broken and contrite heart, the crushed spirit. Contrition is the inner precondition for genuine repentance: without it, repentance is merely a change of strategy rather than a change of heart. [Covered by repentance (#135), brokenness (#18).]


**Working description:** Drawing from the registry description, the Q&A answers, and the observations:

**Contrition is the inner-being condition of genuine brokenness before God arising from honest self-knowledge about moral failure or creaturely limitation.** It is located in the spirit and heart, characterised by downward movement (lowliness, self-reduction), and structurally opposed to pride and self-sufficiency. In its penitential-relational mode, it is the inner precondition for genuine repentance — the state from which repentance arises rather than the act of repentance itself. In its experiential-anguish mode, it is the inner state produced by suffering or enmity, without necessarily carrying penitential content. The holy God specifically attends to, dwells with, and revives the contrite inner being — making contrition the inner posture that receives divine response.


## Name and naming

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

The characteristic is named **contrition** in the programme (Registry 30, word field: "contrition"). The name signals three things about its essential nature immediately:

1. **Interior specificity**: The English word "contrition" derives from the Latin *contritus* — ground together, thoroughly broken. The name encodes the crushing image that is the dominant metaphor of the Hebrew vocabulary (dkh, ktt, nkh). It is not a surface word — it specifies an interior condition of being ground down, not merely saddened or ashamed.

2. **Moral grounding**: Contrition implies not merely suffering but suffering arising from moral failure. The name distinguishes genuine inner grief over wrong done from distress caused by external circumstances. The registry description makes this explicit: "not the surface shame of being caught but the interior grief of having actually caused harm or broken trust."

3. **Relational frame**: Contrition presupposes a relationship — a wrong done to someone (God, or another person). The name implies a prior injury and a self-awareness about one's role in it. This distinguishes it from anguish (which may arise without moral fault) and from shame (which is oriented toward observers rather than toward the wrong done).

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

The vocabulary is exclusively Hebrew — there are no Greek terms in this registry (NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag applies; the entire corpus is Old Testament). At the definitional level, the primary Hebrew terms reveal the following:

- **H1792 da.kha** ("to crush") and its cognates H1793A dak.ka ("contrite"), H1793B dak.ka ("dust"), H1794 da.khah ("to crush"), H1795 dak.kah ("crushing"): these terms cluster around the physical image of being crushed, pressed down, pulverised. The inner-being meaning arises from the passive forms — the one who has been crushed becomes contrite. The definitional signal is that contrition is received, not constructed — it is what happens to the inner being under certain conditions.

- **H3807 ka.tat** ("to crush, beat"): a parallel crushing verb with predominantly physical or military uses. Its single inner-being group (7556-001) occupies the terror/panic register — the furthest from the penitential meaning. Definitionally, this term marks the outer edge of the vocabulary: the crushing that produces inner dismay under external defeat.

- **H5223 na.kheh** ("crippled/smitten"): from the nkh root (to smite, strike). Definitionally, this term carries the image of the person who has been struck or crippled — applied metaphorically to the spirit as the one who has been struck in their inner person. It is the most explicitly metaphorical of the terms at the definitional level.

The combined definitional signal of the vocabulary: contrition is the inner-being state produced by some form of crushing or striking — whether from without (suffering, enmity, defeat) or from within (moral self-awareness before God).

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

Yes — all three.

**Directional**: The name and its vocabulary imply a downward direction — the inner being is brought low, crushed toward the ground. This directional signal immediately orients the enquiry toward the question of what produces the downward movement and what follows from it. The data answers both: moral self-awareness or suffering produces the downward movement; divine revival lifts the contrite.

**Relational**: The name implies a two-party structure — the one who is contrite and the one before whom they are contrite. In the verse evidence, the relational frame is almost exclusively vertical: the human before God. This orients the enquiry toward divine-human relationship as the primary context, and toward the question of how contrition functions in the horizontal (human-to-human) direction — a question the data largely leaves unanswered.

**Constitutional**: The name points to an inner-being condition rather than an external act. Contrition is located in "spirit" and "heart" (Psa 34:18; Isa 57:15; Psa 51:17) — the constitutional core of the person. This orients the enquiry toward the constitutional location question (T2) and toward the distinction between genuine contrition (an inner-being state) and ritual or performative penitence (an external act that may or may not reflect the inner state).


## Kind

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

Contrition is primarily a **condition** — a settled inner-being state that a person inhabits, not a single act they perform. The grammatical forms of the vocabulary support this: H1793A dak.ka is an adjective ("contrite"), H5223 na.kheh is an adjective ("crippled/contrite in spirit"), and participial uses of H1792/H1794 describe the person characterised by the state. Isa 66:2 identifies "the one who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word" — an ongoing orientation, not a discrete act.

However, the condition has a history: it is produced by a process (crushing, breaking). This suggests contrition has both a **formative moment** (the crushing encounter that produces the condition) and a **sustained state** (the condition of lowliness that results). The data does not describe contrition as a capacity (something the person has in reserve to deploy) but as something that happens to the inner being and from which a posture results.

A secondary classification: in its penitential-relational mode, contrition functions as an **inner-being posture before God** — the orientation of lowliness from which the person stands before the holy God. This is condition-sustained-as-posture: a stable orientation rather than a momentary state.

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

Contrition is not simple in structure. Three distinct modes of operation have been identified (Q003 answer; OBS-030-023):

1. **Penitential-relational mode** — the spirit/heart crushed by moral self-awareness before God (5 of 9 groups; dominant axis). This is the conventionally named "contrition."
2. **Experiential-anguish mode** — the inner being crushed by suffering, enmity, or divine chastisement without explicitly penitential content (3 of 9 groups; Emotion — Negative dimension).
3. **Substitutionary mode** — the Servant's crushing on behalf of others, producing peace and healing for the community (1 group; Divine-Human Correspondence dimension).

Within the penitential-relational mode, there are further structural elements: the three-fold posture of Isa 66:2 (humble + contrite + trembling at word) suggests contrition is part of a constellation of inner-being states that together constitute the posture before God. It is not a single note but part of a chord.


## Boundary — what it is not

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

The structural opposite is **pride** — explicitly evidenced in the verse data. Isa 57:15 places the high and holy God alongside the contrite and lowly: the implicit alternative structure is the proud self-elevated person who does not receive divine nearness. Jer 44:10 provides the most explicit negative case in the data: people who have not humbled themselves, not feared, not walked in God's law — the hardened, closed, self-sufficient posture that is the direct opposite of contrition's lowliness.

OBS-030-034 confirms: "Pride is the structural opposite of contrition — the high and lifted up God dwelling with the contrite and lowly rather than the proud. This is a direct structural antithesis relationship visible in a single verse." R123 (pride) shares Isa 57:15 as an anchor; the antithesis is textually grounded, not inferred.

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

The verse evidence shows contrition explicitly excluding or resisting:

1. **Merit-claim**: Psa 51:17 displaces the sacrificial system as the currency of relationship with God — the broken and contrite heart is the true sacrifice, not ritual performance. Contrition resists the logic of earning divine favour.

2. **Self-elevation**: The posture of lowliness (Isa 57:15 — "lowly spirit"; Isa 66:2 — "humble") directly excludes self-assertion and self-sufficiency.

3. **Hardness and closure**: Jer 44:10 presents the closed alternative — failure to humble, failure to fear, failure to walk in God's law. Contrition resists the inner-being posture that refuses divine assessment.

4. **Surface performance without inner reality**: The registry description distinguishes genuine contrition from the "surface shame of being caught." Contrition excludes the appearance of brokenness without its interior reality (which connects to the Psa 51:16–17 sequence: God does not delight in external sacrifice — the interior broken heart is what is sought).

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

The data provides partial answers to the boundary questions:

**Contrition is not shame**: The registry description distinguishes them explicitly — shame is oriented toward social observers and the fact of being caught; contrition is oriented toward the actual harm done and toward God. The verse evidence uses no shame vocabulary (busah/bosheth) in the contrition groups.

**Contrition is not repentance**: Contrition is the inner precondition for repentance; repentance (turning) is the directional act that follows from the contrite state. The relationship is sequential: contrition precedes and grounds repentance (Q040 answer). The boundary: contrition is a condition; repentance is an act.

**Contrition is not sorrow/mourning**: Related (Isa 53:5 shared with R151 sorrow) but distinguishable. Sorrow is the affective experience of grief; contrition is the moral-relational inner posture before God that may include sorrow but is not identical to it.

*Gap identified: the precise boundaries between contrition and brokenness (R018) are not established in this registry's data. The registry description notes they are covered by brokenness (#18) as a related registry, but no verse-level distinction is drawn. SP-030 pointers flag the repentance connection (R135) and the pride connection (R123) as Session D questions; the brokenness boundary is a further gap not yet captured in an SD pointer.*


## Modes of operation

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

Three modes, as established in Q003 and OBS-030-023, OBS-030-025, OBS-030-026:

1. **Penitential-relational mode**: The spirit and heart are crushed by moral self-awareness before God; the inner being is brought low; this posture draws divine nearness, revival, and acceptance. Dominant mode (5 of 9 groups, Dependence/Creatureliness dimension).

2. **Experiential-anguish mode**: The inner being is crushed by suffering, enmity, or divine chastisement. No explicitly penitential element; the inner state is grief/dismay/pain. Three groups: enemy-produced anguish (Psa 143:3), corporate desolation under divine crushing (Psa 44:19), inner terror under military defeat (Jer 46:5).

3. **Substitutionary mode**: The Servant's inner person is crushed on behalf of others' iniquity, producing peace and healing for the community (Isa 53:5). Unique — operates through vicarious bearing rather than personal moral self-awareness. One group, Divine-Human Correspondence dimension.

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

Yes — the modes of operation identified at T1.4 Prompt 1 map onto different contexts and directions:

- **Context-dependence**: The penitential-relational mode requires a context of moral self-knowledge before God. The experiential-anguish mode requires a context of suffering or defeat. The substitutionary mode requires a context of vicarious bearing — it does not arise from personal moral failure.

- **Direction**: In the penitential-relational mode, the crushing is self-recognised (the person knows their condition) and the orientation is upward-to-God. In the experiential-anguish mode, the crushing is externally produced (enemy, God's chastisement, military defeat) and the inner state is produced by the blow, not by self-recognition. In the substitutionary mode, the direction inverts: the innocent receives what belongs to the guilty.

- **Constitutional level**: Spirit and heart are the consistent loci across all modes. The experiential-anguish mode also surfaces soul-language (Psa 143:3 — "he has crushed my life/soul to the ground"). The substitutionary mode produces its outcomes (peace, healing) at the relational level rather than specifying a constitutional location.

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

The data evidences a partial communicative dimension. Psa 38:8 — "I groan because of the tumult of my heart" — presents the contrite/crushed state expressing itself through vocal groaning. This is a speech-adjacent form: not articulate communication but the inarticulate voiced expression of inner anguish. Psa 51:8 — "let me hear joy and gladness; let the bones that you have broken rejoice" — is petition: the contrite person addressing God in speech from their broken position. Psa 143:3 is a lament — structured speech from the crushed condition.

The communicative mode of contrition appears to be **lament and petition**: the contrite inner being speaks to God from its broken position, not from articulateness or confidence but from need. The groan, the lament, the petition are the characteristic speech-forms. However, the data does not develop this dimension comprehensively.

*Partial: lament/petition as the speech-mode of contrition is evidenced but not fully analysed.*


## Immediate response

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

The framing of this question — "receiving" contrition — is imprecise for this characteristic. Contrition is not typically received from another; it is a condition that arises in the person. Adjusting the question to the more appropriate framing: what is the immediate inner-being response to the crushing/encounter that produces contrition?

The data evidences: the immediate response to the crushing is **lowliness and open need**. The crushed/contrite person is immediately characterised by an absence of self-claim and a posture of disclosed need before God. Psa 38:8 adds **groaning** as an immediate expression. The sequence (Q015 answer) begins with crushing → contrite posture: the contrite posture is the immediate state.

*Partial: the immediate response to the producing encounter is identifiable (lowliness, open need, groaning); the question of what contrition produces as an immediate response in another person who "receives" it is not evidenced in the data — the vocabulary is not configured that way.*

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

The immediate inner-being response varies across the three modes:

- **Penitential-relational mode**: Lowliness, open need, and orientation toward God are the immediate states (Psa 34:18; Isa 57:15; Psa 51:17).
- **Experiential-anguish mode**: Dismay, anguish, and groaning are the immediate responses (Psa 143:3; Psa 38:8; Jer 46:5 — terror).
- **Substitutionary mode**: The Servant's bearing produces peace and healing in the community — the immediate response is in the recipients, not the one bearing the crushing.

Variation is therefore intrinsic to the vocabulary's semantic range: the three modes produce different immediate states because they arise in different contexts and through different mechanisms.


## Sustained effect

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

The verse evidence describes what divine response to contrition produces (revival, nearness, salvation) rather than what contrition itself produces over time as a sustained inner-being state. Contrition is not typically described as a long-duration condition whose effects accumulate — it is presented as a posture that draws divine response, after which the revival/restoration changes the inner-being condition.

What can be said from the data: sustained contrition (as a settled orientation of lowliness) produces the quality of **humility** as a character disposition. Isa 57:15 pairs contrite spirit with lowly spirit as simultaneous characteristics. Isa 66:2 presents humble + contrite + trembling as a settled three-fold orientation. The sustained effect of the contrite orientation appears to be a character formation in the direction of lowliness, genuine self-knowledge, and receptivity to God's word.

*Partial: character formation toward lowliness and receptivity is a reasonable inference from the data; the data does not directly address sustained effects over time.*

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

The same data as T1.6 Prompt 1 applies. The states and qualities associated with contrition's sustained presence: **lowliness, humility, receptivity to God's word, and openness to divine revival**. These are presented as accompanying characteristics (Isa 66:2) rather than effects produced by contrition over time.

There is a suggestive sequence in the verse evidence: Psa 51:8 presents the contrite person longing for joy and gladness from their broken state. If divine revival follows (Isa 57:15), then the sustained effect of the contrition-revival sequence might be a capacity for deeper joy — the broken bones that rejoice. This is interpretive rather than directly stated.

*Partial: the data evidences accompanying orientations (lowliness, receptivity) but does not trace sustained effects over time in a way the data directly supports.*

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

Yes — clearly. The immediate response is longing for joy and openness to divine approach (Q&A-025). The sustained effect is the settled character of lowliness, word-permeability, and receptivity (Q&A-029). The immediate response is affective and directional; the sustained effect is characterological. The person does not remain in the acute phase of contrition permanently — they emerge into a character shaped by it. OBS-030-009, OBS-030-046, OBS-030-055.


## Conditions of reception

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

The conditions enabling genuine contrition (reframing "reception" as the conditions that make the contrite condition available) are established in Q002 and Q014 answers:

- **Humility** — the prior orientation of lowliness before God (Isa 66:2 presents this as accompanying contrition; Q002 identifies it as enabling)
- **Fear of God / trembling at God's word** — the inner-being orientation of attentiveness and awe before divine authority (Isa 66:2)
- **Honest self-knowledge** — the recognition that one has actually caused harm or broken trust, as opposed to the surface shame of being caught (registry description; Q014)
- **Openness to divine assessment** — the willingness to receive God's evaluation of one's condition without deflecting (implied by the contrast with Jer 44:10's hardened posture)

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

From Q028, OBS-030-028, OBS-030-047:

- **Failure to humble oneself** — the refusal of lowliness (Jer 44:10)
- **Failure to fear** — the closed orientation that does not acknowledge divine authority (Jer 44:10)
- **Failure to walk in God's law/word** — the practical orientation away from divine assessment (Jer 44:10)
- **Pride** — the active posture of self-elevation (Isa 57:15 — implied structural antithesis)
- **Merit-logic** — the inner orientation that approaches God with performance-claims rather than acknowledged need (Psa 51:16–17 — explicit displacement)

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

The verse evidence provides a direct answer in Jer 44:10 and in the refusal clause of group 7552-003 ("the condition of brokenness before God that occasions divine presence, *and its refusal*"). The person who encounters the conditions for contrition — who is confronted with their moral condition before a holy God — but refuses the contrite posture is characterised by:

**Hardness** — the inner being that does not yield to the crushing encounter remains closed and resistant. The people of Jer 44:10 have been confronted with the full weight of their condition but have not humbled themselves, feared, or turned. Their inner being is, in the vocabulary of the structural opposite, **proud and closed** — self-sufficient against divine assessment.

The consequence of this non-reception is notable: Jer 44:10 is within the group that also contains Isa 57:15 (the divine dwelling with the contrite). The God who dwells with the contrite does not dwell with the hardened. The inner-being state of the one who refuses contrition is: continued self-sufficiency, divine absence, and — as the trajectory of Jer 44 shows — eventual catastrophe without divine covering.

*New finding: the inner-being state of the one who refuses contrition is not merely neutral absence of the quality; it is a specific closed condition (hardness) with a specific consequence (divine absence). This was implicit in OBS-030-028 but not assembled as an answer to the reception-refusal question.*


## Dimension classification

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

Dependence / Creatureliness — confirmed by the dimension distribution: 5 of 9 groups assigned Dependence/Creatureliness, representing the dominant dimension. This is the penitential-relational mode as the primary register. The Dimension Review confirmed this (WA-DimensionReview-Instruction-v2.2-2026-04-11). OBS-030-022, OBS-030-045.

**T1.8.2 — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

The classification is supported across all five Dependence/Creatureliness groups. The evidence: all five describe a broken/crushed posture specifically *before God* — contrite before the divine presence, in a relational/covenantal frame where the broken posture is the condition from which divine nearness, revival, dwelling, or attention is given. The verse anchors: Psa 34:18 (crushed in spirit → Lord near), Isa 57:15 (contrite and lowly spirit → God dwells and revives), Psa 51:17 (broken and contrite heart → God will not despise), Isa 66:2 (humble and contrite in spirit → God looks to). All present the human in a condition of creaturely lowliness before the holy God. This is Dependence/Creatureliness precisely expressed.

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

Yes — two secondary dimensions are present:

1. **Emotion — Negative** (3 of 9 groups: 7552-001, 7554-002, 7556-001): the experiential-anguish register — suffering, corporate desolation, terror/panic. These are not penitential; they are affective states of pain and dismay. This secondary dimension is complementary rather than competing: the crushing vocabulary operates in both registers because the same physical-to-inner metaphor (being crushed) applies to both penitential and experiential brokenness.

2. **Divine-Human Correspondence** (1 group: 7552-002 — Isa 53:5 substitutionary crushing): This group carries a unique dimension — the Servant's crushing is in a different category from either penitential contrition or experiential anguish. It is complementary to the primary dimension in that it shares the crushing vocabulary, but it operates in a distinct theological register.

The secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary — they expand the semantic range of the vocabulary beyond its penitential core.

**Vocabulary analysis (T7.1):**

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

The vocabulary is exclusively Hebrew — no Greek terms are present in this registry (NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag for all 22 terms; the corpus is entirely Old Testament).

**Primary term families:**

1. **dkh root family** (H1792 da.kha, H1793A dak.ka, H1793B dak.ka, H1794 da.khah, H1795 dak.kah): The root meaning is physical crushing, pressing down, pulverising. The root reveals that contrition is conceptualised through the image of something being physically ground down — a grain crushed by a millstone, an enemy crushed underfoot. The inner-being meaning is the extension of this physical image to the spirit and heart: the inner being that has been ground down by its encounter with moral reality and divine holiness.

2. **ktt root family** (H3795 ka.tit, H3807 ka.tat): A parallel crushing/beating root, with the ka.tat verb (H3807) producing the most physically intensive crushing image — used of military defeat, of beating metal, and of the internal terror/panic of routed warriors. The root reveals the violent, thoroughgoing nature of the crushing: not a mild pressing but an intensive beating-down.

3. **nkh root** (H5222 ne.kheh, H5223 na.kheh): From the verb na.khah ("to smite, strike"). The root meaning is a blow from outside — a striking or smiting action. The nkh group's contribution to contrition is the image of the one who has been struck or crippled — applied metaphorically to the spirit as one that has received an external blow. The root reveals the externally-inflicted dimension: contrition in the nkh vocabulary is the state of one struck from outside, not simply of one who has collapsed from within.

The three root families together reveal that contrition's essential nature is: **received, not generated** — the contrite person is the one who has been acted upon (crushed, beaten, struck), not the one who has produced their own condition. This passive structure is built into the root meanings.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

The grammatical range of H1792 da.kha (the lexical anchor):
- **Niphal** (passive/reflexive): "to be crushed," "to be contrite (figuratively)" — the inner-being meaning emerges specifically from the passive form
- **Piel** (active intensive): "to crush" actively — used of the Servant being crushed (Isa 53:5) and of God crushing enemies
- **Hithpael** (reflexive/permissive): "to allow oneself to be crushed" — the volitional-receptive dimension
- **Adjectival forms**: H1793A/B dak.ka (contrite/dust) and H1795 dak.kah — describe the settled condition

The grammatical range reveals: contrition is most precisely a **state arising from passive reception** (Niphal) but involves a **volitional element of consent** (Hithpael). The adjectival forms establish it as a **settled condition** rather than a momentary event. The Piel shows that the crushing action can be performed by God or by an agent on another. The range as a whole confirms: contrition is the condition of the one who has been crushed (passive), who may have either permitted (Hithpael) or resisted the crushing, and whose condition has become a settled posture (adjective).

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

H1792 da.kha operates across:
- Physical crushing of persons, enemies, or objects (literal)
- Inner anguish produced by external crushing (enemy-produced suffering — group 7552-001)

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary implicitly distinguishes:

- **Conditional** (the state produced): H1793A dak.ka as adjective — "the one who is contrite" — the settled condition
- **Active (of another)**: H1792 da.kha Piel — "to crush" — the active production of the crushing
- **Receptive/permissive**: H1792 da.kha Hithpael — "to allow oneself to be crushed" — the volitional-receptive dimension
- **Adjectival state**: H1794 da.khah — "crushed/contrite" as a participle describing the current condition of the heart

The vocabulary does not include a term specifically for the dispositional aspect (a stable inclination toward the contrite posture) — this is the gap between the vocabulary's adjectival forms (settled condition) and a fully dispositional term. Whether contrition can be a disposition rather than only a condition is not lexically established.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — H1793A dak.ka ("contrite") and H5223 na.kheh ("contrite/smitten in spirit") function as person-type descriptors when used with the genitive of spirit or heart. The contrite person seeks God through prayer but the seeking vocabulary belongs to separate root families. Gap noted — the seeking dimension of contrition is lexically present in context but not in the characteristic's own vocabulary. OBS-030-052.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc — from the most physical to the most theological — reveals a characteristic with a remarkably coherent semantic structure built on a single conceptual image (being crushed/struck) deployed across a wide range:

**Physical pole**: ka.tit (beaten olive oil — Exod 29:40 type use), ka.tat (beaten down militarily), da.kha (crushing enemies) — the vocabulary's most concrete, physical uses.

**Experiential-inner pole**: da.kha applied to the soul/spirit under anguish and suffering (Psa 143:3; Psa 38:8) — the physical crushing image applied to inner-being experience without moral content.

**Penitential-theological pole**: dak.ka, da.khah, na.kheh applied to the spirit and heart in penitential posture before God (Psa 34:18; Isa 57:15; Psa 51:17; Isa 66:2) — the full inner-being meaning.

**Redemptive-typological pole**: da.kha applied to the Servant's substitutionary crushing (Isa 53:5) — the vocabulary's most theologically dense use.

The arc reveals that the Hebrew vocabulary for contrition is conceptually unified (all instances derive from the physical crushing image) but semantically rich — the same root cluster carries physical, experiential, penitential, and redemptive meaning. The inner-being meaning is not an alien addition to the vocabulary but a natural extension of its physical core: what happens to a thing when it is crushed is precisely what happens to the spirit and heart in genuine contrition.
