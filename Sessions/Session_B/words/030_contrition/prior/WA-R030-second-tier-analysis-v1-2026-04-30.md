### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**Prompt 1:** What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

The verse evidence for contrition reflects two aspects of divine character simultaneously, and the relationship between them is theologically precise.

First, **divine holiness as the generative context**: Isa 57:15 opens with an extended divine self-identification — "the One who is high and lifted up, who inhabits eternity, whose name is Holy." It is this God — the utterly holy, transcendent, eternal one — who dwells with the contrite. The holiness of God is not incidental framing; it is the very quality whose encounter produces contrite collapse in the human inner being. The crushing vocabulary of the registry (dkh, ktt, nkh) images what happens to the human inner being when it genuinely encounters the reality of divine holiness: it is brought low, broken, reduced.

Second, **divine attentiveness to lowliness**: The pattern across all five Dependence/Creatureliness groups is that God actively moves toward the contrite — nearness (Psa 34:18), dwelling (Isa 57:15), looking (Isa 66:2), non-despising (Psa 51:17). The divine character reflected is not indifference to inner posture but a specific, consistent orientation: God is drawn toward the inner being that has been brought low. This is not a mechanical trigger but a relational disposition — divine character expressed as attentiveness to vulnerability.

The nature of God reflected is therefore: holy (transcendent, set apart, causing the contrite to collapse) and simultaneously near to the low (dwelling, attending, reviving). Contrition in the human being images this double divine reality — the creature that is genuinely before the holy God cannot maintain self-elevation; the one who is genuinely low finds divine nearness. The divine character that contrition images is the holiness that does not consume the lowly but revives them.

*New finding: The double divine quality — holy and near-to-the-low — has not been assembled as a single characterisation in the prior analysis. The registry data names these separately across multiple observations; this prompt assembles them into a unified characterisation of what divine character contrition reflects.*

---

**Prompt 2:** Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

Scripture does not explicitly attribute contrition to God as a divine characteristic. The vocabulary of this registry (dkh, ktt, nkh — crushing, being broken, being smitten) is consistently applied to human subjects or to the Servant (Isa 53:5), not to God as the one who is contrite. God is the subject who crushes (Psa 44:19 — "you have broken us"; Psa 51:8 — "the bones that you have broken"), not the one who is crushed.

The structural logic runs in one direction: divine holiness produces contrite collapse in the human; divine nearness responds to that collapse. Contrition is not a divine attribute in the verse evidence — it is the characteristically creaturely response to the holy God.

This directional asymmetry is analytically significant. It means contrition does not image God by resembling God in this quality (as, for example, love images a God who is love). Rather, contrition images God by imaging the *right creaturely response* to divine reality — the appropriate form of human inner being before the holy God. The significance for the human person is that contrition is not optional piety but the structurally correct inner posture for a creature before the Creator-God. Its absence (Jer 44:10) is not merely a personal failing but a failure of creaturely orientation.

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

The silence is noted above — Scripture does not attribute contrition to God. The instruction notes that for characteristics like forgiveness, grace, or mercy, this prompt will typically be N (not applicable) because Scripture *does* explicitly attribute them to God. For contrition, the silence is the data, and it is informative.

The silence suggests that contrition belongs to the exclusively creaturely aspect of the divine image rather than to the shared aspect. The divine image includes both what humans share with God (love, wisdom, creativity, moral character) and what is proper to creaturely existence before God (dependence, limitation, responsiveness). Contrition belongs to the second category — it is the appropriate inner-being posture of a creature who knows itself to be creature before a holy Creator. God does not and cannot occupy this posture because God is not creature before Creator. The silence of Scripture on divine contrition is therefore not a deficiency — it is structurally correct. Contrition expresses the creaturely dimension of the divine image: being made in God's likeness includes being constituted as a being that can and should know its own lowliness before God.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**Prompt 1:** What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**Status: P** | *New finding*

The verse evidence speaks clearly to what contrition *does* in the post-fall condition — it produces the inner-being posture that draws divine nearness, revival, and acceptance (Psa 34:18; Isa 57:15; Psa 51:17; Isa 66:2). But the question of *created purpose* — what contrition equips the human being for in their constitution as a creature — is only partially addressed.

What the data does support: contrition equips the human person for **genuine receptivity before God**. The contrite inner being is characterised by openness, lowliness, and absence of self-claim — it is constituted to receive rather than to assert. This receptivity is not merely a post-fall emergency measure; the three-fold posture of Isa 66:2 (humble, contrite, trembling at God's word) suggests an orientation of creaturely attentiveness that is constitutive of the right creature-Creator relationship. In this sense, contrition may belong to the *creaturely* dimension of the human design — the capacity for appropriate self-knowledge before a holy God.

What is missing: the data does not directly address whether contrition is part of the original created design (before moral failure) or whether it is specifically a response to the fallen condition. This is a genuine gap, addressed further at T0.2 Prompt 2.

*Partial: the data establishes the receptive-orientation function but does not definitively locate it within created design versus fallen response.*

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**Prompt 1:** In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Contrition expresses the divine image through what might be called the **relational-creaturely axis** of the image: the capacity for genuine self-knowledge before God, and the inner-being responsiveness to divine holiness that this produces. The divine image includes the capacity to stand in right relationship to God — to know oneself truly as creature, and to respond to the holy God with appropriate inner posture. Contrition is the activation of this capacity in the specific context of moral failure or creaturely limitation.

The verse evidence supports this in two ways:
1. The contrite person is specifically the one God dwells with, looks at, and revives (Isa 57:15; Isa 66:2; Psa 34:18). The image-bearing quality of contrition is attested by divine recognition — God identifies the contrite as the one in whom the right creaturely posture is present.
2. The structural opposite — hardness, refusal to humble oneself, failure to walk in God's law (Jer 44:10) — is presented as a distortion of right creaturely orientation. The absence of contrition marks a person who is not functioning as a genuine image-bearer before God but is asserting an autonomy that belongs to God alone.

*New finding: contrition expresses the image specifically in its creaturely-relational dimension — the human capacity for truthful self-knowledge and appropriate lowliness before the holy God. This aspect of the image is distinct from the creative, rational, or moral dimensions that are typically named in the divine image discussion.*

**Prompt 2:** Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

As established at T0.1 Prompt 2, contrition is not shared between God and the human person. It is exclusively creaturely. There is, however, a creaturely analogue to something in God: the verse evidence consistently shows God as the one who produces the crushing (Psa 44:19; Psa 51:8) and responds to the crushed (Psa 34:18; Isa 57:15). The divine analogue is not contrition but the **holy attention that makes contrition possible and meaningful**. God's holiness confronts the inner being; the inner being's right response is contrition. The divine version of this dynamic is the act of confrontation and subsequent reviving — God's own justice and mercy interacting with human brokenness.

The creaturely analogue relationship: contrition in the human corresponds to, and is response to, the holy-and-near character of God.

**Prompt 3:** What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

The divine image is present but functionally inverted: they are constituted to be low before God but have refused lowliness.

*New finding: the presence/absence of contrition functions as a diagnostic of whether the creaturely-relational axis of the divine image is functioning correctly. This is not destruction of the image but its functional distortion.*

**Prompt 2:** If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**Prompt 1:** What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The characteristic is named **contrition** in the programme (Registry 30, word field: "contrition"). The name signals three things about its essential nature immediately:

1. **Interior specificity**: The English word "contrition" derives from the Latin *contritus* — ground together, thoroughly broken. The name encodes the crushing image that is the dominant metaphor of the Hebrew vocabulary (dkh, ktt, nkh). It is not a surface word — it specifies an interior condition of being ground down, not merely saddened or ashamed.

2. **Moral grounding**: Contrition implies not merely suffering but suffering arising from moral failure. The name distinguishes genuine inner grief over wrong done from distress caused by external circumstances. The registry description makes this explicit: "not the surface shame of being caught but the interior grief of having actually caused harm or broken trust."

3. **Relational frame**: Contrition presupposes a relationship — a wrong done to someone (God, or another person). The name implies a prior injury and a self-awareness about one's role in it. This distinguishes it from anguish (which may arise without moral fault) and from shame (which is oriented toward observers rather than toward the wrong done).

**Prompt 2:** What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The vocabulary is exclusively Hebrew — there are no Greek terms in this registry (NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag applies; the entire corpus is Old Testament). At the definitional level, the primary Hebrew terms reveal the following:

- **H1792 da.kha** ("to crush") and its cognates H1793A dak.ka ("contrite"), H1793B dak.ka ("dust"), H1794 da.khah ("to crush"), H1795 dak.kah ("crushing"): these terms cluster around the physical image of being crushed, pressed down, pulverised. The inner-being meaning arises from the passive forms — the one who has been crushed becomes contrite. The definitional signal is that contrition is received, not constructed — it is what happens to the inner being under certain conditions.

- **H3807 ka.tat** ("to crush, beat"): a parallel crushing verb with predominantly physical or military uses. Its single inner-being group (7556-001) occupies the terror/panic register — the furthest from the penitential meaning. Definitionally, this term marks the outer edge of the vocabulary: the crushing that produces inner dismay under external defeat.

- **H5223 na.kheh** ("crippled/smitten"): from the nkh root (to smite, strike). Definitionally, this term carries the image of the person who has been struck or crippled — applied metaphorically to the spirit as the one who has been struck in their inner person. It is the most explicitly metaphorical of the terms at the definitional level.

The combined definitional signal of the vocabulary: contrition is the inner-being state produced by some form of crushing or striking — whether from without (suffering, enmity, defeat) or from within (moral self-awareness before God).

### T1.2 — Kind

**Prompt 1:** What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a capacity, or something else?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

Contrition is primarily a **condition** — a settled inner-being state that a person inhabits, not a single act they perform. The grammatical forms of the vocabulary support this: H1793A dak.ka is an adjective ("contrite"), H5223 na.kheh is an adjective ("crippled/contrite in spirit"), and participial uses of H1792/H1794 describe the person characterised by the state. Isa 66:2 identifies "the one who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word" — an ongoing orientation, not a discrete act.

However, the condition has a history: it is produced by a process (crushing, breaking). This suggests contrition has both a **formative moment** (the crushing encounter that produces the condition) and a **sustained state** (the condition of lowliness that results). The data does not describe contrition as a capacity (something the person has in reserve to deploy) but as something that happens to the inner being and from which a posture results.

A secondary classification: in its penitential-relational mode, contrition functions as an **inner-being posture before God** — the orientation of lowliness from which the person stands before the holy God. This is condition-sustained-as-posture: a stable orientation rather than a momentary state.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**Prompt 1:** In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

Three modes, as established in Q003 and OBS-030-023, OBS-030-025, OBS-030-026:

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**Prompt 1:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, in what form?

2. **God as the one who revives the contrite**: Isa 57:15 — God dwells with the contrite "to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." The principle is not merit-based exchange but **character-consistent divine disposition**: the holy God's character includes attention to the lowly. This is not unconditional in the sense of indifference to the human posture — the posture of contrition is required (Jer 44:10 shows the alternative clearly). The pattern is unambiguous and consistent: God is drawn toward the contrite inner being. This reveals a divine disposition of care for creaturely vulnerability — specifically the vulnerability that arises from honest self-disclosure before God. The God of this data is not indifferent to inner states but specifically responsive to the posture of lowliness.

- **Developmental/character formation psychology**: the formative arc of contrition over the life — how the contrite orientation is cultivated or deepened — is a developmental question.
- **Affect theory**: the affective dimensions of contrition (grief, groaning, anguish as genuine emotional responses to moral failure) are well-addressed by affect research on negative self-conscious emotions.
- **Existential psychology**: the encountering-of-one's-limits dimension of contrition (the inner being confronted with its creaturely condition) connects to existential accounts of limit-situations and authenticity.

**Prompt 2:** Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or visible — what is that finding?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

The guilt-shame distinction from moral psychology makes two findings in the verse evidence more coherent:

1. **The registry description's distinction between genuine contrition and surface shame**: The psychological distinction between guilt (behaviour-focused, motivating repair) and shame (self-focused, motivating withdrawal or concealment) maps onto the biblical distinction with notable precision. Contrition in the penitential-relational mode is guilt-structured: it is about the harm done, not about the self's standing, and it motivates approach to God (the equivalent of repair) rather than withdrawal.

2. **The spatial-directional logic**: The psychological observation that guilt produces approach (toward the harmed party, toward repair) while shame produces withdrawal (from others, concealment) maps onto the verse evidence's spatial logic: contrition moves the inner being toward God (downward and inward in posture, then upward toward divine encounter), not away from God. The hardened posture of Jer 44:10 (which avoids divine assessment) is more structurally similar to the shame-withdrawal pattern.

*New finding: the guilt-shame psychological distinction illuminates the registry description's core distinction and provides a human science vocabulary that renders the biblical distinction more precise. This is a framework that genuinely aids understanding of the verse evidence.*

**Prompt 3:** Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

The primary divergence: **the object of contrition**. In the psychological guilt framework, guilt is typically directed toward the *person harmed* (interpersonal repair motivation). In the biblical evidence, contrition is directed overwhelmingly toward *God* — the vertical axis dominates. The horizontal dimension (contrition toward the human person harmed) is largely absent from the verse evidence. This divergence reveals that the biblical understanding of contrition has a **theocentric rather than anthropocentric** orientation: the primary relational disruption addressed by contrition is the human-God relationship, not the human-human relationship.

This is not a deficiency in the biblical understanding but a structural difference in frame: the psychological framework operates within an immanent horizontal frame; the biblical vocabulary operates within a transcendent vertical frame. Contrition in the biblical evidence is not primarily about repairing human relationships but about right creaturely posture before the holy God.

*New finding: the theocentric vs. anthropocentric divergence between the biblical and psychological frames reveals something important about the distinctive character of biblical contrition — it is fundamentally about the creature-Creator relationship, with horizontal relational repair as a secondary consequence rather than a primary orientation.*

**Prompt 4:** Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not addressed?

**Status: A** | *Gap identified*

Yes — the psychological framework surfaces two aspects not addressed in the verse evidence:

1. **The formation of the contrite disposition over time**: Developmental psychology addresses how moral self-conscious emotions are formed, cultivated, and integrated into character. The verse evidence provides no account of how the contrite orientation is formed — how a person becomes the person of Isa 66:2 (settled humble, contrite, trembling posture). The psychological framework prompts the question of formation processes that the verse evidence does not address.

2. **The pathological forms of contrition**: Moral psychology distinguishes healthy guilt (motivating repair) from chronic guilt, self-punishment, and scrupulosity (dysfunctional forms). The verse evidence does not address whether contrition can become pathological — whether excessive or misdirected brokenness is a distortion of the genuine condition. The psychological framework surfaces this as a question the verse evidence has not examined.

*Gap identified: formation processes and pathological forms of contrition are dimensions surfaced by the human science framework that are absent from the verse evidence. These are potential Session D questions if cross-registry synthesis can address them.*
