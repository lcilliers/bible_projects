## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

H2896A (tov): root TOV with 10 sub-senses (SBF-067-Q088) — aesthetic pleasantness (1a), higher-nature pleasantness (1b), intellectual goodness (1h), gladness/well-being (1g), ethical rightness (1j). Conceptual origin is phenomenological: begins with concrete experience of what is agreeable and beneficial, rises to moral and covenantal categories. The root meaning reveals that goodness is not first an abstract ethical standard — it is first the quality of things that genuinely satisfy and benefit, recognisable in creation and in proximity to God. G0019 (agathōsunē): root AGATHOS — the classical Greek adjective for that which is functionally good/useful. The abstract noun suffix (-syne) creates a virtue-noun — naming goodness as a stable inner quality, not merely a predicate. The root reveals the NT's intentional move from adjective (applied to many things) to abstract virtue (naming a specific inner characteristic). G5544 (chrēstotēs): root CHRĒSTOS — serviceable, useful, good in a relational-functional sense. The root reveals a relational quality: what is useful and generous toward another. The NT elevates this from horizontal utility (serviceable) to vertical character (Spirit-produced generosity of disposition). (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-009, SBF-067-Q088)

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

H2896A (tov) operates primarily as an adjective — modifying persons, things, words, actions, and states. Its adjectival nature means it names a quality of its subject rather than an action performed. However, H2895 (tov verbal — be pleasing) shows the root also generates a stative verb — describing the state of being good/pleasing. The grammatical range (adjective + stative verb + verbal noun in related forms) reveals that goodness in Hebrew is primarily characterological (it names what something is) and secondarily experiential (it names the state of being good/pleasing to someone). G0019 (agathōsunē) is exclusively an abstract noun — naming goodness as a substantive virtue. G5544 (chrēstotēs) is also exclusively an abstract noun. The NT's grammatical form (abstract nouns only) reveals a shift from the Hebrew's adjectival breadth (goodness applied across contexts) to a specialised inner-being virtue-noun. (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-008, SBF-067-Q088)

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

H2896A (tov) has the widest semantic range of any OWNER term in this registry: aesthetic pleasantness, moral rightness, covenantal reliability, comparative excellence, experiential benefit, evaluative good, ontological goodness (Gen 1 creation pronouncement), volitional preference, and inner well-being. The 12 distinct modes identified at Q&A-022 correspond to 12 distinct positions within this range. The breadth is confirmed by the 10 sub-senses of the TOV root and the 9 distinct H2896A groups. G0019 (agathōsunē): narrow range — goodness as Spirit-produced inner virtue/resolve (4 NT occurrences, all Pauline, all pneumatological). G5544 (chrēstotēs): medium range — kindness/goodness/gentleness as inner character quality and divine attribute (7 occurrences). The semantic range across the three OWNER terms covers breadth (H2896A's 12 modes) and precision (G0019 and G5544's specialised NT meanings). (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-009, OBS-067-OBS-048)

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the programme's XREF architecture structurally encodes the disposition/act distinction: G0019 (agathōsunē — goodness as inner disposition, R67 OWNER) vs G0015 (agathopoieo — to do good, R65 XREF). The disposition/act distinction is real and lexically supported. Received vs given: the same term chrēstotēs (G5544) names God's goodness given to humanity (886-001) and the Spirit-produced human goodness given to others (886-002) — the received/given distinction is not lexically marked but dimensionally assigned (Dimension 11 vs Dimension 05). Condition vs quality: H2896A's 884-009 (shalom-condition of inner well-being) is a condition; 884-002 (human moral character and conduct assessed as good) is a quality. The vocabulary distinguishes condition and quality within the same root. (OBS-067-OBS-017, OBS-067-OBS-090, SBF-067-Q090)

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

Yes — three forms. (1) Direct negation: H2896A used with negative particle — "not good" (Group 884-006, 22 verses). The vocabulary generates its own opposite through simple negation rather than a separate root. (2) Universal absence: chrēstotēs used in its negative in Rom 3:12 ("no one does good, not even one" — from Ps 14:3 LXX). The same term names the characteristic and its complete absence. (3) Etymological distortion: G5542 (chrēstologia — smooth talk) is the chrēsto root's distortion form — the outward language of goodness without its substance. The distortion-opposite is lexically related, not a separate root. (OBS-067-OBS-021, OBS-067-OBS-049, SBF-067-Q021)

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

G0017 (agathopoios — doing good, "the one who does good") is in the XREF inventory — this is a person-type term for the one who does good (1 Pet 2:14 / 2:15 context). However, it is assigned to R65 (generosity) rather than R67. No direct OWNER-term person-type exists in R67 for "the good person" as a named inner-being category. H2896A in its adjectival form functions descriptively ("a good person") but is not a fixed person-type label. G0017 is the closest — but it is an action-person type (one who does good) rather than a character-person type (one who is good). The distinction is analytically significant — the vocabulary distinguishes the person who does good from the person who is good. Partial gap: action-person type present (in R65 XREF); character-person type absent as a fixed lexical label. (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-090, SBF-067-Q090)

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

No dedicated supplication term for goodness is present in the OWNER vocabulary. However, Lam 3:25 names "the soul who seeks him" in the context of seeking God's goodness — seeking (biqqesh / darash implied) is the posture. Psa 119:68 ("teach me your statutes") contains a petition grounded in God's goodness, functioning as a seeking-from-God. But these are not dedicated goodness-seeking terms — they are the general seeking/petition vocabulary applied in goodness contexts. A dedicated supplication form (like "ask for goodness") is absent. Partial gap. (OBS-067-OBS-033, OBS-067-OBS-026)

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The LXX connection is not directly addressed in the anchor verses, but inferrable evidence is present (SBF-067-Q097). Rom 3:12 cites Ps 14:3 LXX — confirming that chrēstotēs was used in the LXX to render a Hebrew moral-quality term in the goodness domain. This establishes lexical continuity: the LXX chrēstotēs bridges H2896A's moral register (OT) to the NT's chrēstotēs usage. G5543 (chrēstos — good/kind) and G5541 (chrēsteuomai — be kind) in the related-word set suggest a LXX-rooted word family inherited by the NT. For H2896A → Greek: the LXX renders tov primarily as agathos and kalos, providing the lexical bridge for the NT's agathos vocabulary. However, LSJ data was absent for both G0019 and G5544 (NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flags cover both — flagged in quality flags §7). The LXX analysis is therefore only partially supported by the available data. Genuine gap — NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flags on both Greek OWNER terms. (OBS-067-OBS-009, SBF-067-Q097)

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

G0019 (agathōsunē) is almost certainly a NT coinage — not attested in classical Greek or LXX (SBF-067-Q099). Its entire 4-verse NT corpus (Rom 15:14, Gal 5:22, Eph 5:9, 2Th 1:11) is Pauline and pneumatological. The coinage reveals: the NT needed a term for goodness as a named inner-being virtue distinct from the adjective agathos that applies broadly. Agathōsunē says: goodness is now a specific inner-being quality, not merely a predicate applied to things. The development from H2896A's ten-sub-sense breadth to agathōsunē's four-verse precision is pneumatological concentration: goodness in its fullest expression named as a Spirit-enabled virtue. The coinage represents the NT's sharpening of the concept from broad phenomenological range (Hebrew) to named Spirit-fruit virtue (NT). (OBS-067-OBS-009, SBF-067-Q099)

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc from TOV to agathōsunē to chrēstotēs reveals a three-stage development: (1) H2896A (Hebrew breadth) — goodness as a pervasive quality covering the full range of what is genuinely beneficial, right, and satisfying: aesthetic, moral, covenantal, volitional, experiential, evaluative, and ontological. This is goodness at its maximum semantic reach. (2) G0019 (NT precision — inner virtue) — goodness concentrated into a named Spirit-produced virtue: a characterological inner quality of the Spirit-filled person. From 10 sub-senses to one named category. (3) G5544 (NT precision — relational disposition) — goodness concentrated into its relational-dispositional expression: the generous goodwill that characterises both God's stance toward humanity and the Spirit-produced stance of the believer toward others. The arc is: phenomenological breadth (Hebrew) → virtuous precision (NT abstract noun) → relational precision (NT dispositional noun). The complete semantic range encompasses all three stages — they are not replacements but cumulative layers of specification. (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-009, OBS-067-OBS-048, SBF-067-Q099)


## Verse and literary interpretation (T7.2)

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

Mic 6:8 as primary definitional anchor: H2896A (tov — good) functions as the predicate of the divine definition: "He has told you, O man, what is good." Tov here is the predicate nominative of the divine declaration — it names the content of what God has revealed. The term functions as: (1) The criterion of divine requirement ("what does the Lord require of you but..."); (2) The content of revelation ("He has told you what is good"); (3) The implicit standard against which the three-fold expression (justice, kindness, humility) is measured. The primary term in the primary verse is definitionally and revelatorily placed — it is the named object of divine instruction to the human person. (OBS-067-OBS-027, SBF-067-Q042)

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

The anchor verse corpus spans multiple literary forms, each with distinct interpretive requirements: (1) Prophetic address (Mic 6:8; Eze 36:31): legal covenant-speech, including divine accusation and requirement. Mic 6:8 functions as the verdict of a covenant lawsuit (rib) — God's requirement is stated as the standard against which Israel's failure is measured. Requires: attention to covenantal frame; the "requirement" is not a new imposition but a restoration of what was always known. (2) Wisdom sayings (Pro 15:16; 16:32): comparative instruction for individual moral formation. Requires: attention to the rhetorical purpose of persuasion toward wise living; the better-than structure is pedagogical. (3) Psalms of lament and praise (Psa 34:8; 73:28; 34:8 — crisis context; Psa 119:68 — torah psalm): personal address and corporate declaration. Requires: attention to the experiential context from which the declaration arises. (4) NT epistolary (Rom 2:4; 11:22; Gal 5:22; Col 3:12): doctrinal instruction and paraenesis. Requires: attention to the theological argument in which the verse is embedded. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-042, SBF-067-Q042)

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

Three key argument structures: (1) Mic 6:8 — definitional argument: if goodness is defined as X, and X consists of justice + kindness + humility, then goodness is not a simple quality but a tri-fold orientation. The logical structure is definitional-analytical. (2) Rom 2:4 — teleological argument: God's chrēstotēs has a purpose ("meant to lead you to repentance") — the argument is that the purpose of divine goodness is the human's turning. The logical structure is purposive. (3) Rom 11:22 — conditional argument: God's kindness operates under a conditional that requires human continuance ("provided you continue in his kindness"). The logical structure is conditional. These three argument structures (definitional, purposive, conditional) represent three different logical relationships between goodness and other inner-being realities. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-043, OBS-067-OBS-044, SBF-067-Q042)

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

The anchor verse corpus spans a notably broad contextual range: (1) Covenantal-judicial setting (Mic 6:8 — covenant lawsuit; Jos 23:14 — covenant farewell address): the primary human moral goodness verse is set in a judicial-covenantal frame — goodness is the standard of covenant obligation. (2) Liturgical-doxological setting (Psa 34:8; 119:68; doxological refrain): corporate worship and individual prayer as the context for declaring and encountering divine goodness. (3) Wisdom-instructional setting (Pro 15:16; 16:32): educational and formational context for human moral development. (4) Prophetic-restorative setting (Eze 36:31): post-exilic restoration context where goodness is encountered through recognition of its absence. (5) Pauline-pneumatological setting (Gal 5:22; Col 3:12): community formation in the new covenant context. The breadth of contextual settings confirms that goodness is not restricted to a single register of life — it operates across judicial, liturgical, instructional, prophetic, and community contexts. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-042, SBF-067-Q042)

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

Mic 6:8 is the primary anchor for human goodness in its most comprehensive definitional statement: "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?" It functions as: (1) Revelatory — goodness is defined as divinely disclosed, not humanly generated; (2) Definitional — it names the content (justice + kindness + humility); (3) Comprehensive — it covers horizontal (justice, kindness) and vertical (humble walk with God) dimensions simultaneously; (4) Cross-registry — it is the highest-density shared anchor in the programme (6 co-anchoring registries). Psa 119:68 functions as the primary anchor for the divine-human correspondence structure of goodness: "You are good and do good" — the being/doing model at the divine level. (OBS-067-OBS-027, SBF-067-Q042, SBF-067-Q138)

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

Mic 6:8 uniquely reveals: (1) That goodness is divinely revealed and humanly required — "He has told you" positions goodness as knowledge given from God to the human, and "the Lord requires" positions it as covenantal obligation, not optional aspiration. No other verse in the anchor set places goodness simultaneously as revealed knowledge and covenantal requirement. (2) That goodness as a human characteristic is internally structured — justice, kindness, and humble walk are not three separate characteristics but the tri-fold content of what it means to be good. No other verse provides this internal structure. (3) That goodness requires comprehensive orientation — both horizontal (toward others) and vertical (toward God) — simultaneously. Psa 73:28 focuses on the vertical; Rom 15:14 on the horizontal; Mic 6:8 holds both together as constitutive of goodness. This is the verse's unique analytical contribution. (OBS-067-OBS-027, SBF-067-Q042)


## Somatic dimension — body significance, direction, deposit

How (and whether) the characteristic is grounded in the body — whether Scripture links it to specific body parts, the direction of body↔soul interaction, and any constitutional deposit produced over time.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

The "glad of heart" (tov-lev) compound at Est 5:9 uses lev (heart), which in Hebrew serves double duty as both the inner-being centre and the physical organ — though the phrase is clearly functioning at the inner-being level, not the physical cardiac level. No other specific body part is explicitly linked to goodness in the anchor verse evidence. The Psa 34:8 "taste and see" metaphor uses perceptual language (taste, see) that implies sensory or quasi-physical engagement, but this is figurative language for inner perception rather than a literal body-part linkage. The data is thin here. (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

For "glad of heart" (tov-lev, Est 5:9): the heart-compound is emphatic — locating the inner well-being state specifically in the heart gives it a constitutive rather than circumstantial character. For Psa 34:8 ("taste and see"): the perceptual language is experiential and invitational — Scripture is using sensory vocabulary to name a quality of inner encounter that has a quasi-perceptual character. The "tasting" of goodness is not abstract cognition but participatory encounter. Scripture is inviting an embodied-in-principle inner perception — the kind of knowledge that comes from experience rather than inference. Beyond these two instances the somatic evidence is limited. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

For the tov-lev (glad of heart) compound: the direction appears to run soul → expression — the inner well-being state manifests as gladness of heart (Est 5:9: Haman "went out joyful and glad of heart" — the inner state expressed in visible disposition). Psa 34:8 "taste and see" runs in the direction: divine goodness → inner perceptual encounter → inner being transformed. The direction is consistently from the divine or inner source outward/downward to embodied expression — not from the body upward to produce goodness. However, the tov-lev instance could also indicate a feedback loop: inner gladness of heart is both an inner state and expressed in visible comportment. The data is insufficient to fully resolve the directionality. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

If the direction runs soul → body (inner well-being produces gladness visible in comportment, as in Est 5:9), the consequence is that goodness is a deep inner quality that naturally expresses itself outwardly — it cannot be purely internal without visible expression. But the Haman diagnostic also confirms that the outward expression of gladness (tov-lev) can be present without genuine inner goodness-rootedness. This distinction is analytically significant: visible gladness and genuine goodness are not identical, and the body's expression of well-being can be present even in the absence of its genuine source. The directional consequence is that inner goodness properly produces outward expression but outward expression does not guarantee inner goodness. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041)


## Synthesis — what the language reveals


### Intra-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTRA-067-007 | T7** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T7 as a whole reveals a characteristic whose evidential foundation is both exceptionally strong in some dimensions and genuinely constrained in others. Lexically: three OWNER terms across two languages with distinct semantic ranges, a full root-family analysis, a NT coinage (agathōsunē), and a comprehensive vocabulary arc from phenomenological breadth (H2896A) to virtuous precision (agathōsunē) to relational precision (chrēstotēs — Q&A-177). The lexical evidence is rich. Literary: the primary anchor (Mic 6:8) uniquely combines revelatory and covenantal-obligation frames with an internal tri-fold structure that no other verse replicates (Q&A-183); the anchor corpus spans prophetic, wisdom, psalmic, and epistolary forms across multiple contextual settings (Q&A-181). Human science: virtue ethics is the closest parallel framework, with the critical divergence at the source of goodness (divine gift vs natural achievement — Q&A-186). Constraints: NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flags on both Greek OWNER terms limit the LXX analysis (Q&A-175); LSJ data is absent. The evidential foundation is strong at the lexical-semantic and verse-literary levels; it is constrained at the LXX-continuity and human-science elaboration levels. This asymmetry is itself an analytic finding — it identifies where future investigation would most strengthen the registry's evidential base.


### Inter-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTER-067-013 | T1, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1 identified that goodness is complex in structure — the name "goodness" does not capture the semantic breadth of the vocabulary (Q&A-013). T7 confirms and explains this complexity through the vocabulary arc (Q&A-177): the three-stage development (H2896A's phenomenological breadth → agathōsunē's virtuous precision → chrēstotēs's relational precision) corresponds precisely to T1's finding that goodness operates across quality, disposition, condition, and evaluative act (Q&A-016). Each stage of the vocabulary arc covers a different aspect of T1's definitional complexity: H2896A covers quality, condition, and evaluative act; agathōsunē covers the character-quality and virtuous-resolve aspect; chrēstotēs covers the dispositional-relational aspect. T7 thus provides the lexical grounding for T1's multi-modal portrait — the vocabulary complexity matches the definitional complexity.

**SYN-INTER-067-018 | T2, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T2's Spirit-level location finding (Q&A-037-039) — that genuine goodness requires Spirit-level intervention and is not naturally generated from within the person — is confirmed and enriched by T7's vocabulary arc (Q&A-177). The NT coinage of agathōsunē (Q&A-176) as a Spirit-fruit term reflects precisely the T2 finding: the NT needed a new term to name goodness at the Spirit-production level because the existing vocabulary (H2896A, agathos) covered the full semantic range without isolating the Spirit-produced inner virtue. The coinage is the lexical response to a constitutional reality (Spirit-level goodness as distinct from natural goodness). T7's evidential strength in lexical analysis and T2's constitutional depth together explain why the NT created agathōsunē: to name a constitutional phenomenon (Spirit-produced inner virtue at the deepest level of the person) that the existing vocabulary did not isolate with sufficient precision.

**SYN-INTER-067-022 | T3, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T3's finding that goodness has an inherently perceptual character — it must be encountered and tasted, not merely inferred (Q&A-068) — is confirmed and grounded by T7's lexical and literary analysis. The phenomenological root of H2896A (Q&A-168 — begins with concrete experience of what is agreeable and beneficial, rises to moral categories) explains why T3's perceptive faculty is so prominently engaged: the Hebrew root of goodness is itself experiential-perceptual before it is ethical-conceptual. T7's etymological grounding explains the T3 perceptual finding: the vocabulary encodes the experiential mode of knowing goodness that T3's faculty analysis confirms.

**SYN-INTER-067-025 | T4, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T4 confirmed that the God-to-human direction of goodness is initiatory, generous, and sustaining (Q&A-101). T7 confirms that the primary literary form carrying divine goodness (prophetic covenantal address at Mic 6:8, doxological Psalm declarations, Pauline epistolary) all use forms that emphasise divine initiative and human receptivity. T7's literary analysis (Q&A-178-179) reveals that the literary forms carrying goodness are theologically intentional: the covenant lawsuit form (Mic 6:8) establishes divine initiative in defining the good; the invitation form ("taste and see" — Psa 34:8) establishes divine initiative in the encounter; the indicative-imperative structure of Col 3:12 (God has made you chosen → therefore put on kindness) establishes divine prior action before human response. T7's literary analysis confirms T4's relational architecture through the literary forms that carry them: the literature itself models the God-initiates-human-responds structure.

**SYN-INTER-067-027 | T5, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T5 named three transformation mechanisms (encounter, Spirit-infusion, recognition-confrontation) operating across three contextual registers (experiential/worship, pneumatological-community, prophetic/apostolic). T7 confirms that these three mechanisms correspond to three of the four literary forms carrying the anchor verse evidence: the encounter mechanism is carried by Psalms (experiential, invitational literature — Q&A-178-179); the Spirit-infusion mechanism is carried by NT epistolary (pneumatological instruction); the recognition-confrontation mechanism is carried by prophetic literature (covenant lawsuit and moral address). The literary form carries the transformation mechanism appropriate to it. T7's literary analysis (Q&A-179) also confirms T5's formation/sanctification finding (Q&A-135): the "fruit" metaphor of Gal 5:22 is an agricultural-developmental image embedded in a paraenetic (instructional-formative) letter — the literary form confirms that formation is the intent. T7's evidential foundation illuminates why T5's transformation mechanisms are distributed as they are: the mechanisms are embedded in literary forms designed to activate them.
