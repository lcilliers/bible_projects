# Chapter 2 — How It Works

**Registry 067 — Goodness** · *Word Impact Description*

Operations, faculties, constitutional location, relational axis, formative dimension. Drawn from T2 (Constitutional Location), T3 (Inner Faculties), T4 (Relational Interfaces), and T5 (Formative & Developmental).

## Constitutional location and boundaries

Where this characteristic operates within the spirit/soul/heart/mind/body structure.


### T2.1 — Spirit-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

Spirit-level location is confirmed through the pneumatological framework of the NT evidence. Gal 5:22 names agathōsunē and chrēstotēs as "fruit of the Spirit" — the Spirit is the active producing agent and goodness is the fruit produced. The locus of Spirit-production is the human spirit/inner being. 2 Cor 6:6 lists chrēstotēs alongside "the Holy Spirit" in a single phrase, further linking the characteristic's operation to the Spirit's presence. Rom 15:14 ("full of goodness") implies an infilling — a Spirit-level saturation of the inner being with goodness. The Hebrew side does not use "spirit" language directly for tov but the covenantal-renewal context of Eze 36:31 is followed by Eze 36:26–27 (God's Spirit placed within) — goodness recognised and received is structurally connected in the Ezekiel context to Spirit-indwelling. The spirit-level location is confirmed for NT goodness and implied for the covenantal renewal context. (OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-025)

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

For Spirit-produced goodness (885-001, 886-002): yes — the Spirit is the originating agent. For the Hebrew H2896A doxological and covenantal groups (884-001, 884-005): the origin is God himself, whose goodness is encountered and received by the human inner being. The characteristic does not originate in the human spirit unaided — in all cases the origin is external to the person (God's character, God's Spirit, God's word). The human spirit/inner being is the site of reception, not generation. This structural finding — that goodness is not self-generated — is consistent across all 12 modes. (OBS-067-OBS-001, OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-039)

**Prompt 3: What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

Spirit-level location reveals that goodness, in its fullest NT expression, is not a surface behaviour or a cultivated habit — it is a deep constitutional transformation produced at the level where the Spirit operates in the person. The contrast with the fallen condition (Rom 3:12 — complete absence of goodness in the unregenerate inner being) confirms that genuine goodness requires Spirit-level intervention. The NT's pneumatological concentration of goodness (agathōsunē as Pauline coinage, chrēstotēs as Spirit-fruit) signals that the deepest form of goodness is not achieved but infused — it comes from the deepest level of divine engagement with the human inner being. This implies that human attempts to produce goodness without Spirit-engagement will produce the surface form — the Haman problem (Est 5:9) — rather than the genuine article. (OBS-067-OBS-046, OBS-067-OBS-041, OBS-067-OBS-039)


### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?**

Constitutional movement is present and traceable. The primary movement is: Spirit-level origin (Spirit-production, Gal 5:22) → soul-level reception and orientation (Lam 3:25; Psa 73:28 — the soul seeks and is satisfied) → heart-level engagement (knowing in hearts, Jos 23:14; glad of heart, Est 5:9) → outward expression in conduct (Mic 6:8 — doing justice; Rom 15:14 — instructing others). This is a top-down and outward movement: from the Spirit's action in the innermost level, through the soul's orientation and the heart's response, to visible conduct and community expression. The inverse movement (body feeding back to soul) is not strongly evidenced for goodness. The constitutional movement is consistently inward-to-outward. (OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-039)

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?**

The sequence is: (1) Divine source acts (God's goodness, Spirit's production, God's word) → (2) Spirit-level reception in the inner being (Spirit-fruit, infilling with goodness) → (3) Soul reorientation (Psa 73:28 — repositioned toward God; Lam 3:25 — the soul seeking) → (4) Heart engagement (knowing, gladness, moral recognition) → (5) Volitional alignment (choosing good, willing submission, Jer 26:14) → (6) Outward expression (conduct, justice, instruction of others). This is a formation sequence, not an instantaneous transformation. The 2Th 1:11 language ("fulfil every resolve for good") implies that step 5 (volitional resolve) is followed by step 6 (completion by God's power) — the full sequence requires both human resolve and divine completion. (OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, SBF-067-Q031)


### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

Soul-level location is partially evidenced. Jos 23:14 names "heart and soul" as the site of covenantal knowing — "you know in your hearts and souls that not one word has failed of all the good things the Lord your God promised." The reception of God's good covenantal word (884-005) is explicitly a heart-and-soul event. Psa 73:28 names the soul-level experience of nearness to God as the supreme good — the soul's orientation toward God as its reference is the inner-being state of 884-003. Lam 3:25 ("The Lord is good to those who wait for him, to the soul who seeks him") explicitly names the soul as the faculty that seeks and receives God's goodness. The soul-level location is confirmed for the experiential and receptive registers of goodness. (OBS-067-OBS-033, OBS-067-OBS-029, SBF-067-Q009)

**Prompt 2: What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?**

Soul-level location reveals that the experiential register of goodness (884-003 — nearness to God as the supreme good; Lam 3:25 — the soul seeking God's goodness) is not a surface experience but a deep inner-being orientation. The soul as the seat of personal experience and desire is the natural site for goodness-as-experienced-good — the characteristic reaches the person's innermost capacity for orientation, seeking, and satisfaction. Lam 3:25's pairing of "the soul who seeks" with goodness received confirms that the seeking soul and the good God are structurally connected — goodness is what the soul in its deepest orientation is designed to receive. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-033)


### T2.3 — Heart

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

Heart-location is confirmed across multiple instances. Est 5:9 (884-009): "glad of heart" (tov-lev) — the compound phrase explicitly locates the inner well-being state in the heart. Jos 23:14: "you know in your hearts and souls" — the heart is named first as the site of covenantal knowing of God's good word. Eze 36:31: "you will remember your evil ways and your deeds that were not good, and you will loathe yourselves" — the memory and moral self-assessment that precede the recognition of not-goodness are heart-faculty operations. The heart is engaged across goodness in its emotional (glad of heart), cognitive (knowing), and moral-assessment (remembering and recognising not-good) registers. (OBS-067-OBS-034, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?**

Heart-location reveals that goodness engages multiple integrating functions of the heart simultaneously. (1) Knowing — Jos 23:14: the heart's cognitive function receives and holds the knowledge of God's good covenantal word. (2) Feeling — Est 5:9: the heart is the site of the affective gladness-state (tov-lev). (3) Moral awareness — Eze 36:31: the heart's moral self-assessment capacity is activated when not-goodness is recognised; memory and loathing are heart-operations. The heart as the integrating centre of the inner being engages goodness at all three levels — knowing, feeling, and moral awareness — which reflects the breadth of the tov vocabulary itself. (OBS-067-OBS-034, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T2.4 — Mind

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?**

Direct mind-location is not explicitly named in the verse evidence. However, the cognitive register of goodness is evidenced through the dimension assignment: 884-004 (comparative wisdom evaluation) and 884-006 (moral verdict) are assigned Cognition (03), indicating that the mind's evaluative and discerning function is engaged. The comparative wisdom idiom ("better than") is a cognitive-evaluative operation — assessing and ranking goods. This is a mind-faculty operation even if the term "mind" is not explicitly used. Rom 15:14 pairs goodness with "all knowledge" in the same clause — "full of goodness, filled with all knowledge" — suggesting that goodness and cognitive knowledge are related inner-being qualities. The evidence locates goodness at the evaluative level of mind-function, but explicit mind-language is absent. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-025)

**Prompt 2: What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?**

The evaluative and discerning aspect of the mind is what goodness engages. The comparative wisdom mode (884-004) engages the mind's capacity to rank and assess — distinguishing greater goods from lesser goods. The moral verdict mode (884-006) engages the mind's capacity to assess conduct against a standard. The volitional-preference mode (884-008) engages the mind's evaluative role in choice — "what seems good to you" implies an assessment before a decision. This is the mind functioning as moral evaluator — not primarily as rational analyst but as the faculty of moral discernment. The fact that Cognition is a secondary dimension for this characteristic (Q&A-036) confirms the engagement but at a secondary level. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-014, SBF-067-Q088)


### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?**

Lam 3:25 names "the soul who seeks him" — this is a whole-soul seeking orientation, not specifically heart or mind. The soul as the seat of desire and orientation (relevant to the experiential good groups 884-003) functions as a seeking faculty distinct from the cognitive or affective heart-functions identified above. However, the data does not subdivide the soul-subsets with precision beyond heart — the Hebrew anthropological vocabulary in these verses is not granular at the sub-soul level. Conscience is a potential soul-subset engagement: the self-loathing of Eze 36:31 implies a conscience operation (moral inner witness producing conviction), but this is addressed under T3.9 below. No further soul-subset locations are clearly evidenced beyond what is captured at heart and the general soul-seeking orientation. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-033)


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

The "glad of heart" (tov-lev) compound at Est 5:9 uses lev (heart), which in Hebrew serves double duty as both the inner-being centre and the physical organ — though the phrase is clearly functioning at the inner-being level, not the physical cardiac level. No other specific body part is explicitly linked to goodness in the anchor verse evidence. The Psa 34:8 "taste and see" metaphor uses perceptual language (taste, see) that implies sensory or quasi-physical engagement, but this is figurative language for inner perception rather than a literal body-part linkage. The data is thin here. (OBS-067-OBS-007, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

For "glad of heart" (tov-lev, Est 5:9): the heart-compound is emphatic — locating the inner well-being state specifically in the heart gives it a constitutive rather than circumstantial character. For Psa 34:8 ("taste and see"): the perceptual language is experiential and invitational — Scripture is using sensory vocabulary to name a quality of inner encounter that has a quasi-perceptual character. The "tasting" of goodness is not abstract cognition but participatory encounter. Scripture is inviting an embodied-in-principle inner perception — the kind of knowledge that comes from experience rather than inference. Beyond these two instances the somatic evidence is limited. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

For the tov-lev (glad of heart) compound: the direction appears to run soul → expression — the inner well-being state manifests as gladness of heart (Est 5:9: Haman "went out joyful and glad of heart" — the inner state expressed in visible disposition). Psa 34:8 "taste and see" runs in the direction: divine goodness → inner perceptual encounter → inner being transformed. The direction is consistently from the divine or inner source outward/downward to embodied expression — not from the body upward to produce goodness. However, the tov-lev instance could also indicate a feedback loop: inner gladness of heart is both an inner state and expressed in visible comportment. The data is insufficient to fully resolve the directionality. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

If the direction runs soul → body (inner well-being produces gladness visible in comportment, as in Est 5:9), the consequence is that goodness is a deep inner quality that naturally expresses itself outwardly — it cannot be purely internal without visible expression. But the Haman diagnostic also confirms that the outward expression of gladness (tov-lev) can be present without genuine inner goodness-rootedness. This distinction is analytically significant: visible gladness and genuine goodness are not identical, and the body's expression of well-being can be present even in the absence of its genuine source. The directional consequence is that inner goodness properly produces outward expression but outward expression does not guarantee inner goodness. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**Prompt 1: Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

Origin is consistently external to the person — received from God and bestowed through the Spirit. Three origin paths: (1) Divine character as origin — God is good and goodness flows from his character (Psa 119:68; 884-001); (2) Covenantal word as origin — God's good word is spoken and received in the human inner being (Jos 23:14; 884-005); (3) Spirit-production as origin — agathōsunē and chrēstotēs are Spirit-fruit (Gal 5:22; 885-001, 886-002). The key negative evidence: Rom 3:12 names the complete absence of goodness in the unregenerate condition — confirming that goodness is not naturally generated from within. The origin is not generational (no evidence for generational goodness transmission as a constitutional mechanism, though the covenantal promise "to thousands" implies relational continuity). (OBS-067-OBS-001, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-046)

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

The origin is formally singular (God as ultimate source) but operationally multiple in its delivery mechanisms. The ultimate origin is God's own goodness — this is singular and consistent. The delivery mechanisms are: God's character encountered (experiential), God's word received (covenantal), God's Spirit infused (pneumatological). These three mechanisms are not competing origins but three modes through which the singular divine source of goodness reaches the human inner being. This is analytically significant: the programme's 12 modes of goodness all trace back to the same originating source (God's goodness) but through different delivery paths. (OBS-067-OBS-001, OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-039)

**Prompt 3: Does the origin of this characteristic change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?**

The ultimate origin (God's goodness) does not change. What changes is the delivery mechanism and the register of goodness received. In the Hebrew experiential register (884-003), the origin reaches the person through encounter and nearness. In the covenantal register (884-005), through God's word and promise-keeping. In the NT pneumatological register (885-001, 886-002), through the Spirit's production. These represent a progressive clarification and concentration of how goodness is delivered — from encounter and word in the OT to Spirit-production in the NT — but the source is constant. What shifts is not the origin but the mechanism of delivery and the specificity of naming. The NT's pneumatological precision is a clarification, not a change of origin. (OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-099)


## Inner faculties engaged

How this characteristic engages the eleven inner-being faculties: perception, cognition, memory, affect, creativity, volition, agency, moral evaluation, conscience, conscientiousness, and relational capacity.


### T3.1 — Perception

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?**

The perceptive faculty is significantly engaged. Psa 34:8 ("taste and see that the Lord is good") invokes two inner senses simultaneously: taste (a contact-sense implying intimacy and direct experience) and sight (a distance-sense implying recognition and discernment). The use of perceptual language for encountering goodness is deliberate — divine goodness is not known by inference but by experiential perception. The "tasting" of goodness is an inner-perception act: a direct, participatory encounter that produces inner-being knowledge. "Seeing" goodness implies a discernment of its reality. The shared anchor at Psa 34:8 with R49 (discernment) confirms that tasting goodness and exercising discernment are structurally connected (SP-067-007). The perceptive faculty is also engaged in the comparative wisdom mode (884-004): "seeing" which is better requires evaluative perception. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-007)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?**

Goodness deepens and enables the perceptive faculty. Psa 34:8 presents the encounter with divine goodness as sharpening inner perception — those who "take refuge" and "taste" develop a refined capacity to recognise what is genuinely good. The comparative wisdom mode (884-004) requires and develops discernment — the better-than sayings train the evaluative perception to distinguish inner goods from lesser goods. Conversely, the absence or distortion of goodness impairs perception: Mal 2:17 / Jer 44:17 name the inversion of the moral perception faculty as the condition where evil is called good. This implies that goodness-present sharpens perception; goodness-absent or distorted corrupts it. The perceptive faculty and the goodness-characteristic are mutually reinforcing: genuine goodness cultivates perception; accurate perception recognises genuine goodness. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-021, SBF-067-Q065)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The perceptive engagement of goodness reveals that it is not primarily an abstract ethical category but an experiential reality that must be encountered and tasted. Goodness is known through contact, not inference. This is structurally consistent with the Hebrew tov's phenomenological root (what is genuinely pleasant, beneficial, and right — experienced as such). The pattern reveals that goodness has an inherently perceptual character — it calls for encounter, not merely assent. The Haman diagnostic (Est 5:9) confirms this: he experienced a surface perception of well-being without genuine perceptual encounter with goodness as rooted in God. Perception without genuine encounter produces the counterfeit. The nature of goodness is therefore inherently experiential and perceptually demanding. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-041, SBF-067-Q088)


### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?**

Conscientiousness is the integrating faculty that brings together moral awareness, will, and action — and goodness is one of the most comprehensively integrated characteristics in the programme precisely at this level. Mic 6:8 is the clearest evidence: what is good is defined as doing justice (action), loving kindness (dispositional-volitional), and walking humbly with God (orientational) — the tri-fold definition requires the simultaneous engagement of moral awareness (knowing what is good), volition (choosing and loving it), and sustained action (walking, doing). Col 3:12's "put on therefore... kindness" similarly integrates moral awareness (recognition of one's transformed identity), volition (the act of putting on), and action (expressing kindness). Conscientiousness is the faculty that holds these three together in integrated lived expression — and goodness requires and produces this integration. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

Goodness deeply enables and integrates conscientiousness. The Spirit-produced goodness (885-001, 886-002) provides the inner motivational and characterological ground from which conscientious action flows — it is not merely adherence to a standard but an inner quality that produces sustained integrated responsiveness. The six-step constitutional movement identified at Q&A-064 is itself a description of conscientiousness being developed from Spirit-origin through soul, heart, will, and into sustained action. The absent or distorted form impairs conscientiousness: Rom 3:12's "no one does good" names the condition of total disintegration — the absence of goodness produces the collapse of conscientious moral action. (OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-046)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The conscientiousness engagement reveals that goodness is fundamentally an integrating characteristic — it does not operate in isolation of other faculties but draws perception, cognition, memory, affect, volition, and agency together into coherent moral action. Mic 6:8's tri-fold definition is itself a picture of integrated conscientiousness: not a list of independent virtues but three dimensions of a single orientated life. This integrating function distinguishes goodness from characteristics that operate more narrowly — goodness as quality of the whole inner being integrates all faculties into its expression. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-048)


### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?**

Yes — relational capacity is extensively engaged. Chrēstotēs (886-001 and 886-002) is inherently relational — it names the disposition of generous goodwill toward another (divine toward human; Spirit-produced human toward others). The inner quality expressed through chrēstotēs is the disposition to act with goodness toward another — a relational orientation by definition. Mic 6:8 frames goodness as relational in its human expression: doing justice (right action toward others), loving kindness (relational affection and loyalty), walking with God (vertical relational orientation) — all three are relational in direction. Rom 15:14 confirms: goodness-filled persons are "able to instruct one another" — relational capacity as an expression of goodness. Col 3:12 lists chrēstotēs as a "garment" of the person in community — a settled relational disposition within the body. (OBS-067-OBS-017, OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-025)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens relational capacity. The Spirit-produced chrēstotēs (886-002) is itself a relational capacity — it is the inner disposition enabling generous engagement toward others. Rom 15:14's "able to instruct one another" names a relational capacity (mutual edification) produced by goodness. The giving of goodness toward others (Col 3:12 — put on kindness; Mic 6:8 — love kindness and do justice) requires and develops relational capacity. Goodness in its distorted or absent form impairs relational capacity: Rom 3:12 names the unregenerate condition as one of total goodness-absence — which implies relational incapacity for genuine goodness toward others. The Haman instance (Est 5:9) shows how goodness-without-root produces a fragile inner state that makes genuine relational goodness toward others unstable. (OBS-067-OBS-017, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-046)


### T3.2 — Cognition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?**

Yes — cognition is a secondary dimension of goodness (confirmed at Q&A-036). The cognitive engagement takes three forms: (1) Evaluative cognition — the comparative wisdom mode (884-004) requires ranking and assessing goods against each other. "Better than" implies a knowing that orders and ranks. (2) Moral assessment cognition — the not-good verdict mode (884-006) requires applying the standard of the good to conduct and naming the verdict. This is a moral-cognitive act. (3) Receptive cognition — Jos 23:14 ("you know in your hearts and souls") names the reception of covenantal knowing as a cognitive act located in the inner being. The mind knows and holds God's good word as an inner-being cognitive reality. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-033, OBS-067-OBS-034)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens cognition. The comparative wisdom mode actively trains the evaluative intellect — the better-than sayings of 884-004 require and cultivate discerning moral cognition. Rom 15:14 pairs goodness with knowledge in a single characterological portrait: "full of goodness, filled with all knowledge" — suggesting a natural coherence between goodness and cognitive fullness. The absence of goodness impairs cognition: the moral inversion of Mal 2:17 / Jer 44:17 (calling evil good) is a cognitive failure — the evaluative faculty is corrupted. The direction is clear: goodness present deepens moral cognition; goodness absent or distorted impairs it. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-021, OBS-067-OBS-025)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The cognitive engagement reveals that goodness is not ethically naive or merely affective — it requires intellectual content. The wisdom literature's comparative evaluations demand a capacity for moral reasoning. The pairing of goodness with knowledge (Rom 15:14) suggests that genuine goodness has a cognitive substrate — the person who is good knows what is good and why. This is consistent with the Mic 6:8 revelatory frame ("He has told you what is good") — goodness is disclosed knowledge, not merely felt impulse. The pattern further reveals that goodness in its distorted form (calling evil good) specifically corrupts the cognitive faculty — which implies that genuine goodness and genuine cognition are structurally coherent. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-025)


### T3.3 — Memory

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?**

Memory is substantively engaged. Eze 36:31 ("Then you will remember your evil ways, and your deeds that were not good, and you will loathe yourselves") names memory as the faculty through which moral awareness of not-goodness is produced. The sequence is: memory activated → deeds recalled as not-good → self-loathing as moral response. Memory is the trigger for the moral-recognition that precedes transformation. Jos 23:14 also activates memory: "you know in your hearts and souls... that not one word has failed" — the covenantal knowing of God's goodness is held in the inner being through remembered experience of his faithfulness. Goodness engages memory in both its positive and negative registers: remembering God's faithfulness (covenantal knowing) and remembering one's own not-goodness (moral reckoning). (OBS-067-OBS-034, OBS-067-OBS-035)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens memory in its truthful operation. The covenantal knowing of Eze 23:14 (God's faithfulness held in memory) is a deepened form of inner-being knowledge held over time. The moral reckoning of Eze 36:31 (remembering deeds as not-good) is memory enabled by an act of God — the divine work that brings the person to genuine self-recognition includes activating memory truthfully. Conversely, the self-justifying memory of Jer 44:17 ("when we burned incense to the queen of heaven, we had plenty of food and prospered") is an impaired memory — goodness misidentified produces false memory of what the good was. Genuine goodness enables truthful memory; false goodness impairs it. (OBS-067-OBS-034, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-043)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The memory engagement of goodness reveals that it is temporally extended — it works through the inner being's capacity to hold experience over time and draw present moral assessment from it. The Eze 36:31 sequence (remember → recognise as not-good → loathe) shows goodness operating through a temporal moral process, not merely a momentary conviction. The covenantal knowing of Jos 23:14 shows goodness held in memory as a form of inner-being certainty across time. This reveals that goodness has a historical and narrative dimension in the inner being — the person whose inner life is shaped by goodness has a truthful memory of what God has done and a truthful reckoning of their own conduct. (OBS-067-OBS-034, OBS-067-OBS-035)


### T3.4 — Affect

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?**

The affective faculty is substantively engaged across multiple groups. The inner well-being group (884-009) is predominantly affective: tov-lev (glad of heart, Est 5:9) and the associated inner state of gladness, prospering, and well-being are emotional-experiential states. The doxological group (884-001) has an affective dimension: praise and thanksgiving are emotional responses to encountering divine goodness. The self-loathing of Eze 36:31 is a negative affective response to recognition of not-goodness. The trust-response to Psa 34:8 has an affective dimension. The experiential group (884-003) as a whole is oriented toward inner satisfaction and well-being — an affective register of goodness. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-041)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens affect when genuine. Genuine encounter with divine goodness produces trust, praise, and gladness rooted in God — these are authentic affective states produced by the encounter. The comparative wisdom mode also has an affective dimension: the person who genuinely values "better than" outcomes experiences a right ordering of their desires and affections. Distorted goodness impairs affect: Haman's gladness-of-heart (tov-lev, Est 5:9) is genuine affect but immediately destroyed — it is affect without its proper root, which renders it constitutionally fragile. The diagnosis is that goodness properly produces deep, stable, rooted affective states; goodness-mimicry produces shallow, fragile affective states. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041, OBS-067-OBS-029)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The affective engagement pattern reveals that goodness is not a purely cognitive or moral-rational characteristic — it has a genuine affective root. The Haman diagnostic is the sharpest evidence: the inner-well-being state (tov-lev) is a real affective state, not a moral fiction. But its fragility reveals that affect without proper grounding in genuine goodness is structurally unstable. The stable positive affect of Psa 73:28 (the repositioned inner life of Asaph) contrasts with Haman's fragile gladness and reveals that genuine goodness produces affect that is resilient precisely because it is rooted in the enduring goodness of God, not in circumstance. Goodness is therefore affectively generative — it produces genuine, stable, and resilient positive inner states — but only when the goodness is genuine. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T3.6 — Volition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?**

Yes — Volition (04) is a confirmed secondary dimension. The volitional engagement is extensive. 884-008 (40 verses, volitional preference idiom, anchor Jer 26:14) is the single largest group: the "good-in-one's-eyes" / "what seems good to you" formula names goodness as the predicate of volitional choice and evaluative agreement. 885-001 (Spirit-produced goodness as resolve): agathōsunē in 2Th 1:11 is "every resolve for good" — the Spirit enables the will toward goodness. Goodness engages volition both as the criterion (what the will should choose) and as the reconstituted capacity (Spirit-enabled resolve toward goodness). Two groups carry Volition: 884-008 (Hebrew volitional-preference) and 885-001 (Greek Spirit-enabled resolve). (OBS-067-OBS-014, OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?**

Goodness reconstitutes volition rather than bypassing or overriding it. 2Th 1:11 makes this precise: "may God fulfil every resolve for good" — the human resolve is real and operative; God's power fulfils it (does not replace it). The structure is: Spirit-produced goodness produces a genuine resolve in the human will → God's power fulfils that resolve → the human agent acts. The will's capacity is enabled (Spirit-production enables the resolve), the will's interaction with other characteristics is shaped (goodness reconstitutes what the will chooses as its object), and the will operates under the constraint of the good as revealed (Mic 6:8 — "He has told you what is good" sets the constraint within which the will's proper operation is defined). Volition without this reconstitution produces self-direction (Isa 65:2) or wilful refusal (Jer 6:16) — the will's natural operation in the fallen condition is away from the good. (OBS-067-OBS-014, OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, SBF-067-Q040)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The volitional engagement reveals that goodness is not merely a character quality but an active principle that must be chosen and willed. Goodness does not produce compliance or passive absorption — it produces resolve ("every resolve for good," 2Th 1:11) and active choosing ("do what seems good," 884-008). The person who possesses goodness chooses it actively — Col 3:12 frames this as "put on therefore" — a deliberate volitional act enabled by prior transformation. This reveals that goodness has a volitional depth that distinguishes it from passive character states: it is both a quality possessed and a quality actively willed. The reconstitution of volition by goodness means that the good person is not merely compliant but genuinely desirous of the good — the will itself has been reoriented rather than overridden. (OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, SBF-067-Q040)


### T3.7 — Agency

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?**

Agency is engaged and required. Mic 6:8 requires active human doing — "to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly." The goodness defined here is not a state but a practice — it requires the person to initiate and sustain action. The "garment" language of Col 3:12 ("put on therefore... kindness") frames goodness as an active choice to take up — an agentive act. Pro 16:32 ("he who rules his spirit than he who takes a city") names self-governance as an agency act with positive moral value. 2Th 1:11's "every resolve for good" is a person's own resolve — the agentive capacity is intact and engaged. Goodness does not bypass agency; it requires it and enables it. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-040, SBF-067-Q040)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens agency. The reconstitution pattern (Q&A-082): Spirit-produced goodness produces a genuine resolve that the human agent executes, with God's power completing it (2Th 1:11). This is not a reduction of agency but an amplification of it — the Spirit-enabled person has a deeper capacity for agentive goodness than the unregenerate person (whose agency is misdirected toward self-direction, Isa 65:2). Col 3:12's "put on" is an agency act — the Spirit's prior transformation is the enabling ground, and the human agentive choice is the expression. Goodness also provides the criterion for agency's proper exercise (Mic 6:8 — doing justice and loving kindness require active choosing and doing). (OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-027)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The agency engagement reveals that goodness is an active, outward-directed characteristic — it does not rest in inner states but reaches toward expression. The six-step constitutional movement identified at Q&A-064 culminates in outward action (step 6: expression in conduct, justice, instruction). The deepening of agency through goodness confirms that genuine goodness is not quietist or passive — it is an inner quality that demands expression. The contrast with the fallen condition (Rom 3:12: "no one does good, not even one" — agency directed away from the good) confirms that the agency-restoration produced by goodness is a constitutive element of the characteristic's full expression. (OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-046)


### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?**

Moral evaluation is one of the most extensively engaged faculties. The not-good verdict mode (884-006, 22 verses) is itself a sustained moral evaluation activity — the prophetic and wisdom literature applies the standard of the good to conduct and names the verdict. The comparative wisdom mode (884-004) engages moral evaluation in its comparative form — ranking goods hierarchically. Eze 36:31 engages moral evaluation in the person's own self-assessment: the person comes to evaluate their own deeds as not-good. Gen 1:31 (884-007) is a divine moral evaluation — "God saw that it was good." Mic 6:8 presents goodness as the criterion against which conduct is evaluated. Moral evaluation is both engaged by goodness (goodness calibrates the moral evaluation faculty) and expressed through goodness (the good person applies the standard rightly). (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-036, SBF-067-Q065)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens moral evaluation when genuine; distorted goodness specifically impairs it. The enabling function: the standard of the good, as revealed and received (Mic 6:8 — "He has told you what is good"), calibrates the moral evaluation faculty. The person who possesses genuine goodness can accurately assess what is and is not good. The deepening function: the comparative wisdom mode actively trains the evaluative capacity — the better-than sayings develop discernment. The impairing function: Mal 2:17 / Jer 44:17 describe the condition where moral evaluation is inverted — the faculty is functioning but its standard has been corrupted. This is the most severe impairment: the evaluative faculty applied to a false standard produces confident but wrong moral verdicts. (OBS-067-OBS-012, OBS-067-OBS-021, SBF-067-Q065)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The moral evaluation engagement reveals that goodness is inherently normative — it carries a standard against which everything is assessed. The programme's registry is named "goodness" but its data is full of moral verdicts, comparative rankings, and assessments. This confirms that goodness and the moral evaluation faculty are structurally co-dependent: genuine goodness calibrates moral evaluation (goodness is the standard); moral evaluation expresses goodness (the good person judges rightly). The corruption of goodness corrupts moral evaluation — the most severe distortion is not the absence of evaluation but its inversion. This reveals that goodness is not just a positive quality but a normative anchor for the entire moral evaluation architecture of the inner being. (OBS-067-OBS-021, OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-049)


### T3.9 — Conscience

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?**

Conscience is engaged most directly at Eze 36:31: "you will remember your evil ways, and your deeds that were not good, and you will loathe yourselves for your iniquities and your abominations." The self-loathing described is a conscience response — the inner witness of moral failure producing conviction. The sequence (remember → recognise as not-good → loathe) is a conscience sequence: the moral inner witness activates, names the failure, and produces the appropriate inner response (loathing/conviction). Rom 2:4's chrēstotēs "leading to repentance" also implies a conscience engagement — God's kindness acts on the inner being's conscience to produce the movement toward repentance. The goodness-conscience connection operates through the negative register: the recognition of not-goodness activates conscience; the encounter with divine goodness produces the conditions in which conscience can operate truthfully. (OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-049, SBF-067-Q049)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?**

Goodness enables and deepens conscience — specifically through the not-goodness register. The recognition of not-goodness (884-006 and Eze 36:31) is a conscience operation enabled by the standard of the good: only a person who has access to the standard of genuine goodness can accurately recognise their own not-goodness and respond with appropriate conviction. The distortion form (Mal 2:17 / Jer 44:17) shows conscience impaired: when goodness is redefined, the conscience's witness becomes unreliable — it applies the wrong standard and produces false moral comfort. Divine chrēstotēs (Rom 2:4) deepens conscience by making repentance both possible and compelling — God's goodness creates the conditions in which the conscience can function toward its proper end (turning). (OBS-067-OBS-021, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-043)

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The conscience engagement reveals that goodness functions as the calibrating standard for the conscience's operation. Conscience requires a standard against which it measures; goodness provides that standard. This means that the health of the conscience is tied to the health of the goodness-characteristic: a corrupted goodness standard produces a corrupted conscience witness; a genuine goodness standard enables a truthful conscience. The Eze 36:31 sequence — divine action producing moral self-recognition and loathing — suggests that the restoration of truthful conscience requires a prior restoration of the goodness standard. Goodness and conscience are structurally co-dependent: conscience witnesses accurately to the degree that the good it witnesses against is genuine. (OBS-067-OBS-021, OBS-067-OBS-035, SBF-067-Q049)


## Relational interfaces

How this characteristic operates across the relational interfaces of the human person — God-to-human, human-to-God, human-to-human (giving / receiving / boundaries), and the spiritual beings interface.


### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

God-to-human operation is one of the best-evidenced dimensions of goodness across the whole registry. Four distinct modes: (1) Character declaration — God declares himself good and his goodness toward his people (Psa 119:68; 884-001 doxological group). The declaration is itself an act of divine goodness toward the human — it names what the human may trust. (2) Covenantal word and promise — 884-005 (17 verses, anchor Jos 23:14): God's good word given and kept is goodness operating from God toward the people through promise and faithfulness. (3) Creative provision — Gen 1:31 (884-007): God's pronouncement of creation as good is the foundational act of goodness-toward-creation. (4) Pneumatological gift — Gal 5:22 / Rom 15:14: the Spirit produces goodness in the believer — goodness flows from God to the human through the Spirit's infilling. (5) Chrēstotēs as divine disposition — Rom 11:22; Eph 2:7: God's generous goodwill toward humanity is named explicitly and described as displayed supremely in Christ. (OBS-067-OBS-006, OBS-067-OBS-016, OBS-067-OBS-034, SBF-067-Q006)

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

The evidence reveals a structural tension between the unconditional and conditional registers — and this tension is itself the analytical finding. On the unconditional side: Gen 1:31 establishes goodness as a creative-ontological act prior to any human response — God declares creation good without condition. The doxological refrain "for he is good, his steadfast love endures forever" names an enduring, non-contingent character attribute. On the conditional side: Rom 11:22 names a condition on the ongoing experience of divine chrēstotēs — "provided you continue in his kindness. Otherwise you too will be cut off." This is not a condition on God's character but on the human's ongoing reception of its benefits. Rom 2:4 implies responsiveness — God's kindness "is meant to lead you to repentance," with an implied expectation that repentance follows. The resolution: God's goodness as a character attribute is unconditional; the human's ongoing experience of its relational benefits is covenantal-responsive. This distinction is analytically significant and carries to SP-067-018 (the conditionality question). (OBS-067-OBS-006, OBS-067-OBS-043, OBS-067-OBS-044, SP-067-018)

**Prompt 3: What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

God's extension of goodness reveals a disposition of initiatory, generous, and sustaining goodwill toward the human person. Three dimensions: (1) Initiatory — God acts first in goodness (Gen 1:31; Eph 2:7 — "so that in the coming ages he might show the immeasurable riches of his grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus"). The initiative is always divine. (2) Generous — chrēstotēs names not merely adequate goodness but generous goodwill (886-001): God's disposition is one of overflowing benevolence. (3) Sustaining — the covenantal promise (884-005, Jos 23:14: "not one word has failed") names a goodness that holds through time and maintains its commitment. The disposition is not reactive but proactive, generous, and reliable. Lam 3:25 ("the Lord is good to those who wait for him") names waiting as the human posture corresponding to this divine sustaining goodness — the relationship is not passive but involves a human receptive posture. (OBS-067-OBS-006, OBS-067-OBS-033, OBS-067-OBS-034, SBF-067-Q006)


### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

Human-to-God operation is evidenced through three modes: (1) Doxological worship — the liturgical refrain "for he is good, his steadfast love endures forever" (group 884-001) is goodness operating as the content of the human's praise and confession toward God. The human declares God's goodness in worship — this is goodness moving upward from the human toward God as acknowledgement and adoration. (2) Seeking and trust — Psa 34:8 ("blessed is the man who takes refuge in him") and Lam 3:25 ("the soul who seeks him"): the human's movement toward God in trust and seeking is enabled by the encounter with divine goodness. Goodness produces the movement toward God. (3) Covenantal request — Psa 119:68 ("you are good and do good; teach me your statutes"): the human addresses God's goodness as the ground for petition. God's goodness is invoked as the basis of the human's request — "because you are good, teach me." (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-026, OBS-067-OBS-033)

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

Three inner postures are named for human-toward-God movement in goodness: (1) Waiting — Lam 3:25 ("the Lord is good to those who wait for him"). Waiting is the inner posture of active expectation without premature action — a stance of dependence and trust. (2) Seeking — Lam 3:25 ("the soul who seeks him"). Seeking is directional — the soul oriented actively toward God. (3) Trust and refuge — Psa 34:8 ("blessed is the man who takes refuge in him"): refuge-taking is the inner posture of surrender and reliance. These three postures share a common structure: they all involve the person relinquishing self-sufficiency and directing the inner being toward God. They contrast directly with the closing inner orientations (Q&A-032): self-direction, wilful refusal, and departure from faith. The enabling postures are fundamentally un-self-centred; the blocking postures are fundamentally self-centred. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-033, SBF-067-Q014)

**Prompt 3: What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

The human-to-God direction reveals that the relationship is one of responsive dependence grounded in prior divine initiative. The sequence is always: God's goodness is encountered or declared first → the human moves toward God in trust, praise, and seeking → God sustains and fulfils. The human's movement toward God in goodness is not autonomous spiritual achievement but a response enabled by prior encounter with divine goodness. Psa 119:68's "you are good... teach me" names the relationship structure directly: God's goodness is the basis; human receptive orientation follows. The relationship revealed is one of: initiated by God, received by the human, responded to in trust and worship, sustained in covenantal fidelity. The human's part is not passive but it is always derivative — the human's goodness-orientation toward God is itself produced by God's prior goodness toward the human. (OBS-067-OBS-026, OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-001)


### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

Human-to-human giving of goodness is evidenced in four forms: (1) Instruction and edification — Rom 15:14 ("full of goodness... able to instruct one another"): the goodness-filled person extends goodness toward others through teaching and building up. (2) Kindness as relational disposition — Col 3:12 (put on chrēstotēs as garment): the deliberate expression of generosity of spirit toward others in the community. (3) Justice and right action — Mic 6:8 ("to do justice"): goodness extended toward others as the active practice of just treatment. (4) Covenantal kindness — the tov vocabulary in its relational register covers loyalty and steadfast faithfulness in human relationships, paralleling the divine hesed-goodness pattern. Each of these is an extension of inner goodness toward another person. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-039, SBF-067-Q039)

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

Three enabling inner conditions for giving goodness (SBF-067-Q039): (1) Prior Spirit-transformation — Rom 15:14's community goodness is a Spirit-enabled capacity. Col 3:12's "put on" is explicitly grounded in prior identity transformation ("God's chosen ones, holy and beloved") — the giver must first have received a transformation before they can genuinely give. (2) Encounter with and reception of divine goodness — Psa 73:28's inner reorientation produces the overflow toward others in Psa 73's conclusion (telling of God's works). The person who has genuinely encountered divine goodness has a source of goodness to extend. (3) Spirit-filling — Gal 5:22 / Rom 15:14: the Spirit-produced chrēstotēs and agathōsunē are inner capacities the Spirit supplies. Without Spirit-filling, the person lacks the genuine internal resource for extending goodness. Source is transformation; discipline may shape expression but cannot generate the capacity. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-039, SBF-067-Q039)

**Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?**

The evidence requires that the giver must have: (1) Received Spirit-transformation — Rom 3:12 names the unregenerate condition as one of total goodness-absence. The implication is that genuine goodness-giving requires a prior Spirit-regeneration that creates the goodness-capacity. (2) Undergone moral reorientation — Psa 73:28's Asaph has moved from crisis to repositioned inner orientation before he can overflow goodness toward others. The person who has not undergone inner reorientation cannot genuinely give what they do not possess. (3) Been recognised as God's chosen and beloved — Col 3:12 explicitly grounds the "put on kindness" instruction in the prior identity declaration ("God's chosen ones, holy and beloved"). Giving genuine goodness requires the identity transformation that comes from being received by God in grace. This is the structural parallel: human goodness-giving to others requires the same prior divine-goodness-giving to the human that divine goodness-giving to creation requires nothing from creation (Gen 1:31 is unconditional). The human's giving is conditional on having first received. (OBS-067-OBS-039, OBS-067-OBS-046, OBS-067-OBS-029)


### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

Human-from-human receiving of goodness is less directly evidenced than the divine-to-human direction, but is present. Col 3:12 names the community context where chrēstotēs is expressed and received — the "one another" frame of Rom 15:14 implies both giving and receiving. The Mic 6:8 framework implies a community in which justice and kindness are both given and received — the person who does justice and loves kindness creates a relational environment in which others receive goodness. The most explicit receiving is from God — the covenantal reception (Jos 23:14: "you know in your hearts and souls"), the Spirit-reception (Gal 5:22), the encounter reception (Psa 34:8). Human-from-human receiving is implied by the community structure but not directly named in individual anchor verses. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-027, SBF-067-Q004)

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

The blocking conditions identified at Q&A-032 (self-direction, wilful refusal, moral redefinition, departure from faith) are stated primarily in the divine-to-human direction but logically apply also to human-to-human goodness reception: the self-directed person who refuses the good way (Jer 6:16) would resist receiving goodness from a human source just as from a divine one. The enabling conditions from Q&A-031 (nearness to God, fear of the Lord, Spirit-filling) apply most directly to divine reception. For human-to-human reception specifically: the data does not give a direct verse naming what enables or blocks receiving goodness from another person. This is a genuine partial gap — the blocking conditions are extrapolatable from the divine-direction evidence, but explicit verse evidence for the human-to-human receiving conditions is absent. (OBS-067-OBS-028, OBS-067-OBS-043)

**Prompt 3: What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

The verse evidence addresses non-reception primarily in the divine-to-human direction. Jer 6:16 ("we will not walk in it") is the clearest picture: the inner state is one of offered-and-refused goodness — active resistance of what is genuinely good. The will has been engaged and has chosen refusal. The person's inner state is: morally informed (they have encountered the good way), volitionally closed (they have refused it), and unaware of the full consequence of their refusal. For the human-to-human non-reception context specifically: the data is silent on the inner state of a person who encounters human goodness and refuses it. The Haman narrative (Est 5:9) names the inner state of the person who misidentifies goodness — but this is self-deception rather than active refusal of another's goodness. This is a partial gap. (OBS-067-OBS-040, OBS-067-OBS-043)


### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**Prompt 1: Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

The Col 3:12 context places chrēstotēs within the covenant community — the garment metaphor implies a sustained relational environment (the body of Christ) where goodness is the ongoing character of the relational field. Rom 15:14 is similarly community-internal ("my brothers"). These instances suggest that goodness in its NT expression operates primarily within existing relational bonds. Whether goodness operates differently across these contexts is not directly addressed. (OBS-067-OBS-027, OBS-067-OBS-017)

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

The evidence supports both covenantal and cross-covenantal operation. Within covenantal contexts: 884-005 (God's covenantal word, Jos 23:14), 886-001 and 886-002 (Spirit-produced goodness in the new covenant community). These are explicitly covenantal. Cross-covenantal: H2896A in 884-002's moral character register applies to human moral life broadly — the prophetic verdict on not-good conduct (884-006) is addressed to covenant people but the ethical standard (Mic 6:8) has a universal logic. The creation-goodness of Gen 1:31 (884-007) is pre-covenantal — God's goodness extends to all creation before any covenant is formed. Rom 2:4 ("God's kindness leads you to repentance") is addressed to those outside the covenant community as well as within — the mechanism of divine chrēstotēs is not restricted to the covenant. **Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

The relational scope of goodness is wide but not fully mapped in the data. Inclusion evidenced: creation as a whole (Gen 1:31 — all that God made is good); covenant community (Jos 23:14; Col 3:12; Rom 15:14); those outside the covenant who receive divine chrēstotēs leading to repentance (Rom 2:4); the soul who seeks (Lam 3:25 — the seeking person, without boundary). Possible exclusion: Rom 11:22 names "those who have fallen" as the recipients of severity rather than chrēstotēs — but this is presented as a relational condition rather than a categorical exclusion. The data does not name any class of persons categorically excluded from the scope of divine goodness; the blocking conditions (Q&A-032) describe inner orientations that close reception, not divine decisions to exclude. Whether the human expression of goodness has specific scope limits is not directly addressed. Partial gap. (OBS-067-OBS-006, OBS-067-OBS-043, OBS-067-OBS-044)

## Formative and developmental dimension

How this characteristic shapes the person over time — nature of transformation, sequence of inner states, mechanism of change, suffering and affliction, formation and sanctification, eschatological trajectory, deposit consequence.


### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

Both forms of transformation are present and distinguishable (SBF-067-Q031). (1) Orientation change — Psa 73:28: Asaph's inner life transformed not by changed external circumstances but by a shift in what he orients toward. "It is good to be near God" is the repositioned orientation — his condition (circumstances, opponents) has not changed but his inner orientation has. (2) Character transformation — Spirit-production (Gal 5:22; Rom 15:14): the Spirit changes the inner character itself, not merely its orientation. The person "full of goodness" (Rom 15:14) has undergone a character-level transformation — goodness as an inner quality has been established. (3) Moral awareness transformation — Eze 36:31: the movement from not seeing deeds as not-good to recognising and loathing them is a condition change — from moral blindness to moral sight. This is a change of inner state (the perceptual-moral condition) that precedes orientation change. All three transformation forms are present. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-039, SBF-067-Q031)

**Prompt 2: Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

The evidence raises reversibility as a concern without fully resolving it. Rom 11:22 names a conditional dimension: "provided you continue in his kindness. Otherwise you too will be cut off." The use of "cut off" implies that the relational experience of divine goodness can be lost — which implies some form of reversibility in the human's receipt of goodness's benefits. However, this addresses the ongoing experience of chrēstotēs rather than the Spirit-level character transformation. Whether Spirit-produced goodness (agathōsunē and chrēstotēs as fruit) can be reversed is not directly addressed. The distinction between the character transformation (Spirit-produced inner quality) and the relational experience (ongoing receipt of divine chrēstotēs) is analytically important for this question, and the data does not fully resolve it. This is a genuine partial gap — formal vs genuine receipt distinction is not drawn in the evidence. (OBS-067-OBS-043, OBS-067-OBS-044, SP-067-018)


### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

Two distinct sequences are named. Sequence 1 — positive transformation arc (divine goodness → reception → reorientation): (a) Encounter with divine goodness — Psa 34:8 ("taste and see"); (b) Trust and refuge — "blessed is the man who takes refuge in him"; (c) Stable inner orientation — Psa 73:28 ("it is good to be near God; I have made the Lord God my refuge"); (d) Overflow toward others — "that I may tell of all your works" (Psa 73:28 conclusion). Sequence 2 — moral awareness arc (recognition → repentance → transformation): (a) Memory of deeds — Eze 36:31 ("you will remember your evil ways"); (b) Recognition as not-good — "and your deeds that were not good"; (c) Self-loathing — "you will loathe yourselves"; (d) Movement toward repentance — implied by context (Eze 36:26 God's Spirit given follows 36:31's loathing). Rom 2:4 names the divine chrēstotēs → repentance sequence directly. Both sequences are coherent and traceable in the verse evidence. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-029, SBF-067-Q031)

**Prompt 2: What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

The two sequences reveal that goodness works through different mechanisms depending on its register. The positive arc (encounter → trust → reorientation → overflow) reveals that goodness works by producing a progressive deepening of inner orientation — encounter leads to trust, trust leads to stable orientation, orientation leads to outward expression. The characteristic builds from first contact to sustained character. The moral awareness arc (memory → recognition → loathing → repentance) reveals that goodness works through the confrontation with its own absence — the standard of the good, when encountered, produces awareness of not-goodness that is itself a mechanism of transformation. Goodness therefore works both through positive encounter (receiving divine goodness) and through negative recognition (confronting one's absence of goodness). The sequence also reveals the role of time: both arcs are extended through time, not instantaneous — goodness is a formative characteristic, not a momentary experience. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-035)


### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**Prompt 1: What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

Three distinct mechanisms are evidenced, corresponding to the three transformation types identified at Q&A-123: (1) Encounter mechanism — Psa 34:8 / Psa 73:28: the mechanism is perceptual encounter with divine goodness. The change is produced by tasting and seeing — a direct experiential contact that reorients the inner being. This is neither discipline nor gradual formation but encounter-transformation. (2) Spirit-infusion mechanism — Gal 5:22 / Rom 15:14: the Spirit fills the person with goodness. The mechanism is pneumatological infusion — goodness as fruit is the Spirit's work in the inner being. This is not achieved by effort but received as Spirit-gift. (3) Recognition-confrontation mechanism — Eze 36:31 / Rom 2:4: the mechanism is confrontation with one's own not-goodness in the presence of the divine goodness standard. Memory + recognition + loathing = the mechanism of moral transformation toward goodness. All three mechanisms are present; they are not competing but operate in different registers and at different moments. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-039, SBF-067-Q031)

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

Yes — the mechanisms are context-differentiated. The encounter mechanism operates in experiential and worship contexts (Psalms — crisis, praise, seeking). The Spirit-infusion mechanism operates in the pneumatological-community context (NT epistolary — Gal, Eph, Rom, Col). The recognition-confrontation mechanism operates in prophetic and apostolic contexts that address moral failure (Eze, Lam, Rom 2). The differentiation is not random — each mechanism is appropriate to its context: the seeking soul receives through encounter; the redeemed community receives through Spirit; the morally blind person is reached through recognition-confrontation. The mechanisms together cover the full range of contexts in which goodness produces transformation. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-035, OBS-067-OBS-039)


### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

Yes — the goodness-suffering connection is evidenced in two registers (SBF-067-Q048): (1) Divine goodness sustaining in affliction — Lam 3:25 ("the Lord is good to those who wait for him, to the soul who seeks him") is composed in the context of Lamentations — the most concentrated suffering in the Hebrew canon. God's goodness is named as accessible and sustaining precisely in the condition of profound affliction. Suffering does not negate goodness; it becomes the context in which waiting for God's goodness is practised and discovered. (2) Divine goodness available in crisis — Psa 34:8 ("taste and see that the Lord is good") is written by David in the context of his distress — Psalm 34 is a crisis psalm. The invitation to taste goodness is issued from within suffering. The goodness-suffering connection in R67 is not incidental — it is structurally embedded in the anchor verse contexts. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-033, SBF-067-Q048)

**Prompt 2: Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

The evidence supports all four modes but with different emphases. Deepening: Psa 73:28 — Asaph's suffering (crisis of doubt, seeing the prosperity of the wicked, Psa 73:1–16) precedes and produces his reorientation toward God as the supreme good. The suffering is the condition that makes the goodness of nearness to God visible by contrast. The characteristic is deepened by the suffering — Asaph discovers what is truly good precisely through the crisis. Revealing: suffering reveals that goodness rooted in God is resistant to circumstantial disruption (Psa 73:28 — stable inner orientation despite unchanged circumstances); it also reveals the fragility of goodness not so rooted (Est 5:9 — Haman's gladness destroyed instantly). Testing: Lam 3:25 — waiting for God's goodness in the context of catastrophic national suffering is a test of whether the person's confidence in goodness is genuine. Producing: the Eze 36:31 sequence (suffering of exile → moral reckoning → repentance) suggests that suffering can be the context in which goodness is ultimately produced through the confrontation with its own absence. (OBS-067-OBS-029, OBS-067-OBS-033, OBS-067-OBS-041)


### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

Yes — the formation arc is evidenced in the NT evidence. Col 3:12–14 places the "putting on" of chrēstotēs within a longer paraenetic arc: the person has already received new identity transformation ("God's chosen ones, holy and beloved"); is now to clothe themselves with the character qualities that express this identity (3:12); culminating in love that binds everything together in perfect harmony (3:14). This is a formation sequence — the Spirit's prior work, the intentional character-expression, and the sustained orientation toward love as the integrating whole. Gal 5:22–23 similarly places goodness within the fruit of the Spirit — a cluster of nine character qualities that are the full expression of Spirit-formed character. The "fruit" metaphor implies organic growth over time — formation, not instantaneous achievement. (OBS-067-OBS-017, OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-039)

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?**

Goodness plays a dual role in the formation arc: (1) As a formation product — goodness is the result of the Spirit's sanctifying work in the person. It is formed in the person through encounter, Spirit-infusion, and sustained character development (Gal 5:22; Col 3:12). In this sense goodness is the goal of formation — what a Spirit-transformed person becomes. (2) As a formation agent — goodness in the person contributes to the formation of others. Rom 15:14 names persons "full of goodness" as those "able to instruct one another" — goodness enables the person to participate in the formation of the community. The good person becomes a formative resource for others. This dual role — goodness as formation product and formation agent — is a distinctive structural finding for this characteristic. (OBS-067-OBS-025, OBS-067-OBS-039, SBF-067-Q039)


### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?**

Partial eschatological evidence is present. Eph 2:7 ("so that in the coming ages he might show the immeasurable riches of his grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus") explicitly places chrēstotēs in a future-oriented eschatological frame — God's chrēstotēs will be displayed in its fullness in the coming ages. This names an eschatological trajectory: present experience of divine kindness anticipates its future unrestricted display. 2Th 1:11 ("fulfil every resolve for good") implies a completion — God's completing act brings present goodness-resolve to its fullness. However, the specifically eschatological texture of goodness (what it will be in the new creation, what fullness means) is not directly described in the anchor verses beyond these two instances. The trajectory is indicated but not fully developed. (OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-016, SBF-067-Q006)

**Prompt 2: What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate about its future fullness?**

Eph 2:7 implies that present experience of chrēstotēs is a foretaste of the "immeasurable riches" of its future expression — present goodness is real but partial relative to its coming fullness. The "immeasurable" qualifier suggests that the eschatological form of goodness exceeds present experience in scale and richness. 2Th 1:11's "fulfil every resolve for good" implies that present resolves for goodness, even when genuine, are not yet complete — their completion is a divine eschatological act. The present experience of goodness therefore anticipates: (1) A fullness of divine kindness-display that surpasses present experience; (2) A completion of human goodness-resolve that requires divine power to finish. The gap is that the data does not describe what the experiential content of eschatological goodness will be — only that it will be fuller and completed. (OBS-067-OBS-008, OBS-067-OBS-016)


## Synthesis — what T2 through T5 reveal as a whole


### Intra-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTRA-067-002 | T2** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T2 as a whole reveals that goodness has a comprehensive multi-level constitutional presence — from Spirit-level origin through soul-orientation, heart-engagement, and mind-evaluative functions to outward expression — but with a marked asymmetry in evidence quality. Spirit-level (Q&A-037-039), soul-level (Q&A-040-041), and heart-level (Q&A-043-044) are all substantively evidenced. Mind-level is partially evidenced through cognitive dimensions. Body-level is thin — the gladness-of-heart compound (tov-lev) and the taste-and-see perceptual metaphor are the somatic markers, but no constitutional body deposit is evidenced (T2.8 silent). The most structurally significant T2 finding is the six-step constitutional movement (Q&A-064): Spirit-level origin → soul reorientation → heart engagement → volitional alignment → agentive resolve → outward expression. This movement is top-down and outward, with no evidenced reversal from body to inner being. Goodness is constitutionally deep but does not leave a somatic deposit.

**SYN-INTRA-067-003 | T3** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T3 reveals that goodness is the most comprehensively faculty-engaging characteristic in the programme's current analytical record. Every faculty except creativity (T3.5 — silence confirmed) shows substantive engagement: perception (tasting and seeing goodness), cognition (moral evaluation and ranking), memory (covenantal knowing and moral reckoning), affect (stable rooted gladness vs fragile surface gladness), volition (reconstituted toward the good), agency (active expression required and enabled), moral evaluation (goodness as the normative anchor of the evaluative faculty), conscience (goodness calibrates the conscience's standard), conscientiousness (integration of all faculties into sustained moral expression), and relational capacity (dual-directional overflow). The tier as a whole reveals that goodness is not a specialist characteristic — it is an integrating one. The person who possesses genuine goodness has every faculty engaged and aligned. This comprehensive faculty-engagement is what distinguishes genuine goodness from its surface forms: surface goodness (the Haman problem) engages affect without engaging conscience or volitional alignment; genuine goodness integrates all.

**SYN-INTRA-067-004 | T4** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T4 as a whole reveals that goodness operates across all relational directions simultaneously — God-to-human, human-to-God, and human-to-human — but with a fundamental asymmetry: the God-to-human direction is prior, generative, and abundant in evidence; the human-to-God direction is responsive and derivative; the human-to-human direction is overflow from prior reception. This is not a simple tripartite scheme but a structured cascade: God's goodness initiates and flows toward the human person (Q&A-099-101); the human responds in trust, seeking, and worship (Q&A-103-105); the goodness received overflows toward others through instruction, justice, and kindness (Q&A-107-109). The most structurally significant T4 finding is the unconditional/conditional distinction (Q&A-100): God's goodness as a character attribute is unconditional; the human's ongoing experience of its relational benefits is conditional on continued trust. This distinction has direct implications for how goodness is taught, received, and sustained. The spiritual beings interface is largely silent — the relational architecture of goodness is thoroughly human-divine in orientation.

**SYN-INTRA-067-005 | T5** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T5 as a whole reveals that goodness is one of the most developmentally rich characteristics in the programme. Three transformation types (condition change, orientation change, character transformation — Q&A-123), two distinct transformation sequences (positive encounter arc and moral awareness arc — Q&A-126), and three context-differentiated transformation mechanisms (encounter, Spirit-infusion, recognition-confrontation — Q&A-129) together produce a portrait of a characteristic that reaches the person through multiple developmental pathways depending on their condition and context. The formation/sanctification section confirms that goodness plays a dual developmental role: it is a product of formation (Spirit-formed character) and an agent of formation (the good person enables the formation of others — Q&A-136). The relationship to suffering deepens this: suffering is not incidental but a context in which goodness is deepened, revealed, tested, and even produced through confrontation with its absence (Q&A-133). T5.7 closes formally — no somatic deposit evidenced. The eschatological trajectory is present but underdeveloped in the data (Q&A-138-139).


### Inter-tier syntheses
