# Chapter 1 — Meaning

**Registry 117 — Peace** · *Word Characteristic Summary*

---

## What it is

> Peace is the condition of wholeness, right relationship, and rest — the Hebrew shalom covering far more than the absence of conflict. It means completeness, prosperity, welfare, the flourishing of everything and everyone in their proper place. It is used of the end of hostilities, of the quality of a life well-lived, of the relationship between a person and God when that relationship is restored. The New Testament declares that Christ himself is our peace: through him the fundamental rupture between humanity and God is healed. Peace is the signature of the kingdom.


**Working description:** The negative definition is richly evidenced: (1) Peace is not the absence of war alone — the shalom vocabulary covers inner states that have nothing to do with military conflict (Isa 26:3, Psa 4:8, Lam 3:17); (2) Peace is not self-generated security — the false shalom pattern (2509-002, Deu 29:19) shows that self-claimed peace without covenant ground is its precise corruption; (3) Peace is not available to the wicked — Isa 48:22, 57:21 ("there is no peace for the wicked"); the moral character of the person determines whether shalom is accessible; (4) Peace is not silence or passivity — the silence vocabulary (H2790B, H2814, G4623) is distinct from shalom; silence is a condition that enables or expresses peace but is not peace itself; (5) Peace is not complacent ease — sha'anan (H7600 group 413-001) names self-generated ease as a vice (Amo 6:1: "woe to those who are at ease in Zion"), demonstrating that ease without divine ground is not shalom. The negative boundary: peace is not a condition the person achieves by themselves, claims without God's ground, or inhabits while in moral disorder.


## Name and naming

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

The primary Hebrew name is shalom (sha.lom, H7965G and sub-forms) — the most semantically wide term in the OT inner-being vocabulary. Its breadth is encoded in the STEP gloss: completeness, soundness, welfare, peace, quiet, tranquillity, contentment, friendship, covenant peace, peace from war (OBS-117-009). The name itself reveals that peace is not a simple state but a structural condition of completeness — everything in its proper place. Secondary Hebrew names: nachat (na.chat — quietness/quietude, H5183A), shaqat (sha.qat — to quiet/be quiet, H8252), nuach (nu.ach — rest, H5117), menuchah (me.nu.chah — resting place, H4496H). The primary Greek name is eirēnē (G1515) — which in the LXX became the translation word for shalom, absorbing its full range. The sub-names (YHWH-Shalom: "LORD is peace"; Prince of Peace: sar shalom) reveal that peace is not merely a human condition but a divine identity: the name carries what the named one IS.

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

Strong continuity. The LXX translates shalom with eirēnē throughout the Hebrew Bible, establishing the semantic correspondence as the foundational datum of the NT peace vocabulary. The NT eirēnē carries the full shalom range: (1) Inner felt state (Phili 4:7 — "the peace of God... will guard your hearts and your minds," corresponding to shalom 2508-001); (2) Covenantal gift (Rom 5:1 — "peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ," corresponding to 2508-002); (3) Moral orientation (Eirēneuō, G1514 — "live peaceably with all," corresponding to 2508-003); (4) Messianic embodiment (Eph 2:14 — "he himself is our peace," corresponding to 2508-004). The correspondence is not merely semantic but structural — the NT's peace vocabulary is the OT's shalom vocabulary transposed into the key of Christ. No significant discontinuity; the NT deepens rather than revises.


## Kind

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

The core inner-being essence of shalom is integrative wholeness — the condition of the whole person (body, soul, mind, relationships, and orientation toward God) in which everything is in its proper place and functioning rightly. This is not a narrow emotional state but an existential condition. The data establishes this through convergence: (1) The etymology of shalom (completeness, soundness — OBS-117-009) names the absence of fragmentation or deficiency; (2) Lam 3:17 (OBS-117-144) — "my soul is bereft of peace; I have forgotten what happiness is" — shows that shalom-deprivation is total inner destitution, not merely sadness; (3) Jer 29:11 (OBS-117-149) — "welfare... future... hope" — the three outcomes of God's sha.lom-intention show peace as the condition that makes flourishing possible; (4) Isa 26:3 (OBS-117-144) — perfect peace for the mind stayed on God — shows that shalom is the integrative outcome of right orientation. The essence is best stated: peace is the condition of integrative wholeness in which the person is complete, secure, and in right relation — capable of their full creaturely purpose.

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

All four, in structured combination: (1) State: shalom as the inner condition of the person (2508-001 — inner peace as felt experience; 2509-001 — welfare as existential condition); (2) Act: shalom as active orientation and pursuit (2508-003 — "I am for peace"; Psa 34:14 — "seek peace and pursue it"; G1514 eirēneuō — actively being at peace with others); (3) Quality: shalom as the stable character quality of the peaceable person (Mal 2:6 — walked in peace and uprightness; G1516 eirēnikos — peaceableness as quality of heavenly wisdom; G2272 — gentle and quiet spirit as character); (4) Relationship: shalom as the condition of the God-human relationship (2508-002 — covenant peace; G1517 — making peace through the blood of Christ; Rom 5:1 — peace with God). The evidence does not privilege any one of these over the others — they are structurally interdependent. The relational dimension (covenant) is the ground; the state dimension (felt condition) is the fruit; the quality dimension (character) is the formed expression; the act dimension (pursuit, peacemaking) is the volitional engagement.


## Boundary — what it is not

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

Peace vs. rest (nuach, menuchah): rest is the condition of cessation from striving, the physical and inner stopping; peace is the positive condition of wholeness that rest may produce or accompany. The two overlap heavily (nuach and shalom co-occur throughout the corpus) but are distinguishable: nuach can be refused by God as judgment (Deu 28:65 — no resting-place); shalom may persist even in movement and striving. Peace vs. joy (H7600/G1515 vs. R97): joy is the positive emotional response; peace is the positive existential condition that may produce joy but is distinct from it. Psa 35:27 (the Lord delights in welfare of his servant) — joy is the emotional response to shalom-welfare, not the same thing. Peace vs. trust (R163): trust is the inner act of reliance on God; peace is the inner condition that trust produces (Isa 26:3 — he trusts in you → perfect peace; Psa 4:8 — you alone make me dwell in safety → I lie down in peace). Trust precedes peace; peace is trust's outcome. Gap: the boundary between peace and righteousness (tsedaqah) is particularly important but incompletely addressed in Stage 2a — Isa 32:17 ("the effect of righteousness will be peace") makes peace the fruit of righteousness, but whether they are distinct or overlapping characteristics requires Session D analysis.

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

Yes — and this is among the most analytically significant features of the peace registry. The false-peace pattern (2508-003 and 2509-002) shows that shalom is precisely the characteristic that can be falsely claimed: (1) False prophecy of shalom: Jer 6:14, 8:11, Eze 13:10, 13:16, Mic 3:5 — prophets cry "Peace, peace" when there is no peace (prior session, incorporated as source material); (2) Self-deceptive shalom: Deu 29:19 (2509-002, OBS-117-150) — "I shall be safe, though I walk in the stubbornness of my heart"; (3) Complacent ease (sha'anan, H7600 group 413-001) — the comfortable feel of worldly security mistaken for shalom; (4) Speaking shalom with evil in the heart: Psa 28:3, Jer 9:8 — "speaks peace to his neighbor while evil is in his heart." The implication: shalom is the most counterfeitable of inner-being characteristics because its absence-of-distress phenomenology can be replicated by complacency, self-deception, or false prophecy. Authentic shalom requires its proper ground (God, covenant, trust) while false shalom is the same phenomenological condition without that ground.

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

Yes — the data shows at least four levels: (1) Shalom shalom (Isa 26:3 — perfect/complete peace, OBS-117-144) — the doubled word signals the superlative form, the fullest inner peace available to the trust-directed mind; (2) Great peace (Psa 119:165 — "great peace have those who love your law," OBS-117-144) — abundant but not doubled; (3) Present peace — the ordinary condition of shalom available to the covenant person (Psa 4:8, Num 6:26); (4) Absent peace — the condition of the bereft soul (Lam 3:17, Jer 30:5, Eze 7:25). Additionally: eschatological peace (Isa 9:7 — "no end to his peace") represents a fullness beyond present experience. The data does not articulate a formal scale but the textual evidence implies progression from absent through ordinary through great to perfect. The doubled shalom (Isa 26:3) and the "no end" formulation (Isa 9:7) mark the extreme ends of the spectrum.


## Modes of operation

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

The data shows a rich cognate field. Hebrew near-synonyms functioning alongside shalom: (1) Nuach/menuchah (rest, resting-place) — the cessation-dimension of peace; (2) Nachat (H5183A — quietness) — the inner stillness dimension, co-occurs with shaqat in Isa 30:15 at the analytical summit; (3) Shaqat (H8252 — quiet/quieted) — the active quieting, most prominent in its paired form "quietness and trust" (Isa 30:15); (4) Sha'anan (H7600 — at ease) — the ease dimension, positively (Isa 32:18) and negatively (Amo 6:1); (5) Demamah (H1827 — profound silence) — the threshold silence before divine speech. Greek near-synonyms: eirēnē (G1515) is not a synonym but the translation equivalent; hēsuchia/hēsuchios (G2271/G2272) — quietness, the NT equivalent of the Hebrew silence vocabulary (1Pe 3:4 — gentle and quiet spirit); anesis (G0425) — relief/rest from pressure. The cognate field reveals that peace has an inner-face (the silence/stillness vocabulary) and an outer-face (the welfare/wholeness vocabulary), and shalom is the integrative term that spans both.

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

The data provides explicit antonyms: (1) War (milchamah) — Psa 120:7 ("I am for peace, but when I speak, they are for war"); the most direct antonym in the moral-orientation register; (2) Evil (ra'ah) — Jer 29:11 ("plans for welfare and not for evil"); the antonym in the divine-intention register; (3) Calamity/disaster — Isa 45:7 ("I make well-being and create calamity"); the antonym in the creational register; (4) Terror/panic — Jer 30:5 ("a cry of panic, of terror, and no peace"); the experiential antonym; (5) Stubbornness/iniquity — Deu 29:19, Mal 2:6 ("turned many from iniquity"); moral disorder as peace's contrary; (6) Anguish — Eze 7:25 ("when anguish comes they will seek peace, but there shall be none"). The antonym profile confirms that peace is not a neutral absence of distress but a positive condition with multiple contraries — war (relational), evil (moral), calamity (existential), terror (experiential), stubbornness (dispositional).

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

Yes — three paradoxes are documented: (1) Peace through suffering: Isa 53:5 ("the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed") — peace is purchased through suffering, not despite it (OBS-117-147). The mechanism of peace is its opposite (pain, punishment); (2) Peace through bitterness: Isa 38:17 ("it was for my welfare [shalom] that I had great bitterness") — Hezekiah's retrospective: his suffering was the path to his welfare. The paradox: shalom-welfare arrived via its experiential opposite; (3) Complacent ease as false peace: H7600 group 413-001 (Amo 6:1) and group 413-002 (Isa 32:18) — the same experiential condition of ease is either vice (self-generated) or blessing (divinely given). Peace and its counterfeit may be phenomenologically indistinguishable. The paradoxes converge on a single insight: authentic shalom is not produced by the absence of suffering or difficulty but by the presence of the peace-giver. Its opposite conditions (suffering, bitterness, difficulty) can be the means by which it arrives.


## Immediate response

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

The immediate responses to shalom's presence documented in the data: (1) Rest and sleep — Psa 4:8 ("in peace I will both lie down and sleep"); shalom immediately enables the cessation of vigilance; (2) Absence of fear — Judg 6:23 ("Peace be to you. Do not fear") — OBS-117-144; the divine peace immediately displaces terror; (3) Gladness and praise — Psa 35:27 ("shout for joy and be glad... Great is the Lord, who delights in the welfare of his servant") — OBS-117-149; shalom's presence triggers praise; (4) Heart-joining — 1Ch 12:17 ("my heart will be joined to you") — OBS-117-151; shalom's presence in friendship produces heart-union; (5) Daniel's strengthening — Dan 10:19 ("peace be with you; be strong and of good courage") — the angelic peace immediately produces inner strength. The pattern: shalom's immediate effect is the displacement of its contraries (fear, vigilance, inner fragmentation) and the enabling of their alternatives (rest, praise, courage, heart-union).

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

Documented immediate responses to shalom's absence: (1) Soul bereft — Lam 3:17 ("my soul is bereft of peace; I have forgotten what happiness is") — total inner destitution, loss of access to happiness itself; (2) Bones wasting — Psa 32:3 (prior session: "when I kept silent, my bones wasted away through my groaning all day long") — somatic deterioration; (3) Trembling heart — Deu 28:65 ("a trembling heart and failing eyes and a languishing soul") — the triple somatic-inner expression of peacelessness in judgment; (4) Anguish seeking — Eze 7:25 ("when anguish comes they will seek peace, but there shall be none"); (5) Job's triple negation — Job 3:26 ("I am not at ease, nor am I quiet... I have no rest, but trouble comes"). The withdrawal of shalom is experienced as total inner collapse — not discomfort but destitution. The soul's access to its own capacity for happiness is severed (Lam 3:17).

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

Yes — the refusal of peace pattern is one of the most analytically significant in the registry. The data shows peace offered and refused: (1) Isa 28:12 ("This is rest, give rest to the weary... but they would not hear") — rest/peace offered and refused by Israel; (2) Jer 6:16 ("stand by the roads, and look, and ask for the ancient paths, where the good way is; and walk in it, and find rest for your souls. But they said, 'We will not walk in it'") — rest explicitly located in the "ancient path" and refused; (3) Isa 30:15 ("In returning and rest you shall be saved; in quietness and in trust shall be your strength. But you were unwilling") — the most concentrated statement of peace refused. The inability to receive peace is not ignorance but refusal — the will actively declines the condition. This pattern is concentrated in the OT prophets and names a structural feature of the human condition: peace is available but resisted.


## Sustained effect

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

The sustained effects of shalom documented in the data: (1) Stumble-proof stability — Psa 119:165 ("great peace have those who love your law; nothing can make them stumble") — sustained shalom produces inner stability resistant to external shocks; (2) Wisdom reception — Isa 32:17 ("the effect of righteousness will be peace, and the result of righteousness, quietness and trust forever") — sustained peace is the condition in which trust is formed and wisdom operates; (3) Turning many from iniquity — Mal 2:6 — Levi's sustained walk in peace produced transformative effects in others; (4) Righteousness harvest — Jam 3:18 ("a harvest of righteousness is sown in peace by those who make peace") — peace-orientation produces righteousness as its long-term fruit; (5) Strength — Isa 30:15 ("in quietness and in trust shall be your strength") — the sustained inner posture of quiet trust produces strength. The sustained profile: shalom is not only an experienced condition but a formative one — it produces stability, trust, strength, and the capacity to impact others.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

Sustained absence of shalom produces progressive deterioration: (1) Somatic degradation — Psa 38:3 ("no soundness in my flesh... no health in my bones because of my sin") — sustained peacelessness shows in the body; (2) Exile and homelessness — Deu 28:65 ("you shall find no respite, and there shall be no resting place for the sole of your foot") — sustained peacelessness produces the ultimate displacement; (3) Universal desolation — Jer 12:12 ("no flesh has peace") — when shalom is withdrawn from a community, the deprivation becomes total; (4) The wicked's false prosperity — Job 21:9 ("their houses are safe from fear") reveals by contrast: the wicked have external safety but not genuine shalom — the sustained absence of authentic peace produces the counterfeit of complacent ease. The trajectory: inner → somatic → communal → eschatological.

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

Yes — and this is theologically significant. The forming dimension: (1) Mal 2:6 — walking in peace as the character-across-time of the faithful priest; the formed person whose sustained walk is peaceful; (2) G1516 eirēnikos — peaceableness as the fruit of discipline (Heb 12:11 — "the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it"); (3) Gal 5:22 — peace as Spirit-fruit — the Spirit forms peace as a characteristic quality in the believer over time; (4) G2272 hēsuchios — the gentle and quiet spirit (1Pe 3:4) as the "hidden person of the heart" — a formed inner quality precious to God. The evidence confirms: sustained shalom is not merely experienced but formed — it becomes a character quality, an inner orientation, and eventually the "hidden person" of the heart. The transformative dimension connects directly to T5 (formative and developmental).


## Conditions of reception

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

Yes — as established in Q&A-020. The data shows: absent (Lam 3:17 — soul bereft), present (Psa 4:8 — lying down in peace), great (Psa 119:165 — great peace for law-lovers), perfect (Isa 26:3 — shalom shalom for the trust-directed mind), eschatological-unlimited (Isa 9:7 — no end to his peace). The degrees are not always explicitly graduated but the comparative constructions confirm graduation is present: "great peace" (rav shalom) implies more-than-ordinary peace; shalom-shalom (doubled) implies maximal completeness. The NT adds: "peace of God which surpasses all understanding" (Phili 4:7) — the eschatological peace exceeds cognitive categories entirely.

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

The data reveals a causal structure governing the degree of shalom: (1) Orientation of the mind toward God (Isa 26:3 — "you keep him in perfect peace whose mind is stayed on you, because he trusts in you") — the directing of the mind toward God produces perfect peace; (2) Love of God's law (Psa 119:165 — "great peace have those who love your law") — orientation toward God's word produces great peace; (3) Righteous character (Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace") — the degree of shalom follows from the degree of righteousness; (4) Trust (Psa 4:8, Isa 30:15) — trust is the inner act that enables peace's reception; (5) The absence of stubbornness/self-deception (Deu 29:19, Jer 23:17) — the factors that reduce shalom are the absence of trust and the presence of self-deception. The causal structure: covenant faithfulness → trust → orientation toward God → peace. Each factor contributes; disruption at any point reduces the degree of shalom available.

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

Yes — and the eschatological fullness is among the most extensively documented in the programme's data. Evidence: (1) H7965G group 2508-004 (DHC) — the messianic reign of peace: "of the increase of his government and of peace there will be no end" (Isa 9:7); (2) Eph 2:14 (G1515 426-003) — Christ IS peace, achieved once-for-all through the blood of the cross (Col 1:20); (3) Phili 4:7 — the peace that surpasses all understanding as the eschatological reality experienced in anticipation; (4) The covenant of peace that shall not be removed (Isa 54:10, Eze 37:26) — the eternal character of eschatological shalom; (5) Cosmic peace at the Lamb's seventh seal (Rev 8:1 — sigē, silence in heaven — G4602) — the eschatological threshold of total cosmic shalom. The present attainment of shalom (Isa 26:3, Psa 4:8) is real but partial; the eschatological fullness (Isa 9:7, Eph 2:14, Phili 4:7) is the unrestricted reality toward which the present experience points.


## Dimension classification

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

The most important single finding from T1: peace (shalom/eirēnē) is not primarily an emotional state or an absence of conflict, but an integrative wholeness grounded in divine identity and structured through covenant. The evidence establishes four dimensions that are not alternatives but a sequence: (1) Covenantal ground: shalom exists because God covenants it — the covenant of peace (Isa 54:10) is the basis; (2) Relational gift: God gives shalom to those in covenant (Num 6:26), speaks it to his saints (Psa 85:8), plans it for his people (Jer 29:11); (3) Inner condition: the received shalom becomes the experienced condition of the whole person — perfect peace for the trust-directed mind (Isa 26:3), soul at rest (Psa 4:8), welfare of the body and soul (Psa 38:3, 55:18); (4) Eschatological telos: the peace given and experienced is not complete until it is embodied in a Person — the Prince of Peace (Isa 9:6), he who shall be their peace (Mic 5:5), he who himself is our peace (Eph 2:14). DIM-117-002 (the pending finding about the nature of shalom) is hereby resolved: shalom is all four dimensions simultaneously, as a structured and connected reality. The central analytical finding is the sequence: divine identity → covenant gift → inner condition → eschatological embodiment in a Person. [RESOLVING DIM-117-002]

**T1.8.2 — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

Two genuine gaps: (1) Pre-fall peace: the data does not address what shalom was in the original creation before the fall. The earliest peace references (Gen 15, 26, 28) already presuppose a world needing covenant — the pre-fall condition of shalom is not attested in the vocabulary evidence. This is a structural gap in the lexical approach: the peace vocabulary emerges in a fallen world and is therefore always shaped by covenant-restoration; (2) The relationship between the silence/quietness vocabulary (the "inner face" of peace) and shalom itself: the rest/quiet/silence Hebrew terms (H5117, H4496H, H5183A, H8252, and others) form the large majority of the registry's groups, yet their relationship to shalom is not formally defined in the data — they co-occur and co-occur in the same theological contexts but the data does not explicitly state whether shalom produces silence/quiet or silence/quiet produces shalom (though Isa 30:15 — quietness and trust = strength — suggests the latter). Gap deferred to Session D.

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

Yes — three major implications: (1) The shalom-as-covenant finding implicates R34 (covenant) directly — the covenant of peace (Isa 54:10, Eze 37:26) is both a peace term and a covenant term. Session D must examine whether the peace-covenant is a distinct covenant or the inner dimension of the Sinai/new covenant; (2) The shalom-as-trust-product finding implicates R163 (trust) — Isa 26:3 (trust → perfect peace) and Psa 4:8 (trust in God → lie down in peace) establish trust as peace's causal precondition. Session D must examine whether every instance of shalom-without-trust is either false peace (2509-002) or eschatological peace (2508-004); (3) The "Person IS peace" finding (Mic 5:5, Eph 2:14) implicates R130 (reconciliation) — Col 1:20 (making peace by the blood of the cross) and R130's apokatallassō vocabulary converge at the same event. The peace the Person IS and the reconciliation the cross achieves are the same event named from different angles.

**Vocabulary analysis (T7.1):**

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

Primary terms: (1) Hebrew: sha.lom (H7965G and sub-forms) — root SHA.L/SHA.L.M, meaning completeness, soundness, wholeness; from sha.lem (to be complete, to be at peace, to be paid in full); the root encodes the idea of nothing lacking, everything in its proper place. This is the most semantically significant root etymology in the registry — shalom is not "peace" in the modern reductive sense but "wholeness-completeness-settlement." The root's appearance in sha.lam (to pay, to make restitution, H7999) confirms: shalom is what exists when every debt is settled, every breach is healed, every deficiency is supplied. (2) Greek: eirēnē (G1515) — root uncertain, possibly from eirō (to join) — the condition of things joined together; in the LXX, eirēnē becomes the translation of shalom, absorbing its full semantic range. NT eirēnē is not a Greek concept Hellenised but a Hebrew concept translated. OBS-117-009, Q&A-013.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

Sha.lom (H7965G) functions as: (1) Noun (primary): the condition of peace — "in peace I will lie down" (Psa 4:8), "covenant of peace" (Isa 54:10); (2) Noun used as greeting formula: "Peace be to you" (1Sa 25:6, H7965K); (3) Noun functioning as an identity statement: "I am for peace" (Psa 120:7 — ani sha.lom, where sha.lom is the predicate nominative); (4) Noun used adverbially (H7965L): "wholly" (Jer 13:19 — all-verses-fail, no inner-being); (5) Noun in construct chain as descriptor: "covenant of peace" (brit sha.lom), "man of peace" (ish sha.lom, Psa 37:37), "Prince of Peace" (sar sha.lom, Isa 9:6). The identity-statement form (ani sha.lom — "I am peace") is analytically distinctive: sha.lom becomes a predicate nominative, identifying the person with the characteristic. This grammatical form is unique in the registry and is the most concentrated expression of the moral-character dimension.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Sha.lom's semantic range is the widest of any single term in the programme's vocabulary: (1) Completeness/soundness (Psa 38:3 — "no sha.lom in my bones"); (2) Safety/welfare (Jer 29:11 — "plans for sha.lom"); (3) Peace as inner condition (Psa 4:8 — "in sha.lom I will lie down"); (4) Peace as relational condition (Num 25:12 — "covenant of sha.lom"); (5) Peace as eschatological reality (Isa 9:7 — "no end to his sha.lom"); (6) Peace as messianic identity (Mic 5:5 — "he shall be their sha.lom"); (7) Welfare/prosperity (H7965I — prosperity of the wicked, Psa 73:3); (8) Greeting (H7965K — peace-blessing in greeting, 1Sa 25:6); (9) Friendship (H7965J — bond of close friendship, 1Ch 12:17); (10) Divine identity (H7965H — YHWH-Shalom, Judg 6:24). The range confirms: shalom is the most semantically integrative term in the registry.

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary distinguishes: (1) State: sha.lom (H7965G — the condition), menuchah (H4496H — the resting state), shaqat (H8252 — the state of quiet); (2) Disposition/character: sha.lom (H7965G, group 2508-003 — moral orientation), hēsuchios (G2272 — gentle and quiet spirit as character), eirēnikos (G1516 — peaceableness as quality); (3) Act: shaqat (H8252 — to quiet, verbal form), eirēneuō (G1514 — to be at peace actively, volitional act), eirēnopoieō (G1517 — to make peace, the specific act of peacemaking); (4) Process/fruit: Gal 5:22 — peace as Spirit-produced fruit (the formative outcome). The vocabulary is unusually rich in distinguishing the state (what shalom is), the disposition (the orientation toward shalom), the act (making or pursuing shalom), and the fruit (peace formed over time).

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

The vocabulary does not include a single dedicated antonym term, but several terms name peace's absence or corruption: (1) The "no sha.lom for the wicked" formula (Isa 48:22, 57:21) — sha.lom with negation particle; (2) The restlessness vocabulary: balah (to be disturbed/troubled), ra.ga (H7280A — to stir up/disturb) — these name the opposite condition; (3) Isa 57:20 — "like the tossing sea, for it cannot be quiet (sha.qat)" — inability to be quiet as the functional opposite of shaqat-peace; (4) The false-shalom pattern: the vocabulary distinguishes false sha.lom (the words of the false prophets, Jer 6:14) from genuine sha.lom, but the same word is used — the opposition is moral, not lexical. The absence of a dedicated antonym term is itself analytically significant: the biblical vocabulary defines peace positively (through its presence and character) rather than negatively (through a named opposite). Peace is not primarily defined by what it is not.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — multiple person-type terms: (1) Ish sha.lom (Psa 37:37 — "man of peace") — the person whose character is peace; the construct chain ish + sha.lom makes peace the defining characteristic of the person; (2) Ani sha.lom (Psa 120:7 — "I am for peace/I am peace") — the most concentrated self-designation; sha.lom as personal identity not merely possession; (3) Anshei sha.lom (Jer 20:10, 38:22 — "men of sha.lom") — plural person-type term; men defined by their sha.lom-bond; used notably in the betrayal context; (4) G1516 eirēnikos — the peaceable person, one whose character is eirēnē (Jam 3:17, Heb 12:11); (5) Eirēnopoios (G1518 — "peacemaker," Mat 5:9) — owned by R103 but in the peace vocabulary's scope: the person whose characteristic act is making peace. The person-type vocabulary is rich: the registry defines not only the characteristic but the person whose nature is the characteristic.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

Yes: (1) Psa 34:14 — "seek peace (sha.lom) and pursue it" — the imperative "seek" (baqash) applied to sha.lom; seeking-peace as a commanded act; (2) Eze 7:25 — "when anguish comes they will seek (baqash) peace, but there shall be none" — desperate seeking of sha.lom; (3) Jer 8:15, 14:19 — "we looked for peace" — qavah (to wait/hope for) applied to sha.lom; the seeking-of-peace-that-does-not-come; (4) Jer 29:7 — "seek (darash) the welfare" — darash (to seek/inquire) applied to the sha.lom of the city. The seeking-vocabulary is significant: sha.lom is not only given but sought — the data shows humans actively pursuing sha.lom, sometimes successfully (Psa 34:14 — commanded pursuit) and sometimes futilely (Eze 7:25 — sought but unavailable under judgment). The seeking of peace is itself theologically meaningful: it reflects the deep human orientation toward shalom even when it is unavailable.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The LXX's consistent rendering of sha.lom with eirēnē is the foundational datum: the translation decision imports sha.lom's full semantic range into the Greek word, enabling the NT to use eirēnē with full shalom resonance. This is not a gap (the LXX-translation relationship is established and confirmed). However: the data flags NO_WORD_ANALYSIS for some terms, and LSJ (Greek lexicon) data is absent for the Greek terms in the readiness output — the §D lexical section showed no LSJ entries for the Greek eirēnē family. This means the LXX-development analysis is based on the translation relationship rather than full lexicographic comparison. Gap: a formal LXX analysis comparing sha.lom-eirēnē translation patterns (where sha.lom is not translated as eirēnē, and where eirēnē translates something other than sha.lom) would sharpen the boundary. This is beyond the current data's scope.

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

Yes — G1517 eirēnopoieō (to make peace, Col 1:20) is attested primarily in the NT context and represents a verbal neologism: combining eirēnē (peace) with poieō (to make/do) to create a term for the specific act of cosmic peace-making. This term is thin (1 active verse in the corpus, Col 1:20) but carries maximum theological weight — it names what Christ accomplished at the cross as a unique act. G1518 eirēnopoios (peacemaker, Mat 5:9) similarly compounds the root to create a person-type term. The NT coinage of these compound terms reflects the NT's need to describe both the act (eirēnopoieō) and the agent (eirēnopoios) of peace-making — concepts that the OT vocabulary named differently (sha.lom as noun, baqash sha.lom as seeking-peace). The new terminology reflects new theological content: the specific Christological act of making peace through the cross.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc — from SHA.L (root: completeness) through the shalom sub-forms (G/H/I/J/K/L) through the Hebrew silence/rest vocabulary (H5117/H5183A/H8252 etc.) through the Greek eirēnē family (G1515, G1514, G1516, G1517) — reveals that peace encompasses: (1) The root: completeness-as-ground; (2) The inner condition: wholeness, welfare, rest, quiet; (3) The relational form: friendship, greeting-blessing, covenant; (4) The divine identity: YHWH-Shalom, Prince of Peace; (5) The act: making peace (eirēnopoieō), being at peace (eirēneuō), pursuing peace (baqash sha.lom); (6) The person: man of peace, the peaceable (eirēnikos); (7) The eschatological telos: no end to his peace, eternal covenant. The arc confirms the T1 central finding (Q&A-033): shalom is a structured sequence (divine identity → covenant gift → inner condition → eschatological embodiment) whose vocabulary reflects every stage of that structure.
