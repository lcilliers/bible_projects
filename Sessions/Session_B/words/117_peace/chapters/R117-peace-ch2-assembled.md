# Chapter 2 — How It Works

**Registry 117 — Peace** · *Word Impact Description*

Operations, faculties, constitutional location, relational axis, formative dimension. Drawn from T2 (Constitutional Location), T3 (Inner Faculties), T4 (Relational Interfaces), and T5 (Formative & Developmental).

## Constitutional location and boundaries

Where this characteristic operates within the spirit/soul/heart/mind/body structure.


### T2.1 — Spirit-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

Partial evidence. The most direct instance: 2Cor 2:13 ("my spirit [pneuma] was not at rest [anesis] because I did not find my brother Titus there") — G0425 group 429-001 locates peace-as-relief directly in Paul's spirit. This is the clearest spirit-level location in the NT portion of the registry. In the OT, the spirit-level is less explicitly named for peace: ruach (spirit) does not appear as the explicit bearer of shalom in the anchor verse evidence. However, the Spirit's involvement is constitutive in the NT: Gal 5:22 (peace as Spirit-fruit, G1515 group 426-003) — peace is a product of the Spirit's presence, not explicitly located in the human spirit but produced by the divine Spirit within the person. The distinction matters: peace is produced by the Spirit but its experiential location in the data is primarily soul (nephesh) and heart (lev/kardia). Source: OBS-117-122 (prior session, Gal 5:22), Q&A-011.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

Spirit-level origin is supported in the NT but not dominant. Evidence: (1) Gal 5:22 — peace as Spirit-fruit means its origin is the divine Spirit working in the person; the human spirit is the reception point; (2) 1Th 5:23 ("the God of peace himself sanctify you completely; your whole spirit [pneuma] and soul [psuche] and body [soma] be kept blameless") — the God of peace addresses the whole person trichotomously, with spirit listed first; peace's sanctifying reach is total, covering the spirit level; (3) Rom 8:6 ("to set the mind on the Spirit is life and peace") — the Spirit-oriented mind produces peace, suggesting spirit-level orientation is the generator of inner peace. The evidence suggests peace originates at the spirit level (through the Spirit's presence) but is experienced throughout the person. Not primarily located in the spirit as its constitutional home — the heart and soul are the more explicit locations.

**Prompt 3: What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

The spirit-level data reveals that peace has its generative source in the deepest layer of the person — where the divine Spirit interacts with the human person (Gal 5:22, Rom 8:6). The significance: peace is not a surface emotional adjustment but a characteristic that involves the deepest interface between the human person and God. 2Cor 2:13 (spirit not at rest) shows that spirit-level restlessness is possible — the absence of peace penetrates to the spirit. 1Th 5:23 (sanctification of the whole person including spirit) shows that peace's reach is total and deep. The nature revealed: peace is a whole-person integrative condition whose deepest root is the spirit-level relationship with God, even though its most explicit anatomical location in the data is the soul and heart.


### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?**

Yes — and this is the most analytically significant T2 finding for the peace registry. The constitutional movement of peace is extensively documented, operating in multiple directions: (1) Spirit → soul → body (downward cascade under loss of peace): The absence of peace radiates from the inner person outward: Deu 28:65 — peacelessness produces trembling heart (volitional/heart level) + failing eyes (body level) + languishing soul (soul level); Psa 32:3 — suppressed inner condition → bones wasting (body); Psa 38:3 — sin disrupting inner peace → no soundness in flesh, no health in bones. The movement under un-peace: inner disorder → somatic deterioration. (2) God → spirit → soul → body (downward cascade under the gift of peace): Gal 5:22 (Spirit produces peace as fruit) → Phili 4:7 (peace guards hearts and minds) → Psa 4:8 (peace enables the body to lie down and sleep). The gift moves from its divine source through the spirit/Spirit level into the heart and mind, and from there into somatic rest. (3) Body → inner (the redemptive reversal): Isa 53:5 — the Servant's wounded body → peace received in others' inner being; the movement against the normal direction, accomplished through the cross.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?**

Three patterns are identifiable: (1) Cascading loss pattern: inner peace → somatic peace is the normal condition; inner dis-peace → somatic deterioration follows the causal chain inner-to-body (Psa 32:3, 38:3, Deu 28:65). The sequence is consistent: first the inner person is disordered (by sin, by judgment, by absent trust), then the body registers the disorder. The body is the last to fail and the indicator of deep inner trouble. (2) Cascading gift pattern: God gives peace (Num 6:26, Joh 14:27) → heart and mind receive it (Phili 4:7, Isa 26:3) → body enacts it (Psa 4:8 — lying down in sleep). The sequence moves from divine source through inner-being faculties into somatic expression. The body's rest is the downstream expression of the inner peace received. (3) Redemptive reversal pattern: suffering body of the Servant (Isa 53:5) → inner peace produced in others; this reversal moves counter to the normal direction and is unique in the corpus. The three patterns together establish peace as a constitutionally mobile characteristic — it does not reside statically in one location but flows through the whole person, and flows in different directions under different conditions.


### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

Yes — explicitly. The strongest evidence: (1) Lam 3:17 ("my soul [nephesh] is bereft of peace [shalom]; I have forgotten what happiness is") — nephesh is named as the bearer of peace's absence; OBS-117-144; (2) Psa 62:5 ("For God alone, O my soul [nephesh], wait in silence; for my hope is from him") — prior session, the soul commanded to silence itself before God; H4496H group 2585-001 — "the soul's own rest"; (3) Psa 131:2 ("I have calmed and quieted my soul [nephesh], like a weaned child with its mother; like a weaned child is my soul [nephesh] within me") — soul quieted, peace as the soul's condition; (4) Psa 55:18 ("He redeems my soul [nephesh] in safety [shalom] from the battle that I wage") — the soul directly named as the bearer of shalom-safety; OBS-117-149; (5) Jer 6:16 ("walk in it, and find rest for your souls [nephesh]") — rest/peace located explicitly in the soul. The soul is the most explicitly named inner-being location of peace in the data — more so than the spirit or heart in the Hebrew evidence.

**Prompt 2: What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?**

Soul-level location establishes peace as a fundamental condition of the whole inner person's experience — not a cognitive state (mind-level) or a moral quality (character-level) but the experiential condition of the nephesh as living, feeling, wanting creature. The nephesh is the person as they are most fully themselves — most vulnerable, most alive, most in need. Locating shalom in the nephesh means: (1) When peace is present, the whole person flourishes (Psa 55:18 — soul in safety); (2) When peace is absent, the whole person is destitute (Lam 3:17 — soul bereft, happiness forgotten); (3) Peace can be commanded to the soul (Psa 62:5, 131:2) — the soul can be directed toward peace by addressing it as a subject. This last point is analytically significant: the soul is not passive in relation to peace but can be called to orientation. The soul-level location means peace is the deepest personal experiential reality — the condition in which the self-as-person rests or is bereft.


### T2.3 — Heart

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

Yes — with rich evidence. Key heart locations: (1) Deu 29:19 (2509-002 anchor, OBS-117-150) — "blesses himself in his heart [lev]" claiming shalom while walking in "stubbornness of my heart" — false peace is a heart-generated claim; the heart is the seat of the false shalom; (2) 1Ch 12:17 (2510-001 anchor, OBS-117-151) — "my heart will be joined to you" — the shalom of friendship is a heart-bond; (3) Joh 14:27 — "Let not your hearts [kardia] be troubled" — Christ's peace addresses the heart directly; the troubled heart and the peace are antithetical (G1515 426-001 anchor, prior session OBS-117-120); (4) Phili 4:7 — "the peace of God... will guard your hearts [kardia] and your minds" — the heart is the primary guarded territory; (5) Pro 12:20 — "those who plan peace have joy" — planning peace is a heart-act (prior session OBS-117-096 noted the heart-disposition of planning); (6) Psa 28:3 — "speak peace with their neighbors while evil is in their hearts" — heart as the locus of authentic or false orientation toward peace. The data shows 33 co-occurring verses with R183 (heart) — the highest co-occurrence count in the entire registry (G.2, OBS-117-016).

**Prompt 2: What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?**

The heart-location of peace engages all four of the heart's integrating functions: (1) Knowing (cognitive): Rom 8:6 ("to set the mind/heart on the Spirit is life and peace") — the heart's cognitive orientation toward God produces peace; the directed heart knows what produces peace; (2) Willing (volitional): Psa 120:7 ("I am for peace") — the heart's volitional orientation toward peace as identity; Isa 30:15 ("but you were unwilling") — the refusal of peace is a heart-act of will; (3) Feeling (affective): Joh 14:27 ("let not your hearts be troubled") — the troubled heart as the experiential opposite of peace; the heart as the seat of distress or rest; (4) Moral awareness (conscience): Deu 29:19 — the heart's stubbornness as the moral condition that generates false shalom; Psa 28:3 — evil in the heart contrasted with speaking peace. The heart is the integrating centre of peace's constitutional address: peace must be received, held, directed, and expressed from the heart. The heart-peace connection is the most comprehensive single-location engagement in the data.


### T2.4 — Mind

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?**

Yes — explicitly and centrally. The primary mind-location: Isa 26:3 ("You keep him in perfect peace whose mind [yetser — formed thought/disposition] is stayed on you, because he trusts in you") — OBS-117-144. This is the most direct mind-peace statement in the corpus: perfect peace (shalom shalom) is the condition of the mind that is directed toward and stayed upon God. The yetser is the formed inclination or disposition of the mind — its sustained directedness. In the NT: Phili 4:7 — "the peace of God... will guard your hearts and your minds [noema — thoughts, understanding]" — the mind is jointly named as the territory peace guards alongside the heart; Rom 8:6 — "to set the mind [phronema] on the Spirit is life and peace" — the mind's orientation determines whether peace is present. The mind is the second major constitutional location of peace after the soul, and the most explicitly causal: directing the mind toward God produces peace; directing the mind elsewhere forfeits it.

**Prompt 2: What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?**

The mind-peace connection primarily engages the mind's directional function — the capacity of the mind to fix itself upon an object. Isa 26:3 (yetser) — the "stayed" or "fixed" mind: peace requires the mind to be anchored, not drifting. The mental act that produces peace is not thought-content (what the mind thinks about) but mental orientation (what the mind is stayed upon). Rom 8:6 (phronema — the mind's settled disposition/inclination) — the mind's habitual orientation toward the Spirit produces peace; toward the flesh produces death. Phili 4:7 (noema — the products of thought, understanding) — the mind's thoughts are what peace guards: peace stands sentinel over the mind's outputs. Three aspects: (1) Orientation (Isa 26:3) — where the mind is directed; (2) Disposition (Rom 8:6) — what the mind is habitually inclined toward; (3) Output (Phili 4:7) — the contents of thought that peace protects. The mind's peace is a total engagement — orientation, disposition, and output are all in view.

Yes — two additional soul-subset locations appear: (1) The inward parts / belly (meah — inward parts, bowels): Job 30:27 ("My inward parts [meah] are in turmoil and never still") — OBS-117-066 from prior session; the deepest visceral inner location of restlessness; Jer 4:19 ("My anguish, my anguish! The hidden person is not heart or spirit alone but the person-as-inner-being in its most comprehensive sense. OBS-117-128 from prior session.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?**

(1) Inward parts/belly (meah): locates peace-restlessness at the level of the deepest visceral experience — the gut-level inner experience. This is not a cognitive or volitional location but a felt-body-interior location. Its significance: restlessness and peace penetrate below the level of deliberate consciousness to the visceral core of the person. This deepens the somatic evidence (T2.6 below). (2) Hidden person of the heart (1Pe 3:4): this is the most theologically rich additional location. The "hidden person" is the inner self in its deepest reality — the person as God sees them, not as they appear outwardly. The quality of this hidden person is the gentle-quiet spirit. The revelation: the ultimate location of peace as character is not the publicly visible person but the hidden inner person — the one precious to God (1Pe 3:4: "which in God's sight is very precious"). Peace at this level is not performance but being. The hidden-person location connects to the T1 finding about the formed character of peace (Q&A-029) — the peace that is formed becomes the hidden person's defining quality.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

Yes — and this is one of the most extensive somatic evidence sets in the programme. Multiple body parts explicitly linked to peace's presence or absence: (1) Bones (etsem): Psa 32:3 ("my bones wasted away through my groaning all day long") — withheld confession/peace produces bone deterioration; Psa 38:3 ("there is no health [shalom] in my bones because of my sin") — OBS-117-149; shalom named as the soundness-of-bones; (2) Flesh (basar): Psa 38:3 ("there is no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation") — flesh linked to peace-as-soundness; (3) Eyes: Deu 28:65 ("failing eyes") — peacelessness in judgment produces ocular failure; (4) Feet/sole: Deu 28:65 ("no resting place for the sole of your foot") — the body's connection to the land as peace's somatic expression; (5) Inward parts/belly (meah): Job 30:27 — OBS-117-066; (6) Body-sleep (Psa 4:8) — the whole body at rest as the expression of peace ("I will both lie down and sleep"); (7) Wounds (Isa 53:5 — "with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's body as the instrument of peace's purchase.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

The body-part links serve multiple functions: (1) Indicative — the body's condition indicates the inner-being condition: Psa 38:3 (no soundness in flesh/no health in bones because of sin) — the body reads out the inner state of peacelessness; the body is a diagnostic display of the inner condition; (2) Emphatic — Deu 28:65 accumulates three somatic signals (trembling heart, failing eyes, languishing soul) to intensify the portrait of peacelessness in judgment; the body amplifies the inner condition; (3) Expressive — Psa 4:8 (lying down and sleeping) — the body enacts peace by doing what peace enables; sleep is the body's expression of inner security; (4) Mediating — Isa 53:5 ("with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's body mediates peace to others; the physical wounds are the mechanism of the relational restoration. The most significant body-link is Psa 38:3: the explicit naming of shalom as soundness-of-bones under sin's influence establishes that inner peace and somatic integrity are structurally connected — not metaphorically but causally.


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

Both directions are evidenced, with the dominant direction being inner-state → body: (1) Inner → body (primary direction): Psa 38:3 — sin disrupts inner peace → no health in bones; Psa 32:3 — suppressed confession (inner withholding) → bones wasting; Deu 28:65 — peacelessness in judgment → trembling heart, failing eyes, languishing soul. The inner condition of un-peace expresses itself through somatic deterioration. (2) Body → inner: Isa 53:5 ("with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's somatic wounds produce inner healing/peace for others; the body of the Servant becomes the mechanism of inner restoration for the many. This is the most theologically significant body→inner direction in the corpus — a body bearing suffering that produces inner peace in others. (3) Body expressing inner: Psa 4:8 — the body lying down and sleeping expresses an already-present inner peace; the body does not produce peace but enacts it. The primary causal direction is inner → body; the Isa 53:5 direction (body → inner) is the unique redemptive reversal.

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

The primary direction (inner → body) establishes peace as a genuinely somatic characteristic — its absence or presence registers in the body because the body is the outward expression of the inner state. This has two consequences: (1) Peace cannot be fully present in the inner person while the body is in somatic distress caused by inner disorder — the body is a truth-telling indicator of the inner condition (Psa 38:3, 32:3); a person who claims inner peace while their body deteriorates under the weight of unconfessed sin is in the false-shalom pattern; (2) The redemptive reversal (Isa 53:5 body → inner) is extraordinary precisely because it runs against the normal causal direction: normally inner disorder produces somatic deterioration; in the Servant's case, somatic suffering produces inner healing in others. The directionality finding deepens the T1 paradox (Q&A-023): peace comes through its opposite conditions not only experientially but physically.


### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**Prompt 1: Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

The evidence provides indirect but not conclusive evidence for a bodily deposit. What is present: (1) Psa 38:3 and 32:3 establish that sustained absence of peace (inner disorder) produces somatic deterioration — bones wasting, no soundness in flesh. This implies that sustained presence of peace should produce the inverse — somatic health and soundness; (2) Pro 3:2 ("length of days and years of life and peace they will add to you") — wisdom-and-peace produces length of life; shalom is associated with longevity as its somatic fruit; (3) Pro 3:17 ("all her paths are peace") — wisdom's characteristic product is peace as a sustained life-condition. The wisdom literature implies that sustained shalom has somatic extension effects (length of life). However: the data does not explicitly address constitutional deposit in the technical sense (DNA, generational transmission). The silence is methodological rather than substantive — the biblical vocabulary does not operate with the category of genetic or physiological deposit. Gap: the question of generational or developmental deposit for sustained peace is not addressable from this data. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.

**Prompt 2: What evidence supports or contradicts this?**

Supporting: Pro 3:2 (peace associated with longer life — a life-outcome that implies somatic extension); the somatic-deterioration pattern under sustained peacelessness (Psa 38:3, 32:3, Deu 28:65) implies by negation that sustained peace protects somatic integrity. Contradicting: no verse explicitly claims that shalom leaves a genetic or physiological deposit. The closest is Isa 66:12 ("like a river... like an overflowing stream") — the image of peace as overflowing suggests abundance rather than deposit. Neutral: 1Th 5:23 (the God of peace sanctifying the whole person — spirit, soul, body) suggests peace's reach is total but does not specify deposit mechanisms. The evidence supports somatic consequence of peace (in both directions) but not constitutional deposit in the genetic/biological sense. Gap explicitly noted for T5.7.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.**

The evidence is partially silent on constitutional deposit in the technical sense. The data establishes somatic consequence (in both directions) but not deposit. T5.7 consequence: since the evidence for a constitutional body deposit is indirect and inconclusive, T5.7 will not be able to affirm a deposit finding — all three T5.7 prompts will receive N (not applicable) unless T5 analysis surfaces new evidence. This is recorded here for that dependency. The somatic consequence findings (T2.6, T2.7) remain valid independent of the deposit question.


### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**Prompt 1: Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

The data establishes a primary origin and two secondary origins: (1) Primary — bestowed by God: the overwhelming weight of the evidence shows shalom as divine gift. The Aaronic blessing (Num 6:26 — "the Lord... give you peace"), God's covenant of peace (Isa 54:10), God speaking peace to his saints (Psa 85:8), God planning welfare (Jer 29:11), Christ as the peace-giver (Joh 14:27 — "my peace I give to you"), Spirit-fruit peace (Gal 5:22) — all establish God as the constitutional source. The human person cannot generate authentic shalom from within. (2) Secondary — formed from within through orientation: Isa 26:3 — the mind stayed on God produces perfect peace; the person's own cognitive act of orientation enables the reception of what God provides. This is not generation from within but reception through orientation; (3) Generated falsely from within: the false-shalom pattern (Deu 29:19, Jer 6:14) — the person can generate a counterfeit inner condition of peace by self-deception or false claim, but this is precisely what the data condemns. Authentic constitutional origin: divine gift received through trust and covenant orientation.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

The origin is singular in ground but multiple in mechanism. Ground: God alone is the ultimate source — whether through covenant (2508-002), through Christ (426-001, Eph 2:14), or through the Spirit (Gal 5:22), all peace traces back to God as its constitutional source. Mechanism: three distinct modes of origination appear in the data: (1) Covenantal gift — God gives shalom through covenant (Num 6:26, Isa 54:10); the covenant is the structural channel; (2) Christological accomplishment — peace purchased through the cross (Isa 53:5, Col 1:20, G1517 430-001); Christ's act constitutes the peace; (3) Pneumatological formation — peace formed as Spirit-fruit (Gal 5:22); the Spirit produces peace over time within the person. These three mechanisms correspond to the three persons of the Trinity — the origin is Trinitarian in structure, though the data does not use that language explicitly. The singular ground (God) and multiple mechanisms prevent both monism (peace from only one channel) and pluralism (peace from multiple independent sources).


## Inner faculties engaged

How this characteristic engages the eleven inner-being faculties: perception, cognition, memory, affect, creativity, volition, agency, moral evaluation, conscience, conscientiousness, and relational capacity.


### T3.1 — Perception

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?**

Yes — peace engages perception primarily in its negative form: the perceptive faculty is disturbed, dulled, or altered when peace is absent. Evidence: (1) Deu 28:65 — "failing eyes" as a somatic expression of peacelessness; the eyes (a perceptive organ) fail under the condition of un-peace in judgment; (2) The silence vocabulary in this registry (H1827 demamah, G4602 sigē) names the threshold-quiet before perception of the divine voice — 1Ki 19:12 (still small voice after the demamah-silence), OBS-117-069 from prior session; the silence that is peace's companion is precisely the condition that enables divine perception; (3) Psa 131:2 — "I have calmed and quieted my soul... like a weaned child" — the quieted soul is positioned for receptive perception; restlessness prevents this.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?**

Peace enables perception — specifically, the silence/stillness dimension of peace enables the perception of the divine voice and divine reality. The pattern: demamah (profound silence, H1827) is the threshold before divine speech (1Ki 19:12, Job 4:16 — prior session OBS-117-069, 070); the quieted soul is receptive; the restless soul cannot hear what silence carries. Conversely, the absence of peace impairs perception — "failing eyes" (Deu 28:65) is the somatic expression. The mechanism: peace as stillness creates the condition for perception; restlessness as noise prevents it. This is the hearing-direction of the silence vocabulary: peace enables the person to be still enough to perceive God.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The perception engagement reveals that peace is not only a passive condition (a state received) but an enabling posture — the person at peace is constitutionally equipped to perceive things they cannot hear when restless. The demamah threshold (1Ki 19:12) is analytically significant: the divine voice comes not in wind, earthquake, or fire (the dramatic) but in the still small voice — and only the person who has moved through silence can hear it. This means peace has a gnomic/perceptual consequence: the peace-bearing person perceives differently. Peace as the enabling condition for divine perception is a new finding.


### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?**

Yes. Conscientiousness as the integration of moral awareness, volition, and action is precisely what the peace-as-character data describes. Evidence: (1) Mal 2:6 — Levi "walked with me in peace and uprightness, and turned many from iniquity" — the three elements: moral awareness (uprightness), volitional commitment (walking with God), and consequential action (turning many from iniquity) are all present in the portrait of the peaceable priest; (2) Jer 29:7 — "seek the welfare of the city... pray to the Lord on its behalf, for in its welfare you will find your welfare" — conscience (awareness of responsibility), volition (seek, pray), and action (community engagement) integrated in the peace-orientation; (3) H2790B group 408-001 — holding silence under emotional provocation — the conscientious restraint of impulse in the service of peace. Conscientiousness is the enacted form of the peace-as-moral-character dimension.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

Authentic peace deepens conscientiousness; false peace impairs it. (1) Deepens: the person who is genuinely at peace is in a stable inner condition from which conscientious action flows — Mal 2:6 (walked in peace → turned many from iniquity); the stable inner orientation of peace produces consistent moral response; (2) Impairs: complacent ease (H7600 group 413-001, Amo 6:1 — "woe to those at ease in Zion") represents peace's corruption as conscientiousness-impairment — the at-ease person fails to respond to what conscience and moral awareness demand; false shalom produces precisely the opposite of conscientiousness: comfort without response. The distinction between authentic shalom and complacent ease is most sharply felt here: one deepens conscientiousness, the other destroys it.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The conscientiousness pattern reveals that authentic peace is the inner condition that enables sustained moral-volitional-active integrity — the person at genuine peace is the person who can maintain consistent moral awareness, make the right volitional choices, and act on them over time. The portrait of Levi (Mal 2:6) is the paradigm case: the peaceful person is the most consistently conscientious person, not the least. This directly answers the potential misunderstanding that peace means passivity or disengagement — the data establishes the opposite. Peace is the enabling condition for the most integrated form of moral-volitional-active response.


### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?**

Yes — centrally. Peace is among the most relationally engaged characteristics in the programme. Evidence spans multiple dimensions: (1) Friendship bond (H7965J, 2510-001) — shalom as the inner relational bond of loyalty and heart-union (1Ch 12:17); (2) Greeting-blessing (H7965K, 2511-001) — the invocation of peace as the act of goodwill toward another; (3) Covenant peace (2508-002) — the relational condition between God and his people; (4) Peacemaking (G1517, Jam 3:18) — the capacity to make peace between parties; (5) G1514 eirēneuō — actively being at peace with all (Rom 12:18); (6) Eph 2:14-16 — Christ making peace between Jew and Gentile, creating one body. The relational dimension of peace is not peripheral but constitutive — the peace vocabulary is inherently directed toward-another.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

Authentic peace deeply enables and deepens relational capacity. Evidence: (1) 1Ch 12:17 — the sha.lom-friendship produces heart-union; (2) Eph 2:14-16 — Christ's peace breaks down the "dividing wall of hostility" and creates one body from two peoples — peace is the engine of the most dramatic relational restoration in the NT; (3) Jer 29:7 — seeking the welfare of the exile city requires expanding relational capacity to include the former enemy's community; (4) 1Sa 25:6 (2511-001) — the greeting-blessing as an act of relational goodwill. Conversely: peacelessness impairs relational capacity — Gen 37:4 ("they hated him and could not speak peacefully to him") — the absence of sha.lom-orientation makes genuine relational connection impossible; Jer 20:10 (anshei sha.lom who betray) — false sha.lom destroys relational capacity through betrayal. The instruction note for T3.11 distinguishes characteristics that build/sustain connection from those that restore/repair it — this registry does both: shalom builds connection (friendship, greeting, covenant) and restores broken connection (Eph 2:14).

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The relational pattern reveals that peace is constitutionally relational — it cannot be fully described as an inner private condition without reference to the other toward whom it is directed. The "I am for peace" (Psa 120:7) is not merely an inner statement but a relational declaration against the other party's war-orientation. The covenant of peace (2508-002) is inherently two-party. The friendship bond (2510-001) is inherently toward-another. The peace that guards the heart (Phili 4:7) is received in the context of prayer and petition to God. The conclusion: peace is both inner and relational simultaneously — the inner condition and the relational orientation are not separable dimensions but two faces of the same reality. The interiority and the relationality of peace are co-constitutive.


### T3.2 — Cognition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?**

Yes — centrally. The cognitive engagement of peace is established in T2.4: Isa 26:3 (yetser — the formed mental disposition/thought, "whose mind is stayed on you"), Rom 8:6 (phronema — mind's settled orientation), Phili 4:7 (noema — thoughts guarded by peace). The cognitive faculty is both the condition for peace (mind stayed on God → peace) and the territory peace guards (peace guards thoughts/minds). Two-directional engagement: cognition produces peace when rightly directed; peace guards cognition when received.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?**

Peace enables and deepens cognition. Evidence: (1) Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace, and the result of righteousness, quietness and trust forever" — the sustained peace-condition produces trust which is the basis for deeper knowing; (2) Ecc 9:17 — "the words of the wise heard in quiet [nachat] are better than the shouting of a ruler among fools" — the quiet condition enables wisdom reception (prior session OBS-117-100 context); (3) The wisdom-peace correlation: Pro 3:17 ("all her paths are peace") — wisdom's paths produce peace; peace and wisdom are mutually enabling. Conversely: the restless inner state ("cannot be quiet," Isa 57:20 — prior session OBS-117-114 context) produces "mire and dirt" — cognitive disorder and moral opacity. Peace is cognitively enabling; restlessness impairs.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The cognition pattern establishes a reciprocal relationship: right cognition (mind stayed on God) generates peace; peace in turn enables deeper cognition (wisdom reception, trust-grounded knowing). This is not a simple linear chain but a reinforcing loop — the peaceful mind perceives more, knows more, trusts more, and thereby receives more peace. The implication for Session D: R100 (knowledge) and R163 (trust) are structurally connected to peace through this cognitive loop.


### T3.3 — Memory

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?**

Yes — in its negative form most directly. Lam 3:17 ("my soul is bereft of peace; I have forgotten what happiness is") — peace-deprivation produces the loss of remembered happiness. The person bereft of shalom cannot recall what flourishing was like — memory of wellbeing is inaccessible in the peaceless condition. This is a striking data point: peace's absence disrupts not only the present experience but the person's ability to remember peace. Conversely: Isa 38:17 ("it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness") — Hezekiah's retrospective memory of peace-through-suffering suggests that the person who has received peace carries a memory of its arrival that shapes their understanding of God.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?**

Peace-deprivation impairs memory (Lam 3:17 — forgotten happiness); peace-restoration enables retrospective memory of God's faithfulness. The Lam 3 context is particularly rich: the movement from Lam 3:17 ("forgotten") to Lam 3:21-22 ("but this I call to mind, and therefore I have hope: the steadfast love of the Lord never ceases") — the act of remembering God's hesed breaks through the peaceless condition and restores hope. The memory of God's covenant faithfulness is the cognitive act that enables the return to peace. Peace and memory have a mutual relationship: peace enables the memory of grace; the memory of grace enables the recovery of peace.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The memory engagement reveals that peace has a temporal dimension — it exists in time, can be remembered, can be forgotten, and can be recovered through the act of remembering God's faithfulness. The Lam 3 pattern (forgotten → remembered → hope) shows that the memory of shalom is recoverable through deliberate recollection of covenant faithfulness, even from the depth of peacelessness. This connects to the T5 formation question: peace is not only a present condition but a remembered and hoped-for reality — it has a narrative arc in the person's experience.


### T3.4 — Affect

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?**

Yes — extensively. Peace is itself primarily an experiential/affective condition in many of its manifestations. The Emotion-Positive dimension assignments (groups 2508-001, 2509-001, 419-002, 418-004, 2585-001, 2585-002, and others) directly name the affective dimension. Key affective evidence: Psa 4:8 — lying down and sleeping in peace (the affect of deep security); Psa 35:27 — shout for joy and be glad, because of the Lord's delight in welfare (joy triggered by shalom); Jer 30:10 — "Jacob shall return and have quiet and ease, and none shall make him afraid" — ease as the affective state of the peacefully restored. The affect-peace connection is foundational: peace is, at the affective level, the felt condition of security, rest, welfare, and the absence of fear.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?**

Peace enables positive affect and resolves negative affect. Evidence: (1) Judg 6:23 — "Peace be to you. Do not fear" — divine peace directly displaces terror; (2) Est 9:22 — "sorrow turned into gladness and mourning into a holiday" (in the context of Purim rest/peace); (3) 1Sa 1:17-18 — Hannah went away and "her face was no longer sad" after Eli's peace-blessing; (4) Phili 4:7 — peace guards the heart against anxiety (Phili 4:6 context: "do not be anxious about anything... and the peace of God will guard"). Peace is the affective resolution of anxiety, fear, and sorrow. Its sustained presence does not bypass affect — peace-bearing people still weep, still feel — but it prevents affect from becoming debilitating or destructive. The person in peace can feel sorrow without being overwhelmed by it.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The affective engagement reveals that peace is not emotional neutrality or the suppression of feeling but the condition in which affect functions rightly — neither overwhelming the person (anxiety, terror) nor being inaccessible to them (the peaceless Lam 3:17 — "forgotten what happiness is"). Peace is the affective ground: the person who has shalom can feel the full range of affect without being destroyed by negative affect or alienated from positive affect. This is a crucial distinction for the programme: peace is not the absence of feeling but the condition in which feeling is held and expressed rightly.


### T3.5 — Creativity

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Silence on the creative faculty for peace is consistent with the registry's character: peace is not primarily described as a creative or generative condition but as a receptive, restful, and relational one. The data's silence on creativity suggests that the creative dimension of peace is not theologically foregrounded in the biblical vocabulary — the emphasis throughout is on reception, orientation, and the condition of the whole person rather than on creative output. This is worth noting as a gap for Session D if creativity's relationship to peace is to be explored.


### T3.6 — Volition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?**

Yes — prominently. The volitional engagement of peace is one of its most theologically significant features. Evidence: (1) The refusal-of-peace pattern (Q&A-026): Isa 30:15 ("but you were unwilling") — the will actively refuses the peace God offers; Isa 28:12 ("they would not hear"), Jer 6:16 ("we will not walk in it") — peace is declined by volitional act; (2) The pursuit-of-peace pattern: Psa 34:14 ("seek peace and pursue it") — peace requires active volitional seeking; G1514 eirēneuō ("live peaceably with all") — the volitional act of choosing peace; (3) Psa 120:7 ("I am for peace") — the identity-level volitional commitment to peace as a fundamental choice of orientation; (4) H2790B group 408-001 — "holding silence in response to emotional provocation" — the volitional restraint of impulse in service of peace. Peace is not only received but chosen and refused.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?**

Peace both requires and enables volition: (1) Requires volition: the pursuit of peace (Psa 34:14), the choice to be at peace with others (Rom 12:18 — G1514), the volitional orientation of the mind toward God (Isa 26:3) — all are deliberate acts of will; (2) Enables volition: the quieted person is in a better position to choose rightly — H2790B group 408-001 (holding silence under emotional provocation) shows peace-as-restraint enabling volition over impulse; H2814 group 409-004 (prophetic inner resolve, refusing silence for God's purposes) shows the person of peace being more decisively volitional, not less; (3) False peace bypasses true volition: Deu 29:19 — the self-deceived person's volitional claim ("I shall be safe") is not a genuine act of will directed toward peace but a bypass of the real volitional cost of covenant faithfulness. Peace-bearing volition is the will rightly informed and directed; false peace is the will disengaged from reality.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The volitional pattern reveals that peace is not a deterministic state but a characteristic that engages the will at every point — in its reception (choosing to orient toward God), its pursuit (actively seeking peace with others), its refusal (the structural human refusal documented in the prophets), and its false claim (bypassing the real volitional cost). This has significant anthropological implications: the human person is not a passive recipient of peace but an active participant in whether and how peace is received, held, and expressed. The refusal pattern (Isa 30:15) is the most theologically significant volitional evidence — God's offer of peace is genuine, the refusal is genuine, and the consequence is the person's responsibility.


### T3.7 — Agency

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?**

Yes — peace both expresses and enables agency. Evidence: (1) Peacemaking as an act of agency: Jam 3:18 ("a harvest of righteousness is sown in peace by those who make peace") — peacemakers are agents who do something in the world through their peace; (2) Mordecai (Est 10:3, OBS-117-071 from prior session) — "sought the welfare of his people and spoke peace to all his people" — the sha.lom orientation expressed as leadership agency; (3) G1517 eirēnopoieō (Col 1:20 — "making peace by the blood of his cross") — Christ as the supreme agent of peace; (4) The prophetic refusal of silence for advocacy (H2814 group 409-004) — the person of peace choosing to speak when silence would be complicit. The peacemaker (Matt 5:9 — not in anchor verses but in the eirēnē vocabulary's scope) is explicitly an agent who does something.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?**

Peace enables agency — the peaceful person is constitutionally equipped to act in the world from a stable inner base rather than from reactive anxiety. Evidence: (1) Dan 10:19 — "peace be with you; be strong and of good courage" — the angelic peace-gift produces strength and courage for action; peace precedes and enables Daniel's capacity to act on the vision; (2) Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace, and the result of righteousness, quietness and trust forever" — the peace condition produces trust, which is the basis for courageous action; (3) Deu 28:65 reverse — the absence of peace produces the "trembling heart" and "languishing soul" that impair agency; fearful and anxious people cannot act decisively. Conversely: complacent ease (H7600 group 413-001) impairs agency by producing false security and contempt for consequences — a corrupted form of "peace" that disengages agency.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The agency pattern reveals that authentic peace is not passive or quietist but enables robust, courageous action in the world. The peaceful person is not inactive — Mordecai seeks welfare, the peacemakers sow righteousness, Christ makes peace through the cross. The silence/stillness vocabulary names the inner orientation that enables agency, not the absence of agency. The contrast with complacent ease (H7600 413-001) is decisive: the person who is "at ease in Zion" (Amo 6:1) is precisely the person who does not act when they should — complacency impairs agency. Authentic shalom liberates agency; false peace suppresses it.


### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?**

Yes — extensively. Peace has a moral evaluation function: the presence or absence of shalom is a moral indicator. Evidence: (1) Isa 48:22, 57:21 — "there is no peace for the wicked" — moral condition is the determinative factor for peace's availability; (2) Isa 59:8 — "the way of peace they do not know" — moral disorder produces cognitive/perceptual blindness to peace's way; (3) Mal 2:6 — "walked with me in peace and uprightness" — shalom paired with uprightness as the moral-character portrait; (4) The false-peace pattern (Jer 6:14, Deu 29:19) — the moral faculty has been corrupted when false peace is claimed. Peace's connection to moral evaluation is among the richest in the programme: the peaceful person evaluates rightly; the wicked person cannot perceive peace's way; the self-deceived person evaluates falsely.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?**

Peace enables moral evaluation; its absence and its false form impair it. (1) Enabling: the quiet person evaluates rightly (Pro 14:33 — "wisdom rests in the heart of a man of understanding... but it makes itself known even in the midst of fools" — associated with the quiet-wisdom vocabulary); the peaceful mind (Isa 26:3) is in a better position to discern rightly; (2) False peace impairs: Deu 29:19 — the stubborn heart claiming false shalom "bypasses" the moral evaluation faculty — the person evaluates their own condition falsely because they are in the false-peace condition; Jer 6:14 — the prophets who cry "Peace, peace" have corrupted their moral evaluative function; (3) Absence impairs: Isa 57:20 ("the wicked are like the tossing sea... tossing up mire and dirt") — the restless inner state produces moral opacity, not discernment.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The moral evaluation pattern reveals that peace is both the condition that enables rightly-ordered moral judgment and itself a moral diagnostic tool. The person whose inner life is genuinely at peace is in a position to evaluate moral reality clearly; the person who has claimed false peace is systematically unable to see their condition rightly (Deu 29:19 — "I shall be safe" is the self-description of the self-deceived). This makes peace not only a moral characteristic but an epistemological one in the moral domain — the state of one's peace is a condition of moral perception.


### T3.9 — Conscience

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?**

Yes — the connection between peace and conscience is among the most analytically significant in the registry. The primary evidence: (1) Psa 32:3-5 — "when I kept silent, my bones wasted away through my groaning all day long... your hand was heavy upon me" (prior session OBS-117-076 context): suppressed confession = withheld inner acknowledgment of sin = somatic deterioration. The sequence: conscience pressing → person suppressing → bones wasting → person confessing → peace restored (Psa 32:5 — "I acknowledged my sin... and you forgave"). The conscience is the inner faculty whose suppression destroys peace and whose exercise restores it; (2) H2814 group 409-001 — "inward suppression of speech under emotional or spiritual pressure, with deteriorating inner consequence" — conscience-suppression as a pattern; (3) 2509-002 (Deu 29:19) — the false shalom claimed against the conscience's witness; the conscience is what the stubborn heart overrides to claim false peace.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?**

Three conscience-patterns: (1) Suppressed conscience impairs peace (the primary direction): Psa 32:3 — suppressed conscience → bones wasting, no peace; the relationship is causal — withheld confession prevents peace; (2) Exercised conscience enables peace: Psa 32:5 — acknowledged sin → forgiveness → relief; the confession that exercises conscience leads to peace as its outcome; (3) False peace bypasses conscience: Deu 29:19 — the stubborn heart generates false peace by bypassing the conscience's witness; this is the bypass pattern — false shalom is what remains when conscience is overridden. The conscience is thus both the gateway to peace (through confession) and the faculty whose suppression most directly destroys peace (Psa 32:3). No other inner faculty has this bidirectional gateway function as clearly documented in the data.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The conscience pattern reveals that peace and conscience are structurally interdependent: genuine peace requires a functioning and exercised conscience; the suppressed conscience is the primary internal mechanism of peacelessness; and the false peace that bypasses conscience is the most dangerous corruption of the characteristic. Psa 32 is the governing text: the person cannot have peace while suppressing conscience, and when conscience is exercised (confession), peace becomes available. This is a new finding: conscience is the gateway faculty for peace — more than any other inner faculty, the state of the conscience determines whether genuine peace is constitutionally accessible.


## Relational interfaces

How this characteristic operates across the relational interfaces of the human person — God-to-human, human-to-God, human-to-human (giving / receiving / boundaries), and the spiritual beings interface.


### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

Yes — this is the dominant direction in the peace corpus. The God-to-human operation is extensively documented: (1) Covenant gift: Num 6:26 ("the Lord give you peace" — Aaronic blessing), Isa 54:10 ("my covenant of peace shall not be removed"), Jer 29:11 ("plans for welfare and not for evil"); (2) God speaking peace: Psa 85:8 ("he will speak peace to his people, to his saints"); (3) Christ-given peace: Joh 14:27 ("my peace I give to you"); (4) Spirit-given peace: Gal 5:22 (Spirit-fruit); (5) God making well-being: Isa 45:7 ("I make well-being"); (6) The God of peace: Rom 15:33, 16:20, Phili 4:9, 1Th 5:23 — God's identity as "the God of peace" who acts toward the human person. The God-to-human direction is established at every level: covenant, speech, person of Christ, Spirit, and title.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

The data establishes covenant as the primary basis. God's peace is extended: (1) Through covenant: Num 25:12 ("my covenant of peace"), Isa 54:10 (covenant of peace that shall not be removed), Eze 34:25, 37:26 (covenants of peace in the new covenant context); (2) Through covenant faithfulness: Psa 85:8 — God speaks peace to his saints "but let them not turn back to folly" — the condition is covenant fidelity, not merit; (3) Through the cross: Col 1:20 ("making peace by the blood of his cross") — the basis shifts in the NT from the Sinai covenant to the new covenant accomplished in Christ's sacrifice; (4) Through grace not merit: Joh 14:27 ("not as the world gives do I give to you") — the basis of Christ's peace-gift is explicitly distinguished from transactional exchange; the gift is given freely. The ground shifts from covenant-as-obligation to covenant-as-grace across the canon.

**Prompt 3: What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

God's extension of peace reveals his constitutive orientation of goodwill and wholeness toward the human person. Three dimensions: (1) God delights in the welfare of his servant (Psa 35:27 — OBS-117-149) — peace-giving is the expression of divine delight, not merely duty; (2) God plans for welfare and not for evil (Jer 29:11) — the divine intention toward the human person is purposively peaceful; (3) God identified as peace (YHWH-Shalom, 406-001) — God's own identity includes peace as a constitutive attribute, meaning his extension of peace is not situational but essential to who he is. The convergence of these three dimensions reveals that God's peace-giving is not a response to human deserving but the overflow of his own identity — the God who IS peace gives peace because that is what he is.


### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

Yes — several forms. (1) Making peace with God: Isa 27:5 ("let them make peace with me, let them make peace with me") — the human act of coming into covenant relationship with God; (2) Walking with God in peace: Mal 2:6 — the sustained human posture of covenant faithfulness expressed as peace-with-God; (3) Returning to God: Isa 30:15 ("in returning and rest you shall be saved") — returning to God as the peace-generating human act (even though the peace itself is God's gift, the returning is the human movement toward God); (4) Praying for peace: Psa 122:6 ("pray for the peace of Jerusalem") — intercession as the human peace-orientation toward God on behalf of community; (5) The soul waiting in silence (Psa 62:5 — 2585-001) — "for God alone, O my soul, wait in silence" — the posture of the soul directed toward God in peaceful expectation.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

The data is specific about the inner posture required: (1) Trust: Isa 26:3 ("because he trusts in you" → perfect peace); Psa 4:8 ("for you alone, O Lord, make me dwell in safety" — trust as the inner posture); (2) Returning / repentance: Isa 30:15 ("in returning and rest you shall be saved") — the inner orientation toward God involves turning from the previous direction; (3) Quietness: Isa 30:15 ("in quietness and in trust shall be your strength") — the inner condition of stillness is required before the movement toward God can be genuinely receptive; (4) Non-stubbornness: Deu 29:19 reverse — the stubborn heart is the posture that prevents movement toward God; Isa 30:15 ("but you were unwilling") — the refusal is the polar opposite of the required posture. The required inner posture: quiet trust, returning orientation, and non-stubborn receptivity.

**Prompt 3: What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

The human-to-God movement in peace reveals that the human person has the constitutional capacity to orient toward God and thereby receive peace — the movement is not coerced but voluntary, and it is genuinely possible. The refusal pattern (Isa 30:15 — "but you were unwilling") demonstrates that the movement can be declined — the human person retains the capacity to refuse peace. This reveals: the human's relational standing with God is not static but dynamically engaged by the direction of the inner person's orientation. When the human turns toward God in quiet trust, peace flows from God to the human; when the human turns away (stubbornness, false peace), the peace-channel is closed from the human side. God remains ready to give peace; the human's inner posture determines whether it is received.


### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

Yes — extensively. Human-to-human extension of peace: (1) The greeting-blessing (H7965K, 2511-001): 1Sa 25:6 — the triple sha.lom blessing extended by messengers to Nabal; (2) Seeking the welfare of others: Est 10:3 (Mordecai seeking the sha.lom of his people), Jer 29:7 (seeking the welfare of the exile city for others' benefit); (3) Peacemaking: Jam 3:18 — the peacemaker sows righteousness in peace; (4) G1514 eirēneuō: Rom 12:18 — "as far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all"; (5) Apostolic peace-blessing: Rom 1:7 and all Pauline letter openings — "grace and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ" — the apostle extending peace in blessing; (6) Speaking peace: Psa 122:8 — "for my brothers and companions' sake I will say, 'Peace be within you'" — peace spoken toward community.

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

The data establishes several inner conditions for genuine peace-giving: (1) Having received peace: the apostolic blessing (Rom 1:7) is the outflow of the apostle's own experience of grace-and-peace from God; one extends what one has received — Joh 14:27's "my peace I give to you" is the paradigm: Christ gives what he IS; (2) Authentic inner orientation toward peace: Psa 120:7 ("I am for peace") — the person who genuinely extends peace is one whose fundamental orientation IS peace; the one whose heart is toward war cannot genuinely extend peace (Psa 28:3 — speaking peace while evil is in the heart is its corruption); (3) Seeking the other's welfare: Jer 29:7 — the extension of peace to the exile city requires an inner orientation of care for those who might be regarded as enemies; (4) Wisdom: G1516 eirēnikos — peaceableness is the fruit of heavenly wisdom (Jam 3:17) — wisdom is the inner condition enabling peaceful extension.

**Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?**

The data implies that genuine peace-extension requires prior reception and formation: (1) Prior reception: the person who has not received peace cannot authentically give it — the greeting-blessing (1Sa 25:6) is an invocation of God's peace upon another, not a self-generated gift; the extension is mediated from the divine source; (2) Character formation: G1516 eirēnikos — peaceableness as quality of heavenly wisdom and of discipline (Heb 12:11 — "the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained by it") — the person who extends peace has been trained; (3) Identity-level commitment: Psa 120:7 ("I am for peace") — the giver of peace is a person whose identity is peace; the extension flows from being, not merely from choosing. The data does not require perfect peace in the giver — Jer 29:7 (exiles seeking the welfare of their captor city) shows peace-extension is possible even from a position of suffering — but it requires genuine orientation.


### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

Yes — but the data for human-from-human reception is less explicit than for God-from-human or human-to-human giving. Evidence: (1) The greeting-blessing: the recipients of the sha.lom blessing (1Sa 25:6's addressees) receive peace from the messenger; (2) The apostolic blessing-recipients: every Pauline letter recipient receives "grace and peace" in the greeting — the community receives peace through the apostolic proclamation; (3) G1516 — the discipline produces eirēnikos fruit in "those who have been trained by it" (Heb 12:11) — the person who submits to a trainer/community receives peace as a fruit of that relationship; (4) The woman healed in Luk 8:48 — "go in peace" — she receives peace from Christ's word; (5) Children of Zion taught by God: Isa 54:13 ("great shall be the peace of your children") — the covenant community's children receive peace through being taught.

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

Enabling conditions: (1) Receptivity — the quieted person is positioned to receive what the peaceful person extends (Psa 131:2 — the weaned-child posture of receptive calm); (2) Genuine seeking: 1Ch 12:17 — "if you have come to me in friendship to help me" — the intention that enables the sha.lom relationship is genuine orientation toward the other's good; (3) Non-hostility: Gen 37:4 — the brothers "could not speak peaceably to Joseph" because of hatred — hatred blocks peace-reception. Blocking conditions: (1) Hatred: Gen 37:4; (2) Stubbornness: Isa 30:15 — the stubborn heart refuses peace even when God offers it; human stubbornness would equally block human-to-human peace-reception; (3) False peace already in place: Deu 29:19 — the person who has self-generated false peace is not available to receive genuine peace from another because they believe they already have it.

**Prompt 3: What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

The person who encounters peace and does not receive it is in the refusal pattern documented at Isa 30:15 and Jer 6:16. Their inner state: (1) Continuing inner restlessness — they do not find the rest they were offered; (2) Moral hardness — stubbornness persists as the inner posture (Isa 30:15 — "but you were unwilling"); (3) Compounding peacelessness — the refusal of offered peace does not leave the person where they were; the sword reaches their life (Jer 4:10) even after the prophetic promise of peace was extended. The inner-being consequence of refusing offered peace: the condition deteriorates rather than remaining static. This is theologically significant — peace refused is not neutral; it worsens the peaceless condition. The person who encounters peace from another and rejects it is not unaffected by the encounter.


### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**Prompt 1: Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

Yes — the data shows different modes of operation. Within existing covenant relationship: God's covenant of peace (Isa 54:10) is addressed to those already in covenant with him — it deepens and confirms the existing relationship. Within existing friendship: H7965J (2510-001) — the sha.lom-friendship bond is most dramatically revealed when it is violated (Psa 41:9, Jer 20:10) — the betrayal is devastating precisely because the relationship was pre-existing. In new relationships: G1514 eirēneuō (Rom 12:18 — "with all") — the call to peace extends to all people, including strangers; Jer 29:7 — seeking the welfare of the exile city involves extending peace to a new (and hostile) community. Eph 2:14 — Christ making peace between Jew and Gentile is the formation of a new relationship between previously hostile parties. The data does not show a sharp distinction in the characteristic itself — peace is offered in both contexts — but the vulnerability in existing relationships (betrayal wounding) and the cost in new relationships (hostile parties) are different.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

Both — with a clear movement across the canon from primarily covenantal to universally-extended. OT: most explicit peace-giving is within covenant (Num 6:26 — to Israel; Isa 54:10 — to the covenant people; Psa 85:8 — to "his saints"). **Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

The data establishes both inclusion and exclusion: (1) Exclusion: "there is no peace for the wicked" (Isa 48:22, 57:21) — moral condition determines peace-access; (2) Conditional exclusion: Psa 85:8 — "but let them not turn back to folly" — covenant-unfaithfulness risks peace; Deu 23:6 — "you shall not seek their peace" for Ammonites/Moabites — historical covenant boundaries; (3) Expanding inclusion: Isa 57:19 — "peace, peace to the far and to the near" — peace offered to those formerly excluded; Isa 52:7 — the herald of peace to the nations; Eph 2:14 — Gentiles included through Christ. The trajectory of scope is progressive expansion: from Israel → to the nations → to all peoples in Christ → to all things (Col 1:20). The only sustained exclusion in the data is moral: "there is no peace for the wicked." The covenantal exclusions are overcome in Christ.

## Formative and developmental dimension

How this characteristic shapes the person over time — nature of transformation, sequence of inner states, mechanism of change, suffering and affliction, formation and sanctification, eschatological trajectory, deposit consequence.


### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

Yes — peace produces transformation at multiple levels: (1) Inner condition change: 1Sa 1:17-18 — Hannah departs after the blessing and "her face was no longer sad" — the peace-word transforms her inner emotional state immediately; (2) Orientation change: Isa 26:3 — the mind's orientation toward God produces perfect peace, which then shapes how the person views all of reality; (3) Self-understanding: Isa 38:17 — Hezekiah's retrospective ("it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness") — the receipt of peace through suffering transforms his self-understanding of the suffering; suffering is reread as the path to welfare; (4) Relational transformation: Eph 2:14-16 — Christ's peace breaks down the dividing wall, producing new corporate identity (one body); formerly hostile groups are transformed into one; (5) Character transformation: Gal 5:22 — Spirit-produced peace forms the person's character over time.

**Prompt 2: Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

The data shows both: (1) Reversible: Jer 16:5 ("I have taken away my peace from this people") — God can withdraw peace, reversing the relational condition; Psa 85:8 ("let them not turn back to folly") — the covenant condition of peace can be forfeited through turning; the human person can move from peace to peacelessness through rebellion; (2) Irreversible (covenantal): Isa 54:10 ("my covenant of peace shall not be removed") — the eschatological covenant of peace is explicitly declared irrevocable by God's unilateral commitment; (3) Irreversible (accomplished): Eph 2:14-16 — Christ's peace-making through the cross is a completed historical act; the dividing wall has been broken down — this cannot be re-erected from the human side. The data distinguishes between experiential peace (reversible, depending on human orientation and divine response to sin) and covenantal/accomplished peace (irreversible, grounded in God's identity and Christ's cross).


### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

Yes — the data documents several sequences: (1) Lam 3 sequence: soul bereft of peace (3:17) → remembering God's hesed (3:21-22) → hope restored (3:24) → quiet waiting (3:26) — the sequence from peaceless despair through memory to hope through waiting; (2) Isa 30:15 sequence: returning → rest → quietness → trust → strength/salvation — a five-stage sequence where each stage enables the next; (3) Psa 32 sequence: sin → suppressed conscience → somatic deterioration → confession → divine forgiveness → peace; (4) Gift-to-fruit sequence: God gives peace (Num 6:26) → received in the mind/heart (Isa 26:3) → enacted in the body (Psa 4:8) → expressed in the community (Rom 12:18) — the reception-to-expression sequence; (5) Formation sequence: discipline → trained → peaceful fruit (Heb 12:11) — the formative sequence through suffering into peace.

**Prompt 2: What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

The sequences converge on three structural insights: (1) Peace is not arrived at directly but through precondition states — trust, returning, quietness, confession, discipline are all precondition stages that enable peace's reception; (2) Peace received produces enabling states — strength, hope, stability, and community expression flow from peace received; peace is both an end-state (received) and an enabling-state (productive); (3) The Lam 3 sequence is the most instructive for the peaceless person: the route from peacelessness back to peace is through memory of God's faithfulness (not through achieving peace directly). The sequence reveals that peace has a pathway structure — it is neither instantaneous (always) nor arbitrary in how it comes and goes.


### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**Prompt 1: What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

The data identifies multiple mechanisms: (1) Divine word/proclamation: Judg 6:23 ("Peace be to you") — the spoken word of peace from God or his messenger produces the inner shift from terror to peace; Joh 14:27 ("my peace I give to you") — Christ's word-gift; the proclamation is a mechanism; (2) Conscience-activation: Psa 32:3-5 — the mechanism is confession activating what suppressed conscience had blocked; the act of acknowledgment opens the peace-channel; (3) Cognitive re-direction: Isa 26:3 — the mechanism is the deliberate directing of the mind toward God; (4) Discipline and training: Heb 12:11 — the mechanism is the sustained experience of discipline by a trainer, producing peace as fruit over time; (5) Cross-accomplishment: Col 1:20 — the mechanism is Christ's blood of the cross making peace; a historical accomplished act; (6) Spirit indwelling: Gal 5:22 — the Spirit's presence produces peace as fruit through the mechanism of indwelling and progressive formation.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

Yes — the mechanisms differ by context: (1) Crisis/encounter context: divine word is the immediate mechanism (Judg 6:23, Dan 10:19, Joh 14:27) — peace comes through an encounter with divine presence or word; (2) Moral failure context: conscience-activation and confession are the mechanism (Psa 32:3-5) — the route back from peacelessness through sin is through honest acknowledgment; (3) Cognitive orientation context: sustained mind-direction is the mechanism (Isa 26:3, Rom 8:6) — for the ongoing inner condition of peace, the mechanism is daily cognitive orientation; (4) Formative context: discipline over time is the mechanism (Heb 12:11) — peace-as-character is formed through sustained training rather than instantaneous receipt; (5) Eschatological/cosmic context: Christ's cross is the mechanism (Col 1:20, Isa 53:5) — for the foundational peace-with-God and cosmic peace, the mechanism is unique historical accomplishment. No single mechanism covers all contexts.


### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

Yes — and the peace-suffering relationship is one of the most analytically significant in the entire registry. Evidence: (1) Isa 53:5 — "upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed" — peace purchased through the Servant's suffering; (2) Isa 38:17 — "it was for my welfare that I had great bitterness" — Hezekiah's welfare arrived through his suffering; (3) Heb 12:11 — "for the moment all discipline seems painful rather than pleasant, but later it yields the peaceful fruit of righteousness" — peace is the outcome of endured discipline (suffering); (4) Lam 3:17 — "my soul is bereft of peace" — the peaceless condition in suffering; (5) 2Cor 2:13 — Paul's spirit not at rest amid his afflictions — the tension between suffering and peace; (6) 2Th 1:7 — "relief [anesis] to you who are afflicted... when the Lord Jesus is revealed" — eschatological peace as relief from present suffering.

**Prompt 2: Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

All four relationships appear: (1) Produces: Heb 12:11 — discipline produces peaceful fruit in those trained; Isa 38:17 — bitterness was the path to welfare; suffering is the generative mechanism in certain contexts; (2) Tests: Lam 3:17 — the soul bereft of peace is the person whose peace is tested to its limit; the sustained peacelessness under catastrophe tests whether the person's peace has deep roots or is situational; (3) Reveals: the person who maintains peace through suffering reveals a peace grounded in God rather than circumstance (Phili 4:7 — "surpasses all understanding" suggests it transcends circumstantial explanation); (4) Deepens: the retrospective memory of peace-through-suffering (Isa 38:17) deepens understanding of God's ways; suffering that produces peace creates a richer experiential knowledge of shalom than suffering-free peace. The paradox of T1 (Q&A-023) is confirmed here: suffering is not peace's enemy but its formative companion in the data.


### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

Yes — and this is explicitly evidenced: (1) 1Th 5:23 — "the God of peace himself sanctify you completely; your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless" — peace and sanctification are explicitly paired; the God of peace is the agent of complete sanctification; (2) Heb 12:11 — "the peaceful fruit of righteousness to those who have been trained" — peace is the outcome of the formative discipline that constitutes sanctification; (3) Gal 5:22 — Spirit-fruit peace — peace is a product of the Spirit's sanctifying work in the person over time; (4) Mal 2:6 — "he walked with me in peace and uprightness, and turned many from iniquity" — the long-arc portrait of the sanctified priest; peace is both the condition and the outcome of his sustained walk with God.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?**

Peace plays a dual role in sanctification: (1) Condition for sanctification: 1Th 5:23 — the God of peace sanctifies; the peace-ground is the condition under which sanctification proceeds; (2) Fruit of sanctification: Gal 5:22 (Spirit-fruit), Heb 12:11 (fruit of discipline) — peace is what sanctification produces. This dual role (condition and fruit) makes peace both the environment of growth and its outcome — uniquely integrative. The implication: a person cannot be sanctified while persistently refusing peace (the refusal-pattern of Isa 30:15 is simultaneously a refusal of the conditions for sanctification); and when sanctification proceeds, peace is among its visible fruits.


### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?**

Yes — the eschatological trajectory of peace is the most extensively documented in the programme's data. Established in T1 (Q&A-032): Isa 9:7 ("no end to his peace"), Isa 54:10 (eternal covenant of peace), Jer 29:11 (future and hope), Psa 72:7 (peace abounding till the moon be no more), Isa 32:18 (people dwelling in peaceful habitation), Isa 66:12 (peace extended like a river), Eph 2:14 (accomplished in Christ), Phili 4:7 (surpassing all understanding), Rev 8:1 (cosmic silence at the seventh seal). **Prompt 2: What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate about its future fullness?**

Present peace is explicitly described as anticipatory: (1) Phili 4:7 — "the peace of God which surpasses all understanding will guard your hearts and minds" — the present peace is already beyond cognitive comprehension but not yet the fullness; (2) Rom 5:1 — "peace with God" as the present justified state is the foretaste of the eschatological reconciliation; (3) 2Th 1:7 — "relief to you who are afflicted when the Lord Jesus is revealed" — present peace under affliction anticipates the eschatological relief-from-all-affliction; (4) Rev 8:1 — the half-hour silence in heaven is the eschatological fullness of the demamah-silence pattern (1Ki 19:12) — the present silence before God's voice anticipates the cosmic silence before the final unveiling. Present peace is a genuine foretaste of future fullness, not merely a symbol — it is the same peace (peace of God, Phili 4:7) experienced partially rather than a different or lesser peace.
