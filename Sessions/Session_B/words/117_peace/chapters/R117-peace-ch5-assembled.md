## Structural relationships (T6)


### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**Prompt 1: Which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently alongside this one in the verse evidence?**

From §G.2 co-occurrence data (OBS-117-016): Top 10 co-occurring registries by shared verse count: R183 heart (33), R187 strength (28), R103 love (26), R43 desire (24), R197 authority (24), R61 fear (23), R78 hope (21), R97 joy (21), R51 distress (19), R182 soul (18). The top five by verse count: heart, strength, love, desire, authority. The quantitative data is confirmed by the anchor verse analysis: Isa 30:15 involves strength (R187); Phili 4:7 involves heart (R183) and mind (R112); Gal 5:22 involves love (R103), joy (R97); Deu 28:65 involves fear (R61), distress (R51). The distress and fear co-occurrences are expected (peace-by-contrast); the strength, love, and joy co-occurrences are more structurally significant.

**Prompt 2: What does the pattern of co-occurrence reveal about this characteristic's place in the inner-being landscape?**

The co-occurrence pattern reveals that peace is a central hub characteristic — it co-occurs with both positive characteristics (joy, love, hope, strength) and negative ones (fear, distress, soul in crisis). This hub position reflects peace's nature as an integrative condition: it is the ground from which positive characteristics flower and the condition whose absence is accompanied by negative inner states. The heart co-occurrence (33 — highest) confirms T2.3's finding that the heart is the primary constitutional location and the most comprehensive relational address of peace. The strength co-occurrence (28) confirms the Isa 30:15 structural finding: peace and strength are causally related (quiet trust → strength). The soul co-occurrence (18) confirms T2.2. The overall picture: peace is not peripheral but structurally central in the inner-being ecosystem.


### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic consistently preceding, following, or accompanying another characteristic in a sequence?**

Multiple sequential relationships are documented: (1) Trust → peace (peace follows trust): Isa 26:3 — "because he trusts in you" → perfect peace; Psa 4:8 — "you alone... make me dwell in safety" → "in peace I will lie down"; trust consistently precedes the peace condition; (2) Peace → strength: Isa 30:15 — "in quietness and in trust shall be your strength" — peace-quietness produces strength as its fruit; (3) Righteousness → peace (peace follows righteousness): Isa 32:17 — "the effect of righteousness will be peace"; Jam 3:18 — "sown in peace by those who make peace... a harvest of righteousness" — the sequence is bidirectional: righteousness produces peace and peace is the condition for sowing righteousness; (4) Peace → joy: Psa 35:27 — shout for joy in response to shalom-welfare; Isa 55:12 — "you shall go out in joy and be led forth in peace" — here peace accompanies and enables joy; (5) Confession → peace: Psa 32:5 — confession → forgiveness → relief; conscience-exercise enables peace.

**Prompt 2: What does the sequence reveal — is the relationship causal, developmental, or correlational?**

The sequences are predominantly causal: (1) Trust → peace is explicitly causal: Isa 26:3 ("because he trusts in you" — causal clause); (2) Peace → strength is explicitly causal: Isa 30:15 ("in quietness and in trust shall be your strength" — instrumentally causal); (3) Righteousness → peace is causal: Isa 32:17 ("the effect of righteousness" — effect = causal output); (4) Confession → peace (Psa 32) is causal: confession removes the suppression blocking peace; (5) Peace → joy appears correlational or mutually enabling rather than strictly causal — joy accompanies peace without being mechanically produced by it. The primary sequences are causal in both directions: trust produces peace; peace produces strength; this creates a reinforcing system where trusting more deeply produces more peace, which produces more strength, which enables more trust.


### T6.3 — Causal and Constitutive Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce another characteristic in the verse evidence — and if so, which one, and by what mechanism?**

Yes — documented: (1) Peace produces strength: Isa 30:15 — "in quietness and in trust shall be your strength"; peace-quietness is instrumentally causal for strength; (2) Peace produces joy: Psa 35:27 — shout for joy because the Lord delights in the servant's welfare; Isa 55:12 — peace accompanies joy in the restoration; (3) Peace produces righteousness: Jam 3:18 — "sown in peace... a harvest of righteousness"; the peace-condition enables the sowing that produces righteousness; (4) Peace produces stability: Psa 119:165 — "great peace have those who love your law; nothing can make them stumble" — peace produces stumble-proof stability; (5) Peace produces receptivity: the silence-dimension of peace enables wisdom reception (Ecc 9:17 — the wise heard in quiet); peace creates the condition for receiving other goods.

**Prompt 2: Is this characteristic produced by another — and if so, which one?**

Yes — documented: (1) Trust produces peace: Isa 26:3 (causal — "because he trusts in you"), Psa 4:8; (2) Righteousness produces peace: Isa 32:17 ("the effect of righteousness will be peace"); (3) Confession produces peace: Psa 32:5 (confession → forgiveness → peace); (4) Discipline produces peace: Heb 12:11 (training → peaceful fruit); (5) The Spirit produces peace: Gal 5:22 (Spirit-fruit); (6) Wisdom produces peace: Pro 3:17 ("all her paths are peace"); (7) Love of God's law produces peace: Psa 119:165 ("great peace have those who love your law"). The inputs to peace are numerous: trust, righteousness, wisdom, discipline, the Spirit, confession, love of God's law. This confirms that peace is not a simple direct gift but a multi-factored condition — multiple inner and relational realities contribute to its presence.

**Prompt 3: Is this characteristic a constituent element of another, or does another characteristic constitute part of this one?**

Peace appears to be a constituent of several larger realities: (1) Shalom is a constituent of the Spirit-fruit cluster (Gal 5:22) — peace is one element within the multi-characteristic Spirit-production; it is not the whole but a part; (2) Peace is a constituent of wisdom's path (Pro 3:17) — "all her paths are peace" makes peace the characteristic quality of wisdom's way; (3) Peace is constituted by covenant: the covenant of peace (Isa 54:10) makes peace a product of the larger covenantal structure. Conversely: the characteristic of reconciliation (R130) appears to constitute peace at the structural level — Col 1:20 (making peace through reconciliation) and Eph 2:14-16 (peace = the breaking down of the dividing wall = reconciliation) suggest that reconciliation is the mechanism that constitutes relational peace. This is the clearest constitutive relationship in the data: reconciliation constitutes relational peace; peace is reconciliation's experiential outcome.


### T6.4 — Vocabulary and Root Sharing

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic share vocabulary terms with other characteristics in the programme?**

Yes — from §G.1 (OBS-117-015): XREF sharing with 13 other registries. The most significant: R34 covenant (6 shared OWNER terms — entire H7965 shalom family); R163 trust (6 shared OWNER terms — same); R103 love (4 — Greek eirēnē family); R213 listen (2 — charesh terms); R194 blessing (1 — H1293). Additionally: the SHALOM root family appears in R34 (sha.lam, sha.lem, shil.lem etc.) — the covenant-completion vocabulary is shared. The G1515 eirēnē family (G1514, G1515, G1516, G1517) appears as XREF in R103 love (G1518 peacemaker). The vocabulary sharing is concentrated in two clusters: the Hebrew SHALOM root (shared with R34, R163) and the Greek eirēnē family (shared with R103).

**Prompt 2: Does vocabulary sharing extend to root-level architecture — a shared root that generates terms across two or more characteristics?**

Yes — the SHALOM root (sha.lem = completeness, sha.lam = to complete/ally, sha.lom = peace) generates both peace (R117) and covenant-completion vocabulary (R34 — sha.lam to ally, sha.lem to be complete). The root-level architecture reveals that peace and covenant-completion are generated from the same root: shalom is constitutively a completeness-word, and the covenantal vocabulary (completeness, allied, full payment) shares the same semantic base. This is not merely vocabulary overlap but a root-level semantic bond: the peace of R117 and the covenant-completion of R34 both derive from sha.l/sha.l — the root of wholeness, completeness, and full settlement. This root-level sharing is the deepest structural evidence for the peace-covenant relationship.

**Prompt 3: What does vocabulary sharing reveal about the conceptual relationship between characteristics?**

The vocabulary sharing reveals that peace is not semantically self-contained but is constitutively linked to covenant (through the shared SHA.L root) and to love (through the eirēnē-family connection). The practical implication: the biblical concept of peace cannot be adequately understood apart from covenant (its structural ground) and love (its relational context). The peacemaker (G1518, owned by R103 love) being an XREF to the peace vocabulary suggests that the practice of peacemaking belongs semantically to the love domain — peace is given and received within love's relational framework. The root-sharing with covenant confirms the T1 finding (Q&A-033): peace is covenant-grounded at the deepest semantic level.


### T6.5 — Distinctions

**Prompt 1: Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what precisely distinguishes them?**

The closest neighbours requiring distinction work: (1) Rest (nuach/menuchah — major component of R117 itself): rest and peace co-reside in this registry, which creates an internal distinction challenge. Rest is the cessation-of-striving dimension; peace is the positive wholeness-condition that rest may produce or accompany. Rest can be given and withdrawn (Deu 28:65 — no resting place); peace encompasses more than the cessation of striving (shalom includes welfare, friendship, covenant, and messianic embodiment). Rest is a component of peace's semantic range but not co-extensive with it; (2) Joy (R97, 21 shared verses): joy and peace are frequently paired (Gal 5:22 — adjacent Spirit-fruits; Isa 55:12 — "go out in joy... led forth in peace"). Joy is the positive affective response; peace is the positive existential condition. Joy can be present without complete peace (James 1:2 — "count it all joy... when you meet trials of various kinds" — joy in trials doesn't require peaceful resolution); peace at its fullest produces joy but is not joy itself. Gap: the boundary between peace and rest (as internal to R117) requires Session D clarity — is rest a dimension of peace or a distinct characteristic captured by the same registry?

**Prompt 2: Where the evidence shows apparent overlap, what is the precise boundary?**

The most precisely delineable boundary is peace vs. trust (R163): trust is the inner act of reliance; peace is the condition trust produces (Isa 26:3 — trust → peace; Psa 4:8 — trust posture → peace condition). They are causally ordered: trust precedes and produces peace; peace confirms and deepens trust. The boundary: trust is volitional (an act), peace is resultant (a state). They are not interchangeable. The peace-joy boundary: peace is the ground-condition; joy is the response-condition. Joy arises from peace but is the felt affective expression, not the existential ground. The peace-rest boundary remains partially unclear — these two may be dimensions of a single complex rather than distinct characteristics. Deferred to Session D.

**Prompt 3: Is the distinction between this characteristic and its nearest neighbour one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?**

Peace vs. trust: kind and temporal position — they are different kinds of inner reality (volitional act vs. resultant condition) at different temporal positions (trust precedes, peace follows). Peace vs. joy: kind — they are different kinds of inner reality (existential ground vs. affective response). Peace vs. rest: degree or dimension — the distinction is unclear; rest may be a dimension of peace (a component) rather than a distinct kind. Full answer requires Session D.

### T6.7 — Dimensional Sharing

**Prompt 1: How many of this characteristic's confirmed analytical dimensions are shared with another characteristic in the programme?**

R117 peace spans 10 dimensions (01 EP, 02 EN, 03 Cog, 04 Vol, 05 MC, 06 RD, 07 VE, 08 Trans, 10 DC, 11 DHC). Within cluster C17: R103 love similarly spans multiple dimensions (EP, EN, Cog, Vol, MC, RD — 6+ confirmed). R111 mercy spans MC, RD, DC, DHC (4). R99 kindness spans EN, Cog, Vol, MC, RD, DC (6). R34 covenant spans EP, EN, Vol, MC, RD, VE (6+). The dimensional overlap is wide — C17 registries predominantly share RD (Relational Disposition) and MC (Moral Character). Peace's distinctive dimensions within C17 are DHC (11 — shared with mercy and compassion but not all cluster members) and VE (07 — distinctive for peace in this cluster) and EP (01 — widely shared). The unique combination of 10 dimensions makes R117 the widest-ranging registry in C17.

**Prompt 2: What does the pattern of dimensional sharing reveal about the relationship between this characteristic and those it shares dimensions with?**

The dimensional sharing pattern reveals two things: (1) Peace is the most integrative characteristic in C17 — it shares dimensions with every other cluster member while also having distinctive dimensions (VE, DHC-in-specific-form) that are less prominent in other C17 registries. This integrative position is consistent with peace as the structural outcome of all the relational-grace characteristics: love, mercy, compassion, forgiveness, grace, kindness, covenant, fellowship all contribute to the conditions under which shalom arises; (2) The DHC dimension (11) shared with mercy and compassion but expressed uniquely in peace through the "X IS peace" ontological structure (YHWH-Shalom, messianic peace, Eph 2:14) — this DHC expression is distinctive in peace and not shared in the same form by the other cluster members. The dimensional sharing reveals peace as both the cluster's most integrative member and its most theologically distinctive (through DHC).
