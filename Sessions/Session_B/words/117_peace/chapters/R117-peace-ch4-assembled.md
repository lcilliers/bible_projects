## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

Primary terms: (1) Hebrew: sha.lom (H7965G and sub-forms) — root SHA.L/SHA.L.M, meaning completeness, soundness, wholeness; from sha.lem (to be complete, to be at peace, to be paid in full); the root encodes the idea of nothing lacking, everything in its proper place. This is the most semantically significant root etymology in the registry — shalom is not "peace" in the modern reductive sense but "wholeness-completeness-settlement." The root's appearance in sha.lam (to pay, to make restitution, H7999) confirms: shalom is what exists when every debt is settled, every breach is healed, every deficiency is supplied. (2) Greek: eirēnē (G1515) — root uncertain, possibly from eirō (to join) — the condition of things joined together; in the LXX, eirēnē becomes the translation of shalom, absorbing its full semantic range. NT eirēnē is not a Greek concept Hellenised but a Hebrew concept translated. OBS-117-009, Q&A-013.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

Sha.lom (H7965G) functions as: (1) Noun (primary): the condition of peace — "in peace I will lie down" (Psa 4:8), "covenant of peace" (Isa 54:10); (2) Noun used as greeting formula: "Peace be to you" (1Sa 25:6, H7965K); (3) Noun functioning as an identity statement: "I am for peace" (Psa 120:7 — ani sha.lom, where sha.lom is the predicate nominative); (4) Noun used adverbially (H7965L): "wholly" (Jer 13:19 — all-verses-fail, no inner-being); (5) Noun in construct chain as descriptor: "covenant of peace" (brit sha.lom), "man of peace" (ish sha.lom, Psa 37:37), "Prince of Peace" (sar sha.lom, Isa 9:6). The identity-statement form (ani sha.lom — "I am peace") is analytically distinctive: sha.lom becomes a predicate nominative, identifying the person with the characteristic. This grammatical form is unique in the registry and is the most concentrated expression of the moral-character dimension.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Sha.lom's semantic range is the widest of any single term in the programme's vocabulary: (1) Completeness/soundness (Psa 38:3 — "no sha.lom in my bones"); (2) Safety/welfare (Jer 29:11 — "plans for sha.lom"); (3) Peace as inner condition (Psa 4:8 — "in sha.lom I will lie down"); (4) Peace as relational condition (Num 25:12 — "covenant of sha.lom"); (5) Peace as eschatological reality (Isa 9:7 — "no end to his sha.lom"); (6) Peace as messianic identity (Mic 5:5 — "he shall be their sha.lom"); (7) Welfare/prosperity (H7965I — prosperity of the wicked, Psa 73:3); (8) Greeting (H7965K — peace-blessing in greeting, 1Sa 25:6); (9) Friendship (H7965J — bond of close friendship, 1Ch 12:17); (10) Divine identity (H7965H — YHWH-Shalom, Judg 6:24). The range confirms: shalom is the most semantically integrative term in the registry.

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary distinguishes: (1) State: sha.lom (H7965G — the condition), menuchah (H4496H — the resting state), shaqat (H8252 — the state of quiet); (2) Disposition/character: sha.lom (H7965G, group 2508-003 — moral orientation), hēsuchios (G2272 — gentle and quiet spirit as character), eirēnikos (G1516 — peaceableness as quality); (3) Act: shaqat (H8252 — to quiet, verbal form), eirēneuō (G1514 — to be at peace actively, volitional act), eirēnopoieō (G1517 — to make peace, the specific act of peacemaking); (4) Process/fruit: Gal 5:22 — peace as Spirit-produced fruit (the formative outcome). The vocabulary is unusually rich in distinguishing the state (what shalom is), the disposition (the orientation toward shalom), the act (making or pursuing shalom), and the fruit (peace formed over time).

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

The vocabulary does not include a single dedicated antonym term, but several terms name peace's absence or corruption: (1) The "no sha.lom for the wicked" formula (Isa 48:22, 57:21) — sha.lom with negation particle; (2) The restlessness vocabulary: balah (to be disturbed/troubled), ra.ga (H7280A — to stir up/disturb) — these name the opposite condition; (3) Isa 57:20 — "like the tossing sea, for it cannot be quiet (sha.qat)" — inability to be quiet as the functional opposite of shaqat-peace; (4) The false-shalom pattern: the vocabulary distinguishes false sha.lom (the words of the false prophets, Jer 6:14) from genuine sha.lom, but the same word is used — the opposition is moral, not lexical. The absence of a dedicated antonym term is itself analytically significant: the biblical vocabulary defines peace positively (through its presence and character) rather than negatively (through a named opposite). Peace is not primarily defined by what it is not.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — multiple person-type terms: (1) Ish sha.lom (Psa 37:37 — "man of peace") — the person whose character is peace; the construct chain ish + sha.lom makes peace the defining characteristic of the person; (2) Ani sha.lom (Psa 120:7 — "I am for peace/I am peace") — the most concentrated self-designation; sha.lom as personal identity not merely possession; (3) Anshei sha.lom (Jer 20:10, 38:22 — "men of sha.lom") — plural person-type term; men defined by their sha.lom-bond; used notably in the betrayal context; (4) G1516 eirēnikos — the peaceable person, one whose character is eirēnē (Jam 3:17, Heb 12:11); (5) Eirēnopoios (G1518 — "peacemaker," Mat 5:9) — owned by R103 but in the peace vocabulary's scope: the person whose characteristic act is making peace. The person-type vocabulary is rich: the registry defines not only the characteristic but the person whose nature is the characteristic.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

Yes: (1) Psa 34:14 — "seek peace (sha.lom) and pursue it" — the imperative "seek" (baqash) applied to sha.lom; seeking-peace as a commanded act; (2) Eze 7:25 — "when anguish comes they will seek (baqash) peace, but there shall be none" — desperate seeking of sha.lom; (3) Jer 8:15, 14:19 — "we looked for peace" — qavah (to wait/hope for) applied to sha.lom; the seeking-of-peace-that-does-not-come; (4) Jer 29:7 — "seek (darash) the welfare" — darash (to seek/inquire) applied to the sha.lom of the city. The seeking-vocabulary is significant: sha.lom is not only given but sought — the data shows humans actively pursuing sha.lom, sometimes successfully (Psa 34:14 — commanded pursuit) and sometimes futilely (Eze 7:25 — sought but unavailable under judgment). The seeking of peace is itself theologically meaningful: it reflects the deep human orientation toward shalom even when it is unavailable.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The LXX's consistent rendering of sha.lom with eirēnē is the foundational datum: the translation decision imports sha.lom's full semantic range into the Greek word, enabling the NT to use eirēnē with full shalom resonance. This is not a gap (the LXX-translation relationship is established and confirmed). However: the data flags NO_WORD_ANALYSIS for some terms, and LSJ (Greek lexicon) data is absent for the Greek terms in the readiness output — the §D lexical section showed no LSJ entries for the Greek eirēnē family. This means the LXX-development analysis is based on the translation relationship rather than full lexicographic comparison. Gap: a formal LXX analysis comparing sha.lom-eirēnē translation patterns (where sha.lom is not translated as eirēnē, and where eirēnē translates something other than sha.lom) would sharpen the boundary. This is beyond the current data's scope.

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

Yes — G1517 eirēnopoieō (to make peace, Col 1:20) is attested primarily in the NT context and represents a verbal neologism: combining eirēnē (peace) with poieō (to make/do) to create a term for the specific act of cosmic peace-making. This term is thin (1 active verse in the corpus, Col 1:20) but carries maximum theological weight — it names what Christ accomplished at the cross as a unique act. G1518 eirēnopoios (peacemaker, Mat 5:9) similarly compounds the root to create a person-type term. The NT coinage of these compound terms reflects the NT's need to describe both the act (eirēnopoieō) and the agent (eirēnopoios) of peace-making — concepts that the OT vocabulary named differently (sha.lom as noun, baqash sha.lom as seeking-peace). The new terminology reflects new theological content: the specific Christological act of making peace through the cross.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc — from SHA.L (root: completeness) through the shalom sub-forms (G/H/I/J/K/L) through the Hebrew silence/rest vocabulary (H5117/H5183A/H8252 etc.) through the Greek eirēnē family (G1515, G1514, G1516, G1517) — reveals that peace encompasses: (1) The root: completeness-as-ground; (2) The inner condition: wholeness, welfare, rest, quiet; (3) The relational form: friendship, greeting-blessing, covenant; (4) The divine identity: YHWH-Shalom, Prince of Peace; (5) The act: making peace (eirēnopoieō), being at peace (eirēneuō), pursuing peace (baqash sha.lom); (6) The person: man of peace, the peaceable (eirēnikos); (7) The eschatological telos: no end to his peace, eternal covenant. The arc confirms the T1 central finding (Q&A-033): shalom is a structured sequence (divine identity → covenant gift → inner condition → eschatological embodiment) whose vocabulary reflects every stage of that structure.


## Verse and literary interpretation (T7.2)

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

Three candidate primary verses — the instruction notes that T7.2.5 will designate the single primary anchor. For now, reading function across the strongest candidates: (1) Isa 26:3 — sha.lom sha.lom functions as the predicate ("you keep him in [shalom shalom]") — the term is the destination/condition to which the trusting mind is kept; the doubled form is the intensifier marking fullness; (2) Psa 4:8 — sha.lom functions as the mode or condition of lying down and sleeping ("in sha.lom I will lie down") — instrumental/modal; (3) Lam 3:17 — sha.lom functions as the complement of a deprivation statement ("bereft of sha.lom") — the term names what is absent. The grammatical function of sha.lom varies: predicate, modal adjunct, complement of deprivation — confirming its range as a condition that can be present, inhabited, or lost.

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

The peace registry draws from all major biblical literary forms: (1) Prophecy (dominant in OT): Isa 9:6/7, 26:3, 30:15, 53:5, 54:10, Jer 29:11, Mic 5:5, Eze 34:25, 37:26 — the prophetic corpus carries the eschatological and messianic peace evidence and the refusal-of-peace pattern; (2) Psalms: Psa 4:8, 28:3, 32:3, 34:14, 62:5, 85:8, 119:165, 120:7, 131:2 — carries the experiential/devotional inner-peace evidence; (3) Wisdom: Pro 3:2/17, Ecc 9:17, Jam 3:17/18 — carries the peace-wisdom correlation; (4) Epistle: Rom 5:1, 8:6, Phili 4:7, Gal 5:22, 1Th 5:23, Col 1:20, Eph 2:14 — carries the NT theological synthesis; (5) Torah/Narrative: Num 6:26, Deu 28:65, Deu 29:19, Gen 28:21 — foundational covenant structures; (6) Lament: Lam 3:17 — the peaceless-soul at depth. The breadth of literary form is among the widest in the programme — peace is not a genre-specific concept but spans the full biblical literary spectrum.

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

Three dominant argument structures: (1) Conditional-causal (if X then peace): Isa 26:3 — "you keep him in perfect peace [because] his mind is stayed on you [because] he trusts in you" — trust → orientation → peace; (2) Covenantal-declarative (God declares peace as covenant commitment): Isa 54:10 — "my covenant of peace shall not be removed, says the Lord" — the declarative form grounds peace in divine word; (3) Paradox-resolution (peace through its opposite): Isa 53:5 — "upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace" — the logical structure is the suffering of one producing peace for others; the conclusion is not deducible from ordinary cause-effect reasoning but from the logic of vicarious atonement. The three argument structures correspond to the three major peace dimensions: personal (conditional-causal), covenantal (declarative), and redemptive (paradox-resolution).

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

Primary contextual settings: (1) Covenantal: the covenant-of-peace texts (Num 6:26, Isa 54:10, Eze 34:25/37:26) — peace is given and confirmed in covenantal ceremony and prophetic announcement; (2) Liturgical/worship: Psa 4:8, 62:5, 85:8, 119:165, 131:2 — the psalmic context is personal worship and prayer; peace is experienced and expressed in the devotional posture; (3) Prophetic warning/lament: Isa 28:12, 30:15, Jer 6:14, Lam 3:17 — the prophetic context of peace refused, false peace proclaimed, and the soul bereft; (4) Eschatological vision: Isa 9:6/7, 32:18, Mic 5:5, Eze 34:25 — messianic and eschatological peace in prophetic vision; (5) Epistolary/communal: Rom 5:1, Phili 4:7, Eph 2:14, 1Th 5:23 — NT peace in the context of the Christian community and Paul's pastoral letters; (6) Crisis/encounter: Judg 6:23, Dan 10:19 — peace given in moments of personal encounter with the divine. The richest contextual setting is prophetic (both warning and vision) — the prophets carry the fullest peace theology.

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

Two verses serve as joint primary anchors across the corpus — one OT, one NT: (1) OT primary anchor: Isa 26:3 — "You keep him in perfect peace [shalom shalom] whose mind is stayed on you, because he trusts in you." This verse names: the divine agent (God as keeper), the condition (shalom shalom — the doubled superlative), the constitutional location (mind — the directed mind), and the ground (trust). It is the most complete single-verse statement of the inner-peace reality. (2) NT primary anchor: Eph 2:14 — "For he himself is our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility." This verse names: the person of peace (Christ = peace — ontological identification), the accomplishment (made one), the mechanism (his flesh/cross), and the scope (between peoples). Together these two verses — one naming the experience (shalom shalom for the trust-directed mind, Isa 26:3) and one naming the person (Christ IS peace, Eph 2:14) — constitute the definitive biblical statement of what peace is.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

Isa 26:3 uniquely: the doubled shalom (shalom shalom) — no other verse uses this superlative form; the combination of cognitive direction (mind stayed on God), trust, and the doubled-shalom outcome in one verse is unique. It establishes that perfect peace is not a goal to be achieved independently but a condition God actively maintains ("you keep him") in the person whose mind is properly directed. The subject-object relationship is: God keeps; the person's mind is the instrument; trust is the ground; shalom shalom is the outcome. No other verse combines all four elements so economically. Eph 2:14 uniquely: the ontological identification of a person with peace (he himself IS our peace) — no other verse in any biblical corpus makes this claim. "He IS peace" (not "he gives peace" or "he makes peace" — though both also appear) is the unique claim. The dual primary anchors together cover the experiential and the ontological dimensions of peace — neither alone is sufficient.


## Somatic dimension — body significance, direction, deposit

How (and whether) the characteristic is grounded in the body — whether Scripture links it to specific body parts, the direction of body↔soul interaction, and any constitutional deposit produced over time.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

Yes — and this is one of the most extensive somatic evidence sets in the programme. Multiple body parts explicitly linked to peace's presence or absence: (1) Bones (etsem): Psa 32:3 ("my bones wasted away through my groaning all day long") — withheld confession/peace produces bone deterioration; Psa 38:3 ("there is no health [shalom] in my bones because of my sin") — OBS-117-149; shalom named as the soundness-of-bones; (2) Flesh (basar): Psa 38:3 ("there is no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation") — flesh linked to peace-as-soundness; (3) Eyes: Deu 28:65 ("failing eyes") — peacelessness in judgment produces ocular failure; (4) Feet/sole: Deu 28:65 ("no resting place for the sole of your foot") — the body's connection to the land as peace's somatic expression; (5) Inward parts/belly (meah): Job 30:27 — OBS-117-066; (6) Body-sleep (Psa 4:8) — the whole body at rest as the expression of peace ("I will both lie down and sleep"); (7) Wounds (Isa 53:5 — "with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's body as the instrument of peace's purchase.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

The body-part links serve multiple functions: (1) Indicative — the body's condition indicates the inner-being condition: Psa 38:3 (no soundness in flesh/no health in bones because of sin) — the body reads out the inner state of peacelessness; the body is a diagnostic display of the inner condition; (2) Emphatic — Deu 28:65 accumulates three somatic signals (trembling heart, failing eyes, languishing soul) to intensify the portrait of peacelessness in judgment; the body amplifies the inner condition; (3) Expressive — Psa 4:8 (lying down and sleeping) — the body enacts peace by doing what peace enables; sleep is the body's expression of inner security; (4) Mediating — Isa 53:5 ("with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's body mediates peace to others; the physical wounds are the mechanism of the relational restoration. The most significant body-link is Psa 38:3: the explicit naming of shalom as soundness-of-bones under sin's influence establishes that inner peace and somatic integrity are structurally connected — not metaphorically but causally.


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

Both directions are evidenced, with the dominant direction being inner-state → body: (1) Inner → body (primary direction): Psa 38:3 — sin disrupts inner peace → no health in bones; Psa 32:3 — suppressed confession (inner withholding) → bones wasting; Deu 28:65 — peacelessness in judgment → trembling heart, failing eyes, languishing soul. The inner condition of un-peace expresses itself through somatic deterioration. (2) Body → inner: Isa 53:5 ("with his wounds we are healed") — the Servant's somatic wounds produce inner healing/peace for others; the body of the Servant becomes the mechanism of inner restoration for the many. This is the most theologically significant body→inner direction in the corpus — a body bearing suffering that produces inner peace in others. (3) Body expressing inner: Psa 4:8 — the body lying down and sleeping expresses an already-present inner peace; the body does not produce peace but enacts it. The primary causal direction is inner → body; the Isa 53:5 direction (body → inner) is the unique redemptive reversal.

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

The primary direction (inner → body) establishes peace as a genuinely somatic characteristic — its absence or presence registers in the body because the body is the outward expression of the inner state. This has two consequences: (1) Peace cannot be fully present in the inner person while the body is in somatic distress caused by inner disorder — the body is a truth-telling indicator of the inner condition (Psa 38:3, 32:3); a person who claims inner peace while their body deteriorates under the weight of unconfessed sin is in the false-shalom pattern; (2) The redemptive reversal (Isa 53:5 body → inner) is extraordinary precisely because it runs against the normal causal direction: normally inner disorder produces somatic deterioration; in the Servant's case, somatic suffering produces inner healing in others. The directionality finding deepens the T1 paradox (Q&A-023): peace comes through its opposite conditions not only experientially but physically.


### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**Prompt 1: Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

The evidence provides indirect but not conclusive evidence for a bodily deposit. What is present: (1) Psa 38:3 and 32:3 establish that sustained absence of peace (inner disorder) produces somatic deterioration — bones wasting, no soundness in flesh. This implies that sustained presence of peace should produce the inverse — somatic health and soundness; (2) Pro 3:2 ("length of days and years of life and peace they will add to you") — wisdom-and-peace produces length of life; shalom is associated with longevity as its somatic fruit; (3) Pro 3:17 ("all her paths are peace") — wisdom's characteristic product is peace as a sustained life-condition. The wisdom literature implies that sustained shalom has somatic extension effects (length of life). However: the data does not explicitly address constitutional deposit in the technical sense (DNA, generational transmission). The silence is methodological rather than substantive — the biblical vocabulary does not operate with the category of genetic or physiological deposit. Gap: the question of generational or developmental deposit for sustained peace is not addressable from this data. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.

**Prompt 2: What evidence supports or contradicts this?**

Supporting: Pro 3:2 (peace associated with longer life — a life-outcome that implies somatic extension); the somatic-deterioration pattern under sustained peacelessness (Psa 38:3, 32:3, Deu 28:65) implies by negation that sustained peace protects somatic integrity. Contradicting: no verse explicitly claims that shalom leaves a genetic or physiological deposit. The closest is Isa 66:12 ("like a river... like an overflowing stream") — the image of peace as overflowing suggests abundance rather than deposit. Neutral: 1Th 5:23 (the God of peace sanctifying the whole person — spirit, soul, body) suggests peace's reach is total but does not specify deposit mechanisms. The evidence supports somatic consequence of peace (in both directions) but not constitutional deposit in the genetic/biological sense. Gap explicitly noted for T5.7.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.**

The evidence is partially silent on constitutional deposit in the technical sense. The data establishes somatic consequence (in both directions) but not deposit. T5.7 consequence: since the evidence for a constitutional body deposit is indirect and inconclusive, T5.7 will not be able to affirm a deposit finding — all three T5.7 prompts will receive N (not applicable) unless T5 analysis surfaces new evidence. This is recorded here for that dependency. The somatic consequence findings (T2.6, T2.7) remain valid independent of the deposit question.
