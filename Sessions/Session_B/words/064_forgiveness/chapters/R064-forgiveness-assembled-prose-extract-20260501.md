# Chapter 1 — Meaning

**Registry 064 — Forgiveness** · *Word Characteristic Summary*

## What it is

> Forgiveness is the release of a legitimate claim against someone who has wronged you — the decision not to hold the debt against them, to cancel the account of injury. The Hebrew and Greek vocabulary covers sending away, releasing, covering over. Forgiveness in Scripture is consistently modelled on what God does: he forgives by genuinely releasing the debt, not by pretending the wrong did not happen. Human forgiveness is called for as a response to having been forgiven, and its absence poisons the inner life of the one who withholds it.


**Working description:** Forgiveness is the relational act of releasing a legitimate claim against an offender — the cancellation of a debt rather than its denial. It operates in four modes (covenantal-objective, divine relational-personal, human horizontal, intercessory), each sharing the single act-structure of releasing-a-hold. It is grounded in divine character (the offended party's disposition, not the offender's merit), produces two specific downstream inner states (love, reverence), and creates the relational condition for restored access. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-018, OBS-064-029.


## Name and naming

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

"Forgiveness" signals the release of a legitimate claim — not the denial that the claim existed but the cancellation of it. The Hebrew salach names a divine-exclusive releasing act. The Greek aphiēmi names a releasing/sending-away. The name signals: (1) an act (not a feeling); (2) releasing (not denying or minimising); (3) a claim that was legitimate (the offence was real). The programme name "forgiveness" therefore carries three implicit signals: something real was owed, the creditor releases the debtor, the debt is cancelled not transferred. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-004, OBS-064-005.

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

H5545 (sa.lach — verb): to forgive; exclusively divine subject in OT. H5546 (sal.lach — adjective): forgiving as dispositional character attribute of God (Psa 86:5 alone). H5547 (se.li.chah — noun): forgiveness as abstract quality possessed by God (Psa 130:4, Dan 9:9, Neh 9:17). The three Hebrew forms name the same reality from three angles: act (verb), disposition (adjective), possession (noun). At the definitional level the Hebrew roots reveal that forgiveness is: exclusively divine at its source, characterological in God's being (it describes who God is, not just what God does), and a possession that God has and can extend. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-019, OBS-064-020.

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

Directional: forgiveness moves from God toward the offender (Psa 130:4 — forgiveness is "with" God and moves toward the one who calls); from the offended toward the offender (horizontal mode). Relational: forgiveness is constitutively relational — it requires at minimum two parties and a prior breach. Constitutional: forgiveness received constitutes the person differently — it produces love (Luk 7:47) and reverential fear (Psa 130:4), reshaping the inner-being orientation of the recipient. OBS-064-018, OBS-064-019, OBS-064-029.


## Kind

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

Forgiveness is primarily an act — the releasing of a claim. It has a dispositional form in the divine character (sal.lach — dispositional readiness to forgive, Psa 86:5) and produces a settled relational condition (the forgiven state). It is not primarily an emotion or feeling. The commanded human forgiveness ("from your heart," Mat 18:35) adds the inner-being dimension: the act must be rooted in a genuine heart-level release, not merely verbal. OBS-064-019, OBS-064-031.

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

Four constituent modes (from v1 Q&A-017): (1) Covenantal-objective — external ritual mechanism producing declared forgiveness; (2) Divine relational-personal — personal declaratory act with inner access-postures (faith, repentance, confession); (3) Human horizontal — commanded release of inner claim against offender, from the heart; (4) Intercessory — seeking forgiveness on behalf of another, grounded in God's character not the community's merit. These four modes are layered — each assumes the prior modes are operative. OBS-064-014, OBS-064-015, OBS-064-016, OBS-064-031.


## Boundary — what it is not

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

The structural opposite is retention of the claim — the inner holding of the offender's debt rather than releasing it. The verse evidence presents this as: (1) unforgiveness poisoning the withholder's inner life (OBS-064-001 — the registry description's fifth element); (2) Mat 18:34 — the unforgiving servant handed over to the jailers ("tormentors" in some translations), imaging inner-being imprisonment as the consequence of withheld forgiveness. The structural opposite is not merely absence of the act but active retention that damages the retainer. OBS-064-001, OBS-064-032.

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

Forgiveness explicitly excludes: (1) Merit-based release — forgiveness cancels the debt unconditionally; it is not a reward for the offender's improvement. The intercessory mode grounds the plea in God's character, not the community's merit (Dan 9:19 — "for your own sake"). (2) Denial of the offence — forgiveness is not the claim that nothing wrong happened; cancellation of a real debt presupposes the reality of the debt. (3) Merely verbal release without heart-level engagement — Mat 18:35 ("from your heart") explicitly requires inner-being depth. OBS-064-016, OBS-064-031.

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

Forgiveness ends and something else begins at the outer limit of the unforgivable sin (Mar 3:29 — eternal sin against the Holy Spirit). This outer limit is not a failure of forgiveness capacity but of the conditions required for receiving forgiveness — the verse implies permanent closure at the level of the sinner's receptivity. The boundary between forgiveness and mercy, or between forgiveness and reconciliation (R130), is not drawn from this registry's data. OBS-064-008, OBS-064-027.


## Modes of operation

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

Five operational modes (expanding v1 Q&A-017 with the G0863 sub-entry analysis): (1) Covenantal-objective; (2) Divine relational-personal; (3) Human horizontal (commanded); (4) Intercessory; (5) Access-permitting (G0863I — the releasing/permitting mode that enables or blocks access — "let the children come to me," "do not forbid"). The fifth mode is distinct in direction: it does not concern debt-release but access-granting, though it shares the releasing act-structure. OBS-064-009, OBS-064-014.

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

The mode determines both direction and inner-being requirement. Covenantal-objective: external → no named inner-being prerequisite. Divine relational-personal: God-to-human → inner access-postures (faith, repentance, confession — not merit-conditions but reception-postures). Human horizontal: human-to-human → heart-level release required ("from your heart"). Intercessory: human-to-God on behalf of others → dependence posture, grounds plea in God's character. OBS-064-015, OBS-064-016, OBS-064-022, OBS-064-031.

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

Forgiveness has a verbal/declaratory mode — the pronouncement "your sins are forgiven" (Luk 7:47-48, Mar 2:5). This is a speech-act form: the declaration itself effects the forgiveness, not merely announces it. Jesus' forgiveness pronouncements function as sovereign declarations that produce the state they name. The horizontal commanded forgiveness may also have a declaratory component (verbal declaration to the offender), but "from your heart" (Mat 18:35) requires the inner-being act to precede or accompany the speech-act. OBS-064-022, OBS-064-031.


## Immediate response

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

Two specific immediate inner states are named: (1) Love — Luk 7:47 establishes a quantity-proportional causal chain: "she loved much because she was forgiven much; he who is forgiven little loves little." Love is the immediate inner-being response to received forgiveness. (2) Reverential fear/awe — Psa 130:4 ("there is forgiveness with you, that you may be feared"). Encountering forgiveness as a divine quality produces reverence rather than presumption. OBS-064-020, OBS-064-021, OBS-064-029.

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

The immediate response varies by mode and direction. Receiving forgiveness personally: love (Luk 7:47), relief of guilt-burden (implied by the "you are forgiven" pronouncement pattern). Encountering forgiveness as divine quality: reverential fear (Psa 130:4). Extending forgiveness: inner release of the claim in the withholder (implied by Mat 18:35 — heart-level release as the act itself). Withholding forgiveness: inner-life poison/torment (Mat 18:34, OBS-064-001). OBS-064-029, OBS-064-021.


## Sustained effect

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

Sustained forgiveness-received produces two sustained inner conditions: (1) Restored direct knowledge of God — Jer 31:34 ("they shall all know me... for I will forgive their iniquity") — the permanent new covenant forgiveness removes the barrier to direct personal knowledge of God. (2) Enabled worship — Mat 5:24 (the command to first be reconciled before offering — forgiveness as prerequisite for worship) implies that sustained unforgiveness blocks sustained worship. OBS-064-017, OBS-064-028.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

Sustained forgiveness-received establishes: (1) Freed relational access to God (Jer 31:34 — the guilt-barrier removed permanently); (2) Love-orientation (Luk 7:47 — the love-response is proportional to the forgiveness received and presumably grows as understanding of the forgiveness deepens); (3) Worshipping capacity (Mat 5:24 — forgiveness as prerequisite for acceptable worship); (4) Capacity to extend forgiveness (Mat 18:35 — the logic implies received forgiveness generates the capacity to extend it). OBS-064-017, OBS-064-028, OBS-064-029.

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

Yes — immediate response (love, reverence) gives way to sustained conditions (freed access to God, enabled worship, capacity to extend forgiveness). The sustained effect is characterological — the person who has genuinely received forgiveness is progressively formed into one who can genuinely extend it. OBS-064-029, OBS-064-032.


## Conditions of reception

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

Inner conditions for receiving forgiveness: (1) Faith — Act 10:43 ("everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness"); (2) Repentance — Act 2:38 ("repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness of your sins"); (3) Confession — 1Jo 1:9 ("if we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive"). These are access-postures, not merit-conditions — they name the inner orientation through which forgiveness is received, not the ground on which it is earned. OBS-064-022.

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

What blocks reception: (1) Pride/self-sufficiency — the Pharisee of Luk 7:36-50 "forgiven little" because his self-assessment requires little forgiveness (OBS-064-029); (2) Prior unforgiveness in the withholder — Mat 6:14-15 implies that refusing to extend forgiveness creates a barrier to receiving it; (3) The outer limit of the unforgivable sin (Mar 3:29) — permanent closure of the receptive condition. OBS-064-029, OBS-064-032, OBS-064-027.

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

Mat 18:34 presents the paradigm: the unforgiving servant is handed to the tormentors — an inner-life state of continued bondage, the claim against the other person becoming a form of inner imprisonment. The inner state of one who encounters forgiveness but refuses to extend it is one of self-contradictory imprisonment: holding the offender's debt while owing an incomparably larger debt that was cancelled. OBS-064-001, OBS-064-032.

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

The data supports at least two secondary dimensions, and they do not compete with the primary but enrich it:

1. **Volition / Agency:** The human act of forgiving is a volitional decision — "from the heart" (Mat 18:35); commanded (Luk 17:3-4). The inner act is an exercise of the will. Volition is a persistent secondary dimension.

2. **Transformation:** The causal chain of forgiveness-received → love-orientation-produced (Luk 7:47; OBS-064-029) and the permanent condition change (afesis; guilt released) both carry the transformation dimension. 3. **Dependence / Creatureliness:** The intercession corpus (group 5379-002) and OBS-064-015 name the human inner posture of radical dependence — "not resting on any claim of merit." This dimension is clearly secondary but present in a significant portion of the corpus.

**Competing?** The secondary dimensions do not compete with the Relational/Social classification; they describe the internal mechanics of how forgiveness operates as a relational phenomenon. Volition is the faculty it engages; Transformation is what it produces; Dependence is the access-posture it requires.

### T3.3 — Memory

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?**

Memory is explicitly engaged — uniquely. Jer 31:34 ("I will remember their sin no more") names a divine memory act as the content of the forgiveness. **Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?**

Forgiveness transforms memory in both directions. Divine: God's memory of sin is permanently set aside in forgiveness (Jer 31:34). Human: the act of forgiving requires the offended person to release the claim held in memory — not to deny the memory but to choose not to hold it against the offender. Unforgiveness is precisely the continuing active use of memory against the offender (the "tormentors" of Mat 18:34 image the inner state of one whose memory continues to exact the debt). OBS-064-017, OBS-064-032.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The memory engagement pattern reveals that forgiveness is fundamentally concerned with **the management of the past in the present** — how the record of what was done is held, released, and reframed across time.

Forgiveness does not make the past event disappear; it changes the relational and cognitive status of the past event. "Remember no more" (Jer 31:34) does not mean the event did not happen — it means it will no longer be held against the person. The past is acknowledged (the legitimate claim is real) and then released (the claim is cancelled). Memory's role is precisely this: to hold the past in the present for relational accounting — and forgiveness is the act that resolves that accounting.

This reveals forgiveness as a **temporally-oriented** characteristic — it addresses the accumulation of the past (guilt, grievance, debt) and releases the person (and the relationship) from being determined by it. No other characteristic in the data addresses the past-in-the-present dynamic as directly as forgiveness. This is a new analytical framing not named in existing observations.

Scope expands from individual to national but reaches an absolute limit at the unforgivable sin. OBS-064-027.


### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial — and if so, how?**

The unforgivable sin (Mar 3:29 — blasphemy against the Holy Spirit) involves the Spirit as the agent whose role in forgiveness is being denied. This is the clearest evidence of forgiveness operating in relation to a spiritual being — the Holy Spirit is the one who mediates the forgiveness transaction, and the one who blasphemes him places himself outside the forgiveness arc. OBS-064-027.

**Prompt 2: Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?**

Mar 3:29's outer limit (blasphemy against the Holy Spirit) implies adversarial resistance to the Spirit's mediating role in forgiveness. Whether this involves a spiritual-beings adversarial structure is not developed in the registry's data. Gap noted. OBS-064-027.

**Prompt 3: Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry in the evidence?**

Joh 20:22-23 ("receive the Holy Spirit; if you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven") explicitly names the Spirit as the enabler of the authority to declare forgiveness. The Spirit strengthens and enables the forgiveness transaction — this is the clearest evidence of a spiritual being's positive role in the forgiveness interface. OBS-064-027.


## Formative and developmental dimension

How this characteristic shapes the person over time — nature of transformation, sequence of inner states, mechanism of change, suffering and affliction, formation and sanctification, eschatological trajectory, deposit consequence.


### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

Both. Condition: forgiveness received transforms the person's standing before God (from guilt-bearing to guilt-free — the forensic condition changes). Orientation: the sustained receipt of forgiveness produces love-orientation (Luk 7:47) and reverential fear (Psa 130:4) as stable inner dispositions. OBS-064-029, OBS-064-020.

**Prompt 2: Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

Jer 31:34 presents new covenant forgiveness as irreversible in its divine declaration ("I will remember their sin no more"). The eschatological dimension is permanent. In the human horizontal direction, individual acts of forgiveness may need to be renewed (Luk 17:4 — "seven times a day"). But the divine forgiveness declaration is structurally permanent once given in the new covenant frame. OBS-064-017.


### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

Sequence: (1) Offence/guilt present (prior condition); (2) Access-posture adopted (faith, repentance, confession — reception-preparation); (3) Forgiveness received (status-change); (4) Love-response generated (Luk 7:47); (5) Capacity to extend forgiveness formed (Mat 18:35 structural logic); (6) Extended forgiveness to others (horizontal expression). For the new covenant: steps 3 and 4 produce permanent new conditions (Jer 31:34 — known by God, sins remembered no more). OBS-064-017, OBS-064-022, OBS-064-029, OBS-064-032.

**Prompt 2: What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

The sequence reveals forgiveness as a relational-transitional event — it moves the person from one inner condition (guilt-bearing, blocked access) through the act to a different inner condition (love-oriented, access-freed). Forgiveness is not a terminal state but a transitional act that produces further inner states. The "torment" of the unforgiver (Mat 18:34) reveals the alternative sequence when the transition is blocked: retained claim → inner imprisonment. OBS-064-001, OBS-064-029, OBS-064-032.


### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**Prompt 1: What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

Two mechanisms: (1) Declaratory mechanism — the forgiveness pronouncement effects a status change. God's declaration ("your sins are forgiven") or the human's inner release creates the new condition. (2) Atoning mechanism — the blood of Christ (Eph 1:7) is the structural ground that enables the declaration. The atonement mechanism is the basis; the declaration is the application. OBS-064-022, OBS-064-028.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

The mechanisms differ across modes: covenantal-objective mode uses the ritual-atonement mechanism (external, structural). Divine relational-personal mode uses the declaratory mechanism grounded in Christ's atonement. Human horizontal mode uses the heart-level-release mechanism (internal, volitional). OBS-064-014, OBS-064-022, OBS-064-031.


### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

Yes — forgiveness is structurally linked to suffering. The atoning mechanism (Eph 1:7 — "through his blood"; Heb 9:22 — "without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness") grounds forgiveness in suffering on the forgiver's side. The somatic compassion (splagchnizomai — Mat 18:27) is a suffered response in the forgiver's body. OBS-064-022, OBS-064-028.

**Prompt 2: Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

Suffering is the cost-side of forgiveness on the forgiver's part. The divine cost is the highest: the death of Christ (Eph 1:7). The human cost is the absorbed wrong: the forgiver absorbs the cost of the offence rather than extracting it. Suffering does not produce forgiveness mechanically but is structurally necessary to it — the release of the claim always involves the claim-holder absorbing what was owed. OBS-064-022, OBS-064-028.


### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

Yes — the commanded forgiveness ("seventy times seven" — Mat 18:22) positions forgiveness as a formation discipline. Habitual forgiveness-giving forms the character of the forgiver toward generosity of relational posture. The Eph 4:32 command ("forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you") places horizontal forgiveness within the sanctification framework of corporate Christian life. OBS-064-031.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?**

Forgiveness functions in sanctification as both a discipline and a formation vehicle: the habitual act of releasing claims shapes the person toward the divine model (Eph 4:32 — "as God in Christ forgave you"). The person who habitually forgives is being progressively formed into the image of the divine forgiver. OBS-064-031.


### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?**

Yes — Jer 31:34 is explicitly eschatological: the permanent new covenant forgiveness is the eschatological state in which sins are permanently released and the guilt-barrier is permanently removed. Col 2:13 ("having forgiven us all our trespasses") names the already-accomplished aspect of this eschatological forgiveness. OBS-064-017.

**Prompt 2: What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate about its future fullness?**

Present forgiveness-received anticipates the permanent guilt-free standing before God (Jer 31:34 — "remember their sin no more"). Present love-orientation (Luk 7:47) anticipates the fullness of love in the eschatological condition. The present acts of horizontal forgiveness (Mat 18:22) anticipate the eschatological community in which relational breaches are fully and finally resolved. OBS-064-017, OBS-064-029.


### T5.7 — Deposit Consequence

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence indicate generational consequence — a deposit carried forward beyond the individual?**

Although T5.7 is closed on the body-deposit question, the generational dimension warrants a separate note because the data touches it obliquely:

DIM-064-SD015 raises the question of **corporate forgiveness** — whether a community's receipt of divine forgiveness has collective inner-being dimensions. Daniel's intercession (Dan 9:19) is on behalf of the nation; Moses intercedes for the nation (Num 14:19 referenced in DIM-064-SD015). The communal scope of forgiveness in the OT intercessory corpus implies that forgiveness can be received at a corporate level — and what is received corporately may carry generational consequence.

The new covenant promise (Jer 31:34) is addressed to the covenant community — "the house of Israel and the house of Judah" — and its scope ("from the least to the greatest") has a generational or at least corporate breadth.

*Gap noted:* Whether corporate receipt of forgiveness produces a generational deposit in the community (a different question from the body-deposit question of T2.8) is not examined in the data. This is a gap that DIM-064-SD015 identifies as a lower-priority Session D question.

**SYN-INTER-064-008 | T1, T2** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1's four modes map directly onto T4's relational directions. Covenantal-objective mode = God-to-human (cultic ritual mediated); divine relational-personal = God-to-human (personal declaratory); intercessory = human-to-God on behalf of others; human horizontal = human-giving and human-receiving. The vertical-horizontal interdependence (T4 — the structural claim that receiving divine forgiveness enables extending human forgiveness) is the relational expression of T1's layered modes (the horizontal mode assumes the divine relational-personal mode has operated). T1 establishes forgiveness as transitional (not terminal); T5 confirms this and maps the formation arc. T1's downstream states (love, reverence) are T5's immediate formation products. T4's vertical-horizontal interdependence (the structural claim that receiving divine forgiveness enables extending human forgiveness) is T5's formation arc described at the relational level. ## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

H5545 (sa.lach — verb): to forgive; exclusive divine subject. H5546 (sal.lach — adjective): forgiving as divine character; Psa 86:5 sole occurrence. H5547 (se.li.chah — noun): forgiveness as divine possession; Psa 130:4, Dan 9:9, Neh 9:17. G0859 (afesis — noun): forgiveness as state/condition; predominantly in "forgiveness of sins" phrase. G0863G/H/I: three sub-entries of aphiēmi ("to release: leave"; "to release: forgive"; "to release: permit"). Root meaning H5545: uncertain etymology — a technical term for divine forgiveness with no clear concrete root image. Root meaning G0863: apo (away from) + hiēmi (to send/let go) = to send away, release a hold. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-004, OBS-064-005.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

H5545 grammatical range: Qal (active — God forgives), Niphal (passive — "they shall be forgiven" — the worshipper as receiver in the cultic context). The Niphal passive is the covenantal-objective mode's grammatical form — the worshipper receives the declared status without being named as the active agent. G0863 (aphiēmi) range: active (forgive, permit, leave), passive (be forgiven). G0859 (afesis) is a state-noun — the condition produced by the act. The grammatical range distinguishes: divine act (Qal H5545, active G0863H) vs creaturely received condition (Niphal H5545, G0859 noun). OBS-064-003, OBS-064-005.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Semantic range: (1) Divine cultic declaration (H5545 Niphal — "they shall be forgiven" in the atonement formula); (2) Divine character attribute (H5546 — God's dispositional readiness); (3) Divine possession (H5547 — forgiveness as quality God has); (4) State of being forgiven (G0859); (5) Personal declaratory forgiveness (G0863H — "your sins are forgiven"); (6) Commanded horizontal forgiveness (G0863H — human commanded to release claim); (7) Releasing attachment (G0863G — leaving family, goods for discipleship); (8) Permitting/granting access (G0863I); (9) Withholding permission (G0863I — moral obstruction). OBS-064-003, OBS-064-009, OBS-064-010.

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

The vocabulary distinguishes: disposition (H5546 — God's character-readiness to forgive) vs act (H5545 — God's performing the act) vs possession (H5547 — the quality God has). Act vs state: G0863H (forgiving act) vs G0859 (state of having been forgiven). Given (God's act — Qal H5545) vs received (human's condition — Niphal H5545, G0859). The grammar is precise about who holds and who receives the initiative. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-005, OBS-064-019.

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

The unforgivable sin (Mar 3:29 — G0863H negative form with "never," "eternal condemnation") is the explicit outer-limit negative. The structural negative of forgiveness is unforgiveness in the withholder (Mat 18:34-35 — the tormentors-state). Both are present: the lexical outer-limit negative (Mar 3:29) and the inner-being consequence of withheld forgiveness (Mat 18:34). OBS-064-027, OBS-064-032.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

H5546 (sal.lach — adjective) names the person-type: "you, O Lord, are good and forgiving (sal.lach)" — God as the dispositional forgiver. G2844-adjacent: no specific "forgiver" person-type term but the agent is named by the active verb construction. The state-noun G0859 (afesis) identifies the one in the state of forgiveness. The person-type vocabulary is asymmetric: the Forgiver (divine, uniquely designated by H5546) and the forgiven (the one in the G0859 state). OBS-064-019.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

Yes — multiple supplication/seeking terms. Dan 9:19 ("O Lord, forgive" — direct supplication using H5545 imperative). The seeking of forgiveness is a rich vocabulary event — petition, confession, intercession. OBS-064-015, OBS-064-022.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The LXX applied aphiēmi/afesis to render OT forgiveness vocabulary (H5545 → aphiēmi in the cultic passages), and the NT continues this rendering — extending it to commanded human practice. The cross-testament development is the use of the same Greek vocabulary for both divine and human forgiveness, which is not present in the Hebrew (where H5545 is exclusively divine). This lexical bridging is analytically significant: the NT does not create a new vocabulary for human forgiveness but applies the divine-forgiveness vocabulary to the commanded human act. OBS-064-006.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The vocabulary arc reveals forgiveness as a characteristic with two distinct lexical domains — a divine-exclusive Hebrew core (H5545 — salach) and a structurally shared Greek vocabulary (aphiēmi — used for both divine and human forgiveness). The arc moves from OT restriction (forgiveness is exclusively divine) to NT expansion (the same act is commanded of humans) through lexical bridging. This arc is unique in the programme: forgiveness is the one characteristic where the cross-testament vocabulary development carries an explicit structural claim — that human forgiveness is shaped by and modelled on divine forgiveness. OBS-064-003, OBS-064-006.
