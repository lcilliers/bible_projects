## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**Prompt 1:** What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The verse evidence directly and explicitly names forgiveness as an attribute of God's character. Three data points converge:

1. **H5546 (sal.lach — Psa 86:5):** "You, O Lord, are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love to all who call upon you." Group 5380-001 description names this as "forgiveness as a characteristic attribute of God's inner being — the disposition of readiness to pardon that belongs to his essential goodness and steadfast love." This is the most direct single-verse attribution of forgiveness to divine character.

2. **H5547 (se.li.chah — Psa 130:4):** "With you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared." Group 880-001 names this as "forgiveness as a divine attribute and possession — a quality that belongs to God's character, that grounds the possibility of the human-divine relationship, and that evokes reverential fear."

3. **H5545 (sa.lach) — divine exclusivity:** OBS-064-003 establishes that the entire Hebrew salach root is exclusively divine in subject — no human ever forgives with salach in the OT. This lexical restriction embeds in the vocabulary itself the theological claim that forgiveness at its deepest level belongs to God's nature.

The character of God revealed: forgiveness is not an occasional divine act but a settled disposition belonging to his essential goodness (Psa 86:5), located structurally with him as a divine possession (Psa 130:4), and exercised exclusively by him in the OT register (salach-root). The nature reflected is: God is the one whose character is constitutively oriented toward releasing the legitimate debt-claim against the sinner.

---

**Prompt 2:** Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Yes. Scripture attributes forgiveness to God explicitly and in multiple registers:

- As **disposition** (sal.lach — the adjective: God is "forgiving" as a character description, Psa 86:5)
- As **possession** (se.li.chah — abstract noun: "with you *there is* forgiveness," Psa 130:4; Dan 9:9)
- As **exclusive act** (sa.lach — the verb: God alone forgives with this root; Mar 2:7 in the NT: "who can forgive sins but God alone?")
- As **declared NT authority** (G0863H — Jesus claiming and exercising the divine act, Luk 7:47-48; Joh 20:23 — the Spirit-given authority to forgive extended to the disciples)

What this reveals about significance in the human person: the human capacity to forgive is a derived capacity — it mirrors and participates in something that originates in God. OBS-064-003 notes: "The spirit-level origin of forgiveness (divine disposition, divine act) is not merely theological framing but is embedded in the lexical restriction of the OT salach vocabulary." The significance for the human person is therefore: when a human forgives (using G0863H in the NT), they are exercising an act whose deepest structural origin is divine. The human act of forgiveness is an imaging of divine character, not a purely natural or social capacity.

**Prompt 2:** Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The evidence is clear and internally differentiated:

- **In the OT:** Forgiveness with the salach root is **exclusively divine** — no human exercises it (OBS-064-003; F-004). The human person has no OT vocabulary for forgiving with the same term God uses. This suggests the OT register of forgiveness is not shared but creaturely-analogue at most — the human person can *seek* forgiveness (intercession corpus, group 5379-002) and can *receive* forgiveness, but the act itself belongs exclusively to God.
- **In the NT:** The aphiēmi vocabulary is **shared** — God forgives (group 5377-001), humans are commanded to forgive (group 5377-002), and Jesus grants forgiveness-authority to the disciples (Joh 20:23). The NT shift is significant: forgiveness is now genuinely shared at the lexical and structural level between divine and human actors.

The data therefore reveals a **developmental pattern**: in the OT, forgiveness is predominantly a divine-exclusive act which humans approach only as petitioners; in the NT, the same act is extended to human exercise through Christ's authority and the Spirit's bestowal. The characteristic moves from creaturely-analogue (OT) to genuinely shared (NT). This is not named as such in the existing observations but is implicit in the term inventory and OBS-064-003/OBS-064-006.

*New finding:* The data together implies a spectrum — from the person in whom the forgiveness-capacity is actively expressed (image functioning), through the person in whom it is suppressed (image distorted), to the person in whom it is permanently closed (image at its most extreme point of damage). This spectrum is not assembled in the existing observations but is implicit in the combined evidence of F-003, OBS-064-001, and OBS-064-024.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**Prompt 1:** What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The characteristic is named **forgiveness** in the programme (Registry 64, word field: "forgiveness"). The name signals three things about its essential nature immediately:

1. **Relational structure:** Forgiveness implies a prior injury and a relational claim. The name presupposes a two-party structure — an offender and an offended — and names the act or disposition of the offended party toward the claim. The name cannot be understood in isolation from the relationship it inhabits.

2. **Direction of movement:** Forgiveness is directional — it moves *from* the one holding a claim *toward* the one against whom the claim is held. The name encodes a one-way release: the forgiver initiates; the recipient receives. This distinguishes it from reconciliation (which is bilateral) or confession (which moves in the opposite direction).

3. **The cancelled debt:** The English word "forgiveness" carries the sense of releasing a liability — "to give" what is "for" (against) another, releasing it. This aligns with the Hebrew and Greek vocabulary (sending away, releasing, covering over — as named in the registry description).

The registry description itself confirms: "Forgiveness is the release of a legitimate claim against someone who has wronged you — the decision not to hold the debt against them, to cancel the account of injury."

**Prompt 2:** What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

At the definitional level, three primary terms establish the immediate semantic profile:

**Hebrew:**
- **H5545 (sa.lach)** — "to forgive": a verb naming the act itself, with divine-exclusive subject in all OT occurrences. At the definitional level this signals: forgiveness in its deepest register is something God does, not something that originates with the human person.
- **H5547 (se.li.chah)** — "forgiveness" (abstract noun): names forgiveness as a possession or quality, something that "belongs" to God (Psa 130:4 — "with you there is forgiveness"). At the definitional level: forgiveness can be named as a thing that exists, located with a person, not merely a passing act.

**Greek:**
- **G0859 (afesis)** — "forgiveness, release": a noun naming the state of release — the condition of having been let go. At the definitional level: forgiveness produces a state in the recipient, not only an event at the moment of granting.
- **G0863H (afiēmi)** — "to release, forgive": the primary NT verb, with the widest usage (64 occurrences in the forgive-sense). At the definitional level: the root meaning is *sending away* or *releasing* — the act removes something (the debt, the claim) from the relational field.

First-level observation: the vocabulary encodes both the *act* (verb forms) and the *resultant condition* (noun forms) of forgiveness. This duality is present from the opening of the vocabulary.

### T1.2 — Kind

**Prompt 1:** What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The data establishes that forgiveness is **all four simultaneously**, with lexical differentiation across forms. Q039 answer states this directly:

1. **Act** — the discrete event of pronouncing or releasing forgiveness ("your sins are forgiven" — Luk 7:47-48; Lev 4:20 atonement outcome). Verb forms: sa.lach, afiēmi.
2. **State** — the condition of being forgiven (afesis — the state of release from guilt; group 879-001). The recipient enters a new condition as a result of the act.
3. **Disposition** — readiness-to-forgive as divine character (sal.lach — Psa 86:5: God is "forgiving" as a character description). The adjective form names an ongoing orientation.
4. **Quality** — the abstract possession of forgiveness as a divine attribute (se.li.chah — Psa 130:4; Dan 9:9). The abstract noun names forgiveness as something God possesses.

The four forms are linguistically distinct and analytically distinct. The kind of phenomenon is therefore **multiple and layered**, not simple. The vocabulary itself requires this conclusion.

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Forgiveness is **not simple in structure**. Even at first encounter, the vocabulary and registry structure reveal constituent elements:

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**Prompt 1:** In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Q003 answer and OBS-064-003 together establish four distinct modes, each with supporting evidence:

1. **As divine vertical act:** God releases the person from guilt and penalty — the inner person receives a new standing before God (groups 5377-001, 5379-001, 5379-002, 5379-003, 879-001). This mode operates from outside the person inward.

2. **As human horizontal act:** The person releases another from the inner claim of injury (group 5377-002). This mode is commanded, volitional, and operates from within the person outward toward another.

3. **As disposition (readiness-to-pardon):** Sal.lach (Psa 86:5) names forgiveness as a settled character orientation — not a discrete act but an abiding posture of the inner being. This mode is constitutional rather than episodic.

4. **As received condition:** Afesis (G0859, groups 879-001, 879-002) names the state of being forgiven — the condition the inner person occupies as a result of the divine act. This mode is passive — it is what the inner person *is* rather than what they *do*.

F-003 adds a fifth mode: **as structural enabler** — forgiveness received creates the inner conditions from which horizontal forgiveness flows. This is the indirect mode: divine forgiveness → inner transformation → human forgiveness.

**Prompt 3:** What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

This reveals forgiveness as a **temporally-oriented** characteristic — it addresses the accumulation of the past (guilt, grievance, debt) and releases the person (and the relationship) from being determined by it. ### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**Prompt 1:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Yes — the God-to-human direction is the **dominant and foundational direction** of forgiveness in the entire corpus. The evidence is comprehensive:

**OT cultic mode (group 5379-001):** God forgives through the mechanism of sacrifice and priestly mediation. Lev 4:20 — "the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven." The forgiveness is received passively by the worshipper as the outcome of the covenantal mechanism. God's act is the subject; the human person is the recipient.

**OT intercessory mode (group 5379-002):** Daniel pleads for divine forgiveness (Dan 9:19 — "O Lord, forgive"). The entire intercessory corpus presupposes that forgiveness originates with God and is sought from him. The direction is explicitly God-to-human in its expected movement.

**OT new covenant declaration (group 5379-003):** Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more." The divine initiative is total — the declaration is unilateral, unconditional within the covenant, and permanent. No human act is named as the trigger.

**NT vertical forgiveness (group 5377-001):** God's act of releasing the person from guilt and penalty — 26 verses in which the divine subject exercises forgiveness toward the human recipient. Luk 7:47-48 — Jesus declaring: "your sins are forgiven."

**NT atoning mechanism (group 879-001):** Eph 1:7 — "in him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses." The Christ-event is the God-to-human act at cosmic scale — the forgiveness grounded in the divine initiative of redemption.

---

**Prompt 2:** What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The data reveals a nuanced answer — the basis varies by context and the variation is analytically significant:

**Covenantal:** The OT cultic mode is covenantal — forgiveness is granted on the basis of correct covenantal performance (sacrifice, priestly mediation). The mechanism is external and the basis is covenant-compliance (Lev 4:20). F-006 answer confirms: "the OT cultic mode — the mechanism (correct sacrifice) defines the conditions."

**Character-based (unconditional within its scope):** Psa 86:5 — "to all who call upon you." The basis is God's own character (goodness, steadfast love) and the only condition is calling. H5546 (sal.lach) names forgiveness as an attribute of God's essential goodness — not conditioned on the quality of the petitioner but on the character of God. Similarly, Jer 31:34 — the new covenant declaration has no stated inner-being prerequisite beyond covenant membership.

**Responsive (to access-postures):** NT access-postures (repentance, faith, confession) name the basis as responsive — God forgives when the inner-being conditions are met. F-007: "These are access-postures, not merit-conditions." The basis is not merit but orientation — God responds to the inner posture of repentance, faith, and confession.

**Christological:** Eph 1:7 — "according to the riches of his grace." The ultimate basis in the NT is grace — divine character overflowing in the Christ-event. The basis is neither covenantal compliance nor human merit but divine generosity grounded in the atoning act.

The basis therefore moves historically: covenant mechanism (OT cultic) → divine character appeal (OT intercessory) → new covenant declaration (prophetic) → grace through atonement (NT). The trajectory is toward increasing unconditionality, with the NT access-postures naming not conditions of merit but orientations of reception.

**Prompt 3:** What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The corpus reveals God's disposition toward the human person through the forgiveness vocabulary with unusual clarity:

**God's disposition is fundamentally oriented toward release, not retention.** The sal.lach attribute (Psa 86:5) names forgiveness as belonging to God's essential goodness — it is not a reluctant concession but an expression of his character. He is *forgiving* as a settled disposition.

**The scope of God's forgiveness-disposition is maximally inclusive.** Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man, and whatever blasphemies they utter" — the comprehensive inclusivity of divine forgiveness is stated before the one exception (Mar 3:29). God's disposition is to forgive broadly; the limit is the single exception, not the rule.

**God's forgiveness is costly, not cheap.** Eph 1:7 — "redemption through his blood." The extension of forgiveness required the atoning act of Christ. The disposition to forgive is genuine and costly — not indifference to the wrong done but the absorption of its cost. OBS-064-033 (Q033 answer) notes: "forgiveness at the highest level is costly to the one who forgives."

**God's forgiveness disposition generates relational openness.** Jer 31:34 — the new covenant forgiveness enables direct knowing of God. The disposition that forgives is the same disposition that opens itself to be known. The forgiveness-character and the relational-availability-character are linked.

The evidence is not silent — the God-to-human direction is the most fully evidenced direction in the entire corpus. The closing condition applies.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**Prompt 1:** What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?

**Atonement as cosmic mechanism:** Eph 1:7 — "in him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses." **Cultic mechanism (OT):** Sacrifice + priestly mediation = forgiveness. Lev 4:20 — entirely external mechanism. Change is produced through correct performance of the prescribed rite. The mechanism is **ritual and corporate** — the individual participates in a prescribed external act.

**Gradual formation (horizontal):** The command to forgive repeatedly (Luk 17:4 — seven times in a day) and always in prayer (Mar 11:25) suggests that horizontal forgiveness operates as a **habitual practice** — a repeated volitional act that, over time, forms the inner-being disposition of readiness-to-pardon. **Internal transformation (downstream):** The love-orientation produced by received forgiveness (Luk 7:47) is not a further mechanism but a **consequence of the declarative encounter** — the inner being transforms in response to what has been received, not through further discipline.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**Prompt 1:** What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The primary verse is identified as Luk 7:47 (Q043 answer) — the verse of highest inner-being density in the corpus:

"Therefore I tell you, her sins, which are many, are forgiven — for she loved much. But he who is forgiven little, loves little."

**Grammatical function:** The primary term (afiēmi) appears in the perfect passive: "are forgiven" (apeontai). The perfect tense signals: the act is complete and its effects are present. The passive voice signals: forgiveness has been received, not achieved. The verb functions as the **explanatory premise** of the clause — the woman's love (the observed fact) is explained by the forgiveness she has received (the interpretive reality). The term carries the entire causal weight of the sentence.

**Argumentative function:** The verse functions as a **causal argument**: observed behaviour (love) ← explained by invisible inner reality (forgiveness received). The argument moves from outer evidence to inner cause. The primary term is the pivot — it names the invisible inner event that makes the observable outer behaviour intelligible.

**Within the broader argument:** Luk 7:36-50 — the Pharisee host doubts Jesus's authority on the basis of the woman's moral history; Jesus's argument (the two-debtor parable, then the woman's actions, then the declaration) culminates in Luk 7:47 as the synthesis: the love visible in her actions is evidence of the forgiveness invisible to the Pharisee. The term "forgiven" is the argumentative climax.

**Prompt 3:** What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?
