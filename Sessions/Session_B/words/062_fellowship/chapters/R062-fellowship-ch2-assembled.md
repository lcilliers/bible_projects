# Chapter 2 — How It Works

**Registry 062 — Fellowship** · *Word Impact Description*

Operations, faculties, constitutional location, relational axis, formative dimension. Drawn from T2 (Constitutional Location), T3 (Inner Faculties), T4 (Relational Interfaces), and T5 (Formative & Developmental).

---

## Constitutional location and boundaries

Where this characteristic operates within the spirit/soul/heart/mind/body structure.


### T2.1 — Spirit-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

The spirit (*ruach*) is not explicitly named as the locus of fellowship in the primary anchor verses. The closest: 2Cor 13:14 ("the fellowship of the Holy Spirit") places the Spirit as the medium or agent of fellowship, not the human spirit as locus. Phili 3:10's "becoming like him in his death" implies spirit-level transformation through fellowship-in-suffering. Partially evidenced. OBS-062-055, OBS-062-030.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

2Pe 1:4's divine nature participation implies the spirit-level as the site of the deepest fellowship — participation in what God is would occur at the innermost constitutional level. This is inferential from the magnitude of the claim rather than from explicit naming of the spirit locus. OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 3: What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

If fellowship's deepest mode (divine nature participation) operates at the spirit level, it reveals fellowship as a constitutional-depth characteristic — not a surface relational category but an event reaching the innermost constitutional centre of the person. The depth of the fellowship event matches the depth of its object: fellowship with the most holy produces the deepest constitutional change. OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 4: If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**

The verse evidence is largely silent on explicit spirit-faculty naming as the locus of human fellowship. The spirit of God mediates fellowship (2Cor 13:14) but the human spirit as the site of fellowship reception is not explicitly named. Gap noted.


### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?**

Yes — Pro 20:30 establishes a body-to-inner movement: physical strokes → cleansing of innermost parts. 2Pe 1:4 implies outer-to-inner: escape from external corruption → inner participation in divine nature. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?**

The sequence runs: external joining/encounter → inner reception and formation → constitutional character fixed. For divine nature participation: escape from corruption (outer) → participation in divine promises (relational) → sharing in what God is (constitutional). For disciplinary fellowship: physical strokes (somatic) → inner cleansing (constitutional). OBS-062-039, OBS-062-058.


### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**Prompt 1: Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

The soul (*nephesh*) is implicated in Ecc 9:4 ("whoever is joined with all the living") — the fellowship of being-among-the-living is a soul-level reality: the nephesh as the living self participating in the community of the living. OBS-062-045.

**Prompt 2: What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?**

Soul-level fellowship (existential mode — Ecc 9:4) reveals fellowship as a condition of the living self — not primarily an act or choice but a given of creaturely existence. The soul that is alive is by that fact in fellowship with the living. This is fellowship at its most basic constitutional level. OBS-062-045.


### T2.3 — Heart

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

Yes — the heart is implicated in the companionship and character-formation modes. Psa 119:63 ("I am a companion of all who fear you") is a heart-declaration: a settled orientation of the whole inner being. Jer 13:23 names fixed moral character (heart-level constitutional formation) as the product of habitual fellowship. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 2: What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?**

Heart-location reveals fellowship as a moral-evaluative act as well as a relational one — choosing companions (Psa 119:63) is a heart-level decision that has moral character consequences (Jer 13:23). The heart that consistently fellowships with the righteous is itself formed righteous; the heart that fellowships with the corrupt is itself formed corrupt. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.


### T2.4 — Mind

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?**

The mind as a distinct faculty is not explicitly named in the fellowship anchor verses. Cognitive assessment of the fellowship object is implied — 2Cor 6:14 ("what fellowship has light with darkness?") is a cognitive-evaluative question — but the mind is not named as the locus of fellowship itself. OBS-062-049.

**Prompt 2: What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?**

Fellowship likely requires cognitive discernment of the fellowship object — 2Cor 6:14 implies the mind can assess compatibility of fellowship objects. Mis-directed fellowship (Hos 4:17) may involve cognitive failure — inability to recognise the nature of the idol being joined to. Gap noted. OBS-062-049, OBS-062-042.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent on mind-location, note this explicitly.**

Verse evidence is largely silent on explicit mind-location. The cognitive dimension is implied (discernment of fellowship object) but not named. Gap noted.


### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?**

The desire-faculty (*epithumia* — 2Pe 1:4 names sinful desire as what must be escaped; H2266 co-occurs with desire vocabulary across 6 verses — SP-062-001). Desire is the inner motivating state directing fellowship toward its object. Fellowship engages the desire-faculty as its primary directional engine. OBS-062-028, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?**

Desire-faculty location reveals that the direction of fellowship is determined by the direction of desire — what the person desires, they fellowship with; what they fellowship with, they are formed by. The desire → fellowship → formation sequence is the constitutional logic of how fellowship shapes the person. OBS-062-028, OBS-062-058.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

Yes — wounds and stripes are the somatic fellowship vocabulary. H2250 (wound/bruise — from the chavar root) links somatic wounding to the fellowship vocabulary. Pro 20:30 ("strokes make clean the innermost parts") and Isa 53:5 ("by his wounds we are healed") deploy somatic wound-vocabulary in inner-being registers. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-032.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

The wound-body link does two things: (1) It grounds the fellowship-through-suffering mode in somatic reality — the suffering that enables fellowship in Christ (Phili 3:10) is real bodily suffering; (2) It links external physical event (wound) to internal moral/spiritual effect — Pro 20:30 "strokes make clean the innermost parts" establishes the physical-to-inner transformative direction. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-040.


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

Body-to-inner: Pro 20:30 — strokes (somatic) → cleansing of innermost parts (inner). Isa 53:5 — Servant's wounds (somatic) → healing in the community (inner-being). The direction runs from external physical event inward to inner-being transformation. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-032.

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

The body-to-inner directionality means the somatic event of suffering/wounding is the vehicle through which inner-being fellowship and transformation are produced. The consequence: fellowship-through-suffering (Phili 3:10) is not metaphorical but physically grounded — the suffering that produces fellowship is real bodily participation in Christ's death. OBS-062-055, OBS-062-039.


### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**Prompt 1: Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

The wound vocabulary (H2250 — bruise/stripe) leaves a mark that persists — a wound is a joining that remains visible. Whether this constitutes a constitutional body deposit from sustained fellowship-through-suffering is not directly evidenced. Phili 3:10's "becoming like him in his death" implies a constitutive bodily transformation but the deposit is not named explicitly. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-055.

**Prompt 2: What evidence supports or contradicts this?**

The wound as a persisting mark (H2250 — its physical logic) supports a body-deposit reading for the suffering-fellowship mode. Against: the inner-being outcomes (cleansing of innermost parts, healing) are internal, not somatic residue. The evidence is ambiguous. Gap noted.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.**

The evidence is partially silent — the wound-mark persists physically, but whether this constitutes a programme-relevant constitutional body deposit is not established. Recording as gap. T5.7 to be addressed at the level of the evidence available.


### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**Prompt 1: Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

Fellowship originates from two sources depending on mode: (1) Divine initiative — God joins (Eze 37:19), God promises (2Pe 1:4), the Spirit mediates (2Cor 13:14). (2) Human volitional alignment — the person joins themselves to (Hithpael reflexive), declares companionship (Psa 119:63), pursues (Phili 3:10). Existential fellowship (Ecc 9:4) is a given of existence — neither divine special initiative nor human choice but the created condition. OBS-062-037, OBS-062-051, OBS-062-045.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

Multiple — three distinct origin points as evidenced in Q&A-062: divine initiative, human volitional alignment, existential given. The origin differs across the five modes. OBS-062-037.

**Prompt 3: Does the origin of this characteristic change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?**

Yes — origin shifts across modes: divine → human → existential, corresponding to the ontological → companionship → existential mode levels. OBS-062-019.


## Inner faculties engaged

How this characteristic engages the eleven inner-being faculties: perception, cognition, memory, affect, creativity, volition, agency, moral evaluation, conscience, conscientiousness, and relational capacity.


### T3.1 — Perception

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?**

Yes — perception is engaged in the discernment of fellowship objects and their nature. 2Cor 6:14 ("what fellowship has light with darkness?") is a perceptive question — the question itself presupposes that the fellowship-seeker can perceive the nature of what they are joining with. 2Cor 6:14's implicit answer is that light and darkness are perceptively distinguishable, and fellowship-seeking requires this distinction. OBS-062-049.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?**

Fellowship enables perception in two directions: it enables accurate perception of the fellowship object's nature (2Cor 6:14 — the call to discern light from darkness), and inverted fellowship impairs perception — Hos 4:17 presents Ephraim "joined to idols" as a condition in which the joining itself has disabled exit. Habitual wrong-fellowship may progressively impair the perception needed to recognise the error. OBS-062-042, OBS-062-049.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The pattern reveals perception as a prerequisite for rightly directed fellowship and a casualty of misdirected fellowship. The person who fellowships well must first perceive well; the person whose fellowship is misdirected is progressively less able to perceive the error. OBS-062-042, OBS-062-049.


### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?**

Yes — Psa 119:63 ("I am a companion of all who fear you, of those who keep your precepts") is the paradigm conscientiousness statement within fellowship: an integrated moral alignment (those who fear God and keep his precepts) expressed as a stable relational posture. OBS-062-048.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

Right fellowship deepens conscientiousness by embedding the person in a community of moral seriousness (Psa 119:63). Wrong fellowship degrades conscientiousness — the moral complicity of Mat 23:30 and the idolatrous bonding of Hos 4:17 both describe a progressively less conscientious inner-being state. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-042, OBS-062-059.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Fellowship is a primary vehicle of conscientiousness formation — the community one fellowships with shapes the moral seriousness and integrated moral response of the person. Right community → deepened conscientiousness; wrong community → degraded conscientiousness. OBS-062-048.


### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?**

Relational capacity is constitutive of fellowship — the characteristic is relational in its definition. 1Jo 1:3 makes the vertical-horizontal relational structure explicit: "our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ." The human-to-human and human-to-God relational capacities are both directly engaged. OBS-062-056.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

Fellowship deepens relational capacity — it is the mechanism by which the person enters and sustains participation with God and others. 2Pe 1:4 represents the deepest possible relational capacity: participation in divine nature. OBS-062-058, OBS-062-056.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The pattern confirms that fellowship is constitutionally relational in its deepest nature — it is not an emotion, cognition, or volitional act that *has* a relational dimension; it *is* a relational phenomenon whose other dimensions (moral, volitional, affective, somatic) are all secondary to and dependent on the relational. This is consistent with the Relational Disposition classification and with the cluster assignment to C17. The relational capacity faculty is not just engaged by fellowship — it is the faculty that fellowship primarily inhabits, shapes, and expresses.


### T3.2 — Cognition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?**

Cognition is engaged in the choice and evaluation of fellowship objects. Psa 119:63 is a cognitively deliberate alignment — the psalmist has assessed who fears God and aligned with them. 2Cor 6:14 is a cognitive incompatibility argument. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-049.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?**

Rightly directed fellowship deepens cognition — fellowship with God-fearers (Psa 119:63) aligns the person with the community of those who fear God, deepening their own capacity for accurate moral-cognitive assessment. Wrong fellowship progressively impairs cognition — the corruption that comes from habitual wrong-alignment (Jer 13:23, Isa 1:23) is in part a cognitive impairment (the corrupt person cannot see their own corruption as Jer 13:23 implies). OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The pattern reveals cognition as both prerequisite for and product of fellowship direction — initial cognitive discernment enables right fellowship; habitual right fellowship deepens cognitive capacity. The reverse is also true: initial cognitive failure enables wrong fellowship; habitual wrong fellowship deepens cognitive impairment. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.


### T3.3 — Memory

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?**

Memory is not explicitly named in the fellowship anchor verses. However, Mat 23:30 ("if we had lived in the days of our fathers, we would not have taken part with them in shedding the blood of the prophets") implies moral memory — the persons being addressed make claims about what they would have done in the past, which is a memory-inflected statement about fellowship. The moral complicity mode involves memory of (or self-distancing from) past fellowship acts. OBS-062-059.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?**

Fellowship may deepen or fix memory — habitual fellowship creates the fixed character of Jer 13:23, which may include a constitutive memory dimension (the person who has been habitually aligned cannot simply forget their alignment). Gap: the memory faculty as a named engagement of fellowship is not evidenced. OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Verse evidence is largely silent on explicit memory faculty engagement with fellowship. Gap noted.


### T3.4 — Affect

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?**

Yes — deeply. 2Cor 1:7: "as you share in our sufferings, you will also share in our comfort." Phili 3:10: the fellowship of Christ's sufferings. Hope (Ecc 9:4). The affective dimension of fellowship is constitutive — sharing in the fellowship object includes sharing in its affective reality. OBS-062-031, OBS-062-055, OBS-062-045.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?**

Fellowship deepens affect — shared suffering produces shared comfort (2Cor 1:7); shared hope (Ecc 9:4) is the affective product of existential fellowship. The affective deepening is proportionate: "as you share in our sufferings, so also you share in our comfort" — the depth of shared suffering determines the depth of shared comfort. OBS-062-031.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Fellowship is constitutively affective — it is not a purely cognitive or volitional alignment but a genuine sharing in the affective reality of the fellowship object. The pattern reveals that genuine fellowship produces genuine shared affect: no shallow affective engagement is possible in true fellowship. OBS-062-031, OBS-062-055.


### T3.5 — Creativity

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?**

**Job 16:4 — rhetorical construction:** *I could join words together against you* — the cognitive-verbal act of joining words is an explicitly constructive act: Job is describing his capacity to *compose* an argument, to creatively assemble linguistic material into a structure that affects another person. The chavar vocabulary (joining) is applied to the act of verbal composition. This is a creative faculty engagement: fellowship-joining vocabulary describes the imagination's capacity to construct.

**1Jo 1:3 — fellowship-chain construction:** The act of proclamation that extends fellowship is a generative act — it creates a new fellowship bond where none existed before. The one who proclaims does not merely transmit information; they originate a new relational community. This is the creative dimension of fellowship's proclamatory mode.

**New finding:** Fellowship vocabulary in the Hebrew (chavar — joining) is used in Job 16:4 to describe verbal and cognitive construction, connecting fellowship's semantic range to the capacity to originate and compose. This may be the clearest instance in the programme of fellowship vocabulary crossing into the creative faculty domain.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

The marginal engagement with creativity (Job 16:4 as the primary evidence point) suggests that fellowship's primary constitutional operation is not creative but participatory and relational. Fellowship enables persons to *enter* existing relationships, not primarily to *originate* new ones (though the proclamation mode in 1Jo 1:3 does show a generative dimension). The characteristic is more about joining what is already there than creating what is not yet there — with the exception of the fellowship-through-proclamation mode.


### T3.6 — Volition

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?**

Yes — volition is central. The companionship mode (Psa 119:63 — "I am a companion") is a volitional declaration. The pursuit mode (Phili 3:10 — "that I may know him") is aspirational volition. The Hithpael stem of H2266 (join oneself to — reflexive volitional) is the grammatical form of volitional self-joining. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-055, OBS-062-007.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?**

Fellowship directs and eventually fixes volition. Initial volitional alignment toward an object (Psa 119:63 declared companionship; Phili 3:10 aspirational pursuit) produces habitual alignment that over time becomes constitutional (Jer 13:23 — fixed character). The volitional act of self-joining (Hithpael) is the beginning; constitutional fixing is the endpoint. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052, OBS-062-055.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Volition is both the initiating act of fellowship and its progressive captive — the will that begins by choosing companions is eventually formed by them such that alternative choices become progressively harder (Jer 13:23 — "can the leopard change its spots?"). OBS-062-052.


### T3.7 — Agency

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?**

Yes — fellowship involves agency in the self-joining modes. The Hithpael stem (reflexive — "I join myself to") is an agentive act. Paul's pursuit of fellowship (Phili 3:10) is an agentive pursuit. Declaratory companionship (Psa 119:63) is an agentive declaration. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-055, OBS-062-007.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?**

Fellowship initially engages agency and then — in the constitutional mode — transforms it. Habitual fellowship with God-fearers produces a person who naturally orients toward the righteous (agency reshaped). Habitual wrong-fellowship produces a person who can no longer choose differently (agency progressively constrained by constitutional formation — Jer 13:23). OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Agency is progressively shaped and eventually fixed by fellowship choices. The pattern reveals that fellowship is an agency-shaping characteristic — the initial choice of companions is made freely, but over time the fellowship object shapes the will until alternative choices are genuinely diminished. OBS-062-052.


### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?**

Yes — deeply. Fellowship with God-fearers produces righteous character (Psa 119:63); fellowship with thieves produces corrupt character (Isa 1:23). Moral complicity (Mat 23:30) is explicitly a moral-evaluative fellowship act. 2Cor 6:14 poses the incompatibility of light-and-darkness fellowship as a moral-evaluative question. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-049, OBS-062-057, OBS-062-059.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?**

Fellowship both requires and shapes moral evaluation. Initial rightly directed fellowship requires accurate moral evaluation of the fellowship object (2Cor 6:14). Sustained fellowship then shapes the person's moral evaluative capacity — right fellowship deepens it; wrong fellowship degrades it (Jer 13:23 — the corrupt cannot perceive their own corruption). OBS-062-049, OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Moral evaluation and fellowship direction are mutually determining: accurate moral evaluation directs fellowship rightly; right fellowship deepens moral evaluation. The pattern reveals a positive spiral (right fellowship → better moral vision → better fellowship) and a negative spiral (wrong fellowship → degraded moral vision → worse fellowship). OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.


### T3.9 — Conscience

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?**

Yes — conscience is engaged in the moral complicity mode. Mat 23:30 ("we would not have taken part with them in shedding the blood of the prophets") is a conscience-inflected statement — the attempt to dissociate from inherited moral complicity is a conscience response. 2Cor 6:14's incompatibility argument implicitly invokes conscience as the faculty that recognises the wrongness of light-darkness fellowship. OBS-062-059, OBS-062-049.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?**

Right fellowship deepens conscience — fellowship with God-fearers produces a community of moral discernment (Psa 119:63). Wrong fellowship degrades conscience — habitual moral complicity (Mat 23:30 — the inherited blood-guilt pattern) gradually mutes the conscience's capacity to recognise the wrongness of the fellowship. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-059.

**Prompt 3: What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

Conscience is the moral sensitivity that enables the initial distinction between right and wrong fellowship objects; fellowship then either deepens or degrades this sensitivity. The pattern reveals conscience as both the gatekeeper and the casualty of fellowship direction. OBS-062-049, OBS-062-059.


## Relational interfaces

How this characteristic operates across the relational interfaces of the human person — God-to-human, human-to-God, human-to-human (giving / receiving / boundaries), and the spiritual beings interface.


### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

Yes — divine initiative is the origin of the highest fellowship modes. God joins (Eze 37:19 — divine action). God promises divine nature participation (2Pe 1:4). The Spirit mediates fellowship (2Cor 13:14). OBS-062-051, OBS-062-058, OBS-062-030.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

God extends fellowship on the basis of divine promise and character — 2Pe 1:4: "precious and very great promises." The basis is entirely divine — not human merit but divine self-giving. OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 3: What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

God's extension of fellowship reveals a divine disposition of genuine self-sharing — God does not merely observe or command but joins, participates, and invites participation in what he is. 2Pe 1:4 is the most radical expression: God opens his own nature to creaturely participation. OBS-062-058.


### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

Yes — and this is the most richly evidenced relational direction in the registry. Psa 119:63 (declaring companionship with God-fearers as orientation toward God), Phili 3:10 (pursuing fellowship of Christ's sufferings), 1Jo 1:3 (fellowship with Father and Son), 2Pe 1:4 (escaping corruption to participate in divine nature). OBS-062-048, OBS-062-055, OBS-062-056, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

The inner posture required: desire oriented toward the fellowship object (SP-062-001); escape from competing sinful desire (2Pe 1:4); volitional self-joining (Hithpael stem); willingness to participate in suffering (Phili 3:10). OBS-062-028, OBS-062-058, OBS-062-055.

**Prompt 3: What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

The human-to-God direction reveals that the human person is constitutively participatory — made for fellowship with God. 2Pe 1:4's divine nature participation is not a foreign addition but the fulfilment of the image-bearer's created participatory capacity. 1Jo 1:3 establishes horizontal fellowship as derived from and dependent on vertical fellowship. OBS-062-056, OBS-062-058.


### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

Yes — richly evidenced. Ecc 4:10 (the friend who lifts when one falls), Psa 119:63 (companion of God-fearers as community formation), Mal 2:14 (covenant companion), Isa 1:23 (companion of thieves as moral-formation warning), 2Cor 1:7 (sharing comfort proportionate to sharing suffering). OBS-062-050, OBS-062-054, OBS-062-057, OBS-062-031.

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

Inner conditions enabling genuine extension of fellowship: sustained desire orientation toward the person (SP-062-001); genuine participation in their reality (not mere proximity — 2Cor 6:14). The Ecc 4:10 friend who lifts has genuinely participated in the fallen person's condition enough to be present to help. OBS-062-050.

**Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?**

1Jo 1:3 makes this explicit: the writers extend fellowship to the readers and ground it in their own fellowship with the Father and Son. The person who extends fellowship genuinely must themselves have received the vertical fellowship that grounds the horizontal. Prior reception of divine fellowship enables extension of human fellowship. OBS-062-056.


### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

Yes — Ecc 4:10 ("woe to him who is alone when he falls — there is no companion to lift him") presents the person who has not received the fellowship of a companion as the structurally vulnerable condition. 2Cor 1:7 — the Corinthians receive the comfort that is proportionate to their sharing in the sufferings. OBS-062-050, OBS-062-031.

**Prompt 2: What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

Inner conditions enabling reception: genuine need (Ecc 4:10 — the fallen person who cannot rise alone); openness to the participatory reality of the fellowship object (2Pe 1:4 — escape from corruption as the enabling condition). OBS-062-050, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 3: What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

Hos 4:17 presents the paradigm case: Ephraim, already joined to idols, cannot receive the call to different fellowship ("let him alone"). The inner state is one of prior competing fellowship that blocks reception of the right fellowship. Misdirected fellowship prevents reception of rightly-directed fellowship. OBS-062-042.


### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**Prompt 1: Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

Yes — covenantal context makes a clear difference. Mal 2:14 grounds companionship fellowship explicitly in covenant (berith): "she is your companion and your wife by covenant." The covenantal bond defines the relational scope of the fellowship. SP-062-007 captures the fellowship-covenant structural question. OBS-062-054.

**Prompt 2: Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

The primary covenantal fellowship is within the covenant community (Israel, the church). The Servant's wounds (Isa 53:5) cross covenantal limits — healing is for "we" who may extend beyond the immediate covenant community. 2Pe 1:4 addresses those who have "received faith of equal standing" — within the new covenant frame. The tabernacle joining vocabulary (H4225, H4226 — set aside) operates within the covenantal structure of the sanctuary. OBS-062-022, OBS-062-058, OBS-062-054.

**Prompt 3: What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

Scope: (1) Existential — all the living (Ecc 9:4); (2) Companionship — those who fear God (Psa 119:63); (3) Covenantal — the covenant partner (Mal 2:14); (4) Ecclesial — the fellowship of believers (1Jo 1:3, 2Cor 1:7); (5) Ontological — those who "received a faith of equal standing" (2Pe 1:4). Scope narrows as the depth of fellowship increases. OBS-062-019.


### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial — and if so, how?**

The occult-charming groups (H2267 — spell/charmer; Psa 58:5, Isa 47:9) involve the use of fellowship vocabulary in relation to non-human spiritual forces — charms are a form of joining with occult power. This is the nearest evidence of fellowship operating in relation to spiritual beings. OBS-062-024, OBS-062-035.

**Prompt 2: Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?**

Hos 4:17 ("Ephraim is joined to idols") may involve spiritual-beings-level adversarial activity — idolatrous joining may involve binding to spiritual forces behind idols. This is inferential from the verse evidence and not named explicitly. OBS-062-042.

**Prompt 3: Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry in the evidence?**

2Cor 13:14 ("the fellowship of the Holy Spirit") presents the Spirit as the mediator of fellowship — the divine Spirit strengthens and enables fellowship rather than merely witnessing it. This is the nearest evidence of a spiritual being strengthening fellowship. OBS-062-030.


## Formative and developmental dimension

How this characteristic shapes the person over time — nature of transformation, sequence of inner states, mechanism of change, suffering and affliction, formation and sanctification, eschatological trajectory, deposit consequence.


### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

Both. Condition: fellowship transforms the inner condition from corruption (2Pe 1:4 — escape from corruption) to divine nature participation. Orientation: habitual fellowship fixes the person's stable orientation toward their fellowship objects (Jer 13:23 — character as fixed as leopard spots). OBS-062-058, OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 2: Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

Constitutional formation from habitual fellowship is presented as difficult to reverse — Jer 13:23 ("can the leopard change its spots?") implies that deep formation becomes resistant to change. Whether this is fully irreversible is not stated, but the formative direction is resistant to simple volitional reversal. OBS-062-052.


### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

Yes — a formation sequence: (1) Volitional alignment / self-joining (Psa 119:63, Phili 3:10 — initiating acts); (2) Habitual participation (sustained fellowship over time); (3) Constitutional formation (Jer 13:23 — fixed character as the endpoint). For divine nature participation (2Pe 1:4): (1) Receipt of divine promise; (2) Escape from corrupting desire; (3) Participation in divine nature. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

The sequence reveals that fellowship works through accumulation: each act of fellowship deposits into the person's character, and over time the deposits become constitutive. The formation sequence is not dramatic-and-instantaneous but gradual-and-cumulative. The endpoint (Jer 13:23 — fixed character) is reached through many acts of habitual fellowship, not a single decision. OBS-062-052.


### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**Prompt 1: What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

Two distinct mechanisms: (1) Participatory transfer — the person participates in what the fellowship object is, and through sustained participation, is formed into conformity with it (Psa 119:63 → Jer 13:23 line; 2Pe 1:4). (2) Wound-as-cleansing — external physical event (Pro 20:30 — strokes) producing internal purification (cleaning of innermost parts). Mechanism 1 operates through habitual relational alignment; Mechanism 2 operates through somatic event with inner-being consequence. OBS-062-019, OBS-062-039.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

Yes — the companionship/habituation mechanism (Mechanism 1) and the wound-as-cleansing mechanism (Mechanism 2) are distinct. Mechanism 1 is gradual and volitional; Mechanism 2 is sudden and somatic. Both produce inner-being transformation but through different pathways. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-052.


### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

Yes — the fellowship-through-suffering mode is central. Phili 3:10: "that I may know him and the power of his resurrection and the fellowship of his sufferings, becoming like him in his death." Isa 53:5: wounds as vehicle of healing-fellowship. 2Cor 1:7: shared suffering enabling shared comfort. Pro 20:30: strokes producing inner cleansing. OBS-062-055, OBS-062-032, OBS-062-031, OBS-062-039.

**Prompt 2: Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

Suffering deepens fellowship — Phili 3:10 makes suffering-participation the path to knowing Christ most fully. Suffering reveals fellowship — 2Cor 1:7 shows that the depth of shared suffering determines the depth of shared comfort (the fellowship is exposed and measured by the depth of sharing). Suffering produces inner fellowship-through-cleansing — Pro 20:30 (strokes → inner purification). OBS-062-055, OBS-062-031, OBS-062-039.


### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

Yes — habitual fellowship is the primary mechanism of character formation in this registry. The companionship-habituation arc (Psa 119:63 → Jer 13:23) is a sanctification arc: sustained alignment with God-fearers forms righteous character. 2Pe 1:4's divine nature participation is the telos of the fellowship-sanctification arc. OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052, OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?**

Fellowship is the primary formation vehicle in this registry — character is formed not through solitary disciplines but through participatory community alignment. The person becomes what they habitually fellowship with. Fellowship functions as the relational mechanism of sanctification. OBS-062-019, OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.


### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?**

Yes — 2Pe 1:4 is explicitly eschatological in trajectory: "become partakers of the divine nature." This is the telos to which fellowship points — not a present possession but a participatory movement into what God is. OBS-062-058.

**Prompt 2: What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate about its future fullness?**

Present fellowship-with-God anticipates the fullness of divine nature participation (2Pe 1:4). Present fellowship-through-suffering (Phili 3:10) anticipates resurrection — "that I may know him and the power of his resurrection." The eschatological trajectory is embedded in the vocabulary: fellowship with Christ in suffering points toward fellowship in resurrection. OBS-062-058, OBS-062-055.


### T5.7 — Deposit Consequence

**Prompt 1: Where T2.8 has identified a constitutional deposit from sustained operation of this characteristic, what developmental consequence does that deposit produce over time?**

T2.8 found partial evidence of body deposit — the wound mark (H2250) persists as a physical trace. If that constitutes a deposit, the developmental consequence is that the wound-fellowship leaves a visible marker of participation. Phili 3:10's "becoming like him in his death" implies a constitutive bodily transformation whose developmental consequence is conformity to Christ's death-and-resurrection pattern. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-055.

**Prompt 2: Does the evidence indicate generational consequence — a deposit carried forward beyond the individual?**

Generational consequence: not evidenced from this registry's anchor verses. The fellowship formations described are personal and corporate, not explicitly generational. Gap noted. OBS-062-052.

**Prompt 3: If T2.8 found no deposit, note this explicitly and close T5.7.**

**T2.8 found no constitutional bodily deposit for fellowship. T5.7 is formally closed.**

The evidence is silent on constitutional deposit in the body, including DNA or generational consequence. No verse in the registry addresses this question directly. The Jer 13:23 image (indelible character) approaches this territory but operates at the constitutional-character level rather than the somatic-deposit level. T5.7 formally closes on the basis of T2.8 silence.


## Synthesis — what T2 through T5 reveal as a whole


### Inter-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTER-062-008 | T1, T2** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1's five-mode structure maps precisely onto T2's constitutional levels: existential mode (soul/nephesh), companionship mode (heart), desire-driven mode (desire-faculty), suffering mode (somatic), ontological mode (spirit — inferential). Definition and constitutional location are co-determining: the mode of fellowship determines the constitutional level at which it operates. The T1 definition (object determines character) is confirmed by T2's constitutional evidence — the desire-faculty as the constitutional engine of fellowship direction means desire determines the object, and the object determines which constitutional level is most engaged.

**SYN-INTER-062-009 | T1, T3** *(outcome: D; structural: enabling)*

T1 establishes that fellowship is object-determined and progressively constitutional; T3 shows that fellowship shapes every inner faculty in conformity with its object. These are two descriptions of the same reality: "object determines character" (T1) is the principle; "every faculty shaped by fellowship direction" (T3) is the mechanism. The five-mode structure (T1) explains why T3 finds diverse faculty effects: existential mode primarily engages affect and relational capacity; companionship mode primarily engages volition, moral evaluation, and conscientiousness; suffering mode primarily engages affect and agency; ontological mode primarily engages relational capacity at its deepest level.

**SYN-INTER-062-010 | T1, T4** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1's five modes correspond directly to T4's relational directions: existential mode = all-the-living (widest scope); companionship mode = human-to-human (giving and receiving); covenantal mode = covenant partner; ontological mode = human-to-God (deepest). The scope-narrows-as-depth-increases finding (T4, Q&A-119) is the relational expression of T1's definition: as the fellowship object becomes more specific (from all-the-living to divine nature), the depth increases while the scope narrows. T1's structural negative (misdirected fellowship) maps onto T4's receiving-direction analysis (Hos 4:17 — prior wrong fellowship blocks reception of right fellowship).

**SYN-INTER-062-011 | T1, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: sequential)*

T1 establishes that habitual fellowship produces constitutional character formation; T5 maps this as the primary formation mechanism and identifies the sequence (volitional alignment → habituation → constitutional fixing). Together T1 and T5 reveal the full arc: the definitional claim that fellowship's object determines character (T1) is the endpoint of a formation process that moves through distinct phases (T5). The five-mode structure (T1) generates two distinct formation mechanisms in T5: participatory-transfer (companionship → habituation → character) and wound-as-cleansing (suffering → inner purification). T1's most radical mode (ontological, 2Pe 1:4) is T5's eschatological telos (divine nature participation as the formation endpoint).

**SYN-INTER-062-014 | T2, T3** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T2's constitutional locations map directly onto T3's faculty engagements. The desire-faculty location (T2.5) corresponds to T3.6 volition and T3.7 agency — desire and will are structurally adjacent in directing fellowship. The heart location (T2.3) corresponds to T3.8 moral evaluation, T3.9 conscience, T3.10 conscientiousness — the heart-level constitutional engagement with fellowship produces the moral-faculty effects. The soul-level (T2.2 — Ecc 9:4) corresponds to T3.4 affect — the existential fellowship produces hope as an affective state at the soul level. Constitutional location determines which faculties are primarily engaged.

**SYN-INTER-062-015 | T2, T4** *(outcome: D; structural: causal)*

T2's constitutional origin analysis (multiple origins — divine initiative, human volitional alignment, existential given) directly maps onto T4's relational directions: divine initiative (God-to-human direction — T4.1); human volitional alignment (human-to-God and human-giving — T4.2, T4.3); existential given (the condition of all-the-living — T4.5 scope analysis). The constitutional origin determines the relational direction: the mode of fellowship's origin determines who is the primary relational agent in that mode.

**SYN-INTER-062-016 | T2, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T2's constitutional map and T5's formative account describe the same reality from space and time perspectives. The desire-faculty (T2.5) as constitutional engine maps onto T5's formation sequence beginning point (volitional alignment — desire-directed). The heart-level formation (T2.3 — Psa 119:63, Jer 13:23) is T5's constitutional-formation endpoint. The body-deposit question (T2.8) opens into T5.7's developmental consequence analysis — the wound-mark as a formative trace raises the question of whether suffering-fellowship leaves a permanent constitutional marker, a question that remains open pending Session D.

**SYN-INTER-062-019 | T3, T4** *(outcome: D; structural: enabling)*

T3's faculty engagements all run in the same relational direction that T4 identifies as primary (vertical — human toward God). Perception, volition, moral evaluation, conscientiousness, and relational capacity are all deepened when directed toward the right fellowship object (God and God-fearers). T3's bilateral finding (right fellowship deepens; wrong fellowship degrades) explains T4's structural negative (misdirected fellowship is more dangerous than absence — Hos 4:17, Mat 23:30): when every faculty is progressively shaped by the fellowship object, misdirected fellowship progressively degrades every faculty, not just the relational capacity.

**SYN-INTER-062-020 | T3, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: sequential)*

T3's snapshot (what fellowship does to faculties in the moment) and T5's longitudinal account (what habitual fellowship produces over time) together reveal that faculty deepening (T3) is the process of which constitutional character formation (T5) is the endpoint. The right-direction faculty deepening (T3 — perception, cognition, conscience, moral evaluation each deepened by right fellowship) accumulates over time into the fixed character of Psa 119:63's declared God-fearer companion, which is on its way to Jer 13:23's constitutional formation. T3 → T5 is the moment-to-character arc.

**SYN-INTER-062-023 | T4, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: sequential)*

T4's relational architecture (vertical primary; horizontal derived) and T5's formation arc (habituation → constitutional character) are structurally connected through 1Jo 1:3: the horizontal fellowship is derived from and grounded in the vertical (T4), and the vertical fellowship is the source from which the horizontal formation extends (T5 — the person who has received divine fellowship can extend human fellowship that is grounded in that divine reality). T4's scope-narrows-as-depth-increases finding is T5's formation telos: the deepest fellowship (divine nature participation — 2Pe 1:4) is also the narrowest in scope and the furthest eschatological endpoint.
