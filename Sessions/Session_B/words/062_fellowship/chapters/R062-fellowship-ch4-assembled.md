## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

Hebrew chavar family (H2250–H2279): root meaning of physical joining/binding. Greek koinōn family (G2841–G2844, G4791): root meaning of holding in common (koinos — common, shared). Together: the Hebrew vocabulary emphasises the act of binding/joining; the Greek vocabulary emphasises the shared object that defines the fellowship. OBS-062-005, OBS-062-007, OBS-062-017.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

H2266 grammatical range: Qal (join/unite), Piel (unite with), Pual (be joined — passive settled state), Hiphil (join words together), Hithpael (join oneself to — reflexive volitional). This range distinguishes: volitional self-joining (Hithpael), passive being-joined (Pual — Hos 4:17 "Ephraim is joined"), and divine-agentive joining (Hiphil). The passive (Pual) at Hos 4:17 is analytically significant — Ephraim's idolatrous fellowship has become a fixed passive condition, no longer volitional. G2842/G2844 are nominal/adjectival; verb G2841 (koinōneō — to participate). OBS-062-007, OBS-062-008, OBS-062-042.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Semantic range: (1) Physical joining/seaming (tabernacle vocabulary — H4225, H4226 — set aside); (2) Occult binding (H2267 — charms, spells); (3) Existential participation (Ecc 9:4 — being-among-the-living); (4) Relational companionship (Psa 119:63, Ecc 4:10, Mal 2:14); (5) Moral complicity (Mat 23:30, Isa 1:23); (6) Wound/suffering fellowship (H2250 — Isa 53:5, Pro 20:30; G2842 — Phili 3:10, 2Cor 1:7); (7) Ontological participation (G2844 — 2Pe 1:4; G2842 — 1Jo 1:3). OBS-062-019.

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary distinguishes: Disposition (Psa 119:63 declared companionship — settled orientation) vs act (Phili 3:10 pursuit — deliberate agentive movement). Received (Pual — Ephraim is joined; God joins — Eze 37:19) vs given/initiated (Hithpael — self-joining; Hiphil — God joins actively). Condition (Jer 13:23 — fixed constitutional state) vs quality (Psa 119:63 — stable relational orientation). OBS-062-007, OBS-062-048, OBS-062-052.

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

The structural opposite/absence appears in the verse evidence (Ecc 4:10 — "woe to him who is alone"; 2Cor 6:14 — incompatibility of light/darkness fellowship) but not in a dedicated lexical form. The absence of fellowship is described circumstantially, not named by a single antonym. OBS-062-050, OBS-062-049.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — H2270 (cha.ver — companion) and H2278 (cha.ve.ret — consort/companion, feminine) are person-type terms. G2844 (koinōnos — one who participates, partner) is the Greek person-type term. Psa 119:63 ("I am a companion of all who fear you") uses H2270; Mal 2:14 uses H2278; 2Pe 1:4 uses G2844. OBS-062-021, OBS-062-026, OBS-062-054, OBS-062-058.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

Phili 3:10 ("that I may know him") is an aspiration/seeking expression directed toward fellowship-with-Christ. Psa 119:63 is a declaration of fellowship-orientation. Neither is strictly a supplication term, but Phili 3:10's aspirational framing ("that I may...") carries a seeking dimension. OBS-062-055.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

LSJ data is absent for G2842/G2844 in this registry (OBS-062-017 notes the gap explicitly). Classical koinōnia was a civic and philosophical term — shared life in the polis, business partnership, shared goods. The NT use retains the "holding-in-common" semantic core but extends it into vertical divine-human fellowship and redemptive suffering-participation — registers not present in classical usage. OBS-062-017.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The vocabulary arc reveals a characteristic whose physical root logic (joining/binding) spans from material seams and wounds through human companionship through occult binding through ontological divine-nature participation — all unified by the single logic of genuine participation-in. The arc is the widest of any characteristic in the programme: from the tabernacle seam to divine nature participation, from wound to koinōnia. This breadth is not random but reflects the universality of the joining-logic: anything can be joined-to, and the nature of the joining determines everything. OBS-062-019.


## Verse and literary interpretation (T7.2)

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

1Jo 1:3 — G2842 koinōnia functions as the programmatic term naming both the content and the invitation: "our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ... so that you too may have fellowship with us." It functions as the relational reality being described and the offer being extended simultaneously. OBS-062-056.

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

Five literary forms carry the primary verse evidence: (1) Wisdom poetry — Psa 119:63, Ecc 4:10, Ecc 9:4, Pro 20:30; (2) Prophecy — Isa 53:5, Isa 47:9, Hos 4:17, Jer 13:23, Eze 37:19; (3) Apostolic letter — Phili 3:10, 2Cor 1:7, 2Cor 6:14, 2Pe 1:4; (4) Gospel/narrative — Mat 23:30; (5) Epistle — 1Jo 1:3, Mal 2:14. Each form shapes interpretation — wisdom poetry presents fellowship as existential and formative; prophecy presents it as diagnostic (right or wrong alignment); epistolary presents it as the goal and ground of Christian life. OBS-062-019.

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

2Pe 1:4 argument structure: "divine power has granted us (basis) → become partakers of the divine nature (telos) → through escaping the corruption in the world (mechanism)." The logic: divine enabling → participatory goal → escape as the pathway. 1Jo 1:3 argument: "we proclaim (witness) → so that you may have fellowship with us (horizontal invitation) → and our fellowship is with the Father and Son (vertical ground)." The logic: vertical ground → horizontal extension → communal invitation. Both arguments establish fellowship as grounded in a prior divine reality that enables and defines the human participation. OBS-062-056, OBS-062-058.

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

Settings: Wisdom (existential-formative), Prophetic (diagnostic-corrective), Covenantal (Mal 2:14 — marriage and faithlessness), Apostolic (theological-pastoral — the fullest theological development of koinōnia). The apostolic setting carries the most developed theological account; the wisdom setting carries the most universal anthropological account. OBS-062-019.

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

2Pe 1:4 is the architecturally complete verse — it names the divine ground (great and precious promises), the human telos (partakers of the divine nature), and the mechanism (escape from corruption). 1Jo 1:3 is the most relational verse — naming both the vertical (Father and Son) and horizontal (fellowship with us) dimensions. Both verses together constitute the primary anchor pair. OBS-062-056, OBS-062-058.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

2Pe 1:4 uniquely reveals the ontological depth: fellowship reaching to divine nature participation. No other verse in the programme's registry corpus reaches this depth — the koinōnos of 2Pe 1:4 genuinely participates in what God is. This is the characteristic's maximal expression. 1Jo 1:3 uniquely reveals the horizontal-derived-from-vertical structure: human fellowship is grounded in and shaped by divine fellowship — this structural derivation is explicit only here. OBS-062-056, OBS-062-058.


## Somatic dimension — body significance, direction, deposit

How (and whether) the characteristic is grounded in the body — whether Scripture links it to specific body parts, the direction of body↔soul interaction, and any constitutional deposit produced over time.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

Yes — wounds and stripes are the somatic fellowship vocabulary. H2250 (wound/bruise — from the chavar root) links somatic wounding to the fellowship vocabulary. Pro 20:30 ("strokes make clean the innermost parts") and Isa 53:5 ("by his wounds we are healed") deploy somatic wound-vocabulary in inner-being registers. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-032.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

The wound-body link does two things: (1) It grounds the fellowship-through-suffering mode in somatic reality — the suffering that enables fellowship in Christ (Phili 3:10) is real bodily suffering; (2) It links external physical event (wound) to internal moral/spiritual effect — Pro 20:30 "strokes make clean the innermost parts" establishes the physical-to-inner transformative direction. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-040.


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

Body-to-inner: Pro 20:30 — strokes (somatic) → cleansing of innermost parts (inner). Isa 53:5 — Servant's wounds (somatic) → healing in the community (inner-being). The direction runs from external physical event inward to inner-being transformation. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-032.

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

The body-to-inner directionality means the somatic event of suffering/wounding is the vehicle through which inner-being fellowship and transformation are produced. The consequence: fellowship-through-suffering (Phili 3:10) is not metaphorical but physically grounded — the suffering that produces fellowship is real bodily participation in Christ's death. OBS-062-055, OBS-062-039.


### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**Prompt 1: Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

The wound vocabulary (H2250 — bruise/stripe) leaves a mark that persists — a wound is a joining that remains visible. Whether this constitutes a constitutional body deposit from sustained fellowship-through-suffering is not directly evidenced. Phili 3:10's "becoming like him in his death" implies a constitutive bodily transformation but the deposit is not named explicitly. OBS-062-039, OBS-062-055.

**Prompt 2: What evidence supports or contradicts this?**

The wound as a persisting mark (H2250 — its physical logic) supports a body-deposit reading for the suffering-fellowship mode. Against: the inner-being outcomes (cleansing of innermost parts, healing) are internal, not somatic residue. The evidence is ambiguous. Gap noted.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.**

The evidence is partially silent — the wound-mark persists physically, but whether this constitutes a programme-relevant constitutional body deposit is not established. Recording as gap. T5.7 to be addressed at the level of the evidence available.


## Synthesis — what the language reveals


### Inter-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTER-062-012 | T1, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1's five-mode structure explains T6's co-occurrence pattern. The desire-R43 co-occurrence (6 shared verses — strongest signal) reflects T1's finding that desire is the internal motivating state directing fellowship — they co-occur because desire directs fellowship. The unity-R167 co-occurrence reflects T1's finding that fellowship is the participatory inner reality of which unity is the corporate outcome — they co-occur because fellowship produces unity. The structural connections in T6 are the cross-registry expression of T1's definitional relationships.

**SYN-INTER-062-013 | T1, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T1's five-mode structure is encoded in the vocabulary's semantic arc (T7): existential → companionship → moral complicity → suffering → ontological maps precisely onto the semantic range (Ecc 9:4 → Psa 119:63 → Mat 23:30 → Phili 3:10 → 2Pe 1:4). The vocabulary is not a loose collection but a precisely structured arc corresponding to the definitional five modes. T7's grammatical analysis confirms T1's dispositional/act/constitutional distinction: the Hithpael (self-joining act) corresponds to T1's active dimension; the Pual (being-joined condition — Hos 4:17) corresponds to T1's constitutional dimension; the adjectival terms (H2270 companion, H2278 consort) correspond to T1's dispositional dimension.

**SYN-INTER-062-017 | T2, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T2's desire-faculty location (H2266 and desire vocabulary — 6-verse co-occurrence with R43) explains why desire (R43) is the strongest co-occurrence signal in T6. The co-occurrence is constitutionally grounded — desire and fellowship operate at the same constitutional level (the faculty that directs choice of object). T2's somatic location (H2250 wound — body) explains the cross-registry connections to R30 contrition and R151 sorrow at Isa 53:5 — all three characteristics meet at the somatic-constitutional level in that verse.

**SYN-INTER-062-018 | T2, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T2's constitutional levels (soul, heart, desire-faculty, somatic) are each evidenced by specific lexical items that T7 analyses. Soul-level (Ecc 9:4 — H2266 Qal); heart-level (Psa 119:63, Jer 13:23 — H2270, H2266 Hithpael); somatic (H2250 — Pro 20:30, Isa 53:5). The grammatical range (T7.1.2) is the lexical expression of the constitutional range (T2): the Hithpael (volitional self-joining) is the heart-level agentive act; the Pual (being-joined — passive settled state) is the constitutional fixing endpoint; the adjectival terms (H2270/H2278 — companion/consort) are the heart-level dispositional forms. T7 provides the vocabulary basis for T2's constitutional account.

**SYN-INTER-062-021 | T3, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: parallel)*

T3's moral evaluation and conscience faculty engagements explain the co-occurrence pattern with desire (R43) in T6. Desire (R43) directs fellowship; conscience assesses the moral quality of the fellowship object; moral evaluation confirms or challenges the direction. The three characteristics (desire, fellowship, conscience) operate in sequence: desire directs → conscience evaluates → fellowship forms. The T6 co-occurrence signal between R43 and R062 is the shared-verse evidence of this sequential relationship that T3's faculty analysis illuminates.

**SYN-INTER-062-022 | T3, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T7's literary forms explain why T3 finds different faculty engagements in different evidential contexts. The wisdom poetry setting (Psa 119:63, Jer 13:23, Ecc 9:4) engages primarily the volitional, conscientiousness, and moral evaluation faculties. The apostolic epistolary setting (Phili 3:10, 2Cor 1:7, 2Pe 1:4, 1Jo 1:3) engages primarily the relational capacity, affect, and agency faculties. The prophetic setting (Hos 4:17, Isa 53:5, Jer 13:23) engages primarily the moral evaluation and conscience faculties (diagnostic-corrective function). Literary form determines which faculty register the fellowship vocabulary is deployed in.

**SYN-INTER-062-024 | T4, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T4's relational directions and T6's structural connections are expressing the same reality from two perspectives. The God-to-human direction (T4.1 — divine initiative) corresponds to the R180 yielding connection (T6 — shared anchor 2Pe 1:4): the divine initiative at 2Pe 1:4 requires an inner human yielding (escape from corruption) as its receiving condition. The human-to-human giving direction (T4.3 — companionship, covenant, comfort-sharing) corresponds to the R34 covenant, R192 comfort, and R43 desire connections in T6. The relational directions (T4) and the structural cross-registry connections (T6) are the inner-being and cross-registry expressions of the same relational movements.

**SYN-INTER-062-025 | T4, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T4's relational directions map directly onto T7's literary forms. The divine-to-human direction (T4.1) is primarily carried by the apostolic epistolary setting (2Pe 1:4, 2Cor 13:14, 1Jo 1:3). The human-to-God direction (T4.2) is primarily carried by wisdom poetry (Psa 119:63, Phili 3:10) and apostolic letter (Phili 3:10). The human-giving direction (T4.3) is carried by wisdom poetry (Ecc 4:10), apostolic letter (2Cor 1:7), and covenantal text (Mal 2:14). The literary form determines which relational direction is expressed: the apostolic letter form enables the most explicit vertical formulation; wisdom poetry enables the most explicit horizontal formulation.

**SYN-INTER-062-026 | T5, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: causal)*

T5's formation mechanisms (participatory transfer and wound-as-cleansing) explain why fellowship is structurally connected in T6 to both desire (R43 — participatory-transfer mechanism begins with desire-directed alignment) and to contrition/sorrow (R30, R151 — wound-as-cleansing mechanism meets contrition and sorrow at Isa 53:5). T5's two formation mechanisms generate two distinct clusters of T6 structural connections: the habitual-alignment cluster (R43 desire, R167 unity, R34 covenant) and the suffering-cleansing cluster (R30 contrition, R151 sorrow, R192 comfort). Formation mechanism determines structural cluster.

**SYN-INTER-062-027 | T5, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: constitutive)*

T5's formation sequence (volitional alignment → habituation → constitutional fixing) is grounded in T7's verse evidence. The sequence beginning point (volitional alignment) is carried by Psa 119:63 (wisdom poetry — declared dispositional alignment). The habituation arc is carried by the prophetic diagnostic setting (Jer 13:23, Hos 4:17 — naming the fixed endpoint of wrong habitual fellowship). The constitutional telos (divine nature participation) is carried by the apostolic-theological setting (2Pe 1:4). T7's literary forms track the formation sequence from beginning (wisdom poetry) through middle (prophetic diagnosis) to eschatological telos (apostolic theology).
