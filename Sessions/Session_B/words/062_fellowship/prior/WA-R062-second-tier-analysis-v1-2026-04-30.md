## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**Prompt 1:** What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis / Adds structure*

The verse evidence reveals three dimensions of God's nature that fellowship images in the human person.

**First: God as the initiating giver of participation.** 2Pe 1:4 presents God as the one who grants precious and very great promises specifically so that human beings may *become partakers of the divine nature*. The initiative is entirely God's — he grants, humans receive and become. This images a God whose nature is not self-enclosed but self-communicating: he opens his own nature to creaturely participation. Fellowship, at its highest expression, is therefore not a human social arrangement but an analogue to a divine self-giving that precedes it.

**Second: God as the one who actively joins what is divided.** Eze 37:19 presents God as the agent who takes the stick of Ephraim and the stick of Judah and makes them one in his hand. The action is direct divine joining — God himself performs the act of unification. This images a God whose nature includes active relational restoration: the divided does not remain divided when God acts. Fellowship is the inner-relational participation in a divine nature that is itself unifying.

**Third: God as witness and guardian of covenant fellowship.** Mal 2:14 presents God as witness to the covenant between husband and wife — specifically to the companion bond (*chaverah*) that faithlessness violates. God cares about the relational bond at the level of covenant. This images a God whose nature includes attentiveness to relational faithfulness: the covenantal companion-bond is not merely human but is held by God as witness.

Together, the data reveals that God's nature reflected in fellowship is: self-communicating (he shares divine nature), unifying (he joins the divided), and covenant-attentive (he holds relational bonds in witness). Human fellowship, at its best, images all three.

---

**Prompt 2:** Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

Scripture does not use the explicit vocabulary of fellowship (koinōnia, chavar, chaver) to describe God's own inner life or inter-Trinitarian relations within the verse evidence of this registry. However, God is represented as the *source and enabler* of fellowship in the highest register (2Pe 1:4 — divine nature participation) and as the *agent of fellowship-action* (Eze 37:19 — active joining). The attribution is functional rather than direct: God does not describe himself as a *fellow* or participant in the same way he describes himself as loving or merciful, but his actions constitute fellowship at its deepest level.

This has a significant implication for the human person: if fellowship in its highest form is what God grants access to (his own nature), and what God enacts (joining of the divided), then the human capacity for fellowship is not merely a social faculty. It is the creaturely analogue of a divine activity. The human person who participates genuinely in fellowship is doing something that structurally resembles what God does when he joins the divided into one.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**Prompt 1:** What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**Status: P** | *Adds structure / Gap identified*

The verse evidence establishes several purposes fellowship serves in the human person, though the data does not frame these explicitly in terms of *created design* — the evidential frame is primarily soteriological, moral, and covenantal rather than protological.

**What fellowship equips the person to become:** 2Pe 1:4 presents fellowship with divine nature as the mechanism by which persons *escape from the corruption that is in the world because of sinful desire* and *become partakers of the divine nature*. The telos is participation in the nature of God — fellowship equips the person for ontological transformation toward divine likeness.

**What fellowship equips the person to do:** Ecc 4:10 shows fellowship in its mutual-support function: *if they fall, one will lift up his fellow*. Fellowship equips the person for practical inter-relational action — lifting up the fallen. Psa 119:63 grounds companionship in moral alignment: fellowship with those who fear God equips the person for righteous life. 1Jo 1:3 shows fellowship equipping persons to extend the relational chain: hearing leads to fellowship, and fellowship is proclaimed to create further fellowship.

**What fellowship equips the person to be:** The co-occurrence with unity (R167), love (R103), and comfort (R192) suggests that fellowship is part of a cluster of characteristics that constitute the person's relational inner being. Fellowship equips the person to be a participant — not merely an observer or individual — in shared life.

**Gap identified:** The data does not directly address whether fellowship is part of the *original created design* or whether its higher registers (divine nature participation) are responses to the fallen condition. This is noted below in Prompt 2.

**Prompt 3:** Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?

**Status: P** | *New finding*

The evidence carries a forward orientation, though it is not framed in explicit eschatological language.

**2Pe 1:4** — the construction *you may become partakers* is a future-oriented becoming, not a present possession. The divine nature participation is presented as a trajectory: the promises are given so that participation may be entered. This is forward movement.

**New finding:** The registry data, read as a whole, supports the observation that fellowship in its highest register (koinōnia vocabulary) is structurally forward-facing: it is entered progressively, it grounds hope, and its fullness is not yet possessed. This is a structural feature of fellowship that is not explicitly assembled in the existing findings. It has implications for T5.6 (Eschatological Trajectory).

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**Prompt 1:** Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate (covenantal, eschatological, christological)?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Yes. The evidence establishes typological use in at least two directions.

**Christological:** Phili 3:10 (*that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death*) — fellowship with Christ's sufferings and death is explicitly oriented toward resurrection. The fellowship is not contained within the suffering moment; it points through suffering toward transformation. This is typological in the sense that the fellowship with Christ's death pattern images the death-resurrection pattern of the Gospel.

**Eschatological:** 2Pe 1:4 — divine nature participation is presented as an escape from corruption *in the world*. The trajectory of this participation points beyond present conditions. The fellowship with divine nature is a present entry into something whose fullness belongs to the age to come.

**Covenantal:** Eze 37:19 — the joining of Judah and Ephraim into one is explicitly an act of covenant restoration. The two sticks become one in God's hand — a physical/political sign pointing to the restoration of the covenant people. The fellowship of the restored community points to the covenant fulfilment.

**New finding:** The fellowship vocabulary across this registry carries typological weight in all three registers simultaneously (christological, eschatological, covenantal). This assembly has not been made explicitly in the prior findings, which addressed these dimensions individually.

---

**Prompt 2:** If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

The direction is bidirectional and varies by group.

**Divine-to-human direction:** In 2Pe 1:4, the divine nature is the *telos* toward which human participation moves. God's nature establishes the pattern; human fellowship points toward it. The direction is: divine origin → human participation → divine fullness. God's nature is the destination.

**Human-to-divine direction (via Christ):** In Phili 3:10, Paul's fellowship with Christ's sufferings participates in the christological pattern (death-resurrection). The human instance (Paul's suffering) points toward and participates in the divine pattern (Christ's resurrection). The direction is: human suffering → fellowship with Christ's death → resurrection. Here the human instance *points toward* the divine reality by participating in it.

**Covenantal-to-eschatological direction:** In Eze 37:19, the physical joining of the sticks points toward national restoration, which itself points toward the eschatological community. The human/national instance points toward the divine fulfilment.

**New finding:** Fellowship is not typologically unidirectional. Its typological structure is participatory — the human enters the divine pattern, and in entering it, is oriented toward its fullness. This is distinct from the simpler form of typology where a human event merely foreshadows a divine one without genuine participation.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**Prompt 1:** What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

The characteristic is named **fellowship** in the programme (Registry 62, word field: "fellowship"). The name signals three things about its essential nature immediately.

**Participation as structure:** Fellowship is not a simple state or quality but a structural relation — to be in fellowship is to be *in* something with another. The name presupposes at least two parties and a shared object or reality. This distinguishes it from kindness (which can be directional from one) or sorrow (which can be solitary).

**The shared content:** The English word "fellowship" carries the sense of *shared life* — life held in common. This aligns directly with the registry description: "the experience of genuine shared life with others — not just being in the same place but actually participating together in something real." The name encodes participation as distinct from mere proximity.

**The communal anchor:** Fellowship names the quality of the bond from the inside, from the perspective of the one who participates. It is not a structural category (like covenant) but an experiential-relational one: the inner reality of being a genuine participant.

---

**Prompt 2:** What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**Status: P** | *Adds structure / Gap identified*

**Greek — koinōnia (G2842):** The root *koinos* means "common, shared." At the definitional level, koinōnia signals that what is held is common — belonging to both parties. This is not merely relational proximity but genuine sharing of a common possession. The registry description confirms: "having in common, sharing in, participating in." The term carries implications of joint ownership or joint access.

**Greek — koinōnos (G2844):** A person-type term — the one who shares or participates. At the definitional level, it names the fellowship-person, not the fellowship-quality. This adds an agentive dimension: fellowship can be a constituting identity, not only a state experienced.

**Hebrew — chavar root family (H2266 cha.var, H2270 cha.ver, H2267 che.ver, H2271–H2279, H4225–H4226):** The root sense of *chavar* is joining or binding together. At the definitional level, the Hebrew vocabulary foregrounds the *act of joining* — fellowship is fundamentally about things or persons being bound together. This is more structural and even physical in its primary resonance than the Greek, which foregrounds the shared content. The Hebrew says: things are joined; the Greek says: things are held in common.

**Gap:** The NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag applies to all 13 terms in this registry (15 terms total, 13 OWNER). This means no lexical analysis was conducted at Session A stage for any of these terms. The definitional observations above are grounded in the registry description and the STEP search glosses, not in a completed word-analysis. This is a material limitation on what can be said with lexical precision.

**Prompt 3:** Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

**Relational implication:** The name fellowship is inherently relational — it cannot be possessed or exercised in isolation. Unlike joy (which can be solitary) or strength (which is an individual quality), fellowship by definition requires an other. The name itself does not specify the direction, but the term inventory encodes both axes.

**Constitutional implication:** The joining vocabulary of the Hebrew root family (chavar) suggests that fellowship is not merely a relational event but potentially a constitutional reality — persons or things that are joined are bound together at a structural level. This anticipates T2 (Constitutional Location) as a productive tier.

### T1.2 — Kind

**Prompt 1:** What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure / New finding*

The registry data establishes that fellowship is not one kind of phenomenon but several operating simultaneously under the same vocabulary.

**As act:** H2266 cha.var is used as a verb — active joining. Group 7565-001 (Hos 4:17: *Ephraim is joined to idols*) and group 7566-004 (Eze 37:19: God joining the sticks) both represent fellowship as an action event — a specific moment of joining.

**As disposition:** Groups 5367-001 and 5367-002 (koinōnos) describe inner orientation — the person as a sharer-by-disposition in divine nature or in moral complicity. Group 7566-001 (Psa 119:63: *I am a companion of all who fear you*) names a sustained inner orientation, not a single act.

**As condition:** Group 873-001 (koinōnia — vertical fellowship with God) names an ongoing relational condition — the believer's communion with Father and Son. This is not initiated at each prayer but is a constituted state.

**As quality:** Group 7573-001 (Jer 13:23: leopard's spots as fixed inner moral character) names fellowship vocabulary in the service of describing indelible character — the joining is internal, constitutive of who the person is.

**New finding:** Fellowship is structurally plural — it operates as act, disposition, condition, and quality depending on which group and which term is in view. This plurality is not a weakness of definition but a structural feature: the joining-participation vocabulary covers the full range from moment of joining to constituted state of being joined. The classification as "disposition" in the dimension label does not capture this plurality adequately.

**Prompt 2:** Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Fellowship is structurally complex at first encounter. Finding 062-OPER-001 identifies six distinct modes of operation: ontological participation, moral complicity, volitional alignment, transformational mechanism, spiritual bondage, and cognitive construction. These are not variants of a simple core — they represent genuinely distinct structural operations of the same vocabulary.

**Constituent elements visible at first encounter:**
1. **The joining-agent:** who or what performs the act of joining (divine or human)
2. **The joining-object:** what is being joined (persons, communities, sticks — physical or spiritual)
3. **The joining-content:** what is held in common or shared through the joining
4. **The joining-orientation:** toward what or whom the joining is directed (God, others, idols, evil)
5. **The joining-effect:** what the joining produces (participation in divine nature, moral culpability, hope, purification, bondage)

Each group in the registry specifies these five elements differently. The characteristic is not simple — it is a relational-structural complex with five variable constituent elements.

**Prompt 3:** What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis / Adds structure*

Drawing from the registry description, the confirmed findings, and the group evidence:

**Working description:** Fellowship is the inner-relational reality of genuine shared participation — the state of being bound together with another person, reality, or being in a way that constitutes, orients, and effects the inner person. Its constitutional location is primarily the relational dimension of the inner being, engaging the faculties of volition (choice of fellowship object), moral evaluation (discernment of fellowship compatibility), and affect (hope, comfort from fellowship; sorrow, bondage from wrong fellowship). Its impact on the person depends entirely on the object of fellowship: participation in divine nature produces ontological transformation; companionship with the fear-of-God produces moral formation; joining with idols produces spiritual bondage; fellowship through wounds produces purification and healing; covenant fellowship establishes relational identity.

The characteristic is therefore constitutively *object-dependent*: unlike joy (which has a generally positive valence regardless of object) or fear (which has a consistent inner structure regardless of object), fellowship's inner-being impact is completely determined by what or whom the person is joined to.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**Prompt 1:** What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

**Status: P** | *New finding*

The verse evidence names several realities that stand against or exclude genuine fellowship.

**Isolation/aloneness:** Ecc 4:10 — *woe to him who is alone when he falls and has not another to lift him up*. The structural opposite of mutual fellowship is solitude without support — not merely physical aloneness but the condition of having no one joined to you in the moment of need.

**Faithlessness (covenant rupture):** Mal 2:14 — faithlessness against the companion-wife violates the fellowship bond. The inner-being opposite of covenantal fellowship is faithlessness — the disposition that breaks the joining rather than sustaining it.

**Moral incompatibility:** 2Cor 6:14 — *what fellowship has light with darkness?* The rhetorical question names moral incompatibility as a fellowship-excluding condition. Lawlessness is not merely an absence of fellowship but structurally contrary to it.

**Spiritual abandonment:** Hos 4:17 — God's response to Ephraim's idolatrous joining is *leave him alone* — abandonment. The opposite of divine fellowship-inclusion is divine abandonment resulting from misdirected joining.

**New finding:** Fellowship has multiple structural opposites depending on which mode is in view: for covenant fellowship, faithlessness; for righteous fellowship, lawlessness; for divine fellowship, idolatrous joining; for mutual fellowship, isolation. These are not the same inner-being reality — they are mode-specific opposites. This plurality of structural opposites is consistent with the plurality of fellowship modes (finding 062-OPER-001).

**Gap:** The programme's dimension vocabulary and adjacency analysis (Session D) would need to determine which of these opposites is primary.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**Prompt 1:** In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Finding 062-OPER-001 identifies six distinct modes. These are grounded directly in the group evidence:

1. **Ontological participation** — 2Pe 1:4, group 5367-001: fellowship as becoming a partaker of divine nature; a change of ontological status through participation.

6. **Cognitive construction** — Job 16:4, group 7565-003: joining words together as a form of inner rhetorical construction; a cognitive-verbal mode.

**Prompt 2:** Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Yes, and the differentiation is substantial.

**By direction (vertical vs. horizontal):** Vertical fellowship (God to human or human to God: 873-001, 5367-001, 2Pe 1:4, Phili 3:10, 1Jo 1:3) operates at the ontological level — it constitutes the person's deepest relational status before God. Horizontal fellowship (human to human: 873-002, 7566-001, 7566-003, Ecc 4:10, Psa 119:63) operates at the moral-formation and mutual-support levels. These are not merely different expressions of the same phenomenon; they operate at different constitutional depths.

**By object (righteous vs. corrupted):** Fellowship with the fear-of-God community produces moral formation; fellowship with wickedness produces moral culpability; fellowship with idols produces spiritual bondage. The same constitutional faculty (the relational-joining capacity) produces radically different inner-being outcomes depending on the object.

**By temporal mode (initiating act vs. sustained condition):** Finding 062-TEMP-001 establishes that fellowship describes both one-time acts (divine promises enabling participation, God's joining of the sticks) and ongoing conditions (companionship with the fear-of-God community, covenantal companionship). The constitutional operation differs accordingly.

**New finding:** The constitutional level at which fellowship operates depends on the direction and object: divine-facing fellowship operates at the deepest constitutional level (ontological participation in divine nature), while human-facing fellowship operates at the moral-formation and mutual-support levels. This is a constitutional hierarchy within a single characteristic — not explored as a hierarchy in the existing findings.

**Prompt 3:** Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis*

Yes. Two distinct speech-based modes are evidenced.

**Proclamatory mode:** 1Jo 1:3 — *that which we have seen and heard we proclaim also to you, so that you too may have fellowship with us*. Here proclamation is the instrument by which fellowship is extended: the speaking of what has been witnessed creates the relational bond of shared participation. Speech produces fellowship.

These are distinct: in 1Jo 1:3, speech creates relational fellowship externally; in Job 16:4, the joining capacity operates internally as cognitive-verbal construction. Finding 062-OPER-001 identifies both within the six modes.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**Prompt 1:** Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Yes. The evidence supports constitutional movement across all three levels, though not uniformly for every fellowship mode.

**Spirit to soul:** The divine nature participation sequence (2Pe 1:4) — God grants promises → escape from corruption → participation in divine nature — implies a movement from the divine-offered (spirit level) through the inner moral condition (soul level) to ontological transformation. The escape from corruption is a soul-level change enabled by spirit-level access.

**Soul to body:** The disciplinary wounding sequence (Pro 20:30) — blows → wound → cleansing of innermost parts — moves from the somatic event (body) inward to the moral core (soul/heart). This is body-to-soul movement.

**Spirit to soul to body:** The Phili 3:10 trajectory — knowing Christ (spirit-level relational knowledge) → sharing sufferings (somatic engagement) → conformity to his death (somatic-constitutional) → resurrection (return to spirit-level transformation) — traces a full constitutional circuit: spirit orientation → somatic participation → transformational output at spirit level.

**New finding:** Fellowship is one of the few characteristics in the programme for which the verse evidence establishes constitutional movement across all three levels (spirit, soul, body) in a traceable pattern. The full circuit is most visible in the Phili 3:10 trajectory. This has significant implications for Session D synthesis about fellowship's structural role in the inner-being framework.

**Prompt 2:** What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?

**Status: A** | *New finding*

Two patterns are discernible:

**Pattern 1 — Inward/downward:** External or divine action → physical engagement → inner constitutional transformation. This is the wound-fellowship pattern: external wounding reaches inward to the constitutional core. The movement runs from outside-somatic to inside-constitutional.

**Pattern 2 — Upward/transformational:** Inner-constitutional orientation → somatic participation → eschatological transformation. This is the Phili 3:10 pattern: inner knowing of Christ → somatic sharing of sufferings → constitutional conformity to death → resurrection. The movement runs from inside outward through the somatic and then beyond.

Both patterns operate simultaneously in different fellowship modes. The characteristic is not constitutionally fixed at one level — it moves, and the direction of movement depends on which mode of operation is active.

This perceptive character is consistent with the Hebrew vocabulary's emphasis on joining and binding, which are contact-based physical images.

### T3.6 — Volition

**Prompt 2:** Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition — including capacity, interaction, and constraints?

Agency in fellowship is therefore register-dependent: the higher the fellowship register, the more dominant divine agency becomes.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**Prompt 1:** What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?

**Status: P** | *Consistent with prior analysis / Gap identified*

**Primary Greek — G2842 koinōnia (17 active verses):** Root *koinos* (common, shared). The root meaning reveals that the essential concept is *holding in common* — something belongs to both parties simultaneously. It is not a transfer (from one to another) but a co-possession. This shapes the entire Greek side of the vocabulary: fellowship is about shared possession, shared participation, shared standing.

**Primary Hebrew — H2266 cha.var (25 active verses, the highest count):** Root meaning: *to join, to bind together, to be coupled*. The root meaning reveals that the essential concept is the *act of joining* — the binding together of what was separate. Where the Greek focuses on what is held in common (the content of the sharing), the Hebrew focuses on the act and condition of being joined (the binding itself).

**Structural observation:** The Greek root foregrounds *content* (what is shared); the Hebrew root foregrounds *structure* (the joining itself). This is a genuine cross-Testamental semantic shift: the OT chavar vocabulary emphasises the binding act; the NT koinōnia vocabulary emphasises the shared content of what is held in common. Fellowship is both — the joining and what is held in common through the joining.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**Prompt 1:** What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

**Status: A** | *Consistent with prior analysis / Adds structure*

The registry carries two anchor verses for its largest group (873-001): Phili 3:10 and 1Jo 1:3. The anchor verse for the broadest definitional group is 1Jo 1:3 (as the verse most fully expressing the horizontal-vertical fellowship structure).

**1Jo 1:3:** *that which we have seen and heard we proclaim also to you, so that you too may have fellowship with us; and indeed our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ.*

The term koinōnia (fellowship) functions *twice* in this verse: first as the telos of the proclamation (*so that you too may have fellowship with us*) and second as the constituting reality that grounds the proclamation (*our fellowship is with the Father and his Son*). The term functions as:
1. **The goal of the communicative act** (proclamation exists to create fellowship)
2. **The constituting ground of the capacity to extend it** (we can offer fellowship because ours is with God)

The verse's argument structure: *we have seen and heard → we proclaim → you may have fellowship with us → because our fellowship is with the Father and Son*. Fellowship is both the purpose of proclamation and the ground of its possibility.

**Prompt 2:** What literary form carries the primary verse evidence — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?

**Status: A** | *Adds structure*

The fellowship evidence is carried across multiple literary forms:

**Epistle (1Jo 1:3, 2Cor 6:14, Phili 3:10, 2Pe 1:4):** The majority of the koinōnia evidence is in epistolary form — letters written to specific communities addressing their particular circumstances. Responsible interpretation requires attention to the communal context: these are not abstract theological propositions but pastoral directives for actual communities.

**Psalm (Psa 119:63, Psa 58:5):** Wisdom-devotional poetry. The companionship language of Psa 119:63 is part of an acrostic psalm of sustained devotion. Responsible interpretation attends to the cumulative, repetitive character of the psalm — it is not making a single logical claim but rehearsing sustained commitment.

**Wisdom literature (Ecc 4:10, Ecc 9:4, Pro 20:30, Job 16:4):** Observational wisdom form. These verses are empirical observations about how life works, not direct theological propositions. Responsible interpretation recognises the observational character: *this is how it goes* rather than *this is the theological definition*.

**Prophecy (Isa 53:5, Isa 1:23, Isa 47:9, Hos 4:17, Eze 37:19, Jer 13:23, Mal 2:14):** Prophetic address — often accusation, lament, or promise. Responsible interpretation attends to the prophetic *direction* of each verse (accusation, comfort, promise) and does not flatten them into neutral descriptive statements.

**Narrative-argument (Mat 23:30):** Controversy dialogue. The Pharisees' claim is part of a complex rhetorical exchange. Responsible interpretation reads the claim in its polemical context.

**Prompt 3:** Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

The verse evidence is not morally neutral about fellowship objects; attachment theory is.

**New finding:** The divergence reveals that the biblical fellowship concept is constitutively *teleological* in a way attachment theory is not: it is not merely about meeting a constitutional need for connection but about orienting the joining capacity toward the right object (God, the fear-of-God community). The telos of fellowship is not satisfied by any secure attachment but only by the correctly directed one. This is a genuinely distinctive biblical insight not captured by the most relevant human science framework.
