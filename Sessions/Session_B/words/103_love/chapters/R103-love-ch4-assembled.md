## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

Primary Hebrew terms and root meanings: **chesed** (H2617A) — root implies binding loyalty, covenant-fidelity; possibly related to the idea of steadfastness or bending toward; the most theologically laden term in the OT love vocabulary. **ahavah** (H0160) — root ahav (to breathe after, to desire) — implying love as a form of directed longing or desire. **racham** (H7355) / **rachamim** (H7356B) — root rechem (womb) — love as womb-like envelopment and tender compassion; the most somatically grounded root. **dod** (H1730G/H) — root unclear; used for lover/beloved in the Song — the erotic-relational vocabulary. Primary Greek terms: **agapē** (G0026) / **agapaō** (G0025) — of uncertain pre-classical etymology; LXX/NT coinage whose meaning is constituted by Scripture rather than importing a prior secular meaning (OBS-103-016). **phileō** (G5368) / **philia** (G5373) / **filos** (G5384) — root from philos (friend); classical Greek for natural affectionate love. Root meanings together reveal: love spans willed-covenant (chesed), longing-desire (ahavah), somatic-compassion (racham), erotic-beloved (dod), self-giving-willed (agapē), and natural-affection (phileō).

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

Taking agapaō/agapē as the primary NT term: **agapaō** (verb, 344 occurrences) — love as an action, exercisable and commandable; the verbal form affirms love's volitional dimension. **agapē** (noun, 133 occurrences) — love as an entity, a reality that can be 'poured into hearts' (Rom 5:5), that 'never fails' (1Cor 13:8), that is the greatest (1Cor 13:13). **agapētos** (adjective/participle, 79 occurrences) — beloved, the passive recipient of love; love as constituting an identity-state in the object. Together: verb + noun + adjective-participle spans the full grammatical range: love as act (verb), love as reality/quality (noun), and love as identity-constitutive state (adjective). This three-part grammatical range confirms Q&A-016's finding that love is simultaneously act, disposition, and condition. For chesed: primarily noun (covenant-quality) with verbal cognate (chasad — to act with chesed) — the noun-primary form confirms love as a stable quality before it is an act.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Agapē's semantic range in the NT evidence: (a) divine love for humanity (Joh 3:16); (b) human love for God (Mat 22:37); (c) mutual love in community (Joh 13:34); (d) love of enemy (Mat 5:44); (e) disordered love (love of darkness — Joh 3:19; love of the world — 2Ti 4:10; love of the pre-eminent position — 3Jo 9). The semantic range spans divine, human-covenantal, communal, and misdirected forms. Chesed's range: divine faithfulness, human loyalty, the person-type (chasid), the failure of chesed (withdrawal, demand). The combined semantic range of the primary terms covers the full moral landscape — from the highest (God's love as his being) to the lowest (love misdirected toward darkness). This breadth is unique in the programme — no other characteristic has primary terms whose semantic range spans from the ontologically highest (divine nature) to the morally inverted (love of darkness).

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary structure itself distinguishes these: **Disposition vs act**: agapē (noun — disposition) vs agapaō (verb — act); chesed (noun — the quality) vs chasad (verb — to act with chesed). **Received vs given**: agapētos (passive — received) vs agapaō (active — given); 'beloved of my soul' (Jer 12:7, received by Israel) vs 'I have loved you' (Jer 31:3, given by God). **Condition vs quality**: the lover-as-identity (chasid as person-type, OBS-103-026; filos as person-type, OBS-103-018) names the quality that has become a stable condition; the act-forms name love as an exercised capacity. The vocabulary is architecturally precise — it distinguishes what love is (quality/disposition), what love does (act), who love comes from (given), who love is directed toward (received), and who love constitutes (condition/identity).

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

Yes — embedded as OWNER. H8130 sane (to hate, 139 active / 156 total) is the structural opposite within the registry (OBS-103-030). H8131 sena (hating, 1 active) is a second form. The hate vocabulary is not XREF but OWNER — the programme treats the structural opposite as constituent of the love vocabulary's boundary (OBS-103-019). Additionally: the philo-compound negative forms (afilarguros — not-money-loving; pseudadelfos — false-brother) name the absence of specific love-forms. The disordered love vocabulary names love's distorted presence rather than its absence. True lovelessness (absence without misdirection) is not prominently named — the data suggests the inner mechanism is always present and always directed at something; the question is always the object, not the presence or absence of love-capacity.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — two primary person-type terms: (a) **H2623 chasid** (pious/godly one, 33 active) — the person-type of chesed. The chasid is defined by their inner character of covenantal love toward God (OBS-103-026). (b) **G5384 filos** (friend, 27 active) — the person-type of phileō. The filos is one whose inner orientation of loyal affection has become their defining relational identity (OBS-103-018). Additional person-type compounds: filagathos (lover of good — positive), filarguros (money-lover — negative), filotheos (God-lover), filēdonos (pleasure-lover), filautos (self-lover — via 2Ti 3:2). The philo-compound system generates person-types for each form of love (OBS-103-017). The programme has the richest person-type vocabulary in the programme for any single characteristic — the love-vocabulary directly generates who you are from what you love.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

The data contains supplication-for-love contexts but not a dedicated seeking term. Lam 3:22 (OBS-103-088): the community crying out for chesed in the midst of desolation is implicit supplication. The Psalms' consistent 'according to your chesed' (Psa 63:3, Psa 78:38) pattern is love-seeking as petition. Hos 3:5 (OBS-103-082): 'they shall come in fear to the Lord and to his goodness' — movement toward God's love/goodness as a seeking-act. However, a specific lexical term for 'seeking love' is not identified in the current data package. Gap: the seeking-term for love may exist in the broader corpus (the Psalms' petition-for-chesed formulae) but is not captured as a named group or anchor in R103's current structure.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The key LXX observation from the data: the LXX translators used agapē/agapaō to translate both ahav and chesed, embedding the Hebrew covenantal sense of steadfast love into the Greek agapē vocabulary. This is historically significant — agapē in the NT carries the weight of the OT chesed through the LXX translation. When the NT says 'God so loved (ēgapēsen)' (Joh 3:16), it uses the term that the LXX used for God's chesed toward Israel. Continuity is established at the lexical level: the NT's agapē is not a new concept but the Hebrew chesed in Greek form. Gap: the NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag (142 instances, OBS-103-156) limits the depth of LXX-analysis available from the data package. Full LXX lexical analysis is not possible from the current data.

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

Yes — agapē itself is effectively a NT-period coinage in its theological-central usage. The word exists rarely in classical Greek but carries no comparable weight; its meaning is constituted by its LXX/NT usage (OBS-103-016). The coinage-significance: the NT needed a word for love that was not pre-loaded with the Platonic eros tradition or the friendship-philia tradition — agapē fills this gap, its meaning entirely constituted by Scripture. This is analytically significant: agapē is the programme's strongest example of a characteristic whose vocabulary definition is wholly internal to the scriptural corpus. What agapē means cannot be determined by reference to classical Greek usage — it must be read from the LXX and NT contexts themselves.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc reveals love as the programme's most semantically comprehensive characteristic. The arc spans: (a) **Ground of being** — 1Jo 4:8 (God is love — the ontological summit); (b) **Covenantal permanence** — chesed (never ceasing, Lam 3:22); (c) **Somatic compassion** — rachamim (womb-like movement, Isa 49:15); (d) **Erotic-intense** — dod, ahavah (Song vocabulary, OBS-103-041); (e) **Natural affection** — phileō (Joh 11:36); (f) **Volitional self-giving** — agapē (enemy-love, soul-laying-down); (g) **Object-directed attachment** — philo-compounds (love of money, God, pleasure, brothers); (h) **Structural opposite** — sane (hatred). The arc is a spectrum from the ontologically highest (divine being) through the morally normative (covenantal chesed, commanded agapē) to the naturally affective (phileō, dod) to the morally disordered (love of darkness, self, money). No characteristic in the programme covers a comparable arc. Love is the vocabulary's outermost circle — encompassing the greatest range of inner-being phenomena under a single governing concept.


## Verse and literary interpretation (T7.2)

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

Identifying 1Jo 4:8 ('God is love') as the primary theological claim and Joh 15:13 as the primary ethical-definitional verse: In 1Jo 4:8: the predicate nominative construction ('God is love') makes love not merely an attribute of God but an identification — love as God's being. The verb 'is' (estin) establishes identity, not activity. In Joh 15:13: 'greater love has no one than this: that someone lay down his life for his friends' — love is the subject of a comparative superlative; the function is to name the outer limit of love by identifying its maximum expression (soul-giving). The primary term in both cases functions as the frame of reference against which all other love-instances are measured: Joh 15:13 defines love's maximum; 1Jo 4:8 defines love's origin. Together they establish the top and bottom of love's definitional range: what love ultimately is (God's being) and what love ultimately does (gives itself to death for the beloved).

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

Love's evidence base spans every major literary form: (a) **Wisdom/Proverb** (Pro 10:12, 27:6): requires attention to the comparative and aphoristic structure — proverbs describe patterns, not absolutes; (b) **Psalm** (Psa 63:3, 16:11, 97:10): requires reading as first-person prayer/meditation — the psalmist's inner orientation, not universal dogma; (c) **Prophetic oracle** (Jer 31:3, Eze 16:8, Isa 49:15): the love-language is covenantal and often metaphorical (marital imagery); responsible reading holds the metaphorical dimension; (d) **Epistle** (1Cor 13, 1Jo 4, Rom 5): the most theologically discursive form — allows propositional claims about love's nature; (e) **Gospel narrative** (Joh 3:16, Luk 7:38, Joh 15:13): love embedded in narrative events requiring attention to the narrative's argument; (f) **Song/Poem** (Song of Songs): erotic love in a canonical wisdom-poem; the allegorical interpretive tradition and the literal reading must both be held. No single form dominates — love's evidence is multi-formal.

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

Three key argument structures: (a) **Causal** — 1Jo 4:19: 'we love because (hoti) he first loved us' — divine love is the causal ground of human love. 1Cor 13:13: 'the greatest of these is love' — superlative argument establishing love's primacy. (b) **Conditional** — Joh 14:15: 'if (ean) you love me, you will keep my commandments' — love as condition for obedience. Mat 5:44: 'love your enemies (command) so that (hopōs) you may be sons of your Father' — love as condition for identity-as-child-of-God. (c) **Comparative** — Joh 15:13: 'greater love has no one than this' — superlative-comparative establishing love's maximum. Psa 63:3: 'your steadfast love is better (tov) than life' — comparative valuing love above the highest natural good. The argument structures cluster around: love as cause, love as condition, and love as the superlative good. The data argues that love is causally prior, conditionally necessary, and comparatively supreme.

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

Three dominant contextual settings: (a) **Covenantal** — the OT love vocabulary (chesed, ahavah, racham) is carried primarily in covenantal contexts (Sinai formula, Deuteronomy love-command, prophetic covenant-marriage imagery). This reveals love as the inner disposition of covenant — it is what the covenant relationship requires and produces at the inner-being level. (b) **Communal/ecclesial** — the NT love vocabulary (agapē in Paul and John) is carried in the context of the believing community (1Cor 13 in worship-chaos context; Joh 13:34 at the Last Supper; 1Th 3:12 in community growth prayer). This reveals love as the community's constitutive practice. (c) **Lament/petition** — Lamentations, Psalms of lament: love's permanence is specifically evidenced under conditions of suffering and judgment. This reveals that love's most definitive testimony is given precisely when circumstances deny it — chesed is most powerfully stated when experience is most desolate (Lam 3:22).

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

Two verses function together as the primary definitional anchor for love in the data: (a) **Lam 3:22**: 'The steadfast love (chesed) of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end' — the OT's most concentrated statement of love's permanence under maximum adverse conditions. The theological claim is absolute: love does not cease. (b) **Joh 15:13**: 'Greater love has no one than this: that someone lay down his life for his friends' — the NT's primary definition of love by its maximum expression. Together they bracket the love vocabulary: Lam 3:22 defines love by its permanence (it never stops); Joh 15:13 defines love by its cost (it gives everything). If only one is chosen: Lam 3:22 is the more foundational — it states what love ultimately is (permanent, reliable, the ground of hope in desolation); Joh 15:13 states what love ultimately does.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**Lam 3:22** reveals that love's most definitive affirmation comes in the moment of maximum absence — the desolation of Jerusalem, the silence of God, the evidence of judgment everywhere. No other verse places love's permanence claim at the precise moment when it is most empirically falsifiable. The verse does not argue for love's permanence from evidence of love's presence — it asserts permanence amid evidence of absence. This reveals that chesed in the OT is not an empirical observation (I observe that God has been loving) but a theological confession (I confess that God's love does not cease, even when it does not appear). This is love at its most epistemically demanding: the confession of love against the evidence of its apparent absence. No other verse in the data makes this claim with this evidence-structure.
