# Chapter 1 — Meaning

**Registry 103 — Love** · *Word Characteristic Summary*

---

## What it is

> Love is the most foundational and most theologically dense word in the entire corpus. The Hebrew and Greek vocabulary spans a vast range: the steadfast covenant love of God (hesed), the deep personal affection (ahava), the tender compassion (racham), the Greek agape (self-giving, willed, unconditional love), phileo (warm affection), and eros (passion and longing). God is love: not merely loving but constituted by it. Human love is modelled on and enabled by divine love. Everything else in the moral life is an expression of love rightly ordered.


**Working description:** Working description: Love is the primary inner-being orientation of the human person — the directed attachment of the whole inner self toward God, persons, and the good, operating as both the deepest constitutional disposition and the foundational act of the will, producing and sustaining all relational bonds. It locates principally at the heart-soul interface (Spirit-Soul classification confirmed). It engages the will (commanded), the cognition (requires knowledge of the beloved), the emotion (produces joy, grief, rest depending on the beloved's presence/absence), and the body (expressed somatically in kiss, embrace, tears). Its impact: it constitutes the person's relational identity (who you love defines who you are), organises all other inner-being characteristics (love of God requires hatred of evil; love of God produces fruit), and is the externally legible mark of the community's identity (Joh 13:35). Its absence is the diagnostic indicator of the divine image's distortion (1Jo 4:8).


## Name and naming

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

The characteristic is called 'love' — a name that in English collapses a vocabulary of multiple Hebrew and Greek terms into a single referent. The name signals an orientation outward: love is always toward something or someone; it cannot be self-contained. Even the word's grammar in Scripture consistently requires a direct object (love of God, love of neighbour, love of enemy, love of money). The name also signals relational constitution: the one who loves is partly constituted by who and what they love. The programme's description note ('most foundational and most theologically dense word in the entire corpus,' OBS-103-004) signals that the name functions as an organising category for the entire inner-being vocabulary.

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

At the definitional level, the primary terms reveal a structured vocabulary rather than a single concept. Hebrew: chesed (covenant-faithfulness, binding loyalty), ahavah (intense personal love including eros), racham/rachamim (compassion — love responding to suffering), dod (beloved, erotic love of the Song). Greek: agapē (willed self-giving love, LXX/NT coinage), phileō (natural affection), philia compounds (love directed toward a specified object). The terms do not all name the same inner state at different intensities — they name structurally distinct modes of love's operation. Chesed is obligatory-covenantal; agapē is volitional-unconditional; phileō is naturally affective; racham is compassion-responsive. At the definitional level, 'love' in this registry is not one thing but a family of related inner orientations sharing the common structure of directed-attachment-to-an-other (OBS-103-033).

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

All three. Directional: love always has a direction — toward God, toward neighbour, toward the beloved, or toward disordered objects (money, pleasure, world). The moral question of love is always the question of its direction (OBS-103-017). Relational: love cannot exist in isolation — it is constitutively relational, requiring at minimum a lover and a beloved. The relational structure is not incidental but essential (OBS-103-035). Constitutional: love operates at the deepest level of the inner person — the great commandment (Mat 22:37) requires 'all your heart and soul and mind,' indicating that love engages the entire constitutional depth of the inner being (OBS-103-139). The enquiry is oriented from the outset toward a characteristic that is simultaneously moral (choice of direction), relational (requires another), and constitutional (engages the whole person).


## Kind

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

Love in the data is simultaneously a disposition (stable inner orientation toward), an act (commanded, exercised, expressed — agapaō is consistently verbal), and a condition (one who is loved — agapētos — is in a constitutive relational state). It is not primarily an emotion, though it produces emotional states (joy, grief, rest). The commanded form (Mat 22:37 — 'you shall love') establishes love as an act of the will, not purely a felt state. The dispositional form (chesed as God's defining attribute, OBS-103-025) establishes love as a stable character quality. The received form (agapētos as identity, OBS-103-040) establishes love as a constitutive condition the person exists within. Love is therefore tripartite: disposition + act + condition — inner orientation that produces acts and exists within a condition of being-loved. This is the most structurally complex inner-being phenomenon in the programme.

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

Structurally complex from the first encounter. The four-mode taxonomy (OBS-103-033) — willed covenantal, compassionate responsive, relational-social, intense erotic — is the first-level complexity. Beneath that, the common structure: directed attachment toward an object, whose moral quality is entirely determined by the object (OBS-103-009, OBS-103-017). The disordered forms (OBS-103-036) share the same structure as the righteous forms. The three-axis directional structure (OBS-103-035) adds spatial complexity: love operates in three directions simultaneously. The tripartite kind (Q&A-016) adds ontological complexity: disposition, act, and condition. Love appears to be the programme's most structurally composite inner-being characteristic — not simple but architecturally layered.


## Boundary — what it is not

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

The structural opposite is hatred (sane — H8130, 139 active verses, OWNER term, OBS-103-030). Sane is embedded in R103 as an OWNER term — the programme treats hate as constitutive of the love vocabulary's boundary, not external to it. Lev 19:17 ('you shall not hate your brother in your heart') places hate as the inner alternative to love of neighbour (OBS-103-114). Pro 10:12 ('hatred stirs up strife, love covers all offences,' OBS-103-097) places them as causal-opposite forces with opposed social effects. The secondary structural opposite at the ultimate level is enmity with God: friendship with the world is enmity (echthra) with God (Jam 4:4, OBS-103-058). But hatred is the primary inner-being opposite — it is the same inner mechanism as love (directed orientation) running in the opposed direction.

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

1Cor 13:4-7 provides the programme's most explicit catalogue of what love excludes: envy, boasting, arrogance, rudeness, self-seeking, irritability, resentfulness, rejoicing in wrongdoing (OBS-103-127). The list names love's resistances across moral, relational, and emotional registers. At the structural level, love resists division of ultimate allegiance (Jam 4:4 — cannot love God and world simultaneously, OBS-103-058), resists self-assertion as the primary orientation (love-of-self as the first item in the last-days catalogue, OBS-103-137), and resists the withholding of self (Joh 15:13 — love's maximum is the giving of the soul/life, OBS-103-125). Love also explicitly resists concealment of inner truth from the beloved: Lev 19:17 contrasts hate-as-silence with love-as-frank-correction (OBS-103-114).

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

What love is not, from the data: love is not sentiment — it is commanded (Mat 22:37) and exercised irrespective of feeling (enemy-love, Mat 5:44). Love is not mere natural affection — phileō is insufficient as the full standard; agapē and chesed exceed phileō in obligatory reach. Love is not the same as desire (R043) — the desire registry owns the primary ahav verbal form (H0157G); desire and love share a mechanism but the programme treats them as distinct (OBS-103-010, OBS-103-022). Love is not the same as goodness (R067) — the tov family is XREF; love produces goodness but is not identical with it (OBS-103-014). The precise boundary between love and compassion (R023), kindness (R099), and mercy (R111) cannot be fully drawn from Stage 2a evidence alone — these are the programme's adjacent characteristics whose distinctions require dedicated treatment in Session D. Partial: the internal boundary is evidenced; the inter-registry distinctions are named as gaps deferred to Session D.


## Modes of operation

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

Four primary modes of operation, each evidenced across the anchor verse corpus:
(1) **Covenantal-dispositional mode** (chesed, agapē): Love as stable inner orientation of faithful loyalty — not feeling-contingent, not revocable by the beloved's failure. God's chesed 'never ceases' (Lam 3:22, OBS-103-088); human chesed is written on the heart as character (Pro 3:3, OBS-103-090). This mode operates at the level of character — the deep stable orientation.
(2) **Compassionate-responsive mode** (racham, splanchnizō): Love that is activated by the beloved's suffering — the inner-organ movement (OBS-103-105, OBS-103-116). This mode responds to need; it is not passive but activated.
(3) **Volitional-costly mode** (agapē — enemy-love, self-giving): Love that operates against the inner grain of natural preference. Mat 5:44 (OBS-103-143), Joh 15:13 (OBS-103-125). This mode requires the will's deliberate engagement to exceed what natural affection would produce.
(4) **Affective-relational mode** (phileō, ahavah-intense, dod): Love as naturally felt warm attachment — the affection for friends, the longing for the beloved. This mode is experienced; it is love in its felt dimension.
The four modes are not always distinguishable in a single verse but are analytically distinct operations of the same inner orientation.

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

Yes — context and direction both shape the mode. Direction: love toward God operates in the volitional-dispositional mode most prominently (commanded; requires the whole person — Mat 22:37, OBS-103-139); love toward the neighbour operates in both the dispositional and the costly-volitional modes; love toward the beloved (marital/erotic) operates in the affective-intense mode (dod, ahavah — Song vocabulary, OBS-103-041); love toward the enemy operates exclusively in the costly-volitional mode (OBS-103-143). Constitutional level: at the spirit/soul boundary (sb_classification confirmed), love appears to have its deepest location at the spirit level (the willed, faithful chesed) and its experiential expression at the soul level (the felt ahavah/phileō). At the heart level (OBS-103-126) love is infused by the Spirit and inscribed as character. The constitutional level shapes how love presents: deep-spirit love is more chesed-like; soul-surface love is more phileō-like.

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

Yes — evidenced across several groups. Joh 15:15 (OBS-103-052): love's communicative mode is knowledge-disclosure — Jesus names the content of his friendship with the disciples as 'all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you.' Love shares the inner world of the lover with the beloved. Lev 19:17 (OBS-103-114): frank correction (hocheach tochi'ach — honest rebuke) is love's communicative act, in contrast to hate's silence. The kiss (filēma, katafileō — OBS-103-054, OBS-103-056) is love's somatic-communicative mode — the body's expression of the inner word. The divine declaration ('this is my beloved Son' — Mat 3:17, OBS-103-120) is love's constitutive speech act: the naming of belovedness is itself the conferral of identity. Love therefore has a speech-mode that functions as: disclosure (sharing the inner world), correction (honest engagement), and declaration (constitutive naming).


## Immediate response

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

The data evidences three immediate response patterns, each associated with a primary love-encounter type:
(1) **Responsive love** — 1Jo 4:19: the immediate response to receiving divine love is love in return. Love received generates love given — the response is the same characteristic activated in the receiver.
(2) **Rest/stillness** — Zep 3:17 (OBS-103-094): God's love toward Israel produces quieting — the immediate inner response to the experience of being loved is rest, not striving. Psa 127:2 (OBS-103-084): being-beloved produces effortless sleep (rest as divine love-gift).
(3) **Grief as love-evidence** — Joh 11:36 (OBS-103-149), 2Sa 1:26 (OBS-103-095), Joh 21:17 (OBS-103-153): the immediate response to love's loss or to love's cost is grief — the inner collapse or distress that reveals the depth of the prior love. This is not a response to love per se but to love's absence or cost, and it is consistently love's most visible immediate evidence.

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

The response varies by the type of love-encounter. When divine love is received: the immediate response is love-in-return (1Jo 4:19) or rest (Zep 3:17, Psa 127:2). When love is expressed at cost (forgiveness-love, enemy-love): the immediate response in the giver is not named directly. When love is lost: the immediate response is grief (Song 5:6, OBS-103-062; 2Sa 1:26, OBS-103-095). When the beloved is encountered: the immediate response is delight and longing (Song 2:16, OBS-103-061; Luk 15:20 — the father's compassion-movement at sight of the returning son, OBS-103-057). The variation is systematic, not random: the type of encounter determines the response type. There is no single invariant immediate response to love — the response depends on the direction of the encounter (giving vs receiving vs losing vs encountering).


## Sustained effect

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

The verse evidence identifies several sustained effects of love in the inner being:
(1) **Character formation** — Pro 3:3 (OBS-103-090): chesed written on the heart over time becomes character. 1Pe 1:22 (OBS-103-124): purified souls move toward sincere brotherly love — love as the goal of sustained inner formation.
(2) **Joy and fullness** — Psa 16:11 (OBS-103-112): in God's presence (sustained love-relationship) there is fullness of joy and pleasures forevermore. The sustained love-relationship with God produces an abiding experiential fullness.
(3) **Growth** — 1Th 3:12 (OBS-103-130): love can increase and abound over time. Love is not static but grows under the Lord's enabling.
(4) **Community cohesion** — Joh 13:35 (OBS-103-129): sustained mutual love becomes the externally legible identity of the community — a sustained social effect rather than a momentary one.
(5) **Obedience pattern** — Joh 14:15 (OBS-103-140): sustained love of Christ produces a sustained pattern of commandment-keeping. The inner orientation produces an enduring behavioural pattern.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

From the data: sustained possession of love establishes — (a) **identity as beloved** (agapētos — the one constituted by divine love, OBS-103-040, OBS-103-122): sustained divine love toward the person establishes the identity of being-loved; (b) **relational capacity** — sustained love of neighbour establishes the inner orientation of fairness, care, and honesty that Lev 19 describes (OBS-103-071); (c) **community legibility** — the community constituted by mutual love becomes publicly readable (Joh 13:35, OBS-103-129); (d) **faith-pattern** — sustained love of God produces sustained commandment-keeping (Joh 14:15, OBS-103-140); (e) **soul-satisfaction** — sustained experience of God's goodness/love satisfies the soul's deepest hunger (Jer 31:14, OBS-103-081). The overarching capacity established: the person becomes capable of giving what they have received — sustained reception of divine love equips the person to love others.

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

Yes — the distinction is clear. The immediate response to love is primarily experiential: rest/stillness (Zep 3:17), responsive love (1Jo 4:19), or grief (Song 5:6). The sustained effect is primarily constitutional and communal: character formation (Pro 3:3), identity-establishment (agapētos), community legibility (Joh 13:35), and soul-satisfaction (Jer 31:14). The immediate response is felt; the sustained effect is what the person becomes and what the community is constituted as. This distinction maps onto the programme's spirit-soul boundary: the immediate response operates at the soul-affective level; the sustained effect operates at the spirit-character level and the relational-community level.


## Conditions of reception

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

From the data: (a) **obedience** — 1Pe 1:22 (OBS-103-124): soul-purification through obedience enables sincere love. The purified soul is the condition for authentic love. (b) **faith** — Jam 2:23 (OBS-103-053): Abraham's faith is what constitutes him as 'friend of God.' Faith as the inner condition through which love-relationship with God becomes possible. (c) **openness / not loving the darkness** — Joh 3:19 (OBS-103-147): those who love darkness cannot receive the light. The inner orientation away from evil is the negative condition for receiving love. (d) **being the returning prodigal** — Luk 15:20 (OBS-103-057): the son's return (movement toward the father) is the condition for the father's embrace. Some movement toward the source of love appears to be a condition for its reception. Gap: the data does not contain an explicit systematic account of reception-conditions. The conditions above are inferred from contexts where love is given and received. The gap is noted.

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

The data names several inner blockers: (a) **love of darkness** — Joh 3:19 (OBS-103-147): the inner orientation toward darkness is the condition that prevents reception of love-as-light. The person who loves the darkness does not receive the light; the blocker is the prior love-commitment to a wrong object. (b) **love of money** — 1Ti 6:10 (OBS-103-132): love of money causes departure from faith — the love of a wrong object displaces the reception of divine love. (c) **pride/self-seeking** — 1Cor 13:4-7 (OBS-103-127): love of self expressed as arrogance and self-seeking is explicitly named as what love is not — the self-orientation blocks the other-orientation that genuine love requires. (d) **false-brotherhood orientation** — Gal 2:4 (OBS-103-068): the concealed inner agenda of the false brother prevents genuine love-reception within the community. The pattern: all inner blockers are disordered loves — prior commitments to wrong objects that displace the capacity for rightly directed love.

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

The data provides several instances: (a) Joh 3:19 (OBS-103-147) — the person who encounters light-as-love and does not receive it 'loved the darkness' — the inner state is commitment to the alternative; the non-reception is an inner-being act of love misdirected. (b) Luk 16:14 (OBS-103-136) — the Pharisees who were money-lovers ridiculed Jesus' teaching; their inner state is intellectual rejection grounded in love-of-money. (c) 2Ti 4:10 (OBS-103-148) — Demas, in love with the present world, deserts: the non-reception of love-community is an act of love toward the world instead. The pattern is consistent: the person who does not receive love is not loveless — they are loving the wrong thing. Lovelessness in the sense of having no love-capacity does not appear in the data; what appears is love misdirected.


## Dimension classification

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

Primary dimension: **Relational Disposition**. This is the confirmed Dimension Review result (v1.9, 2026-04-09) and is well-supported by the data. Love is fundamentally orientation-toward-another — the directed attachment of the inner person toward an object. The relational structure is constitutive, not incidental: love cannot exist without a direction and an object (OBS-103-015, OBS-103-035). The Dimension Review classification is confirmed as well-grounded by the full anchor verse reading in Stage 2a.

**T1.8.2 — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

The Relational Disposition classification is supported by: (a) the consistent directional structure of the vocabulary — love always has an object toward which it is oriented (OBS-103-017); (b) the three-axis relational architecture (GOD → HUMAN, HUMAN → GOD, HUMAN → HUMAN, OBS-103-035); (c) the 14+ GOD-dominant groups naming divine love as relational orientation (OBS-103-034); (d) the person-type terms (filos, chasid, agapētos) that name the relational identity constituted by love (OBS-103-018, OBS-103-026, OBS-103-040); (e) the group descriptions which consistently describe love as 'inner orientation toward' or 'relational disposition' in their language.

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

Secondary dimensions evidenced: (a) **Moral Character** — chesed written on the heart (Pro 3:3, OBS-103-090), chasid as the person of covenant-character (OBS-103-026), love-of-good (filagathos) as a character disposition (OBS-103-134). Love as stable character (not just act) carries Moral Character alongside Relational Disposition. (b) **Emotion-Positive** — the affective-relational modes (phileō, ahavah-intense, dod) carry positive emotional content: delight, joy, longing (OBS-103-061, OBS-103-112). (c) **Volition** — enemy-love (Mat 5:44) and the ranking of loves (Mat 10:37) are explicitly volitional — choices against natural preference. These secondary dimensions do not compete with Relational Disposition; they describe the faculties through which love's relational orientation is expressed. The primary dimension correctly captures the constitutive structure; the secondary dimensions capture the modes.

## Synthesis — what T1 reveals as a whole

**SYN-INTRA-103-001 | T1** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

Love's definition resolves to a structured family of inner-being orientations sharing one mechanism — directed attachment toward an object — whose moral character is entirely determined by the object chosen. Within this shared mechanism, the vocabulary distinguishes three structural registers: love as disposition (the stable inner orientation — chesed, agapē as character), love as act (the commanded and exercised expression — agapaō, enemy-love), and love as condition (the identity-constituting state of being-loved — agapētos). These are not three separate loves but three aspects of love's single operation: the person has a deep stable orientation (disposition), exercises it in specific acts (act), and exists within the love-relationship as constituted by it (condition). The vocabulary arc runs from the ontologically highest (God is love — 1Jo 4:8) through the morally normative (chesed, commanded agapē) to the affective-natural (phileō, dod) to the morally inverted (love of darkness, love of self). No other characteristic in the programme commands a comparable vocabulary range. The definition's boundary with adjacent characteristics (compassion, mercy, kindness) is partially drawn: love is the broader orientation; compassion is love's suffering-triggered mode; mercy and kindness are love's formal relational expressions. The boundaries require Session D precision.

**Vocabulary analysis (T7.1):**

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

Primary Hebrew terms and root meanings: **chesed** (H2617A) — root implies binding loyalty, covenant-fidelity; possibly related to the idea of steadfastness or bending toward; the most theologically laden term in the OT love vocabulary. **ahavah** (H0160) — root ahav (to breathe after, to desire) — implying love as a form of directed longing or desire. **racham** (H7355) / **rachamim** (H7356B) — root rechem (womb) — love as womb-like envelopment and tender compassion; the most somatically grounded root. **dod** (H1730G/H) — root unclear; used for lover/beloved in the Song — the erotic-relational vocabulary. Primary Greek terms: **agapē** (G0026) / **agapaō** (G0025) — of uncertain pre-classical etymology; LXX/NT coinage whose meaning is constituted by Scripture rather than importing a prior secular meaning (OBS-103-016). **phileō** (G5368) / **philia** (G5373) / **filos** (G5384) — root from philos (friend); classical Greek for natural affectionate love. Root meanings together reveal: love spans willed-covenant (chesed), longing-desire (ahavah), somatic-compassion (racham), erotic-beloved (dod), self-giving-willed (agapē), and natural-affection (phileō).

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

Taking agapaō/agapē as the primary NT term: **agapaō** (verb, 344 occurrences) — love as an action, exercisable and commandable; the verbal form affirms love's volitional dimension. **agapē** (noun, 133 occurrences) — love as an entity, a reality that can be 'poured into hearts' (Rom 5:5), that 'never fails' (1Cor 13:8), that is the greatest (1Cor 13:13). **agapētos** (adjective/participle, 79 occurrences) — beloved, the passive recipient of love; love as constituting an identity-state in the object. Together: verb + noun + adjective-participle spans the full grammatical range: love as act (verb), love as reality/quality (noun), and love as identity-constitutive state (adjective). This three-part grammatical range confirms Q&A-016's finding that love is simultaneously act, disposition, and condition. For chesed: primarily noun (covenant-quality) with verbal cognate (chasad — to act with chesed) — the noun-primary form confirms love as a stable quality before it is an act.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Agapē's semantic range in the NT evidence: (a) divine love for humanity (Joh 3:16); (b) human love for God (Mat 22:37); (c) mutual love in community (Joh 13:34); (d) love of enemy (Mat 5:44); (e) disordered love (love of darkness — Joh 3:19; love of the world — 2Ti 4:10; love of the pre-eminent position — 3Jo 9). The semantic range spans divine, human-covenantal, communal, and misdirected forms. Chesed's range: divine faithfulness, human loyalty, the person-type (chasid), the failure of chesed (withdrawal, demand). The combined semantic range of the primary terms covers the full moral landscape — from the highest (God's love as his being) to the lowest (love misdirected toward darkness). This breadth is unique in the programme — no other characteristic has primary terms whose semantic range spans from the ontologically highest (divine nature) to the morally inverted (love of darkness).

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary structure itself distinguishes these: **Disposition vs act**: agapē (noun — disposition) vs agapaō (verb — act); chesed (noun — the quality) vs chasad (verb — to act with chesed). **Received vs given**: agapētos (passive — received) vs agapaō (active — given); 'beloved of my soul' (Jer 12:7, received by Israel) vs 'I have loved you' (Jer 31:3, given by God). **Condition vs quality**: the lover-as-identity (chasid as person-type, OBS-103-026; filos as person-type, OBS-103-018) names the quality that has become a stable condition; the act-forms name love as an exercised capacity. The vocabulary is architecturally precise — it distinguishes what love is (quality/disposition), what love does (act), who love comes from (given), who love is directed toward (received), and who love constitutes (condition/identity).

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

Yes — embedded as OWNER. H8130 sane (to hate, 139 active / 156 total) is the structural opposite within the registry (OBS-103-030). H8131 sena (hating, 1 active) is a second form. The hate vocabulary is not XREF but OWNER — the programme treats the structural opposite as constituent of the love vocabulary's boundary (OBS-103-019). Additionally: the philo-compound negative forms (afilarguros — not-money-loving; pseudadelfos — false-brother) name the absence of specific love-forms. The disordered love vocabulary names love's distorted presence rather than its absence. True lovelessness (absence without misdirection) is not prominently named — the data suggests the inner mechanism is always present and always directed at something; the question is always the object, not the presence or absence of love-capacity.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — two primary person-type terms: (a) **H2623 chasid** (pious/godly one, 33 active) — the person-type of chesed. The chasid is defined by their inner character of covenantal love toward God (OBS-103-026). (b) **G5384 filos** (friend, 27 active) — the person-type of phileō. The filos is one whose inner orientation of loyal affection has become their defining relational identity (OBS-103-018). Additional person-type compounds: filagathos (lover of good — positive), filarguros (money-lover — negative), filotheos (God-lover), filēdonos (pleasure-lover), filautos (self-lover — via 2Ti 3:2). The philo-compound system generates person-types for each form of love (OBS-103-017). The programme has the richest person-type vocabulary in the programme for any single characteristic — the love-vocabulary directly generates who you are from what you love.

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

The data contains supplication-for-love contexts but not a dedicated seeking term. Lam 3:22 (OBS-103-088): the community crying out for chesed in the midst of desolation is implicit supplication. The Psalms' consistent 'according to your chesed' (Psa 63:3, Psa 78:38) pattern is love-seeking as petition. Hos 3:5 (OBS-103-082): 'they shall come in fear to the Lord and to his goodness' — movement toward God's love/goodness as a seeking-act. However, a specific lexical term for 'seeking love' is not identified in the current data package. Gap: the seeking-term for love may exist in the broader corpus (the Psalms' petition-for-chesed formulae) but is not captured as a named group or anchor in R103's current structure.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The key LXX observation from the data: the LXX translators used agapē/agapaō to translate both ahav and chesed, embedding the Hebrew covenantal sense of steadfast love into the Greek agapē vocabulary. This is historically significant — agapē in the NT carries the weight of the OT chesed through the LXX translation. When the NT says 'God so loved (ēgapēsen)' (Joh 3:16), it uses the term that the LXX used for God's chesed toward Israel. Continuity is established at the lexical level: the NT's agapē is not a new concept but the Hebrew chesed in Greek form. Gap: the NO_WORD_ANALYSIS flag (142 instances, OBS-103-156) limits the depth of LXX-analysis available from the data package. Full LXX lexical analysis is not possible from the current data.

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

Yes — agapē itself is effectively a NT-period coinage in its theological-central usage. The word exists rarely in classical Greek but carries no comparable weight; its meaning is constituted by its LXX/NT usage (OBS-103-016). The coinage-significance: the NT needed a word for love that was not pre-loaded with the Platonic eros tradition or the friendship-philia tradition — agapē fills this gap, its meaning entirely constituted by Scripture. This is analytically significant: agapē is the programme's strongest example of a characteristic whose vocabulary definition is wholly internal to the scriptural corpus. What agapē means cannot be determined by reference to classical Greek usage — it must be read from the LXX and NT contexts themselves.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc reveals love as the programme's most semantically comprehensive characteristic. The arc spans: (a) **Ground of being** — 1Jo 4:8 (God is love — the ontological summit); (b) **Covenantal permanence** — chesed (never ceasing, Lam 3:22); (c) **Somatic compassion** — rachamim (womb-like movement, Isa 49:15); (d) **Erotic-intense** — dod, ahavah (Song vocabulary, OBS-103-041); (e) **Natural affection** — phileō (Joh 11:36); (f) **Volitional self-giving** — agapē (enemy-love, soul-laying-down); (g) **Object-directed attachment** — philo-compounds (love of money, God, pleasure, brothers); (h) **Structural opposite** — sane (hatred). The arc is a spectrum from the ontologically highest (divine being) through the morally normative (covenantal chesed, commanded agapē) to the naturally affective (phileō, dod) to the morally disordered (love of darkness, self, money). No characteristic in the programme covers a comparable arc. Love is the vocabulary's outermost circle — encompassing the greatest range of inner-being phenomena under a single governing concept.
