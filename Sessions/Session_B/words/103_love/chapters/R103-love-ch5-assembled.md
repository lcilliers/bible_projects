## Structural relationships (T6)


### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**Prompt 1: Which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently alongside this one in the verse evidence?**

Top co-occurrences from §5 data (OBS-103-042 through OBS-103-051):
R023 compassion (196), R187 strength (105), R197 authority (69), R117 peace (68), R059 faith (62), R044 despair (55), R112 mind (52), R173 will (52), R019 calling (51), R182 Soul (50), R043 desire (49), R051 distress (49), R183 heart (47).
The pattern reveals three structural clusters: (a) **Love's relational cluster** — compassion (23), peace (117), kindness (99), mercy (111) — love co-occurs with its closest relational companions within C17, confirming cluster integrity; (b) **Love's constitutional cluster** — soul (182), heart (183), mind (112) — love is present in verses that name the inner constitutional locations; (c) **Love's theological cluster** — faith (59), calling (19), covenant (34) — love co-occurs consistently with the programme's foundational theological vocabulary. The surprise: strength (105) and authority (197) are co-occurrence #2 and #3 — love and power are structurally co-present in the data at high frequency.

**Prompt 2: What does the pattern of co-occurrence reveal about this characteristic's place in the inner-being landscape?**

Three surprising co-occurrences: (a) **Strength (R187, 105) and authority (R197, 69)** — love and power as the second and third highest co-occurrence registers. This is analytically unexpected — strength and authority are C20 cluster characteristics, not C17. The data suggests that love and power are not merely adjacent but structurally co-present in biblical contexts. The 1Cor 13 framework places love above gifts of power ('tongues of angels, gifts of prophecy') — the comparison implies they occupy the same evidential terrain. SD-026 (already raised) addresses this. (b) **Despair (R044, 55)** — despair appearing in the top 10 co-occurrences with love. The lament/petition pattern (despair crying to love for rescue) generates this co-occurrence. Love and despair are structurally linked as the inner state that appeals to love and the love that responds to despair. (c) **Guilt (R073, 32)** — less surprising given SD-028 (forgiveness-love chain) but notable that guilt appears in the top 25 — love and guilt are structurally related at the verse level through the forgiveness-love causal chain.

**Prompt 3: If no significant co-occurrence patterns emerge, note this explicitly.**

The co-occurrence pattern confirms the Relational Disposition primary dimension. Love co-occurs most densely with: compassion (the compassionate form of relational orientation), peace (the relational-outcome), faith (the trust-orientation toward God), calling (identity constituted relationally), and the soul/heart/mind (the constitutional locations of relational experience). This is the pattern expected for a Relational Disposition characteristic. The strength/authority co-occurrence adds texture (love operates in contexts of power) but does not complicate the primary classification — love remains relational disposition even when co-present with power vocabulary.


### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence show this characteristic consistently preceding, following, or accompanying another characteristic in a sequence?**

The data is unusually rich in love's sequential relationships. Love as effect (something precedes it): (a) Received divine love → human love (1Jo 4:19, OBS-103-146); (b) Forgiveness received → love expressed (Luk 7:47, via SD-020); (c) Obedience → soul purification → sincere love (1Pe 1:22, OBS-103-124); (d) Spirit infusion → love in heart (Rom 5:5, OBS-103-126). Love as cause (love produces): (e) Love of God → commandment-keeping (Joh 14:15, OBS-103-140); (f) Love → all other Spirit-fruits (Gal 5:22, OBS-103-128 — if love is generative); (g) Love of money → craving → departure from faith → anguish (1Ti 6:10, OBS-103-132 — the disordered sequence); (h) Perfect love → displacement of fear (1Jo 4:18, via SP-103-029). The sequential data reveals love as both deeply downstream of divine action and richly upstream of the ethical and moral life. Love is the programme's most central sequential node.

**Prompt 2: What does the sequence reveal — is the relationship causal, developmental, or correlational?**

Love appears to occupy a **mediating and generative** position in the programme's sequential architecture. Mediating: it stands between received divine love (prior) and the ethical-moral life (subsequent). It is not the origin (God is the origin) but it is the indispensable middle — without love, no commandment-keeping, no Spirit-fruit, no community-identity. Generative: love heads the fruit-of-Spirit list (Gal 5:22) and defines the measure of all subsequent virtues (1Cor 13:13 — 'the greatest is love'). Love functions as the programme's organising characteristic at the ethical level: it is what the other Spirit-fruits emerge from and what the commandments resolve into. This is consistent with the registry description ('most foundational word') and DIM-103-001's call to map love's governing position relative to other characteristics.


### T6.3 — Causal and Constitutive Relationships

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic produce another characteristic in the verse evidence — and if so, which one, and by what mechanism?**

Yes — love is causally productive in the data at both the inner-being and the social levels: (a) **Love → joy** (Psa 16:11, OBS-103-112): love-in-God's-presence produces fullness of joy; (b) **Love → rest** (Zep 3:17, OBS-103-094): God's love produces inner quieting; (c) **Love → grief** (Song 5:6, OBS-103-062; 2Sa 1:26, OBS-103-095): love under loss produces grief — love constitutes the grief by making the beloved's absence unbearable; (d) **Love → obedience** (Joh 14:15, OBS-103-140): love of Christ produces commandment-keeping; (e) **Love → praise** (Psa 63:3, OBS-103-087): love expressed as response to chesed-above-life produces praise. Love does not merely accompany these states but appears to constitute or cause them. It is the programme's most causally productive characteristic.

**Prompt 2: Is this characteristic produced by another — and if so, which one?**

From the data: (a) Love may constitute or generate all Spirit-fruits (Gal 5:22, Q&A-105) — if love is the single fruit of which joy, peace, patience, etc. are expressions, then love partially constitutes those characteristics. (b) Forgiveness may constitute the condition from which love emerges (Luk 7:47 via SD-020) — if 'she loved because she was forgiven much,' forgiveness is constitutive of the love-response. (c) Compassion and love appear to be constitutively related — compassion may be love's form under the condition of the other's suffering (OBS-103-042, OBS-103-057). Gap: the precise constitutive relationships between love and compassion, mercy, kindness cannot be fully determined from Stage 2a evidence alone — these require Session D treatment. Partial: love-as-constituent-of-Spirit-fruits and forgiveness-as-constituent-of-love-response are the best-evidenced claims.


### T6.4 — Vocabulary and Root Sharing

**Prompt 1: Does this characteristic share vocabulary terms with other characteristics in the programme?**

Extensively — as noted in the XREF analysis (Unit 2, OBS-103-007 through OBS-103-015): (a) **Compassion (R023)**: racham/rachamim family shared — the compassion vocabulary is OWNER in R103 and primary in R023; (b) **Goodness (R067)**: tov/agathos family — H2896A-C tov is XREF in R103, OWNER in R067; (c) **Desire (R043)**: ahav verbal root (H0157G) is XREF in R103, primary in R043; (d) **Peace (R117)**: eirēnē family is XREF in R103; (e) **Hatred**: sane is OWNER in R103, also relevant to other registries. The term sharing ratio (0.701 — 96 of 137 terms shared, OBS-103-006) is among the highest in the programme. Love shares vocabulary with almost every adjacent registry in C17.

**Prompt 2: Does vocabulary sharing extend to root-level architecture — a shared root that generates terms across two or more characteristics?**

Yes — three root-level connections are analytically significant: (a) **Racham root** — spans love (103), compassion (23), and mercy (111) as a unified etymological family. The womb-compassion-love nexus (OBS-103-007, OBS-103-029) is a root-level connection — the same bodily root generates three distinct registry characteristics; (b) **Tov root** — spans love (103) and goodness (67), crossing cluster boundaries (C17 and implied goodness cluster). The goodness-as-love-expression connection (SD-009) is root-level; (c) **Ahav root** — spans love (103) and desire (43). H0157H (friend-ahev, OWNER R103) and H0157G (lover-ahev, OWNER R043 via XREF placement) are the same root, different programme assignments. Root-sharing confirms that the programme's registries are not sealed semantic units but connected through shared etymological architecture.

**Prompt 3: What does vocabulary sharing reveal about the conceptual relationship between characteristics?**

Vocabulary sharing at this density reveals that love is not a bounded standalone characteristic but the semantic centre of a vocabulary cluster. The characteristics surrounding love (compassion, mercy, kindness, goodness, desire, peace) share vocabulary with it because they are all expressions or dimensions of a common inner-being reality — oriented-attachment-to-the-good-and-to-persons. The registries do not name wholly distinct phenomena; they name distinguishable aspects of a single broadly relational inner-being reality. The practical implication: Session D synthesis for love cannot treat the other C17 characteristics as merely adjacent — they are lexically and conceptually continuous with love. The distinctions drawn in the programme's registry structure are analytical tools, not natural kinds.


### T6.5 — Distinctions

**Prompt 1: Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what precisely distinguishes them?**

The closest adjacent characteristic from co-occurrence data is compassion (R023, 196 shared verses). What distinguishes them from the data: compassion is love's responsive form under the condition of the other's suffering — the rachamim/womb-movement is activated by the beloved's need or distress. Love without suffering context does not require the racham-movement; love in the presence of suffering always produces it. Stated differently: compassion is the subset of love that is suffering-triggered; love is the broader orientation of which compassion is one mode (OBS-103-029, OBS-103-042). However: the data does not fully distinguish chesed from compassion at the verse level — both can be present simultaneously (Lam 3:22). The distinction is partial. Gap: the precise boundary between love and compassion requires dedicated Session D analysis of both registries' verse corpora together.

**Prompt 2: Where the evidence shows apparent overlap, what is the precise boundary?**

The racham/chesed overlap at Lam 3:22 ('his chesed never ceases, his rachamim never come to an end') is the programme's clearest example of the boundary difficulty — the two terms are paired in parallel without distinction. At the definitional level the boundary is: chesed = covenantal-faithful love (disposition of the covenant partner); rachamim = compassion-love (response to suffering). But at the verse level they co-occur so consistently that their experiential separation is unclear. Gap: the boundary is analytically drawn but evidentially porous. Session D is the appropriate resolution context.

**Prompt 3: Is the distinction between this characteristic and its nearest neighbour one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?**

Between love and compassion: the distinction appears to be one of **trigger** rather than degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level. Both are inner dispositions of orientation toward another (same kind, same direction, similar constitutional level). What differs is the activating condition: compassion is triggered by the other's suffering; love can be present without suffering. This makes them the same characteristic differently activated, rather than different characteristics. This reading is consistent with SD-007 (compassion as love-responding-to-suffering hierarchy). However, the data does not definitively confirm this — it is an analytical interpretation of the co-occurrence and definitional data. Deferred to Session D for resolution.


### T6.6 — Shared Verse Anchors

The 48-anchor shared map is the richest in the programme — love's evidential base is the most cross-registered in the corpus.

**Prompt 2: What does the shared anchor reveal about the relationship between the two characteristics?**

The shared anchor map reveals that love is the semantic core around which the programme's most structurally significant verses cluster. Exo 34:6 (shared with 5 registries) is the OT's most cross-registry verse — it names love/chesed as the centre of a five-attribute divine portrait. Gal 5:22 (shared with 5 registries) places love as the head of the Spirit-fruit list and all subsequent fruits as co-anchors. These are not random co-occurrences — they are structurally chosen: the programme anchored the most important characteristic-verses at love because love is the conceptual centre from which the adjacent characteristics derive their meaning. The shared anchor pattern confirms love's generative position (Q&A-105): love is what the surrounding characteristics are expressions of, not merely what appears alongside them.

The Emotion-Positive dimension is shared with joy, delight, and related affective registries. The Volition dimension is shared with will, faith, and obedience registries. This breadth suggests love is not confined to a single dimensional register but operates across the programme's full vocabulary. Gap: systematic Session D analysis needed to determine whether this breadth is characteristic of love specifically or reflects the programme's general dimensional overlap.

**Prompt 3: If dimensional sharing data is not yet available, note this explicitly.**

## Synthesis — what the cross-registry analysis reveals


### Intra-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTRA-103-006 | T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

Love's structural position in the programme is that of the central node in the inner-being vocabulary's relational architecture. Co-occurrence data: 196 with compassion, 105 with strength, 68 with peace, 62 with faith — confirming love's connections to the full range of C17 characteristics and extending to C20 (strength/authority). Shared anchor data: 48 verses shared across multiple registries, with Exo 34:6 (5 registries) and Gal 5:22 (5 registries) as the highest-density shared anchors — confirming love as the vocabulary cluster around which the programme's most significant cross-registry verses gather. Sequential position: love stands downstream of divine action (received) and upstream of the ethical-moral life (generative of commandment-keeping, Spirit-fruits, community identity). Causal productivity: love generates joy, rest, grief, obedience, praise — the programme's most causally productive characteristic. Vocabulary sharing: term sharing ratio 0.701, the highest in C17 — love shares vocabulary with compassion, mercy, kindness, goodness, desire, and peace. The primary gap: distinctions from adjacent characteristics (compassion, mercy, kindness) remain partially drawn; Session D is the resolution context.


### Inter-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTER-103-005 | T1, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

Love's definition as the programme's most semantically comprehensive inner-being characteristic is confirmed and deepened by the structural relationship analysis. The term sharing ratio (0.701 — highest in C17) and the co-occurrence breadth (196 with compassion through to 29 with mercy) reflect the definition's conclusion: love is not a bounded standalone characteristic but the semantic centre of a cluster. The sequential finding (love as mediating-and-generative node — Q&A-105) maps onto the definition's tripartite structure: love is downstream (condition-received from God), central (disposition that organises), and upstream (act that generates all subsequent ethical life). The definition's vocabulary arc (from divine being to love of darkness — Q&A-131) is confirmed by the structural relationship data: the vocabulary extends into every adjacent registry (through XREF sharing, co-occurrence, and shared anchors), touching every corner of the programme. The structural analysis thus confirms the definitional claim: love is not merely a characteristic among others but the organising principle of the inner-being vocabulary.

**SYN-INTER-103-010 | T2, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

Love's comprehensive constitutional location (all five levels) is structurally consistent with its position as the programme's central co-occurrence node. A characteristic that operates at every constitutional level will co-occur with the broadest range of other characteristics — because any verse that names soul, heart, mind, spirit, or body is potentially within love's evidential terrain. The co-occurrence breadth (top 25 covering registries from C17 through C20 and beyond) reflects the constitutional breadth: strength and authority (C20) co-occur with love because the same verses that address God's inner character include both love and power vocabulary — the divine portrait (Exo 34:6) is constitutionally comprehensive. The vocabulary sharing ratio (0.701 — highest in C17) similarly reflects the constitutional comprehensiveness: love shares vocabulary with compassion (at the somatic/racham level), with goodness (at the heart/tov level), with desire (at the ahav/phileō root level), with peace (at the soul-rest level). Constitutional breadth and vocabulary breadth are the same phenomenon viewed from different angles.

**SYN-INTER-103-014 | T3, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

The faculty engagement breadth in T3 is consistent with love's structural position as the programme's central sequential node in T6. A characteristic that engages all eleven faculties will necessarily stand at the intersection of the programme's full inner-being vocabulary — because any characteristic that engages one faculty will co-occur with love insofar as love also engages that faculty. The memory-faculty engagement explains part of the co-occurrence with despair (R044 — 55 shared verses): lament appeals to the memory of God's love (Lam 3:22) as its ground. The identity-faculty engagement explains the co-occurrence with calling (R019 — 51 shared): identity-as-beloved and identity-as-called are grammatically co-ordinate (Rom 1:7). The moral-sensitivity engagement explains co-occurrence with guilt (R073 — 32 shared): moral sensitivity calibrated by love recognises guilt as the state that precedes the forgiveness that generates love (Luk 7:47). Faculty engagement and structural relationships thus form a coherent explanatory system: the faculty profile generates the co-occurrence pattern.

**SYN-INTER-103-017 | T4, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

The three relational axes in T4 generate the structural relationship pattern in T6. The GOD→HUMAN axis explains love's highest co-occurrences with the programme's theological vocabulary: calling (R019 — 51 shared, identity-as-beloved and identity-as-called), faith (R059 — 62 shared, the inner condition for receiving divine love), and compassion (R023 — 196 shared, the compassionate-responsive mode of divine love). The HUMAN→HUMAN axis explains the co-occurrence with peace (R117 — 68 shared, peacemaking as love's horizontal expression) and community-related vocabulary. The sequential structure (divine love prior → human love responsive → ethical life) explains love's mediating position (Q&A-105): it stands between the GOD→HUMAN axis and the HUMAN→HUMAN axis as the transmission point. Love received from God becomes love given to neighbour — the sequential position is the relational interface described structurally. The 48 shared anchor verses are the evidential sites where these relational intersections are most visible: Exo 34:6 (GOD→HUMAN axis at maximum density), Gal 5:22 (HUMAN→HUMAN axis generated by Spirit), Joh 13:34 (HUMAN→HUMAN axis constituted by GOD→HUMAN love as model).

**SYN-INTER-103-019 | T5, T6** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

The formation process and the structural relationships are mutually reinforcing systems. The formation pathway (divine love received → Spirit infusion → soul purification → community expression) maps onto the sequential structural position: love's formation follows the same sequence as love's structural position in the programme — first received (downstream of divine action), then formed (mediating), then expressed (generative of ethical life). The growth-capacity of love (1Th 3:12 — Q&A-092) explains the programme's most puzzling co-occurrence finding: strength and authority (C20, 105 and 69 shared verses respectively). Love growing under the Lord's enabling encounters contexts of power — covenantal love among the people of God is exercised within structures of authority, governance, and strength. The love-power co-occurrence reflects the formation context: love formed within communities is formed within structures of authority. The eschatological trajectory (toward perfect love and direct knowledge) describes the structural relationships' completion: when love reaches its eschatological form, the partial sequential relationships (love received → love given → ethical life) are resolved into the direct love-knowledge of the new covenant (Jer 31:34).

**SYN-INTER-103-021 | T6, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

The structural relationship data and the evidential foundation are the most mutually confirming pair in the synthesis matrix. The co-occurrence breadth (T6.1) is directly explained by the vocabulary breadth (T7.1.10 — the widest semantic arc in the programme): a vocabulary that spans from divine being to love of darkness will co-occur with every other inner-being characteristic because it occupies every register of the inner-being vocabulary. The shared anchor density (48 verses — T6.6) is explained by the primary anchor's function (Lam 3:22 — T7.2.5/T7.2.6): the programme anchors the most significant cross-registry verses at love because love is the definitional centre from which adjacent characteristics derive their meaning. The vocabulary sharing ratio (0.701 — T6.4) is explained by the root-level connections (T7.1.2): the racham root connects love, compassion, and mercy; the ahav root connects love and desire; the tov root connects love and goodness. The structural relationship data is, at its core, the observable programme-level effect of the lexical architecture that the evidential foundation analysis reveals. Structural breadth and lexical breadth are the same reality at different levels of analysis.
