# Grace — Pointers for Further Research and Synergy with Other Characteristics

*Registry 068 — Grace | Framework B Soul Word Analysis Programme | 2026-04-09*

---

## Opening Note

Grace does not stand alone. Of the 13 terms identified in this word study, 11 are shared with other word studies in the programme — a sharing ratio of 84.6%. Only 2 terms belong exclusively to grace. This is one of the highest overlap figures in the programme so far, and it signals something theologically important: grace is not a self-contained characteristic. It is a relational concept that by its nature touches almost every other dimension of the inner life. The connections below are not incidental; they are structural.

---

## 1. Mercy

**The closest and most urgent connection.**

The research identified a direct formal link between grace and mercy, marked in the data at the point where the supplication term *tachanun* (H8469) explicitly cross-references the mercy word family. The two concepts travel together throughout the Old Testament — God is repeatedly described as *gracious and merciful* in a paired formula (Exo 34:6; Psa 103:8; 145:8; Joel 2:13; Jonah 4:2). They are not synonyms. Grace names the disposition to give freely; mercy names the disposition to respond to need and suffering. Grace operates even where there is no distress; mercy is activated specifically by distress. Mercy is, in a sense, grace with its eyes open to pain.

The supplication terms (*tachanun*, *techinnah*) sit formally in the mercy word family rather than the grace word family — which means the human act of pleading for grace is categorised by the programme as a mercy behaviour. This boundary is worth examining carefully when the mercy word study is complete. The two registries will need to be read together.

**Synergy question:** Does the biblical data support a distinction between grace received in blessing and grace received in suffering — and does that distinction map onto the grace/mercy boundary?

---

## 2. Guilt and Conscience

**A formally documented connection.**

Multiple terms in this word study carry an explicit note in the research data: *"Guilt resolution vocabulary — grace, graciousness, and supplication as response to guilt."* This note applies to *chen* (H2580), *chanan* (H2603A), *channun* (H2587), *techinnah* (H8467), and *tachanun* (H8469). The connection is not interpretive; it is structural. These terms were identified during the guilt word study as part of the vocabulary describing how guilt is resolved — through the seeking and receiving of grace.

This means grace and guilt are in a formal transactional relationship in Scripture. Guilt creates the need; grace supplies the resolution. The supplication terms are the hinge — they name the interior movement from guilt toward grace.

**Synergy question:** The guilt word study (Registry 073) and conscience word study (Registry 023 — which owns *channun* and *chanan*) will need to be mapped against grace to trace the full guilt-supplication-grace-forgiveness arc. Grace cannot be fully understood without guilt, and guilt cannot be resolved without grace.

## 3. Forgiveness

**Embedded within grace rather than separate from it.**

The verb *charizō* (G5483) — one of the five core grace terms — is the specific word Paul uses for interpersonal forgiveness (Eph 4:32; Col 2:13; Col 3:13; 2Cor 2:7–10). Forgiveness in the New Testament is not a separate concept with its own independent vocabulary; it is grace enacted in a specific relational situation. To forgive is to *charizō* — to give grace to the person who has wronged you.

This means the forgiveness word study, when it is run, will find significant overlap with this registry. The relationship is not one of synergy between two parallel concepts; it is one of inclusion — forgiveness is a particular expression of grace.

**Synergy question:** Where does grace end and forgiveness begin? The data suggests forgiveness is the relational form grace takes when there has been a specific offence. Grace is the broader disposition; forgiveness is the specific act it produces in the presence of wrongdoing.

## 4. Humility

**The soil in which grace takes root.**

The research surfaced a direct statement in Proverbs: *"Toward the scorners he is scornful, but to the humble he gives favour"* (Pro 3:34 — *chen*, H2580). The New Testament echoes this: James 4:6 and 1 Peter 5:5 both quote this verse explicitly — *"God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble."* Grace and humility are paired not incidentally but as a governing principle.

The implication is that humility is the posture that receives grace, and pride is the posture that blocks it. The supplication terms (*tachanun*, *techinnah*) themselves encode humility — to supplicate is to acknowledge need and dependence, which requires the laying down of self-sufficiency.

**Synergy question:** Is humility a precondition of grace, a product of grace, or both? The data points in both directions — humility opens the channel, but receiving grace also produces humility (as in Ruth 2:10, where Ruth's astonishment at undeserved favour is itself a grace-deepening moment).

## 5. Love (*Chesed* / Steadfast Love)

**The most significant gap in this registry — and the most important connection.**

The registry description itself names the gap: *"The Hebrew vocabulary (hen, favour; chesed, steadfast love) and the Greek (charis) together carry the sense of the one who has the power to grant or withhold choosing freely to give."* The word *chesed* (H2617 — steadfast love, lovingkindness, covenant loyalty) is mentioned in the registry description as a conceptual partner of grace but does not appear in the term inventory. It belongs to a different word study.

This is the most theologically significant outbound connection. *Chesed* and *chen* travel together throughout the Psalms and prophets. Grace (*chen*) tends to name the initial act of favour; *chesed* names the sustained, covenant-faithful love that continues to give. Grace opens; *chesed* sustains. Together they describe the full arc of God's relational commitment to human beings.

The formal connection also appears at Exodus 33–34, the definitive self-disclosure of God's character, where *chen*, *chanan*, *chesed*, and *racham* (mercy/compassion) all appear together as facets of the same divine character. Any synthesis of grace that does not account for *chesed* will be incomplete.

**Synergy question:** How does grace relate to covenant faithfulness? Grace appears to be the *mode* of giving; *chesed* appears to be the *commitment* to keep giving. The love word study (Registry 103) will be essential reading alongside this one.

## 6. Peace and Hope

**Cluster neighbours.**

Grace is assigned to Cluster C17 in the programme's cross-registry relational architecture. Peace (Registry 117) and Hope (Registry 078) have previously emerged as part of a positive cluster alongside Love. The connection with grace is textually direct: nearly every Pauline letter opens with *"Grace and peace to you"* — a paired greeting that is not formulaic but theological. Grace is the given state; peace is the experienced condition it produces. They are sequential, not synonymous.

Hope connects through Romans 5:2 — *"Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God."* Standing in grace and rejoicing in hope are presented as aspects of a single relational reality. Grace is the present standing; hope is the forward orientation of the same standing.

**Synergy question:** Does the programme data support grace as the generative source of both peace and hope — the condition that makes both possible?

## 7. Gratitude / Thankfulness

**An internal connection within the grace vocabulary itself.**

The Greek word *charis* carries both the *giving* of grace and the *returning* of thanks as part of its own semantic range. This is not a coincidence of translation — it encodes a theological circuit. Grace given produces gratitude; gratitude is the soul's recognition of grace received. Several New Testament occurrences of *charis* in this registry are translated *thanks* or *thankfulness* (Rom 6:17; 1Cor 15:57; 2Cor 9:15; Luk 6:32–34).

Gratitude does not appear as a named word study in the programme's current list, but its vocabulary overlaps with grace so directly that any treatment of grace as a human characteristic will need to account for gratitude as its natural response-form. The ungrateful person (*acharistos*, G0884 — literally *without charis*) is the one in whom the grace circuit has broken down.

**Synergy question:** Is gratitude a sub-characteristic of grace, or a separate characteristic that grace produces? The linguistic data suggests it is internal to grace — the same word covers both.

## 8. Strength and Sufficiency

**An unexpected connection surfaced by the anchor verses.**

The anchor verse for grace as the relational sphere in which the believer lives is 2 Corinthians 12:9 — *"My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness."* This verse pairs grace directly with divine power (*dunamis*) and positions it as the source of sufficiency in weakness. Grace here is not merely relational warmth; it is empowering presence.

This connects grace to the strength word family (Registry 174 — Wisdom also appears here as a parallel empowering concept). The Pauline argument is that grace is the mechanism by which divine strength operates in and through human weakness. This is a significant soul-level claim: grace is not just a relational posture; it is an enabling power.

**Synergy question:** How does the strength word study characterise the source of inner strength? If it points toward grace as that source, the two registries will need explicit cross-mapping.

## 9. Eschatological Dimension

**A forward-pointing signal that needs further development.**

The anchor verse Zechariah 12:10 — *"I will pour out on the house of David... a spirit of grace and pleas for mercy"* — appears as an anchor for both the grace term (*chen*) and the supplication term (*tachanun*). The eschatological flag was raised on *tachanun* by the programme's analytical process. This verse describes grace poured out as a spirit at the end of the age, producing mourning and transformation.

The significance for further research is that grace in its fullest expression is presented in Scripture as an eschatological event — something not yet fully given but promised. The grace known now is a foretaste of a grace still coming. This connects to the Hope registry and potentially to the Repentance registry (Registry 135), since the mourning produced in Zechariah 12:10 is a form of deep repentance.

**Synergy question:** How do the repentance and hope word studies handle the Zechariah 12:10 text? This verse is claimed by at least three registries (grace, supplication, and repentance-adjacent mourning) and will need coordinated treatment in the synthesis phase.
