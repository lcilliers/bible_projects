## Lexical and semantic analysis (T7.1)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

The sole OWNER term is H5414G na.tan (Hebrew verb, "to give, put, set") from the NATAN root family. The root meaning reveals: giving as the fundamental act of placing something in the possession or experience of another. The root generates the name family (Elnathan, Nathaniel, Mattan — "God gives") confirming that giving is one of Israel's primary theological naming concepts. The XREF vocabulary holds the disposition-quality terms: H0530 emunah (faithfulness as stable quality), H0539 aman (be faithful/trustworthy), H0571G emet (truth/faithful), H2617A hesed (steadfast love), G4102 pistis (faith/faithfulness), G4103 pistos (faithful). The root meanings reveal: emunah/aman from a root meaning firmness, stability, reliability; emet from a root meaning truth, constancy; hesed with connotations of loyal covenant love; pistis/pistos from a root meaning persuasion, conviction, reliability. Together the OWNER and XREF roots reveal two conceptual poles: enacted giving (na.tan/NATAN) and stable reliability (aman/emunah/emet/hesed/pistis). Both poles are necessary for the full biblical account of faithfulness.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

H5414G na.tan is a verb — it has no primary nominal or adjectival form in this registry's OWNER terms. Grammatical range: Qal (active — the subject gives), Niphal (passive — something is given/permitted), Hophal (passive causative — something is made to be given). The exclusively verbal character of the OWNER term is analytically significant: this characteristic, as accessed through na.tan, is known only through action — not through a noun naming a quality or an adjective describing a person. It is grammatically actional at its core. This contrasts with the XREF vocabulary: emunah, emet, hesed are all nominals — they name the quality itself. The grammatical gap between OWNER (verb) and XREF (nouns) mirrors the analytical gap between enacted faithfulness (na.tan) and named faithfulness (emunah/emet/hesed). The characteristic is grammatically event-shaped: it happens, it is given, it occurs — it is not primarily described as a state one possesses.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

Na.tan's semantic range is exceptionally broad (OBS-060-011, OBS-060-012): at minimum 25 distinct English glosses — give, bestow, grant, permit, ascribe, employ, devote, consecrate, dedicate, pay wages, sell, exchange, lend, commit, entrust, give over, deliver up, yield, occasion, produce, requite, report, mention, utter, stretch out, extend. The breadth reflects the verb's generativity from a single root concept (placing something in another's possession/experience). For this registry's inner-being analysis, only a subset of this range is active — the configurations where giving engages inner-being content (OBS-060-012): (a) object given is inner-being quality, (b) giver acts from disclosed inner-being state, (c) giving expresses inner orientation. The 122 UNCLASSIFIED verses represent the remaining semantic range — mundane transactional giving without inner-being dimension. The semantic range is maximally wide at the lexical level but analytically constrained at the inner-being level.

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

Yes — the vocabulary as a whole (OWNER + XREF) structurally distinguishes the aspects. Na.tan (OWNER) = the act; emunah/emet/hesed/pistis (XREF) = the disposition and quality. The vocabulary thereby encodes the distinction between faithfulness as acted and faithfulness as characterised — two genuinely distinct aspects of the same inner-being reality. The Niphal/Hophal forms of na.tan = received giving (the passive — what is given to the person); the Qal = given by the person (active). So even within na.tan itself the vocabulary distinguishes giving and receiving. The XREF terms do not distinguish received from given at the root level — emunah describes the quality whether expressed or received. This vocabulary architecture confirms that the enacted dimension (na.tan) and the dispositional dimension (emunah family) are not merely different ways of saying the same thing — they are structurally different aspects of faithfulness requiring distinct analysis.

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

Yes — from the XREF vocabulary: apistia (G0570 — unbelief/unfaithfulness), apistos (G0571 — unbelieving/unfaithful), apisteō (G0569 — to disbelieve), owned by R165 (unbelief) and R059 (faith). These are the lexical negations of pistis/pistos (faith/faithful). For the na.tan OWNER term: no specific negation verb exists — the structural opposite of giving (withholding, refusing, withdrawing) is expressed through other vocabulary in the Bible (mana — to withhold, H4513, as in Hos 11:8's rahamim motif). Within the classified corpus, the structural opposite of inner-being giving-toward is giving-over (the judicial mode, 872-004) — which uses the same verb (na.tan) but in an adverse direction. The absence of a dedicated negation for na.tan confirms the verb's semantic neutrality — it is directionally flexible, not inherently positive or negative.

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

Yes — from the XREF vocabulary: pistos (G4103 — faithful, trustworthy person; 62 verses, owned by R059). Pistos is used substantively as a person-type term: "the faithful one," the person characterised by pistis/faithfulness. H0529 emun (faithful — 8 verses, R059) is a similar Hebrew adjectival/person-type term. For na.tan itself: no person-type derivative exists in the data — "the giver" is not a character type in the classified corpus; the emphasis is on the act, not the character type of the one who acts. The person-type vocabulary (pistos, emun) belongs to the XREF / dispositional dimension, not to the OWNER / actional dimension. This confirms the grammatical finding (Q&A-171): the actional dimension of faithfulness (na.tan) does not generate person-type vocabulary; the dispositional dimension does.

**T7.1.8 — What does the LXX use of the vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

The data provides partial evidence. The LXX typically renders na.tan with didōmi (to give) — this is a standard LXX equivalent that carries the same semantic breadth into the Greek. The XREF Greek pist- vocabulary (pistis, pistos, pisteuō) represents the LXX/NT development of the aman/emunah root into the faith-faithfulness vocabulary of the Greek Bible. The gap: the data does not include specific LXX analysis of the anchor verses — the `lsj_parse` field is not applicable for Hebrew terms, and no word analysis data specifically addressing LXX rendering of na.tan in these inner-being contexts is present in the extract. The cross-testament continuity of the enacted-faithfulness concept (na.tan → didōmi → NT giving vocabulary) is inferrable but not confirmed from the data as extracted. LXX continuity assertion should be flagged as inference.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

The full vocabulary arc — OWNER (na.tan) plus XREF (aman family, emet, hesed, pist- family) — reveals two conceptual poles that together constitute faithfulness as a biblical characteristic: (1) Enacted faithfulness (na.tan): the act by which inner-being commitment is externalised — relational, directional, volitional, contextually polymorphous (performative, responsive, endowing, judicial, transformative). (2) Constituted faithfulness (emunah, emet, hesed, pistis): the stable dispositional quality of being reliably what one is committed to be — the character quality, the inner orientation, the ground from which enacted faithfulness flows. The full vocabulary arc reveals that biblical faithfulness is not reducible to either pole alone: faithfulness without enactment is disposition that never becomes real; faithfulness without disposition is act that lacks moral ground. The two poles are analytically distinct but experientially inseparable. This is the governing finding of the T7.1 lexical analysis.


## Verse and literary interpretation (T7.2)

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

Taking Gen 15:18 as one primary anchor (covenantal mode): "To your offspring I give (na.tan) this land." Na.tan functions as the main performative verb of the covenant speech-act — it is both the grammatical predicate and the constitutive act of the covenant. Without this verb, the covenant is not enacted. The sentence is structured around na.tan: subject (Lord) → verb (give) → object (land) → recipient (offspring). The argument is: I commit by giving. In Hos 11:8: "How can I give you up (na.tan)?" — na.tan functions as the main verb of a rhetorical question that structures the entire divine inner-being disclosure. The withholding of the verb's action is the structural turn of the verse. In both key anchors, na.tan is grammatically and argumentatively central — not a background descriptor but the hinge on which the meaning turns.

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

The anchor verse corpus spans four literary forms. (1) Narrative (Gen 12:7, Gen 15:18, Gen 29:33, Job 1:21): responsible interpretation requires attention to plot and character — the giving is embedded in a specific relational and historical moment. (2) Prophecy (Isa 61:3, Isa 50:6, Hos 11:8, Hos 13:11, Jer 12:7, Jer 13:16): responsible interpretation requires attention to rhetorical address and the distinction between divine speech and prophetic interpretation. The first-person divine speeches ("I gave," "How can I give you up") require particular care about the nature of divine inner-being language. (3) Wisdom/divine speech (Job 38:36, Job 1:21): responsible interpretation requires attention to the dramatic frame — Job's blessing is within a frame where Satan has made a claim, and God's question from the whirlwind is within a legal disputation. (4) Liturgical/prayer (Neh 9:20): responsible interpretation requires attention to the retrospective narrative form — this is communal confession of divine faithfulness, not direct reportage. The diversity of literary forms means no single interpretive lens covers the whole corpus.

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

Three logical structures are evidenced. (1) Hos 11:8: Premise 1 — Israel deserves giving-over (implied: covenant violation). Premise 2 — God's heart recoils, his compassion grows warm. Conclusion — the giving-over is withheld. The logical form is: deserved-consequence is overridden by inner-being quality. This is not ordinary logic — it is the logic of grace overriding desert. (2) Gen 15:18 (covenant structure): Premise — I am the Lord who brought you out (prior redemptive act). Conclusion — I give you this land (covenantal consequence). The form is: established relationship → covenantal giving as its natural expression. (3) Jer 13:16 (imperative): Premise — God brings darkness to those who withhold glory. Conclusion — Therefore give glory before darkness comes. The form is: consequence-of-failure → urgency-of-action. Each logical structure reveals a different aspect of the characteristic's inner logic.

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

The anchor verses are distributed across four contextual settings. (1) Covenantal: Gen 12:7, Gen 15:18, Mal 2:5, Neh 9:8 — formal covenant-establishment or covenantal renewal contexts. These settings require the giving to be read as covenantally binding, not merely generous. (2) Prophetic-judicial: Jer 12:7, Hos 13:11, Isa 42:8, Isa 47:6 — prophetic contexts of divine judgement and address to Israel. These require the giving to be read as morally consistent with the divine character, not arbitrary. (3) Eschatological-prophetic: Isa 61:3, Hos 2:15, Isa 35:2 — contexts of divine restoration announcement. These require the giving to be read as new-creation initiative, not mere restoration to prior state. (4) Narrative-individual: Gen 29:33, Job 1:21, Job 38:36 — individual persons encountering divine giving. These require attention to the specific relational situation of the individual. The diversity of settings confirms that inner-being giving is not a specialised sub-category of divine action but a pervasive mode present across the full range of biblical contexts.

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

Two candidates present themselves for different reasons, and the choice depends on which dimension of the characteristic is primary. (1) Hos 11:8 — the most comprehensive: it holds the judicial giving-over and the compassionate withholding in tension, names the divine inner-being faculties (leb, rahamim) explicitly, shows the inner-being movement that determines giving, and reveals the structural relationship between justice and compassion in divine faithfulness. No other verse holds this much of the characteristic's architecture simultaneously. (2) Isa 61:3 — the most eschatologically complete: it shows the transformative mode at full expression, names the spirit faculty directly, holds mourning and praise in one verse, and points toward the endpoint of the formation arc. Both verses are programme-density nodes (shared with 3-5 other registries). Assessment: Hos 11:8 is the primary analytical anchor — it reveals the inner-being decision-structure of divine giving most directly. Isa 61:3 is the primary transformative anchor.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

Taking Hos 11:8 as primary (Q&A-184): what it reveals that no other verse reveals is the divine inner-being conflict between judicial consistency and compassionate love — and its resolution. The verse discloses that faithfulness-as-giving is not mechanically determined by covenant rules; it is determined by the interaction of divine inner-being qualities (justice and compassion) at the level of the heart (leb). No other anchor verse shows this internal divine deliberation. Gen 15:18 shows the covenantal giving but not its inner deliberation. Isa 61:3 shows the outcome of transformative giving but not the inner-being movement that produces it. Jer 12:7 shows love coexisting with judicial giving but not the mechanism of their relationship. Hos 11:8 alone shows: (1) the competing inner-being logics in the divine person; (2) the faculty (leb/rahamim) where resolution occurs; (3) that faithfulness is not determined by moral logic alone but by the primacy of compassion within the divine character. This makes Hos 11:8 irreplaceable.


## Somatic dimension — body significance, direction, deposit

How (and whether) the characteristic is grounded in the body — whether Scripture links it to specific body parts, the direction of body↔soul interaction, and any constitutional deposit produced over time.


### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**Prompt 1: Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

Yes — Isa 50:6 (OBS-060-045) provides the most explicit body-part linkage: "I gave my back to those who strike, and my cheeks to those who pull out the beard; I hid not my face from disgrace and spitting." Three body parts named: back, cheeks, face. The self-giving is enacted through and upon these specific body surfaces. Additionally, Isa 42:5 (group 872-003 corpus): "who gives breath (neshamah) to the people on it and spirit (ruach) to those who walk in it" — breath (neshamah) as the animating body-level endowment given by God. Breath is the physiological carrier of the spirit-endowment, grounding the giving of life at the bodily level.

**Prompt 2: If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

Two distinct functions. (1) Isa 50:6 — the body-part link is expressive: the back, cheeks, and face are the external surfaces through which the inner disposition of covenantal self-giving is expressed. The body does not cause the giving — the inner trust and covenantal identity does — but the body is the medium through which the giving becomes visible and real in the world. The function is inner-to-outer expression: the inner-being act (self-giving in trust) finds its form in the bodily surfaces offered to suffering. (2) Isa 42:5 — the breath-giving is functional: God gives breath to constitute bodily life. The giving of neshamah is the act that makes the body alive and capable of carrying the person. This is constitutional-functional giving at the body level.


### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**Prompt 1: Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

The direction is inner-to-body (soul expressing through body) in Isa 50:6. The Servant's inner disposition — trust in divine vindication, covenantal obedience — expresses itself through the bodily surfaces offered to suffering. The body does not generate the giving; it is the medium through which the inner act becomes external. The soul/inner orientation is prior and causal; the body is the expressive vehicle. For Isa 42:5 (breath-giving): the direction is outer-to-inner (God gives breath constituting bodily life → bodily life is the carrier of personal existence). This is God's action moving from the physical level to constitute the person. The two instances show opposite directions: Isa 50:6 is inner → body (human self-giving expressed somatically); Isa 42:5 is outer → body → inner (divine giving constituting the physical basis of inner existence).

**Prompt 2: What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

The inner-to-body directionality (Isa 50:6) confirms that human self-giving in this registry is fundamentally an inner-being act that finds somatic expression — not a somatic act that generates an inner experience. This has a significant consequence: the body's suffering in Isa 50:6 (back struck, beard pulled, face spat upon) is not the source of the faithfulness but its evidence. The inner act of giving precedes and determines the somatic form. This prevents a reading in which bodily endurance is itself the virtue — the virtue is the inner orientation that produces the bodily self-offering. For the programme's spirit-soul-body mapping: the characteristic has inner-to-body directionality in its human expression, confirming its location in the inner-being register while showing somatic expression.


### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**Prompt 1: Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

The data provides one possible instance of generational consequence: the covenant-giving texts in group 872-001 (Gen 12:7, Gen 17:8, Gen 26:3) address Abraham and his offspring — the land-giving is explicitly intergenerational ("to you and to your offspring forever"). The covenantal giving carries across generations as a constitutive element of Israelite identity. Whether this constitutes a constitutional body-deposit in the technical sense the prompt intends (including DNA-level consequence) cannot be determined from this data. The covenantal transmission is relational-identity rather than somatic-constitutional in the evidence available. The Neh 9:20 Spirit-giving to the wilderness generation suggests each generation receives the endowing giving fresh rather than inheriting it somatically. No explicit body-level deposit is evidenced.

**Prompt 2: What evidence supports or contradicts this?**

Supporting generational transmission (not body-deposit per se): the covenant-giving texts address Abraham's offspring across multiple generations — Gen 12:7, Gen 17:8, Gen 26:3, Neh 9:35-36. The land-giving is inherited identity, not re-given to each individual. Contradicting constitutional body-deposit: the Spirit-giving in Neh 9:20 is presented as a specific historical act to the wilderness generation, not as a permanent somatic inheritance. The eschatological giving in Isa 61:3 and Hos 2:15 is presented as new initiative, not as retrieval of an inherited deposit. The evidence does not support a constitutional body deposit in the somatic sense; what transmits generationally is covenantal identity and promise, which is relational-narratival rather than biological.

**Prompt 3: If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.**

The evidence is not silent but does not confirm a constitutional body deposit. No explicit somatic deposit is evidenced. The data is silent on DNA-level or physiological generational deposit. T5.7 consequence: per the catalogue instruction, since no constitutional body deposit is confirmed at T2.8, T5.7 (Deposit Consequence) closes formally — all three T5.7 prompts receive N with this dependency stated explicitly.


## Synthesis — what the language reveals


### Intra-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTRA-060-005 | T5** *(outcome: D; structural: N/A)*

T5 as a whole reveals that this characteristic has an eschatological orientation as its formative telos. The formation arc moves from constitutive endowment (Spirit and wisdom given to form inner-being capacity) through sustained covenantal narrative (the memory of received giving as formation environment) through suffering-testing (revealing whether inner-being giving is genuine) toward eschatological fullness ("oaks of righteousness," total communal transformation). The mechanisms are plural and context-specific — encounter (covenantal), gradual formation (endowing), sovereign reversal (eschatological) — revealing that formation is not a single pathway but a multi-modal process. The suffering relation is analytically significant: the characteristic is suffering-tested (revealed in human direction) and suffering-responsive (triggered in divine direction), showing that suffering is not incidental to the formation process but an integral context. T5.7 closes formally — no constitutional body deposit. What T5 reveals as a whole: faithfulness-as-giving is constitutively developmental — its present forms anticipate and move toward a fullness that is not yet fully realised. Every giving is, in some sense, an eschatological act: it participates in the pattern of divine giving that is moving toward total, permanent, communal transformation.


### Inter-tier syntheses

**SYN-INTER-060-004 | T1, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: sequential)*

The working description of the characteristic (T1.2.3, Q&A-018) — faithfulness as an inner-being quality that enacts commitments, orientations, and compassions through giving — is directly illuminated by T5's formation account. **SYN-INTER-060-009 | T2, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: causal)*

The constitutional movement patterns (T2.10 — constitutive spirit→mind, expressive inner→body, transformative outside-in→inside renewed→inside-out) directly correspond to the formation mechanisms (T5.3 — gradual formation, encounter/event, sovereign reversal). The constitutive movement (spirit→mind) corresponds to the gradual-formation mechanism: Spirit given to instruct forms inner capacity over time through repeated constitutional deepening. What this pair reveals: constitutional movement and formative mechanism are two descriptions of the same transformative process. **SYN-INTER-060-016 | T4, T5** *(outcome: D; structural: sequential)*

The relational interfaces (T4 — divine-initiative, human-responsive, covenantal scope) and the formation arc (T5 — constitutive endowment, sustained narrative, eschatological orientation) reveal that formation happens through relational interfaces. The formation arc (T5 — constitutive endowment → sustained covenantal narrative → suffering-testing → eschatological fullness) and the structural position (T6 — hub producing worship/hope/wisdom, produced by love/trust/compassion, connecting characteristic across all five dimensions) reveal that the formation arc is itself a structural arc through the inner-being landscape. **SYN-INTER-060-020 | T5, T7** *(outcome: D; structural: causal)*

The formation arc (T5 — developmental, eschatologically oriented, suffering-tested) and the methodological analysis (T7 — virtue ethics illuminates, diverges on received-constitutive character; Hos 11:8 as primary anchor) produce a pointed finding. The virtue ethics framework (T7.3.2) illuminates the formation arc well — disposition formed through repeated enacted commitment toward stable character — but diverges at the key point: virtue ethics assumes the person generates virtue through practice, while the biblical account places constitutive divine giving (Spirit, wisdom) as the prior condition of formative capacity (T5.5, T2.1). The divergence maps exactly onto the T5 finding that formation is constitutively dependent on received divine giving. Hos 11:8 as primary anchor (T7.2.5) is also the key verse for the formation arc: divine compassion overriding judicial logic is itself a formative act — it preserves the relational context within which human formation can continue. What this pair reveals: the evidential and methodological analysis (T7) independently identifies the same structural priority that the formation analysis (T5) finds: the constitutive primacy of divine giving as the ground of human formation.
