**OBS-060-039:** Hos 11:8 continued — structural cross-group finding. The same structural pair appears across the most theologically opposite contexts. Q5: Divine inner states (anger, wrath) as the determinants of what God gives and takes.

#### Group 872-006 — Divine-Human Correspondence — Glory Ascription (GOD/HUMAN)
Anchors: Isa 42:8, Jer 13:16

**OBS-060-046:** Isa 42:8 — "I am the Lord; that is my name; my glory I give to no other, nor my praise to carved idols." God will not give (natan) his glory to another. The withheld giving here defines divine uniqueness: glory (kabod) is the outward expression of divine inner being. To give glory is to share the expression of one's inner reality with another. God refuses this — his glory is irreducibly his own. The negation ("I give to no other") is itself a positive statement about the structure of the divine-human correspondence: the giving of glory has a determined direction — from human to God, not from God to another. Q5: The verse defines the DHC dimension by negation — the correspondence is structured around the proper direction of glory-ascription. Q2: R121 (praise) named alongside glory — both are natan-objects belonging exclusively to God.

**OBS-060-047:** Jer 13:16 — "Give glory to the Lord your God before he brings darkness, before your feet stumble on the twilight mountains, and while you look for light he turns it into gloom and makes it deep darkness." The imperative: humans commanded to give glory (natan kabod) to God. The temporal urgency ("before darkness") frames the failure to give glory as producing inner-being disorder — darkness, stumbling, gloom replacing light. Q3: The verse establishes a sequence: giving glory → light/orientation; failing to give glory → darkness/stumbling. The act of glory-ascription is causally connected to inner-being ordering. Q5: The command "give glory" is a directive toward the proper relational orientation — returning to God what is his. The human who gives glory to God is aligned; the one who withholds it is disordered. This makes glory-giving a regulative act for the inner life's relationship to God.

#### Group 872-007 — Transformation — Eschatological Giving (GOD)
Anchors: Isa 61:3, Hos 2:15

**OBS-060-048:** Isa 61:3 — "to grant to those who mourn in Zion — to give them a beautiful headdress instead of ashes, the oil of gladness instead of mourning, the garment of praise instead of a faint spirit; that they may be called oaks of righteousness, the planting of the Lord, that he may be glorified." The triple exchange: ashes → beautiful headdress; mourning → oil of gladness; faint spirit (ruach kehah) → garment of praise. Each exchange is structured as na.tan replacing a negative inner-being state with a positive gift. The faint spirit (ruach kehah — spirit grown dim, depleted inner life-energy) → garment of praise is the most directly inner-being exchange: an inner-being state (depleted spirit) is replaced by an inner-being orientation (praise). Q4: Ruach (spirit) is explicitly named as the faculty transformed — the giving operates directly on the spirit dimension. Q3: Mourning → gladness → praise: a sequential inner-being movement produced by the giving. Q1: Shared with R006 (anointing), R071 (grief), R121 (praise), R151 (sorrow) — transformation, grief-to-praise arc is a cross-registry structural node.

**OBS-060-049:** Isa 61:3 continued — the transformation is eschatological-restorative, not merely compensatory. "Instead of" (takhat) signals replacement, not supplement. What is given exceeds the prior state: "oaks of righteousness" is not the restoration of what mourning interrupted — it is a new identity bestowed. The giving transforms inner conditions into a new inner-being state that was not there before. This is qualitatively different from the giving-back of what was taken (Hos 2:15) — here the giving creates something new from depletion. The eschatological character: this kind of giving belongs to the new-age initiative of God, not to ordinary provision.

**OBS-060-050:** Hos 2:15 — "And there I will give her her vineyards and make the Valley of Achor a door of hope. And there she shall answer as in the days of her youth, as at the time when she came out of the land of Egypt." The Valley of Achor was the site of shame and judgement (Josh 7 — Achan's sin and stoning). God transforms this site of failure into "a door of hope" (petach tikvah). The giving is restorative-transformative: what was the locus of inner-being shame becomes the entry point into renewed relationship. "She shall answer" (anah — to respond, to sing responsively) is the inner-being measure of the transformation: the restored giving produces restored responsiveness. Q3: Giving of vineyards → transformation of shame-site → restored inner responsiveness: a sequence ending in relational-inner capacity restored. Q1: R078 (hope) — tikvah appears as the gift; 5 shared co-occurrence verses confirmed. Q5: The Exodus reference frames the eschatological giving as re-enactment of the original covenant formation — God gives again as he gave at the beginning, reconstituting the relationship. The transformation is not forward only but also backward: re-grounding in the original giving.

**OBS-060-051:** Cross-group synthesis observation — Unit 7 reading complete. The 14 anchor verses reveal a structural architecture of divine giving across the registry: (1) Covenantal giving constitutes commitment by naming it specifically (Gen 12:7, Gen 15:18). (2) Compassionate giving is responsive — triggered by perceived inner suffering (Gen 29:33) and overrides judicial logic through inner-being movement (Hos 11:8). (3) Endowing giving deposits inner-being capacities (Spirit, wisdom, understanding) into the human (Neh 9:20, Job 38:36). (4) Judicial giving-over proceeds from inner-being states (anger, love) and is structurally parallel in form to compassionate giving-to (Hos 13:11, Jer 12:7). (5) Human self-giving is either receptive relinquishment (Job 1:21) or active bodily self-offering grounded in inner trust (Isa 50:6). (6) Glory-giving is the proper directional act of the divine-human correspondence — withheld by God from others (Isa 42:8), commanded of humans toward God (Jer 13:16). (7) Eschatological giving reverses inner depletion and creates new inner-being states that exceed restoration (Isa 61:3, Hos 2:15). Across all seven, giving is never merely transactional — it is always the outward expression of an inner-being orientation or state.
