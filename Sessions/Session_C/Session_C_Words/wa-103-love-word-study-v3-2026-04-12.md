## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Love is the most foundational characteristic in the entire vocabulary of the human inner life. It is not simply one quality among others — it is the orientation from which the whole moral and relational life of a person takes its shape. Where it is rightly ordered, everything else in the inner life tends toward its proper form. Where it is disordered, everything else follows the disorder.

What makes love unusual as a human characteristic is that it operates across every dimension of inner life simultaneously. It is a feeling — the warm attachment of a friend, the burning longing of the Song, the tenderness a mother feels for a child. It is also a settled disposition — a stable orientation of faithfulness and loyalty that does not depend on how it feels on a given day. And it is a choice — something that can be commanded, cultivated, trained, and directed. The biblical vocabulary holds all three together: the same word that describes God's unwavering covenant faithfulness can describe the Song's intoxicated longing, and the same imperative that commands love of neighbour commands love of enemy.

Love is radically directional — it is always love *toward* someone. And love defines itself by its consequences as much as by its quality. When Jesus says "if you love me, you will keep my commandments," he is not adding an obligation to love but naming what love is: love of Christ expresses itself as obedience. The direction of a person's love, and what it produces in their conduct, reveals the orientation of their inner life with unusual clarity. What a person loves most deeply, and in what order, is what they fundamentally are.

The vocabulary is also frank about love's potential for disorder. The same capacity for attachment that makes the greatest virtue possible makes the deepest corruption possible when it is misdirected. A person can love the darkness rather than the light — and that misdirection is not merely a mistake but a moral verdict on their inner state. Self-love (the person centred entirely on themselves rather than on God and others), love of money, love of pleasure, love of status: each is the same inner capacity of attachment turned back on itself or toward what cannot sustain it.

There is a bodily dimension to love that the research makes clear. The kiss, the womb, the bowels moved with compassion — the body participates in love's expression, and sometimes its physical gestures are the primary evidence that love is present at all. This is not accidental. The inner disposition and the outward act are not competitors; the body is how love becomes visible in the world. There is also a dimension that lies deeper than the body: love persists below conscious attention. The heart continues its orientation toward the beloved even in sleep.

Love, at its deepest level, does not originate in the human person. The data is consistent on this point: the love that defines the moral life is first given before it is exercised. Human love is modelled on, enabled by, and in its fullest form a participation in a prior divine reality — received before it is given, poured in before it flows out.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The opposite of love is not primarily indifference — it is hatred. The vocabulary is explicit: hatred is named as the constitutive inner disposition opposed to love, a settled enmity that motivates action and can be concealed. It stands at the furthest pole from love in the inner-being landscape, and the research treats the two as structural contraries — both can be caused in another, both operate first in the heart before they become visible in conduct. But love has subtler enemies too. Self-love — the person centred entirely on themselves rather than on God and others — is named as a foundational corruption. The last-days catalogue in 2 Timothy places self-love and money-love at the head of the list, not as consequences of inner collapse but as its generative root. The philo- compound family maps this systematically: the same root (philo-) applied to God, brothers, and good names the virtues; applied to money, pleasure, and self-priority names their corruptions. Disorder is not the absence of love's energy but its substitution — disordered people love intensely, but toward the wrong objects.

What feeds love is primarily a prior experience of being loved. The data points consistently to divine love as the source that enables human love — love poured into hearts by the Spirit (not commanded, modelled, or instructed, but delivered as a substance), preceded by God's act that makes the human response possible. The causal sequence is stated most simply in 1 John: "We love because he first loved us." From that ground, love is deepened by the practice of faithfulness — covenant loyalty that does not withdraw when circumstances become costly — and by received forgiveness. The woman who loves much in Luke 7 does so because she has been forgiven much: forgiveness is named explicitly as the cause that produces love as its inner response. Love can also grow: it is the subject of apostolic prayer, something that can increase and abound through divine gift and community formation.

What diminishes love is precisely what turns the inner person back toward themselves: the accumulation of goods that promise security, the pursuit of status, the preference for the world's approval. Misdirected desire hollows love out — not suddenly but through a prior attachment that, when tested, proves stronger than the relational bond it displaces. Demas did not desert Paul from cowardice; he deserted him because he was already in love with the present world.

What love produces in the person who has it is stability of character — the settled qualities of patience, kindness, freedom from envy and arrogance. These are not momentary states but stable dispositions, the inner life organised away from self-assertion. Love also produces freedom from anxiety about self, since the self is no longer the primary concern, and an openness toward those who make no natural claim on affection — including enemies.

What love produces in others and in relationships is the formation of genuine community. It is the publicly legible mark of discipleship — "by this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another." It inverts normal social dynamics: where status competition produces hierarchy, love produces a race toward servanthood. And it makes possible the eschatological vision of the new covenant, where universal knowledge of God means no one needs to instruct their neighbour in love because love will be the inner condition of all.

Love's ultimate source is not self-generated. What the research reveals at every layer — covenantal, relational, personal, eschatological — is that love at its fullest is received before it is given. The verb in 1 John 4:19 has no specified object in the Greek — not "we love him" but simply "we love" — which means the prior divine love is the ground not just of love for God but of the human capacity for love as such.

### Annotated Verse Evidence — Section 1 Material

Love is the most foundational characteristic in the entire vocabulary of the human inner life.

> **1 John 4:19** — *We love because he first loved us.*
>
> The simplest possible statement of the most consequential relationship in the research: love is secondary before it is primary. The causal sequence is unambiguous and the verb has no object — not "we love him" but simply "we love." The prior divine love is the ground of the entire human capacity for love, not merely of love directed back toward God. Every subsequent statement about what love is or does rests on this prior fact.

> **Romans 5:5** — *...hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us.*
>
> The verb (*ekcheo* — to pour out) is the same used for the Spirit's outpouring at Pentecost. Love is not gradually acquired but given in a pneumatic act. Its location — "our hearts" — places it at the affective-volitional centre of the person. This verse grounds the spirit-soul-body classification: love arrives at the spirit level (given by the Spirit) and takes up residence at the soul level (the heart). It is Spirit-Soul Interface in its constitutive form.

Love operates across every dimension of inner life simultaneously — feeling, settled disposition, and choice.

> **Song of Songs 8:6** — *Set me as a seal upon your heart, as a seal upon your arm, for love is strong as death, jealousy is fierce as the grave. Its flashes are flashes of fire, the very flame of the Lord.*
>
> This verse holds together love's intensity and its permanence with rare compression. The seal upon the heart and the arm places love simultaneously at the affective centre and the site of action and identity. The comparison to death signals love's absolute claim — it admits of no rival. The final phrase, "the very flame of the Lord," is the Old Testament's nearest approach to a theology of love's divine nature: human love's intensity participates in divine fire.

> **Matthew 22:37** — *You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.*
>
> The tripartite formula — heart, soul, and mind — does not divide love into three types but names the integration of the entire inner person in a single act of directed love. Love is commanded: it is volitional before it is affective. The comprehensiveness of the formula confirms that love cannot be fulfilled by one faculty directed while others remain disengaged.

Love defines itself by what it produces in conduct.

> **John 14:15** — *If you love me, you will keep my commandments.*
>
> The conditional is definitional rather than conditional: it does not say "if you truly love me, prove it by obedience" but states what love *is* — love of Christ expresses itself as obedience. This prevents any reading of love as a purely interior state without external form. The love-obedience nexus is one of the most structurally important relationships in the Johannine vocabulary.

Love is radically directional.

> **John 3:16** — *For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life.*
>
> The paradigmatic act of love: giving what is most precious, directed toward an undeserving object. Love moves outward toward its object at great cost. The world is named as love's object — not the faithful, not the covenant community, but the world in its estrangement. This directional quality — love toward what does not merit it — is the distinctive shape of agape throughout the New Testament.

> **Matthew 5:44** — *But I say to you, Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you.*
>
> The enemy-love command is the hardest evidence that agapao is volitional rather than affective. Enemies are not lovable objects — they actively oppose. The command cannot be addressed to the emotions; it is addressed to the will. It also co-occurs with prayer and the promise of divine sonship, linking love simultaneously with prayer, will, and calling and identity — the enemy-love command is one of the most cross-referencing verses in the New Testament.

> **John 3:19** — *And this is the judgment: the light has come into the world, and people loved the darkness rather than the light because their works were evil.*
>
> Love directed toward the wrong object is not a neutral error but a moral verdict. The love of darkness is the inner state from which evil works proceed — it is the cause, not the symptom. The same capacity that constitutes the greatest virtue constitutes the deepest disorder when turned toward what is not God.

The body participates in love's expression.

> **Luke 15:20** — *But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him.*
>
> Five physical acts in sequence: saw, felt compassion (splanchnon — bowels/visceral interior), ran, embraced, kissed. Dignity is abandoned. The compassion is bodily before it is expressed by the body. The running, the embrace, the repeated kiss are the excess of love that cannot contain itself. This verse is the New Testament's most concentrated somatic description of love in movement.

> **Luke 7:38** — *...weeping, she began to wet his feet with her tears and wiped them with the hair of her head and kissed his feet repeatedly and anointed them with the ointment.*
>
> Tears, hair, repeated kisses, anointing — every bodily resource given. The woman does not speak. Jesus' subsequent verdict names the cause: "she loved much because she was forgiven much." The bodily acts are evidence of inner love, which is itself evidence of received forgiveness. This verse establishes forgiveness as a trigger that produces love as its inner response — one of the most explicit causal chains in the New Testament.

> **Isaiah 49:15** — *Can a woman forget her nursing child, that she should have no compassion on the son of her womb? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you.*
>
> The womb and the nursing body ground the divine compassion claim. God argues from the most physically intimate human love relationship toward something that surpasses it. The body is doing theological work: it is not an illustration of divine love but the strongest possible evidence from human experience toward something beyond human capacity.

Love's fullest expression is self-giving.

> **John 15:13** — *Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends.*
>
> Love's maximum is measured by the completeness of the self-giving. The verse functions as a definition by limit: it names love's apex, against which all lesser expressions are calibrated. Love at its deepest is not an emotion but an act, and it can cost everything.

> **Galatians 2:20** — *...the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me.*
>
> The singular — "who loved me" — signals that divine love, though universal in scope, is also irreducibly particular. It is not diffused across an abstract humanity but concentrated on a specific person. Paul does not merely believe in a love that happened in the past — he lives from within it as an ongoing reality constituting his inner life.

### Annotated Verse Evidence — Section 2 Material

The opposite of love is hatred — a structural contrary, not merely an absence.

> **Leviticus 19:17** — *You shall not hate your brother in your heart, but you shall reason frankly with your neighbour.*
>
> Hatred is located in the heart — the innermost space, where it can exist without being visible in conduct. The remedy is not simply the removal of hatred but frank conversation — the relational act that externalises the inner state and opens it to resolution. Love and hatred are both matters of the heart before they become matters of conduct.

> **Proverbs 10:12** — *Hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offences.*
>
> The structural contrast: hatred is generative of conflict; love is absorptive of it. The verb "covers" carries the sense of protective concealment — not denial but the deliberate act of not using another's failure against them. Love and hatred are true contraries, not merely opposite ends of an affective spectrum.

Disorder is misdirected love, not absent love.

> **2 Timothy 3:2, 4** — *For people will be lovers of self, lovers of money... lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God.*
>
> Three philo- compounds mark the last-days' inner collapse: philarguros, philēdonos, and the absence of philotheos. The same root applied to God and brothers names virtue; applied to money, pleasure, and self names its corruption. Disorder is substitution — disordered people love intensely, but toward the wrong objects. The structure of the philo- vocabulary performs the diagnosis.

Love grows from received forgiveness.

> **Luke 7:47** — *Her sins, which are many, are forgiven — for she loved much. But he who is forgiven little, loves little.*
>
> One of the most explicit causal chains in the New Testament: forgiveness → love. The love expressed in Luk 7:38 (tears, kisses, anointing) is here named as the consequence of received forgiveness. Love is not a precondition for forgiveness but its fruit. The verse grounds love's formation in a prior act of grace toward the person — consistent with the programme's broader finding that love is received before it is exercised.

Love's steadfastness holds under pressure.

> **Lamentations 3:22** — *The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end.*
>
> The preceding verses describe God as the source of the speaker's affliction. The turn to chesed in v.22 is therefore not natural sentiment but a counter-claim against circumstances. The "never ceasing" quality of divine steadfast love is asserted precisely when circumstances suggest it has ceased. Chesed's steadfastness is not its absence of feeling but its refusal to let circumstances determine its conclusion. This verse defines the human aspiration for covenant faithfulness: not a stable emotion but a stable orientation.

> **Ruth 3:10** — *You have made this last kindness greater than the first, in that you have not gone after young men, whether poor or rich.*
>
> Ruth's chesed — loyalty shown at cost, overriding self-interest, directed toward a widow with nothing to offer — is the human analogue of divine steadfast love. The verse shows love as formed by faithfulness and proven by circumstances that would have justified its withdrawal.

Love produces the inversion of status competition.

> **Romans 12:10** — *Love one another with brotherly affection. Outdo one another in showing honour.*
>
> The imperative to outdo one another in honour inverts normal social dynamics. Where status competition produces hierarchy, love produces a race toward servanthood. The inner disposition of brotherly affection is the ground; the outward pattern is a community organised entirely against self-promotion.

> **John 13:35** — *By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another.*
>
> Love among believers is the publicly legible mark of Christian identity — not moral achievement or doctrinal soundness, but love. This elevates communal love from a virtue to an epistemic and missional signal: the means by which the world reads the inner reality of Christian discipleship.

Love's ultimate source is prior to the human person.

> **Hosea 6:6** — *For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings.*
>
> God desires the inner orientation, not the external act. The parallelism with "knowledge of God" establishes chesed and the knowledge of God as the two fundamental inner orientations required by covenant relationship — not feelings but directed states of the whole person. The outer act of sacrifice can occur without either.

> **Micah 6:8** — *He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God.*
>
> The verb love applied to chesed itself — not merely to practise it but to love it. The person of inner integrity loves loving-kindness as a quality. The triad (justice, chesed, humble walking with God) names the three fundamental inner orientations of the covenant life — the Old Testament's most concentrated summary of the rightly ordered inner person.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

Love is one of the most linguistically complex words in the programme. The research identified 142 terms across Greek and Hebrew, of which 138 are active (following review). Seventy-nine terms are owned by this registry; fifty-nine are shared with adjacent word studies. The term-sharing ratio of 0.701 means that seven out of ten terms in this registry also belong to another word study — reflecting love's structural position at the centre of the inner-being vocabulary. The vocabulary covers four Hebrew root families (AHAV, CHESED, RACHAM, DOD) and two primary Greek families (AGAP, FILOS), together with a large field of compound and derivative terms in Greek that name specific orientations of love — toward God, brothers, money, pleasure, or self.

---

**Hebrew vocabulary**

**Ahava / Aheb** (H0160 / H0157H, *ahaváh / ahev*) is the primary Hebrew word for love, occurring 38 times as a noun and 13 times in its verbal form. Its senses divide into three registers: human love for human objects (between persons, within families, between men and women, including erotic love); God's covenantal love for his people (the everlasting love of Jer 31:3; the rejoicing love of Zep 3:17); and love as moral loyalty and kindness between persons (Pro 10:12 — love covers offences; Jer 2:2 — the bride-devotion of youth). Its breadth means it cannot be reduced to any single English word. The verbal form (H0157H) carries a causative stem — love can be both experienced and caused in another — which is theologically significant for understanding the relationship between divine initiative and human response. The related term H0158 (ahav, lover) names the lover as object of marital delight or, in its corrupted form, the hired substitute who marks spiritual infidelity (Pro 5:19; Hos 8:9) — the rightful and adulterous lover as structural opposition within the same root family.

**Dod** (H1730G/H, *dod*) occurs across two senses: the beloved as the object of intense longing in the Song of Songs ("my beloved is mine and I am his"), and love as abstract noun in prophetic literature (Isa 5:1; Eze 16:8), where God imports the Song's language of intimate delight into his covenant relationship with Israel. The DOD root family is shared with the goodness and desire registries.

**Chesed** (H2617A, *chesed*, 261 occurrences) is the most theologically concentrated word in the Hebrew love vocabulary and the most frequent. Its senses operate at three levels: (1) goodness and kindness as character quality; (2) human covenant faithfulness that mirrors divine faithfulness — Ruth's loyalty to Naomi, the person who keeps faith when self-interest would recommend otherwise; (3) God's steadfast love as his defining inner attribute — the covenantal faithfulness that never ceases, the quality invoked in every psalm of lament, what God desires above sacrifice (Hos 6:6). Hosea 6:6 makes chesed an inner disposition, not merely a relational act: what God desires is the orientation, not the performance. Micah 6:8 places the love of chesed within the triad of justice, chesed, and humble walking with God — the Old Testament's most concentrated summary of the rightly ordered inner life.

**Racham / Rachamim** (H7355 / H7356B, *racham / rachamim*) carries a causative stem in the verb form — compassion can be both experienced and caused in another. The etymological connection to *rechem* (womb) is structural, not incidental: to say God has rachamim is to say he carries his people the way a mother carries a child — with bodily intimacy, instinctive protectiveness, and a love whose object is not chosen by merit but by prior attachment. The racham root spans love, compassion, and mercy, suggesting a three-way inner structure: love as the broadest orientation, compassion as love responding to suffering, mercy as love overriding just consequence. *Note:* The H7356B verse corpus contains approximately 23 contamination verses from H7358 (rechem, physical womb) — these are anatomical references unrelated to the compassion sense and carry a different sense (physical womb rather than compassion) and do not contribute to the inner-being analysis.

**H2898 (tuv, goodness):** Included following verse-level inspection of all 31 verses. Fourteen of its 31 verses name divine goodness as an inner-being attribute — the abundant goodness of God stored up for those who fear him (Psa 31:19), the goodness the psalmist expects to look upon in the land of the living (Psa 27:13), the goodness that satisfies the soul (Jer 31:14). These verses show divine goodness as the object of inner orientation and eschatological longing. The TOV root is shared with the goodness study, pointing to a structural relationship between love and goodness across the vocabulary.

**Sane** (H8130, *sane*, 156 occurrences) names hatred — love's structural contrary. With a causative stem parallel to ahev, hatred can be both experienced and caused. Two registers: hatred as a prohibited inner orientation toward the neighbour (Lev 19:17 — "do not hate your brother in your heart"); and hatred commanded as the consequence of love for God (Psa 97:10 — "you who love the Lord, hate evil"). Hatred is not uniformly condemned — its moral character depends entirely on its object, just as love's character depends on its object.

---

**Greek vocabulary**

**Agapaō / Agapē** (G0025 / G0026, *agapaō / agapē*) are the two most important Greek terms for love in the New Testament. Agapaō (344 occurrences) is the verb; agapē (133 occurrences) is the noun. The sense structure divides into five registers: love of God/Christ as the commanded whole-person orientation (Mat 22:37); God's or Christ's love toward the person as the prior divine act (Joh 3:16; Gal 2:20); love of neighbour and enemy as the inner-being orientation outward that fulfils the law (Mat 5:44); mutual love among believers as the communal orientation that marks discipleship (Joh 13:34; 1Jo 4:19); and disordered love directed toward the world, darkness, or money as the defining inner orientation of the fallen or departing person (Joh 3:19; 2Ti 4:10). The agapē noun concentrates what the verb scatters across objects: it names love as a quality that can be possessed, measured, poured into hearts (Rom 5:5), declared the greatest (1Cor 13), and cultivated through prayer (1Th 3:12). *Agapētos* (G0027, 79 occurrences) is the corresponding adjective — beloved. Applied to Christ at baptism ("my beloved Son" — Mat 3:17) as the Father-Son love that is the origin-point of all love in the New Testament; applied to believers as their primary identity before any description of conduct (Rom 1:7 — "loved by God and called to be saints").

**Phileō and the philo- family** (G5368, *phileō*, 52 occurrences) names affectionate love, the warmth of natural attachment and personal regard. The agapaō/phileō distinction in John 21 is analytically real, not merely stylistic: the programme's grouping assigns the Joh 21 dialogue its own context group (572-003) because phileō names the natural warmth Peter can affirm when agapaō feels too great a claim. The distinction lies not in quality but in register — phileō is the love of natural affection and friendship; agapaō is the love of total willed orientation. Both appear in the Johannine vocabulary and are not interchangeable in their key appearances. The philo- root generates the New Testament's most systematic taxonomy of loves: *philadelphia* (brotherly love), *philanthropia* (benevolence toward humanity), *philarguria* (love of money), *philēdonos* (pleasure-loving), *philotheos* (God-loving), *philoprōteōn* (to love to be first), *philoneikos* (dispute-loving). Every compound names love as inner orientation toward a specific object — and the taxonomy includes both the virtues (philotheos, philadelphia, philagathos) and their corruptions (philarguros, philēdonos, philoneikos). The philo- compound family is the New Testament's most explicit conceptual map of how love's single capacity for attachment can be directed toward any object, with moral character determined entirely by the direction chosen.

**Philos** (G5384, *philos*, 101 occurrences) names the friend. In John 15:15, Jesus redefines the category: what constitutes friendship with Christ is shared knowledge of the Father's purposes — "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you." Love here takes the form of disclosure: the inner life made known to another. Abraham is called God's friend (Jam 2:23; Isa 41:8) — the term thus carries both its ordinary social meaning and an extraordinary theological extension in which love and knowledge are structurally linked.

---

**Synthesis observation**

The complete vocabulary reveals a word that spans the entire range from divine nature to human disorder. At one end stands agapē as the quality that constitutes God's inner being (1 John 4:8, 16) and is poured into human hearts by the Spirit. At the other end stands the disordered love vocabulary — love of money, pleasure, self, dispute, primacy — representing the same capacity for attachment redirected toward what cannot bear its weight. The full vocabulary discloses a single human inner capacity — the power of attachment and self-giving — that can be rightly or wrongly ordered, received from its divine source or turned back on the self, expressed in the body or concealed in the heart, directed toward God and others or toward what is not God. What a person loves, in what direction and with what consequences, is what they are.

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Love has the highest term-sharing ratio in the programme at 0.701. It co-occurs with 114 partner registries across its verse corpus, shares confirmed dimensions with 22 registries, and holds shared anchor verses with 20 registries. It is not a self-contained characteristic — it is the relational hub through which most of the inner-being vocabulary passes.

---

**Compassion **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary, co-occurrence (196 verses), shared dimensions, shared root family, shared anchor verses — the most densely connected partner in the research.*
The RACHAM root spans both registries and the vocabulary is substantially shared. The 196-verse co-occurrence is almost double the next-highest partner. The question for Session D is structural: is compassion a form of love — specifically love responding to suffering — or is it a distinct inner state that love generates but does not contain? The womb etymology grounds both in the same bodily-relational origin, suggesting a family relationship rather than identity. The spirit-soul-body classification may differ between them: love is Spirit-Soul Interface; compassion may be Soul-Body Interface given its stronger somatic grounding.
*Priority: High*

**Kindness **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary (33 terms), co-occurrence (34 verses), six shared dimensions, shared anchor verses.*
The chesed vocabulary sits at the boundary of both registries — 33 shared terms is the largest xref count in the programme for this registry. The question for Session D: is chesed a quality of love (covenant faithfulness as love's expression) or a characteristic distinct from love (faithfulness as its own inner virtue that love produces but does not contain)?
*Priority: High*

**Mercy **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary, three shared dimensions, shared anchor verses.*
The racham vocabulary is shared across love, compassion, and mercy. Mercy may be the specific form love takes toward those who have failed — love overriding just consequence. Session D question: do love, compassion, and mercy form a semantic family with an inner structure (love → compassion → mercy as increasing specificity of response), or are they genuinely distinct inner states?
*Priority: High*

**Covenant **
*Nature: Formal — co-occurrence (33 verses), six shared dimensions.*
Six shared dimensions for a registry pair that does not share terms is structurally significant. Chesed is the inner quality that animates covenant, and the covenant is the relational framework within which chesed operates. Session D question: does love generate covenant, or does covenant generate love — or is the relationship bidirectional and co-constitutive?
*Priority: High*

**Goodness **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary, shared root family, shared anchor verses. Cross-cluster.*
The TOV root family connects love and goodness across cluster boundaries. Psa 25:7 places tuv (divine goodness) and chesed in the same verse — "according to your steadfast love remember me, for the sake of your goodness." Session D question: is divine goodness the content of divine love — what love gives — or are they parallel divine attributes?
*Priority: High*

**Desire **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary (20 terms), co-occurrence (49 verses), shared root family. Cross-cluster.*
The AGAV root connects the registers. The boundary between love and desire is one of the most important unresolved questions in the vocabulary: love includes desire but is not reducible to it — love can remain when desire has changed. Session D question: where does desire end and love begin, and is ordered desire a form of love or a structurally distinct faculty?
*Priority: High*

**Peace **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary, co-occurrence (68 verses), seven shared dimensions, shared anchor verses.*
Four signal types including high co-occurrence makes this a stronger connection than the previous v1 assessment. Zep 3:17 ("he will quiet you by his love") suggests love produces rest and inner stillness. Session D question: is peace the inner state that love produces in the person who receives it — love's gift to the soul?
*Priority: High*

**Grace **
*Nature: Formal — two shared dimensions.*
Dimension signal only — no shared vocabulary. Grace is unmerited love — love that does not respond to merit but creates it. Whether this makes grace a species of love or a structurally prior act is a Session D question. The theological entanglement is deep; the lexical separation is equally clear.
*Priority: Medium*

**Faith **
*Nature: Formal — shared vocabulary, co-occurrence (62 verses), shared anchor verses.*
Three signal types support a stronger connection than the previous v1 Lower priority. The armour metaphor (1Th 5:8 — breastplate of faith and love, helmet of hope) maps faith and love to adjacent bodily centres. Session D question: does the faith-love-hope triad represent the programme's three primary inner orientations toward God — trust, attachment, expectation?
*Priority: Medium*

**Forgiveness **
