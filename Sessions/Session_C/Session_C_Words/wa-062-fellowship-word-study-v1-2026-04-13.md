## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Fellowship names something more than proximity. Two people can share the same table or serve in the same organisation without having fellowship at all. What the biblical vocabulary identifies is a different quality: the experience of genuinely holding something in common at a level that matters — a shared life, a shared hope, a shared belonging to something larger than either person alone.

At its heart, fellowship describes a person who is genuinely joined to others — not loosely affiliated but joined in the way a partner in a shared undertaking is joined, where what happens to one matters to the other. The Greek word at the centre carries the sense of having in common: fellowship is not about feeling warm toward others but about sharing in something real — a common participation in something that precedes and grounds the relationship.

Scripture widens this considerably. The same vocabulary that names human community describes participation in the divine nature itself — in the suffering of Christ, in the Spirit, in the life of the Father and the Son. Fellowship names the inner orientation that makes a person a genuine participant rather than an onlooker.

The Hebrew roots reveal the moral dimension. Companionship in Scripture is never neutral: to be in fellowship with someone is to align oneself with what they stand for. Companions of the wicked take on their moral character; companions of those who fear God are defined by that same fear.

There is also a darker register. The same vocabulary covers wrongful joining — alliance with idols, complicity with evil, forbidden spiritual practices. The capacity for fellowship can be directed well or badly. It is not self-generated — it is received, responded to, and formed by the quality of what one participates in.

The natural enemy of fellowship is isolation — not the circumstance of being alone, but the inner posture that withholds genuine participation. Alongside isolation stands complicity: the joining capacity attached to what corrupts rather than restores. These are fellowship's twin deformations.

What feeds it is prior participation in something real. The deepest human fellowship in Scripture is grounded in something that precedes it: a common life in God, a shared experience of grace, a mutual belonging to the same covenant. Fellowship is fed by depth of participation, not by social effort.

What diminishes it is misalignment — joining oneself to what is false or self-seeking. The prophetic literature is clear that companions who pursue their own interests destroy genuine community. When the covenantal bond is broken, the companionship it grounded collapses with it.

What fellowship produces in the person who has it is solidarity: the willingness to bear another's suffering, contribute resources, hold another's cause as one's own. The wound vocabulary from the same Hebrew root reveals that genuine participation can include physical suffering — that wounds received on behalf of others can carry redemptive consequence.

In relationships, fellowship produces belonging — the experience of being genuinely held, knowing that one's life is intertwined with others who will act when one falls. The practical test in both Testaments is what a person does when their companion is in need.

Fellowship at its deepest is not self-generated. The most significant uses place God as the one who draws persons into participation — in the divine nature, in the fellowship of the Son, through the Spirit. Fellowship with God precedes and grounds fellowship with others.

Fellowship names something more than proximity. Two people can occupy the same room, share the same table, or serve in the same organisation without having fellowship at all. What the biblical vocabulary identifies is a different quality altogether — the experience of actually holding something in common at a level that matters: a shared life, a shared hope, a shared belonging to something larger than either person alone.

> **Ecclesiastes 4:10** — *For if they fall, one will lift up his fellow. But woe to him who is alone when he falls and has not another to lift him up!*
>
> This verse tests whether fellowship is real by asking what happens in crisis. The companion who lifts the fallen is not merely a warm presence — the relationship has proven itself through action. The warning about being alone in one's falling names what is lost when fellowship is absent: not comfort, but practical rescue. The verse grounds the abstract quality of shared life in a concrete moment of vulnerability, and makes the absence of fellowship a form of danger.

At its heart, this characteristic describes a person who is genuinely joined to others. Not loosely affiliated, not merely adjacent — but joined in the way that a partner in a shared undertaking is joined, where what happens to one matters to the other, where what is owned or suffered or celebrated is held together.

> **Malachi 2:14** — *Because the Lord was witness between you and the wife of your youth, to whom you have been faithless, though she is your companion and your wife by covenant.*
>
> The word translated "companion" here is the same Hebrew root that underlies the whole fellowship vocabulary of the Old Testament. Its use in the marriage context is significant: it names a bond that is simultaneously covenantal, relational, and moral. The companionship between husband and wife is not merely emotional closeness — it is a joined life witnessed by God, governed by covenant, and capable of being violated. Faithlessness does not merely hurt a companion; it breaks the bond itself. This suggests that fellowship at depth is constituted by covenant rather than by affection alone.

The Greek word at the centre of the concept carries the sense of having in common, and this is precise: fellowship is not about feeling warm toward others, but about actually sharing in something real — a common life, a common orientation, a common participation in something that precedes and grounds the relationship.

> **1 John 1:3** — *that which we have seen and heard we proclaim also to you, so that you too may have fellowship with us; and indeed our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ.*
>
> This verse is the clearest statement in Scripture of the structure of fellowship. It moves from proclamation to reception to participation: hearing the apostolic witness draws a person into the same fellowship the apostles already inhabit — and that fellowship turns out to be nothing less than participation in the life of the Father and the Son. The horizontal community among believers is not the primary thing; it is the extension of something vertical. Fellowship with other believers is real because it is grounded in a shared participation in God himself. The verse makes explicit what the rest of the data implies: that the horizontal and vertical axes of fellowship are not independent.

Scripture widens this considerably. The same vocabulary that names human community is used to describe a person's participation in the divine nature itself — in the suffering of Christ, in the Spirit, in the life of the Father and the Son.

> **2 Peter 1:4** — *by which he has granted to us his precious and very great promises, so that through them you may become partakers of the divine nature, having escaped from the corruption that is in the world because of sinful desire.*
>
> The word translated "partakers" here is the Greek companion word to fellowship — naming the person who actually shares in something rather than the shared quality itself. That what is shared is the divine nature makes this verse remarkable: participation in God is not metaphorical here but substantive, granted through promise. The contrast with corruption is equally significant — becoming a partaker in the divine nature is described as an escape from the corruption produced by sinful desire. Fellowship with the divine is framed as a movement away from one quality of inner life and into another.

> **Philippians 3:10** — *that I may know him and the power of his resurrection, and may share his sufferings, becoming like him in his death.*
>
> Fellowship vocabulary appears here in the context of suffering — to share in Christ's sufferings is to participate in them, not merely to observe them from a distance. This is a striking extension of the concept: genuine participation in the life of another includes entry into their suffering. The verse suggests that fellowship at its deepest is not bounded by what is pleasant — it extends to death. Paul's use of this vocabulary in this context connects fellowship directly to transformation, since sharing in the sufferings becomes the path to conformity with the death and resurrection of Christ.

The Hebrew roots add depth by showing the moral dimension of joining. Companionship in Scripture is never neutral — to be in fellowship with someone is to align oneself with what they stand for.

> **Psalm 119:63** — *I am a companion of all who fear you, of those who keep your precepts.*
>
> The Psalmist does not say he is fond of those who fear God, or that he attends the same gatherings. He says he is their companion — using the same root vocabulary for fellowship and joining. The statement is a declaration of alignment: to identify as a companion of God-fearers is to locate oneself morally and spiritually. The pairing with "those who keep your precepts" makes this explicit: the companionship is grounded in a shared practice and a shared orientation toward God. Fellowship here is an act of self-identification with a particular community defined by its relationship to God.

> **Isaiah 1:23** — *Your princes are rebels and companions of thieves. Everyone loves a bribe and runs after gifts. They do not bring justice to the fatherless, and the widow's cause does not come to them.*
>
> The indictment names the leaders' fellowship with thieves before naming their corrupt behaviour — as if the companionship explains the conduct. This is the moral logic of joining: those who are companions of the corrupt become corrupt themselves. The failure to bring justice to the fatherless and the widow is not presented as an independent failing but as the consequence of wrong fellowship. The verse embeds the fellowship vocabulary in a prophetic context that makes the stakes of misaligned companionship concretely visible.

> **Job 34:8** — *who travels in company with evildoers and walks with wicked men?*
>
> Elihu's challenge names fellowship with evildoers in the language of travel — going in company with them, walking where they walk. The spatial imagery is significant: shared direction, shared path, shared destination. Fellowship is not only a matter of inner orientation but of where that orientation carries a person over time. The verse functions as a moral question precisely because the idea of genuine participation in evil is treated as self-evidently damaging to the person who does it.

There is also a darker register. The same vocabulary covers wrongful joining — alliance with idols, complicity with evil, involvement in forbidden spiritual practices.

> **Hosea 4:17** — *Ephraim is joined to idols; leave him alone.*
>
> The word translated "joined" here is the same Hebrew root that elsewhere names legitimate companionship and covenantal union. Its application to Israel's relationship with idols is deliberate and disturbing: what should name covenant loyalty names instead idolatrous attachment. God's instruction — "leave him alone" — follows as a consequence: a person who has joined themselves to what is false has placed themselves beyond reach of correction for the time being. The verse reveals that the capacity for genuine joining can be turned entirely toward what destroys, and that this turning has consequences the individual cannot see from within it.

> **Hosea 6:9** — *As robbers lie in wait for a man, so the priests band together; they murder on the way to Shechem; they commit villainy.*
>
> Here the fellowship vocabulary appears in the description of corporate violence. The priests who "band together" are using the same joining capacity that might otherwise describe covenant community — and directing it toward murder. This is not fellowship corrupted at the edges; it is the inner capacity for joining turned toward collective evil. The institutional context — priests — makes the reversal more pointed: those whose role is to sustain genuine community with God are using the language and reality of community for its opposite.

> **Psalm 58:5** — *so that it does not hear the voice of charmers or of the cunning enchanter.*
>
> The charming and enchanting vocabulary in the Old Testament draws from the same root family as fellowship and joining. This is significant: the forbidden spiritual practices of binding and charming are named using joining language, suggesting that the inner capacity for attachment and participation can be directed toward occult manipulation as readily as toward genuine community. The verse warns against the enchanter's influence — naming the danger that a misdirected joining power poses.

> **Isaiah 47:9** — *These two things shall come to you in a moment, in one day; the loss of children and widowhood shall come upon you in full measure, in spite of your many sorceries and the great power of your enchantments.*
>
> Babylon's reliance on its sorceries — named with the same charming vocabulary — is presented as misplaced confidence: the joining of spiritual force to human ambition produces not security but devastation. The verse is an announcement of judgment, but its interest here is that it shows the inner disposition of trust and reliance attached to forbidden practices. This mirrors the fellowship vocabulary's positive uses, where trust and participation in God produce life; here the same orientation directed toward occult power produces the opposite.

---

What fellowship produces in the person who has it is a particular quality of solidarity: the willingness to bear another's suffering, to contribute material resources, to hold another's cause as one's own.

> **Daniel 2:18** — *and told them to seek mercy from the God of heaven concerning this mystery, so that Daniel and his companions might not be destroyed with the rest of the wise men of Babylon.*
>
> Daniel's companions are named as such in the context of shared mortal crisis. Their fellowship is not a warm social bond — it is a shared stake in survival, which they address by shared prayer. The companions seek God together, not because that is what companions do in general, but because their joined condition means that what threatens one threatens all. The verse grounds fellowship in shared vulnerability and corporate dependence on God, and shows how the inner quality of genuine joining expresses itself in communal action before God.

> **Matthew 23:30** — *saying, 'If we had lived in the days of our fathers, we would not have taken part with them in shedding the blood of the prophets.'*
>
> The Pharisees use the participation vocabulary to disclaim complicity in their ancestors' sin — ironically, in the very act of building the prophets' tombs, which Jesus reads as confirmation rather than repudiation of that complicity. The verse is instructive because it reveals that participation is not simply a matter of declared alignment: to participate in a tradition, to benefit from its fruits, to continue its patterns — even while claiming innocence — is in some sense to take part. Fellowship carries corporate and generational dimensions, not merely individual ones.

---

The characteristic of fellowship at the level of wound and purification carries a bodily dimension that deserves separate attention.

> **Proverbs 20:30** — *Blows that wound cleanse away evil; strokes make clean the innermost parts.*
>
> The wound vocabulary here — the same Hebrew root that underlies the fellowship family's somatic edge — is used in the context of moral purification. The blow that wounds is described as cleansing the innermost parts: the body's experience of pain is directly connected to the inner transformation of character. This verse locates fellowship-adjacent vocabulary at the intersection of physical sensation and moral interiority in a way that is unusual in the wisdom literature. The wound is not merely punishment; it is medicine. Whether the wounding comes from another person or from God is not specified, which opens the verse toward the next.

> **Isaiah 53:5** — *But he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed.*
>
> The wound vocabulary that appears in Proverbs as moral purification reaches its fullest expression here. The wounds of the Servant are not self-inflicted — they are received on behalf of others, and their consequence is healing for those others. This is the deepest movement in the whole fellowship semantic field: the one who bears wounds in place of the companions becomes the ground of their restoration. The verse does not use the explicit fellowship vocabulary, but the wound term belongs to the same root family, and the dynamic it describes — one person's suffering received in place of another's — is the inner logic of the deepest biblical fellowship.

---

> **Jeremiah 13:23** — *Can the Ethiopian change his skin or the leopard his spots? Then also you can do good who are accustomed to do evil.*
>
> The "spots" vocabulary here shares its root with the wound and fellowship family: it names the fixed, indelible marking of character. Jeremiah applies it to inner moral character that has become so habitual as to be, humanly speaking, fixed. The verse is a question about whether fellowship with evil can be undone by human effort alone — and the implied answer is no. Change at this depth requires something more than self-correction. The verse thus names the limitation of the human capacity for self-transformation, and implies that the restoration described elsewhere in the fellowship vocabulary (Ezekiel 37; Isaiah 53) requires an external agent.

> **Ezekiel 37:19** — *Behold, I am about to take the stick of Joseph (that is in the hand of Ephraim) and the tribes of Israel associated with him. And I will join with it the stick of Judah, and make them one stick, that they may be one in my hand.*
>
> The joining vocabulary here is explicitly divine action: God is the one who joins the two sticks, not human political effort. The restoration of the divided nation is described as an act of joining — making the two one — which is the physical and structural image of what the fellowship vocabulary means at the inner level. That God himself is the actor is significant: the communal oneness that the fellowship vocabulary names at the human level is, at the point of genuine restoration, produced by divine initiative. This mirrors the New Testament pattern where fellowship with God grounds and generates fellowship among people.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

The vocabulary of fellowship in Scripture draws from two distinct language families that meet at the concept of joining. The Greek terms form a compact family built from a single root; the Hebrew terms form an extensive family that radiates outward from a single verb of uniting, producing a wide range of nouns, adjectives, and specialised forms covering companionship, wounding, charming, structural joining, and marked character. Fourteen active terms were identified across the two languages, all owned by this registry. No terms are shared with other registries, giving fellowship a term-sharing ratio of zero — an unusually self-contained vocabulary profile for a word of its theological weight, which suggests that what makes fellowship distinctive in Scripture is not its vocabulary overlap with adjacent characteristics but rather the semantic depth within its own word family.

---

**Greek terms**

**Koinōnia** (G2842, *κοινωνία*) appears 21 times in the New Testament, where it is the primary term for what the programme is studying. Mounce defines it as fellowship, the close association between persons emphasising what is common between them, with extension into participation, sharing, contribution, and gift — as outcomes of that close relationship. The range of English translations it receives reveals its breadth: fellowship, partnership, participation, communion, contribution, sharing. Each translation captures a different moment in what is essentially a single dynamic — the experience of genuinely holding something in common with others.

The classical Greek background from LSJ is instructive. From the fifth century BC, the word names partnership, communion, and association. It appears in the phrase *anthropinē koinōnia* — human society — in Aristotle. A particularly revealing classical use names the bond of marriage as *koinōnia biou*, sharing of life: the totality of a joined existence rather than merely an emotional connection. That the New Testament employs this same word for the believer's relationship with the Father and the Son suggests that the writers are reaching for the deepest available Greek concept of genuine participation when they describe what God offers in Christ. The single phase 2 flag for this term — noting breadth across four or more distinct semantic domains — confirms that this word is doing exceptional conceptual work in the New Testament.

**Koinōnos** (G2844, *κοινωνός*) appears 15 times in the New Testament. Mounce defines it as partner or participant — the person who joins in with another in some enterprise or activity. It is the person-form of koinōnia: where koinōnia names the quality of shared participation, koinōnos names the one who participates. Both terms belong to the root KOINŌN (participant), and they appear together in the closely associated words list, which also includes the verb *koinōneō* (to participate), the adjective *koinos* (common, shared), and *synkoinōnos* (fellow-sharer). The word family as a whole describes every aspect of the shared-participation concept: the quality, the person, the act, the basis, the outcome.

The LSJ entry for koinōnos reveals a detail of significance: among its meanings is "familiar spirit" — its presence in the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures. This is not incidental: the word for a genuine participant and partner is the same word used for the spirit that illegitimately claims partnership with a person in occult practice. The tension between rightful and wrongful participation is embedded in the Greek vocabulary itself, mirroring what the Hebrew vocabulary shows with greater explicitness.

---

**Hebrew terms**

The Hebrew vocabulary of fellowship centres on the verb **chavar** (H2266, *חָבַר*), which means to unite, join, bind together, be joined, be coupled, be in league, have fellowship with. It appears 29 times across the Old Testament and is the source root from which the entire Hebrew fellowship family grows. Its verbal range is immediately striking: in the Qal stem it means to unite or be joined; in the Piel stem it means to join together; in the Pual it means to be joined or to be charmed. The appearance of charming alongside joining within the same verb's stem variations is not accidental — both draw on the same underlying notion of binding or bringing into connection. The verb covers both the legitimate and forbidden dimensions of joining from within a single lexical form.

**Chaver** (H2270, *חָבֵר*, companion) is the relational noun form, appearing 21 times and naming the person who is united or joined — the companion, associate, fellow worshipper. This is the word the Psalmist uses to identify himself as a companion of all who fear God (Psalm 119:63), and the word Isaiah uses for the companions of thieves among the princes (Isaiah 1:23). It functions as the Hebrew counterpart to the Greek koinōnos — naming the person defined by their joining.

**Chever** (H2267, *חֶבֶר*) appears 7 times and covers two senses that sit in uncomfortable proximity: association or company on one hand, and spell, charm, or magical band on the other. The word's range maps the same dual territory the verb navigates — the capacity for joining can produce community or sorcery, depending on its direction. This term appears in the enchanter texts (Psalm 58:5; Isaiah 47:9), and the fact that it also names legitimate association means the borderline between gathering and binding, between community and control, is linguistically thin.

**Chabborah** (H2250, *חַבּוּרָה*, wound) appears 7 times and is the term that most visibly extends the fellowship vocabulary into the body. Its root is the same joining verb, but here what is joined is flesh — a blow that makes contact, that wounds, that leaves a mark. The word appears in the moral purification text of Proverbs 20:30 and in Isaiah 53:5, where the wounds of the Servant become the ground of healing. That wounding belongs to the same root family as joining is semantically significant: the wound is a form of contact, a point at which two things meet, with consequences that can be punitive or redemptive depending on whose wounds are being described and for what purpose.

The remaining Hebrew terms radiate from the central verb into more specialised territory. **Chabberet** (H2278, consort) and **choveret** (H2279, set or junction) name feminine forms of the companion and the act of joining respectively. **Machberet** (H4225, joining or seam) and **mechabborah** (H4226, clamp or binder) are structural terms naming the physical joining-points of the tabernacle — seams where curtains were coupled, clamps that held sections together. These structural terms were classified as physical in the verse context analysis and set aside from the inner-being corpus, but their presence in the root family creates an important note: the vocabulary of inner joining in Scripture is embedded in a language that also uses the same root for the physical architecture of sacred space.

**Chavarurah** (H2272, spot or stripe) appears once, in Jeremiah 13:23, where the leopard's fixed markings are used as an image of indelibly formed inner moral character. The same root that names joining here names an indelible mark — the fixed character that prolonged fellowship with evil has produced. This is the fellowship vocabulary at its most sobering: the trace that wrongful joining leaves on the inner person.

**Chevrah** (H2274, company) and **chabbor** (H2271, associate or partner in trade) round out the family with further person-and-group nouns, each naming the social product of joining: a company, an association, a business partner.

---

**Synthesis observation**

The complete vocabulary of fellowship traces a single arc from inner orientation to outer expression, from legitimate joining to wrongful joining, and from surface association to the deepest possible participation. At one end of the arc: the structural clamps and seams of the tabernacle — physical joining that holds sacred architecture together. At the other: participation in the divine nature itself, the believer joined to the Father and the Son through the Spirit. Between these poles: companionship as moral alignment, association as corporate identity, wounds as points of mortal contact that can carry purification or redemption, charms as forbidden binding, spots as the fixed trace of habitual joining with evil.

What the whole vocabulary reveals is that the capacity for genuine joining is among the most consequential inner-being capacities a person possesses. It can be directed toward God, toward righteous companions, toward the shared life of the believing community — and in each case it produces participation in something larger than the self. Or it can be directed toward idols, toward wickedness, toward forbidden powers — and in each case it binds the person to what diminishes and ultimately destroys them. The Hebrew vocabulary does not sentimentalise this. The same root that names the deepest possible fellowship also names the wound, the charm, and the indelible moral stain. Fellowship is not safe. It is a characteristically human capacity for joining that shapes — and is shaped by — what a person chooses to participate in.

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Fellowship has a term-sharing ratio of zero: none of its vocabulary is shared with other registries. This is unusual for a word with such theological weight — it means that fellowship's inner-being contribution is primarily carried by its own word family rather than through shared lexical overlap with adjacent characteristics. However, the correlation data tells a different story about connectivity: there are 21 co-occurrence pairs, 29 dimension overlap pairs, and 13 shared anchor verse pairs. Fellowship is lexically self-contained but relationally dense. The connections it carries are primarily of three kinds: what it travels with in Scripture (co-occurrence), what it shares in terms of inner-being territory (dimension overlap), and what specific verses it inhabits alongside other characteristics (shared anchors).

---

**1. Covenant** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (Moral Character, Relational Disposition, Transformation, Emotion — Positive) with 4 shared dimensions.*

Covenant and fellowship share four dimension categories, the highest overlap among the C17 cluster words. Malachi 2:14, an anchor verse for fellowship, names the marital companion as "your wife by covenant" — the two concepts appear in the same clause. The SD pointer from the Dimension Review (DIM-62-SD001) explicitly raises the question of whether fellowship functions as covenant's participatory inner reality: covenant names the bond, fellowship names what it feels like from the inside to inhabit that bond. Whether these two characteristics are structurally related in this way — one naming the container, the other the experience — is a question for Session D.
*Priority: High*

**2. Love** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap across all six dimensions; 3 co-occurrence shared verses.*

The dimension overlap between fellowship and love is the most extensive in the entire correlation set: all six of fellowship's dimension categories are shared with love. This is a remarkable signal for two characteristics in the same cluster. Whether love is the motivating ground of fellowship, or whether fellowship is the relational form that love produces, or whether the two are describing the same inner-being territory from different angles — these questions cannot be answered from within this registry's data alone.
*Priority: High*

**3. Kindness** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (4 shared dimensions); 4 co-occurrence shared verses; shared anchor verse (Dan 2:18).*

Daniel 2:18 is shared between fellowship and kindness as anchor verses: Daniel and his companions seek mercy from God in their shared crisis. The compound signal — co-occurrence, dimension overlap, and shared anchor — makes this one of the stronger multi-signal connections in the data. The question it raises: does genuine companionship in Scripture regularly invoke kindness as its quality, or is the co-occurrence here incidental to the specific narrative context?
*Priority: High*

**4. Mercy** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (3 shared dimensions); 3 co-occurrence shared verses; shared anchor verse (Dan 2:18).*

The same anchor verse (Dan 2:18) is shared between fellowship and mercy, alongside the kindness connection above. That a single fellowship anchor verse is simultaneously an anchor for both kindness and mercy suggests something about the relational texture of genuine companionship in the Old Testament: it is frequently the context in which kindness and mercy are invoked together. Whether this is a structural pattern or contextual convergence requires investigation.
*Priority: Medium*

**5. Grace** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (2 shared dimensions: Moral Character, Relational Disposition); 3 co-occurrence shared verses.*

Grace and fellowship share the same cluster and overlap on the two dimensions that carry the most weight in fellowship's own profile — Relational Disposition and Moral Character. The grace word study (wa-068) is complete and available for comparison. The question is whether grace functions as the divine ground on which fellowship rests, or whether fellowship is what grace produces in the person who receives it — or both.
*Priority: Medium*

**6. Peace** (C17, same cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (5 shared dimensions).*

Peace shares five of fellowship's six dimension categories, placing it among the highest-overlap connections. The two characteristics appear to inhabit much of the same inner-being terrain. Whether peace is the condition that makes fellowship possible, the atmosphere fellowship produces, or a parallel relational characteristic is a question the data raises but does not answer.
*Priority: Medium*

**7. Reconciliation** (C13, cross-cluster)
*Nature: Formal — dimension overlap (3 shared dimensions: Divine-Human Correspondence, Relational Disposition, Transformation).*

Reconciliation shares the three dimensions that are most distinctively significant in fellowship's profile — the axis from divine-human correspondence through relational disposition to transformation. This suggests that what fellowship is as an ongoing state may be what reconciliation is as an event: one names the restored relationship, the other names what it means to live inside it.
*Priority: Medium*

**8. Sorrow / Contrition** (C05, C13; cross-cluster)
*Nature: Formal — shared anchor verse (Isa 53:5).*

Isaiah 53:5 is the anchor verse for fellowship's wound group (7568-002) and simultaneously an anchor verse for both sorrow and contrition in their respective registries. This triple convergence on a single verse is significant: the wound that heals, described with fellowship vocabulary, is also the ground of sorrow and contrition. Whether the relationship between wound and grief, between suffering-received and inner brokenness, reflects a structural inner-being connection between fellowship and the sorrow characteristics is a question for Session D.
*Priority: Medium*

**9. Likeness / Deadness** (C19, C21; cross-cluster)
*Nature: Formal — shared anchor verse (Phi 3:10).*

Philippians 3:10 — the sharing in Christ's sufferings, becoming like him in his death — is shared between fellowship and both likeness and deadness as anchor verses. This verse holds three inner-being characteristics in a single verse: participation in suffering (fellowship), conformity to death (likeness), and what dying to self means at the inner level (deadness). The convergence is not accidental; Paul's language here is deliberately dense. What Session D needs to investigate: does the sharing in Christ's sufferings constitute a hinge point where fellowship, transformation, and self-negation meet?
*Priority: High*

**10. Unity** (C18, cross-cluster)
*Nature: Formal — 4 co-occurrence shared verses.*

Unity is among the top co-occurrence partners for fellowship, sharing 4 verses. The Ezekiel 37:19 anchor verse, where God joins the two sticks into one, is the most visible convergence: the act of joining (fellowship vocabulary) produces oneness (unity vocabulary). Whether fellowship and unity name the same inner movement from different vantages — one the act of joining, the other the state of being joined — is a structural question for Session D.
*Priority: Medium*

**11. Desire** (C04, cross-cluster)
*Nature: Formal — 7 co-occurrence shared verses (highest co-occurrence count in the data).*
