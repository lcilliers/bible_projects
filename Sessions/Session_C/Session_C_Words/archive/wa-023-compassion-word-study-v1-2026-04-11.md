## Section 1 — The Characteristic

Compassion is the inner movement toward another person's pain. It is not a decision or an act of will, not primarily. It is a stirring — a being moved, from within, by what is suffered by another. The biblical vocabulary for compassion makes this visceral quality explicit: the Hebrew root at the heart of this word family is the same root as the word for womb (*racham*/*rechem*). This is not incidental. It names the quality of compassion as that of a mother for the child she has carried — tender, protective, instinctive, and arising from the body's most interior place.

Compassion always faces outward. It is not a state of self-absorption but an orientation toward the one who suffers. And it produces action. When the biblical texts show compassion — in God or in a person — something follows: mercy is given, healing occurs, judgement is withheld. The inner movement and its outward consequence are not separable.

The programme's own description of this registry captures it precisely: compassion is *feeling the pain of another from the inside — not observing suffering from a distance but being moved by it in the gut*. The womb-metaphor embedded in the Hebrew root marks it as something more primal than sentiment and more personal than policy. It is the response of one who cannot remain unmoved.

**Range of usage across the data**

The 58 context groups across this registry map a wide semantic territory, spanning two languages and two testaments. Reading through the group descriptions, five recognisable areas emerge.

*Visceral compassion* — the deep inward stirring toward one who suffers — is the core. This is expressed by the Greek *splanchnizō* (being moved in the bowels, gut-level compassion) and the Hebrew *ra.cham* family (compassion rooted in the womb). These groups describe both divine and human compassion as a physically-located inner movement.

*Covenantal mercy* — the steadfast, loyal love expressed in the *chesed* family — describes compassion as a settled character quality within covenantal relationship. This is compassion expressed not as a moment of feeling but as a disposition of the whole person. The *chesed* groups show compassion persisting through time, underlying all relationship with God and, by the prophets' demand, in relationship between persons.

*Comfort and consolation* — expressed through the *ni.chum* and *na.cham* family (XREF terms from the Comfort registry) — name compassion as it comes to completion in restoration. The inner stirring reaches its goal in the comforting of grief.

*Fellow-suffering* — the Greek *sym-* compounds (*sumpatheō*, *sumpaschō*, *sumpathēs*) — name a specifically New Testament development: compassion as participation in another's experience. This is not sympathy from a safe distance but genuine entry into shared suffering, exemplified most sharply in the person of Jesus Christ (Heb 4:15).

*Gracious favour* — the *chan.nun*/*chen* cluster — describes the disposition of favour and grace as a form of compassion's expression, particularly God's. This overlaps substantially with the Grace registry (reg 68) and the Mercy registry (reg 111).

The groups that describe the *withholding* of compassion are equally significant. Several groups name the judicial withdrawal of pity as an act of righteous judgement (H2347 groups 3182-002; H5162 groups 443-001/446-002; group 487-002). Compassion in Scripture is not a soft overriding of justice; its suspension in judgement contexts establishes that it is a genuine disposition and not a reflex.

**Dimensional character**

Across 22 dimension-index rows, the dominant dimension is *Relational Disposition* (16 of 22 groups). This places compassion primarily in the category of an inner-being orientation directed outward — toward God, toward others, toward the sufferer. Three groups carry *Dependence / Creatureliness* (the human posture of appeal and petition before God), two carry *Divine-Human Correspondence* (the structural mirror between divine compassion and human response), one carries *Moral Character* (the settled human virtue of covenantal loyalty), and one carries *Emotion — Negative* (the pitiable condition). No dimension has been assigned at registry level; the group-level picture is sufficient for this document.

## Section 2 — How It Works

**The movement-structure of compassion**

Compassion in the biblical texts operates as a movement with three phases: an inner stirring, a turn toward the sufferer, and an action that meets the need. The Hebrew and Greek both name primarily the first phase — the inner event — but the contexts consistently complete the arc. When Jesus is *splanchnizō* (moved in his gut) at the sight of the crowd (Matt 9:36), what follows is teaching and healing. When God's *rachamim* is stirred in Hosea 11, what follows is restoration. The inner movement is the spring of the action.

This means compassion in Scripture is not merely an emotional state. It is a motivational structure. What is felt generates what is done.

**Directionality: God toward humanity, humanity toward each other**

The data shows a clear axis. The core *racham* family and the most intensive *splanchnon* vocabulary are used predominantly of God, and of Jesus in his earthly ministry. The divine compassion is the primary referent, and it is described in terms of its freedom (God has compassion on whom he wills, Rom 9:15), its depth (the *polusplanchnos* of James 5:11 — *very* compassionate), and its grounding in God's own character (Exod 34:6, Ps 103:8).

Human compassion is derived. The prophets demand it from Israel because they have experienced it from God. The New Testament calls believers to clothe themselves in *oiktirmos* (compassion, Col 3:12) — placing it on as a garment, which implies it is something acquired and sustained, not merely given. The *sym-* compounds (fellow-suffering, sympathy) describe the Christian community's life together, and the incarnation establishes the theological ground for human compassion by showing that genuine fellow-feeling is possible even for the Son of God.

**The womb-metaphor and its implications**

The RACHAM root binds compassion to the womb at a lexical level that most translation conceals. The word for womb (*rechem*, H7358) and the word for compassion (*rachamim*) are from the same root. Group 729-001 names the structural role of this connection explicitly: *the word for womb is used as the vehicle of a maternal metaphor grounding divine compassion in the image of carrying from before birth — the most intimate care expressed through the body's innermost space*. Isaiah 46:3 deploys this explicitly: *borne by me from before your birth, carried from the womb*. The somatic ground of compassion — where in the body it is felt — is thus named in the very vocabulary used to express it.

The Greek *splanchnizō* / *splanchnon* (G4697/G4698) carries the same somatic logic: the word means bowels or intestines — the body's interior visceral organs. In ancient physiology these were the seat of strong emotion, especially compassion, grief, and love. When Jesus is *splanchnizō*, the text is reporting not only that he was moved emotionally, but that the movement was located in the body's deepest interior — a parallel structure to the Hebrew womb-metaphor.

**Chesed: when compassion becomes character**

The *chesed* vocabulary introduces a different mode of compassion. Here it is not a moment of inner movement but a settled disposition of loyal love — a character quality that persists through time and undergirds all of God's dealings with his people. The three groups from H2617B (*che.sed*) show this across a range: divine chesed as the ground of all covenant relationship (Exod 34:6; Lam 3:22), human chesed as the requirement of faithful relationship (Hos 6:6; Mic 6:8), and the human posture of appeal to and meditation on divine chesed as a sustaining inner orientation (Ps 25:7; 48:9).

The negative semantic pole here is notable: the same root CHASAD yields both *be kind* (H2616A) and *shame* (H2617B). The co-presence of kindness and disgrace in a single root family signals a semantic tension that deserves further study — it appears to name the way covenantal faithfulness, when violated, becomes its opposite.

**Compassion's limits: the judicial suspension**

Not all compassion texts in this registry describe compassion expressed. A significant cluster names its deliberate withdrawal: in judgment, God withholds pity (Ezek 7:4); the law commands Israel not to show it toward the guilty (Deut 19:13). This judicial withholding is not a failure of compassion but its structural complement — it names the moral seriousness of the inner disposition. Compassion that can never be withheld is indistinguishable from mere sentiment.

**H7356A** | *ra.cham* | womb | Group 729-001

*Isa 46:3 — "Listen to me, O house of Jacob, all the remnant of the house of Israel, who have been borne by me from before your birth, carried from the womb."*

This verse does not use the compassion vocabulary directly. What it does is deploy *ra.cham* (womb) as the image through which divine carrying and sustaining are expressed. God presents himself as the one who has carried Israel from the womb — the intimate, bodily sustaining of a mother for the child she forms and bears. The verse establishes that the compassion-vocabulary's root is not an arbitrary linguistic connection but a living metaphor in prophetic speech. God's compassion toward Israel is womb-love: it preceded Israel's capacity to know or respond, it is of the body's most interior origin, and it implies sustained holding rather than momentary feeling.

**G4697** | *splanchnizō* | to pity (be moved in the gut) | Group 730-001

*Luke 7:13 — "And when the Lord saw her, he had compassion on her and said to her, 'Do not weep.'"*

The widow of Nain. Her son has died; she has no remaining male protector. The verb *splanchnizō* names what happens in Jesus when he sees her grief — the visceral inward movement that is his compassion. What follows immediately is a word to the widow, and then the raising of her son. The sequence is instruction: the inner stirring is the source of the act. Note that Jesus speaks first to the grieving mother, not to the dead son. The compassion goes to her before it demonstrates itself in power.

*Matt 9:36 — "When he saw the crowds, he had compassion for them, because they were harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd."*

Here *splanchnizō* is directed not at a single person's grief but at the condition of a multitude. The simile is diagnostic: harassed and helpless like sheep without a shepherd — the suffering is structural and spiritual, not merely circumstantial. Jesus' compassion sees beneath surface behaviour to the underlying destitution. The result is not healing (here) but teaching and the calling of the twelve. Compassion moves toward what the sufferer actually needs.

**G3627** | *oikteirō* | to have compassion | Group 731-001

*Rom 9:15 — "For he says to Moses, 'I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion.'"*

Paul quotes Exod 33:19. The doubled structure of the saying emphasises sovereignty: God's compassion is not obligated or earned — it flows from his own free determination. The two verbs used (*eleeō* and *oikteirō*) are near-synonyms here, but *oikteirō* carries the sense of deep inner movement. The point is that this deep inner movement belongs to God's own freedom. No external ground compels it. This is a sharp theological statement: compassion cannot be claimed or manipulated; it can only be received.

**H5150** | *ni.chum* | comfort (compassion-as-comfort) | Group 733-001

*Hos 11:8 — "How can I give you up, O Ephraim? How can I hand you over, O Israel?… My heart recoils within me; my compassion grows warm and tender."*

One of the most humanly-expressed statements of divine inner life in all of Scripture. God is depicted in internal conflict: the demand of judgement against his own inability to hand over the people he loves. The word *ni.chum* (comfort) names the compassionate tenderness that wins out — *grows warm and tender* in the literal sense. This is compassion not as static attribute but as active, felt reality within God's own inner life. The verse demonstrates that the registry description's phrase *compelled to help by what he sees* is not only true of human compassion; it describes the divine inner experience itself.

*Isa 57:18 — "I have seen his ways, but I will heal him; I will lead him and restore comfort to him and his mourners."*

The *ni.chum* here is the concrete form compassion takes when it reaches its goal — restoration, comfort, healing. God sees the straying (a morally problematic condition) yet moves toward restoration. Compassion here has an explicitly forward-facing quality: it looks not at what the person deserves but at what they need.

**G4834** | *sumpatheō* | to sympathize (suffer-with) | Group 734-001

*Heb 4:15 — "For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin."*

The Christological anchor of the compassion vocabulary. *Sumpatheō* — to feel with, to suffer alongside — is here attributed to the ascended and glorified Christ as an ongoing capacity grounded in his incarnate experience. He *can* sympathise with human weakness because he has himself lived in the conditions of human weakness (temptation, hunger, grief, death). This verse establishes that the capacity for compassion is not a distance-overcoming act of will but arises from shared experience. For the researcher: this is the hermeneutical ground of human compassion-in-community — the community participates in Christ's compassionate fellowship by genuinely entering each other's suffering.

**Session D pointer noted:** This verse connects the compassion vocabulary to a larger Christological anthropology — the doctrine of Christ's incarnation as the ground of genuine solidarity. This connection extends beyond the Compassion registry and requires cross-registry investigation with Love (reg 103), Suffering, and potentially the soul/spirit terms in C01.

**G4841** | *sumpaschō* | to suffer with | Group 3986-001

*1 Cor 12:26 — "If one member suffers, all suffer together; if one member is honored, all rejoice together."*

The mutual fellow-suffering of the body of Christ. *Sumpaschō* here is not a call to compassion — it is a description of how the body of Christ actually functions. When one suffers, the whole body suffers. This is participation, not sympathy at a distance. **G3356** | *metriopatheō* | be gentle (moderate one's feeling) | Group 3988-001

*Heb 5:2 — "He can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward, since he himself is beset with weakness."*

The high priest's capacity for gentle dealing is grounded in his own shared vulnerability. *Metriopatheō* describes moderation of emotional response — a controlled, measured engagement with the weak that avoids both harshness and sentimental indulgence. The mechanism is identical to Heb 4:15: compassion arising from shared condition. This is an important qualifier on compassion — it is not always raw, immediate feeling; it can be shaped by wisdom into measured, calibrated care.

**G4184** | *polusplanchnos* | very compassionate | Group 4011-001

*Jas 5:11 — "Behold, we consider those blessed who remained steadfast. You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful."*

The compound *polusplanchnos* — *full of splanchnon*, intensely compassionate — appears only here in the NT. The context is suffering and endurance: Job's patience is cited as the backdrop against which the Lord's *polusplanchnos* is announced. This is theologically dense: God's deep compassion is revealed precisely through suffering that he permits. The verse does not resolve the tension between divine permission of suffering and divine compassion; it holds them together and names the compassion as *very* deep.

**H2587** | *chan.nun* | gracious | Group 2330-001

*Exo 34:6 — "The Lord passed before him and proclaimed, 'The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness.'"*

The foundational self-description of God in the Old Testament. *Chan.nun* (gracious) appears alongside *rachum* (compassionate), *slow to anger*, *chesed* (steadfast love), and *emet* (faithfulness). The graciousness named here is not a temporary disposition toward the deserving but a settled character attribute. This verse is cited, echoed, or alluded to more than any other Old Testament passage about God's inner character. Its place as an anchor in this registry (and also in the Grace registry, reg 68) signals it as a programme-wide anchor verse. **Session D pointer: Exod 34:6 functions as the character-definition of God across multiple registries — Compassion (reg 23), Grace (reg 68), Mercy (reg 111) — and its full significance will require cross-registry synthesis.**

*Psa 103:8 — "The Lord is merciful and gracious, slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love."*

A direct echo of Exod 34:6 in the Psalter. The psalm deploys the divine character formula as the ground of personal assurance of forgiveness and compassion (vv. 10–13). The formula is not just a theological statement but a pastoral resource — Israel returns to it in the act of praise.

**H2617B** | *che.sed (shame)* | Groups 1633-001, 1633-002, 1633-003

*Exo 34:6* — see above (shared anchor with H2587).

*Lam 3:22 — "The steadfast love of the Lord never ceases; his mercies never come to an end."*

In the context of Lamentations — the most sustained expression of grief in the canon — the declaration of unfailing *chesed* functions as the programme's clearest example of compassion persisting when all circumstantial evidence argues against it. The word *mercies* here (*rachamim*) pairs directly with *chesed*: steadfast love and tender compassion together define what keeps God's people alive when the city is destroyed. This verse is not optimistic sentiment but defiant confession in the face of catastrophe.

*Hos 6:6 — "For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings."*

The prophetic demand that covenantal loyalty (*chesed*) matters more to God than ritual performance. This verse repositions compassion from the domain of private feeling into the domain of ethical priority: what God requires of persons is the inner disposition of loyal love, not external acts of religion performed without it.

*Mic 6:8 — "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness (*chesed*), and to walk humbly with your God?"*

The triad — justice, loving-kindness, humble walk — defines the whole character of the person who lives in right relationship with God. *Chesed* is here an object of love: one does not merely practise it; one loves it. This makes compassion/loyal love a characteristic that orients the inner life, not only a behaviour to be performed.

*Psa 25:7 — "Remember not the sins of my youth or my transgressions; according to your steadfast love (*chesed*) remember me, for the sake of your goodness, O Lord."*

The petitioner grounds the appeal not in his own merit but in God's *chesed*. Compassion here becomes the basis of prayer — the inner character of God as the reason to approach him at all.

*Psa 48:9 — "We have thought on your steadfast love, O God, in the midst of your temple."*

Meditation on divine *chesed* as a sustained inner practice. This is compassion entering the worshipper's own inner life through contemplation — God's character becoming the content of one's thinking in the sanctuary.

**H7362** | *ra.cha.ma.ni* | compassionate | Group 1616-001

*Lam 4:10 — "The hands of compassionate women have boiled their own children; they became their food during the destruction of the daughter of my people."*

The darkest use of the compassion vocabulary in Scripture. The very women whose defining inner quality is named as *compassionate* (*ra.cha.ma.ni*) are the ones driven by siege and famine to the ultimate violation of that quality. **H2347** | *chus* | to pity | Groups 3182-001 and 3182-002

*Jon 4:11 — "And should not I pity Nineveh, that great city, in which there are more than 120,000 persons who do not know their right hand from their left, and also much cattle?"*

God's rhetorical question to Jonah. Jonah's anger at God's compassion toward Nineveh is met with a question about *pity* — the word *chus* — grounded in the simple fact of their number and their ignorance. The divine pity here is not based on their merit (they had just repented, but the question is put in terms of sheer creaturely need, including the cattle). This is compassion grounded in creatureliness, not only in moral renewal.

*Psa 72:13 — "He has pity on the weak and the needy, and saves the lives of the needy."*

The ideal king's exercise of *chus* (pity) — a royal virtue. Compassion here is linked to political power exercised for the protection of the vulnerable. The king who has pity is not sentimental; he *saves lives*. Inner disposition and practical outcome are again inseparable.

*Deu 19:13 — "Your eye shall not pity him, but you shall purge the guilt of innocent blood from Israel, so that it may be well with you."*

The judicial withdrawal of *chus*. In the law's treatment of capital crime, compassion toward the perpetrator is explicitly prohibited — not because it would be understandable but because it would compromise justice and leave innocent blood unavenged. This is the clearest statement of compassion's structural complement in the data.

*Eze 7:4 — "And my eye will not spare you, nor will I have pity, but I will punish you for your ways, while your abominations are in your midst."*

Divine judicial withdrawal of pity in prophetic judgment. The *chus* language appears in its negative form — God will not pity. The judicial setting and the solemn context make clear that pity's absence here is not cold indifference but the necessary expression of righteousness in the face of persistent abomination.

**G1654** | *eleēmosunē* | charity/almsgiving | Group 3167-001

*Luke 11:41 — "But give as alms those things that are within, and behold, everything is clean for you."*

*Ta entos* — the things within, the inner things — given as alms make everything clean. Jesus is addressing Pharisaic concern for external purity. The alms that originate from the inner orientation of mercy and generosity accomplish inward cleansing that ritual washing cannot. Compassion made visible in giving is connected to purity of inner life — a striking inversion of the religious priority of external practice.

*Matt 6:2 — "Thus, when you give to the needy, sound no trumpet before you, as the hypocrites do…"*

Almsgiving (*eleēmosunē*) as a practice that can become its own corruption. The Sermon on the Mount's concern is not only that compassion is expressed but that it is expressed from the right inner orientation — not as performance for reputation but as the outflow of genuine inner mercy. The context group description (*the outward expression of an inward disposition of mercy and generosity*) is precise: the concern is the integrity of the connection between inner disposition and outward act.

**G1652** | *eleeinos* | pitiful | Group 3168-001

*Rev 3:17 — "For you say, 'I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing,' not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked."*

The Laodicean church. The *eleeinos* (pitiable condition) appears not as an external misfortune but as a spiritual state masked by material prosperity. ## Section 4 — The Vocabulary

The vocabulary of compassion in Scripture divides into three distinct root families, with a fourth important cluster centred on graciousness. Together they cover a semantic range from raw physical movement to settled covenantal character.

**The RACHAM family — Hebrew**

The foundational root is *racham*/*rechem* — womb. From this root come the central terms for compassion in the Old Testament: H7356A *ra.cham* (womb, 6 occurrences in the extract), H7358 *re.chem* (womb, anatomical, 26 occurrences), H7362 *ra.cha.ma.ni* (compassionate, 1 occurrence), and the plural form *rachamim* (compassions/tender mercies — a consistently plural noun across the Old Testament, owned by the Mercy registry, reg 111). The root is also shared with the Love registry (reg 103), establishing a linguistic family that connects love, compassion, and womb-tenderness in a single semantic cluster.

*Ra.cham* (H7356A) carries a documented note: it connects compassion to its somatic root — womb-love. The compassion vocabulary in Hebrew is therefore not metaphorical in a secondary sense; the metaphor is built into the word. This has consequence for how the characteristic is understood: biblical compassion is not conceived as cool sympathy but as the bodily love of a parent for a child formed within them.

The related words list for the RACHAM terms shows the internal coherence of this family: compassionate, to have compassion, womb, compassion, compassionate — the family refers entirely to itself and to the compassion domain.

**The SPLANCHN family — Greek**

The New Testament's primary compassion vocabulary centres on G4697 *splanchnizō* (to be moved in the gut, to pity) and its sibling G4698 *splanchnon* (bowels/entrails, the physical seat of compassion — XREF term, owned by reg 111 Mercy). The verb *splanchnizō* carries a remarkable status note: it is the *compassion-movement verb* and is used exclusively in the Gospels to describe Jesus being moved with compassion (Matt 9:36; 14:14; 15:32; 20:34; Mark 1:41; 6:34; 8:2; Luke 7:13; 10:33; 15:20). In the parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15:20), the father is *splanchnizō* — moved in his gut — when he sees his son returning. Outside the Gospels the word is used of human compassion only in that parable. This concentration is significant: the gut-level visceral compassion appears to be associated in the NT primarily with Jesus and with the Father in the parable. G4184 *polusplanchnos* (very compassionate) is a compound that intensifies this — *full of* splanchnon, only in Jas 5:11.

G4697 carries a Phase 2 flag: *SEMANTIC_RANGE_BREADTH* — its semantic range covers four or more distinct domains, warranting attention in Session B cross-part synthesis.

**The PATH family — Greek**

A distinct Greek root family centres on *pathos* (experience, suffering). This family produces the compassion vocabulary of the New Testament epistles rather than the Gospels. The key terms are G4834 *sumpatheō* (to sympathise, 2 occurrences), G4835 *sumpathēs* (sympathetic, 1 occurrence), G4841 *sumpaschō* (to suffer with, 3 occurrences), G3356 *metriopatheō* (to deal gently, moderate one's feeling, 1 occurrence), and the broader suffering vocabulary (*pathēma* etc.). These terms carry the *syn-* (with/together) prefix that names fellow-suffering: suffering-*with* rather than suffering-*at*. The LSJ entry for *sumpatheō* notes its classical use to describe the sympathy between soul and body — *the soul and body seem to have sympathy with each other* (Aristotle). The New Testament reappropriates this philosophical concept for the community of faith.

*Metriopatheō* (G3356, 1 occurrence, thin extraction) is notable as the term for *moderate* compassion — the measured fellow-feeling of the high priest who is himself subject to weakness. It describes compassion shaped by wisdom rather than expressed in its raw form.

**The CHESED family — Hebrew**

H2617A *che.sed* (kindness/steadfast love — XREF term, owned by Kindness reg 99 or Loyalty reg 104) and its siblings H2616A *cha.sad* (be kind, 2 occurrences), H2617B *che.sed* (shame — the negative semantic pole, 3 occurrences) form the covenantal loyalty cluster. The *chesed* vocabulary describes compassion not as a felt movement but as a settled disposition of loyal relationship. The related word list for H2617B names its semantic scope honestly: *be kind, to shame, kindness* — the positive quality, its opposite, and the term for the quality itself. That *shame* appears as a member of this family is the programme's linguistic evidence for the CHASAD root paradox flagged as a Session D pointer: the same root yields both the deepest covenantal kindness and the concept of disgrace.

H2587 *chan.nun* (gracious, 13 occurrences, extracted) is notable for its status note: *guilt resolution vocabulary — grace, graciousness, and supplication as response to guilt*. This positions graciousness as a component of compassion that specifically addresses the condition of guilt, connecting this registry to the Guilt registry (reg 73) and the Grace registry (reg 68). Its related words list includes beauty (*chin*), favour (*chen*), supplication (*techinah*) — a coherent family around the root of favour extended to those who need it.

**The ELEEIN family — Greek**

G1652 *eleeinos* (pitiful, 2 occurrences) and G1654 *eleēmosunē* (charity/almsgiving, 36 occurrences) are the outer members of the mercy vocabulary family (the central term G1656 *eleos* — mercy — is an XREF term owned by the Mercy registry, reg 111). *Eleēmosunē* is the compassion vocabulary at its most concrete: the inner disposition of mercy expressed in the specific act of giving to the poor. Its LSJ entry shows the semantic development from *pity, mercy* (classical) to *charity, alms* (LXX and NT). The word carries the transition from inner state to outward practice built into its meaning.

**Synthesis observation**

The full vocabulary of compassion in Scripture moves across a spectrum from the somatic to the characterological. At one end, *ra.cham/re.chem* and *splanchnizō* locate compassion in the body's deepest interior — womb and bowels — as a physical resonance with another's suffering. At the other end, *chesed* and *chan.nun* describe compassion as a settled character quality, a disposition that persists through time, requires no triggering event, and shapes the whole orientation of a person (or of God) toward relationship. Between these two poles, the *sum-* compounds name compassion as participation — genuine entry into shared experience, made possible by shared vulnerability. The vocabulary as a whole rejects a purely sentimental understanding of compassion. It is at once more bodily (than sentiment) and more structural (than emotion). It is the inner architecture through which one person is constituted to be moved by another's need.
