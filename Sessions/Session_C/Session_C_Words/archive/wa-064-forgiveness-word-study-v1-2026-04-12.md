## Section 1 — Word Characteristic Summary

Forgiveness is the act of releasing a claim you have a right to hold. It is not the pretence that a wrong did not happen, nor the numbing of the pain it caused. It is the deliberate choice to cancel the account of injury — to stop holding the debt against the person who owes it and to relinquish the right of collection. That choice is one of the most inwardly demanding acts a human being can perform, precisely because it runs against the inner logic of what has happened: something real was taken, and the instinct of the inner life is to register and retain that loss.

Scripture presents forgiveness across a wide semantic field. The Greek vocabulary reaches from the formal noun for release and remission (*aphesis* — a word used in the ancient world for the releasing of prisoners and the cancelling of debts) to the verb *aphiēmi*, which is the same word used for leaving a place, letting go of a possession, or permitting someone to pass. The range is deliberate: forgiveness in Scripture is always a letting-go, a releasing of something the holder had every right to keep. The Hebrew vocabulary from the *sālach* family reinforces this: to pardon, to be of a pardoning disposition, to hold forgiveness as a characteristic.

The most distinctive feature of forgiveness in this evidence is where it ultimately comes from. God is its primary source and its defining model throughout both testaments. He forgives through sacrifice and priestly mediation in the old covenant context; he forgives through Christ's blood in the new. Human forgiveness is consistently presented not as an independent moral achievement but as a response — a pattern of release in one person that mirrors and flows from having been released by God. The more profoundly a person understands how much they have been forgiven, the more freely they can forgive. That causal logic is stated explicitly in the data and shapes the character of the word throughout.

Forgiveness is not primarily an emotion, though it may affect and transform the emotional life. It is closer to a settled inner orientation — a relational posture of release rather than retention — that has both vertical and horizontal dimensions: what is received from God and what is extended to others.

## Section 2 — Word Impact Description

The opposite of forgiveness is the retention of the claim — the inner insistence that the debt remains, that the account is still open, that the person still owes. This is sometimes called unforgiveness or hardness of heart, but in the evidence it appears most sharply in the image of the person who receives mercy and immediately refuses to extend it: the logic of the closed inner posture. Where forgiveness releases, its absence holds. And the data is pointed about the consequences: the one who withholds forgiveness is poisoned by what they retain.

What feeds and strengthens forgiveness is the prior experience of being forgiven. The grammar of the evidence is clear: the person who has understood the magnitude of what has been released in their own case has more resource to release others. Forgiveness received opens the inner capacity for forgiveness given. Worship and atonement — the rituals and processes by which forgiveness is received — are therefore upstream of the human capacity to forgive. Prayer also feeds it: the pattern of asking God for pardon in repentance keeps the forgiver in touch with their own need, which is the ground of their compassion for the needs of others.

What diminishes or blocks forgiveness is the hardening of the inner account-keeping tendency — the cultivated sense of entitlement to the debt, the refusal to allow the record to be cleared. Pride, self-justification, and the comparison of one's own debts to those of others all close the inner hand that forgiveness requires to open.

What forgiveness produces in the person who practises it is a form of inner freedom: the debt no longer organises the person's inner life. The resentment, the injury-rehearsal, the anticipatory bitterness — these lose their hold. The connection between being forgiven and the release of love suggests that the inner orientation released by forgiveness is generative rather than merely neutral.

In relationships, forgiveness makes continuation possible where continuation would otherwise be impossible. It breaks the cycle of accumulating injury and restores the relational possibility between parties who stand on opposite sides of a real wrong.

At its source, forgiveness is not self-generated. The capacity to genuinely release a legitimate claim against another person, at depth and without self-deception, appears in the evidence as something received before it can be given — grounded in an encounter with the character of God, who holds forgiveness as his own essential disposition and who practises it as an act of genuine release, not of overlooking.

>

Paul uses it in the richest of all relational contexts, linking forgiveness to redemption (a buyback, a paying of the cost) and grounding both in blood and in grace. The verse names what forgiveness actually costs: it is not free, even when it is freely given. The account was genuinely owed; it has been genuinely paid. This matters for understanding what human forgiveness means — it takes seriously the reality of the debt before it releases it.

> **Mark 10:29** — *Jesus said, "Truly, I say to you, there is no one who has left house or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or lands, for my sake and for the gospel"*
>
> This is the same word (*aphiēmi*) in its leaving-behind sense. The deliberate leaving of what is precious — family, home, land — as a response to a higher call. The verse does not speak of forgiveness directly, but it reveals the inner structure of the word's root meaning: this is a voluntary act of releasing what one has a right to retain. The wilful, inwardly-driven character of the letting-go is the same act-structure that underlies forgiveness itself. The link is not incidental — it helps explain why forgiveness is described in Scripture as an act of *leaving* the claim rather than destroying it.

> **Revelation 2:4** — *But I have this against you, that you have abandoned the love you had at first*
>
> Here the same word names a failure — the abandonment of love, the leaving-behind of what should have been kept. The negative usage clarifies the positive: when the word describes forgiveness, the leaving is of the *debt*, not of the relationship. The contrast between this verse and the forgiveness uses of the word shows that *aphiēmi* names a morally significant act of release whose rightness depends entirely on what is being released and for what reason.

The Hebrew vocabulary from the *sālach* family reinforces this: to pardon, to be of a pardoning disposition, to hold forgiveness as a characteristic.

> **Psalm 86:5** — *For you, O Lord, are good and forgiving, abounding in steadfast love to all who call upon you*
>
> The Hebrew adjective used here (*sallāch*) appears only once in the Old Testament and only of God. It names forgiveness not as an occasional act but as a stable disposition — a characteristic belonging to God's essential goodness. The pairing with *steadfast love* (*hesed*) places forgiveness inside the relational covenant faithfulness of God rather than alongside an occasional act of mercy. For the question of where human forgiveness ultimately comes from, this verse is foundational: the disposition to pardon belongs, first and constitutively, to the character of God.

The most distinctive feature of forgiveness in this evidence is where it ultimately comes from. God is its primary source and its defining model throughout both testaments. He forgives through sacrifice and priestly mediation in the old covenant context; he forgives through Christ's blood in the new.

> **Leviticus 4:20** — *Thus shall he do with the bull. As he did with the bull of the sin offering, so shall he do with this. And the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven*
>
> The OT cultic act: sacrifice, priestly mediation, atonement, and then — as its outcome — forgiveness. The passive voice matters: *they shall be forgiven*. Forgiveness in this frame is not self-achieved but conferred, as the result of a completed mediatory act. This is the covenantal foundation on which the whole NT development stands. When Hebrews 9:22 states that without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness, it is naming a logic that Leviticus already contains.

> **Jeremiah 31:34** — *For I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more*
>
> The new covenant promise: forgiveness as a divine act so complete that it includes the cessation of divine memory of the sin. This is not amnesia but covenantal resolution — the account is not merely deferred but genuinely closed. The programmatic character of this promise (spoken before any Calvary, in the context of covenant failure and exile) shows that forgiveness is not an emergency measure but the shape of the relationship God intends. It also sets a standard for what human forgiveness means: genuine release, not temporary suspension.

Human forgiveness is consistently presented not as an independent moral achievement but as a response — a pattern of release in one person that mirrors and flows from having been released by God.

> **Matthew 18:35** — *So also my heavenly Father will do to every one of you, if you do not forgive your brother from your heart*
>
> The phrase *from your heart* is the critical addition. The parable preceding this verse distinguishes between a verbal release of debt and an inward one: the servant who was forgiven much goes and chokes the man who owes him little. Jesus names the failure precisely — it is a failure of the heart, not merely of conduct. This verse states that forgiveness must have an inner location to be genuine. It also places human forgiveness within a divine relational framework: the Father's forgiveness of us creates the obligation and provides the model and the resource for our forgiveness of one another.

> **Luke 7:47** — *Therefore I tell you, her sins, which are many, are forgiven — for she loved much. But he who is forgiven little, loves little*
>
> The inner-being grammar stated in its clearest form. Forgiveness received produces love — not as a separate effect but as its inner consequence. The logic runs: the more that is forgiven, the more love is released in the person. This is not merely motivational; it describes an actual inner transformation. The person who has understood the depth of their own forgiveness has an inner life that has been opened. This verse is the single most important data point for understanding what forgiveness does inside the person who receives it. It is the anchor for the programme finding (DIM-64-001) that forgiveness operates as an inner-being generator: divine forgiveness received → love orientation produced.

The opposite of forgiveness is the retention of the claim — the inner insistence that the debt remains, that the account is still open, that the person still owes.

> **Psalm 130:4** — *But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared*
>
> One of the most concentrated theological statements about forgiveness in the Old Testament. Forgiveness is presented here not as an expected divine kindness but as an extraordinary and specifically located reality: it is *with* God, as a possession of his character, not a general feature of reality. The second clause is paradoxical: forgiveness produces fear. Not the fear of punishment — that would be the consequence of *un*forgiveness — but a reverential awe at the character of the one who holds forgiveness as his own. The verse implies that without the reality of forgiveness, the appropriate response to God would be despair or flight; forgiveness makes it possible to approach.

> **Daniel 9:19** — *O Lord, hear; O Lord, forgive. O Lord, pay attention and act. Delay not, for your own sake, O my God, because your city and your people are called by your name*
>
> Intercessory prayer for forgiveness — the active seeking of God's pardon on behalf of a community. Daniel does not appeal to Israel's merit but to God's own name and character. The urgency of the prayer ("delay not") shows that forgiveness is understood as something that can be granted or withheld, sought and received. The intercessory dimension reveals that forgiveness has a corporate as well as an individual scope; it is not only a transaction between a person and God but something that can be sought for communities under judgement.

What feeds and strengthens forgiveness is the prior experience of being forgiven. What forgiveness produces in the person who practises it is a form of inner freedom.

> **Matthew 5:24** — *Leave your gift there before the altar and go. First be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift*
>
> The relational priority: reconciliation precedes religious ritual. The act of leaving the gift (the same word, *aphiēmi*, used for forgiveness) to seek reconciliation first names forgiveness as the enabling condition of worship. The inner posture toward the brother must be resolved before the vertical offering is acceptable. This verse places forgiveness inside a relational priority structure in which horizontal relationships are not secondary to the vertical — they are its necessary precondition.

> **Mark 10:14** — *But when Jesus saw it, he was indignant and said to them, "Let the children come to me; do not hinder them, for to such belongs the kingdom of God"*
>
> This verse does not speak of forgiveness directly but illustrates the permitting sense of *aphiēmi* — the posture of opening, of granting access, of refusing to obstruct. The contrast with the forbidding sense in Matthew 23:13 (below) shows that the same word can name either an act of welcome or an act of blockage. The inner posture of the actor determines the moral character of the release.

> **Matthew 23:13** — *But woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you shut the kingdom of heaven in people's faces. For you neither enter yourselves nor allow those who would enter to go in*
>
> The refusal of access as moral failure. The religious leaders' *not permitting* (*ouk aphiēte*) names an inner posture of closure — a closing of the gate that should be open. There is a meaningful parallel to the structure of unforgiveness: both name the inner act of withholding a release that is owed. The severity of the judgement here ("woe to you") is consistent with the pattern elsewhere: the withholding of what should be released is among the gravest inner failures.

> **Mark 3:29** — *But whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit never has forgiveness, but is guilty of an eternal sin*
>
> The outer limit: the one sin for which forgiveness is said to be permanently unavailable. The verse does not explain the mechanism of this exception — it simply states it. Its function in the word study is to establish that forgiveness, though described as God's characteristic disposition, is not unconditional or automatic. There is a boundary. What lies beyond the boundary is not fully explained, but its existence qualifies any simple account of forgiveness as always available. Session B should investigate what the data shows about this verse in relation to the rest of the forgiveness corpus.

## Section 4 — Original Language Vocabulary

The vocabulary of forgiveness in Scripture divides into two language families: a Greek family centred on the root *aphiēmi* (to release, to send away) and a Hebrew family centred on the root *sālach* (to pardon). These two families do not share a common etymological ancestor, but they converge on the same conceptual ground: the releasing of a legitimate hold on a person or a debt. Seven terms were identified across the research, all owner terms with no shared vocabulary with other word studies in the programme. This is a notably self-contained registry — the sharing ratio is minimal — which signals that forgiveness occupies a distinctive lexical space rather than overlapping heavily with adjacent characteristics.

**Greek terms — the** ***aphiēmi*** **family**

*Aphesis* (G0859, *afesis*) is the nominal form — the state or act of release. It occurs 62 times in the New Testament and is entirely a NT word; it has no OT Hebrew equivalent in this registry. The Mounce range — forgiveness, pardon, release, cancellation of a debt — captures both its legal and its personal register. The LSJ data confirms that in pre-biblical Greek usage, *aphesis* named the releasing of prisoners, the cancellation of financial obligations, the starting of horses in a race (where something is launched from a held position), and the dismissal of persons from service. These secular senses are not merely etymological curiosities: they show that the NT writers were using a word whose primary meaning was the releasing of something held, and applying it to the releasing of sin's hold on a person. The word carries the force of a formal legal release, not merely an emotional letting-go.

*Aphiēmi* (G0863) is the verb from which *aphesis* derives. The programme has recorded it as three distinct sub-glosses — leave (G0863G, 236 occurrences), forgive (G0863H, 64 occurrences), and permit (G0863I, 32 occurrences) — reflecting the word's three primary usage registers. Together these three sub-glosses represent 332 NT occurrences of the same underlying verb across its full semantic range. The forgiveness sense (G0863H) is the most theologically concentrated, but all three senses share the same structural act: the releasing of something held. To leave one's home, to forgive a debt, and to permit a person to pass are all acts of non-retention. The semantic unity of the word family is instructive for understanding what forgiveness actually means as an inner-being act: it belongs to the same family of releasing-acts as voluntary departure and the granting of access.

The Mounce definition for the verb family draws together: *to forgive, pardon, remit, cancel; to leave, abandon; to allow, permit, tolerate; to send away; to give up, yield, resign.* The LSJ data on the verb adds that in classical usage it named the releasing of a ship from harbour, the dismissal of a person from service, and the dying breath (the *pneuma* being released). The NT does not use all these senses, but the core releasing-act runs through all of them.

It is worth noting that *aphiēmi* has both active and receptive senses: one person releases, another receives the release. This makes it capable of describing God as the acting subject (he releases the debt of sin) and the human as the recipient — and equally capable of describing the human as actor (he releases the claim against his brother) and another person as the one released. The same word-act structure in both directions is part of what makes it the natural vocabulary for the divine-human pattern of forgiveness.

**Hebrew terms — the** ***sālach*** **family**

*Sālach* (H5545) is the primary Hebrew verb for divine forgiveness, occurring 49 times in the Old Testament. Its theological profile is striking: in the Old Testament evidence, this word is used almost exclusively of God as the acting subject. It is not the typical word a human being uses to describe forgiving another human being; it is the word for what God does when he pardons. The BDB senses are simple — Qal: to forgive, to pardon; Niphal: to be forgiven (passive). There is no causative or intensive stem in the recorded data, which means the word does not carry the structural implication that one party causes forgiveness in another in the way some Hebrew verbs do. It simply names the act of pardon.

*Sallāch* (H5546, *sallāch*), occurring only once in the Old Testament, is the adjectival form: *forgiving*, as a permanent disposition. This single occurrence — in Psalm 86:5 — is the only place in the entire Hebrew Bible where forgiveness is named as a stable character quality rather than as a specific act. Its rarity makes it the more striking: the disposition of ready forgiveness belongs to God alone.

*Selichah* (H5547, *selichah*) is the nominal form corresponding to *sālach*, occurring three times (Psalm 86:5, Psalm 130:4, Daniel 9:9). It names forgiveness as a possession — something that *belongs* to God, that is *with* him. Both Psalm 86:5 and Psalm 130:4 use this construction of belonging: forgiveness as an attribute located in the character of God rather than as a general availability. The concentration of this nominal form in Psalms and Daniel — the books of prayer and intercession — is consistent with its use: *selichah* is the word for the forgiveness one appeals to in prayer, the divine resource that petition addresses.

**Synthesis observation**

Together the two families describe a vocabulary of release that moves from the structural (the act of letting go of what is held) through the dispositional (the readiness to pardon as a character quality) to the substantial (forgiveness as something God possesses and makes available). The Greek vocabulary is richer in the structural and active senses — the releasing-act, the permitted access, the voluntary departure — while the Hebrew vocabulary concentrates on the vertical and the dispositional: what God does, what God is, what God holds. The NT receives and develops the Hebraic theological content through vocabulary that was already structurally rich in the Hellenistic world. The combination produces a concept that is simultaneously legal (cancellation of a real debt), relational (restoration of a broken standing), and characterological (a disposition that belongs to God and can be cultivated in the human person who has received it).

## Section 5 — Connections and Research Pointers

Forgiveness is a notably self-contained word in terms of vocabulary sharing: the sharing ratio data shows zero XREF term pairs, and the root families do not cross into other registries in the programme. This signals that forgiveness occupies its own lexical space. However, its verse co-occurrence and dimension overlap data tell a different story: the word appears alongside a wide range of other inner-being characteristics in the same verses, and its dimension profile overlaps with many adjacent words. In practice, forgiveness is highly relational — it rarely appears in Scripture in isolation from other inner-being states. The connections below are drawn from the correlation data and from the verse evidence reviewed in Session C.

**Guilt** — verse co-occurrence, shared anchor verses; very high priority

The strongest correlation in the data: 54 shared verses. Guilt and forgiveness travel together throughout the evidence because they are, in a sense, structural counterparts — guilt names the inner state produced by moral failure, and forgiveness names its resolution. The anchor verse Jeremiah 31:34 is shared between the two registries. The key question: does forgiveness *remove* guilt as an inner-being state, or does it *reframe* it? The verse evidence suggests genuine release rather than suppression, but the inner-being mechanism of that release — how forgiveness changes the guilt-state — is worth examining. This is a high-priority connection for Session D.

**Mercy** — verse co-occurrence, dimension overlap, shared anchor verses (Lev 4:20); high priority

Sixteen shared verses and a shared anchor verse. Mercy and forgiveness share the Relational Disposition dimension and co-occur consistently in the Psalms and in the Gospels. Both describe what God extends to a person who is not in a position to demand it. The question is whether mercy is the precondition of forgiveness, its accompaniment, or something distinct. The Leviticus 4:20 anchor is shared — atonement produces both forgiveness and a display of divine mercy. The relationship needs formal examination.

**Grace** — dimension overlap; high priority

Three shared dimensions, including Relational Disposition. The grace registry (68) has already produced evidence that grace operates as an inner-being generator producing supplication. The finding from DIM-64-001 (this registry) suggests that forgiveness operates similarly — producing love as a downstream inner orientation. The hypothesis that forgiveness and grace function as parallel inner-being generators (divine input → specific inner-orientation output) is explicitly raised and should be a priority research question for Session D. The connection is currently inferential but strongly supported by the data pattern.

**Repentance** — verse co-occurrence; high priority

Twelve shared verses. Repentance and forgiveness are closely paired throughout the NT: repentance is consistently presented as the posture through which forgiveness is received (Acts 2:38, Luke 24:47). The question is the direction of priority: does repentance *produce* forgiveness, or does the prior availability of forgiveness *enable* repentance? Luke 7:47 (anchor verse for this registry) suggests something closer to the latter — forgiveness received precedes and generates the loving orientation that looks like repentance's fruit. This is a significant inner-being sequence question.

**Love** — verse co-occurrence; high priority

Ten shared verses. Luke 7:47 already establishes an explicit causal inner-being connection: forgiveness received produces love. This is the most direct inner-being connection in the whole corpus and is formally noted in the programme findings (DIM-64-001). Whether this connection holds across the wider corpus — whether the forgiveness verses consistently show love as a downstream state — is worth formal testing in Session D.

**Covenant** — dimension overlap; medium priority

Five shared dimensions, cluster partner in C17. Forgiveness in the OT is always framed within the covenant relationship — atonement is the mechanism by which a covenant people receive forgiveness. The Jeremiah 31:34 anchor is the programmatic new covenant text: forgiveness is not only a feature of the covenant but its guarantee. Whether the covenant connection shapes the inner-being character of forgiveness (making it structurally covenantal rather than transactional) is a Session D question.

**Sin** — shared anchor verse (Mar 3:29); medium priority

The registry for sin (147) shares the Mark 3:29 anchor — the unforgivable sin. The relationship between the sin and forgiveness registries is inherently structural. Session D should examine the degree to which forgiveness is defined by what it addresses (sin) rather than by an independent positive character.

**Kindness, Peace, Compassion** — dimension overlap; medium priority

These three cluster partners in C17 share dimension profiles with forgiveness, reflecting the broad relational disposition territory they collectively occupy. Whether they are causally connected to forgiveness (kindness and compassion as preconditions; peace as an outcome) is a Session D question. Inferential at present.

**Deadness (spiritual)** — dimension overlap; lower priority

A surprising connection: five shared dimensions. The overlap likely reflects the eschatological and negative-limit territory (Mar 3:29 naming the absolute inaccessibility of forgiveness in certain conditions). The connection deserves flagging even if it is not a primary research target.
