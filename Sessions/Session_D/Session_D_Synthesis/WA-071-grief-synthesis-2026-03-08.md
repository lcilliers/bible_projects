### CHALAH — cha.lah (H2470I) and cho.li (H2483) | Parts 1 and 3 | Cross-part root

*These terms were marked provisional in both Part 1 and Part 3 pending this synthesis.*

**Resolved: the provisional verdicts from both Part 1 and Part 3 are confirmed with additional cross-part observations.**

The CHALAH root spans grief-as-weakness (the verb cha.lah, Part 1) and grief-as-sickness (the noun cho.li, Part 3). What the cross-part view makes visible that neither part could see alone is that both terms appear in Isa 53 in close proximity, and they do different work in that passage. cha.lah appears in Isa 53:10 — "the Lord was pleased to crush him; he has put him to grief" — where the Hiphil causative names what God does to the Servant. cho.li appears in Isa 53:3–4 — "a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief; he has borne our griefs" — where the noun names the grief the Servant inhabits and vicariously bears on behalf of others.

This Isa 53 distribution is analytically significant. Within a short span of text, the CHALAH root covers three distinct inner-being operations: the verb naming a condition caused by God (Hiphil of cha.lah in v.10), the noun naming a condition known and inhabited by the Servant (cho.li in v.3), and the noun extended to the griefs borne on behalf of the people (cho.li in v.4). The same root simultaneously covers caused-grief, known-grief, and carried-grief. No other root family in this word set shows three such distinct operations within the same text.

Outside Isa 53, cha.lah operates primarily in contexts of weakness at the intersection of bodily and inner debility, with its grief meaning as a secondary register across its full 60-occurrence set (only 15% sampled in Part 1). cho.li operates primarily as straightforward physical illness across its 24-occurrence set (88% sampled in Part 3), with the Isa 53 grief usage as a concentrated but theologically elevated sub-register. The tension within cho.li between physical-illness uses and grief-bearing uses is not a confusion in the data — it reflects the Hebrew root's understanding that grief and bodily weakness are expressions of the same underlying condition of becoming unable to function as normal.

The cross-part verdict: the CHALAH root qualifies as an inner-being root family across both its terms. cha.lah qualifies on the basis of the grief-weakness intersection and the Hiphil causative in Isa 53:10. cho.li qualifies primarily through the Isa 53:3–4 grief-sickness-bearing use, which stands in tension with but is not erased by the majority physical-illness distribution. Root-level synthesis is complete. Remaining questions about the Isa 53 theological significance and the NT uptake of this root are restated as Phase 2 questions in Section 6.

## Section 3 — Full-Word OT/NT Register Analysis

**What the full OT grief vocabulary is doing.** Across the 21 OT terms in this word set, the Hebrew vocabulary for grief is doing several things simultaneously. It names grief as a set of distinct physical images — darkening (qa.dar), bitterness (ma.rar), sickness and weakness (CHALAH), wounding (ats.tse.vet), staggering (pu.qah), and mourning-with-body (a.val, a.vel) — each of which has an inner dimension but none of which separates inner and outer cleanly. The Hebrew terms consistently hold body and inner person together: the most explicit evidence is ka.av's meaning block (sub-definitions for physical and mental pain within the same verb), ka.as in Psa 31:9 ("my soul and my body also"), ke.ev in Isa 65:14 ("pain of heart and breaking of spirit"), and makh.ov in 2Ch 6:29 ("each knowing his own affliction and his own sorrow"). Greek lexicography often marks a formal inner/outer distinction; Hebrew does not, and the grief word set reflects this.

The OT vocabulary also places grief in a consistently theological frame. God is agent, observer, and reverser of grief across the full set. At least five terms show a pattern where God sees and attends to the grief: o.ni (God sees affliction), makh.ov (God knows suffering, Exo 3:7), a.tsav (Israel's grief reaches and grieves God), ya.gah (God inflicts grief in covenant judgment), and ka.av (God both wounds and heals, Job 5:18). The prominence of God as agent across the OT grief vocabulary is a full-word feature that no individual part's analysis could quantify. It is not incidental — it appears across diverse root families, literary genres, and historical periods.

A third feature of the OT set is the grief-anger boundary. Three terms sit explicitly at this boundary: ma.rar (Hithpalpel, enraged, Dan 8:7; 11:11), a.tsav (grief and anger simultaneous in 1Sa 20:34; anger-adjacent in Gen 34:7), and ka.as / ka.a.s (primary meaning including anger and provocation alongside grief). This is not a peripheral feature but a characteristic of the OT grief vocabulary: Hebrew does not maintain a sharp border between grief and anger in the same way that modern English psychology distinguishes them.

**What the full NT grief vocabulary is doing.** Across the 6 NT terms (all Greek, all in Part 3), the NT vocabulary introduces three registers with no close parallel in the OT set. The first is the classification of grief by source and outcome: 2Cor 7:10 (lu.pe) explicitly divides grief into godly grief that produces repentance and worldly grief that produces death. No OT term across Parts 1–3 makes this kind of internal classification of grief by its direction and effect. The second is eschatological groaning: ste.na.zo (Rom 8:23; 2Cor 5:2–4) names grief as the inward longing of those who have the Spirit and await the not-yet of bodily redemption. This is grief generated not by loss or sin but by the gap between what the Spirit gives and what is not yet received. The OT has reversal promises for grief but does not name the grieving-toward-a-future as a distinct inner state. The third is Beatitude mourning: pen.the.o (Matt 5:4) positions mourning itself as a present disposition that qualifies a person for divine blessing and comfort — not a state to be removed (as in ya.gon's reversal contexts) but a state to be inhabited and blessed.

**The most significant difference between OT and NT across the full term set.** The most significant OT/NT difference is not that the NT introduces new grief terms — it is that the NT introduces a new relationship between grief and outcome. In the OT, grief is consistently named, caused, witnessed, carried, and reversed; its character is determined by its context (covenant judgment, lament, bereavement, exile) and its relationship to God (who causes, knows, and resolves it). In the NT, grief is explicitly classified by its interior orientation and its productive direction: grief from God produces repentance and life (lu.pe, 2Cor 7:10); grief as eschatological longing moves toward redemption (ste.na.zo); mourning as disposition receives blessing (pen.the.o, Matt 5:4). The NT grief vocabulary is characterised by teleological framing — grief is assessed not only by what it is but by where it is going.

**Cross-part patterns visible only from the full word set.** Four cross-part patterns were identified during synthesis.

First, the pattern of naming grief as what God observes and responds to appears in two separate root families: o.ni (ONI, Part 1) and makh.ov (KAV, Part 2). Both terms name suffering consistently as the condition that God sees, knows, and acts upon. This is not a feature of any single root but a recurring functional orientation that two unrelated roots share.

Second, the grieving-the-Spirit correspondence: Isa 63:10 (a.tsav, ATSAV, Part 2, Hiphil) and Eph 4:30 (lu.pe.o, LUPE, Part 3) both name the action of grieving the Holy Spirit through human behaviour. These are the only two instances across the 27-term set where a grief verb is applied specifically to the Holy Spirit as the recipient of grief. Their cross-Testament alignment is the most specific lexical link between the OT and NT grief vocabularies in this word set.

Third, the grief-and-anger boundary pattern: ma.rar (MAR, Part 1, Hithpalpel), a.tsav (ATSAV, Part 2, simultaneous grief-anger), and ka.as (KAS, Part 2, provocation-grief compound) all sit at the boundary between grief and anger. This pattern is distributed across three different root families in two parts. Grief and anger share semantic space in the Hebrew inner-being vocabulary in a way that the individual part analyses could register but not quantify. The three terms define an anger-adjacent sub-field within the grief word set.

Fourth, the sorrow-replaced-by-joy pattern appears across three root families and both Testaments: a.val (AVL, Part 1, sorrow turning to gladness), ya.gon (YGN, Part 2, sorrow and sighing fleeing away; sorrow replaced by joy in Jer 31:13), lu.pe (LUPE, Part 3, John 16:20–22, sorrow into joy), and penthos (PENTHOS, Part 3, Rev 21:4, mourning abolished). The OT instances describe a future divine act that replaces sorrow; the NT instances include both the same pattern (Rev 21:4) and a distinctly different one (Matt 5:4 — mourning blessed in its present state rather than replaced). The full-word picture reveals that the OT and NT handle the relationship between grief and its resolution through partially overlapping but not identical structures.

## Section 4 — Qualification Summary

Of the 27 terms across this word set, 22 qualify fully as inner-being words, 3 qualify partially, and 2 remain undetermined or at the margin. The fully qualifying terms cover all 6 NT Greek terms and 16 of the 21 OT Hebrew terms. The partial qualifiers are ra.ah (names calamity that produces grief rather than the inner state itself), a.mel (names the sufferer rather than the state), and pu.qah (hapax with figurative grief meaning only). mo.rah is undetermined on one verse, and its consolidation with ma.rar is pending. ka.a.s qualifies provisionally, contingent on the consolidation decision with ka.as.

The qualifying terms are predominantly nominal in form — the word set contains more nouns and noun-pairs than verbs. The verbs present (cha.lah, a.val, qa.dar, ma.rar, a.tsav, ya.gah, ka.av, lu.pe.o, ste.na.zo, pen.the.o) consistently carry causative forms, which is itself a feature of the grief word set as a whole: grief in both Testaments is not merely experienced passively but is imposed, caused, inflicted, and in some cases deliberately administered. The presence of causative forms across at least seven of the qualifying verbs is a full-word signal feature that distinguishes grief from some other inner-being words where the inner state is more purely receptive.

The dominant register of the qualifying terms is somatic-inner: the grief vocabulary across both Testaments consistently holds physical and inner dimensions together. The somatic_link flag is true for 19 of the 27 terms, and in most cases the somatic connection is not merely incidental (tears, bodily exhaustion) but structural — the same word names both the physical and the inner condition (ka.av, ke.ev, cho.li, CHALAH root generally). This somatic-inner integration is more pronounced in the OT terms than in the NT terms, where the Greek vocabulary begins to make formal distinctions between body and mind pain, though it does not abandon the somatic register.

The single most analytically significant term in the full set is lu.pe (LUPE, Part 3), not because it has the most occurrences but because it is the only term in the word set that makes explicit an internal classification of grief by its source and outcome (2Cor 7:10). This classification — godly grief that produces repentance, worldly grief that produces death — is without formal parallel in any of the 21 OT terms, and it represents the clearest theological claim made about grief as an inner state within the entire 27-term set. A strong case can also be made for the CHALAH root (cha.lah + cho.li) as the most analytically significant root family for a different reason: it is the only cross-part root, it appears in the theologically central Isa 53 text in both its verb and noun forms, and its co-occurrence in that passage illuminates a dimension — grief caused by God, grief known and inhabited, grief vicariously borne — that no other single root family shows within a single text.
