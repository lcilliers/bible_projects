**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence explicitly attributes Joy to God across multiple registers. God is named as the one who produces and multiplies Israel's joy (Isa 9:3 — "you have increased its joy; they rejoice before you"); creates Jerusalem to be a joy and her people a gladness (Isa 65:18 — gi.lah); rejoices in his people's festive songs (Judg 9:13 — "wine that cheers God and men"); rejoices over his people with gladness (Zep 3:17 — sim.chah); rejoices in Jerusalem (Isa 65:19 — sa.mach); and rejoices over the repentant sinner (Luk 15:7 — "more joy in heaven"; Luk 15:10 — "joy before the angels of God"). In the Johannine corpus, Jesus has his own joy (Joh 15:11 — "my joy may be in you") and declares his joy fulfilled in the disciples (Joh 17:13). The evidence reveals God as a being whose inner life participates in Joy — he rejoices over his people, over restored covenant, and over recovered sinners. Joy is not merely a human response to God but a divine inner state that God himself inhabits and directs toward his creation.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, explicitly and extensively. Beyond the references in T0.1.1: God gives joy directly into the heart (Psa 4:7 — "you have put more joy in my heart"); makes people joyful (Ezr 6:22 — "the Lord had made them joyful"); is himself the source of fullness of joy (Psa 16:11 — "in your presence there is fullness of joy"); commissions Jesus to carry joy (Heb 12:2 — "for the joy that was set before him"); and God as the master whose joy the faithful servant enters (Mat 25:21, 23). The attribution reveals that Joy is not incidental or peripheral in God's inner life but is bound up with his covenantal relationship with his people, his redemptive purpose, and his own eschatological gladness at restoration. For the human person, this means Joy is not merely an emotional bonus but an image-bearing capacity — the person who rejoices genuinely participates in a mode of being that God himself inhabits.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Scripture is not silent — divine joy is extensively evidenced. The more significant silence is in the VCG-06 (inverted/corrupt/absent joy) register: God's withdrawal of joy from the community (Deu 28:47; Hos 2:11; Jer 7:34; 16:9; 25:10; Joe 1:16) is the silencing of what God himself possesses and intended — the removal of the community's joy is shown as the most complete act of covenantal judgment precisely because joy belongs to the life God designed. The absence of divine joy toward a people (Isa 9:17 — "the Lord does not rejoice over their young men") is itself a severe verdict. The evidence thus shows divine joy as the norm and its absence or withholding as the exception under judgment.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence reveals multiple created purposes: (1) **Alignment with God's reality** — joy is the appropriate inner response to divine sovereignty and goodness; the commanded feast-rejoicings (Deu 12:7, 12, 18; 16:11, 14, 15; 26:11; 27:7; Lev 23:40) establish rejoicing before God as the designed posture of the covenant community in God's presence; (2) **Relational completion** — Deu 24:5 (the husband exempted from military service to make his wife glad) shows that joy serves the completion of human relational bonds; Pro 10:1; 15:20; 23:24–25; 29:3 (wise children gladding parents) show joy functioning as the inner fruit of right-ordered human relationships; (3) **Communal cohesion** — M04-D (sunchairo) shows joy functioning as the mechanism of communal solidarity — the body that co-rejoices is the body that is bound together; (4) **Worship** — joy is the inner quality that animates and is the purpose of worship; Deu 28:47 makes failure to serve with joyfulness and gladness of heart the root cause of judgment, revealing joy as the designed inner disposition of the worshipper; (5) **Sustained engagement with life** — the Ecclesiastes evidence (Ecc 8:15; 9:7; 11:8–9) shows joy commended as the inner companion of mortal life across its whole span.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Both, with strong evidence for original design. The feast-commandments in Deuteronomy, Leviticus, and Numbers presuppose joy as the designed inner response of the covenant community before God. The cosmic-rejoicing passages (1Ch 16:31; Psa 96:11; 97:1) place joy as the appropriate response of all creation to divine sovereignty — not a fallen-condition remedy but the inherent response of the creature to the Creator's reign. The NT evidence adds the response-to-fallen-condition dimension: M04-C's emphasis on joy through suffering (Jas 1:2; 1Pe 1:6; 2Cor 6:10; Rom 5:3) and amid persecution (Mat 5:12; Luk 6:23) names joy specifically as the counter-intuitive inner posture required in the fallen world. The Ecclesiastes evidence (commending joy in the face of vanity and mortality) similarly frames it as the fitting inner posture amid creaturely limitation. Joy is both an original-design characteristic and one that takes particular forms in the fallen condition.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, strongly — this is one of Joy's most prominent features. M04-E-VCG-01 (OT restoration joy) is entirely forward-oriented: Isa 35:10 / 51:11 ("everlasting joy shall be upon their heads"); Isa 51:3 (joy and gladness found in restored Zion); Jer 31:13 (God turning mourning into joy); Jer 33:9, 11 (Jerusalem as a name of joy in restoration); Zec 8:19 (fasts becoming seasons of joy). M04-C-VCG-04 extends this: Joh 16:22 ("your hearts will rejoice, and no one will take your joy from you"); Rev 19:7 (commanded corporate rejoicing at the Lamb's marriage); Jude 24 (great joy at blameless presentation before God's glory); Heb 12:2 (the joy set before Jesus that sustained his cross). Within Characteristic 2 itself, fullness of joy is a recurring eschatological goal: Joh 15:11 ("that your joy may be full"); Joh 16:24 ("that your joy may be full"); 1Jo 1:4; 2Jo 12 (joy made complete). Present Joy is consistently experienced as partial and anticipatory.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Joy expresses the divine image as the human person's capacity to actively engage and participate in what is worth celebrating — a capacity that mirrors God's own inner rejoicing. The most direct image-evidence is the parallelism between God's joy over his people and the people's joy in God: God "makes them joyful" (Ezr 6:22); the people rejoice "in him" (Psa 40:16; 70:4; 86:4). The image-bearing quality is the directionality and the object-relatedness: just as God's joy is directed toward his people in covenant relationship, the human person's joy is designed to be directed toward God. The NT deepens this: Jesus' own joy is to be in the disciples (Joh 15:11; 17:13) — the transfer of Christ's joy into the human inner being is the fullest expression of Joy as an image-bearing capacity. Joy in the human person is the response-face of the same inner-state that God has toward them.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Genuinely shared. The same terms (sa.mach, sim.chah, chairo, chara) are used for both divine and human joy in the same passages. Psa 104:31 ("may the Lord rejoice in his works" — sa.mach) applies sa.mach to God. Jer 32:41 applies su.s to God. The NT adds: God rejoices over the repentant sinner (Luk 15:7, 10) using the exact same register as human rejoicing. The most decisive evidence is Joh 15:11 — "my joy may be in you": Christ's own joy (chara) is to indwell the disciples, making their joy a participation in divine joy rather than a mere analogy to it. The sharing is constitutive: Joy in the human person is described as the Lord's joy being present in them (Neh 8:10 — "the joy of the Lord is your strength").

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The inverted and absent joy evidence (M04-B-VCG-06, M04-C-VCG-05 corrupt elements) is the most diagnostic: (1) **Image corrupted** — Pro 2:14 (those who "rejoice in doing evil and delight in the perverseness of evil"); Eze 25:6 (Ammon's joy arising from "malice within your soul" at Israel's ruin); Eze 36:5 ("wholehearted joy and utter contempt" of those who seized God's land) — joy directed at evil, at others' ruin, or at one's own pride reveals the image severely distorted; (2) **Image silenced by sin** — Psa 51:8, 12 (David's joy silenced by his sin, requiring restoration through confession and forgiveness); the loss of joy is a diagnostic of sin's damage; (3) **Image emptied by judgment** — Deu 28:47 (failure to serve with joyfulness as the root of covenant curses); Joe 1:16 (joy cut off from God's house under judgment) — the absence of joy before God reveals the covenant relationship broken; (4) **Image functioning** — Psa 97:11 ("joy for the upright in heart") — the presence of genuine joy in the uprightly-oriented inner being signals the image in its designed operation.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, extensively. Several clusters: (1) **Feast joy as type of eschatological joy** — the commanded feast rejoicings (Deu 16:11, 14, 15; Lev 23:40) are the OT type of the eschatological feast (Rev 19:7; Mat 25:21, 23 — "enter into the joy of your master"); (2) **The lost-found parables** (Luk 15:6–7, 9–10) deploy joy typologically: the shepherd's joy over the recovered sheep is the type of heaven's joy over repentance; (3) **Abraham's joy** (Joh 8:56 — "your father Abraham rejoiced that he would see my day") frames OT faith as typological joy that Christ's day fulfils; (4) **The oil of gladness** (Psa 45:7; Heb 1:9) — the king's anointing with the oil of gladness is applied to Christ, making the royal joy of the OT king a type of Christ's pre-eminent anointed joy; (5) **M04-E's sa.s.von corpus** — the restoration joy promised (Isa 35:10; 51:11; 61:3) functions typologically as the OT anticipation of the NT and eschatological joy brought by Christ's work.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Both directions are evidenced: (1) **Human-to-divine / present-to-eschatological**: The OT feast joy points forward to the eschatological banquet; the OT restoration promises (sa.s.von) point to the NT gospel joy; Abraham's faith-joy points to Christ's day; (2) **Divine-to-human / heavenly-to-earthly**: Joh 15:11 and 17:13 establish the downward direction — Christ's own joy is transferred into the disciples as the pattern the human joy approximates. The master's joy (Mat 25:21, 23) sets the divine-relational joy as the destination into which the faithful servant enters. The two directions form a circular pattern: God's joy descends into the human person; human joy rises in response; both are oriented toward the eschatological fullness of the kingdom feast.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Typological use is extensively evidenced (see T0.4.1–T0.4.2).

## T1 — Definition and Structural Analysis

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic is called Joy. The name signals: (1) **Active engagement** — joy is participatory; it rejoices *in* something; it is directed and engaged, not a passive inner warmth; (2) **Inner-being scope** — the name is a recognisable inner-being state, distinct from Exultation (which surges), Gladness (which is warm and diffuse), and Pleasure (which is receptive). Joy as a name names the whole person's active rejoicing capacity; (3) **Communal resonance** — unlike Exultation (which is more individually intense), Joy has a communal dimension built into its common usage — one "rejoices with" others, and the commanded feast-joy of Deuteronomy is inherently social. The name correctly orients the enquiry toward an active, participatory, engageable inner state.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Primary terms across the four sub-groups:

**OT (M04-B, M04-E):** **sa.mach** (H8055 — to rejoice, be glad; verb; the dominant term with 147 instances in M04-B, appearing across every literary register); **sim.chah** (H8057 — joy, gladness; noun; 89 instances in M04-B; names the state that sa.mach produces); **sa.me.ach** (H8056 — joyful, glad; adjective; 21 instances in M04-B; describes the person in the state of joy); **ma.s.vo.s** (H4885 — joy, exultation, rejoicing; noun; 16 instances in M04-B; often associated with festive and communal contexts); **sa.s.von** (H8342 — gladness, joy, exultation; noun; 22 instances in M04-E; strongly restoration-oriented); **gi.lah** (H1525 — rejoicing, exultation; noun; 2 instances in M04-E); **ched.vah** (H2304 — joy, gladness; Aramaic/Hebrew; 2 instances in M04-C-VCG-01 and M04-D-VCG-01).

**NT (M04-C, M04-D, M04-E):** **chairo** (G5463 — to rejoice, be glad; verb; 58 instances in M04-C; the primary NT rejoicing verb); **chara** (G5479 — joy; noun; 57 instances in M04-C; names the state); **agalliao** (G0021 — to exult, rejoice greatly; verb; 11 instances in M04-E-VCG-02; a more intense exultant-joy term); **agalliasis** (G0020 — exultation, joy; noun; 4 instances in M04-C); **eufrosune** (G2167 — gladness, good cheer; 2 instances in M04-C); **sunchairo** (G4796 — to rejoice with, co-rejoice; verb; 8 instances in M04-D); **eucharistia/eucharistos** (G2169/2170 — thanksgiving/thankful; M04-C-VCG-06); **euthumeō/eupsucheō** (G2114/2174 — to be cheerful, encouraged; M04-C-VCG-04).

At the definitional level, the primary roots consistently name an inner state of positive, engaged, participatory gladness (sa.mach/chairo — active rejoicing; sim.chah/chara — the resulting inner state of joy). The Aramaic ched.vah and Greek eufrosune confirm the characteristic's cross-linguistic stability.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — All three. **Directional**: joy consistently requires an object — "rejoice in the Lord" (Deu 12:7; Psa 32:11; Phili 4:4); "rejoice at his birth" (Luk 1:14); "rejoice in hope" (Rom 12:12). The name implies directionality. **Relational**: the sunchairo corpus (M04-D) and the Pauline joy (1Th 2:19–20; Phili 4:1; 3Jo 3–4) show joy as constitutively relational — persons are described as the content of joy. The name carries the implication of joy-between-persons. **Constitutional**: the heart is the most frequently named constitutional locus across both OT and NT (Exo 4:14 — "glad in his heart"; Psa 4:7 — "more joy in my heart"; Psa 33:21 — "heart is glad in him"; Pro 15:13 — "glad heart makes a cheerful face"; Jer 15:16 — "joy and the delight of my heart"; Joh 16:22 — "your hearts will rejoice"; Song 3:11 — "gladness of his heart"). Joy is constitutionally located in the heart.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows Joy operating as all four, but primarily as a **disposition that generates acts**: (1) **Disposition** — "Rejoice in the Lord always" (Phili 4:4; 1Th 5:16) names joy as a standing inner orientation, not a momentary act; (2) **Act** — the commanded feast-rejoicings (Deu 16:11, 14; Lev 23:40) and the responsive rejoicings (Exo 4:14; 1Sa 6:13; Dan 6:23; Mat 28:8) are discrete inner-being acts; (3) **Condition** — "fullness of joy" (Psa 16:11; Joh 15:11; 16:24) names Joy as a state of being the person can inhabit fully or partially; (4) **Quality** — "joyful heart is good medicine" (Pro 17:22); "receiving food with glad and generous hearts" (Act 2:46) — joy as an inner quality that colours the whole of a person's engagement with life. The dominant register across the corpus is joy as a disposition that expresses itself through acts of rejoicing in specific circumstances while remaining the standing inner orientation of the covenant person.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Joy has multiple constituent elements evidenced across the 15 VCGs: (1) **Object-directedness** — a specific cause or object of joy is almost always named or implied (God's salvation, the Lord himself, the restored community, the gospel, the repentant sinner, the festival, the spouse, the child's wisdom); (2) **Volitional element** — the commanded nature of feast-joy (Deu) and NT commands ("Rejoice always" — 1Th 5:16; "Count it all joy" — Jas 1:2) show the will as a constituent; (3) **Affective element** — the heart and soul's gladness is the inner state itself; (4) **Communal element** — feast-joy is inherently social; sunchairo is inherently shared; Pauline joy is consistently relational; (5) **Expressive element** — joy consistently moves outward into singing, shouting, dancing, praise; (6) **Eschatological element** — sa.s.von and agalliao in M04-E show joy as forward-oriented toward restoration. Joy is structurally complex but coherent — these elements are not in tension but constitute the full inner-being experience of the characteristic.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Joy is the active inner experience of rejoicing — a volitional-affective inner state that engages the whole person (heart, soul, whole being) in response to what is genuinely good and worth celebrating. It is constitutionally located primarily in the heart, expressed outwardly through singing and shout, directed toward a specific object (God himself, restored relationship, the gospel, the beloved community), cultivated as a standing inner disposition, capable of transcending adverse circumstances, and oriented toward an eschatological fullness in which all partial present joy is completed. It is distinguished from Exultation by less surging intensity and broader scope of occasions; from Gladness by its active-engaged rather than warm-diffuse character; from Delight by its orientation toward celebration and participation rather than treasuring; from Pleasure by its communal and directed rather than receptive character.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Multiple structural opposites are named across the corpus: (1) **Mourning** — Jer 31:13 ("turn mourning into joy"); Isa 61:3 ("oil of gladness instead of mourning"); Psa 30:11 ("turned my mourning into dancing"); (2) **Sorrow/grief** — Joh 16:20 ("you will be sorrowful, but your sorrow will turn into joy"); Jas 4:9 ("let your joy be turned to gloom"); (3) **Sighing** — Isa 35:10; 51:11 ("sorrow and sighing shall flee away" as joy arrives); (4) **Weeping and lamentation** — Joh 16:20 ("you will weep and lament, but the world will rejoice"); (5) **Groaning** — Heb 13:17 (leaders doing work "with joy and not with groaning"); (6) **Fear/dread** — Joe 2:21–23 (fear displaced by joy through God's acts). The structural opposite of Joy is the cluster of mourning-grief-sighing-weeping that Joy displaces and replaces, most fully in the eschatological reversal passages.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Joy explicitly excludes or resists: (1) **Rejoicing at evil** — 1Cor 13:6 ("love does not rejoice at wrongdoing"); Pro 24:17 ("do not rejoice when your enemy falls"); Job 31:29 (Job denying he rejoiced at enemy's ruin); (2) **Hollow festivity without covenant ground** — Isa 22:13 ("let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die" — reckless joy-without-substance condemned); Ecc 2:1–2 (pleasure tested and found vain); (3) **Illegitimate joy** — Hos 9:1 ("rejoice not, O Israel, for you have played the whore"); (4) **Premature joy** — Isa 14:29 (Philistia warned not to rejoice at the broken rod); (5) **Self-focused delight** — Luk 10:20 (Jesus redirecting joy from power over spirits to the deeper ground of names written in heaven); (6) **Shallow surface-level joy** — Mat 13:20; Mar 4:16; Luk 8:13 (rocky-ground hearer receives word with joy but lacks root). Joy resists being rooted in wrong objects, shallow grounds, or morally corrupted directions.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Joy is not: (1) **Gladness** (Characteristic 3) — Gladness is the warm, settled, positive inner brightness; Joy is more active, directed, participatory, and commands engagement. The two co-occur frequently (Psa 51:8 — "joy and gladness"; Isa 35:10 — "gladness and joy") but are not identical. Gladness is the inner state of one who is well; Joy is the inner state of one actively rejoicing. (2) **Exultation** (Characteristic 1) — Exultation is more intense and surging, specifically Godward. Joy is broader in its occasions and less exclusively upward-directed. (3) **Delight** (Characteristic 4) — Delight is the receptive inner state of treasuring a specific object; Joy is the active state of rejoicing in what is celebratable. Delight rests in its object; Joy engages with it. (4) **Pleasure** (Characteristic 5) — Pleasure is the inner-being's receptive agreeableness; Joy is engaged and participatory. (5) **Thanksgiving** — the eucharistia sub-corpus (M04-C-VCG-06) within this characteristic is adjacent to Joy but specifically concerns Godward gratitude; thanksgiving is an act of the will directed Godward; Joy is the inner state that thanksgiving often accompanies and expresses.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Five distinct modes: (1) **Spontaneous responsive joy** — Aaron's heart-gladness at seeing Moses (Exo 4:14); the people of Beth-shemesh at seeing the ark (1Sa 6:13); the disciples at seeing the risen Lord (Joh 20:20) — immediate inner response triggered by a direct encounter; (2) **Commanded and cultivated joy** — Deuteronomic feast-joy; "Rejoice in the Lord always" (Phili 4:4); "Count it all joy" (Jas 1:2) — joy directed and sustained by volitional practice; (3) **Spirit-produced joy** — "filled with joy and with the Holy Spirit" (Act 13:52); "joy of the Holy Spirit" (1Th 1:6); joy as the Spirit's fruit (Gal 5:22) — joy as a directly Spirit-imparted inner state; (4) **Paradoxical suffering-joy** — "sorrowful, yet always rejoicing" (2Cor 6:10); joy in affliction (2Cor 8:2); co-rejoicing in Paul's imprisonment (Phili 2:17–18) — joy co-existing with and transcending suffering; (5) **Relational co-joy** — sunchairo throughout M04-D; Paul's joy over specific communities (1Th 2:19–20; 3Jo 4) — joy produced by and existing in the bonds between persons.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes. (1) **By context**: Festival/feast contexts produce a communal-structured joy (M04-B-VCG-01); personal narrative contexts produce a spontaneous-responsive joy (M04-B-VCG-04); persecution contexts produce a counter-intuitive commanded joy (M04-C-VCG-05); restoration contexts produce a forward-oriented promised joy (M04-E-VCG-01). (2) **By direction**: Joy directed at God is the normative and most sustained form (M04-B-VCG-05; M04-C-VCG-03); joy directed at persons (M04-B-VCG-04; M04-D) has a different character — more relational and completable; joy directed wrongly (M04-B-VCG-06) is morally condemned. (3) **By constitutional level**: The heart is the primary seat across all registers; the soul (Luk 1:47 — Mary's spirit/soul rejoices; M04-E-VCG-02 agalliao passages) is named in more intense or Spirit-mediated registers; the whole being (Psa 16:9) appears in sustained-joy states.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, prominently. The sim.chah/sa.mach register consistently moves into voice: Neh 12:27–43 (gladness expressed through singing, cymbals, harps, lyres); 1Ch 15:16 (David appointing Levites to "raise sounds of joy"); Jer 31:7 (singing aloud with gladness for Jacob); Joel 2:21–23 (rejoicing paired with praise). In the NT: chairo naturally moves into thanksgiving and praise (Act 13:48 — "began rejoicing and glorifying the word of the Lord"); Phili 4:4's commanded rejoicing is paired with prayer (4:6). In M04-E-VCG-02: Mary's spirit rejoices and her mouth proclaims (Luk 1:47 → 1:46 Magnificat); Act 2:26 — "my heart was glad, and my tongue rejoiced." Joy is inherently communicative — it moves from inner state to voiced expression as its natural overflow.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence across the corpus shows the immediate inner response to the occasion of joy is the arising of gladness in the heart before its expression outward: Exo 4:14 (Aaron "will be glad in his heart" — gladness in the heart is the immediate response); 1Ki 8:66 (people go home "joyful and glad of heart" — the gladness is located in the heart as the first response to what they experienced); Psa 4:7 (God has "put more joy in my heart" — the inner reception is the primary event); Act 12:14 (Rhoda's "joy" at recognising Peter's voice is so immediate it overrides action — she runs in without opening the gate). The heart's gladness is the first movement, before voice or action.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Consistent in kind, varied in intensity and occasion. The heart-gladness response is universal across the corpus. What varies is: (1) Intensity — from the overwhelming joy that causes Rhoda to forget to open the gate (Act 12:14) to the quiet gladness of Solomon's people eating and drinking (1Ki 4:20); (2) Occasion — from cosmic events (the Lord's reign calling the earth to rejoice) to personal moments (Aaron's heart glad at seeing his brother); (3) Presence of volition — some responses are spontaneous (Exo 4:14) and others are commanded and require volitional engagement (Deu 16:11; Phili 4:4).

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — M04-E's sa.s.von corpus is largely future-oriented and thus describes promised joy rather than immediate inner response. Similarly, the VCG-06 (inverted/absent joy) corpus by definition describes the absence or distortion of the immediate response rather than its presence. The inverted joy passages (Pro 2:14; Eze 25:6) do show the immediate inner response to wrong objects — but they are morally condemned precisely because of the wrongness of the object rather than the wrongness of the inner response mechanism itself.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The sustained-joy evidence produces: (1) **Strength** — Neh 8:10 ("the joy of the Lord is your strength") — sustained Godward joy produces inner resilience; (2) **Health** — Pro 17:22 ("a joyful heart is good medicine") — the joyful heart has a medicinal effect on the inner being; (3) **Generosity** — 2Cor 8:2 (joy in severe affliction and extreme poverty overflowing into generous giving) — sustained joy expands the person's giving capacity; (4) **Stability in suffering** — 2Cor 6:10; Col 1:11; Phili 2:17 — sustained joy enables endurance; (5) **Prayer and worship** — the commanded feast-joy (Deu 16:11; Neh 8:12) over time forms the person into a worshipper; sustained joy produces and sustains the inner posture of worship; (6) **Completeness** — 2Jo 12; 1Jo 1:4; Joh 15:11; 16:24 — sustained joy in community moves toward fullness; it grows toward completion.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained joy establish?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Primary terms with root meanings: (1) **sa.mach** (H8055) — to rejoice, be glad; the verbal form used 147 times in M04-B; root meaning connotes brightness and happiness; the causative (hiphil) form frequently shows God as the one who makes people joyful; (2) **sim.chah** (H8057) — joy, gladness, mirth; nominal form, 89 instances in M04-B; the noun names the state that sa.mach produces; (3) **sa.me.ach** (H8056) — joyful, glad; adjectival form; the person characterised by the state; (4) **ma.s.vo.s** (H4885) — joy, exultation, rejoicing; nominal form; associated with festive, communal, bodily-expressive joy; (5) **sa.s.von** (H8342) — gladness, joy, exultation; 22 instances in M04-E; strongly restoration-linked; (6) **chairo** (G5463) — to rejoice, be glad; the primary NT rejoicing verb; 58 instances in M04-C; (7) **chara** (G5479) — joy; noun; 57 instances in M04-C; (8) **agalliao** (G0021) — to exult, rejoice greatly; 11 instances in M04-E-VCG-02; roots carry the sense of leaping or bounding exultation; (9) **sunchairo** (G4796) — to rejoice with, co-rejoice; M04-D; the syn- prefix makes shared participation constitutive; (10) **eufrosune** (G2167) — gladness, good cheer; 2 instances in M04-C.

Root-level summary: The Hebrew roots consistently connote brightness, inner gladness, and outward expression; the Greek roots connote active rejoicing (chairo), the resulting state (chara), and intensified leaping-joy (agalliao). The shared thread across all terms is an active, positive, inner-being brightening that moves outward.

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **sa.mach** (H8055) is exclusively verbal — it always names an act of rejoicing, not a condition or quality. Its dominance (147 verbal instances) over sim.chah (89 nominal) reveals that Joy is fundamentally an act before it is a condition — the grammar of the Hebrew primary term names the characteristic as a dynamic event (something happens) rather than a static state (something is). **chairo** (G5463, 58 instances) is similarly verbal. **chara** (G5479, 57 instances) is the corresponding nominal form — the state that chairo produces. The near-equal frequency of verbal and nominal forms in the NT (58 vs. 57) suggests that Joy operates equally as act and state in the NT register. **sa.me.ach** (H8056, 21 adjectival instances) names the person defined by the state — the one who habitually is glad. Together the three grammatical forms (verb + noun + adjective) show Joy as: an event (sa.mach/chairo), a state (sim.chah/chara), and a character quality (sa.me.ach) — a fully developed inner-being characteristic across all grammatical registers.

---

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **sa.mach** ranges across: (1) prescribed feast-joy (Deu 12:7; 16:11–15 — commanded worship context); (2) responsive-spontaneous joy (Exo 4:14 — heart-gladness; 1Sa 6:13 — joy at seeing the ark); (3) relational/parental joy (Pro 10:1; 23:24–25 — father's joy at wise son); (4) God-directed psalmist joy (Psa 5:11; 9:2; 14:7; 32:11; 40:16 — rejoice in the Lord); (5) inverted/corrupt joy (Pro 2:14; Eze 25:6; Hos 9:1; Jon 4:6 — rejoicing at evil or wrongly-directed); (6) God himself rejoicing (Isa 65:19; Psa 104:31; Jer 32:41 — divine joy). The range is broader than any other term in M04 — sa.mach covers every register of Joy from prescribed worship to personal gladness to divine inner life to corrupt misdirection.

---

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes: (1) **sa.mach** (act) vs. **sim.chah** (state) vs. **sa.me.ach** (character) — grammatical distinctions between the event, the condition, and the person; (2) **sa.mach** (general Joy-act) vs. **agalliao** (intensified exultant Joy) vs. **sunchairo** (co-Joy) — the Greek terms distinguish degree of intensity and whether the Joy is solitary or shared; (3) **chairo** (act) vs. **chara** (state) — NT parallel of the OT sa.mach/sim.chah distinction; (4) **sa.s.von** (restoration-Joy, forward-oriented) vs. **sa.mach** (present Joy) — distinct sub-registers of the OT joy vocabulary.

---

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of Joy?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, by negation and by antonym: (1) **By negation** — "the Lord does not rejoice" (Isa 9:17 — sa.mach negated); "rejoice not" (Hos 9:1 — sa.mach prohibited); "let not your heart be glad" (Pro 24:17); (2) **By antonym** — mourning (Psa 30:11 — sackcloth removed; dancing given); sorrow (Joh 16:20 — sorrow → Joy); sighing (Isa 35:10 — sighing flees at Joy's arrival); grief (1Pe 1:6 — grief alongside Joy); darkness (Isa 24:11 — "all joy has grown dark"). The vocabulary does not have a single dedicated antonym term for Joy but uses the cluster of mourning/sorrow/sighing as its structural opposite across the corpus.

---

**T7.1.6 — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes: **sa.me.ach** (H8056, 21 instances) — the adjectival form naming the joyful/glad person as a type: 1Ki 4:20 ("Judah and Israel were… sa.me.ach"); 1Ki 1:40 ("rejoicing with great joy"); Pro 15:13 ("glad heart"); Isa 24:7 ("merry-hearted"). The NT equivalent is the one who "rejoices in the Lord" as a standing posture (Phili 4:4's commanded "rejoice always" produces a person-type: the one who habitually rejoices in the Lord).

---

**T7.1.7 — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes: Psa 51:12 ("restore to me the joy of your salvation") — the petitionary use of sim.chah (requesting the restoration of lost Joy); Psa 86:4 ("gladden the soul of your servant") — a direct petition for God to produce Joy; Psa 90:14–15 ("that we may rejoice and be glad all our days… make us glad for as many days as you have afflicted us") — intercession for Joy. These confirm Joy is something that can be sought from God as a gift.

---

**T7.1.8 — What does the relationship between OT Hebrew vocabulary and NT Greek vocabulary reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The OT-NT relationship shows both continuity and expansion: (1) **Continuity** — chairo/chara (NT) perform the same function as sa.mach/sim.chah (OT) — naming the inner-being act and state of rejoicing; the NT maintains the characteristic's essential character as active, directed, and heart-located; (2) **Expansion in the Spirit-register** — the NT adds the Spirit as a constitutive agent of Joy (Gal 5:22; 1Th 1:6; Rom 14:17) — a register absent from the OT's primary Joy vocabulary; (3) **Agalliao as bridge** — agalliao (M04-E-VCG-02) appears in both OT quotation contexts (Act 2:26 quoting Psa 16:9 with agalliao replacing sa.mach) and NT-original contexts (1Pe 1:8; Rev 19:7) — showing continuity of the intense-exultant register across testaments; (4) **Sunchairo as NT innovation** — the co-rejoicing term (M04-D) appears only in NT; the OT has communal joy but not the explicit shared-participation term.

---

**T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **Sunchairo** (G4796) is the most distinctive NT coinage for Joy — the syn- prefix making shared participation structurally embedded in the term itself rather than contextually implied. **Agalliasis** (G0020, the noun form of agalliao) also functions distinctively in NT contexts (Luk 1:14; Act 2:46; Jude 24) — it names an intense, overflowing gladness that is not precisely represented in the OT vocabulary. These coinages reveal that the NT extends Joy into the explicitly relational-shared domain (sunchairo) and into the intensified-eschatological domain (agalliasis).

---

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The full vocabulary arc across sa.mach/sim.chah (OT primary), sa.s.von (OT restoration), ma.s.vo.s (OT festive-communal), agalliao/agalliasis (eschatological-intense), chairo/chara (NT primary), sunchairo (NT co-joy), eucharistia (NT thanksgiving within Joy), and euthumeō (NT cheerfulness) reveals Joy as a characteristic with: (a) a stable core (active inner rejoicing in response to divine reality — carried by sa.mach/chairo across both testaments); (b) a festive-communal register (ma.s.vo.s; sunchairo — the social form of Joy); (c) an eschatological-restoration register (sa.s.von; agalliao — Joy oriented toward the not-yet); (d) a Spirit-mediated register (NT additions: the Spirit as Joy-agent); (e) an adjacent thanksgiving register (eucharistia — the Godward-gratitude face of Joy). The arc shows Joy as semantically rich, multi-registered, and consistently oriented across all registers toward genuine participation in what is celebratable before God.

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — In Deu 16:11 ("And you shall rejoice before the Lord your God"), sa.mach functions as the **main predicate of a second-person command** — it is the central imperative of the verse, making Joy the required inner act of covenant participation before God. The "before the Lord your God" prepositional phrase gives the term its constitutive object and direction. The grammatical weight of the term as command-predicate across the Deuteronomic feast-corpus shows Joy as the defining inner act that covenant worship requires.

In Phili 4:4 ("Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice") — chairo functions as a repeated imperative with the unique intensification of double repetition ("I will say it again") — showing Joy's commanded standing as so fundamental that it requires insistence.

---

**T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence — and what does that form require?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Multiple literary forms carry the evidence, each requiring different interpretive care: (1) **Command / Legal-liturgical** (Deu 12:7; 16:11; Lev 23:40) — requires reading the commands as genuine formative directives, not mere regulations; Joy is prescribed because it is a necessary inner posture; (2) **Lament Psalm** (Psa 30; 51; 90; 126) — the Joy within lament is particularly significant: it is requested, anticipated, or recovered after mourning — reading Joy in lament requires attention to the journey from grief to gladness; (3) **Wisdom literature** (Pro; Ecc) — requires attention to the Ecclesiastes examination of joy's limits and commendation alongside wisdom's cautions; (4) **Prophetic** (Isa 35; 51; 55; 61; Jer 31; 33; Zec 8) — requires eschatological reading; the prophetic joy-promises are conditional on restoration and are not yet fully realised; (5) **NT Epistle** (Phili; 2Cor; 1Th; Jas) — requires situational reading (Paul's Joy in imprisonment, Paul's joy amid community conflict) that contextualises the commands.

---

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Several key argument structures: (1) **Because God has done X, therefore rejoice** — Deu 16:11 (because God has blessed you); Psa 126:3 (because God has done great things for us → we are glad); Joe 2:23 (because God has given rain → rejoice); (2) **Rejoice because your reward is eschatological, not present** — Mat 5:12 (rejoice under persecution because your reward is great in heaven — the Joy is grounded in an eschatological reality, not the present circumstances); (3) **Joy is the goal of divine action** — Psa 85:6 (revive us → that your people may rejoice in you — Joy as the telos of revival); (4) **My joy is your joy** — Joh 15:11; 17:13 — the argument that Christ's own Joy is intended to be fully in the disciples; (5) **Count trials as Joy** — Jas 1:2 — the argument that trials, when rightly evaluated, yield Joy (through the tested faith → endurance → completeness chain).

---

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence — and what does that setting reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — (1) **Liturgical/festal** — M04-B-VCG-01 (feast-commandments) and M04-B-VCG-02 (sanctuary joy) reveal Joy as constitutive of Israel's covenant worship; joy belongs to the sanctuary encounter as its designed inner climate; (2) **Covenantal** — the Deuteronomic and prophetic settings (Deu 28:47; Jer 7:34; 31:13) reveal Joy as a covenantal diagnostic — its presence indicates covenant health, its absence indicates covenant failure; (3) **Apostolic pastoral** — M04-C-VCG-03 (Pauline relational and community joy) reveals Joy as the inner climate of the apostolic community; Paul's letters breathe Joy as the characteristic inner tone of the healthy community; (4) **Eschatological** — M04-E (sa.s.von; agalliao) and M04-C-VCG-04 (constant Joy) reveal Joy as eschatologically anchored — its fullest expressions are anticipations of the coming kingdom and marriage of the Lamb.

---

**T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Several candidates: (1) **Phili 4:4** — "Rejoice in the Lord always; again I will say, rejoice" — the most concentrated NT expression of Joy as a commanded, standing, unconditional inner disposition directed at its proper object (the Lord). The double repetition signals this as the characteristic's defining command; (2) **Deu 16:11** — "And you shall rejoice before the Lord your God" — the OT feast-commandment that establishes Joy as Israel's fundamental covenant posture; (3) **Psa 16:11** — "in your presence there is fullness of joy" — the verse that names Joy's eschatological maximum and its constitutive source (God's presence). If one anchor is to be chosen, **Phili 4:4** is the candidate for the NT register and **Psa 16:11** for the OT register's deepest statement.

---

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **Phili 4:4** uniquely reveals: (1) Joy as a standing, unconditional commanded posture — "always" removes any circumstantial qualification; (2) The double-command structure — "again I will say, rejoice" signals that this is the characteristic most needing insistence; Joy is the inner-being state most at risk of being conditional, so it requires the most emphatic command; (3) The Lord as the constitutive object — "in the Lord" is not circumstantially dependent; the command is to rejoice *in* God regardless of what is happening outside. **Psa 16:11** uniquely reveals that Joy reaches its maximum not in circumstances but in God's presence itself — "fullness of joy" is a statement about Joy's maximum capacity, which is found only in the direct encounter with God. No other verse names Joy's ceiling as unambiguously: the fullness of Joy is God's presence.

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **Positive psychology** is the primary useful lens, particularly: (1) Barbara Fredrickson's broaden-and-build theory — positive emotions (joy, love, contentment) broaden momentary awareness and build lasting personal resources; this maps onto Joy's evidence of building strength (Neh 8:10), generosity (2Cor 8:2), and resilience; (2) **Flow theory** (Csikszentmihalyi) — the state of full engagement with a valued activity; the feast-joy passages show a similar full-engagement quality of the whole community participating in worship; (3) **Wellbeing and hedonia/eudaimonia distinction** — the Ecclesiastes evidence (joy commended even under vanity) maps onto the eudaimonic dimension of wellbeing (life-meaning and engagement) rather than the hedonic (pleasure-seeking); (4) **Attachment theory** — the relational dimension of Joy (M04-D; M04-C-VCG-03) maps onto the secure-attachment register: persons who experience Joy in the context of secure relational bonds.

---

**T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Fredrickson's broaden-and-build directly illuminates the feast-joy formation logic: the prescribed feast-joy (Deu 16:11–15) broadens communal awareness and builds lasting inner resources (covenant faithfulness, community cohesion, sustained orientation toward God) in precisely the way the theory predicts positive emotions function. The evidence that Joy produces strength (Neh 8:10), generosity (2Cor 8:2), and continued proclamation (Act 8:39; 13:48) is fully consistent with the building function of positive emotions. The theory also illuminates why Joy under suffering (2Cor 8:2; 1Pe 1:6) produces more lasting resources than Joy in comfortable circumstances — it is the characteristic that, when grounded in the Spirit's work rather than circumstances, most fully demonstrates the broaden-build dynamic.

---

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The most significant divergence: (1) **Unconditional Joy** — Phili 4:4's "Rejoice always" and 1Th 5:16's "Rejoice always" command an inner disposition that positive psychology would not expect to be achievable or sustainable; the human-science framework predicts that Joy is circumstance-responsive; the command to Joy regardless of circumstances points to a theological grounding (in God as Joy's object) that makes Joy independent of the stimulus-response patterns positive psychology describes; (2) **Joy in suffering as a primary register** — positive psychology frames positive emotions as reduced by negative experiences; Characteristic 2 includes Joy in affliction (2Cor 8:2; 1Pe 1:6; Jas 1:2) as one of its defining expressions; the divergence reveals that Joy grounded in eschatological promise and Spirit-presence operates outside the stimulus-response model; (3) **Joy as commanded** — positive psychology treats positive emotions as products of circumstances or conditions; the command to Joy reveals that the Scripture's understanding includes Joy as a volitional act, not only an emotional product.

---

**T7.3.4 — Does the human science framework surface any aspect not addressed by the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The distinction between **hedonic** (pleasure-focused) and **eudaimonic** (meaning-and-flourishing-focused) wellbeing is useful: the Ecclesiastes evidence (commending Joy while acknowledging vanity) is more naturally eudaimonic than hedonic; Joy here is recommended as the fitting inner companion of mortal life rather than as pleasure-maximisation. The verse evidence does not use these categories but the human science distinction helps clarify what Ecclesiastes is doing — it is commending eudaimonic Joy rather than hedonic pleasure-seeking, which is what the Preacher rejects (Ecc 2:1–2). This surfacing does not require further verse investigation but provides a conceptual clarification useful for Session C output.
