## T7 — Lexical, Literary, and Human Science Analysis

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Primary terms: (1) **tov** (H2896A) — noun, adjective, and evaluative particle; the core goodness-root. Root meaning: pleasant, good, beautiful, beneficial; in its evaluative use it names the quality of what satisfies a standard of genuine excellence. 133 instances across M04-L (83) and M04-O (50). The root generates the noun tov (goodness, good thing), the adjective tov (good, better, best), and the verbal idiom "ki-tov" (for he is good) that forms the liturgical refrain; (2) **makarismos** (G3108) — blessing, blessedness; 3 NT instances in M04-O-VCG-04 (Rom 4:6, 9; Gal 4:15). Root: makarios (blessed, happy in divine favour); makarismos names the state of being declared blessed by divine pronouncement; (3) **sa.s.von** (H8342) — gladness, exultation, joy (22 instances in the overlapping M04-E-VCG-01 corpus that serves both Characteristics 2 and 3). Root related to the concept of brightness and joyful gladness; (4) The **ki-tov formula** — not a single term but a liturgical construction ("for he is good, for his steadfast love endures forever") functioning as a fixed doxological expression whose repetition across Psalms 100, 106, 107, 118, 135, 136 makes it a structural feature of Gladness's primary register.

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — **tov** operates across three grammatical registers in this corpus: (1) **Adjectival / predicative** — "God saw that it was good" (Gen 1:4–31); "the Lord is good" (Psa 25:8; 100:5; Nah 1:7) — predicate adjective naming a stable quality; (2) **Comparative** — "better than" forms (Rut 4:15; 2Sa 18:3; Pro 8:11; 16:16; 17:1 — various) — the comparative form "tov min" (good from) names surpassing worth; (3) **Negated / substantive** — "not good" (Exo 18:17; 1Sa 2:24; Neh 5:9; Pro 17:26; Eze 18:18) — tov negated to name moral deficiency. The grammatical range reveals: Gladness is not primarily a verbal action (unlike Joy's dominant sa.mach verb) but a quality-assertion — it predicates goodness of persons, actions, circumstances, and God's character. The predicate form is the primary grammatical mode, confirming Gladness's status as a quality/condition rather than an act.

---

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — tov's semantic range is the widest of any M04 primary term: (1) **Divine character** — "the Lord is good" (M04-L-VCG-01 — intrinsic divine attribute); (2) **Moral quality** — "a good man" (M04-L-VCG-02 — human moral character); (3) **Evaluative judgment** — "better than" (M04-L-VCG-02, VCG-03 — comparative moral or wisdom assessments); (4) **Circumstantial benefit** — "good news," "it will be well with you," "good land" (M04-O — situational goodness); (5) **Covenantal promise-quality** — "good promises," "good hand" (M04-O-VCG-04 — divine reliable provision); (6) **Wellbeing** — "Saul was well" (1Sa 16:23 — experiential inner wellbeing). This breadth confirms tov as the programme's most foundational positive-quality term.

---

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes: (1) **tov** (predicative — quality/condition) vs. **tov-min** (comparative — surpassing worth); (2) **lo-tov** (negated tov — moral deficiency and its condemnation: Exo 18:17; Neh 5:9; Pro 17:26 etc.) — the structural-opposite form built into the primary term; (3) **tov-lev** (compound: good-of-heart — Est 5:9's "glad of heart" — M04-B-VCG-04, cross-assigned to Gladness's relational-personal register: the inner-being compound confirming heart-location of circumstantial gladness); (4) **makarismos** (G3108 — the NT blessedness form, distinguishing declarative-status-gladness from experiential-circumstantial gladness).

---

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of Gladness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes: **lo-tov** (negated tov — "not good") appears 15+ times in M04-L-VCG-02 and M04-O contexts (Exo 18:17; 1Sa 2:24; 2Sa 17:7; Neh 5:9; Pro 17:26; 18:5; 20:23; 24:23; 28:21; Eze 18:18; 20:25; 36:31; Isa 65:2) — the structural opposite is built into the primary term's own negation. This confirms that Gladness is constitutively an evaluative characteristic — its presence and absence are measured by the same standard.

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — In Mic 6:8 ("He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"), **tov** functions as the **object of divine declaration** — "what is good" names the content God has revealed. The term is the syntactic and semantic centre around which the verse's three requirements cluster as its definition. God's declaration establishes the good as a known, revealed reality — not a matter of human invention or preference — and the human person's task is to recognise it (evaluative Gladness) and live it (conscientiousness). The term's function as the object of divine declaration confirms Gladness's cognitive-receptive character: goodness is first known, then lived.

**T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Key argument structures: (1) **Because God is good, therefore praise** — ki-tov formula (Psa 100:5; 106:1; 136:1) — the logical structure is character → commanded response; (2) **A is better than B because A has quality X** — wisdom-comparative form (Pro 16:8 — a little with righteousness better than great revenue with injustice; Pro 8:11 — wisdom better than jewels) — the argument evaluates competing goods against a standard of genuine value; (3) **This conduct is not good, therefore it is condemned** — the lo-tov verdicts (Pro 17:26; 18:5 etc.) — the standard of goodness is the premise; the falling-short is the condemnation; (4) **God's good promises have not failed, therefore trust** — Jos 21:45 — the historical evidence of promise-fulfilment grounds present and future trust; (5) **Blessedness is declared to the one who has faith counted as righteousness** — makarismos argument (Rom 4:6–9) — declarative logic: status is assigned by divine declaration, not achieved.

**T7.2.4 — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence?**

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — **Mic 6:8** uniquely reveals that Gladness in its evaluative face is not a human construction or preference but **divinely declared content** — "he has told you what is good." The good is known because God has spoken it; the human evaluative faculty's proper operation is not autonomous but responsive to divine declaration. No other verse states so explicitly that the content of genuine goodness is known because God has declared it. **Gen 1:31** uniquely reveals that Gladness originates in a divine inner-being act (God's evaluative delight in his own creation) that precedes and grounds all human evaluative-gladness — the "very good" at creation is the ontological baseline from which all departures (the "not good" verdicts) and all returns (the ki-tov praise declarations) are measured.

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for Gladness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Two frameworks are useful: (1) **Moral philosophy / virtue ethics** — particularly Aristotle's concept of eudaimonia (flourishing-wellbeing as the good life for the human person) maps onto M04-O's wellbeing-gladness register ("it is well with the man who deals generously" — Psa 112:5; Lam 3:26–27 — the good of bearing the yoke). The evaluative question "what is good for the human person?" is Aristotle's question and Gladness's question; (2) **Cognitive appraisal theory** (Lazarus and others) — the psychological model in which emotion is constituted by cognitive appraisal of a situation maps directly onto Gladness's evaluative-cognitive structure: Gladness arises from appraising a situation/person/circumstance as genuinely good. This is the human-science analogue of Gladness's tov-evaluation.

---

**T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Cognitive appraisal theory illuminates why Gladness varies so much by circumstance and person: the same circumstance produces different Gladness-responses in different people (Num 14:3 — Israel evaluates the land as dangerous; Caleb and Joshua evaluate it as genuinely good) because their cognitive appraisals differ. The theory explains that forming the evaluative faculty (wisdom-formation in M04-L-VCG-02, 03) is fundamentally the same as forming the cognitive appraisal patterns that produce appropriate Gladness-responses. Virtue ethics illuminates Lam 3:26–27 (it is genuinely good — eudaimonic good — to bear the yoke in youth) by providing the framework for understanding why discipline and adversity are genuinely beneficial, not merely instrumentally useful.

---

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The most significant divergence: cognitive appraisal theory treats the standard of goodness as constructed or culturally relative — "good" is what the appraisal-system has learned to value.
