## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Two primary evidences for the divine dimension of Suffering-Joy: (1) **Jesus' agalliao in the Holy Spirit** — Luk 10:21 ("In that same hour he rejoiced in the Holy Spirit and said, 'I thank you, Father...'") — this is the clearest instance of the characteristic operating in the divine-human person; Jesus' exultation occurs in the context of the disciples having been exposed to opposition and the suffering of itinerant ministry; his Spirit-located rejoicing is not despite the context of difficulty but in and through it; (2) **Heb 12:2** — "for the joy that was set before him [he] endured the cross, despising the shame" — the cross is the suffering context; the joy set before Christ is the eschatological ground that enabled endurance of suffering. Together these reveal God's own inner life as encompassing the capacity to hold joy and suffering together without the suffering cancelling the joy. The characteristic reflects the divine image as the capacity to inhabit genuine rejoicing when circumstances press against it, because the inner being is grounded in a reality that transcends circumstances.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes, most fully in the person of Christ. Heb 12:2 names the joy set before Christ as the ground of his endurance of the cross — the divine-human person exercises Suffering-Joy in its purest form: suffering as extreme as crucifixion held alongside and through joy set before him. Luk 10:21's Jesus rejoicing in the Holy Spirit acknowledges the hiddenness of kingdom realities from the world (a form of the disciples' vulnerability/suffering in the mission context) while exulting. The attribution reveals: Suffering-Joy is not merely a human coping mechanism for unfortunate circumstances but participates in the divine inner life as Christ exercised it — the capacity to hold eschatological joy as more real and determinative than present suffering. For the human person, Suffering-Joy is an image-bearing participation in Christ's own suffering-to-glory pattern.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The silence in the M04-E-VCG-01 sa.s.von restoration corpus (which is primarily about God reversing suffering rather than God experiencing suffering-with-joy) is analytically significant: the OT does not primarily present God as experiencing suffering-that-he-rejoices-through; rather, God is the one who reverses suffering. The characteristic in its paradoxical form (joy through suffering, not merely after it) emerges most fully in the NT through Christ's incarnate suffering and the Spirit's enabling of the church under persecution. This is not silence about the characteristic's divine grounding but about its location — Suffering-Joy's divine archetype is the incarnate Christ, not the transcendent Creator.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The evidence presents Suffering-Joy as primarily a **response-to-fallen-condition** capacity rather than an original-creation capacity (see T0.2.2) — it equips the person to: (1) **Maintain inner-being coherence under persecution** — Mat 5:12 ("rejoice and be glad, for your reward is great in heaven") equips the persecuted to sustain their inner orientation despite suffering; (2) **Participate in Christ's own suffering-to-glory pattern** — 1Pe 4:13 ("rejoice insofar as you share Christ's sufferings, that you may also rejoice and be glad when his glory is revealed") — Suffering-Joy equips the believer to enter the same suffering-glory trajectory Christ entered; (3) **Produce proven character** — Jas 1:2 ("count it all joy when you meet trials of various kinds") frames Suffering-Joy as the inner faculty that enables the trials to accomplish their formative work; (4) **Ground the inner being in eschatological reality** — 1Pe 1:6 ("in this you rejoice, though now for a little while... grieved by various trials") — present suffering is contextualised by the eschatological inheritance; Suffering-Joy equips this recontextualisation.

---

**T0.2.2 — Is this characteristic part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Primarily a **response to the fallen condition**. The M04-F corpus (ba.lag/mav.li.git — Job 9:27; 10:20; Psa 39:13; Jer 8:18) depicts fragile cheerfulness sought under extreme adversity that arises from the brokenness of creation under the curse: Job's suffering, the psalmist's mortality-distress, Jeremiah's grief over the people's ruin. None of these would arise in an uncorrupted creation. The M04-E-VCG-02 agalliao passages (Mat 5:12; Luk 6:23 — rejoicing under persecution; 1Pe 1:6; 4:13 — joy amid trials; 2Cor 6:10 implied by the adjacent characteristic-1 and 7 shared registers) arise from the conflict between the gospel community and a hostile world — again a fallen-condition context. That said, the characteristic anticipates its own eschatological resolution (1Pe 4:13 — "also rejoice and be glad when his glory is revealed") — suggesting it is a transitional capacity that serves the fallen condition while pointing toward the restoration where suffering-joy gives way to unqualified joy.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes — this is one of Suffering-Joy's most prominent features. (1) **Eschatological reward as the ground** — Mat 5:12 ("your reward is great in heaven"); (2) **Transition to glory** — 1Pe 4:13 ("that you may also rejoice and be glad when his glory is revealed") — present Suffering-Joy is explicitly oriented toward a future unqualified rejoicing at Christ's revelation; (3) **Inexpressible and glorious** — 1Pe 1:8 ("joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory") — the present form of Suffering-Joy already participates in the glory-filled quality of eschatological reality; (4) **Heb 12:2** — Jesus' joy-set-before-him is the eschatological ground that enabled the cross. The characteristic is inherently eschatologically-structured: the suffering is present; the joy is eschatologically anchored.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Suffering-Joy expresses the divine image as the capacity to participate in the Christ-pattern: the suffering-toward-glory trajectory that Christ himself embodied (Heb 12:2). The human person who genuinely rejoices amid suffering — not performing relief, not denying the pain, but genuinely finding joy through the suffering — is imaging the inner life of the incarnate Son who endured the cross for the joy set before him. 1Pe 1:8's "joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory" names this participation: the present experience of Suffering-Joy is already filled with the glory that belongs to the age to come, because the inner being is participating in the risen Christ's life through the Spirit (Act 16:34 — the jailer's entire household rejoiced in God despite having just been delivered from an earthquake crisis; Luk 1:47 — Mary's spirit exulting in the Savior despite her vulnerable unmarried-mother-in-a-suspicious-society situation).

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Genuinely shared, through Christ. The same term (agalliao) is used for: Jesus' rejoicing in the Holy Spirit (Luk 10:21); Abraham's anticipatory exultation at Christ's day (Joh 8:56); Mary's exultation in God her Savior in the vulnerable context of her pregnancy (Luk 1:47); the disciples' inexpressible glorious joy in believing the unseen Christ (1Pe 1:8); the jailer's household's exultation at coming to faith (Act 16:34). The sharing is through the Spirit who enables the same quality of rejoicing in the human person that Christ exhibited (Luk 10:21 — "he rejoiced in the Holy Spirit"). Suffering-Joy in the human person is a Spirit-enabled participation in Christ's own joy-through-suffering pattern.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — **Presence** reveals the image being renewed by the Spirit in the Christ-pattern: the person who genuinely rejoices amid suffering (1Pe 1:6–8; Jas 1:2) demonstrates that the Spirit is at work producing a quality of inner life that transcends circumstances — a deeply eschatologically-oriented inner being. **Absence / distortion** is most sharply evidenced in M04-F: Job 9:27 (Job considers attempting cheerfulness but immediately recognises he cannot maintain it — the image's capacity is there but overwhelmed by suffering's weight); Psa 39:13 (the psalmist's request for a little smile before death — the barely-flickering of the capacity under extreme mortality-distress). M04-F's fragile and barely-attainable cheerfulness shows the image's Suffering-Joy capacity as genuinely present but requiring the Spirit's enabling (M04-E-VCG-02) to function in its fuller form.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes: (1) **Abraham's anticipatory Suffering-Joy as type** — Joh 8:56 ("your father Abraham rejoiced that he would see my day") — Abraham's exultation across the gap of non-fulfilment is the OT type of NT faith-joy in the unseen Christ (1Pe 1:8 — "though you do not now see him, you believe in him and rejoice with inexpressible joy filled with glory"); (2) **The prophetic exultation under judgment as type** — M04-F's Jeremiah (Jer 8:18 — "my joy is gone; grief is upon me") is the OT sufferer whose inability to maintain cheer anticipates the fuller articulation of Suffering-Joy in the NT through Christ; (3) **Psa 39:13 as type** — the psalmist's request to "smile again before I depart" anticipates the resurrection-grounded joy that the NT's Suffering-Joy provides as the answer: believers will not merely smile briefly before death but will fully rejoice when glory is revealed (1Pe 4:13).

---

**T0.4.2 — What is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Primarily **human-to-eschatological**: (1) M04-F's fragile OT cheerfulness → NT Spirit-enabled suffering-paradox joy; (2) Abraham's anticipatory agalliao → NT faith-community's present agalliao in the unseen Christ; (3) The Psalmic barely-maintained cheer → the fully-articulated Suffering-Joy of the NT epistles. The direction of typology is: OT fragile/anticipatory form → NT Spirit-enabled fully-realised form, with the eschatological fullness still to come (1Pe 4:13; Rev 19:7).

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Typological use is evidenced; not silent.

## T1 — Definition and Structural Analysis

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The characteristic is called Suffering-Joy. The hyphenated name signals: (1) **Paradox** — the name holds two apparently contradictory states together as a single inner-being reality, not as a compromise between them; (2) **Directionality** — "Suffering" precedes "Joy" in the name, indicating that suffering is the context within which Joy arises; (3) **Distinctness from Joy and Gladness** — the name signals that this is neither Joy (which rejoices in what is good) nor Gladness (which is the warm inner state of one whose circumstances are assessed as good) but a third positive inner-being state that specifically arises through suffering. The name correctly orients the enquiry toward a paradoxical inner capacity, not a surface emotion.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Primary terms: (1) **agalliao** (G0021) — to exult, leap for joy, rejoice greatly; 11 instances in M04-E-VCG-02. Root meaning: to exult, from agan (very much) + hallesthai (to leap) — the intensified-leaping form of joy. At definitional level: agalliao names an intense, outward-reaching, Spirit-enabled rejoicing that is characteristically present when circumstances offer no natural ground for joy. Its appearance in suffering contexts (1Pe 1:6; 4:13; Mat 5:12; Act 16:34) is not despite its intensity but because of it — the intensity is required to leap over the suffering context; (2) **ba.lag** (H1082) — to smile, to be cheerful; 3 instances in M04-F-VCG-01 (Job 9:27; 10:20; Psa 39:13). Root meaning: to gleam, smile; connotes a fragile, brief brightening — the opposite of agalliao's intensity; (3) **mav.li.git** (H4010) — consolation, comfort (1 instance: Jer 8:18). Root meaning related to comfort and solace. At definitional level: the three terms together span the full range from fragile attempted cheerfulness (ba.lag) through grief-without-comfort (mav.li.git) to Spirit-enabled exultation (agalliao) — the characteristic has a wide phenomenological range within its suffering-context definition.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — (1) **Directional** — agalliao is consistently directed toward a specific ground: God my Savior (Luk 1:47), the Father's gracious revelation (Luk 10:21), the unseen Christ believed in (1Pe 1:8), the future glory (1Pe 4:13), Christ's day (Joh 8:56). The name implies a Godward or eschatological direction for the joy component; (2) **Relational** — Act 16:34 (the jailer and his entire household) and Mat 5:12 (the persecuted community) show the characteristic operating in relational and communal contexts; (3) **Constitutional** — Luk 1:47 ("my spirit rejoices") explicitly names the spirit level; Luk 10:21 ("rejoiced in the Holy Spirit") confirms Spirit-level constitutional location as primary for agalliao.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Suffering-Joy is primarily an **act with dispositional grounding**: (1) **Act** — Mat 5:12 ("rejoice and be glad"); 1Pe 4:13 ("rejoice insofar as you share Christ's sufferings") — commanded active responses requiring volitional engagement; Jas 1:2 ("count it all joy") — a deliberate cognitive-volitional act of reframing; (2) **State** — 1Pe 1:8's "joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory" names an ongoing inner state, not a momentary act; the jailer's rejoicing (Act 16:34) is a state that encompasses his entire household in a moment of post-crisis stability; (3) **Fragile attempted condition** — M04-F (ba.lag in Job 9:27; 10:20; Psa 39:13) shows Suffering-Joy attempted as a desired condition that the sufferer cannot fully reach. The characteristic therefore spans from the attempted (M04-F) through the commanded-volitional (Mat 5:12; Jas 1:2) to the Spirit-produced state (1Pe 1:8; Luk 10:21).

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Two constituent elements map directly to the two sub-groups: (1) **The Spirit-enabled exultation register** (M04-E-VCG-02 agalliao) — the intense, outward-reaching joy that arises through and within suffering, produced by the Spirit (Luk 10:21; 1Th 1:6 — adjacent), grounded in eschatological reality (Mat 5:12; 1Pe 4:13), and participatory in Christ's own suffering-to-glory pattern (Heb 12:2); (2) **The fragile-composure register** (M04-F-VCG-01 ba.lag/mav.li.git) — the barely-attainable, desired but elusive inner comfort under extreme adversity, the tenuous attempted cheerfulness of the deeply suffering person. These two registers represent the same inner-being capacity at its Spirit-empowered fullest (agalliao) and its unaided fragile minimum (ba.lag) — the full range of Suffering-Joy from empowered paradox to fragile attempt.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Suffering-Joy is the paradoxical inner capacity to find genuine joy in and through suffering — not merely alongside it, not despite it, but as an inner state that the suffering context itself occasions or enables. In its Spirit-enabled form (agalliao, M04-E-VCG-02), it is an intense, outward-reaching exultation grounded in eschatological reality (the reward in heaven, the glory to be revealed, the unseen Christ believed in) that enables the inner being to genuinely rejoice even when present circumstances offer no natural ground for joy. In its fragile form (ba.lag, M04-F-VCG-01), it is the barely-attainable attempt at inner brightness under extreme adversity — the awareness of the capacity while feeling unable to access its fullness. Constitutional location: primarily at the spirit level (Luk 1:47; Luk 10:21), with heart and tongue as secondary (Act 2:26).

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The structural opposites evidenced: (1) **Grief/mourning as the dominant context** — 1Pe 1:6 ("grieved by various trials" stands alongside the rejoicing); Mat 5:12's context is persecution; Jas 1:2's context is trials. The structural opposite is not merely the absence of Suffering-Joy but its replacement by grief, mourning, and lament; (2) **Absent cheerfulness** — M04-F: Jer 8:18 ("my joy is gone; grief is upon me; my heart is sick within me") — the total absence of the characteristic's fragile register; (3) **Worldly rejoicing over wrong** — in the structural contrast of 1Pe 4:13 ("you may also rejoice and be glad when his glory is revealed" vs. the world that rejoices when Christ's witnesses are silenced — Rev 11:10) — the world's gloating over suffering is the inverted structural opposite of Suffering-Joy.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Suffering-Joy explicitly excludes: (1) **Performed or hollow cheerfulness** — M04-F makes clear that ba.lag is not easy or automatic; Job 9:27's attempted cheerfulness immediately collapses — the characteristic is not a performance of happiness; (2) **Joy based on wrong grounds** — Joh 5:35 ("you were willing to rejoice for a while in his light") — the crowds' transient agalliao in John's light was real but shallowly grounded and did not endure; Suffering-Joy resists joy that lacks the depth to hold through difficulty; (3) **Denial of suffering** — 1Pe 1:6 ("though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved") explicitly acknowledges the grief alongside the rejoicing; Suffering-Joy does not pretend suffering away; (4) **World-aligned rejoicing** — Rev 11:10 (earth-dwellers rejoicing over the prophets' deaths) — this is joy rooted in opposition to the gospel, not in participation in it.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Suffering-Joy is not: (1) **Joy** (Characteristic 2) — Joy rejoices in what is genuinely good and celebratable; Suffering-Joy specifically arises through suffering rather than from the presence of the good; the overlap is in the agalliao term (shared across both characteristics' evidence), but the determining context is different; (2) **Gladness** (Characteristic 3) — Gladness is the warm inner state of one whose circumstances are assessed as genuinely good; Suffering-Joy arises precisely when circumstances are not good; (3) **Resignation or stoicism** — the ba.lag fragile cheerfulness of M04-F is not stoic acceptance; it is a desired but barely-attainable inner brightness, not a philosophical detachment from feeling; (4) **Masochism** — the suffering-context is not sought for its own sake; 1Pe 4:13's "insofar as you share Christ's sufferings" frames the suffering as the context of kingdom participation, not as desired in itself.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Four modes: (1) **Spirit-exultation** — the agalliao mode: Spirit-produced, intense, directed toward God/Christ/glory, operating at the spirit level (Luk 1:47; Luk 10:21; 1Pe 1:8 — "inexpressible and filled with glory"); (2) **Volitional-cognitive reframing** — Jas 1:2 ("count it all joy") — a deliberate cognitive-volitional act of viewing trials through the lens of their formative-gladness output; Mat 5:12's command to "rejoice and be glad" addresses the will to direct the inner state; (3) **Communal sharing in mission-suffering** — Act 16:34 (jailer and whole household rejoicing together at coming to faith in a post-earthquake, post-imprisonment context); Act 5:41 (apostles rejoicing that they were counted worthy to suffer — a communal inner orientation); (4) **Fragile desired brightness** — M04-F's ba.lag mode: the attempted, barely-sustained cheerfulness of Job and the psalmist — the inner person reaching for a brightness it cannot fully access.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes: (1) **By constitutional level**: Spirit-level (agalliao in Luk 1:47; 10:21) produces the most intense and paradox-sustaining form; heart-level produces the fragile form (M04-F — ba.lag as attempted inner brightness); (2) **By eschatological grounding**: when Suffering-Joy is explicitly grounded in eschatological reality (Mat 5:12 — reward in heaven; 1Pe 4:13 — glory to be revealed; Heb 12:2 — joy set before him), it is more durable; when it is sought in the present (M04-F's "let me find a little cheer before I die"), it is fragile; (3) **By Spirit-agency**: Spirit-produced agalliao (Luk 10:21; 1Th 1:6 adjacent) is more consistent and intense than unaided attempts at cheerfulness (ba.lag).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes: Act 2:26 ("my tongue rejoiced" — agalliao) — the tongue as the expressive organ of inner Spirit-joy; Luk 1:47 (Mary's Magnificat is the vocal expression of her spirit's rejoicing); Luk 10:21 (Jesus' Spirit-rejoicing immediately moves into voiced prayer — "I thank you, Father"); Act 16:34 (the jailer's communal rejoicing presumably involved voiced expression). The communicative mode is prayer and praise, not merely verbal declaration — Suffering-Joy in its Spirit-enabled form consistently moves into worshipful, Godward speech.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The evidence shows varied immediate responses: (1) **Spirit-gift form**: immediate Spirit-produced exultation — Luk 1:47 (Mary's spirit immediately exults upon the angel's announcement, despite her vulnerable situation); Act 16:34 (the jailer's rejoicing is immediate upon coming to faith); Act 2:26 (Peter quotes David's immediate heart-gladness at the resurrection promise); (2) **Fragile-sought form**: the immediate response is desire and attempt that may or may not succeed — Job 9:27 (considers attempting cheerfulness but the context makes clear he cannot sustain it); (3) **Commanded form**: the immediate response is volitional reorientation — Jas 1:2 ("count it all joy when you meet trials") — the command assumes the immediate response will not be joy but the will can redirect the inner state.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The immediate response varies by mode: Spirit-enabled agalliao produces immediate intense exultation (Luk 1:47; Act 16:34); ba.lag is explicitly an attempted state that may fail (Job 9:27); the commanded forms (Mat 5:12; Jas 1:2) presuppose that the immediate natural response is grief/complaint rather than joy, requiring deliberate volitional redirection. The inconsistency is itself analytically significant: this is the one M04 characteristic where the immediate response is explicitly not guaranteed to be the characteristic itself — it requires either the Spirit's production or the will's deliberate act.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Joh 8:56 (Abraham's anticipatory rejoicing at seeing Christ's day) and 1Pe 1:8 (inexpressible joy in the unseen Christ) describe a sustained inner state rather than an immediate response to a specific moment. The ongoing-state form of Suffering-Joy is not captured by the "immediate response" framework.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The sustained evidence shows Suffering-Joy producing: (1) **Proven character** — Jas 1:3–4 (the testing of faith through trials → steadfastness → completeness) — Suffering-Joy enables the trials to accomplish their formative work over time; (2) **Resilience across increasing adversity** — Act 5:41 (apostles rejoicing in their suffering → continuing to teach daily) — Suffering-Joy produces the sustained agency to continue mission under opposition; (3) **Eschatological orientation** — 1Pe 1:6–9 (present joy in salvation, trials, tested faith → "the outcome of your faith, the salvation of your souls") — sustained Suffering-Joy deepens the eschatological orientation of the inner being; (4) **Freedom from circumstantial dependence** — 2Cor 6:10 (adjacent, confirmed as Suffering-Joy territory: "sorrowful yet always rejoicing") — sustained Suffering-Joy produces a joy that is not contingent on circumstances improving.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained Suffering-Joy establish?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — (1) **Steadfastness** — Jas 1:4 (the proven character produced by trials endured with joy); (2) **Eschatological confidence** — 1Pe 4:13 (present suffering endured with joy establishes confident expectation of glory); (3) **Freedom from world's assessment** — Mat 5:12 (the persecuted community that rejoices demonstrates it is no longer defined by the world's approval or hostility); (4) **Communal identity** — Act 5:41; Act 16:34 — Suffering-Joy is a communally-shared orientation that strengthens group identity and mission-perseverance.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. The immediate response (Spirit-exultation in a crisis moment — Luk 1:47; Act 16:34) is an intense surge; the sustained effect (steadfastness, eschatological confidence, freedom from circumstantial dependence) is a stable orientation of the inner being toward what is transcendently true rather than circumstantially present. The immediate response is the spark; the sustained effect is the formation of an inner life that no longer requires the removal of suffering in order to inhabit joy.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — (1) **Faith in the unseen Christ** — 1Pe 1:8 ("though you do not now see him, you believe in him and rejoice") — faith is the enabling condition for the Spirit-form of Suffering-Joy; (2) **Eschatological orientation** — Mat 5:12 ("your reward is great in heaven"); 1Pe 4:13 ("that you may also rejoice when his glory is revealed") — a forward-oriented inner stance toward eschatological reality enables present Suffering-Joy; (3) **Spirit-filling** — Luk 10:21 ("rejoiced in the Holy Spirit"); 1Th 1:6 (adjacent — "joy of the Holy Spirit") — the Spirit's presence is the enabling condition for the most intense form; (4) **Participation in Christ's suffering** — 1Pe 4:13 ("insofar as you share Christ's sufferings") — genuine participation rather than mere external observation is required.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — (1) **Overwhelming grief** — M04-F shows that extreme suffering can make even the attempt at cheerfulness impossible (Job 9:27 — he considers attempting it and immediately finds it cannot be sustained); (2) **Shallow grounding** — Joh 5:35 ("willing to rejoice for a while in his light") — agalliao without deep grounding does not endure under pressure; (3) **World-aligned values** — Rev 11:10 (earth-dwellers rejoice over the prophets' deaths) — the person whose inner life is aligned with the world's values will find the world's triumphs over the gospel cause for rejoicing rather than suffering-with-joy; (4) **Absence of eschatological orientation** — Suffering-Joy without eschatological grounding collapses into what M04-F depicts — the hopeless, barely-maintained attempt at brightness before death.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — M04-F provides the answer: Job 9:27 (grief-saturated, attempting cheerfulness and failing); Psa 39:13 (requesting to smile briefly before death — joy almost entirely beyond reach); Jer 8:18 ("my joy is gone; grief is upon me; my heart is sick within me" — the prophet in the total absence of the characteristic's comfort). These show the person who cannot receive Suffering-Joy in a state of unrelieved grief, heartsickness, and barely-surviving distress. The characteristic's absence is not neutral but marks the full weight of suffering bearing upon the inner person without the Spirit-enabled transcendence.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The primary dimension is **affective** in the Spirit-mediated sense: agalliao is first and most fully a felt inner state of exultation produced by the Spirit within the suffering context. However, this affective state is uniquely constituted by volitional and cognitive elements that distinguish it from ordinary affect: (1) Jas 1:2 ("count it all joy") requires cognitive reframing; (2) Mat 5:12 ("rejoice and be glad") commands volitional engagement; (3) The Spirit's enabling (Luk 10:21; 1Th 1:6) makes it supernaturally-originating affect rather than naturally-arising affect.
