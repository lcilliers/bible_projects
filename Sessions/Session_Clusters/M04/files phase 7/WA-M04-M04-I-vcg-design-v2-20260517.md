## M04-I-VCG-01 — Wonder at God's Marvellous Works: Proclamation, Memory, and Thanksgiving

**Context description:** This VCG names the dominant register of M04-I: wonder as the proper inner response to God's extraordinary acts — acts that are proclaimed, remembered, declared among nations, and that generate thanksgiving. The pa.la and pe.le vocabulary identifies God's acts as categorically extraordinary: marvels never seen in all the earth (Exo 34:10), wonders performed before the fathers in Egypt (Psa 78:12 pe.le), the works of God that exceed all number and searching (Job 5:9; 9:10). The response called for is proclamation — "tell of God's wondrous works" (1Ch 16:9), declare among the nations (1Ch 16:24; Psa 96:3), sing aloud (Psa 105:2). Memory is a faculty of wonder: deliberate recollection of wonder-acts sustains faith under distress (Psa 77:11 pe.le), Israel's failure to remember wonders led to rebellion (Psa 78:11; 106:7; Neh 9:17). Thanksgiving is the natural inner overflow: the Psalm 107 refrain cluster (107:8/15/21/24/31) calls for thanks in response to God's wondrous acts toward people. The miph.la.ah (Job 37:16) names the wondrous balance of clouds as exceeding human knowledge. The Wonderful child of Isa 9:6 (pe.le) belongs here: wonder as an essential quality of the Messiah's being, not merely a reaction to him. Also: the opening of eyes to perceive wondrous things in the law (Psa 119:18/27/129 pe.le), God's marvelously wonderful counsel surpassing human wisdom (Isa 28:29), God's extraordinary acts of restoration producing inner recognition (Joe 2:26; Mic 7:15; Zec 8:6), the temple as a wonderful object (2Ch 2:9), Uzziah marvelously helped (2Ch 26:15), anticipation of wonders (Jos 3:5), Gideon's memory of absent wonders (Judg 6:13), the identification of the Lord as wonder-worker (Judg 13:19).

## M04-I-VCG-02 — Wonder as Incomprehensibility: Too-Difficult, Beyond-Reach, Impossible

**Context description:** This VCG names the pa.la register where the term marks something as too extraordinary, too hard, too far beyond normal reach — either as an affirmation of God's limitless power (nothing is too wonderful for him) or as a description of human cognitive limits and impossible barriers. The defining theological statement: nothing is too wonderful for God (Gen 18:14; Jer 32:17/27) — wonder as the acknowledgment of unlimited divine capacity. The human-limit instances: a legal case too complex for local judges (Deu 17:8), afflictions of extraordinary severity evoking dread (Deu 28:59), the commandment declared not-too-hard so obedience is accessible (Deu 30:11), Job's humility before things too wonderful for him (Job 42:3), the psalmist disciplining himself not to occupy his inner faculty with things beyond him (Psa 131:1), wonder rendered so staggering by God's coming deeds that human wisdom is overturned (Isa 29:14 ×2). Non-inner-being instances are included because they evidence the wonder-as-impossible-standard register that is integral to the cluster's character. Also: Jonathan's love described as extraordinary — surpassing ordinary human affection (2Sa 1:26), and the impossible barrier of Amnon's frustrated desire intensifying inner torment (2Sa 13:2).

## M04-I-VCG-03 — Wonder Inverted: Dark Astonishment, Dread, and Appalling Shock

**Context description:** This VCG names the small but distinct cluster of pa.la/pe.le verses where wonder is inverted — the extraordinary quality produces not joyful awe but horror, dread, or appalling shock. Job laments that any sign of recovery would provoke God to work wonders against him — here wonders are terrifying divine power directed in judgment (Job 10:16). The apocalyptic sequences: Dan 8:24 names fearful destruction as extraordinary, inverting wonder into dread; Dan 11:36 names the king's blasphemous astonishing speech as shocking by its audacity; Dan 12:6 names end-time events as bewildering and awe-inducing in their overwhelming character. Jerusalem's fall is described as shocking and extraordinary in its severity — pe.le as dark astonishment at how terribly she has fallen (Lam 1:9).
