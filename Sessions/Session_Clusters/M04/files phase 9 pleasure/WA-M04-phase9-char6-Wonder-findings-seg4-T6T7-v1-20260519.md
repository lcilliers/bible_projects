## T6 — Relationships Between Characteristics

**T6.1.1 — Which adjacent characteristics appear most frequently alongside Wonder?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Primary co-occurrences: (1) **Joy/Thanks/Praise** — the VCG-01 proclamatory corpus consistently pairs Wonder with thanks and praise: Psa 9:1 ("I will give thanks… I will recount all your wonderful deeds"); Psa 107:8, 15, 21, 31 (thanks to the Lord for steadfast love and wondrous works — paired refrain); 1Ch 16:9, 12 (sing, sing praises, tell of wondrous works, remember); (2) **Gladness** — Psa 40:5 (wondrous deeds and thoughts, none can compare — the whole heart of Gladness-and-Wonder together in the response to multiplied divine gifts); (3) **Memory/Faithful remembrance** — While not a named M04 characteristic, memory as a faculty is paired with Wonder throughout VCG-01 (Tell-Remember-Meditate triad); (4) **Fear/Awe** — Exo 34:10 ("for it is an awesome thing that I will do with you"); Psa 89:5 (wonder alongside faithfulness of the holy ones); Mar 10:32 (amazed + afraid) — Wonder and fear are closely paired but distinct.

---

**T6.1.2 — What does the pattern of co-occurrence reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The consistent pairing of Wonder with thanks and praise (Psa 9:1; 107:8, 15, 21, 31) confirms Wonder as the motivational source of doxological praise — not merely an inner feeling but the characteristic that drives the praise-response. The pairing of Wonder and fear (Exo 34:10; Mar 10:32) confirms their adjacency while preserving their distinction: fear is the threat-response to encountered power; Wonder is specifically the astonishment-response to what exceeds categories. The Tell-Remember-Meditate triad's consistent co-occurrence with Wonder confirms memory and proclamation as Wonder's defining co-practices.

The sequence is always: encounter → Wonder → proclamation.

## T7 — Lexical, Literary, and Human Science Analysis

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Primary terms: (1) **pa.la** (H6381) — verb: to be extraordinary, surpass, be too difficult; root meaning: to be separated out, set apart from the ordinary; the fundamental semantic: Wonder arises at what is categorically distinct from normal reality; 47 instances; (2) **pe.le** (H6382) — noun: wonder, marvel; 7 instances; Exo 15:11; Isa 9:6 ("Wonderful Counselor"); Psa 77:11; 88:10, 12; 89:5; Isa 25:1; 28:29; 29:14; Lam 1:9; Dan 12:6; (3) **miph.la.ah** (H4652) — wondrous work, noun: Job 37:16; (4) **thambeō** (G2284) — to be seized with astonishment; root: thamb- — to seize, arrest; the bodily and inner arrest quality of Wonder; Mar 1:27; 10:24 (cross-assigned); 10:32; (5) **thauma** (G2295) — wonder, marvel (noun); 2Cor 11:14; Rev 17:6; (6) **thaumazō** (G2296) — to marvel, be astonished; Gal 1:6; Rev 17:6, 7 (also cross-assigned in M04-N-VCG-02). Root synthesis: pa.la/pe.le names Wonder as arising from what is categorically set apart from ordinary reality; thambeō names the inner seizure-quality of Wonder; thaumazō names the sustained state of marvelling.

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — **pa.la** (H6381) functions across four modes: (1) **Verb** — "God thunders wondrously" (Job 37:5); "who does great things and unsearchable, marvellous things" (Job 5:9); "the Lord will do wonders" (Exo 3:20; Jos 3:5); (2) **Niphal reflexive/passive** — "it is marvellous in our eyes" (Psa 118:23); "things too wonderful for me" (Job 42:3); "too difficult for you" (Deu 17:8; 30:11); (3) **Piel intensive** — "you have wondrously shown your steadfast love" (Psa 31:21); "God deals wondrously with you" (Joel 2:26); (4) **Nominal derivative pe.le** — used as a noun and as a component of the name "Wonderful Counselor" (Isa 9:6). The grammatical spread reveals pa.la as active in divine agent contexts (God does wonders), reflexive in human perceptive contexts (it is marvellous to us), and intensive in contexts of surpassing action (wondrously showing love). This grammatical range confirms Wonder as simultaneously a quality of divine acts (active pa.la) and a perceptive response by humans (niphal pa.la).

---

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — pa.la ranges across: (1) **Divine wonder-acts** — Exo 3:20; 34:10; Psa 78:4; 86:10; 136:4; (2) **Human Wonder-response** — Psa 118:23; 139:14; 119:18; (3) **Cognitive-limit naming** — Deu 17:8 ("too difficult"); 30:11 ("not too hard"); Gen 18:14 ("is anything too hard?"); Job 42:3 ("too wonderful for me"); (4) **Dark-Wonder** — Job 10:16 (wonders worked against Job); Deu 28:59 (extraordinary afflictions); Dan 8:24; 11:36; (5) **Extraordinary love** — 2Sa 1:26 (Jonathan's love as pa.la); (6) **Divine name-quality** — Isa 9:6 (Wonderful Counselor). Semantic range confirms pa.la as the most versatile Wonder term, covering positive, negative, cognitive-limit, and name-quality registers.

---

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes: (1) **pa.la (verb)** — the active doing and passive experiencing of wonder; (2) **pe.le (noun)** — wonder as a substantive quality/reality; (3) **miph.la.ah** — the wondrous work as an object; (4) **thambeō** — the seizure-quality of sudden wonder-astonishment; (5) **thaumazō** — the sustained marvelling state; (6) **thauma** — wonder as a substantive noun (2Cor 11:14's "no wonder"). Together the terms distinguish: the act of doing/experiencing wonder (pa.la verbal), the quality of being wonderful (pe.le nominal), the object that produces wonder (miph.la.ah), the moment of seizure (thambeō), and the sustained state (thaumazō).

---

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of Wonder?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Not a dedicated antonym, but: (1) **Forgetting as the structural opposite** — consistently named (Neh 9:17 — "not mindful"; Psa 78:11 — "forgot his works") rather than through a dedicated opposite-term; (2) **lo-pa.la** (negated) — Deu 30:11 ("this commandment is not too hard/wonderful for you") — the negation of pa.la indicating that something is not in the Wonder-category, accessible rather than exceeding; (3) **"No wonder"** — 2Cor 11:14 (thauma negated — "and no wonder") — the dismissal of Wonder by removing its unexpectedness.

**T7.1.8 — What does the OT Hebrew / NT Greek vocabulary relationship reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Continuity in theme** — both OT pa.la/pe.le and NT thambeō/thaumazō name the same inner seizure-quality of Wonder at the extraordinary; (2) **NT concentration on Jesus** — the NT Wonder-vocabulary (thambeō in Mark; thaumazō in Revelation) is focused on Jesus' teaching/authority and on apocalyptic visions; the OT Wonder is primarily focused on God's historical and creative acts; (3) **Shift from proclamatory to reactive** — the OT VCG-01's proclamatory wonder-tradition (Tell-Remember-Meditate) is absent from the NT's thambeō instances which focus on immediate response rather than sustained proclamatory practice; (4) **Dark Wonder expansion** — the NT's dark Wonder register (Gal 1:6; Rev 17:6, 7) is proportionally larger than the OT's, reflecting the NT's eschatological-conflict context.

**T7.1.10 — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The pa.la/pe.le → thambeō/thaumazō arc reveals Wonder as a characteristic whose lexical identity is remarkably stable across the Testaments: the semantic content (inner seizure before what is categorically extraordinary) is preserved across the Hebrew and Greek vocabularies. The arc reveals Wonder as moving from acts (OT) to person (NT): in the NT, encountering Jesus produces Wonder more directly than encountering his acts, pointing toward the revelation of the Wonderful Counselor as the fullness of all divine wonder.

**T7.2.1 — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — In Psa 118:23 ("this is the Lord's doing; it is marvellous in our eyes"), **pa.la** (niphal — "it is marvellous") functions as the **community's perceptive-evaluative declaration**: the niphal passive form names what is marvellous not as an objective quality claimed by God but as what is experienced as marvellous by the worshipping community ("in our eyes"). The verse structure: "this is the Lord's doing" (attribution) + "it is marvellous in our eyes" (inner perceptive response). The term's function confirms Wonder as a relational-perceptive response — something seen (perceptive) and recognised as extraordinary (evaluative) by the inner being of those who observe it.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two frameworks: (1) **Philosophy of wonder** — primarily Aristotle's account (in the Metaphysics) that philosophy begins in wonder (thaumazein) — the inner state of puzzled astonishment that motivates inquiry; this maps directly onto M04-I-VCG-02's Wonder as the recognition of cognitive limits that begins genuine inquiry (Job 37:16 — "do you know the balancings of the clouds?"); (2) **Cognitive science of awe** — Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt's research on awe as the emotion arising from vast stimuli that exceed existing cognitive frameworks, requiring cognitive accommodation (updating one's mental categories). This maps onto pa.la's "set apart from ordinary categories" and Job 42:3's "things too wonderful for me, which I did not know."

---

**T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Keltner/Haidt's awe research illuminates the VCG-01 corpus's emphasis on communal proclamation: awe research finds that awe is strongly pro-social — it leads people to cooperate, connect, and communicate; the Tell-Remember-Meditate-Declare pattern of VCG-01 is exactly what awe research predicts: the Wonder-response drives social sharing and communal identity formation. Aristotle's wonder-as-beginning-of-inquiry illuminates the VCG-02 corpus: the "is anything too hard for the Lord?" (Gen 18:14; Jer 32:17, 27) and Job's "things too wonderful for me" are precisely Aristotle's thaumazein — the puzzled astonishment that motivates continued seeking. The pa.la "too difficult" in legal contexts (Deu 17:8) is also thaumazein applied to practical wisdom.

---

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Keltner/Haidt's awe research is entirely naturalistic: vast stimuli (nature, music, moral exemplars) produce awe as a social-psychological phenomenon. The verse evidence's Wonder is exclusively directed at God's acts and character — the truly marvellous in Scripture is always divine, not natural or social. Zec 8:6 ("should it also be marvellous in my sight, declares the Lord?") makes the divergence explicit: what is marvellous to humans (God's extraordinary acts) is not marvellous to God because God is not a creature encountering something greater than himself. Natural awe can be experienced by anyone at anything vast; biblical Wonder is specifically the creaturely response to the Creator's incomprehensible acts. The divergence reveals that Scripture's Wonder is theologically bounded in a way that naturalised awe is not.

  2. **Wonder is characteristically creaturely** — Zec 8:6 (God does not stand in wonder at his own acts) is the most analytically significant verse in this corpus for the divine-human boundary: Wonder belongs to creatures confronting the incomprehensible, not to the God who is its source. This makes Wonder the characteristic most specifically creaturely among the M04 positive characteristics.

  3. **Wonder-failure as the direct cause of rebellion in Israel's history** — Neh 9:17; Psa 78:11, 32; 106:7 consistently show that not-attending to God's wonders directly preceded Israel's covenant rebellion; Wonder-failure is not merely a spiritual failure but the precipitating condition for moral and political catastrophe.   4. **Memory is Wonder's second most prominent faculty** — the Tell-Remember-Meditate triad (1Ch 16:9, 12; Psa 9:1; 77:11; 105:2, 5; 111:4; 119:27) makes memory the most extensively documented faculty of Wonder's sustaining practice; Wonder without memory cannot survive; Wonder-failure is consistently named as memory-failure first.
