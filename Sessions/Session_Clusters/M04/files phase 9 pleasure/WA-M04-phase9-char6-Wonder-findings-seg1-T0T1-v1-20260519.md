# M04 Phase 9 — Characteristic 6 (Wonder) — findings

**Date:** 2026-05-19
**Characteristic_id:** 6
**Characteristic name:** Wonder
**Sub-groups in scope:** M04-I (84 verses, 3 VCGs)
**Total verses:** ~84
**Segment:** 1 of 4 — T0 (12 prompts) + T1 (24 prompts) = 36 prompts

**Evidence scope:** M04-I-VCG-01 (61 verses — wonder at God's marvellous works, proclaimed and remembered); M04-I-VCG-02 (13 verses — wonder as incomprehensibility, too-hard/beyond-reach); M04-I-VCG-03 (10 verses — wonder inverted as dark astonishment, dread, appalling shock).

---

## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder is the most explicitly theocentric characteristic in M04 — virtually the entire M04-I-VCG-01 corpus (61 verses) is a catalogue of divine acts that produce wonder. The evidence reveals three dimensions of God's character: (1) **God as the exclusive worker of wonders** — Psa 72:18 ("who alone does wondrous things"); Psa 86:10 ("you are great and do wondrous things; you alone are God") — Wonder is not a response to a powerful class of beings but is uniquely and exclusively evoked by the one God; (2) **God's acts exceed human comprehension** — Job 5:9; 9:10 ("great things and unsearchable, marvellous things without number"); Job 37:5 ("God thunders wondrously… great things that we cannot comprehend"); Psa 40:5 ("they are more than can be told") — the incomprehensibility of God's acts is constitutive of Wonder; (3) **God's name as Wonderful** — Isa 9:6 ("his name shall be called Wonderful Counselor") — the coming child's identity inherently evokes awe; Wonder is an essential quality of Christ's being, not merely a reaction to his deeds.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder is not directly attributed to God as a characteristic God himself possesses in this corpus — rather, God's deeds and character are the objects that produce Wonder in those who encounter them. The partial exception: Zec 8:6 ("if it is marvellous in the sight of the remnant… should it also be marvellous in my sight, declares the Lord?") — God distances himself from the wonder-response, implying his own perspective transcends wonder rather than experiencing it. Isa 9:6's "Wonderful" (pe.le) as a name/title for the coming child is closest to attributing Wonder-quality to God, but this names his character as wonder-inducing rather than as one who experiences wonder. The attribution reveals: in Scripture, Wonder is primarily a human response to God rather than a divine characteristic — it belongs to creatures confronting the incomprehensible Creator.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The near-silence about God experiencing Wonder is analytically significant. Zec 8:6's rhetorical question ("should it also be marvellous in my sight?") implies God does not stand in wonder at his own deeds — he is their originator and therefore understands them fully. Wonder is the creature's response to encountering what exceeds its comprehension; God is not a creature and does not encounter incomprehensible reality. The silence reveals Wonder as characteristically creaturely: it is the inner response appropriate to finite beings encountering the infinite. The human person who wonders is exercising a characteristically creaturely capacity — the capacity to be genuinely overwhelmed by what is greater than oneself — which is an image-bearing capacity but not one God exercises in the same way.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose Wonder serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three created purposes: (1) **Acknowledgment of God's incomparable greatness** — Exo 15:11 ("who is like you, O Lord, among the gods? Who is like you, majestic in holiness, awesome in glorious deeds, doing wonders?") — Wonder is the appropriate inner acknowledgment of God's incomparability; the person who genuinely wonders at God's deeds is rightly calibrated about the relation between Creator and creature; (2) **Worship and praise as the overflow of Wonder** — Psa 9:1 ("I will recount all of your wonderful deeds" — alongside wholehearted thanks); 1Ch 16:24 ("declare his glory among the nations, his marvellous works") — Wonder produces proclamation and praise; (3) **Cognitive humility before the incomprehensible** — Job 42:3 ("things too wonderful for me, which I did not know") — Job's recognition that he spoke of what he could not comprehend; Wonder's purpose is to keep the human mind appropriately humble before what exceeds it (Psa 131:1 — "I do not occupy myself with things too great and too marvellous for me").

---

**T0.2.2 — Is this characteristic part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Both, but with important distinctions by register: (1) **Original design** — M04-I-VCG-01's wonder at God's marvellous works (creation, redemption, providence) is an original-design capacity; Psa 139:14 ("I am fearfully and wonderfully made") — the wonder evoked by human embodiment itself is a created-design reality; (2) **Original design but fallen-context deployment** — most VCG-01 wonders are performed in the context of Israel's history (Exodus, wilderness, conquest) — these arise from God's redemptive responses to the fallen condition, but the wonder-capacity itself is original design; (3) **Fallen condition distortion** — M04-I-VCG-03's dark wonder (Job 10:16 — wonders worked against Job; Dan 8:24; 11:36 — the king's fearful destruction and astonishing blasphemy; Gal 1:6 — Paul astonished at the Galatians' defection; Rev 17:6 — wonder at the harlot) is specifically a fallen-condition phenomenon; wonder-as-dread, wonder-at-evil, and wonder-at-apostasy are not original-design states.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes: (1) **Isa 29:14** ("I will again do wonderful things with this people, with wonder upon wonder") — the escalating wonder of God's future acts promises a fullness that surpasses even the Exodus wonders; (2) **Mic 7:15** ("as in the days when you came out of the land of Egypt, I will show them marvellous things") — the new exodus's marvels pointing toward eschatological fullness; (3) **Joe 2:26** ("the Lord your God, who has dealt wondrously with you") — restoration's wonders as a foretaste of the fullness; (4) **Isa 9:6** — the Wonderful Counselor's coming as the ultimate wonder-event; (5) **Dan 12:6** ("how long till the end of these wonders?") — the apocalyptic wonders as the transitional events leading to the final state.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder expresses the divine image as the creature's capacity to rightly acknowledge what infinitely exceeds its own comprehension. The image-bearing significance: a person formed in God's image is not merely intelligent and volitional but is constitutively open to what exceeds them — Wonder is the experiential evidence of this openness. Psa 139:14 ("I am fearfully and wonderfully made") shows that Wonder can be directed inward — the person who genuinely wonders at their own creation is acknowledging that they themselves are made by and for a God infinitely greater than themselves. The specifically image-bearing quality of Wonder is that it is the inner state appropriate to a finite being encountering the infinite: God made the human person as a finite image of the infinite, and Wonder is the characteristic that keeps the image-bearer aware of its own created finitude before the Creator's infinity.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Primarily creaturely, with the important exception of Isa 9:6's "Wonderful" as a divine name/title. The evidence consistently positions Wonder on the human side of the Creator-creature divide: God does wondrous things (active side); humans marvel at them (receptive side). Zec 8:6 explicitly distinguishes the human "marvellous in the sight of the remnant" from what would be marvellous "in my sight" — suggesting the divine perspective is not wonder but understanding. Wonder is therefore primarily and characteristically creaturely — the response of the finite to the infinite — though "Wonderful" (Isa 9:6) as a divine title shows that God's character and presence are inherently wonder-inducing.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — **Presence of genuine Wonder** reveals the image rightly oriented: (1) Psa 119:18 ("open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things out of your law") — the person who seeks to see wonder in God's law has a properly humble, receptive image-orientation; (2) Psa 139:14's self-wonder confirms an image properly aware of its own created dependency; (3) Job 42:3's wonder-humility after the whirlwind-encounter shows the image restored to its right creaturely orientation. **Absence of Wonder** reveals the image degraded: Neh 9:17 ("did not consider your wondrous works; they… rebelled") — failure to wonder at God's acts hardened into stiff-necked rebellion; Psa 78:11, 32 ("forgot his wonders… despite his wonders, they did not believe") — wonder-failure correlating directly with unbelief; Psa 106:7 ("did not consider your wondrous works") — wonder-absence leading to Red Sea rebellion. The diagnostic: the person who has stopped wondering at God has begun to treat themselves as the measure of all things.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes: (1) **Exodus wonders as type of redemption** — the VCG-01 corpus is heavily dominated by remembrance of Exodus wonders (Exo 3:20; 15:11; 34:10; Neh 9:17; Psa 78:4, 12; 105:2, 5; 106:7, 22; 136:4) — these are the foundational type of all divine wonder-working, the OT archetype that NT redemption in Christ fulfils and surpasses; (2) **Isa 9:6 "Wonderful Counselor"** — the OT type (the coming child's wonder-name) points to Christ whose identity is inherently wonder-inducing; (3) **Isa 29:14 "wonder upon wonder"** — the OT promise of escalating wonders typologically points to the NT's "greater works than these" (Joh 14:12); (4) **Psa 118:23 ("this is the Lord's doing, it is marvellous in our eyes")** — the rejected stone becoming the cornerstone as marvellous — Psa 118:22–23 is quoted in the NT as explicitly christological (Mat 21:42), making it a formal OT typological text for Wonder.

---

**T0.4.2 — What is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Primarily **OT-type → NT/christological fulfillment**: Exodus wonders → Christ's miracles; Psa 118:23 (rejected stone becoming cornerstone as marvellous) → Christ's resurrection and rejection; Isa 9:6's Wonderful Counselor → the incarnate Christ. Also **progressive escalation**: Isa 29:14 ("wonder upon wonder") shows the typological direction as always toward a greater wonder — the pattern is not merely repetition but surpassing.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Typological use is extensively evidenced, especially in the Exodus-wonder type and Psa 118:23; not silent.

## T1 — Definition and Structural Analysis

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The characteristic is called Wonder. The name signals: (1) **Arrested quality** — Wonder names an inner state that is stilled rather than surging; the person in Wonder is arrested by what they encounter, not actively exulting; (2) **Receptive-perceptive** — Wonder is specifically a response to encounter, not an initiation; the inner being encounters something beyond itself and Wonder is the result; (3) **Breadth of register** — the single word "Wonder" covers the positive (marvelling at God's deeds), the cognitive (encountering the incomprehensible), and the negative (appalling dark astonishment); the name's breadth is a programmatic signal that the characteristic spans all three; (4) **Distinctness** — the name distinguishes this from Exultation (which surges), Joy (which participates), Gladness (which settles in the good), Delight (which rests in the beloved), and Pleasure (which receives the agreeable).

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Primary terms: (1) **pa.la** (H6381) — to be extraordinary, surpassing, beyond ordinary; 47 instances in M04-I; the dominant term; root meaning: to be distinct, separated out from the ordinary; at definitional level: Wonder is the response to what is 'separated out' from ordinary reality — what cannot be accounted for by normal categories; (2) **pe.le** (H6382) — wonder, marvel; the nominal form: Exo 15:11 ("doing wonders"); Isa 9:6 ("Wonderful Counselor"); Psa 77:11 ("wonders of old"); Isa 25:1; 28:29; Lam 1:9; Dan 12:6; 7 instances; (3) **miph.la.ah** (H4652) — wondrous work: Job 37:16 ("the wondrous works of him who is perfect in knowledge"); (4) **thambeō** (G2284) — to be amazed/seized with astonishment: Mar 1:27; 10:24; 10:32; the NT wonder term involving being seized with astonishment at Jesus' teaching and advance; (5) **thauma** (G2295) — wonder, marvel: 2Cor 11:14; Rev 17:6; (6) **thaumazō** (G2296) — to marvel, be astonished: Gal 1:6; Rev 17:6, 7, 8 (M04-N-VCG-02 cross-assigned). At definitional level: pa.la/pe.le names Wonder as the response to what is categorically extraordinary; thambeō names Wonder as the inner state of being seized/arrested by what is confronted; thauma names the wondering itself as an experience.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Directional** — Wonder is consistently directed toward God's acts and character (VCG-01 and VCG-02) or toward extraordinary events (VCG-03); it always has an object that exceeds ordinary categories; (2) **Cognitive** — Wonder specifically engages the cognitive-perceptive faculty; Job 37:5 ("great things that we cannot comprehend") names the cognitive limit as the trigger for Wonder; Wonder is constitutionally located at the boundary of comprehension; (3) **Relational-asymmetrical** — Wonder is not a symmetric characteristic; it is experienced by the lesser before the greater; the creature wonders at the Creator; Mar 10:32 (thambeō at Jesus' advance toward Jerusalem) shows Wonder as a response to one who is greater than oneself and who one cannot fully understand.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder is primarily a **state** (a perceptual-cognitive inner condition) that is **momentarily arrested**: (1) **State** — "they were all amazed" (Mar 1:27 — thambeō); "I am fearfully and wonderfully made" (Psa 139:14 — an ongoing recognition, not a momentary act); "things too wonderful for me" (Job 42:3 — an inner state of humility before the incomprehensible); (2) **Response-event** — thambeō in Mark is consistently a sudden inner seizure: Mar 1:27 ("they were all amazed"); Mar 10:32 ("they were amazed"); (3) **Sustained contemplative orientation** — VCG-01's "tell of / proclaim / remember / meditate on" verbs (Psa 26:7; 71:17; 119:27; 145:5) show Wonder as sustained contemplative engagement, not merely a momentary flash; (4) **Cognitive-humility condition** — Psa 131:1 ("I do not occupy myself with things too marvellous for me") — Wonder as a sustained epistemological posture of not overreaching one's cognitive capacity.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three constituent registers mapped to the three VCGs: (1) **Proclaimed and proclaimed-remembered Wonder** (VCG-01, 61 verses) — wonder at God's extraordinary acts in history and creation, expressed in proclamation, thanksgiving, memory, and praise; the dominant and most positive register; (2) **Wonder as incomprehensibility** (VCG-02, 13 verses) — the pa.la/pe.le family used to name what is too extraordinary/impossible for human cognitive reach: Gen 18:14; Deu 17:8; 30:11; Jer 32:17, 27; Isa 29:14; Job 42:3; Psa 131:1; 2Cor 11:14; (3) **Dark wonder** (VCG-03, 10 verses) — Wonder inverted into dread, appalling shock, or troubled astonishment: Job 10:16 (wonders worked against Job); Dan 8:24; 11:36 (fearful destruction, astonishing blasphemy); Lam 1:9 (terrible fall); Mar 10:32 (awe-mixed-with-fear); Gal 1:6 (Paul's troubled astonishment); Rev 17:6, 7 (John's great marvelling at the harlot).

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder is the inner state of astonishment and awe before what exceeds ordinary comprehension — the soul arrested and overwhelmed by what it cannot fully grasp. Wonder is the inner being stilled rather than surging: it confronts what is too great to simply rejoice at. In its primary register (VCG-01), it is a contemplative and perceptive response to God's marvellous works — expressed in proclamation, memory, thanksgiving, and meditation. In its incomprehensibility register (VCG-02), it is the inner acknowledgment of cognitive limits before what is too extraordinary to grasp. In its dark register (VCG-03), it is appalling awe before what is terrible or wrong. Constitutional location: primarily engaged at the perceptive-cognitive level, involving memory and proclamation as its primary expressive modes. Distinguished from Exultation by its arrested, contemplative character; from Joy by its perceptual-receptive rather than participatory character; from Pleasure by the scale and incomprehensibility of what it encounters.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Multiple structural opposites: (1) **Failure to consider / forgetting** — the most frequently evidenced structural opposite in the corpus: Neh 9:17 ("not mindful of the wonders"); Psa 78:11 ("forgot his works and the wonders"); Psa 106:7 ("did not consider your wondrous works"); the structural opposite of Wonder is not denial but simply not-attending, not-registering, not-remembering; (2) **Unbelief** — Psa 78:32 ("despite his wonders, they did not believe") — Wonder-failure immediately preceding unbelief; the structural opposite runs from not-attending to not-believing; (3) **Presumption/Overreach** — Psa 131:1's restraint ("I do not occupy myself with things too great and too marvellous for me") implies its opposite: the person who presumptuously occupies themselves with things beyond their comprehension — overreach as the structural opposite of humble Wonder; (4) **Dark wonder's opposite is true worship** — the dark wonder at the beast (Rev 17:6–7) is structurally opposed to the genuine Wonder of the redeemed who marvel only at God's acts.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder explicitly excludes: (1) **Overreach** — Psa 131:1 ("I do not occupy myself with things too great and too marvellous for me") — Wonder, rightly oriented, also restrains; not everything that is marvellous should be grasped at; (2) **Complacency** — the VCG-01 proclamation-commands ("tell of all his wondrous works"; "sing to him, sing praises; tell of") resist the complacency of forgetting; Wonder resists the drift toward treating God's extraordinary acts as ordinary; (3) **Dark wonder at evil** — the Rev 17:6–7 sequence where John wonders at the harlot and is immediately questioned ("why do you marvel?") — genuine Wonder resists marvelling at evil or at the adversary's apparent power without understanding; (4) **Wonder-failure under accumulation** — Isa 29:14 ("the wisdom of their wise men shall perish, and the discernment of their discerning men shall be hidden") — when human wisdom accumulates to the point of no longer recognising the extraordinary, Wonder resists this closure.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Wonder is not: (1) **Joy** (Characteristic 2) — Joy actively participates; Wonder is arrested and contemplative; (2) **Exultation** (Characteristic 1) — Exultation surges upward in active response; Wonder stills the inner being before what exceeds it; (3) **Gladness** (Characteristic 3) — Gladness is the settled warmth of one who has evaluated and found things genuinely good; Wonder is the arrested astonishment before what is too great to simply evaluate; (4) **Delight** (Characteristic 4) — Delight rests in a specific treasured object; Wonder is arrested before what cannot be fully grasped or contained; (5) **Pleasure** (Characteristic 5) — Pleasure receives what is agreeably pleasant; Wonder is overwhelmed by what exceeds ordinary categories of agreeableness; (6) **Fear** — closely adjacent but distinct; Mar 10:32 shows them paired (amazed + afraid) but they are distinguished; Wonder is specifically the astonishment-component, not the threat-response.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Four operational modes: (1) **Proclamatory Wonder** (dominant VCG-01 mode) — the marvellous work is encountered, wonder arises, and the inner response overflows into proclamation/declaration: "tell of all his wondrous works" (1Ch 16:9, 24; Psa 26:7; 71:17; 78:4; 96:3; 105:2; 145:5); (2) **Remembering-Wonder** — the deliberate inner act of calling to memory God's wonders and thereby refreshing Wonder: "remember the wondrous works" (1Ch 16:12; Psa 77:11; 105:5; 111:4); (3) **Contemplating-Wonder** — the sustained meditative engagement with what is marvellous: "meditate on your wondrous works" (Psa 119:27; 145:5); (4) **Humble-epistemological Wonder** (VCG-02 mode) — the recognition that something is beyond one's comprehension and the appropriate suspension of claims to know: Job 42:3; Psa 131:1; the "too hard for God?" rhetorical questions (Gen 18:14; Jer 32:17, 27) which challenge the human inner sense that anything could exceed God.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes: (1) **By direction**: VCG-01's Wonder is directed toward God's acts in history and creation; VCG-02's Wonder is directed toward the boundary of human comprehension (what cannot be known); VCG-03's dark Wonder is directed toward what is terrible or wrong; (2) **By constitutional level**: VCG-01's proclamatory Wonder engages the voice and memory; VCG-02's incomprehensibility Wonder engages the cognitive faculty at its limits; VCG-03's dark Wonder engages the affective faculty in its disturbed-astonishment register; (3) **By register**: positive (VCG-01 — marvellous works of God producing praise); neutral-humble (VCG-02 — epistemological limit-recognition); dark (VCG-03 — dread and appalling astonishment).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, extensively — proclamation is the dominant expressive mode of VCG-01: (1) **"Tell of"** — 1Ch 16:9 ("tell of all his wondrous works"); Psa 9:1 ("I will recount all of your wonderful deeds"); 1Ch 16:24; Psa 26:7; 71:17; 78:4; 96:3; 105:2 — proclamation as the characteristic speech-act of Wonder; (2) **"Remember" + speech** — "remember the wondrous works" implies both inner memorial and outward declaration; (3) **"Meditate"** — Psa 119:27; 145:5 — the inner speech of contemplative Wonder; (4) **Questions** — the rhetorical "is anything too hard for the Lord?" (Gen 18:14; Jer 32:17, 27) and "who is like you?" (Exo 15:11) — Wonder expressed through the questioning mode that acknowledges God's incomparability. Wonder is uniquely the M04 characteristic that most consistently moves from inner response to outward vocal declaration.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering Wonder?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The immediate responses differ by register: (1) **VCG-01 positive Wonder**: immediate arrested attention — Job 37:14 ("stop and consider the wondrous works of God") — the first response is to stop; Mar 1:27 ("they were all amazed, so that they questioned among themselves") — thambeō seized them immediately; Psa 118:23 ("this is the Lord's doing; it is marvellous in our eyes") — immediate recognition of divine agency; (2) **VCG-02 incomprehensibility**: immediate epistemological humility — Job 42:3 ("things too wonderful for me, which I did not know") — immediate recognition of the limit of one's knowledge; (3) **VCG-03 dark Wonder**: immediately unsettled and disturbed — Mar 10:32 ("they were amazed, and those who followed were afraid") — astonishment mixed with fear; Rev 17:6 ("I marvelled greatly") — overwhelmed inner seizure.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The constant across all registers is the arrested quality: the inner being stops its ordinary flow of activity and is seized by what it encounters. Beyond this, the immediate responses diverge: positive Wonder produces questions, proclamation, and thanks; incomprehensibility Wonder produces humility and suspension of overconfident claims; dark Wonder produces disturbance and questioning ("why do you marvel?"). The arrested quality is the consistent feature; the direction of the subsequent inner response varies.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The dominant VCG-01 corpus (61 verses of "tell of his wondrous works," "remember his wonders," "his wondrous works in the deep") largely describes Wonder in its retrospective-proclamatory mode rather than tracing the immediate inner response at the moment of encounter. The immediate encounter-response is most visible in the Mark thambeō instances (Mar 1:27; 10:24; 10:32) and in the negative cases (Neh 9:17 — failure to respond) rather than in the Psalmic proclamatory corpus.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does Wonder produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Sustained Wonder produces: (1) **Praise and proclamation** — the entire VCG-01 corpus shows Wonder producing proclamation as its sustained expression: "I will recount all of your wonderful deeds" (Psa 9:1); "I still proclaim your wondrous deeds" (Psa 71:17 — from youth to old age); (2) **Faithful memory** — 1Ch 16:12; Psa 77:11; 105:5 — Wonder sustains faithful memorial of God's acts through the inner act of deliberate remembrance; (3) **Epistemological humility** — Job 42:3; Psa 131:1 — sustained Wonder produces a stable inner posture of not-overreaching, recognising the limits of human knowledge before God's incomprehensibility; (4) **Moral faithfulness** — conversely, the absence of sustained Wonder correlates with moral failure: Neh 9:17 ("not mindful of the wonders… they rebelled"); Psa 78:32 ("despite his wonders, they did not believe") — sustained Wonder is implied as the condition for sustained faithfulness.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, and orientations does sustained Wonder establish?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Whole-hearted devotion** — Psa 9:1 ("I will give thanks with my whole heart") — Wonder's sustained presence producing whole-hearted inner orientation toward God; (2) **Lifelong proclamatory orientation** — Psa 71:17 (from youth to old age still proclaiming wondrous deeds) — Wonder as the sustaining thread of a whole life of praise; (3) **Communal and generational transmission** — Psa 78:4 ("we will not hide them from their children, but tell to the coming generation") — sustained Wonder producing a generational inheritance-act; (4) **Contemplative depth** — Psa 119:27 (meditate on wondrous works); 145:5 (meditate on wondrous works) — sustained Wonder deepening into contemplative engagement.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes significantly. The immediate response is arrest and astonishment (stopped in one's tracks, seized by amazement); the sustained effect is the formation of a life-pattern of proclamation, memory, contemplation, and epistemological humility. The immediate response is a momentary seizure; the sustained effect is a characteristic orientation of the inner person toward the marvellous in God. Psa 71:17's "from youth I still proclaim" shows the sustained effect: decades of sustained Wonder has produced a person whose primary inner-expressive mode is the proclamation of God's marvellous deeds.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of Wonder?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Attending and considering** — the most frequently named enabling condition: Job 37:14 ("stop and consider"); Psa 119:18 ("open my eyes, that I may behold") — Wonder requires deliberate attention; (2) **Memory and remembrance** — Psa 77:11; 105:5; 111:4 — the habit of deliberate remembrance of God's acts enables Wonder; (3) **Openness to the incomprehensible** — Job 42:3's posture of recognising one has spoken beyond one's knowledge; Psa 131:1's restraint from overreaching — the epistemological openness that enables genuine Wonder-humility; (4) **Prior consecration** — Jos 3:5 ("consecrate yourselves, for tomorrow the Lord will do wonders") — moral preparation enabling receptive Wonder; (5) **Seeking** — Psa 119:18 ("open my eyes") — asking God to enable the perception of wonder.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Forgetting** — Neh 9:17; Psa 78:11; 106:7 — the inner act of non-remembering blocks Wonder; (2) **Presumption** — Psa 131:1's warning implies the opposite: the person who occupies themselves with what is too marvellous for them may actually lose the capacity for genuine Wonder by overreaching (treating the incomprehensible as if comprehensible); (3) **Accumulated wisdom without humility** — Isa 29:14 ("the wisdom of their wise men shall perish") — self-sufficient human wisdom closing the person to God's unexpected acts; (4) **Dark wonder** — Rev 17:6–7 (marvelling at the harlot rather than at God) — Wonder misapplied to evil prevents reception of genuine Wonder at the divine.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters Wonder but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) **Hardened and rebellious** — Neh 9:17 (failed Wonder → stiffened neck → appointing a leader to return to Egypt) — Wonder-failure leading directly to rebellion; (2) **Unbelief** — Psa 78:32 ("despite his wonders, they did not believe") — the person who witnesses wonders but does not receive them as wonders remains in unbelief; (3) **Restless and sin-prone** — Psa 78 generally shows the pattern: Israel witnessed wonder after wonder and still rebelled, showing that Wonder not received is evidentially ineffective for transformation; (4) **Troubled astonishment** — Mar 10:32 (those who followed Jesus were afraid while those who accompanied him were amazed) — partial Wonder reception producing mixed fear rather than full receptive awe.

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary dimension is **Cognition** in the perceptive-receptive sense — Wonder specifically engages the cognitive faculty at its limits: (1) VCG-01's "consider," "behold," "tell of," "meditate on," "understand" vocabulary is all cognitively engaged; (2) VCG-02's pa.la in the "too hard/wonderful for you" register is explicitly about cognitive limits; (3) Job 42:3's "things too wonderful for me, which I did not know" is cognitively located. Wonder is the inner state at the boundary of cognition — it is specifically the cognitive faculty encountering what it cannot fully process. This makes Wonder the most cognitively-located of the positive M04 characteristics (unlike Suffering-Joy which is Spirit-primary, or Delight which is volitional-primary).

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Cognitive primary: (1) Job 37:5 ("God thunders wondrously… great things that we cannot comprehend") — cognition's limit named explicitly; (2) Job 42:3 ("things too wonderful for me, which I did not know") — knowledge as the measure; (3) Psa 119:18 ("open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things out of your law") — perception-request for cognitive opening; (4) Psa 119:27 ("make me understand the way of your precepts, and I will meditate on your wondrous works") — understanding leading to Wonder-meditation; (5) Job 37:14 ("stop and consider the wondrous works of God") — commanded cognitive attention. All these engage the cognitive-perceptive faculty.

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes: (1) **Affective** — the arrested, overwhelmed quality of Wonder (Mar 1:27's thambeō; Rev 17:6's "I marvelled greatly") is affective in character — an inner feeling-state of astonishment; (2) **Emotion-Positive** — VCG-01's wonder-that-produces-praise is a positive affective state; (3) **Agency** — the proclamation-acts that Wonder generates ("tell of his wondrous works") are agentive expressions; (4) **Relational Disposition** — Exo 15:11's "who is like you, O Lord?" places Wonder within the worshiper's relational posture toward God. The secondary dimensions are significant but do not displace the cognitive primary: Wonder is specifically at the cognitive boundary, not primarily an affective surge.
