## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The verse evidence directly attributes exultation to God himself, revealing it as a genuine divine characteristic before it is a human one. God rejoices over restored Israel with gladness (Zep 3:17 — su.s and gil_verb: "he will rejoice over you with gladness; he will exult over you with loud singing"), takes delight in prospering his people (Deu 30:9 — su.s), rejoices in Jerusalem (Isa 65:19 — gil_verb and su.s), delights in doing Israel good with all his heart and soul (Jer 32:41 — su.s), and rejoices over Zion as a bridegroom over a bride (Isa 62:5 — su.s). This evidence reveals God as a being whose inner life includes triumphant, exultant delight — his exultation is not incidental but flows from his relational investment in his people and the restoration of right order. The human capacity for exultation reflects this divine character: the person who exults in YHWH images the God who exults over his people.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, explicitly and repeatedly. Deu 28:63 shows God taking delight in ruin as he formerly took delight in doing Israel good (su.s); Deu 30:9 shows God again taking delight in prospering the restored (su.s); Isa 62:5 portrays God's delight in Zion as a bridegroom's delight (su.s); Isa 65:19 has God rejoicing in Jerusalem (gil_verb + su.s); Jer 32:41 locates God's rejoicing in doing good "with all my heart and all my soul" (su.s); Zep 3:17 pairs God's rejoicing (gil_verb) and exulting (su.s) over his restored people with gladness and loud singing. The attribution reveals that exultation is not a merely creaturely emotion but participates in the divine life — when the human person exults in God, they are responding with the same category of inner state that God exercises toward them. This makes exultation a fundamentally relational and reciprocal characteristic: God exults over his people; his people exult in him.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Scripture is not silent; it is explicit. There is no silence to interpret. The opposite observation is more significant: the breadth and intensity of divine exultation in the evidence — spanning six distinct passages, employing two primary terms (su.s, gil_verb), and grounding the divine exultation in relational faithfulness and restorative action — suggests that exultation is among the most fully attested divine inner states across the characteristic's corpus.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence consistently shows exultation functioning as the appropriate inner response of the created person to the reality of God's reign, salvation, and presence. Psa 2:11 commands "rejoice with trembling" before the enthroned Lord; Psa 32:11 commands the upright in heart to be glad in the Lord and rejoice; Psa 97:1 calls the earth to rejoice because "the Lord reigns." The purpose exultation serves is alignment: it orients the whole inner being — soul (Psa 35:9), heart (Psa 13:5; 32:11; Zec 10:7), whole being (Psa 16:9) — toward God as its rightful object. Exultation is the inner-being mechanism by which the human person participates in the truth of God's sovereignty and goodness. It equips the person to live from a true account of reality.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows exultation operating in both registers. Its function in the creation narrative is implicit in the cosmic-rejoicing passages (Psa 96:11; 97:1; 1Ch 16:31 — the heavens, earth, and coastlands called to rejoice because the Lord reigns), which presuppose exultation as part of the creation's designed response to its Creator's reign. Its operation in the fallen condition is most explicit in the salvation-exultation cluster (Psa 9:14; 13:5; 35:9; 61:10; Isa 25:9; 61:10; Hab 3:18) — where exultation arises specifically from divine rescue and deliverance. The eschatological exultation passages (Isa 65:18–19; 66:10; Zec 9:9; Jer 32:41; Zep 3:17) show the characteristic moving toward its fullest expression in the age of restoration. Exultation is therefore part of original design, given a particular form by the condition of redemption, and moving toward eschatological completion.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, strongly. The Isaianic exultation passages are concentrated around eschatological restoration: Isa 65:18 commands "be glad and rejoice forever in that which I create," grounding the exultation in a new creative act; Isa 65:19 places God's own rejoicing in Jerusalem in the new-creation context; Isa 66:10 calls all who love Jerusalem to rejoice with her at her vindication; Zec 9:9 directs Zion's exultation at the coming of her king. Jer 32:41 and Zep 3:17 both locate divine exultation over Israel in the final-restoration context. The current experience of exultation — the psalmist's "my soul will rejoice in the Lord" (Psa 35:9) — anticipates a fullness not yet arrived: the "I will rejoice" of Psa 9:14 is future-oriented; Psa 14:7 / 53:6 cast Jacob's rejoicing as awaited. The characteristic is eschatologically open.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation expresses the divine image in the human person as the capacity for God-directed inner triumph — the ability to respond to divine reality (reign, salvation, presence, goodness) with an inner state that rises toward its source. The evidence shows this as a correspondence between God's exultation over his people (Zep 3:17; Jer 32:41; Isa 62:5) and the human person's exultation in God (Psa 35:9; 21:1; Isa 61:10; Hab 3:18). The image is not merely formal (humans can exult as God can exult) but relational: the divine-human exultation pair is structured as mutual delight — God delights over the person, the person delights in God. The divine image in this characteristic is the capacity for that particular relational orientation of triumphant, Godward inner joy.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it a creaturely analogue?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence supports genuine sharing rather than merely analogical relation. The same primary terms (su.s, gil_verb) are used for divine exultation (Zep 3:17; Isa 65:19; Jer 32:41) and human exultation (Psa 35:9; Isa 61:10; Hab 3:18). The same preposition structure ("exult in / over") is used in both directions. Jer 32:41 grounds God's rejoicing in "all my heart and all my soul" — constitutional language applied to God in a passage describing mutual restoration. This suggests the characteristic is genuinely shared across the Creator-creature boundary, with the creaturely form being a real participation in the divine inner life rather than a mere analogy.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The inverted exultation passages (M04-A-VCG-02) provide the most direct evidence. Pro 2:14 shows those who "rejoice in doing evil and delight in the perverseness of evil" — exultation directed at evil is explicitly contrasted with wisdom's way, indicating that the characteristic's object determines whether it images God or distorts the image. Hos 9:1 commands Israel "rejoice not" because their joy is built on spiritual prostitution; Hos 10:5 shows idolatrous priests who rejoiced over a golden calf now mourning its departure — their exultation directed at an idol is now ended. The evidence shows that exultation directed toward God reveals the divine image functioning; exultation directed toward evil, idols, or another person's calamity (Pro 24:17; Psa 13:4) reveals the image distorted or corrupted. Absence of exultation before God — as in Job 3:6–7 (the wished-barrenness of the birth-night) — marks a state of such extremity that even the capacity for directed inner joy is temporarily overwhelmed.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The most direct typological deployment is Zec 9:9 ("Rejoice greatly, O daughter of Zion! Behold, your king is coming to you") — the commanded exultation at the arrival of the humble, saving king is the OT type fulfilled in Christ's entry into Jerusalem (Mat 21:5). The pattern of Godward exultation established in the Psalms (Psa 2:11; 9:14; 21:1; 32:11; 35:9) also functions typologically: the psalmic exultation in YHWH's salvation participates in a larger pattern of divine-human restoration that Christ's coming fulfils. Isa 61:10's "I will greatly rejoice in the Lord; my soul shall exult in my God, for he has clothed me with the garments of salvation" is a passage Jesus quotes as fulfilled in his ministry (Luk 4:18–21), making the exultation of Isa 61 christologically significant.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary direction is human-to-divine: the human instance of commanded exultation at YHWH's salvation (Zec 9:9; Psa 2:11) points forward toward the eschatological-messianic reality Christ fulfils. The secondary direction is divine-to-human: God's exultation over his people (Zep 3:17; Jer 32:41) establishes the pattern for the restored community's exultation in him. The two directions are constitutive of the same relational structure: divine exultation calls forth human exultation, and human exultation points toward the eschatological reality in which God and his people exult in each other permanently.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Typological use is evidenced (see T0.4.1–T0.4.2). No silence to note.

## T1 — Definition and Structural Analysis

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is called Exultation. The name signals three things: (1) intensity — exultation is a heightened inner state, not a mild positivity; (2) directionality — exultation is always toward an object worthy of the surging response; (3) triumph — the term carries the sense of victory and vindication, appropriate to the primary register of the evidence where exultation follows divine rescue (Psa 9:14; 35:9; Isa 25:9; 61:10; Hab 3:18). The name also distinguishes this characteristic from Joy (participatory rejoicing) and Gladness (warm inner brightness) by naming the upward surge rather than the sustained warmth or engaged participation.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary OT terms are: **gil_verb** (H1523 — to rejoice, exult; verbal form of the root that names spinning or leaping movement, capturing the kinetic energy of the inner state); **su.s** (H7797 — to rejoice, exult, take delight in; used both for human and divine exultation, with a strong affective-volitional force); **re.na.nah** (H7445 — triumphing joy, joyful crying, exultant sound; the sonic-expressive form of exultation); **cha.dah** (H2302 — to rejoice, be glad, more infrequent; used notably for Jethro's rejoicing at hearing of God's works, Exo 18:9); **te.ev** (H2868, Aramaic — to be glad, used in Dan 6:23 of the king's exceedingly glad response at Daniel's safety). No primary Greek term appears in this characteristic's corpus — M04-A is entirely OT. The root meanings consistently emphasise movement (spinning, leaping — gil), affective intensity and relational direction (su.s), and sonic expression (re.na.nah) — suggesting exultation is constitutively outward-moving, not merely interior.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, all three. Directional: the evidence shows exultation consistently moving toward an object — "exult in your name" (Psa 89:16), "rejoice in the Lord" (Psa 32:11), "exult in his salvation" (Psa 35:9), "rejoice over you" (Zep 3:17). The characteristic is inherently directional. Relational: the dominant register is the divine-human relation — exultation is the inner state produced by and directed toward the covenant relationship. Constitutional: the evidence explicitly names the soul (Psa 35:9; 63:5; Isa 61:10), the heart (Psa 13:5; 32:11; 66:14; Zec 10:7), and the whole being (Psa 16:9) as constitutional loci, and extends to the bones (Psa 51:8) as the body's participation in the state.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows exultation operating as both an **act** and a **disposition**. As an act: Psa 35:9 ("my soul will rejoice") and Hab 3:18 ("yet I will rejoice") are volitional, deliberate acts of exultation chosen despite circumstances. Psa 118:24 ("let us rejoice and be glad in it") is a commanded act. As a disposition: Psa 89:16 describes exulting "all the day" — a sustained inner orientation, not a momentary act. The commands throughout (Psa 2:11; 32:11; Isa 65:18; 66:10; Zec 9:9) presuppose that exultation can be a cultivated orientation, not only a spontaneous response. The characteristic is primarily **an act that can become a sustained disposition** — the volitional-affective surge that, when habituated, orients the whole inner life Godward.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals constituent elements: (1) **the object** — exultation requires a specific object toward which it is directed; in this corpus it is overwhelmingly YHWH, his salvation, his name, his judgments, his reign; (2) **the inner state** — the affective surge of triumph and delight; (3) **the expressive dimension** — re.na.nah (triumphing cry, Psa 63:5; 100:2) and the repeated pattern of rejoicing-and-shouting show that exultation is constitutively expressive, moving from inner to outer; (4) **the volitional element** — the commands and the "yet I will rejoice" of Hab 3:18 show exultation also involves the will's active engagement. These four elements are always present though weighted differently per context.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is the soul's active, surging, triumphant inner act of rising toward God in response to his reality — his reign, salvation, presence, goodness, and acts of deliverance. Constitutionally located in the soul and heart, and expressed through the whole person including the body, it is a volitional-affective act that can become a sustained inner orientation. It is inherently directional (always toward an object), inherently relational (arising from and directed at the divine-human covenant), and inherently expressive (moving from inner state to outer declaration). Its most concentrated constitutional home is the heart-soul axis; its most characteristic expression is the triumphing cry.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence provides multiple structural opposites: (1) **joylessness as curse** — Job 3:6–7 wishes the birth-night to be barren of rejoicing (re.na.nah absent); Job 3:22 describes those who rejoice at finding the grave — a total inversion of the normal directedness of exultation; (2) **grief, weeping, distress** — Isa 65:19 sets rejoicing against "the sound of weeping and the cry of distress" as its explicit opposite; (3) **mourning** — Isa 16:10 names joy and gladness stripped from the fields as desolation; Joe 1:16 shows joy cut off from God's house; Jer 48:33 names gladness removed from Moab as judgment; (4) **absent-joy as curse** — Deu 28:63 shows that the same divine su.s that delights in doing Israel good can operate in bringing ruin. The structural opposite of exultation is the condition of the person or community from whom joy has been stripped or who has never been able to direct their inner being toward the true object.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation excludes: (1) **self-directed triumph** — nowhere in M04-A-VCG-01 does exultation produce pride directed at the self; the consistently named object is God, his salvation, his name; (2) **enemy-triumph over the righteous** — Psa 13:4 expresses the psalmist's prayer that his enemy should not rejoice over his defeat; (3) **rejoicing at another's fall** — Pro 24:17 explicitly prohibits the heart's gladness over an enemy's stumbling; (4) **hollow joy on false grounds** — Hos 9:1 prohibits Israel's rejoicing because it is built on unfaithfulness; (5) **premature joy in the face of judgment** — Eze 21:10 ("shall we rejoice?") exposes the impossibility of exultation under the sword.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is not Gladness (M04-O, M04-L), which is the settled warm inner brightness without the surge and directional intensity. Exultation is not Pleasure (M04-J, M04-K), which is the receptive agreeableness without the upward movement. Exultation is not Wonder (M04-I), which arrests and stills the inner being rather than surging it. The evidence clarifies the boundary with Joy (M04-B, M04-C): Psa 14:7 uses both gil_verb and sa.me.ach — "let Jacob rejoice (gil), let Israel be glad (sa.me.ach)" — in synonymous parallel, suggesting the boundary is porous in some usages; but the overall evidence pattern shows exultation (gil, su.s) as more intense, more directional, and more expressive than the broader gladness vocabulary.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Four distinct modes appear in the evidence: (1) **spontaneous response** — Jethro's rejoicing (Exo 18:9, cha.dah) on hearing of God's acts is an immediate inner response to external information; the king's exceeding gladness at Daniel's safety (Dan 6:23, te.ev) is a direct relief-and-delight response; (2) **volitional act** — Hab 3:18 ("yet I will rejoice") and Psa 118:24 ("let us rejoice") are deliberate inner choices made independent of circumstances; (3) **sustained orientation** — Psa 89:16 ("who exult in your name all the day") names a perpetual orientation rather than a punctual act; (4) **commanded cultivation** — Psa 2:11; 32:11; Isa 65:18; Zec 9:9 are commands that presuppose the inner state can be directed and sustained by will and formation.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in two observable ways. First, **by object**: exultation directed at God's salvation (Psa 35:9; Isa 25:9; Hab 3:18) has an explicitly eschatological quality — it follows rescue and anticipates fullness. Exultation directed at God's reign (Psa 97:1; 1Ch 16:31) has a cosmic-declarative quality — it participates in the universe's recognition of divine sovereignty. Second, **by inversion**: when exultation is directed at wrong objects (evil — Pro 2:14; idols — Hos 10:5; an enemy's fall — Hab 1:15), it operates as the same inner-state capacity but distorted in direction and morally condemned. The constitutional locus does not appear to differ by context — the heart and soul are consistently named regardless of whether the exultation is spontaneous or cultivated.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, strongly evidenced. re.na.nah (H7445) is the triumphing cry or joyful shout — an explicitly sonic-expressive form of exultation (Psa 63:5; 100:2; Job 3:7 by negation; Job 20:5; 23:582). Psa 32:11 commands "shout for joy, all you upright in heart." Psa 100:2 instructs "Come into his presence with singing." Zep 3:17 shows God himself "exulting over you with loud singing." Isa 49:13 calls the mountains to "break forth into singing." Zec 9:9 parallels "rejoice" with "shout aloud." The evidence shows exultation as constitutively expressive — its natural movement is from inner state to voiced proclamation, making the shout of joy and the triumphing song intrinsic to the characteristic rather than optional additions.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Where the evidence names an occasion for exultation, the immediate response is the surging of the inner state itself: Exo 18:9 (Jethro hears of God's acts → he rejoiced); Isa 25:9 (God's salvation arrives → "let us be glad and rejoice in his salvation"); Dan 6:23 (king sees Daniel unharmed → "was exceedingly glad"). The immediacy is notable — exultation is not described as arising after reflection but as the direct inner response to the encounter with divine reality. Where exultation is commanded rather than spontaneous (Psa 32:11; Zec 9:9), the command implies the will can initiate the state rather than merely waiting for it.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The spontaneous pattern is consistent: when God acts in salvation or deliverance, the immediate inner response is exultation (Psa 9:14; 35:9; Isa 25:9; Hab 3:18). The volitional-initiated pattern is also consistent: when commanded, the command addresses the will directly. No evidence shows exultation arising from a slow reflective process — it is presented throughout as a direct inner-state response.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The VCG-02 (inverted/absent exultation) and VCG-03 (relational and natural) verses are largely silent on the immediate response pattern — they address the object or absence of exultation rather than its mechanism of arising.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sustained-exultation passages (Psa 89:16 — "all the day"; Isa 65:18 — "forever") and the eschatological-joy passages suggest that sustained exultation produces: (1) a settled Godward inner orientation — the person whose exultation is habituated orients all of life toward God as its ground and object; (2) stability under adversity — Hab 3:18's "yet I will rejoice" in the face of total material loss shows that practiced exultation produces an inner stability not dependent on circumstance; (3) proclamation — sustained exultation generates the ongoing declaration of God's greatness (Psa 40:16; 70:4: "may those who love your salvation say evermore, 'God is great!'" — the continuous triumphing cry).

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained exultation establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Security in God (Psa 16:9 — "my whole being rejoices; my flesh also dwells secure"); confidence in God's steadfast love (Psa 13:5; 31:7 — rejoicing grounded in trust in his faithful love); the capacity for volitional joy regardless of circumstance (Hab 3:18); and perpetual orientation toward God as exceeding joy (Psa 43:4 — "God my exceeding joy"). The cumulative portrait across VCG-01 suggests that sustained exultation shapes the person into one whose inner life is constitutionally anchored in the divine reality rather than in fluctuating circumstances.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The immediate response is the surge; the sustained effect is the re-orientation of the whole inner being. Psa 16:9 shows the whole-being as the sustained outcome: "my heart is glad, and my whole being rejoices; my flesh also dwells secure" — the immediate gladness has settled into an encompassing security. The difference is one of scope and duration: the immediate response is a directed act; the sustained effect is a constitutional re-orientation.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence identifies several enabling conditions: (1) **trust in God's steadfast love** — Psa 13:5 ("I have trusted in your steadfast love; my heart shall rejoice") makes trust the explicit precondition for the heart's rejoicing; (2) **righteousness and uprightness of heart** — Psa 32:11 commands the righteous and upright in heart to rejoice; Psa 68:3 names "the righteous" as those who "shall be glad; they shall exult before God"; (3) **seeking God** — Psa 40:16 and 70:4 name those who "seek" God as those who rejoice in him; (4) **waiting for God** — Isa 25:9's exultation follows patient waiting ("we have waited for him"); (5) **obedience** — Isa 64:5 identifies "him who joyfully works righteousness" as the one God meets and receives.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Conditions that block or distort: (1) **unfaithfulness to God** — Hos 9:1 prohibits rejoicing precisely because Israel has "played the whore, forsaking your God"; their inner joy is illegitimate; (2) **idolatrous attachment** — Hos 10:5 shows exultation in an idol produces eventual mourning when the idol fails; (3) **directing exultation at evil** — Pro 2:14 shows those who "rejoice in doing evil" — the characteristic operating in a corrupted form; (4) **extremity of suffering** — Job 3:6–7 shows that overwhelming anguish can make even the capacity for joy seem impossible; though notably Hab 3:18 demonstrates the will can recover exultation even in extreme loss.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The VCG-02 passages show the alternative inner state: mourning (Hos 10:5 — priests mourn when the calf departs); joylessness (Job 3:6–7); inverted exultation directed at death (Job 3:22); enemy exultation over the righteous's defeat (Psa 13:4). The person who cannot receive genuine Godward exultation is either in a state of mourning and grief, or has misdirected their exultation-capacity toward wrong objects. The evidence does not show a neutral middle ground — the person is either exulting rightly, exulting wrongly, or emptied of the capacity by extremity.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary dimension is **affective** — exultation is first and most fully an affective inner state: the surge of triumph, delight, and gladness directed toward God. This is the dominant pattern across VCG-01's 58 verses: the soul exults, the heart rejoices, the whole being is glad. The affective character is further evidenced by the body's participation (bones — Psa 51:8; flesh — Psa 16:9) and by the sonic expression (re.na.nah, the triumphing cry).

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Psa 16:9 — "my heart is glad, and my whole being rejoices; my flesh also dwells secure" (whole person affected); Psa 63:5 — "my soul will be satisfied as with fat and rich food, and my mouth will praise you with joyful lips" (soul's deep satisfaction producing expressive joy); Isa 61:10 — "I will greatly rejoice in the Lord; my soul shall exult in my God" (soul named explicitly as the exulting agent); Jer 32:41 — God's rejoicing located "with all my heart and all my soul" (full affective-constitutional engagement). The affective dimension is primary across all three VCGs.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes: (1) **volitional** — the commanded exultations (Psa 2:11; 32:11; Isa 65:18; Zec 9:9) and Hab 3:18's deliberate choice show a significant volitional dimension; (2) **relational** — exultation is constitutively toward an object (God, his salvation, his people) and is mutual (God exults over his people, his people exult in him); (3) **expressive/performative** — the triumphing cry (re.na.nah), the commanded shout, and the singing are intrinsic to exultation's form. These secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary affective classification but show exultation as a multidimensional characteristic in which affect, will, relation, and expression are integrated.

## T2 — Constitutional Location

**T2.1.1 — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — No verse in M04-A explicitly names the spirit (ruach/pneuma) as the constitutional location of exultation. The spirit-level location is not evidenced in this corpus.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does the evidence indicate exultation originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The evidence does not support spirit-level origin for exultation in this corpus. The primary constitutional language names the soul and heart (see T2.2 and T2.3). Spirit-level location is not evidenced.

---

**T2.1.3 — What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — Not applicable — spirit-level location is not evidenced in this corpus.

---

**T2.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The evidence is silent on spirit-level location for exultation. All constitutional language in the M04-A corpus points to the soul and heart levels, with bodily extension.

---

**T2.2.1 — Is this characteristic identified as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, explicitly. Psa 35:9 — "my soul will rejoice in the Lord, exulting in his salvation" — names the soul (nephesh) as the active agent of exultation. Isa 61:10 — "my soul shall exult in my God" — confirms the soul as the constitutional locus. ## T3 — Faculty Engagement

**T3.1.1 — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Exultation is consistently triggered by perception — hearing (Exo 18:9 — Jethro heard of God's acts), seeing (Dan 6:23 — the king sees Daniel unharmed; Isa 66:14 — "you shall see, and your heart shall rejoice"), and the inner perception of divine reality (Psa 13:5 — trust in steadfast love as the ground of the heart's rejoicing). The evidence shows exultation engaging primarily inner hearing and inner sight — the capacity to perceive God's acts and character as true and real.

---

**T3.1.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation deepens perception: Psa 40:16 and 70:4 show that those who "seek" God rejoice in him — the seeking (a perceptive-seeking act) enables exultation, which in turn sustains the seeking. The inverted passages show impairment: exultation directed at idols (Hos 10:5) is accompanied by spiritual blindness (the idol will depart; the joy will become mourning). Rightly-directed exultation appears to sharpen perception of God's reality; misdirected exultation blinds.

---

**T3.1.3 — What does the pattern of engagement with perception reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is a perceptually-grounded characteristic — it is not emotionally autonomous but arises from and depends upon the accurate perception of who God is and what he has done. Where perception is accurate (God's salvation seen and received), exultation follows. Where perception is distorted (idol-worship, Hos 10:5; spiritual prostitution, Hos 9:1), exultation misfires toward wrong objects.

---

**T3.2.1 — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, as a precondition. Isa 25:9 shows the cognitive recognition ("behold, this is our God; we have waited for him") preceding and grounding the exultation ("let us be glad and rejoice in his salvation"). Exo 18:9 shows Jethro's exultation arising from hearing (cognitive reception) of God's acts. The commanded exultations (Psa 32:11; Isa 65:18) presuppose knowledge of who God is as the ground for the commanded inner state.

---

**T3.2.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation does not bypass cognition in this corpus; it builds on it. The pattern is consistently: know God's act → exult in response. There is no instance of exultation preceding or bypassing the knowledge of its grounds. The characteristic deepens rather than impairs cognitive engagement with divine reality — the jubilant exultation of Psa 89:16 ("exult in your name all the day") suggests that sustained exultation sustains the attentiveness to God's character that produced it.

---

**T3.2.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is cognitively grounded but not cognitively produced — knowing provides the ground; the affective-volitional surge of exultation is the response. The characteristic bridges cognition and affect: the mind provides the true object; the soul rises in response.

---

**T3.3.1 — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The dominant pattern of exultation in the Psalms is directed at God's past acts of salvation: Psa 9:14 (exultation anticipated in the future but grounded in God's known character); Psa 31:7 (rejoicing in steadfast love because "you have seen my affliction"); Psa 35:9 (soul's exultation at "his salvation" — the whole history of God's saving acts). The eschatological exultation passages (Isa 65:18–19; Zep 3:17) also engage a remembered pattern — God's past covenant faithfulness is the warrant for future exultation.

---

**T3.3.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation deepens memory by celebrating what is remembered: the triumphing cry (re.na.nah, Psa 100:2) is a communal re-telling of God's acts. Conversely, the inverted exultation of Hos 10:5 shows that misdirected exultation can displace true memory — the priests' joy in the idol displaced the memory of YHWH's acts.

---

**T3.3.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation functions as a memory-anchoring practice: by exulting in what God has done, the person or community re-engages and reinforces the memory of divine acts. This is most visible in the liturgical context — Psa 100:2's "come into his presence with singing" is a communal memory-and-exultation act.

---

**T3.4.1 — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes — exultation is the affective faculty in its most elevated and directed expression. The soul exults (Psa 35:9; Isa 61:10), the heart is glad (Psa 16:9; 13:5), the whole being rejoices (Psa 16:9), the bones are asked to rejoice (Psa 51:8), and the person is "jubilant with joy" (Psa 68:3). The affective faculty is the primary vehicle of exultation.

---

**T3.4.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation deepens and elevates affect by giving it its rightful object and direction. The evidence shows exultation directing the affective faculty toward God in a way that produces wholeness (Psa 16:9's "flesh also dwells secure") and depth (Psa 63:5's "soul satisfied as with fat and rich food"). It does not bypass or impair affect but brings it to its highest function.

---

**T3.4.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation represents the affective faculty operating at its designed peak — the soul's and heart's deepest positive inner state directed at its proper object. The evidence suggests the affective faculty was made for this: the soul "satisfied as with fat and rich food" and God named as "my exceeding joy" (Psa 43:4) point to exultation as the affective capacity's fulfilment.

---

**T3.5.1 — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, partially. The triumphing cry (re.na.nah) and the association with singing (Psa 100:2; Isa 49:13; Zep 3:17 — God exulting with singing) suggest that exultation naturally generates creative expression — song, poetry, and vocal art. The Psalms themselves are creative acts that arise from and give shape to exultation.

**T3.5.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation enables and generates creative expression — it is the affective-volitional pressure that produces song and poetry. The Psalms in this corpus are themselves evidence of this: exultation pressed into creative literary form (Psa 21:1; 32:11; 89:16; 149:2).

---

**T3.5.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is a generative characteristic — it produces creative expression as a natural overflow. The triumphing cry becomes the song; the inner surge becomes the poem.

**T3.6.1 — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, significantly. The commanded exultations (Psa 2:11; 32:11; Isa 65:18; 66:10; Zec 9:9) presuppose that the will can direct the inner state. Hab 3:18 ("yet I will rejoice") is the clearest evidence of volitional exultation — a deliberate choice to exult in God regardless of circumstances. Psa 118:24 ("let us rejoice and be glad in it") is a communal volitional act.

**T3.6.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation both requires and strengthens volition. The commands require the will to engage; Hab 3:18 shows the will overriding adverse circumstances to choose exultation. The sustained orientation of Psa 89:16 ("all the day") shows a will that has been trained to maintain exultation as a standing posture.

---

**T3.6.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is not purely spontaneous but involves the will as a significant constituent — particularly in adverse circumstances and in cultivated sustained orientation. The volitional dimension distinguishes exultation from passive emotional states and makes it susceptible to formation and practice.

---

**T3.7.1 — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Exultation is expressed through agency — the shout of praise (Psa 32:11), the coming before God in singing (Psa 100:2), the approaching the altar (Psa 43:4). The person who exults acts: they come, they sing, they shout, they proclaim. The inner state moves the person into concrete action.

---

**T3.7.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation enables and motivates agency: Isa 64:5 names "him who joyfully works righteousness" — exultation in God produces active righteous conduct. Hab 3:18 shows the exulting prophet in a posture of perseverance. Exultation does not impair agency.

---

**T3.7.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is an agentive characteristic — it issues in action. The inner surge overflows into the shout, the song, the righteous work, the approach to God's presence. This is consistent with the kinetic root meaning of gil (spinning, leaping movement).

**T3.8.1 — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The evidence repeatedly shows that exultation requires and exercises moral evaluation — distinguishing its legitimate from its illegitimate forms. Pro 24:17 prohibits exultation over an enemy's fall; Pro 2:14 marks exultation in evil as a sign of moral corruption; Hos 9:1 prohibits Israel's exultation on moral grounds; Psa 13:4 frames enemy exultation over the righteous as a feared moral wrong. The person's moral evaluation of the appropriate objects of exultation is itself a moral act.

**T3.8.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Rightly-directed exultation deepens moral evaluation — Psa 97:8 shows the daughters of Judah rejoicing specifically because of God's "judgments," making moral appreciation of divine justice the ground of their joy. Misdirected exultation (Pro 2:14; Hos 9:1; 10:5) is associated with moral blindness and corruption. The characteristic both requires and reinforces moral discernment.

---

**T3.8.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is morally-loaded in its object: it is not morally neutral. The evidence shows that what a person exults in reveals and reinforces their moral orientation. Exultation in God's judgments (Psa 97:8) deepens moral formation; exultation in evil degrades it.

**T3.9.1 — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Partially. Psa 51:8 ("let the bones that you have broken rejoice") is the most direct conscience-connection: the broken bones are the bodily image of a conscience-struck person (Psa 51 is David's penitential psalm); the asked-for restoration of exultation in the broken bones presupposes that conscience has done its work (conviction, confession) and exultation is the state that follows restoration. **T3.9.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation follows conscience's work rather than engaging or bypassing it. The inverted exultation of Hos 10:5 and Pro 2:14 suggests that misdirected exultation is associated with a dulled or bypassed conscience — those who exult in evil are not engaging their conscience. Rightly-directed exultation follows and completes conscience's work.

---

**T3.9.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is the characteristic that comes after conscience — not the one that engages it in its convicting work. The sequence of Psa 51 is paradigmatic: broken → convicted → restored → exultation. Conscience clears the ground; exultation is the inner state that occupies the cleared ground.

**T3.10.1 — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in its sustained and commanded forms. Psa 89:16 ("exult in your name all the day") requires the integrated response of moral awareness, will, and action that constitutes conscientiousness. Isa 64:5 shows "him who joyfully works righteousness" — conscientiousness and exultation joined in a single portrait of the person whose moral awareness, will, and action are unified in glad righteous activity.

**T3.10.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Rightly-directed exultation deepens conscientiousness: the person who exults in God's righteousness and judgments (Psa 97:8) is reinforcing the integrated moral-volitional responsiveness that is conscientiousness. Misdirected exultation (Pro 2:14) is the portrait of conscientiousness collapsed.

---

**T3.10.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation and conscientiousness reinforce each other when properly ordered: the person whose whole life is conscientiously directed toward God (Isa 64:5) is the one who exults; the person who exults rightly reinforces their conscientious orientation.

**T3.11.1 — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, strongly and in both directions. Human-to-God: exultation is consistently a relational act directed toward God — "exult in your name" (Psa 89:16), "rejoice in the Lord" (Psa 32:11), "God my exceeding joy" (Psa 43:4). God-to-human: God exults over his people (Zep 3:17; Jer 32:41; Isa 62:5; 65:19) — the relational direction is mutual. Human-to-human: Pro 23:24–25 (father's exultation over a righteous son), Isa 66:10 (exulting with Jerusalem among those who love her), VCG-03 (parental and creation-participatory exultation).

**T3.11.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

God's exulting over his people (Zep 3:17) deepens the relational bond — the image of God exulting over Zion with loud singing is a portrait of the fullest divine-human relational delight. **T3.11.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Exultation is constitutively relational — it cannot occur in the absence of an object, and its proper object is God in the divine-human relation. The mutuality of exultation (God exults over his people; his people exult in him) makes this characteristic the affective evidence of the covenant relationship at its fullest.

## T4 — Relational Interface

**T4.1.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, explicitly and repeatedly. God exults over his people with gladness and loud singing (Zep 3:17 — su.s and gil_verb); rejoices in doing Israel good with all his heart and soul (Jer 32:41 — su.s); will rejoice over restored Zion as a bridegroom over a bride (Isa 62:5 — su.s); will rejoice in Jerusalem and be glad in his people (Isa 65:19 — gil_verb and su.s); takes delight in prospering the restored (Deu 30:9 — su.s). The God-to-human direction is one of the most strongly evidenced directions in the corpus.

---

**T4.1.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — God's exultation toward his people is consistently **covenantal and responsive**: (1) **Covenantal** — Jer 32:41's context is the new covenant; Deu 30:9's is the restoration covenant after return; Isa 62:5's is the eschatological marriage of YHWH and Zion; (2) **Responsive** — God's exultation is directed at a restored people (Deu 30:9 — "as he took delight in your fathers"; Zep 3:17 — he who will save, in your midst); (3) **Conditional by the people's covenant posture** — Deu 28:63 shows the same su.s applied to God's delight in ruin when Israel is unfaithful, confirming the covenantal conditionality. God's exultation over his people is not unconditional but arises from the fulfilment of covenant relationship.

---

**T4.1.3 — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — God's exultation over his people reveals his disposition as one of genuine relational delight — not merely governance or provision but a surging inner joy directed at his people. Jer 32:41's "with all my heart and all my soul" is the strongest constitutional language applied to God's inner disposition in the entire corpus: it locates his rejoicing at the deepest level of his inner being. Zep 3:17's pairing of exultation with love that quiets — "he will quiet you by his love; he will exult over you with loud singing" — shows exultation as the expression of God's deepest relational tenderness alongside his triumphant joy. God delights in his people.

---

**T4.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on God-to-human operation, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not silent; extensively evidenced.

---

**T4.2.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes — this is the dominant direction in the corpus. The vast majority of VCG-01's 58 verses show human exultation directed toward God: "my soul will rejoice in the Lord" (Psa 35:9); "I will greatly rejoice in the Lord; my soul shall exult in my God" (Isa 61:10); "yet I will rejoice in the Lord" (Hab 3:18); "exult in your name all the day" (Psa 89:16); "to God my exceeding joy" (Psa 43:4). The human-to-God direction is the primary mode of this characteristic.

---

**T4.2.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Trust (Psa 13:5); uprightness of heart (Psa 32:11); seeking God (Psa 40:16; 70:4); waiting for God (Isa 25:9); love of God's salvation (Psa 40:16; 70:4); righteous living (Psa 68:3; 97:8). The characteristic's inner posture is not passive — it requires active orientation toward God through trust, seeking, and righteous alignment.

---
