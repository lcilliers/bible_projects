# WA-M46-consolidated-findings-v1-20260515-part3.md

**Cluster:** M46 — Abundance, Prosperity and Wealth
**Part:** 3 of 4 — Tiers T3 and T4

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty?

**[A]** E — M46-A's most central finding is the impairment of the perceptive faculty. Isa 6:10 names it directly: make the heart fat so they cannot see (eyes), cannot hear (ears), cannot understand (heart), and cannot turn. Inner closure systematically closes down inner and outer perception. Rev 3:17 names the outcome: "not realising" (ouk oidas) — the perceptual blindness of the self-assessed wealthy person who cannot perceive their actual inner condition. Job 12:5 adds: "in the thought of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune" — the at-ease person cannot perceive the reality of suffering as genuine.

**[B]** E — Insatiability engages perception in its distorted form: "eyes never satisfied with riches" (Ecc 4:8 — enayim lo tis.bah). The eyes are engaged but never reach the point of having seen enough — the perceptual organ of desire (the eye as the organ of wanting) is functioning but cannot close. The deceitfulness of riches (apatē, Mat 13:22) also engages perception: riches deceive, which requires the perceptual faculty to be deceived — the person sees riches but sees them incorrectly.

**[C]** S — M46-C does not explicitly engage the perceptive faculty. The wisdom tradition operates through the understanding and evaluative faculties (T3.2) rather than through perception directly.

**[D]** E — M46-D engages perception in the forward-looking mode: Moses "was looking to the reward" (Heb 11:26 — apoblepō, to look away toward something) — his perception was re-oriented from Egypt's visible treasures to the invisible future reward. The revaluation that characterises M46-D requires a redirected perceptive faculty — seeing what is truly valuable as opposed to what is visibly valuable.

---

**T3.1.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?

**[A]** E — Impairs: inner closure systematically impairs perception. Isa 6:10 (cannot see, hear, understand), Rev 3:17 (not realising), Job 12:5 (contempt without genuine perception of misfortune).

**[B]** E — Distorts: insatiability distorts perception by keeping the perceptual faculty in permanent unsatisfied motion — the never-satisfied eye is always engaged but never resting. The deceitfulness of riches bypasses accurate perception, substituting a false impression.

**[D]** E — Redirects: Moses's perception is not impaired but redirected — from Egypt's visible treasure to the invisible reward. This is a different mode of perceptual engagement: the faculty functions but its object is changed.

---

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain systematically engages the perceptive faculty — but primarily to impair or distort it (M46-A: closed; M46-B: never satisfied / deceived) or to redirect it (M46-D: toward the true wealth). The perception of wealth is almost never a neutral perceptual act in Scripture's account. This confirms the domain-cluster character of M46: wealth is never a neutral object of perception; it always either impairs perception of God's reality (M46-A, M46-B) or requires the deliberate redirecting of perception (M46-D).

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?

**[A]** E — Inner closure engages cognition through its failure: "not realising" (ouk oidas, Rev 3:17) — the cognitive-knowing function has failed. The Laodicean church's self-declaration "I am rich" is a cognitive claim (an assessment of their own condition) that is cognitively wrong. Pro 18:11 ("in his imagination" — in his cognitive construct) locates the imagined stronghold in the person's cognitive apparatus. The rich person constructs a false cognitive model of their security.

**[B]** E — Insatiability engages cognition through the self-questioning that the never-satisfied condition eventually produces: Ecc 4:8 ("for whom am I toiling...?") — the insatiable person's cognition finally poses the question of purpose, but only after long unreflective toil. The "for whom" question is a cognitive break in the pattern. The deceitfulness of riches (Mat 13:22) operates on cognition: the false impression riches create is a cognitive distortion.

**[C]** E — M46-C engages cognition most directly through bi.nah (understanding): Pro 23:4 ("be discerning [bi.nah] enough to desist"). The ordered relationship with wealth requires active cognitive discernment — the person must recognise when wealth-pursuit should stop. The wisdom tradition's entire wealth-ethics is a cognitive-discernment enterprise: what is wealth for? What is its proper place? What does it produce?

**[D]** E — M46-D engages cognition through the revaluation act: Moses "considered" (hēgeomai, Heb 11:26) the reproach of Christ as greater wealth. The revaluation is a cognitive act — an explicit reckoning that places one value above another. The instruction "do not toil to acquire wealth; be discerning enough to desist" (Pro 23:4) requires the same cognitive stopping of the acquisition-drive.

---

**T3.2.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**[A]** E — Impairs: inner closure impairs cognitive self-knowledge (Rev 3:17 — not realising their own condition). It also enables a kind of false cognition — the confident but incorrect self-assessment ("I am rich, I need nothing").

**[C, D]** E — Requires and rewards: M46-C requires the active exercise of bi.nah (discernment) for the ordering of character and wealth. M46-D deepens cognition through the revaluation act — the person who has genuinely revalued (Moses, Heb 11:26) has performed a sophisticated cognitive act of comparative assessment across temporal and eternal registers.

---

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern of engagement reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Cognition is the primary faculty that distinguishes M46-C and M46-D from M46-A and M46-B. Inner closure and hardness/insatiability are characterized by cognitive failure (self-knowledge failure) and cognitive distortion (the deceitfulness of riches). The ordering of character and wealth and the inner life beyond material circumstance are characterised by active cognitive engagement — discernment, revaluation, and the understanding of what wealth truly is. The wisdom tradition's engagement with the wealth domain is fundamentally a cognitive-discernment project.

---

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[A]** E — Memory is implicitly engaged in M46-A through its failure. The prosperity-apostasy pattern requires the person to forget what God has done: Deu 32:15 (Jeshurun "forsook God who made him") implies forgetting the God of their making. Neh 9:25-26 narrates the pattern explicitly: Israel received the land, ate, were filled, delighted — and then "were disobedient and rebelled against you and cast your law behind their back and killed your prophets." The casting behind the back is a memory-rejection: what God has done is actively put behind attention.

**[B, C, D]** S — Memory is not explicitly engaged in M46-B, M46-C, or M46-D in the cluster's verse evidence.

---

**T3.3.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**[A]** E — Inner closure impairs memory through the satiation mechanism: the full person does not remember hunger (Deu 32:15 — the well-fed Jeshurun kicks). The satisfaction of material needs removes the felt-need for God that would keep memory of divine provision active.

---

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** The memory faculty is silent across M46-B, M46-C, and M46-D. M46-A's engagement with memory is through its impairment — the prosperity-apostasy pattern involves the forgetting of God in the context of satiation. This is a finding: the wealth domain, in its M46-A expression, is a domain of forgetting. The antidote (M46-D's revaluation and contentment) does not explicitly engage memory either — it operates through reorientation of current perception and valuation rather than through active memory of past divine provision.

---

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[A]** E — The immediate affective engagement of inner closure is the delight of satiation: Neh 9:25 (a.dan — "delighted themselves in your great goodness") — the affective experience that precedes the turning is delight. Pro 30:15 (the never-satisfied condition) implies an affective dimension: insatiability is an experienced condition of never reaching affective satisfaction.

**[B]** E — M46-B has rich affective content. Grief: the merchants weep and mourn (Rev 18:15-19) at the loss of Babylon's goods — deep affective engagement with material goods. Restlessness: "the full stomach of the rich will not let him sleep" (Ecc 5:12) — the affective-physical restlessness of abundance. The grief of the rich young ruler: "he became very sad" (perilupos, Luk 18:23) — deep sorrow produced by the threat to his wealth.

**[C]** E — The affective face of M46-C is the joy of ordered provision: Ecc 5:19 ("God gives wealth and possessions and power to enjoy them") — the capacity for enjoyment (la.a.chol — to eat, to enjoy) is an affective capacity. The ordered reception of material goods produces enjoyment, not anxiety.

**[D]** E — M46-D has the most positive affective content in the cluster. 2Cor 8:2: the Macedonian churches gave "with abundance of joy" (hyperbolē tēs charas) — even in extreme poverty, the act of giving is accompanied by overflowing joy. Phil 4:11-13: the learned contentment is not neutral settledness but a positive inner state. Mat 5:3: the blessing (makarioi) of poverty of spirit names a state of positive affective quality.

---

**T3.4.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and insatiability impair the affective faculty in specific ways: the insatiable person cannot reach affective satisfaction (eyes never satisfied, Ecc 4:8); the inner-closed person cannot experience the grief of their actual condition (Rev 3:17 — not realising they are wretched); and the wealthy person whose stomach is too full cannot experience the positive affect of rest (Ecc 5:12).

**[C, D]** E — M46-C and M46-D enable positive affect: ordered provision enables enjoyment (Ecc 5:19); M46-D's giving enables joy (2Cor 8:2); contentment enables peace (Phil 4:7-11 — the peace of God guards). These are affect-enabling patterns.

---

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain profoundly engages the affective faculty — but again in opposing directions. M46-A and M46-B impair or distort affect (the delight that leads to apostasy; the grief of material loss; the restlessness of insatiability; the inability to enjoy). M46-C and M46-D enable positive affect (enjoyment of ordered provision; joy in generous giving; contentment and peace). The affective quality of a person's experience of the wealth domain is a reliable indicator of which sub-group their inner condition represents.

Pro 18:11's "in his imagination" (be.mash.kit-to) is the closest reference — the imagination constructing the mental stronghold of wealth — but this is cognitive-constructive rather than creative in the fuller sense.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[A]** E — The sha.a.nan (complacent ease) pattern in M46-A operates through the failure of volitional alertness: the at-ease person does not choose to seek, respond, or prepare (Amo 6:1). The volitional faculty is disengaged by satiation. The desire-to-be-rich (1Ti 6:9 — boulomenos plouteō) explicitly names the will as the faculty engaged in the wealth-seeking pattern: boulomai is a faculty of intending and willing.

**[B]** E — Hardness toward others is a volitional failure: 2Sa 12:4 (the rich man "was unwilling" — lo a.vah — to take from his own flock). Lo a.vah is a refusal of the will — the volitional faculty choosing not to give. The hardness of M46-B is enacted through specific acts of volitional refusal: not seeing, not giving, not sparing.

**[C]** E — The ordering of character and wealth engages volition through sustained diligence (Pro 13:4 — the diligent person's soul is richly supplied) and through the command not to toil to acquire wealth (Pro 23:4 — the will commanded to stop its acquisition drive). Both the exercise of diligence and the stopping of the wealth-seeking drive are volitional acts.

**[D]** E — Voluntary release is the cluster's most explicitly volitional content. Gen 14:23 (Abram's refusal — a clear volitional act), Luk 19:8 (Zacchaeus's spoken resolution — volitional commitment), 2Cor 8:9 (Christ's voluntary becoming-poor — ptōcheuō, a deliberate act). The entire M46-D release pattern depends on the volitional faculty choosing to give or refuse in response to a higher valuation.

---

**T3.6.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and hardness impair volition's responsiveness: the at-ease person's will is disengaged from responsive alertness; the hardened person's will is deployed in refusal (will not give, will not see). Satiation disengages the will from active seeking.

**[C, D]** E — M46-C and M46-D engage and exercise volition: the wisdom tradition commands specific volitional acts (stop wealth-seeking, Pro 23:4); M46-D evidences the highest exercises of volition in the cluster (deliberate release, deliberate refusal, deliberate choice of the reproach of Christ over Egypt's treasure).

---

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The volitional faculty is the hinge of the wealth domain. The direction in which the will engages with wealth — toward accumulation (M46-A, M46-B impairment) or toward release and contentment (M46-D) — determines the inner-being outcome. M46-C's wisdom tradition consistently calls the will to ordered exercise (diligent work, deliberate stopping of the wealth-drive). The volitional pattern in M46 confirms that the wealth domain is fundamentally a domain of volitional testing.

---

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?

**[A]** E — The sha.men verb pattern (growing fat) involves a specific form of agency-reduction: Deu 32:15 ("Jeshurun grew fat and kicked") — the kicking is agency, but it is reactive agency directed at God, the one who provided the abundance. The fat-hearted person's agency is turned against God, not toward him. The at-ease person's (sha.a.nan) agency is specifically disengaged from appropriate action: "woe to those at ease in Zion" (Amo 6:1) — they are present but not acting as moral agents.

**[B]** E — Hardness toward others involves active agency deployed in the wrong direction: taking the poor man's lamb rather than giving from abundance (2Sa 12:4), answering roughly (Pro 18:23), dragging the poor into court (Jam 2:6), making the poor stand (Jam 2:3). M46-B's hardness is not passivity but active agency deployed in morally wrong directions.

**[C]** E — M46-C engages agency through diligence (Pro 13:4 — the diligent person's sustained productive activity), through the wisdom commands to act appropriately with wealth (Pro 3:9 — honour God with wealth; Pro 23:4 — desist from wealth-seeking), and through the agency of stewardship.

**[D]** E — M46-D has the cluster's most deliberate agency: Abram refusing enrichment (Gen 14:23), Zacchaeus distributing half his goods (Luk 19:8), Moses choosing reproach over treasure (Heb 11:26), the widow giving all her living (Luk 21:3-4). The voluntary release pattern requires and exercises maximal agency — deliberate, costly, irreversible acts.

---

**T3.7.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and complacent ease impair agency's right exercise: the at-ease person's agency is disengaged from moral responsibility. Hardness deploys agency in the wrong direction — toward the poor's harm rather than their help.

**[C, D]** E — The ordering of character and wealth and the inner life beyond material circumstance require and strengthen agency: diligence is sustained agency; voluntary release is decisive agency. These sub-groups are the agency-richest territory in the cluster.

---

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Agency is consistently engaged across all four sub-groups, but its direction varies entirely. Inner closure and hardness mis-direct or disengage agency. Ordered character and voluntary release fully exercise and direct agency rightly. The wealth domain reveals the person's fundamental agency orientation — it does not merely test the will but exposes the habitual direction of the whole acting self.

---

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[A]** E — Inner closure produces a catastrophic failure of moral self-evaluation: Rev 3:17 ("not realising that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked") — the moral self-assessment has entirely failed. The person evaluates themselves as rich and sufficient; the reality is the opposite. Hos 12:8 ("they cannot find in me iniquity or sin") — Ephraim's self-declared wealth produces the moral self-assessment of innocence. The fat-heartedness of Isa 6:10 prevents the moral turn: "lest they... turn and be healed" — the moral evaluation that would produce turning is blocked.

**[B]** E — The hardness of M46-B involves a specifically distorted moral evaluation of the poor: the assembly that honors the wealthy and dishonors the poor (Jam 2:2-3) is making a moral evaluation of persons based on material condition. James calls this "making distinctions among yourselves and becoming judges with evil thoughts" (Jam 2:4) — the moral evaluation faculty is operating but with wrong criteria.

**[C]** E — M46-C is constitutively a moral evaluation enterprise: the wisdom tradition evaluates wealth as moral or immoral based on the character of the person who holds it. "Better is a poor man who walks in his integrity than a rich man who is crooked in his ways" (Pro 28:6) — this is explicit moral comparative evaluation of wealth conditions. "The house of the righteous has much treasure; trouble befalls the income of the wicked" (Pro 15:6) — wealth evaluated through the moral quality of its source.

**[D]** E — Moses's revaluation (Heb 11:26) is a moral evaluation act: he assessed the reproach of Christ as morally superior in value to Egypt's treasures. Abram's refusal (Gen 14:23) is grounded in a moral evaluation: he would not allow the king to claim moral credit for his wealth. Both are explicit moral evaluation acts applied to the wealth domain.

---

**T3.8.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?

**[A]** E — Profoundly impairs: inner closure is characterized above all by the failure of accurate moral self-evaluation. The fat-hearted person cannot evaluate themselves truthfully.

**[B]** E — Distorts: hardness applies wrong criteria to moral evaluation (honoring the rich because they are rich, dishonoring the poor because they are poor — Jam 2:2-4).

**[C, D]** E — Enables and sharpens: M46-C's wisdom tradition deepens the capacity for moral evaluation by establishing that inner character (not material condition) is the right criterion. M46-D's revaluation (Moses, Abram) exercises the moral evaluation faculty at its highest: comparing temporal and eternal values across registers.

---

**T3.8.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain is one of Scripture's primary testing grounds for the moral evaluation faculty. M46-A and M46-B name the distortions: self-evaluated as rich but actually poor (M46-A); others evaluated by material condition rather than by character (M46-B). M46-C and M46-D name the right exercise of moral evaluation in the wealth domain. The cluster confirms that wealth systematically challenges the person's capacity for accurate moral evaluation — particularly self-evaluation.

---

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[A]** E — Inner closure is precisely the condition in which conscience is suppressed or bypassed. The fat-heartedness of Isa 6:10 prevents the "turn and be healed" that conscience would produce. The Laodicean church's non-realisation (Rev 3:17) names the silence of conscience — the acute witness of their actual inner condition has been muted by the self-assessed wealth. Job 12:5 ("in the thought of one at ease there is contempt for misfortune") names conscience's impairment: the at-ease person lacks the conscience-response of care for the suffering.

**[B]** E — Hardness toward the poor (Luk 16:19-21; Jam 2:2-3) involves specific conscience-bypass: the rich man's failure to see Lazarus implies the silence of the conscience-response that would have produced action. Jam 2:4 ("judges with evil thoughts") names the morally evaluative failure — the conscience's witness has been overridden by wrong criteria.

**[C]** S — M46-C does not explicitly engage the conscience. The wisdom tradition in M46-C operates through discernment and character rather than through the acute inner witness of sin.

**[D]** E — M46-D implicitly engages conscience through the voluntary release: the act of giving to the poor (Luk 14:13; Luk 18:22) and Zacchaeus's restitution (Luk 19:8) suggest conscience's positive role — the awareness of obligation toward the poor and the duty to restore what was wrongly taken. The spontaneous restitution of Zacchaeus implies conscience's positive operation.

---

**T3.9.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and hardness both bypass or impair conscience: the fat heart cannot turn (conscience impaired); the hardened wealthy person ignores the poor (conscience bypassed). M46-A is the sub-group that most clearly shows what an impaired conscience looks like in the wealth domain.

**[D]** E — M46-D's voluntary release appears to operate through and deepen conscience: the awareness that one should give, should release, should restore — these are conscience-operations that M46-D's acts respond to.

---

**T3.9.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain is specifically identified across M46 as a domain in which conscience is impaired (M46-A) and bypassed (M46-B). The rich man's ignoring of Lazarus, the Laodicean church's self-satisfaction, the Corinthians' premature claim to have arrived (1Cor 4:8) — all describe conditions in which the acute inner witness of sin and need has been silenced. M46-D's acts of release suggest that engaging the wealth domain rightly requires conscience to be functioning and heeded.

---

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and hardness both impair conscientiousness: the integrated response of moral awareness + volition + action is precisely what M46-A and M46-B lack. The at-ease person has disengaged moral awareness; the hardened person's volition is deployed in the wrong direction; both fail to produce the appropriate action. M46-A and M46-B are descriptions of conscientiousness-failure in the wealth domain.

**[C, D]** E — M46-C's diligence (Pro 13:4 — the diligent person's soul is richly supplied) is the primary example of conscientiousness in the wealth domain: moral awareness (wealth should be pursued through honest, sustained work) + volition (sustained effort) + action (productive activity that results in material sufficiency) — this is the integrated pattern conscientiousness describes. M46-D's voluntary release is also conscientious: moral awareness (the poor deserve more; wealth should flow to others) + volition (choosing to give) + action (the specific act of giving).

---

**T3.10.2, T3.10.3** — E — Conscientiousness is impaired in M46-A and M46-B, enabled and exercised in M46-C and M46-D. The pattern is consistent with the moral evaluation and volition findings above.

---

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?

**[A]** E — Inner closure impairs relational capacity toward God: the fat-hearted person cannot perceive and respond to God (Isa 6:10 — cannot hear, see, understand, turn). The at-ease person's contempt (Job 12:5) impairs relational capacity toward those in misfortune. The self-assessed wealthy person's "I need nothing" (Rev 3:17) closes the relational capacity toward God — the person who needs nothing cannot receive.

**[B]** E — Hardness toward others is the direct impairment of relational capacity in its human-to-human dimension. The rich man's indifference to Lazarus (Luk 16:19-21), the assembly's differential treatment of the poor (Jam 2:2-3), the rough answer toward petitioners (Pro 18:23) — all name specific relational-capacity failures produced by wealth. The wealth produces an inner contraction that narrows relational capacity to those of similar or higher material status.

**[C]** E — M46-C engages relational capacity through the wisdom tradition's community-embedded observation: the rich and the poor meet together before God who made both (Pro 22:2) — the relational capacity that recognises shared creatureliness. The ordering of character and wealth in M46-C produces a different quality of relationship: the righteous person's wealth does not distort their relational capacity as the wicked person's income does.

**[D]** E — M46-D is the sub-group of relational expansion through the wealth domain. The giving to the poor (Luk 14:13-21 — invite the poor) expands relational capacity to include those the world excludes. The Christological wealth-exchange (2Cor 8:9) is fundamentally relational — Christ's becoming poor is for your sake, in your interest, for your enrichment. The cluster's highest relational content is in M46-D.

---

**T3.11.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure and hardness impair relational capacity in both the vertical (God) and horizontal (others) dimensions. Wealth-produced inner closure narrows the person's relational world to the material and the self-referential.

**[C, D]** E — Ordered character-wealth and the inner life beyond material circumstance enable and expand relational capacity. M46-C recognises the relational equality before God that wealth does not create or destroy. M46-D actively extends relational capacity through giving, releasing, and the Christological pattern of self-impoverishment for others' enrichment.

---

**T3.11.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Relational capacity is one of the most consistently engaged faculties across all four sub-groups of M46. The wealth domain is fundamentally a relational testing ground: whether the person's material condition expands or contracts their relational reach — toward God (M46-A: contracted; M46-D: expanded) and toward others (M46-B: contracted into indifference; M46-D: expanded into giving). The cluster's four sub-groups can be read as four different relational configurations produced by the wealth domain.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?

**[A]** E — Two modes of divine engagement with M46-A. First, divine commission of inner closure as judicial act: Isa 6:10 (God commands the making-fat of the heart as judgment on a people already resistant). Second, divine address to the inner-closed: Rev 3:17-18 (Christ addresses the Laodicean church with diagnosis and counsel — "I counsel you to buy from me gold refined by fire"). God both produces inner closure as judgment and addresses it with redemptive counsel.

**[C]** E — M46-C has rich evidence of God toward the human person. God makes rich (1Sa 2:7), God's blessing makes rich without sorrow (Pro 10:22), God gives wealth and the power to enjoy it (Ecc 5:19), God provides richly (plousiōs) all things for enjoyment (1Ti 6:17). The divine provision of material goods is a recurring theme in M46-C — God is active in the material domain as the giver of wealth.

**[D]** E — M46-D has the definitive God-to-human movement: 2Cor 8:9 (Christ who was rich voluntarily became poor for human enrichment). God's provision is also evidenced in the peace of God guarding hearts and minds (Phil 4:7 — the context of learned contentment); and in God bestowing riches on all who call on him (Rom 10:12 — plouteō as divine act toward the invoking human).

**[B]** E — M46-B shows the divine judgment on wealth that produces hardness: Jam 5:1-5 (judgment pronounced on those who accumulated unjustly) and Rev 18 (judgment on Babylon's commercial wealth-system). God's response to M46-B's patterns is judicial.

---

**T4.1.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?

**[C, D]** E — God's provision of material goods in M46-C operates on multiple bases: covenant-faithfulness (Deu 28 context), sovereign governance (1Sa 2:7 — God makes poor and makes rich as expressions of his governance), and response to right inner priority (1Ki 3:11-13 — Solomon chose wisdom, received wealth as gift). God's provision of the Christological wealth-exchange (2Cor 8:9) is gracious and voluntary — not conditional on human merit but freely given for human enrichment. The basis in M46-D is grace: Christ became poor "for your sake" (hyper hymōn — on your behalf).

---

**T4.1.3** — What does God's extension reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals God's disposition toward the human person in the wealth domain as: generously providential (M46-C — richly providing), judicially attentive (M46-A — using inner closure as judgment and addressing it with counsel), and sacrificially generous (M46-D — Christ's voluntary impoverishment for human enrichment). God does not regard the wealth domain as beneath his interest; he is actively engaged in it as provider, judge, and Christological giver.

---

**T4.1.4** — If the evidence is silent on God-to-human operation, note this explicitly.

All four sub-groups evidence God-to-human operation. No silence.

---

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic in the human person's movement toward God?

**[C]** E — M46-C evidences the most direct human-to-God movement in the wealth domain: Pro 3:9 ("honour the Lord with your wealth and with the firstfruits of all your produce") — the material goods are directed toward God in an act of worship. Isa 33:6 (the fear of the Lord is Zion's treasure) — the human movement toward the fear of God is named as the path to the true cho.sen.

**[D]** E — M46-D's poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) is the fundamental human posture toward God: the condition of having nothing to offer, standing open and receptive before God. The learned contentment of Phil 4:11-13 is framed as a posture toward God — "I can do all things through him who strengthens me" (Phil 4:13) — the contentment rests in relationship with God.

**[A]** E — The human-to-God movement is precisely what inner closure blocks: the person who trusts in their wealth does not trust in God (Psa 52:7). The sha.a.nan (complacent ease) produces the failure to seek God. The M46-A movement is the absence of or resistance to the human-to-God direction.

---

**T4.2.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required?

**[C, D]** E — The inner posture required for right human-to-God movement in the wealth domain: humility and fear (Pro 22:4 — these are the inner conditions whose fruit is riches and honor); poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3 — the constitutive openness before God); and trust that is anchored in God rather than in material goods (Pro 11:28 contrast; Phil 4:13 — through him who strengthens me).

---

**T4.2.3** — What does the human-to-God direction reveal about the person's relationship with God?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain is one of Scripture's primary tests of whether the human person's foundational relationship is with God or with material goods. M46-A shows what the relationship looks like when wealth has displaced God (trust redirected, self-sufficient self-assessment, no seeking). M46-C and M46-D show what the relationship looks like when God remains primary (wealth received as gift, honoured with firstfruits, wealth voluntarily released for the higher valuation of God's purposes). The person's relationship with God is revealed by their relationship with wealth.

---

**T4.2.4** — If silent, note explicitly. No silence — all sub-groups evidence the human-to-God dimension, either in its positive (M46-C, M46-D) or its negated (M46-A, M46-B) forms.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another?

**[D]** E — M46-D is constituted by giving: the widow giving all her living (Luk 21:3-4), Zacchaeus distributing half his goods (Luk 19:8), the Macedonian churches giving from extreme poverty (2Cor 8:2), the apostles enriching many while being poor (2Cor 6:10). The act of material giving is the primary external expression of M46-D's inner postures.

**[B]** E — Giving in M46-B appears through its distortion: the rich man of 2Sa 12:4 who was "unwilling" (lo a.vah cha.mal) to give from his own abundance — the failure of giving reveals the hardness. The command to give to the poor in Luk 14:13 and Luk 18:22 is precisely the command that the hardened wealthy person cannot easily comply with.

**[C]** E — Pro 11:25 (via da.shen — "whoever brings blessing will be enriched") evidences the giving pattern of M46-C: the one who blesses others receives enrichment as consequence. The generous orientation produces the M46-C enrichment pattern.

---

**T4.3.2** — What inner conditions enable genuine extension?

**[D]** E — Enabling conditions for giving in M46-D: encounter with Jesus (Zacchaeus, Luk 19:1-8); joy in the context of poverty (2Cor 8:2 — abundance of joy enables giving from extreme poverty); the prior valuation of eternal wealth over temporal (Moses, Heb 11:26); and poverty of spirit — the person who knows they have nothing before God can give from what they have to others (the widow, Luk 21:3-4).

---

**T4.3.3** — What must the person have received or become?

**[D]** E — The cluster's most striking answer: the person who has become poor (ptōcheuō) in the Christological sense — who has participated in Christ's voluntary impoverishment — is the one who most naturally makes others rich (2Cor 6:10 — "as poor, yet making many rich"). Poverty received through identification with Christ enables the enriching of others. The giver in M46-D has received a revaluation of wealth that enables them to give without experiencing the giving as loss.

---

**T4.3.4** — If silent on giving direction, note explicitly.

All four sub-groups engage the giving direction — even M46-A and M46-B through its absence and distortion. No silence.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received?

**[C, D]** E — M46-C evidences the right reception of material goods: Ecc 5:19 (God gives wealth and the power to enjoy it — the combination of gift and capacity to receive is named). 1Ti 6:17 (God richly provides all things for enjoyment — the enjoyment is the reception of the gift). M46-D shows reception of the kingdom through poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3 — theirs is the kingdom), and reception of enrichment through Christ's poverty (2Cor 8:9 — "you might become rich").

**[A]** E — Inner closure is specifically the impairment of the capacity to receive from God: "I need nothing" (Rev 3:17) is the self-declaration that blocks reception. The counsel to "buy from me gold refined by fire" (Rev 3:18) is the divine invitation to receive — but the inner-closed person cannot receive because they do not believe they need anything.

---

**T4.4.2** — What inner conditions enable or block reception?

**[C, D]** E — Enabling: the fear of the Lord and humility (Pro 22:4 — the reward of humility and fear is riches); poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3 — the condition of neediness enabling reception); trust in God rather than in riches (Pro 11:28 contrast — trust in God as the enabling condition for flourishing rather than falling).

**[A]** E — Blocking: self-sufficiency ("I need nothing," Rev 3:17); trusting in material goods rather than in God (Psa 52:7 — the blocking condition named explicitly); and the inner-closed person's non-realisation that they are actually poor (Rev 3:17 — not knowing their condition prevents seeking what they need).

---

**T4.4.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who does not receive?

**[A]** E — The person who does not receive in M46-A: the rich young ruler's grief (perilupos, Luk 18:23) — sad but unable to receive the counsel to give. The Laodicean church: wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, naked — without knowing it. The inner state of non-reception is the paradox of being materially full and spiritually empty, confident and actually destitute.

---

**T4.4.4** — If silent, note explicitly. No silence.

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing bonds versus across relational distance?

**[B]** E — M46-B evidences that hardness operates across relational distance: the rich man's indifference to Lazarus (Luk 16:19-21) is toward someone at his gate — proximate but relationally distant. The failure of pity in 2Sa 12:4 is toward the poor man's lamb — the rich man takes from poverty, having no relational bond with the poor man. Hardness operates toward those outside existing relational bonds — the rich and the poor, the wealthy visitor and the shabby petitioner.

**[D]** E — Giving in M46-D crosses relational distance: the Macedonian churches' generosity is toward the Jerusalem believers (2Cor 8:1-4) — a cross-cultural, cross-geographical giving. The giving to the poor commanded in Luk 14:13 specifically names those without existing relational bonds (the poor, the crippled, the lame, the blind — those who cannot repay). M46-D's giving is cross-relational-distance giving.

---

**T4.5.2** — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain crosses covenantal boundaries in both directions. The prosperity-apostasy pattern (M46-A) appears in both Israel (Deu 32:15; Hos 10:1; Neh 9:25-26) and pagan nations (Amo 6:1 — Zion's complacency; Hab 1:16 — Chaldean self-worship through military success). The hardness pattern (M46-B) appears in assembly contexts (Jam 2 — within the Christian community) and in broader society (Mic 6:12 — rich men of Jerusalem; Luk 16:19-21 — the parable's setting). The wealth domain is not covenantally bounded.

---

**T4.5.3** — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The relational scope of M46 is universal: all four sub-groups operate across covenantal, ethnic, and social boundaries. The cluster's concern is not with a specific community's wealth-ethics but with the systematic inner-being effects of wealth on any person who holds it. The poor (ptōchos) are a relational category that appears across all communities in the cluster's evidence. The scope of M46 is the universal human encounter with material wealth and poverty.

---

**T4.5.4** — If silent, note explicitly. No silence — relational scope is well-evidenced.

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**T4.6.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to spiritual beings?

**[A]** E — The most significant spiritual-beings interface in M46-A is Rev 3:17-18: the Risen Christ addressing the Laodicean church with the diagnosis of their inner closure and the counsel for its remedy. This is not the human realm but the risen Lord's direct address to the inner-closed community. The letter addresses the wealth-domain spiritual condition.

**[B]** E — Rev 18 (Babylon's wealth-system) has an implicit spiritual-beings dimension: Babylon is described in terms that suggest it is not merely an economic system but a spiritual power (Rev 17:5 — "Mystery, Babylon the Great"). The merchants who grew rich from Babylon's wealth were participating in a spiritual-economic system. The judgement of Babylon is a spiritual-warfare event.

**[C, D]** S — No direct spiritual-beings interface evidenced in M46-C or M46-D beyond the divine interface already addressed in T4.1.

---

**T4.6.2-T4.6.4** — The spiritual-beings interface in M46 is limited to: the Risen Christ's address to the inner-closed community (M46-A / Rev 3), and the spiritual dimensions of Babylon's wealth-power (M46-B / Rev 17-18). Both are NT Apocalyptic contexts. The broader wealth vocabulary in the cluster does not engage spiritual beings.

## CLUSTER-LEVEL ADDITIONS — Phase 9 cross-sub-group review

**T3.5.3** — cluster-level silence finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** S — The creative faculty (imagination and origination) is absent from all four sub-groups' verse evidence. The sole marginal reference is Pro 18:11 ("in his imagination" — be.mash.kit-to, describing the mental construct of wealth as security), which is cognitive-constructive rather than creative in the full sense. The wealth domain does not engage the creative faculty across any register of its vocabulary or any of its 197 relevant verses. This silence is noted as a structural feature.

---

**T3.6.3** — cluster-level pattern finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The volitional faculty is the consistent hinge of the wealth domain across all four sub-groups — but its directional deployment varies entirely. M46-A: the will is disengaged from seeking God by satiation (sha.a.nan, Amo 6:1) or actively deployed against God (Deu 32:15 — Jeshurun "kicked"). M46-B: the will is deployed in volitional refusal toward the poor (lo a.vah — "was unwilling," 2Sa 12:4). M46-C: the will is commanded toward sustained diligence (Pro 13:4) and wisdom-shaped restraint of the wealth-seeking drive (Pro 23:4). M46-D: the will exercises its highest deployment in deliberate, costly, irreversible voluntary release (Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8; 2Cor 8:9 — Christ's voluntary becoming-poor). The wealth domain does not bypass the volitional faculty but consistently reveals and tests the directional deployment of the whole acting person's will. The will's direction in the wealth domain is the most reliable structural indicator of which sub-group's pattern the person inhabits.

---

**T3.8.3** — cluster-level pattern finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The moral evaluation faculty is systematically challenged across all four sub-groups: M46-A shows catastrophic failure of moral self-evaluation (Rev 3:17 — not realising their actual condition; Hos 12:8 — declaring innocence through self-assessed wealth); M46-B shows moral evaluation of persons by wrong criteria (Jam 2:2-4 — evaluating by material condition rather than character); M46-C provides the right framework for moral evaluation in the wealth domain (Pro 15:6; Pro 28:6 — character as the criterion); M46-D shows the highest exercise of moral evaluation in the domain (Heb 11:26 — Moses's reckoning of reproach as greater wealth than Egypt's treasures). The wealth domain is one of Scripture's primary proving grounds for the moral evaluation faculty. Every sub-group shows a distinct mode of engagement with moral evaluation — and the cluster as a whole traces the faculty's distortion (M46-A, M46-B) to its right exercise (M46-C, M46-D) to its highest Christological expression (M46-D, 2Cor 8:9; Heb 11:26).

---

**T3.11.3** — cluster-level pattern finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The person's treatment of and orientation toward the poor is the cluster's primary external index of internal wealth-domain condition across all four sub-groups. M46-A: relational capacity toward God closed — the inner-closed person cannot receive from God, and this closure also produces contempt toward the poor (sha.a.nan, Job 12:5). M46-B: relational capacity toward others contracted — hardness toward the poor (Luk 16:19-21; 2Sa 12:4), differential treatment of rich and poor in the assembly (Jam 2:2-6), and the use of the poor as a rhetorical cover for self-interest (Joh 12:6). M46-C: relational equality before God recognised across the wealth differential — the rich and poor equally made by God (Pro 22:2). M46-D: relational capacity expanded toward those the world excludes — giving to the poor is the commanded and paradigmatic act of release (Luk 14:13; Luk 18:22; Luk 19:8). The cluster's consistent witness: how the person with material goods relates to those without is the most visible surface of their inner wealth-domain condition.

---

**T6.2.1** — supplementary cluster-level structural relationships

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** Three structural relationships are evidenced across the sub-groups by the full review:

First: M46-A and M46-D are structural inverses. M46-A names the condition of self-assessed sufficiency that closes toward God ("I am rich, I need nothing," Rev 3:17); M46-D names the condition of acknowledged dependence that opens toward God (poverty of spirit, Mat 5:3). These are not merely different inner conditions within the same domain — they are the domain's two poles, structurally opposite. The cluster's two primary anchors (Rev 3:17 for M46-A; 2Cor 8:9 for M46-D) mark the domain's greatest risk and its Christological resolution respectively.

Second: M46-C is the generative condition for M46-D. The inner conditions of M46-C (diligence, trust, humility, fear of the Lord) are the formative character dispositions that, when cultivated, lead away from M46-A and M46-B and toward M46-D's postures of voluntary release and contentment. M46-C is the wisdom-shaped middle: the character formation that prevents distortion and enables the ordered response.

Third: M46-A produces M46-B as its outward expression. Inner closure (M46-A) is the constitutional condition; hardness toward others and insatiability within (M46-B) are its visible manifestations in relational behaviour and inner experience. The fat-hearted person is the one whose pity has eroded and whose satisfaction-capacity is gone. M46-A names the deep condition; M46-B names how that condition appears on the surface of relational and affective life.
