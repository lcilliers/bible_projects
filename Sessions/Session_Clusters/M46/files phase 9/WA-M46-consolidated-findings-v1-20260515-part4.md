## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Developmental Origins

**T5.1.1** — Does the verse evidence reveal anything about the developmental origins of this characteristic — how it is first formed in the person?

**[A]** E — Inner closure is formed through the satiation process: Neh 9:25 narrates the sequence from first receiving to delight to fat to turning. The developmental origin is the experience of material abundance without the anchoring of gratitude and dependence. The formation is not an original condition but a product of the person's history with material goods. Childhood-material-security contexts (Kasser's developmental finding) are not evidenced directly but the wisdom tradition's warnings (Pro 23:4 — do not toil to be rich; Pro 28:20 — hastening brings punishment) presuppose that the wealth-orientation is formed through experience and must be countered by formation in wisdom.

**[B]** E — Hardness is formed through sustained wealth-accumulation and social power-differential: the a.shir (rich person) of Pro 10:15, Pro 18:11, and Pro 28:11 all describe persons whose character has been formed over time by the experience of having much. The formation of hardness appears to be progressive — it is not sudden but cumulative.

**[C]** E — M46-C's ordered character-wealth relationship is formed through character formation: diligence (Pro 13:4 — sustained disposition of effort), trust (Pro 28:25 — stable orientation of reliance on God), humility and fear (Pro 22:4 — stable inner dispositions). These are character traits formed over time through sustained practice and relationship with God — not spontaneous conditions.

**[D]** E — Learned contentment (autarkēs, Phil 4:11) is explicitly developmental: "I have learned" (emathon) — the aorist of learning-through-experience. The developmental origin is the repeated experience of varying circumstances: "I know how to be brought low, and I know how to abound" (Phil 4:12 — both forms of experience contribute to the formation of contentment). Poverty of spirit may be an original condition (constitutive openness before God) that is maintained or lost through formation.

---

**T5.1.2, T5.1.3** — The evidence indicates no single developmental origin but multiple pathways. Inner closure and hardness develop through the unchecked accumulation of material experience. Ordered character and contentment develop through the formation of wisdom-shaped inner dispositions across life's varying material circumstances.

---

### T5.2 — Maintenance and Cultivation

**T5.2.1** — What maintains or cultivates this characteristic in the person over time?

**[A]** E — Inner closure is maintained by sustained material prosperity without countervailing spiritual discipline. The sha.a.nan (at-ease) pattern is maintained by the ongoing absence of threat or challenge — the person who is never disturbed from their ease remains at ease. The wisdom tradition implies that the maintenance of inner closure requires no effort — it is the default outcome of unchallenged material prosperity.

**[C]** E — The ordered character-wealth relationship is cultivated through the practices the wisdom tradition names: diligence in work (Pro 10:4), trust in the Lord (Pro 28:25 — ongoing orientation, not a single act), humility and fear of the Lord (Pro 22:4 — dispositions sustained over time), and the honouring of God with wealth (Pro 3:9 — ongoing practice of firstfruits). M46-C's ordered relationship requires active cultivation.

**[D]** E — Learned contentment is cultivated through lived experience across varying circumstances (Phil 4:11-12) and through the ongoing relationship with God that provides the inner resource (Phil 4:13 — "through him who strengthens me"). The cultivation of M46-D's postures requires both experience and relationship with Christ — neither alone is sufficient.

---

**T5.2.2, T5.2.3** — Inner closure requires no cultivation — it is the default of unchecked material prosperity. Ordered character and contentment require sustained cultivation through practice, wisdom-formation, and relationship with God.

---

### T5.3 — Testing and Adversity

**T5.3.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to testing?

**[A]** E — Material prosperity is evidenced as a specific form of testing — not the testing of adversity but the testing of abundance. The prosperity-apostasy pattern (Deu 32:15; Neh 9:25-26; Hos 10:1) shows that abundance is the test: will the person remain tethered to God in material fullness? The wealth-testing of Rev 3:14-22 (the Laodicean church) is the NT crystallisation of this pattern. Wealth is a different form of testing from suffering, but equally — perhaps more subtly — testing.

**[B]** E — The wealthy person's treatment of the poor is evidenced as a test: the parable of 2Sa 12:1-4 is explicitly a test-story that reveals character (Nathan's parable to David — "you are the man," 2Sa 12:7). The community's treatment of the wealthy visitor vs the poor person (Jam 2:2-4) is presented as a test of inner condition.

**[C]** E — M46-C frames the entire wealth domain as a testing ground for character: the quality of the wealthy person's character is revealed by the quality of their wealth (Pro 15:6) and by what they do with it (Pro 3:9; Pro 28:25). The wisdom tradition's wealth-ethics is a sustained testing framework.

**[D]** E — The voluntary release in M46-D is specifically the test of whether the higher valuation is genuine. The rich young ruler's failure (Luk 18:23) is the test of whether his inner valuation of eternal life exceeded his attachment to wealth — the test revealed the attachment. Zacchaeus's success (Luk 19:8) is the same test with the opposite outcome.

---

**T5.3.2** — Does testing deepen, reveal, or produce this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain reveals rather than produces inner conditions. Material prosperity does not create the inner character it reveals; it exposes what is already constituted — the prosperous-apostasy pattern reveals an inner orientation that was present before the prosperity; the voluntary release reveals the genuine valuation that was held before the test. The cluster's verse evidence consistently shows wealth as a revealer, not a creator, of inner condition.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering?

**[D]** E — M46-D's poverty vocabulary is embedded in suffering: the poor (ptōchos) suffer material deprivation; the widow who gave all was in genuine economic vulnerability; the Macedonian churches gave from "extreme poverty" (huperbathē ptōcheia, 2Cor 8:2). The cluster's M46-D evidence shows suffering (material poverty and deprivation) as the context in which the ordered inner postures (giving, contentment) are exercised and revealed.

**[A]** E — M46-A's prophetic judgment passages (Jam 5:1-5; Rev 18:15-19) describe the suffering that comes upon those whose wealth-produced inner closure results in judgment. The merchants weeping (Rev 18:15-19) and the rich howling (Jam 5:1) are the suffering that follows inner-closure's harvest.

**[B]** E — The poor in M46-B suffer from the hardness of the wealthy: Lazarus in suffering (Luk 16:20-21), the poor person dishonored in the assembly (Jam 2:3-6), the poor man whose lamb is taken (2Sa 12:4). M46-B names the suffering that wealth-produced hardness inflicts on the poor.

---

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering deepen, test, or produce this characteristic?

**[D]** E — In M46-D, material deprivation deepens and tests the inner postures: Paul's learned contentment is produced through experience of "being brought low" (Phil 4:12 — etapeiphthēnai, to be humbled/brought low). The Macedonian churches' extreme poverty is the context in which their generosity is revealed. Material suffering is the school in which M46-D's contentment and generosity are formed.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation?

**[D]** E — Learned contentment (autarkēs, Phil 4:11) is explicitly a formation outcome: "I have learned" (emathon) — a perfect learning that implies a process through which the person has been formed. The contentment is not spontaneous but acquired through the formation of character across varied material circumstances. This is the most explicit formation-language in the cluster.

**[C]** E — The wisdom tradition's character-wealth connection (M46-C) presupposes formation: diligence, trust, humility, and fear of the Lord are all formed dispositions — they do not appear fully formed but are cultivated through time, practice, and relationship with God (Pro 22:6 — train up a child in the way they should go; the wisdom of the father addressing the son in Proverbs presupposes formation across a lifetime).

**[A]** E — M46-A's formation trajectory is negative: the prosperity-apostasy pattern represents the formation of closure through unchecked abundance. The at-ease condition (sha.a.nan) is formed over time by sustained material security without spiritual formation countering it.

---

**T5.5.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in the longer arc?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The wealth domain is one of Scripture's primary formation arenas. The person's relationship with material goods shapes their inner character over time in both directions: without wisdom-formation and spiritual discipline, wealth forms inner closure and hardness; with wisdom-formation and spiritual discipline, wealth is the context in which ordered character and learned contentment are formed. M46 is a formation cluster — the wealth domain is where the inner person is formed, tested, revealed, and either deepened or distorted.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness?

**[A]** E — The Laodicean church's inner closure has an eschatological resolution offered: Rev 3:18-20 (counsel to buy refined gold, to be truly rich; to open the door to Christ). The eschatological direction for M46-A is the transformation of false wealth into true wealth through the refining process (Rev 3:18 — "gold refined by fire"). The inner-closed person is not abandoned to their closure but invited toward an eschatological wealth that transcends material condition.

**[B]** E — M46-B has an eschatological reversal: Luk 6:24 ("you have received your consolation"), Luk 16:22-25 (the rich man in Hades, Lazarus at Abraham's side — the eschatological reversal of the wealth-poverty differential), and Rev 18 (the judgment and fall of Babylon's wealth-system). The eschatological trajectory of M46-B is judgment and reversal.

**[C]** E — M46-C's eschatological trajectory: Isa 33:6 (the fear of the Lord as Zion's treasure — the eschatological community whose wealth is the inner richness of knowing God). The eschatological fullness of M46-C is the complete integration of material and spiritual goodness — a community whose true cho.sen is salvation, wisdom, knowledge, and the fear of the Lord.

**[D]** E — M46-D's eschatological orientation is explicit across multiple verses. Mat 5:3 ("theirs is the kingdom of heaven") — the present tense names a present reality with eschatological fullness. Heb 11:26 ("looking to the reward") — the revaluation of wealth in M46-D is explicitly forward-looking. Mat 19:21 ("you will have treasure in heaven") — the voluntary release in M46-D produces eschatological treasure. The inner life beyond material circumstance is the present form of what the eschatological order will fully establish.

**T5.6.2, T5.6.3** — The present experience of M46-D anticipates the eschatological fullness in which the kingdom fully belongs to the poor in spirit, the generous, and the contented. No silence.

### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**T6.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show consistent sequential relationships?

**[A]** E — The most consistent and multiply-evidenced sequence in the entire cluster: material provision received → satiation → fat → turning from God / moral failure. Evidenced in Deu 32:15, Neh 9:25-26, Hos 10:1, Jer 5:27-28. This is a causal developmental sequence — not merely correlation but an evidenced mechanism. The sequence is not inevitable (M46-C and M46-D show the alternative) but it is systematic.

**[D]** E — M46-D shows a consistent counter-sequence: encounter with true value → inner revaluation → material act of release → enrichment of others and/or eschatological treasure. Moses (Heb 11:26 → Heb 11:27-29 — the exodus from Egypt), Zacchaeus (Luk 19:5-8 — encounter → announcement → restitution), the widow (Luk 21:1-4 — in poverty → gave all → declared greater giver). The sequence is: higher valuation → inner reorientation → costly act.

---

**T6.2.2** — Is the relationship causal, developmental, or correlational?

**[A]** E — The M46-A sequence is causal and developmental: material prosperity causes satiation; satiation produces fat-heartedness; fat-heartedness causes turning from God. The multiple independent witnesses (Deu, Hos, Neh, Jer) confirm the causal-developmental character — it is not accidental co-occurrence.

**[D]** E — The M46-D sequence is also causal: the revaluation act (Moses counting reproach as greater wealth) causes the volitional choice; the choice causes the act; the act produces its own enrichment (Pro 11:25 — whoever brings blessing will be enriched; da.shen).

---

**T6.2.3** — If no sequential pattern, note explicitly. Sequential patterns are present and significant in M46.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms and what do their root meanings reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Primary OT terms: H1952 hon (substance, material goods — from a root meaning "to be" or "existence"; names the material stuff of life), H6239 o.sher (riches — from H6238 a.shar "to be/become rich"; the abstract noun of the state of being wealthy), H2633 cho.sen (wealth, treasure — from a root meaning "to lay up/store"), H1878 da.shen (to prosper/grow fat — from a root meaning fatness, richness; the prosperity and the physical fat share a root, naming prosperity as inner-physical richness), H8080 sha.men (to grow fat — same root as sha.men adj, richness/fatness), H7600 sha.a.nan (to be at ease/complacent — from a root meaning peaceful security or carelessness). Primary NT terms: G4145 plousios (rich — from a root meaning "full, to fill, fullness"); G4434 ptōchos (poor — from a root meaning "to crouch, to shrink"; names the poor as those who crouch before others in supplication); G0842 autarkēs (self-sufficient/content — from autos "self" + arkeo "to be enough"; literally "one for whom the self is sufficient", a term coined in Stoic philosophy and adapted by Paul). The root analysis reveals: prosperity in Hebrew is consistently metaphorised through physical fatness/richness (sha.men, da.shen); poverty in Greek is metaphorised through the bodily posture of the supplicant (ptōchos — crouching); contentment in Greek is the philosophical achievement of inner self-sufficiency.

---

**T7.1.2** — What is the grammatical range and what does it reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's remarkable grammatical range: nouns (o.sher, hon, cho.sen, ploutos — naming the state of wealth/riches), verbs (da.shen — to prosper/grow fat; sha.man — to grow fat; a.shar — to become rich; plouteō — to be/become rich; ploutizō — to enrich; ptōcheuō — to be poor), adjectives (sha.men — rich; plousios — rich; ptōchos — poor), adverbs (plousiōs — richly). This full grammatical range (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) is unusually complete. The verbs are particularly significant: da.shen, sha.man, a.shar (becoming rich or growing fat) name the process; plouteō (being rich) names the state; ploutizō (to enrich) names the action of making another rich. The grammatical range reveals that the wealth domain is described in Scripture at every grammatical level — as state, process, quality, action, and degree — confirming the domain-cluster character of M46.

---

**T7.1.3** — What is the semantic range?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The semantic range of the cluster's vocabulary is exceptionally broad: from physical fat/richness (sha.men — the literal fatness of animals and persons) through material wealth (o.sher, hon, cho.sen — various aspects of material goods) through the inner condition of prosperity or poverty (da.shen applied to ne.phesh — inner richness; ptōchos tō pneumati — poverty in spirit) through eschatological and Christological wealth (plousios applied to Christ, ploutos applied to the riches of grace and glory in Ephesians) through the philosophical-theological concept of self-sufficient contentment (autarkēs). The semantic range spans the physical, material, inner, relational, divine, eschatological, and philosophical registers — confirming that the wealth vocabulary in Scripture is not confined to economics but extends into every register of meaning.

---

**T7.1.4** — Does the vocabulary distinguish distinct aspects?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Yes, with precision. Disposition vs act: sha.a.nan (the at-ease condition — a stable disposition) vs sha.man (to grow fat — a process); autarkēs (the stable contentment disposition) vs ploutizō (the active enrichment of others). State vs process: o.sher/ploutos (the state of being wealthy) vs a.shar/plouteō (the process of becoming wealthy). Received vs self-generated: pro 10:22 ("the Lord's blessing makes rich") vs Pro 28:20 ("whoever hastens to be rich") — the divine-given and the self-sought are linguistically distinct contexts within the same verb. The vocabulary makes finer distinctions than the English translations "rich" and "poor" convey.

---

**T7.1.5** — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Yes, and this is one of the cluster's most distinctive features: ptōchos (poor) is the explicit structural counterpart to plousios (rich) in the NT. This rich/poor pair is unique in the programme's vocabulary in having both poles explicitly named as co-terms within the same cluster. The poverty vocabulary (ptōchos, ptōcheuō, penēs — the last not in the cluster but adjacent) provides the counterpart to the wealth vocabulary at every register. The cluster is bi-polar: it names both wealth and poverty as inner-being domains.

---

**T7.1.6** — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Yes: a.shir (H6223 — the rich person), plousios (G4145 — the rich person in adjectival/noun use), ptōchos (G4434 — the poor person). All three are person-type terms naming persons defined by their material condition. This is unusual: most clusters do not have person-type terms; M46 has three — reflecting the OT and NT tradition's use of the rich/poor contrast as a moral category of persons, not merely material conditions.

---

**T7.1.7** — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term?

**[CLUSTER]** S — No specific seeking/supplication term for wealth is evidenced in the cluster's vocabulary. The closest is Pro 23:4 ("do not toil to acquire wealth") — the negative command implies the drive to seek wealth, but the seeking-drive itself does not have a dedicated term in the cluster. This silence is noted.

---

**T7.1.8** — What does the OT-to-NT relationship reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The relationship between the OT Hebrew and NT Greek wealth vocabulary reveals significant continuity and development. Continuity: the wealth-character relationship (M46-C), the wealth-trust relationship (M46-A's misplaced trust), and the rich/poor social-moral contrast (M46-B) all appear in both testaments. The OT wisdom tradition's engagement with wealth (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes) provides the conceptual framework that the NT applies and deepens. Development: the NT adds three elements absent from the OT: (1) the Christological poverty-for-enrichment pattern (2Cor 8:9 — entirely absent from the OT), (2) the ptōchos tō pneumati (poverty of spirit, Mat 5:3 — the inner-being extension of poverty vocabulary beyond the material), and (3) the autarkēs / autarkeia vocabulary (Phil 4:11 — the Stoic-adapted contentment concept that Paul christianises). The movement is from OT material-moral (wealth as the testing ground of character and trust) to NT inner-being (wealth as the domain where Christological patterns of poverty-for-enrichment and contentment are formed and expressed).

---

**T7.1.9** — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Autarkēs (G0842 — self-sufficient/content) and autarkeia (G0841 — self-sufficiency/contentment) are Stoic philosophical terms adopted and adapted by Paul. They are not found in the OT Hebrew tradition. The coinage (or adoption) reveals that the NT's theological account of contentment in the wealth domain draws on the best philosophical vocabulary available (Stoic self-sufficiency) while entirely reframing its ground: Paul's autarkeia is not grounded in Stoic rational self-mastery but in "him who strengthens me" (Phil 4:13). The term is borrowed; the foundation is transformed.

---

**T7.1.10** — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The full vocabulary arc of M46 — from the physical fat/oil of OT sha.men and da.shen through the material-moral wealth of o.sher, hon, cho.sen, a.shar, a.shir through the NT's plousios/ptōchos moral-social pair through the Christological ptōcheuō/ploutizō exchange through the revalued ploutos of generosity (2Cor 8:2; Heb 11:26) through the autarkēs contentment of Phil 4:11 — reveals a complete semantic arc from the physical to the Christological to the formed-inner-life. The cluster's vocabulary does not simplify as it develops but becomes more theologically rich, with each NT deployment of the wealth vocabulary carrying the OT's material-moral weight and adding Christological and eschatological dimensions.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?

**[A]** E — Anchor: Rev 3:17 (plousios). Function: plousios is the predicate of the self-declaration ("I am rich/plousios") — the term names the inner self-assessment that constitutes the Laodicean church's problem. The term functions as the identity-claim whose falsity the rest of the verse exposes. Plousios is the subject of their self-perception and the object of Christ's counter-assessment.

**[C]** E — Anchor: Pro 13:4 (da.shen). Function: da.shen is the predicate applied to the ne.phesh (soul) — "the soul of the diligent is richly supplied (da.shen)." The term applies the prosperity-vocabulary to the soul as its direct object of enrichment. It functions as the connective: character (diligence) → soul → da.shen. The term bridges inner character and inner prosperity.

**[D]** E — Anchor: 2Cor 8:9 (ptōcheuō). Function: ptōcheuō is the main verb of the Christ-clause — "he became poor (ptōcheuō) for your sake." The term names the central christological act. It functions as the pivot of the verse: rich (plousios) → ptōcheuō → rich (plouteō for others). The term is the means of the Christological wealth-exchange.

---

**T7.2.2** — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence, and what does it require?

**[CLUSTER]** E — M46's verse evidence spans the widest literary range of any cluster in this study. Wisdom/Proverbs (M46-C's dominant form — Pro 13:4; Pro 15:6; Pro 22:4): requires reading as crystallised observational wisdom — distilled from repeated observation of the created order, not individual narrative. Epistle/letter (M46-D dominant for autarkēs — Phil 4:11; 2Cor 8:9): requires reading in the context of the apostle's pastoral and theological argument — the contentment statement in Phil 4:11 is embedded in thanksgiving and community instruction. Apocalyptic/letter (M46-A dominant — Rev 3:17): requires reading the symbolic world of Revelation's seven letters — the Laodicean wealth is both literal (the city's known material wealth) and symbolic (the spiritual condition of the community). Prophetic/poetry (M46-A's sha.men verb content — Isa 6:10; Deu 32:15; Jer 5:28): requires reading as prophetic discourse in which physical imagery (fat, fat heart) carries spiritual weight. Narrative (M46-B — 2Sa 12:1-4; Luk 16:19-22): requires reading as exemplary narrative — the rich man's behaviour is not merely descriptive but paradigmatic of the inner condition being named.

---

**T7.2.3** — What is the logical structure of key arguments?

**[A]** E — Rev 3:17 argument: Premise 1: you say "I am rich, I have prospered, I need nothing" (self-assessment). Premise 2: you do not realise that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked (divine counter-assessment). Conclusion (implicit): your self-assessment is false; your actual condition is the opposite of what you believe. The argument is a self-assessment exposure argument — revealing the gap between self-perception and reality.

**[D]** E — 2Cor 8:9 argument: Premise 1: Christ was rich (plousios). Premise 2: Christ became poor (ptōcheuō) for your sake. Conclusion: you became rich (plouteō) through his poverty. The argument is a Christological exchange argument — the voluntarily impoverished one produces richness in others precisely through the impoverishment. The logic inverts the normal wealth-transfer expectation (giving from abundance, not from poverty).

---

**T7.2.4** — What contextual settings carry the primary evidence?

**[CLUSTER]** E — M46's primary contextual settings: Covenantal (Deu 32:15; Neh 9:25 — the covenant relationship between Israel and God, with material prosperity as the context of covenantal faithfulness or failure); Wisdom-instruction (the Proverbs and Ecclesiastes wisdom-school setting — teacher addressing student about wealth and character); Prophetic judgment (Amos 6:1; Mic 6:12; Jer 5:27-28 — the prophetic court-speech addressing the community's moral failures in the wealth domain); Pastoral-epistolary (Phil 4:11; 2Cor 8:9 — Paul addressing communities in specific pastoral situations about their relationship with material goods and generosity); Apocalyptic-epistolary (Rev 3:17-18 — the risen Christ's address to the community about their spiritual-material condition).

---

**T7.2.5** — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — Two verses contend for the cluster's primary anchor across all sub-groups: Rev 3:17 (the definitive statement of the wealth domain's inner-being risk — false self-assessment through material prosperity) and 2Cor 8:9 (the definitive Christological paradigm of the wealth domain's redemption — voluntary impoverishment for others' enrichment). Rev 3:17 anchors M46-A's distortion; 2Cor 8:9 anchors M46-D's resolution. The cluster has not one but two primary anchors at opposite poles — confirming its domain-cluster character: the domain's risk (M46-A) and the domain's resolution (M46-D) are each represented by a definitive anchor.

---

**T7.2.6** — What does the primary anchor reveal that no other verse reveals?

**[A/Rev 3:17]** E — Reveals uniquely: the simultaneity of self-assessed wealth and actual inner poverty. No other verse in the cluster makes the gap between perceived and actual inner condition so precisely explicit. "Not realising that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" — five specific conditions of actual inner poverty simultaneously present alongside the self-declared "I am rich." The verse uniquely names the structure of self-deception in the wealth domain: not that the person is merely wrong, but that they are unable to perceive their actual condition because of their self-assessed richness.

**[D/2Cor 8:9]** E — Reveals uniquely: the Christological legitimation of voluntary impoverishment as the paradigmatic act of the wealth domain. No other verse in the cluster grounds the voluntary release pattern in the person of Christ. The verse uniquely shows that the movement from wealth to poverty for others' enrichment is not primarily a moral strategy or a wisdom observation — it is participation in what Christ did. M46-D's postures are Christologically grounded in a way that 2Cor 8:9 alone makes explicit.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?

**[A, B]** E — Kasser and Ryan's materialism research (social and clinical psychology) is the primary framework for M46-A and M46-B. Materialism research provides the most direct scientific account of the mechanisms the cluster names: the empirical finding that financial goal-prioritisation reduces wellbeing even when achieved correlates with M46-A's inner closure; the hedonic treadmill (never-satisfied condition) correlates with M46-B's insatiability; social comparison amplification correlates with M46-B's false-friendship pattern.

**[C]** E — Self-determination theory (Deci and Ryan) is the primary framework for M46-C: the finding that intrinsic motivation (values-driven activity) produces higher wellbeing than extrinsic motivation (wealth-seeking) provides the scientific account of why M46-C's character-ordered wealth relationship produces flourishing while M46-A's wealth-as-goal produces closure.

**[D]** E — Prosocial spending research (Dunn, Aknin, and Norton) is the primary framework for M46-D's giving patterns: the finding that spending on others produces higher wellbeing than spending on self, across 136 countries, provides the scientific correlate of M46-D's Pro 11:25 observation (whoever brings blessing will be enriched / da.shen). Self-determination theory also applies to M46-D's contentment (autarkēs): the autonomous, intrinsically-grounded inner posture of contentment aligns with SDT's account of psychological wellbeing.

---

**T7.3.2** — Where the science illuminates, what does it reveal?

**[A, B]** E — Materialism research illuminates M46-A and M46-B with precision. The empirical finding that materialism reduces wellbeing even when achieved is the scientific correlate of the cluster's central insight. Specifically: Kasser's aspiration index research shows that financial goal prioritisation above intrinsic goals produces the measurable psychological effects the cluster names — reduced wellbeing, increased anxiety, impaired relational capacity. The hedonic treadmill (Brickman and Campbell) illuminates Ecc 4:8 ("eyes never satisfied with riches"): the mechanism by which material acquisition fails to produce satisfaction is the adaptation of desire to new levels — scientific confirmation of the insatiability the cluster names. This makes M46-B's insatiability more coherent: it is not merely a moral failure but a predictable psychological mechanism.

**[D]** E — Prosocial spending research (Dunn, Aknin, Norton) illuminates Pro 11:25's da.shen mechanism (whoever brings blessing will be enriched): the scientific finding that giving produces higher wellbeing than receiving provides the empirical account of why the giving pattern of M46-D enriches the giver. The "giver's high" (mesolimbic reward system activation) provides a physiological account of the joy in giving evidenced in 2Cor 8:2.

---

**T7.3.3** — Where the verse evidence and science diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

**[D]** E — The most significant divergence: Paul's autarkēs (Phil 4:11 — "I have learned to be content in whatever situation") claims circumstantial independence — the contentment is stable regardless of material condition. SDT places contentment as need-dependent: psychological wellbeing requires satisfaction of autonomy, competence, and relatedness needs. Paul's contentment, by contrast, is explicitly not need-satisfaction-dependent but is grounded in relationship with Christ ("I can do all things through him who strengthens me," Phil 4:13). This is genuine divergence: the ground of contentment differs fundamentally. SDT: internal needs satisfied → contentment. Paul: relationship with Christ → contentment regardless of need-satisfaction state. The divergence reveals that Paul's autarkeia is a theologically distinct category — it names a contentment that is constitutionally beyond what SDT's account can reach.

**[C]** E — A secondary divergence: M46-C's wisdom tradition claims that diligence and character produce wealth (Pro 13:4; 10:4). SDT and positive psychology confirm that character-strengths produce wellbeing — but the specific material-wealth outcome (wealth as the fruit of diligence) is not confirmed by science in the way the wisdom tradition claims. The science shows character → wellbeing; the cluster claims character → material-wellbeing. Register divergence (not genuine contradiction): the wisdom tradition includes the material domain in its account of flourishing; science describes flourishing without necessarily including material outcome.

---

**T7.3.4** — Does the science surface aspects the verse evidence has not addressed?

**[A, B]** E — The materialism research surfaces the developmental-origins dimension of inner closure and insatiability: Kasser's research on childhood insecurity as a predictor of adult materialism provides a developmental account of how M46-A's inner closure is formed over time. The cluster's verse evidence names the prosperity-apostasy sequence but does not specify developmental origins. The science's developmental account (insecure attachment → adult materialism → wealth-produced closure) complements and deepens the cluster's structural account. This is a gap in the verse evidence (no developmental-origins narrative for inner closure in the cluster's evidence) that science partially addresses.

**[B]** E — Mullainathan and Shafir's scarcity research surfaces the cognitive dimension of poverty: the bandwidth tax of financial scarcity impairs cognitive function. The cluster's ptōchos vocabulary does not address the cognitive impairment of poverty — it addresses poverty as a social-moral condition and as an inner posture of dependence. The cognitive-capacity dimension of material poverty is a genuine gap in the cluster's verse evidence that the scarcity research addresses. This may warrant further verse investigation in adjacent clusters (Wisdom, M15).
