## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A — Inner closure]** S — The sub-group does not evidence God's nature in a reflective mode. What it evidences is the divine negative — God using the prosperity-fatness mechanism as judgment (Isa 6:10: "make the heart of this people fat"). This is not a reflection of divine character in the human person but divine judicial action on the human person. No positive divine-nature reflection is evidenced in M46-A.

**[B — Hardness and insatiability]** S — The sub-group evidences how wealth produces failures of pity and satisfaction in the human person. No divine-nature reflection is evidenced — the hardness toward Lazarus (Luk 16:19-21), the contempt in the assembly (Jam 2:2-3), and the deceitfulness of riches (Mat 13:22) all describe human inner failure, not divine character.

**[C — The ordering of character and wealth]** E — Pro 8:18 evidences Wisdom claiming "riches and honour are with me, enduring wealth and righteousness." The divine nature reflected here is the integration of material abundance with righteousness — in God (through Wisdom), prosperity and moral integrity coexist without conflict. Isa 33:6 extends this: "the fear of the Lord is Zion's treasure (cho.sen)" — God himself is identified as the source and substance of what true wealth is. The cluster's M46-C evidence suggests that God's nature is one in which material goodness and moral goodness are unified, not opposed.

**[D — The inner life beyond material circumstance]** E — 2Cor 8:9 evidences the most explicit divine-nature statement in the cluster: "though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich." God's nature — specifically Christ's divine richness — is the origin point of the Christological wealth-exchange. God is rich (plousios) in a way that enables voluntary impoverishment for others' enrichment. Eph 2:4 adds "God, being rich in mercy" — divine richness as inexhaustible generosity. The plousiōs provision of 1Ti 6:17 ("God who richly provides all things for enjoyment") names the quality of divine provision as abundant, generous, and purposefully enabling human flourishing.

---

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[A, B]** S — Neither sub-group's evidence attributes inner-closure or hardness/insatiability to God. These are human-only patterns in the cluster's evidence base.

**[C]** E — Pro 8:18 and Isa 33:6 attribute to God (through Wisdom) the quality of possessing wealth aligned with righteousness. 1Sa 2:7 and 1Ch 29:12 attribute to God direct sovereignty over wealth: "the Lord makes poor and makes rich" (1Sa 2:7), "both riches and honor come from you" (1Ch 29:12). God's relation to wealth is not possession but governance — he gives and withholds wealth as expressions of his sovereign purposes.

**[D]** E — Three explicit attributions: Christ is plousios (rich) before becoming poor (2Cor 8:9); God is plousios in mercy (Eph 2:4); God provides plousiōs (richly, 1Ti 6:17). These attributions reveal that in God, abundance is not self-referential but outward-directed — divine richness exists to be given, not accumulated. The human person's ordered relation to wealth (M46-D) reflects this: the giving-from-poverty pattern mirrors the divine pattern of giving-from-abundance.

---

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[CLUSTER]** The characteristic God explicitly does not possess: inner closure (M46-A) and hardness/insatiability (M46-B). The silence on God's possessing these characteristics confirms that they are disorders — conditions of the image-bearer that represent departure from, not expression of, the divine image. The prosperity-apostasy mechanism (M46-A) and the failure of pity (M46-B) are explicitly absent from the divine nature. This silence is analytically significant: M46-A and M46-B name the distortions of the image, not the image itself.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[A]** S — Inner closure does not appear as a created purpose. The sha.men verb (Isa 6:10) presents it as divine judicial action, and the Deu 32:15 pattern presents it as the failure of the created capacity for gratitude and dependence. Inner closure is the distortion of a created purpose, not itself a created purpose.

**[B]** S — Hardness toward others and insatiability within are not evidenced as created purposes. The cha.mal (pity) whose absence is named in 2Sa 12:4 and Luk 16:19-21 is itself a created capacity — hardness is its absence or erosion, not its exercise.

**[C]** E — M46-C evidences a created purpose clearly: the human person is equipped to receive and steward material goods as the fruit of right character and as the gift of divine provision. Pro 13:4 ("the soul of the diligent is richly supplied") suggests the created design: inner character (diligence) produces material sufficiency that serves the flourishing of the soul (ne.phesh). 1Ti 6:17 ("God who richly provides all things for enjoyment") names the created purpose of material goods: to be received and enjoyed. The purpose is not accumulation but reception with enjoyment.

**[D]** E — M46-D evidences the created purpose of the inner life's independence from material circumstance. Autarkēs (Phil 4:11 — learned contentment) names the capacity the human person is designed to inhabit: an inner stability that is not determined by material condition. Mat 5:3 (blessed are the poor in spirit) names poverty of spirit — the inner posture of dependence and openness — as itself a created capacity that enables kingdom reception. The created purpose evidenced in M46-D: the human person is equipped to receive from God and to give to others, without being anchored to material circumstance as the ground of their inner security.

---

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A]** E — The inner-closure pattern is a response to the fallen condition, produced by material prosperity in the absence of the tethering-to-God that right creation would sustain. Deu 32:15 names the pattern as failure (forsook God, scoffed at the Rock) — it presupposes a prior right relationship that is then broken by satiation. Not original design; a fallen pattern.

**[B]** E — Same analysis: hardness toward the poor (Luk 16:19-21; Jam 2:2-6) and insatiability (Ecc 4:8; 6:2) are descriptions of the fallen condition of the inner person in relation to wealth. They are not original design but the distortion of created capacities (compassion, the capacity to enjoy).

**[C]** E — M46-C reflects original design — the wisdom tradition's observation that character produces ordered wealth is a creation observation, not a fall-recovery observation. Pro 13:4, Pro 10:22 ("the Lord's blessing makes rich, and he adds no sorrow with it") and 1Ti 6:17 all describe how the material domain works when the inner person is rightly ordered. Both original design (wealth as fruit of right character) and response to fallenness (wisdom's warnings against hastening to be rich) are present.

**[D]** E — M46-D is both. Poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) appears to be an original design — the human person before God is constitutively dependent, open, receptive. The learned contentment of autarkēs (Phil 4:11 — "I have learned") is explicitly a formed response through circumstances, not an original state — it is acquired through the experience of abundance and scarcity in the fallen world. The voluntary release pattern (2Cor 8:9; Gen 14:23) is a response to the fallen economic order — it would not be needed in the original creation where material goods were gifts to be received without the distortion of hoarding.

---

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?

**[A]** E — M46-A has an eschatological reversal: Rev 3:17-18 (the Laodicean church counselled to "buy from me gold refined by fire, so that you may be rich") points toward a future resolution of the inner-closure condition. The presently-blind person can move toward genuine richness through the refining process. The eschatological direction of M46-A is remediation — the fat heart can be made perceptive again.

**[B]** E — M46-B has an eschatological reversal in the rich/poor contrast: Luk 6:24 ("you have received your consolation") implies that the consolation available in the age to come is not available to those who have exhausted their consolation in material satisfaction. The direction is toward an eschatological ordering where the poor are blessed (Luk 6:20) and the insatiable are emptied (Luk 1:53: "the rich he has sent away empty").

**[C]** E — M46-C points toward the fullness of the ordered integration of character and wealth in the coming kingdom. Isa 33:6 ("abundance of salvation, wisdom, and knowledge; the fear of the Lord is Zion's treasure") describes an eschatological condition where the true cho.sen is the inner richness of knowing God. The eschatological direction of M46-C is the complete integration of material goodness and inner goodness that the wisdom tradition describes as the goal.

**[D]** E — M46-D has the clearest eschatological orientation. Mat 5:3 ("theirs is the kingdom of heaven") places poverty of spirit as the condition of kingdom possession — present in the imperfect now, but oriented toward the kingdom's fullness. Moses's inner revaluation (Heb 11:26 — counting reproach of Christ as greater wealth than Egypt's treasures) is explicitly forward-looking: "he was looking to the reward." The eschatological direction of M46-D: the learned contentment and voluntary release that characterise M46-D are the present form of what the kingdom's material ordering will fully instantiate.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**[A, B]** S — Inner closure and hardness/insatiability do not express the divine image — they represent its impairment. What they do reveal is the shape of the image by negative: the created person is designed for openness to God (not inner closure), for compassion toward others (not hardness), and for the capacity to receive and enjoy what they have (not insatiability). The image is legible in these sub-groups through its distortion.

**[C]** E — M46-C expresses the divine image in the capacity to receive and steward material goods as gifts from God with righteousness. The righteous person whose house contains wealth alongside enduring righteousness (Psa 112:3) instantiates the divine character in which abundance and moral integrity coexist. This is image-bearing in the economic domain.

**[D]** E — M46-D expresses the divine image most fully in the cluster. The voluntary release pattern (giving from abundance or poverty to enrich others) mirrors the divine pattern (2Cor 8:9). Poverty of spirit — constitutive openness and dependence before God — instantiates the creatureliness that the image-bearer is designed for: not self-sufficient before God but receptive to divine gift. Learned contentment (autarkēs) expresses the divine image in the economic domain: the person who is settled regardless of material circumstance reflects a dimension of divine self-sufficiency that does not depend on created goods.

---

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue?

**[CLUSTER]** E — M46 has both genuinely shared and analogical elements. What is genuinely shared: God is plousios (rich), and Christ voluntarily became ptōchos (poor). The human acts of generous giving (M46-D) participate in the divine pattern established by 2Cor 8:9. The learned contentment of autarkēs may be an analogue to divine self-sufficiency rather than a direct sharing — God's autarkeia is ontological and unconditioned; Paul's is acquired through formation. The ordered reception of wealth (M46-C) is an analogue: God creates wealth and gives it; the human receives and stewards it. Inner closure (M46-A) and hardness (M46-B) are exclusively creaturely disorders — no analogues in the divine nature.

---

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

**[A]** E — The presence of inner closure reveals significant image-impairment: the person who is rich and needs nothing (Rev 3:17) is "wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" — the language names the collapse of the image-bearing condition. Blindness (cannot perceive), wretchedness (inner desolation), poverty (actual deprivation in the register that matters) are all image-diminishment markers.

**[B]** E — The presence of hardness reveals the erosion of the image's compassion-dimension. The rich man ignoring Lazarus (Luk 16:19-21) and the assembly preferring the wealthy visitor (Jam 2:2-3) reveal the social-relational face of image-bearing severely compromised. The capacity for pity (cha.mal) whose absence is named in 2Sa 12:4 is a created capacity — its absence marks image-distortion.

**[C]** E — The presence of ordered character-wealth integration reveals a healthy image. Psa 112:3 ("wealth and riches in his house, and his righteousness endures forever") describes a person in whom image-bearing is intact: material abundance and moral integrity coexist, reflecting the divine character.

**[D]** E — The presence of poverty of spirit and learned contentment reveals an image-bearer who has been formed toward the kingdom. Mat 5:3 names poverty of spirit as a condition of kingdom possession — the most fundamental expression of the image's openness before God. The person who has learned autarkeia (Phil 4:11) has been formed toward the divine independence from circumstance.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?

**[A, B, C]** S — The OT evidence in M46-A, M46-B, and M46-C is largely non-typological. The sha.men, hon, o.sher, a.shir, and a.shar vocabulary operates in the wisdom-observation register (what prosperity does and does not produce in the inner person), not in the typological-pointing register. The sole possible exception is Deu 32:15 (Jeshurun growing fat and kicking) which operates as a pattern-statement rather than a type of a specific future reality.

**[D]** E — M46-D has the cluster's strongest typological deployment: 2Cor 8:9 (Christ became poor so you might become rich) is the explicit Christological type. The Macedonian churches' poverty-producing-generosity (2Cor 8:2) participates in the Christ-pattern. Moses's revaluation (Heb 11:26 — counting reproach as greater wealth) is retrospectively Christological: "the reproach of Christ." The voluntary-impoverishment-producing-enrichment pattern (M46-D) functions typologically — each human instance of release-for-others-enrichment participates in and points to the foundational Christological act.

---

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[D]** E — The direction is clearly established in 2Cor 8:9: the divine instance (Christ's voluntary poverty) establishes the pattern; the human instances (Macedonian generosity, Paul's apostolic poverty, the widow's giving) participate in and point toward it. The divine acts first; the human reflects and participates. This is consistent with the programme's observation across other clusters that the typological direction runs from divine act to human participation.

---

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[A, B, C]** No typological use evidenced in these sub-groups' verse evidence.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster is named "Abundance, Prosperity and Wealth." The name signals three related but distinct aspects: abundance (the condition of having much), prosperity (the dynamic of flourishing and increase), and wealth (the state of having material resources). The name does not signal an inner-being characteristic per se — it names a domain. This is consistent with the analytical finding that M46 is a domain cluster: material wealth and poverty as the domain in which inner-being characteristics operate, are tested, and revealed. The name's breadth (abundance / prosperity / wealth) reflects the semantic range of the 22 terms, which span from the neutral material descriptor (o.sher — riches) to the inner moral dynamic (sha.men verb — growing fat toward apostasy) to the resolved inner posture (autarkēs — contentment).

---

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The primary Hebrew terms operate in four registers at first encounter. Material-state terms (o.sher, hon, cho.sen — riches, substance, wealth) name the condition of having material goods, with no intrinsic moral valence. Process terms (a.shar — to become rich; da.shen — to prosper/grow fat; sha.men verb — to grow fat) name the dynamic of material acquisition or increase, again without intrinsic moral valence but loaded with contextual moral weight. Inner-state terms (sha.a.nan — at ease/complacent; autarkēs — self-sufficient/content; cha.san — to hoard) name specific inner orientations in relation to material circumstance. Person-type terms (a.shir, plousios — the rich person; ptōchos — the poor person) name the social-moral category of persons defined by their material condition. The Greek terms (plousios, ploutos, plouteō, ploutizō, ptōchos, ptōcheuō) follow the same fourfold pattern but with the additional NT theological loading: plousios in Rev 3:17 and ptōchos in Mat 5:3 both carry inner-being extensions that exceed their Greek economic senses.

---

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster name "Abundance, Prosperity and Wealth" does not itself carry directional or relational implications — but the verse evidence immediately imposes them. The moment wealth appears in Scripture it has a direction: toward God or toward self (Luk 12:21); toward others or away from them (Luk 16:19-21; Jam 2:2-6); toward trust in God or trust in riches (Psa 52:7; Pro 11:28). The constitutional implication is that wealth is never a neutral inner state — it is always an inner-being testing domain in which the direction of the person's fundamental orientation is revealed and shaped.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

**[A — Inner closure]** E — M46-A describes a condition — the inner state of the person who has grown fat through abundance. The sha.men verb pattern (Isa 6:10; Deu 32:15) names a condition produced by material prosperity: the heart becomes thick, unresponsive, closed. The sha.a.nan pattern (Amo 6:1) names the condition of complacent ease. The self-assessed wealth pattern (Rev 3:17) names a condition of inner blindness. M46-A is a condition — progressive and cumulative — not a single act or stable disposition.

**[B — Hardness and insatiability]** E — M46-B describes two related conditions: hardness toward others (the eroded capacity for pity) and insatiability within (the inability to be satisfied). Both are states of the inner person, not acts or stable dispositions in the positive sense. Hardness is the absence of cha.mal (pity); insatiability is the absence of the capacity to say "enough" (hon, Pro 30:15 — "never says enough"). M46-B names inner conditions that have been produced by the operation of wealth on the inner person.

**[C — The ordering of character and wealth]** E — M46-C operates across multiple kinds. Da.shen (Pro 13:4 — the soul of the diligent is richly supplied) names a condition produced by character. Cho.sen (Pro 15:6 — righteous has treasure) names a quality of wealth that reflects character. A.shar (Pro 10:22 — the Lord's blessing makes rich) names an act of divine provision. The wisdom tradition in M46-C sees wealth as the outcome of inner dispositions (diligence, humility, trust) — making M46-C primarily a description of the relationship between inner dispositions and material outcomes, rather than a single inner-being kind.

**[D — The inner life beyond material circumstance]** E — M46-D contains both acts and dispositions. Poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) names a disposition — the stable inner posture of dependence before God. The voluntary release (Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8; 2Cor 8:9) names inner acts — specific choices to relinquish wealth. Learned contentment (autarkēs, Phil 4:11) names an achieved disposition — the stable inner settledness that has been formed through experience. M46-D is the most complex sub-group in terms of inner-being kind: it holds posture, act, and achieved disposition in a unified frame.

---

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster is constitutively complex. At first encounter from the gloss list (abundance, be poor, be rich, fatness, poor, rich, riches, richly, secure, self-sufficient, substance, to enrich, to grow fat, to hoard, to luxuriate, to prosper, wealth) the vocabulary spans states, processes, persons, and orientations — no single simple structure. The four sub-groups are themselves the analytical answer to this complexity: M46 is not one inner-being phenomenon but a domain in which four distinct inner-being phenomena (inner closure, hardness and insatiability, ordering of character and wealth, life beyond material circumstance) operate, each with its own structure.

---

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

**[A]** E — Inner closure is the progressive condition of the inner person who has received material abundance without the inner tethering of trust in God and gratitude: the heart grows fat and unresponsive (sha.men, Isa 6:10; Deu 32:15), trust is redirected to material security (Pro 11:28; Psa 52:7), and self-assessment becomes anchored in material condition (Rev 3:17). The primary faculty engaged is trust (ba.tach) in its misdirected form. The impact: the person cannot perceive, cannot receive from God, and cannot evaluate their own inner condition accurately.

**[B]** E — Hardness and insatiability are two related inner-life failures produced by abundance. Hardness: the person whose capacity for pity (cha.mal) has been eroded by wealth becomes unable to see or respond to the suffering of others (Luk 16:19-21; 2Sa 12:4; Jam 2:2-3). Insatiability: the person whose inner capacity for satisfaction has been damaged cannot receive what they have as sufficient (Ecc 4:8; 6:2; Pro 30:15). Both represent the inner person's contraction — unable to give outward, unable to receive inward.

**[C]** E — The ordering of character and wealth: the wisdom tradition's consistent observation that inner character (diligence, trust, humility, fear of God) determines the quality and trajectory of material life. Pro 13:4 is the clearest formulation: the soul (ne.phesh) of the diligent is richly supplied — inner character produces inner richness that includes and exceeds material wealth. Isa 33:6 names the telos: the fear of the Lord is Zion's true treasure. Material wealth is the surface; character is the depth.

**[D]** E — The inner life beyond material circumstance: the cluster of inner postures and acts through which the person is no longer governed by the wealth domain. Poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) — the constitutive openness and dependence before God. Voluntary release (2Cor 8:9; Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8) — the deliberate act of giving or refusing wealth in response to a higher valuation. Learned contentment (autarkēs, Phil 4:11) — the achieved inner stability that material circumstances cannot move. These three do not constitute a single characteristic but a family of inner postures oriented in the same direction: away from material circumstance as the governing force of the inner life.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

**[A]** E — The structural opposite of inner closure is inner perceptiveness and openness — the person who sees, hears, understands, and turns (Isa 6:10 uses these four as what fat-heartedness prevents). The specific opposite of complacent ease (sha.a.nan 413-001) is genuine divine-grounded security (sha.a.nan 413-002, Isa 32:18) — the same phenomenology with a different ground. The opposite of self-assessed wealth (Rev 3:17) is poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) — the condition of knowing one has nothing to offer.

**[B]** E — The structural opposite of hardness is cha.mal — pity, the capacity to spare and feel for the suffering of another (explicitly named by its absence in 2Sa 12:4 and implied in Luk 16:19-21). The structural opposite of insatiability is the capacity to say "enough" (hon as satisfaction-marker, Pro 30:15 showing its absence) — the inner settledness that receives what it has as sufficient.

**[C]** E — The structural opposite of ordered character-wealth integration is the pattern of the wicked whose income brings trouble (Pro 15:6) — character that corrupts the quality of wealth rather than enriching it. The deeper opposite is the worship of wealth itself as the goal, which the wisdom tradition consistently names as folly (Pro 11:28 — trusting in riches as the path to falling).

**[D]** E — The structural opposites of M46-D's three postures: the opposite of poverty of spirit is the Laodicean self-sufficiency of Rev 3:17 ("I need nothing"); the opposite of voluntary release is hoarding (cha.san — accumulating for self rather than releasing for others); the opposite of learned contentment is the anxiety and drive of 1Ti 6:9 ("those who desire to be rich fall into temptation").

---

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A]** E — Inner closure explicitly excludes: the capacity to perceive and respond to God (Isa 6:10 — cannot hear, see, understand, turn); genuine humility and self-knowledge (Rev 3:17 — "not realising that you are wretched"); the fear of God (sha.a.nan 413-001, Amo 6:1 — the at-ease person has no fear of what is coming); and productive repentance (the fat-hearted person is specifically blocked from turning and being healed, Isa 6:10).

**[B]** E — Hardness explicitly excludes: pity (cha.mal — 2Sa 12:4); attention to the poor (Luk 16:19-21 — the rich man does not see Lazarus); genuine reception of what one has (Ecc 6:2 — wealth present but power to enjoy absent); the word of God taking root (Mat 13:22 — deceitfulness of riches chokes the word).

**[C]** E — The ordered character-wealth relationship resists: the equation of wealth with righteousness (Hos 12:8 — Ephraim's false claim that being rich means being without iniquity); the accumulation of wealth through violence or wickedness (cho.sen, Eze 22:25); and the treatment of wealth as an end rather than a fruit of character.

**[D]** E — The inner life beyond material circumstance resists: accumulation for self (cha.san — hoarding); the drive to become rich as the governing inner orientation (1Ti 6:9; Pro 23:4); and the anchoring of identity or security in material condition (the opposite of autarkēs, Phil 4:11).

---

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's boundary with adjacent characteristics: M46 is not greed (M28 — filarguria, pleonexia), which is the inner characteristic of craving, covetousness, and desire-for-more. M28 names the inner orientation of desire; M46 names the domain in which that desire operates and the inner-being effects of that domain on the person. M46 is not trust/distrust (M19) — though the misdirection of trust is a central M46-A finding, the trust faculty itself belongs to M19's domain. M46 is not generosity (M05 Love/Compassion) — though the giving patterns of M46-D are closely related to generosity, the cluster's terms name the domain and the postures within it, not the characteristic of generosity itself. M46 ends where the inner-being characteristic named by those other clusters begins; M46 names the material domain as the testing ground where those characteristics are revealed and exercised.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A]** E — M46-A operates in two distinct modes. Mode 1: gradual satiation-produced dulling — the progressive process by which material abundance removes the edge of inner hunger, eventually producing a closed, fat heart. This is the sha.men verb pattern (Deu 32:15; Jer 5:28). Mode 2: false self-assessment anchoring — the inner act of grounding identity and security in material condition, producing blindness to actual inner state. This is the Rev 3:17 / Hos 12:8 pattern. Mode 1 is a process; Mode 2 is a stable (though false) inner orientation.

**[B]** E — M46-B operates in two distinct modes. Mode 1: outward hardness — the eroded capacity for pity expressed in indifference to the suffering neighbor, rough treatment of petitioners, structural domination of the poor (Luk 16:19-21; 2Sa 12:4; Pro 18:23). Mode 2: inward insatiability — the inability of the inner person to receive what they have as sufficient, expressed in the never-satisfied eyes (Ecc 4:8), the inability to enjoy wealth held (Ecc 6:2), and the grief of material loss (Rev 18:15-19). Both modes are expressions of the same inner contraction but face in different directions.

**[C]** E — M46-C operates in an evaluative-generative mode: the inner character of the person evaluates and generates the quality of the material life. The diligent inner person generates material sufficiency (Pro 13:4). The trusting inner person receives material enrichment as divine gift (Pro 28:25; Pro 10:22). The humble inner person receives riches as the reward of right orientation (Pro 22:4). M46-C's mode is the wisdom tradition's causal account: inner character as the generative principle of the material domain.

**[D]** E — M46-D operates in three distinct modes. Mode 1: receptive openness — the inner posture of poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) that receives from God without self-resistance. Mode 2: volitional release — the deliberate inner act of giving, refusing, or relinquishing material wealth in response to a higher valuation (Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8; 2Cor 8:9). Mode 3: learned settledness — the formed inner disposition of contentment that has been acquired through practice across circumstances (autarkēs, Phil 4:11). These three modes form a progression: openness to receive → willingness to release → settled independence from material circumstance.

---

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A, B]** E — Both M46-A and M46-B show clear context-dependency. The inner-closure and hardness patterns emerge specifically in the context of material abundance — they are not present when the person is poor (the poor are not named as having fat hearts in the cluster's evidence). The directional dependency: M46-A operates in the person's vertical relationship with God (closure toward God); M46-B operates in the person's horizontal relationships with others (hardness toward the poor) and in the person's inner relationship with their own goods (insatiability within).

**[C, D]** E — M46-C shows context-dependency in both the OT (wisdom tradition, Proverbs and Ecclesiastes) and NT (1Ti 6:17 — God's rich provision) but the underlying principle (character determines quality of wealth) appears constant. M46-D operates differently across contexts: poverty of spirit (Mat 5:3) is an original-design capacity; voluntary release (Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8) is an active response to circumstances; learned contentment (Phil 4:11) is explicitly said to be learned through varying circumstances — it is context-responsive (learned across changing conditions) but context-independent as an achieved state.

---

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

**[A]** E — M46-A has a notable speech-based mode: the self-declaration of wealth. Rev 3:17 ("you say, I am rich, I have prospered, I need nothing") and Hos 12:8 ("Ephraim has said, 'I am rich'") and Zec 11:5 ("'Blessed be the Lord, I have become rich'") all name the verbal self-declaration of wealth as the speech-act of inner closure. The fat-hearted person announces their condition. This speech-based mode is distinctive — inner closure finds a specific communicative expression in the verbal claiming of self-sufficiency.

**[B]** E — M46-B has a speech-based mode in the context of hardness: the rich man answering roughly (a.shir, Pro 18:23), the community's verbal direction of the poor man ("stand over there," "sit at my feet," Jam 2:3), and Judas's verbal invocation of concern for the poor to conceal his theft (Joh 12:6). The speech of the hardened person toward the poor is rough, dismissive, or hypocritical.

**[C]** S — M46-C does not have a distinctive speech-based mode. The wisdom tradition's teaching about character and wealth is declarative (wisdom statements) but the characteristic itself does not operate through a specific speech-act.

**[D]** E — M46-D has a speech-based mode in declaration and relinquishment: Abram's spoken refusal ("I would not take a thread or sandal strap," Gen 14:23), Zacchaeus's spoken announcement ("the half of my goods I give to the poor," Luk 19:8), and Paul's written declaration of learned contentment (Phil 4:11). The voluntary release in M46-D is typically a spoken or written declaration — the inner act is made verbal.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

**[A]** E — The immediate response to the condition of inner closure is not experienced from within — the defining feature of inner closure is that the person does not know they are in it. The Laodicean church does not experience their blindness; they experience security and sufficiency (Rev 3:17 — "not realising"). The immediate response from outside (encountering an inner-closed person) is evidenced in the prophetic woe: Amos's "woe to those at ease" and Isaiah's "make the heart fat" — the prophet sees what the person cannot see about themselves.

**[B]** E — The immediate inner response to the wealthy-person's indifference in M46-B is the inner experience of the poor: they desire scraps (Luk 16:21 — Lazarus desired to be fed from the rich man's table), they are told to stand (Jam 2:3), they are neglected. The immediate response of the insatiable person to their wealth is restlessness — the rich man who cannot sleep (Ecc 5:12).

**[C]** E — The immediate inner response to ordered wealth received through character is enjoyment and gratitude. Ecc 5:19 names it: "God gives wealth and possessions and power to enjoy them" — the immediate response to rightly-ordered wealth is the capacity to enjoy it. Pro 13:4 (the soul of the diligent richly supplied) implies the immediate satisfaction of supply meeting the soul's need.

**[D]** E — The immediate response to the encounter with Jesus that produces M46-D's voluntary release is joy and resolution: Zacchaeus stood and announced his giving immediately after Jesus came to his house (Luk 19:6-8). The immediate response to giving from poverty is joy: the Macedonian churches gave "with abundance of joy" even in "extreme poverty" (2Cor 8:2). The immediate response of poverty of spirit to the kingdom-word is blessing: Mat 5:3 declares the present tense (theirs is the kingdom).

---

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

**[A]** The immediate response is consistently a non-response — inner closure produces unperception of itself. The variation is in how the closed condition is eventually encountered from outside (prophetic woe, divine counsel to buy refined gold, the judgment reversal). Consistent across M46-A evidence.

**[B]** Hardness produces consistent non-response to the poor (the rich man doesn't act toward Lazarus; the assembly doesn't honour the poor visitor; Judas doesn't actually care). Insatiability consistently produces the experience of having without receiving. Consistent.

**[C]** The immediate response to ordered wealth varies by the wisdom tradition's nuance: sometimes wealth is the immediate gift of blessing (Pro 10:22 — no sorrow added), sometimes it is the fruit of patient diligence (Pro 13:11 — gathered little by little). The immediate response is not uniform but contextually determined by how the wealth arrives.

**[D]** The immediate response varies across M46-D's three postures. Poverty of spirit produces receptivity (immediate openness). Voluntary release produces joy (immediate felt liberation). Learned contentment is not an immediate response but an achieved state — Phil 4:11 explicitly says it was learned, not instantaneous.

---

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

**[CLUSTER]** The verse evidence is largely silent on the immediate inner experience of insatiability from within (Ecc 4:8 and 6:2 describe the condition observationally, not from the inside); and on the immediate inner experience of ordered wealth in M46-C beyond the capacity-to-enjoy (Ecc 5:19). These silences are noted.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A]** E — Sustained inner closure produces increasing blindness: the Laodicean church is already "wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" — these are the sustained effects of having been at-ease and self-sufficient for an extended period. The sha.men verb evidence (Jer 5:28 — "they have grown fat and sleek; they know no bounds in deeds of evil") shows the sustained effect: unbounded wickedness and the abandonment of justice. The sustained effect of inner closure is moral deterioration.

**[B]** E — Sustained hardness produces increasing social oppression: the rich who have grown hard toward others eventually drag them into court (Jam 2:6), exercise structural dominion (Pro 22:7), and become full of violence (Mic 6:12). Sustained insatiability produces grief: the riches that were hoarded are lost (Ecc 5:13-14), the wealth that was accumulated rots (Jam 5:2), the merchants who grew rich from Babylon weep and mourn (Rev 18:15-19).

**[C]** E — Sustained operation of ordered character-wealth integration produces the pattern of Psa 112:3 — wealth and riches in the house, alongside righteousness that endures forever. The righteousness and the material abundance persist together over time; neither destroys the other. The sustained effect of M46-C is stability: wealth rooted in character is stable wealth (Pro 11:28 — the righteous flourishes rather than falls).

**[D]** E — Sustained poverty of spirit produces the capacity to receive the kingdom across all circumstances. Sustained practice of voluntary release produces the pattern of 2Cor 6:10 — "as poor, yet making many rich; as having nothing, yet possessing everything" — the paradox of increasing inner richness through material relinquishment. Sustained learned contentment produces what Paul describes in Phil 4:7 (the context of contentment) — the peace of God guarding heart and mind.

---

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

**[A]** E — Sustained inner closure establishes: incapacity to receive from God; moral deterioration (unbounded wickedness); false self-knowledge; and — in the divine judgment form (Isa 6:10) — permanent perceptual loss ("lest they see... and turn and be healed" — the fear is that without the fat-heartedness the healing would occur, so it is maintained as judgment).

**[B]** E — Sustained hardness establishes: structural social dominance over the poor (Pro 22:7); the social distortion of false friendship (Pro 19:4 — wealth brings friends whose friendship is conditional); and — through insatiability — the total absence of inner rest (Ecc 5:12 — "the full stomach of the rich will not let him sleep").

**[C]** E — Sustained ordered character-wealth establishes: the enduring righteousness of Psa 112:3; the wisdom-accumulated pattern of Pro 13:11 (gathering little by little produces increase); and the Solomonic pattern (1Ki 3:13) of wealth given as a secondary consequence of right priority that then endures.

**[D]** E — Sustained M46-D postures establish: the capacity to "possess everything" while having nothing (2Cor 6:10); the peace of God (Phil 4:7-11 — the contentment passage is framed by the peace of God); and the kingdom possession of Mat 5:3 — the poor in spirit's present possession of the kingdom as an abiding state, not a future promise.

---

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The most significant sustained-vs-immediate divergence is in M46-A: the immediate experience of material abundance is pleasure (eating, being filled, delighting — Neh 9:25 with a.dan); the sustained effect is inner closure and apostasy (Neh 9:26 — they were disobedient and rebelled). The immediate is positive; the sustained effect is harmful. This divergence is the core dynamic of M46-A: what feels like abundance and satiation in the moment produces inner deterioration over time. The same divergence applies in M46-B: the immediate experience of wealth accumulation may feel like security (hon as strong city, Pro 18:11); the sustained effect is insatiability, grief, and hardness. For M46-D, the immediate-sustained relationship is a progression: immediate joy in voluntary release → sustained capacity for paradoxical richness in poverty.

---

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**[C]** E — M46-C makes the enabling conditions explicit: diligence (Pro 13:4 — soul of the diligent richly supplied), trust in the Lord (Pro 28:25 — trust produces enrichment over greed), humility and fear of the Lord (Pro 22:4 — the reward of humility and fear is riches), and the prior choice of wisdom over wealth (1Ki 3:11-13 — Solomon chose wisdom and received wealth as consequence). The enabling condition is inner character rightly ordered — not the pursuit of wealth but the prior ordering of the inner person toward God, wisdom, and diligence.

**[D]** E — M46-D's enabling conditions: poverty of spirit is enabled by the absence of self-assessed sufficiency — the person who knows they have nothing to offer (Mat 5:3). Voluntary release is enabled by a higher valuation — Moses's looking to the reward (Heb 11:26), Zacchaeus's encounter with Jesus (Luk 19:6-8), Abram's commitment to God as his sole material provider (Gen 14:23). Learned contentment is enabled by lived experience across varying circumstances — it is learned "in whatever situation" (Phil 4:11 — emathon, I have learned, implies repeated experience).

**[A, B]** S — M46-A and M46-B do not describe inner conditions that "enable reception" — they describe conditions produced by wealth in the absence of the enabling conditions named in M46-C and M46-D. The absence of the M46-C and M46-D conditions is what produces M46-A and M46-B.

---

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[C]** E — The ordered character-wealth relationship is blocked by: the wicked inner orientation (Pro 15:6 — trouble befalls the income of the wicked); the inner drive to become rich hastily (Pro 28:20 — hastening brings punishment, not wealth); and the misplaced trust that treats wealth as the goal rather than the fruit (Pro 11:28 — trusting in riches as the path to falling).

**[D]** E — Poverty of spirit is blocked by the self-assessment of wealth (Rev 3:17 — the Laodicean church's "I need nothing" is the specific block). Voluntary release is blocked by excessive attachment to wealth — the rich young ruler's extreme wealth (plousios sphodra, Luk 18:23) produced grief when release was commanded. Learned contentment is blocked by the drive to become rich (1Ti 6:9 — desire to be rich as the alternative orientation).

**[A, B]** E — Inner closure is produced (not blocked) by wealth operating on the person without the countervailing conditions of character, trust, and humility. The block on inner closure is those countervailing conditions — having them prevents inner closure. For hardness: cha.mal (pity) is the inner capacity that wealth erodes; its presence is the block on hardness.

---

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A]** E — The person who encounters material prosperity but does not receive inner closure is the person of M46-C and M46-D: the diligent whose soul is richly supplied (Pro 13:4), the contented person who has learned to be settled regardless (Phil 4:11), the generous person who gives from abundance (2Cor 8:2). They receive the material goods without the inner closure they can produce — the countervailing inner conditions prevent the closure from forming.

**[D]** E — The person who encounters the demand for voluntary release but does not receive it is the rich young ruler (Luk 18:23) — he became very sad but could not release. His inner state: grief (perilupos) without resolution. This is the most precise description in the cluster of what non-reception of M46-D looks like: sorrow without action, encounter without transformation.

---

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**[A]** E — The primary dimension of M46-A (inner closure) is Orientation/Trust — the faculty of trust (ba.tach) is the primary faculty engaged in the misdirection pattern. The trust faculty is redirected from God to material security. Secondary dimension: Perception — the closure produces loss of perceptual capacity (Isa 6:10 — cannot see, hear, understand).

**[B]** E — The primary dimension of M46-B (hardness and insatiability) is Relational Capacity (outward hardness face) and Affective Faculty (insatiability face). The hardness erodes the relational-compassion capacity; the insatiability is an affective condition (the inability to be satisfied). These are distinct dimensions within the same sub-group.

**[C]** E — The primary dimension of M46-C (ordering of character and wealth) is Moral Character / Virtue — the wisdom tradition frames the material domain as the domain in which character (diligence, trust, humility, fear) produces its fruits. The secondary dimension is Agency — the wise person's diligence (Pro 13:4) is an exercise of sustained, character-shaped agency.

**[D]** E — The primary dimension of M46-D (inner life beyond material circumstance) is Orientation/Trust toward God — poverty of spirit is the condition of dependence and openness before God; learned contentment is the inner independence from circumstance that reflects a trust-grounded settledness. Secondary dimension: Volition — voluntary release (Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8; 2Cor 8:9) is explicitly a volitional act.

---

**T1.8.2** — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

**[A]** E — Trust-dimension evidence: Psa 49:6 (trust in wealth and boast of riches), Psa 52:7 (trusted in abundance of riches rather than God), Pro 11:28 (trusts in riches will fall), 1Ti 6:17 (charge rich not to set hopes on uncertain riches). Perception-dimension evidence: Isa 6:10 (cannot see, hear, understand), Rev 3:17 (not realising their actual condition).

**[B]** E — Relational-capacity evidence: 2Sa 12:4 (cha.mal absent — unwilling to spare from own flock), Luk 16:19-21 (ignoring Lazarus at gate), Jam 2:2-3 (differential treatment of poor and rich). Affective evidence: Ecc 4:8 (eyes never satisfied), Ecc 6:2 (wealth present but power to enjoy absent), Pro 30:15 (never says enough).

**[C]** E — Moral-character evidence: Pro 13:4 (ne.phesh of diligent richly supplied — character produces soul-richness), Pro 10:4 (diligent hand makes rich), Pro 22:4 (reward of humility and fear is riches). Agency evidence: Pro 13:11 (gathering little by little — patient sustained activity produces wealth).

**[D]** E — Trust/Orientation evidence: Phil 4:11-13 (learned contentment explicitly grounded in "I can do all things through him who strengthens me" — the contentment is trust-grounded); Mat 5:3 (poor in spirit before God — orientation of dependence). Volition evidence: Gen 14:23 (Abram's deliberate refusal), Luk 19:8 (Zacchaeus's spoken resolution), 2Cor 8:9 (Christ's voluntary act of becoming poor).

---

**T1.8.3** — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

**[CLUSTER]** The secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary classifications; they enrich them. M46-A's trust-misdirection produces the perceptive loss as a consequence — trust (primary) leads to perception (secondary) in a causal sequence. M46-B's relational failure (hardness) and affective condition (insatiability) are distinct but both produced by the same source condition (wealth without right orientation). M46-C's moral character (primary) generates agency (secondary) — the character expressed through sustained action. M46-D's trust-orientation (primary) enables and motivates the volitional acts (secondary) — contentment grounded in God (trust) produces the willingness to release (volition). The dimensions are related in causal sequences, not competitive.

## CLUSTER-LEVEL ADDITIONS — Phase 9 cross-sub-group review

**T1.1.3** — supplementary cluster finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The directional character of the wealth domain is the governing structural principle across all four sub-groups. Every term in M46 operates directionally in its verse evidence — wealth is never a neutral inner state. The inner-being content is always: wealth oriented toward self or toward God and others; trust placed in riches or in God; becoming rich in the self-directed form (M46-A) or in the toward-God form (M46-D). This directionality is not incidental but constitutive of the domain: the question the wealth domain always poses is not "how much?" but "toward whom and for what?"

---

**T1.3.1** — supplementary cluster finding

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The structural inverses within the cluster form its governing polarity: M46-A (inner closure — "I am rich, I need nothing," Rev 3:17) and M46-D (poverty of spirit — constitutive openness and dependence before God, Mat 5:3) are not merely different inner conditions but structural inverses of each other. The same domain (wealth) produces opposite inner orientations depending on whether material condition becomes the ground of self-sufficiency (M46-A) or is transcended through encounter with God and Christological pattern (M46-D). This polarity explains the cluster's two primary anchors: Rev 3:17 (the domain's greatest risk) and 2Cor 8:9 (the domain's Christological resolution).
