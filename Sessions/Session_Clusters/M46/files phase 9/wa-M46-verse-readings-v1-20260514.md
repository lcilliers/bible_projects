## H7230 rov — abundance (mti_id=1142)
**Relevant verses:** 41 total; ~26 G-status (group 1142-001), 2 P-status

### Hos 9:7 (P)
"The days of punishment have come; the days of recompense have come; Israel shall know it. The prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad, because of your great iniquity and great hatred."

The term rov appears twice: "great iniquity" (rov + a.von) and "great hatred" (rov + mas.te.mah). Rov here is not abundance of material things — it is the magnitude or weight of two inner-moral realities: iniquity and hatred. The greatness of iniquity and hatred is so severe that divine punishment has arrived. The prophet who should speak truthfully is called a fool; the spirit-person is called mad. These reversals of inner perception follow from the greatness of the moral failure. Rov names the sheer weight of the inner moral condition — not as a quantity of material goods but as a measure of the depth of moral wrong that has accumulated. The inner-being content: the weight of accumulated inner-moral failure (iniquity) and the intensity of inner hostility (hatred) together constitute the ground of judgment.

### Job 11:2 (P)
"Should a multitude of words go unanswered, and a man full of talk be judged right?"

Rov = multitude of words. Zophar challenges Job: does having many words make a person righteous? The multitude (rov) of words is set against the question of being judged right — as if verbal abundance could substitute for moral justification. The inner-being content: the person who produces a great volume of speech (rov of words) uses verbal proliferation as a kind of self-defence or self-justification. The implicit claim is that talking much is not the same as being right. There is an inner orientation here — the person who speaks at length, who fills silence with words — and Zophar questions whether this inner habit of speech constitutes genuine wisdom or righteousness. Rov names the abundance as the problem: too many words, not enough truth.

### G-status rov verses (group 1142-001) — reading each

**Isa 1:11 (G):** "What to me is the multitude (rov) of your sacrifices? says the Lord; I have had enough of burnt offerings of rams..."
God speaks: the rov of sacrifices is meaningless to him. This is a prophetic critique of ritual abundance without inner reality. The abundance of sacrifices (rov) does not produce the inner-being response God desires. The sheer quantity of offering does not constitute true worship. Rov here names the volume of ritual activity — and its inadequacy. Inner-being content: abundance of outward religious practice that is hollow within; the gap between outward abundance and inner reality before God.

**Isa 37:24 (G):** "With my many (rov) chariots I have gone up the heights of the mountains..."
The Assyrian king's boast: "my many chariots." Rov here names the military abundance that is the ground of arrogance. The inner-being content: the person who trusts in the magnitude of their material-military power and speaks boastfully against God. Rov names the sheer quantity of military resource as the basis of an inner posture of defiance and self-confidence before God. This is pride grounded in abundance.

**Isa 40:26 (G):** "by the greatness (rov) of his might and because he is strong in power, not one is missing."
Here rov names the greatness of God's might — the magnitude of divine power. The inner-being content for the human person: standing before the rov of God's might produces awe and orientation toward dependence. The vastness of divine power sets the context for the human creature's proper inner posture.

**Isa 47:9 (G):** "in spite of your many (rov) sorceries and the great power of your enchantments."
Babylon's rov of sorceries — abundance of occult practice. The inner orientation: reliance on the magnitude of spiritual-occult techniques rather than on God. Abundance of false spiritual practice as the ground of false security.

**Isa 47:12 (G):** "Stand fast in your enchantments and your many (rov) sorceries..."
Same pattern. The rov of sorceries is the thing Babylon trusts in — the quantity of occult practice as perceived inner resource. The irony: rov of sorceries cannot save.

**Isa 47:13 (G):** "You are wearied with your many (rov) counsels..."
Rov of counsels — the abundance of advisors and strategies that Babylon relies on. Inner-being content: the person/nation exhausted by the volume of human strategising, having multiplied counsel without finding wisdom. The many counsels are a substitute for divine direction.

**Isa 57:10 (G):** "You were wearied with the length (rov) of your way, but you did not say, 'It is hopeless.'"
Rov here = length/greatness of the journey (the long way of Israel's spiritual wandering after idols). Inner-being content: the weariness of long spiritual futility that nonetheless does not produce repentance. The rov of the way names the accumulated distance of the inner journey away from God.

**Isa 63:1 (G):** "he who is splendid in his apparel, marching in the greatness (rov) of his strength."
Divine warrior image — God marching in the rov of his strength to accomplish salvation. The rov of divine strength is the ground of salvific action. Inner-being content for the human: the greatness of God's strength is what saves; human responses (awe, trust, hope) are oriented toward this rov.

**Isa 63:7 (G):** "according to his compassion, according to the abundance (rov) of his steadfast love."
The rov of God's steadfast love (che.sed) — the magnitude of divine faithfulness as the basis of salvation history. This is the most theologically rich rov verse. The abundance of God's steadfast love is not a quantity of material goods but the inexhaustible weight of divine relational faithfulness. Inner-being content: the human person who recounts and receives this rov — the abundance of divine love — finds their inner identity anchored in a faithfulness greater than their own failures.

**Jer 13:22 (G):** "it is for the greatness (rov) of your iniquity that your skirts are lifted up and you suffer violence."
Rov of iniquity — the magnitude of moral failure as the direct cause of shame and suffering. Inner-being content: the person who asks "why has this happened to me?" is answered with the rov of their iniquity. The weight of accumulated moral failure is the explanation for the inner collapse.

**Jer 30:14 (G):** "because your guilt is great (rov), because your sins are flagrant."
Rov = great (guilt). The greatness of guilt as the ground of God's punishing action. Inner-being content: guilt whose weight (rov) is acknowledged as the basis of deserved suffering.

**Jer 30:15 (G):** "Because your guilt is great (rov), because your sins are flagrant, I have done these things to you."
Same pattern as Jer 30:14 — the rov of guilt as explanation and cause.

**Lam 1:3 (G):** "Judah has gone into exile because of affliction and hard (rov) servitude."
Rov = hard (servitude) — the weight/abundance of servitude that drove Judah into exile. The rov here is the magnitude of suffering rather than material abundance. Inner-being content: the crushed inner condition of a people under the weight of imposed servitude.

**Lam 1:5 (G):** "because the Lord has afflicted her for the multitude (rov) of her transgressions."
Rov of transgressions — the accumulated weight of covenant violations as the ground of judgment. Inner-being content: the moral accounting of rov — many transgressions, proportional suffering.

**Lam 3:32 (G):** "though he cause grief, he will have compassion according to the abundance (rov) of his steadfast love."
The rov of divine steadfast love as the ground of hope even in suffering. This is the inner-being counterpoint to the rov of transgression: even when God causes grief, the rov of his steadfast love is greater. The inner-being content: the person in grief finding their hope anchored in the rov of divine faithfulness.

**Neh 9:25 (G):** "So they ate and were filled and became fat and delighted themselves in your great (rov) goodness."
Rov = great (goodness of God). The rov of divine goodness as the abundance Israel enjoyed and then abandoned. Inner-being content: the experience of rov goodness is the setting for the prosperity-apostasy pattern — they delighted in the great goodness, became fat, then turned. The rov of God's goodness was not matched by the rov of their gratitude.

**Neh 13:22 (G):** "spare me according to the greatness (rov) of your steadfast love."
Nehemiah's prayer: spare me according to the rov of your che.sed. The rov of divine steadfast love as the personal ground of petition. Inner-being content: the individual soul appealing to the magnitude of divine faithfulness as their hope for mercy.

**Hos 10:1 (G):** "The more (rov) his fruit increased, the more altars he built."
Rov = more/increased. The proportional relationship: the more prosperity increased (rov), the more idolatry multiplied. This is the clearest statement of the prosperity-apostasy pattern: material increase does not produce gratitude but amplified unfaithfulness. Inner-being content: material abundance feeding inner unfaithfulness rather than gratitude.

**Hos 10:13 (G):** "Because you have trusted in your own way and in the multitude (rov) of your warriors."
Rov of warriors — the magnitude of military strength as the object of trust. Inner-being content: trust displaced from God onto the rov of human military capacity. The abundance of warriors becomes the inner security foundation that replaces divine trust.

**Nah 3:4 (G):** "And all for the countless (rov) whorings of the prostitute..."
Rov of whorings — the abundance of spiritual prostitution (idolatry, political-spiritual betrayal). Inner-being content: the magnitude of unfaithfulness, the sheer quantity of the inner turning-away, as the ground of judgment.

**Job 4:14 (G):** "dread came upon me, and trembling, which made all (rov) my bones shake."
Rov = all (my bones). Here rov functions as a totality marker — all of the bones, not a partial response. The rov of physical-inner response to the terror: total dread reaching every part of the person. Inner-being content: rov names the totality of the inner-physical terror response.

**Job 23:6 (G):** "Would he contend with me in the greatness (rov) of his power? No; he would pay attention to me."
Job reasoning about God's rov of power — the magnitude of divine power, but also the expectation that despite that magnitude God would attend to one person. Inner-being content: the inner confidence that the rov of divine power is not deployed against the individual person seeking audience; hope that magnitude is paired with attentiveness.

**Job 33:19 (G):** "Man is also rebuked with pain on his bed and with continual (rov) strife in his bones."
Rov = continual (strife). The ongoing, unrelenting quality of inner-physical suffering — the rov of strife in the bones is not a moment but an enduring condition. Inner-being content: sustained suffering as the inner-physical experience of rebuke.

**Job 35:9 (G):** "Because of the multitude (rov) of oppressions people cry out; they call for help because of the arm of the mighty."
Rov of oppressions — the accumulated weight of suffering at the hands of the powerful. Inner-being content: the inner cry (za.aq) produced by the rov of oppression; the connection between accumulated external suffering and the inner-being response of crying out.

**Job 37:23 (G):** "he is great in power; justice and abundant (rov) righteousness he will not violate."
Rov of righteousness — the magnitude of divine righteousness as the ground of his justice. Inner-being content: the person who considers God's rov of righteousness finds the foundation for trust in his just governance.

**Note on rov pattern:** Reading every verse, a consistent pattern emerges: rov almost never names material wealth directly. It names the magnitude, weight, or abundance of something — whether that is divine attributes (steadfast love, power, righteousness, goodness), human moral conditions (iniquity, transgression, guilt), military capacity (warriors, chariots), inner states (dread, strife), or religious activity (sacrifices, sorceries). The term is a scalar — it amplifies whatever it modifies. Its inner-being relevance comes entirely from what it modifies: when it modifies inner-moral realities, it names their weight; when it modifies divine attributes, it names their magnitude as the ground of human inner-being response. It is not independently a wealth or abundance term in the M46 sense at all.

## H6239 o.sher — riches (mti_id=7586)
**Relevant verses:** ~20 P-status

**Psa 49:6 (P):** "those who trust in their wealth (cha.yil) and boast of the abundance (rov) of their riches (o.sher)?"
O.sher appears alongside boasting (ha.lal) and trust (ba.tach). The inner-being content is clear: riches are the object of both inner trust and public boasting. The person trusts in their riches — they have made riches their inner security foundation. And they boast of the abundance of those riches — they display their wealth as a source of inner pride and social identity. Two inner acts toward riches: trusting them and glorying in them. Both name a fundamental orientation of the inner person toward material wealth.

**Psa 52:7 (P):** "See the man who would not make God his refuge, but trusted in the abundance of his riches (o.sher) and sought refuge in his own destruction."
O.sher as the explicit replacement for God as refuge. The person chose riches as their inner security foundation over God. The text names the inner act — "trusted in the abundance of his riches" — and then names the tragic irony: what he chose as refuge was actually his destruction. The inner-being content: the substitution of material wealth for divine refuge as the foundational orientation of the inner person.

**Pro 3:16 (P):** "Long life is in her right hand; in her left hand are riches (o.sher) and honor."
Wisdom holds riches in her left hand — they come with her. The inner-being content here is the proper ordering: riches are the gift of Wisdom, not the object pursued in themselves. They are in the left hand — secondary to long life (right hand). The person who pursues Wisdom receives o.sher as a consequence, not as the goal.

**Pro 8:18 (P):** "Riches (o.sher) and honor are with me, enduring wealth (hon) and righteousness."
Wisdom speaking: o.sher is hers to give, alongside hon (enduring wealth) and tse.da.qah (righteousness). The pairing of riches with righteousness is striking — they are not in tension in Wisdom's possession. The inner-being content: there is a form of wealth that accompanies righteousness rather than contradicting it; wealth that comes through wisdom is morally ordered.

**Pro 11:16 (P):** "A gracious woman gets honor, and violent men (a.rits) get riches (o.sher)."
A contrasting couplet: the gracious woman receives honor; the ruthless men receive riches. This is an observation about two different ways wealth is obtained — through violence and ruthlessness. The inner-being content: there is a form of wealth that violent, ruthless inner character produces. The riches here are not commended — they are the fruit of an inner condition (ruthlessness) that is implicitly contrasted with the honor that flows from gracious character.

**Pro 11:28 (P):** "Whoever trusts in his riches (o.sher) will fall, but the righteous will flourish like a green leaf."
The direct statement: trusting in riches produces falling. The inner act of trust — ba.tach — is misplaced when directed toward o.sher. The contrast with the righteous who flourish makes clear the comparison: the righteous trust in something else (implicitly God), and this produces flourishing. Inner-being content: trust in riches is a specific inner-being failure that leads to collapse, in contrast to the righteous orientation that produces life.

**Pro 13:7 (P):** "One pretends to be rich (a.shar), yet has nothing; another pretends to be poor, yet has great wealth (o.sher)."
O.sher here is the concealed wealth — the person who has great riches but presents themselves as poor. The inner-being content is about the inner act of concealment and pretence regarding wealth. Two forms of deception around wealth: performing wealth one does not have, and concealing wealth one does have. Both are inner acts of misrepresentation of material reality.

**Pro 13:8 (P):** "A man's riches (o.sher) may ransom his life, but a poor man hears no threat."
O.sher can be used to ransom life — wealth has real transactional value in matters of survival. The inner-being content: wealth creates a specific vulnerability — the wealthy person can be threatened and ransomed; the poor person is beneath such threats. An unexpected observation: wealth exposes the person to a form of danger the poor do not face. The inner condition of the wealthy includes this specific vulnerability.

**Pro 22:1 (P):** "A good name is to be chosen rather than great riches (o.sher), and favor is better than silver or gold."
The wisdom comparison: a good name (shem tov) is preferred over o.sher. The inner-being content: the choice between reputation/character and material wealth is a fundamental inner-being decision. The person who chooses correctly places inner character (the good name) above external material condition. The good name is an inner quality — it reflects who the person is; o.sher is external — it reflects what the person has.

**Pro 22:4 (P):** "The reward of humility and fear of the Lord is riches (o.sher) and honor and life."
O.sher as the fruit of an inner orientation — humility (a.na.vah) and fear of the Lord (yir.at YHWH). The inner-being content: the specific inner dispositions of humility and godly fear produce wealth as their fruit. This is not prosperity theology — it is wisdom's observation that ordered inner character and right orientation toward God result in flourishing that includes material well-being.

**Pro 22:16 (P, checking):** Not visible in the v10 table — proceeding to next confirmed verse.

**Psa 112:3 (P):** "Wealth (hon) and riches (o.sher) are in his house, and his righteousness endures forever."
O.sher alongside hon and tse.da.qah (righteousness). The righteous person's house contains both o.sher and enduring righteousness — they coexist. Inner-being content: for the person who fears the Lord (Psa 112:1 context), material wealth and enduring righteousness are not in conflict. The wealth in such a house has a different character from the o.sher of the wicked.

**1Ki 3:11 (P):** "Because you have asked this, and have not asked for yourself long life or riches (o.sher) or the life of your enemies, but have asked for yourself understanding..."
O.sher appears as one of the three things Solomon did not ask for. The inner-being content: Solomon's choice reveals his inner priority — he did not seek o.sher, long life, or victory over enemies. This is a revelation of what the heart desires. The person who asks for wisdom instead of riches has an inner ordering that God commends.

**1Ki 3:13 (P):** "I give you also what you have not asked, both riches (o.sher) and honor, so that no other king shall compare with you."
O.sher is given as the unasked-for gift. God gives riches to the one who did not ask for them — who asked instead for wisdom. Inner-being content: the pattern of wealth following right inner priority (wisdom sought, riches given). The person who orders their inner desires correctly receives the material goods as a secondary consequence, not as the goal.

**Ecc 4:8 (P):** "There is one person who has no other, either son or brother, yet there is no end to all his toil, and his eyes are never satisfied (sa.va) with riches (o.sher)..."
O.sher as the object of insatiable desire. The eyes that are "never satisfied" with riches — the inner condition of insatiability in the face of wealth. The person toils endlessly for riches but the inner capacity to be satisfied never arrives. The observation includes self-recognition: "for whom am I toiling and depriving myself of pleasure?" — the inner-being question that the wealth-obsessed person fails to ask until too late.

**Ecc 5:12 (P):** "Sweet is the sleep of a laborer, whether he eats little or much, but the full stomach of the rich (a.shir) will not let him sleep."
O.sher (in context) — the abundance of the rich person disturbs their sleep. The inner-being content is physical-inner: the full stomach of the wealthy prevents rest. Material abundance produces a particular form of inner-physical restlessness. The poor person sleeps sweetly regardless of how much or little they ate; the rich person cannot rest.

**Ecc 5:13 (P):** "There is a grievous evil that I have seen under the sun: riches (o.sher) were kept by their owner to his hurt."
O.sher kept — hoarded — to the owner's own damage. The inner-being content: the act of retaining/hoarding riches is itself harmful to the one who holds them. There is an inner-moral damage in the act of keeping wealth that should have been released. The grief (cha.li holi — a grievous evil) is Qohelet's own sense of wrongness at observing this pattern.

**Ecc 5:14 (P):** "and those riches (o.sher) were lost in a bad venture. And he is father of a son, but he has nothing in his hand."
O.sher lost — the precariousness of material wealth. The person hoarded riches (v.13) and then lost them. The inner-being content: the grief and emptiness of watching riches disappear; the particular pain of having nothing to pass to one's child. The father's inner condition — empty-handed — is the outcome of hoarding and then losing.

**Ecc 5:19 (P):** "Everyone also to whom God has given wealth (o.sher) and possessions and power to enjoy them, and to accept his lot and rejoice in his toil — this is the gift of God."
O.sher as divine gift — specifically, wealth given with the capacity to enjoy it. Qohelet distinguishes between having wealth and being able to enjoy it (Ecc 6:2 shows the contrast). The inner-being content: the rare combination of material wealth with inner capacity to receive and enjoy it is specifically named as God's gift. The enjoyment (to eat and drink and be glad in toil) is the inner condition that transforms wealth from burden to blessing.

**Ecc 6:2 (P):** "a man to whom God gives wealth (o.sher), possessions, and honor, so that he lacks nothing of all that he desires, yet God does not give him power to enjoy them, but a stranger enjoys them. This is vanity; it is a grievous evil."
O.sher without the power to enjoy it — the painful disconnect. The inner-being content: it is possible to have every material thing and yet lack the inner capacity to receive it as good. God gives the wealth but withholds the inner enjoyment. The stranger who does enjoy it makes the grief sharper. This is one of Qohelet's most acute observations about the inner-being domain of wealth: the material condition and the inner condition are separable, and the inner condition determines whether the material one is blessing or curse.

**2Ch 1:11 (P):** "Because this was in your heart, and you have not asked for possessions, wealth (o.sher), honor, or the life of those who hate you, and have not even asked for long life..."
O.sher as what Solomon's heart did not seek. The "in your heart" language is direct inner-being access: God sees what is in the heart and commends Solomon for the absence of o.sher-seeking. Inner-being content: the heart that does not ask for riches is the heart that receives commendation and then receives riches as a secondary consequence.

**2Ch 1:12 (P):** "wisdom and knowledge are granted to you. I will also give you riches (o.sher), possessions, and honor..."
O.sher given as God's gift following the right inner choice. Same pattern as 1Ki 3:13.

**1Ch 29:12 (P):** "Both riches (o.sher) and honor come from you, and you rule over all. In your hand are power and might, and in your hand it is to make great and to give strength to all."
David's prayer: riches and honor come from God — they are in his hand. Inner-being content: the acknowledgment that material wealth is not self-produced but divinely given. The inner posture of the one who prays this: gratitude and dependence, not self-congratulation.

**Est 5:11 (P):** "And Haman recounted to them the splendor (ka.vod) of his riches (o.sher), the number of his sons, all the promotions with which the king had honored him..."
O.sher as the first item in Haman's boast — he recounts his riches to his wife and friends. The inner-being content: wealth as the ground of self-display and pride. Haman's recounting of his o.sher, sons, and promotions reveals an inner condition of self-glorification. The display of wealth to one's household is an inner-being act — seeking validation and admiration. The context is that despite all this, the sight of Mordecai not bowing fills him with wrath — wealth does not produce contentment.

**Note on o.sher:** Reading every verse, o.sher names material wealth as a condition — what a person or household has. Its inner-being significance comes almost entirely from the inner acts toward it: trusting it (Psa 49:6; Psa 52:7; Pro 11:28), boasting in it (Psa 49:6), asking for it (1Ki 3:11), hoarding it to one's harm (Ecc 5:13), being unable to enjoy it (Ecc 6:2), receiving it as a gift from Wisdom or from God (Pro 8:18; Pro 22:4; 1Ki 3:13), or displaying it with pride (Est 5:11). The term itself is morally neutral — it is a noun naming a material state. The inner-being dimension is what the person does with o.sher in their inner life. One verse stands out: Ecc 6:2, where wealth is present but the inner power to enjoy it is absent — this is the sharpest observation in the entire o.sher corpus about the separability of material condition and inner condition.

## G4434 ptōchos — poor (mti_id=7583)
**Relevant verses:** ~20 P-status (excluding SA)

**Mat 5:3 (P):** "Blessed are the poor (ptōchos) in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."
Ptōchos is extended explicitly into the inner register: poor in spirit. The Mounce definition notes these are not people lacking in spirit but those who have the positive moral quality of humility — recognising they have nothing to offer God and are entirely dependent on his gifts. The inner-being content: poverty of spirit is a recognised inner disposition. The person who is ptōchos in their inner life is the person who stands before God with no self-resource, no claim, no self-sufficiency — completely open. This is not the same as material poverty, though material poverty may school one in this posture. The kingdom belongs to these: the inner disposition of dependence and emptiness before God is the condition of receiving the kingdom.

**Mat 11:5 (P):** "the blind receive their sight and the lame walk, lepers are cleansed and the deaf hear, and the dead are raised up, and the poor (ptōchos) have good news preached to them."
The poor receiving good news is listed alongside physical healings as a messianic sign. Inner-being content: the poor are those to whom the gospel is proclaimed as its primary address. Their material poverty is not spiritualised here — these are actual poor people receiving actual good news. But the inner-being dimension is that their condition of poverty makes them receptive; they are not defended against need the way the wealthy are. The good news reaches the poor because they know they need it.

**Mat 19:21 (P):** "If you would be perfect, go, sell what you possess and give to the poor (ptōchos), and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me."
The poor as the recipients of what the rich young man must give. The command to give to the poor is the condition for the inner transformation the young man seeks ("perfect"). Inner-being content: giving to the poor is not merely an economic act but the inner act by which the person's attachment to wealth is broken. The poor receive material goods; the giver receives inner liberation and heavenly treasure. The exchange is asymmetric in two registers simultaneously.

**Mar 10:21 (P):** "And Jesus, looking at him, loved him, and said to him, 'You lack one thing: go, sell all that you have and give to the poor (ptōchos), and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me.'"
The love of Jesus is stated before the command — the command to give to the poor comes from love. Inner-being content: the command to give wealth to the poor is the specifically loving diagnosis of what this person lacks. What he lacks is not money — he has it — but the inner freedom from attachment to it. Giving to the poor is the act that would produce that freedom. The poor are the instrument of his inner liberation.

**Mar 12:42-43 (P):** "And a poor widow came and put in two small copper coins... 'Truly, I say to you, this poor widow has put in more than all those who are contributing to the offering box.'"
The ptōchos widow gives all she has. Jesus declares she gave more than everyone else. Inner-being content: the evaluation of giving by inner proportion rather than external quantity. The poor widow's giving is total — she has nothing left; her gift is her entire material existence. Jesus' declaration reverses the visible calculus: the large gifts of the wealthy are small; the tiny gift of the poor widow is great. Material poverty does not diminish the moral weight of the inner act of giving — it may actually intensify it.

**Luk 4:18 (P):** "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor (ptōchos)."
Jesus' inaugural declaration. The poor as the primary address of the messianic mission. Inner-being content: the gospel is specifically directed toward the poor as its primary audience. This is a programmatic statement about who the kingdom is for — not that the rich are excluded, but that the poor are named as the constituency of the good news. The inner-being implication: the condition of poverty may be the condition of greatest openness to divine gift.

**Luk 6:20 (P):** "Blessed are you who are poor (ptōchos), for yours is the kingdom of God."
Material poverty directly blessed: not "poor in spirit" as in Matthew, but "you who are poor." The kingdom belongs to the materially poor. Inner-being content: material poverty is not merely a social misfortune — in Luke's framing it is a condition of blessing. The poor possess the kingdom. This does not romanticise poverty but names it as the condition in which the kingdom arrives most naturally, where there is no buffer of material security between the person and their need for God.

**Luk 7:22 (P):** "Go and tell John what you have seen and heard: the blind receive their sight, the lame walk, lepers are cleansed, and the deaf hear, the dead are raised up, the poor (ptōchos) have good news preached to them."
Same as Mat 11:5. The poor receiving good news as a messianic sign. The list of healings and the preaching to the poor are placed on the same level — both are signs that the kingdom has arrived.

**Luk 14:13 (P):** "But when you give a feast, invite the poor (ptōchos), the crippled, the lame, the blind."
The instruction to reorient hospitality — not toward those who can repay (the wealthy), but toward the poor who cannot. Inner-being content: the inner act of hospitality directed toward the poor is a specific reorientation of the self. It requires releasing the expectation of reciprocity — the inner habit of social exchange based on mutual benefit. Hosting the poor is an act of inner generosity that cannot be instrumentalised.

**Luk 14:21 (P):** "Go out quickly to the streets and lanes of the city, and bring in the poor (ptōchos) and crippled and blind and lame."
The master's command when the invited guests refused: bring in the poor. Inner-being content: the kingdom feast is specifically filled with those the world excludes — the poor are brought in when the self-sufficient refuse. The kingdom reversal pattern.

**Luk 18:22 (P):** "Sell all that you have and distribute to the poor (ptōchos), and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me."
Same as Mat 19:21 — the specific command to give to the poor as the act of inner liberation. The distribution of material wealth to the poor is both the external act and the inner reorientation that constitutes discipleship.

**Luk 19:8 (P):** "Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor (ptōchos). And if I have defrauded anyone of anything, I restore it fourfold."
Zacchaeus's declaration after encountering Jesus. The giving to the poor is the voluntary, spontaneous inner-being response to having been received by Jesus. No command is given to Zacchaeus — he announces his own inner reorientation. Inner-being content: genuine encounter with Jesus produces spontaneous voluntary redistribution of wealth toward the poor. This is the inner transformation of the wealth orientation happening in real time, without external compulsion.

**Luk 21:3 (P):** "Truly, I tell you, this poor widow has put in more than all of them."
Same widow narrative as Mark's version. The evaluation of the poor widow's gift as greater. The inner-being content: the totality of the poor person's giving — she gave all her living — exceeds the partial giving of the wealthy, regardless of the absolute amount.

**Joh 12:6 (P):** "He said this, not because he cared about the poor (ptōchos), but because he was a thief, and having charge of the moneybag he used to help himself to what was put into it."
The narrator exposes Judas's false concern for the poor. Inner-being content: it is possible to invoke the poor rhetorically while having no genuine inner care for them. The poor are used as a pretext — the inner condition (greed, theft) is concealed behind concern-language for the poor. This is one of the most precise inner-being observations in the entire ptōchos corpus: genuine concern for the poor and its counterfeiting are distinguishable by the inner condition of the person who speaks.

**Jam 2:2 (P):** "For if a man wearing a gold ring and fine clothing comes into your assembly, and a poor man (ptōchos) in shabby clothing also comes in..."
The assembly scene: rich man and poor man both enter. The text sets up the contrast to expose the preferential treatment that follows. Inner-being content: the scene itself — two people entering, the visible contrast of their clothing — is the moment at which the inner condition of the assembly is tested. How the community responds to this scene reveals what is in their hearts.

**Jam 2:3 (P):** "and if you pay attention to the one who wears the fine clothing and say, 'You sit here in a good place,' while you say to the poor man, 'You stand over there,' or 'Sit down at my feet'..."
The differential treatment exposed. Inner-being content: the act of directing the poor man to a subordinate position is an inner-being act of contempt and partiality. The "if you pay attention to" (epi-blepō — to look upon with special attention) the wealthy person reveals an inner orientation that values wealth over persons. The instruction given to the poor man ("stand" or "sit at my feet") reveals the inner assessment of their worth.

**Jam 2:5 (P):** "has not God chosen those who are poor (ptōchos) in the world to be rich in faith and heirs of the kingdom, which he has promised to those who love him?"
The divine election of the poor. Inner-being content: God has specifically chosen the poor — the materially ptōchos — to be rich in faith. The inversion is total: material poverty, inner/spiritual richness. The heirs of the kingdom are those who are poor in the world's assessment. This is not because poverty is inherently virtuous but because God's election falls on those the world dismisses.

**Jam 2:6 (P):** "But you have dishonored the poor man. Are not the rich the ones who oppress you, and the ones who drag you into court?"
The community has dishonored (atimazō — to treat as without honor) the poor man. Inner-being content: the act of dishonoring the poor is named as a moral failure. And James adds the observation that the rich — whom they honored — are the ones who oppress them. The inner confusion: honoring those who harm you and dishonoring those God has chosen.

**Rev 3:17 (P):** "...not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor (ptōchos), blind, and naked."
Ptōchos applied to the Laodicean church's actual inner condition — they do not realise they are poor. The inner-being content: the word ptōchos here names an inner-spiritual poverty that is the precise opposite of what the church believes about itself ("I am rich"). The use of ptōchos to name the Laodicean inner condition is the most theologically weighted use of the term in the corpus — material poverty vocabulary applied to spiritual-inner poverty, invisible to the person who has it.

**Note on ptōchos:** Reading every verse carefully, ptōchos operates in two distinct registers: (1) the materially poor person as the primary recipient and constituency of the kingdom — the poor are blessed, the gospel is for them, the kingdom belongs to them, God has chosen them; (2) poverty as an inner-spiritual disposition — poor in spirit, the Laodicean church poor without knowing it. The inner-being content in both registers is profound: material poverty may be the condition of greatest openness to God (nothing between the person and their need for him), and inner poverty (recognising one has nothing to offer God) is the foundational disposition of the kingdom. The command to give to the poor is always simultaneously an act of inner liberation for the giver and material benefit for the receiver. Judas's false concern for the poor and the community's differential treatment of the poor both show that genuine orientation toward the poor is itself an inner-being marker.

## G4145 plousios — rich (mti_id=7577)
**Relevant verses:** ~24 P-status

**Mat 19:23 (P):** "Truly, I say to you, only with difficulty will a rich person (plousios) enter the kingdom of heaven."
The direct statement: the rich enter the kingdom with difficulty. Inner-being content: wealth creates a specific inner difficulty for kingdom entry. Jesus does not say the rich cannot enter — he says with difficulty. This implies the wealthy face an inner obstacle that others do not. The obstacle is not named here but is shown in the immediately preceding story of the rich young man: attachment to wealth, the inability to relinquish it.

**Mat 19:24 (P):** "Again I tell you, it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person (plousios) to enter the kingdom of God."
The graphic impossibility image. The disciples respond "who then can be saved?" — suggesting the image communicates near-impossibility. Inner-being content: the image does not say it is impossible but that it requires what is humanly impossible — divine intervention (v.26: "with God all things are possible"). The inner-being obstacle of wealth is something that only God can overcome in the person.

**Mar 10:25 (P):** "It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person (plousios) to enter the kingdom of God."
Same image, Mark's version. The repetition confirms the saying as a settled teaching of Jesus rather than a one-time remark.

**Luk 6:24 (P):** "But woe to you who are rich (plousios), for you have received your consolation."
The woe to the rich. Inner-being content: the rich have "received your consolation" — they have already received what they were seeking. The inner completion is in the past tense. The person who finds consolation in material wealth has already exhausted their consolation-supply. There is no further comfort available — this age's goods are this age's goods, and the age has had them. The inner condition of the rich: satisfied in this age, empty beyond it.

**Luk 12:16 (P):** "The land of a rich man (plousios) produced plentifully..."
Opening of the parable of the rich fool. The rich man's land produces abundantly. Inner-being content: the abundance arrives without the man's effort — the land simply produces. This sets up the internal deliberation that follows (vv.17-19) where the man reasons entirely within himself about what to do with the abundance.

**Luk 16:1 (P):** "There was a rich man (plousios) who had a manager..."
Opening of the parable of the dishonest manager. The rich man here is not the morally critiqued figure — he is the employer. The parable is about the manager's inner response to crisis, not about the rich man's character.

**Luk 16:19 (P):** "There was a rich man (plousios) who was clothed in purple and fine linen and who feasted sumptuously every day."
The unnamed rich man in the Lazarus parable. His daily feasting is the picture of inner self-satisfaction through material consumption. He wears the most expensive clothing (purple, fine linen); he easts sumptuously every day. Inner-being content: the regularity and excess of the consumption — every day, sumptuously — names an inner orientation of self-indulgence that becomes total. At his gate lies Lazarus, whom he does not see.

**Luk 16:21 (P):** "who desired to be fed with what fell from the rich man's (plousios) table. Moreover, even the dogs came and licked his sores."
Lazarus desired the scraps from the rich man's table. The inner-being content: the rich man's table produces scraps — overflow that falls — but even this overflow does not reach Lazarus deliberately. The contrast between the sumptuousness of the feast and the scraps that Lazarus desires names the scale of the inner indifference.

**Luk 16:22 (P):** "The poor man died and was carried by the angels to Abraham's side. The rich man (plousios) also died and was buried."
The reversal at death. The poor man is carried by angels; the rich man is buried. Inner-being content: death is the moment of total reversal. The rich man's burial implies a proper, honorable burial — his wealth attended him to the grave. But the destination is the point: the poor man is at Abraham's side; the rich man is in Hades (v.23). The inner condition that persisted through life determines the destination.

**Luk 18:23 (P):** "But when he heard these things, he became very sad (perilupos), for he was extremely rich (plousios sphodra)."
The rich young ruler's response. He became very sad (deeply grieved, sorrowful) because he was extremely rich. Inner-being content: the wealth produces the grief. The man's inner response to the command to give everything to the poor is grief — not anger, not defiance, but sorrow. The wealth has such a hold on the inner person that the prospect of its loss produces deep grief. This is one of the most specific inner-being descriptions in the plousios corpus: wealth producing grief in the face of kingdom demands.

**Luk 18:25 (P):** "For it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person (plousios) to enter the kingdom of God."
Luke's version of the camel-needle saying.

**Luk 21:1 (P):** "Jesus looked up and saw the rich (plousios) putting their gifts into the offering box."
The rich are observed giving. The context is the widow who gives all. Inner-being content: the rich give from their surplus — they give and still have plenty. This is not condemned, but set beside the widow's gift it is evaluated differently.

**1Ti 6:17 (P):** "As for the rich (plousios) in this present age, charge them not to be haughty, nor to set their hopes on the uncertainty of riches, but on God, who richly provides us with everything to enjoy."
Two specific inner-being charges to the rich: do not be haughty (hupsēlofroneō — to think highly of oneself), and do not set hope on uncertain riches. The inner-being content is precise: wealth tends to produce two specific inner distortions — pride (thinking oneself above others because of wealth) and misplaced hope (anchoring the inner expectation of security in material wealth rather than God). The alternative: hope in God who richly provides everything for enjoyment. The instruction contains both diagnosis and cure.

**Jam 1:10 (P):** "and the rich (plousios) in his humiliation, because like a flower of the grass he will pass away."
The rich person in their humiliation — the coming reduction and loss. Inner-being content: James says the rich should glory in their humiliation, just as the poor should glory in their exaltation (v.9). The humiliation of the rich is the transience of wealth — it passes like grass and flower. The inner posture called for: not grasping wealth as permanent but accepting its transience as the occasion for proper humility.

**Jam 1:11 (P):** "For the sun rises with its scorching heat and withers the grass; its flower falls, and its beauty perishes. So also will the rich man (plousios) fade away in the midst of his pursuits."
The rich man fades away "in the midst of his pursuits" — while still actively seeking and accumulating, the man himself fades. Inner-being content: the person absorbed in wealth-seeking is consumed even while pursuing. The inner dedication to wealth does not prevent but accompanies the fading. The pursuits themselves are the context of the fading — not idleness but active wealth-seeking is where the person is lost.

**Jam 2:5 (P):** "has not God chosen those who are poor in the world to be rich (plousios) in faith and heirs of the kingdom?"
Plousios applied to the poor: they are rich in faith. The term is inverted — the materially poor are named as plousios in the inner register of faith. Inner-being content: being rich is transferred from the material domain to the domain of faith. The poor who have faith are rich in the way that actually matters.

**Jam 2:6 (P):** "Are not the rich (plousios) the ones who oppress you, and the ones who drag you into court?"
The rich as oppressors — those who use their material power to dominate others. Inner-being content: wealth enabling structural power over others, exercised as oppression (katadunasteuō). The inner moral condition of the wealthy who oppress: their material power has become the instrument of inner domination.

**Jam 5:1 (P):** "Come now, you rich (plousios), weep and howl for the miseries that are coming upon you."
The prophetic address to the rich — weep and howl. Inner-being content: the coming miseries will produce grief in those who accumulated unjustly. The command to weep now anticipates the grief that is coming — a kind of pre-emptive mourning. The inner condition of the rich who have exploited: they are about to discover the cost of their inner orientation toward accumulation.

**Rev 2:9 (P):** "I know your tribulation and your poverty (but you are rich [plousios])..."
Jesus to the Smyrna church: they are materially poor but spiritually rich. The brackets are Jesus's own reversal — the poverty is stated, then corrected. Inner-being content: the plousios designation applied to a materially poor community. Their inner-spiritual richness is invisible to the world but visible to Jesus. The reversal confirms that being rich in the deepest sense is an inner-being state, not a material one.

**Rev 3:17 (P):** "For you say, 'I am rich (plousios), I have prospered, and I need nothing,' not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked."
The Laodicean self-declaration: "I am rich (plousios), I have prospered (plouteō), and I need nothing." Three assertions: wealth, prosperity, self-sufficiency. The inner-being content: the church's self-assessment is total — rich, prosperous, needing nothing. This inner self-sufficiency is the precise inner-being condition that blocks reception of anything from God. The person who needs nothing cannot receive anything. And the divine counter-assessment: wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, naked. Five descriptions of actual inner condition, invisible to the one who has them. This is the sharpest statement in the entire NT about the relationship between perceived and actual inner condition in the wealth domain.

**Rev 6:15 (P):** "Then the kings of the earth and the great ones and the generals and the rich (plousios) and the powerful, and everyone, slave and free, hid themselves in the caves and among the rocks of the mountains."
In the day of wrath, the rich hide alongside kings, generals, and the powerful. Inner-being content: wealth provides no exemption from the coming judgment. The rich are listed among those who hide — their material status is irrelevant before divine wrath. The inner response is the same as everyone else's: hide.

**2Cor 8:9 (P):** "For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich (plousios), yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich."
The Christological wealth statement. Christ was rich — plousios — before the incarnation. He became poor — ptōcheuō. You became rich — plouteō. Inner-being content: the vocabulary of the wealth domain is used to name the Christological movement: from divine wealth to human poverty to human enrichment. This is the paradigm that governs the M46-C register — the voluntary relinquishment of wealth for others' benefit.

**Eph 2:4 (P):** "But God, being rich (plousios) in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us..."
God described as plousios in mercy — rich in mercy. Inner-being content: the wealth vocabulary is applied to the divine attribute of mercy. God's mercy is inexhaustible — it does not run out, deplete, or diminish. The abundance of divine mercy is named with the richness vocabulary. For the human inner person: the one who receives this plousios mercy is the recipient of inexhaustible grace.

**Note on plousios:** Reading every verse, plousios consistently names the rich person as the one whose material condition creates a specific inner-being situation: an obstacle to the kingdom (Mat 19:23-24), grief when wealth is threatened (Luk 18:23), moral blindness to the poor neighbor (Luk 16:19-21), self-sufficient self-assessment that excludes receiving from God (Rev 3:17), haughtiness and misplaced hope (1Ti 6:17), oppression of others (Jam 2:6), absorption in accumulation even while fading (Jam 1:11), and comfort fully received in this age (Luk 6:24). The term is also deployed positively: the poor who are rich in faith (Jam 2:5), the Smyrna church rich despite poverty (Rev 2:9), Christ who was rich (2Cor 8:9), God rich in mercy (Eph 2:4). The material/spiritual reversal is a consistent pattern: being plousios in the material sense creates inner-being obstacles; being plousios in faith or in divine mercy is the true form of richness.

## G4147 plouteō — be rich (mti_id=7579)
**Relevant verses:** ~12 P-status

**Luk 1:53 (P):** "he has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich (ploutountas, participle of plouteō) he has sent away empty."
Mary's song. The rich are sent away empty — the same ones who are currently rich, full, self-sufficient. Inner-being content: the one who is presently rich (plouteō — in the state of being rich) is sent away empty. The present state of richness is precisely the condition that results in emptiness from God. The hungry are filled; the rich are emptied. The inner condition of being-rich produces the outcome of receiving nothing.

**Luk 12:21 (P):** "So is the one who lays up treasure for himself and is not rich toward God (eis theon ploutōn)."
The parable conclusion. The rich fool accumulated for himself — not rich toward God. Inner-being content: plouteō has a directional object — one can be rich toward oneself or rich toward God. Being rich toward God is a specific inner orientation: directing one's resources, energy, and inner investment toward God rather than toward self-accumulation. The fool who stores up for himself is explicitly contrasted with this — he is not ploutōn eis theon. Two inner orientations, two forms of being-rich.

**Rom 10:12 (P):** "for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches (plouteō) on all who call on him."
God actively enriching (plouteō in active sense) all who call on him. Inner-being content: the act of calling on God produces divine enrichment. The one who calls on the Lord — the inner act of invocation and dependence — receives the riches God gives. This is divine-to-human enrichment flowing from the inner act of calling.

**1Cor 4:8 (P):** "Already you have all you want! Already you have become rich (plouteō)! Without us you have become kings! And would that you did reign, so that we might share the rule with you!"
Paul's irony toward the Corinthians. They believe themselves to be already rich — the verb is indicative, describing their self-assessment. Inner-being content: the Corinthian church has a self-perception of inner-spiritual richness and kingly status. Paul uses the wealth vocabulary ironically — they believe they have arrived, that they are fully rich, that the kingdom is already fully theirs. The irony is that Paul and his co-workers are still poor, suffering, dying. The self-assessed richness is premature and disconnected from the apostolic reality.

**2Cor 8:9 (P):** "though he was rich (plousios), yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich (ploutēsēte)."
The Christological exchange. The purpose of Christ's becoming poor: so that you might become rich. Inner-being content: the human enrichment is the goal — plouteō applied to the believers as the outcome of Christ's voluntary poverty. What this enrichment consists of is not specified here but the context (2Cor 8 is about generous giving, 2Cor 9 about sowing generously) suggests it is not primarily material but inner and relational — enriched in every way for generosity (2Cor 9:11).

**1Ti 6:9 (P):** "But those who desire to be rich (ploutein boulomenoi) fall into temptation, into a snare, into many senseless and harmful desires that plunge people into ruin and destruction."
The inner desire to become rich is the starting point. Boulomenoi — those who will, who intend, who desire. Inner-being content: plouteō begins as an inner desire — the wanting to become rich — before it produces any external outcome. That inner desire is named as the trap: it produces temptation, snare, many senseless and harmful desires, and ultimately ruin. The desire to become rich is itself the inner-being problem, prior to any wealth actually being acquired.

**1Ti 6:18 (P):** "They are to do good, to be rich (plouteō) in good works, to be generous and ready to share."
Rich in good works — plouteō applied to virtuous action. The charge to the wealthy: be rich (plouteō) in the doing of good, in generosity, in readiness to share. Inner-being content: the form of being-rich that is commended is richness in good works and generosity. The same verb that describes material wealth-accumulation is turned to describe the accumulation of generous action. The inner orientation shifts from acquiring material goods to producing good works.

**Rev 3:17 (P):** "For you say, 'I am rich (plouteō), I have prospered, and I need nothing...'"
The Laodicean church's self-declaration: "I have prospered" — plouteō as the claim of having become rich. Inner-being content: the claim of having prospered is the inner-being distortion — the self-assessment of having arrived, having enough, needing nothing. The verb here names the completed inner state of self-assessed prosperity.

**Rev 3:18 (P):** "I counsel you to buy from me gold refined by fire, so that you may be rich (plouteō)..."
Christ's counsel to the Laodicean church: buy from me gold refined by fire so that you may truly be rich (plouteō). Inner-being content: the alternative to the self-assessed but false prosperity is genuine richness — plouteō in its true form — acquired from Christ through the refining fire. The inner poverty is real; the remedy is genuine plouteō available through Christ. The same verb names both the false self-assessed state and the true state Christ offers.

**Rev 18:3 (P):** "For all nations have drunk the wine of the passion of her sexual immorality, and the kings of the earth have committed immorality with her, and the merchants of the earth have grown rich (plouteō) from the power of her luxurious living."
The merchants who grew rich through Babylon's luxurious commerce. Inner-being content: plouteō through participation in Babylon's excess — the enrichment of exploitation and luxury commerce. The inner condition of these merchants is moral complicity in Babylon's system; their growing-rich is tied to the power of porneia (immorality, unfaithfulness). Their wealth is morally contaminated.

**Rev 18:15 (P):** "The merchants of these wares, who gained wealth (plouteō) from her, will stand far off, in fear of her torment, weeping and mourning."
The merchants who grew rich from Babylon stand far off, weeping. Inner-being content: the grief of loss at Babylon's judgment. The inner response of those who grew rich from Babylon is fear, weeping, mourning — the emotional cost of having one's wealth-source destroyed. The inner dependence on Babylon's wealth-generating system is exposed when that system falls.

**Rev 18:19 (P):** "And they threw dust on their heads as they wept and mourned, crying out, 'Alas, alas, for the great city where all who had ships at sea grew rich (plouteō) by her wealth! For in a single hour she has been laid waste.'"
Same pattern — the mourning of those who grew rich through Babylon. The dust on the heads (mourning ritual), the weeping and lamenting, the cry "alas" — the inner grief response to the sudden loss of the wealth-generating system. Inner-being content: deep mourning (not repentance) at the loss of wealth. The inner person of these merchants is bound to Babylon's wealth such that its fall produces lamentation rather than relief.

**Note on plouteō:** Reading every verse, plouteō (to be rich, to become rich) consistently operates around the question of direction and source: rich toward God or toward oneself (Luk 12:21); rich through Babylon's exploitation (Rev 18); rich in good works (1Ti 6:18); rich by Christ's poverty (2Cor 8:9); rich in false self-assessment (Rev 3:17-18). The inner desire to be rich is identified as the primary problem before any wealth exists (1Ti 6:9). The verb itself is morally neutral — one can be-rich in faith, in mercy, in good works, toward God — or in self-accumulation, in exploitation, in false self-assessment. The inner orientation determines whether plouteō names flourishing or failure.

## G4149 ploutos — riches (mti_id=7581)
**Relevant P-status verses (excluding SA metaphorical uses):**

**Mat 13:22 (P):** "As for what was sown among thorns, this is the one who hears the word, but the cares of the world and the deceitfulness (apatē) of riches (ploutos) choke the word, and it proves unfruitful."
Ploutos = riches. The "deceitfulness of riches" — apatē, the quality of deceiving. Inner-being content: riches deceive the person — they present themselves as satisfying, as the solution, as worth the inner investment. The word of God comes into the person and the riches choke it — the inner space that the word needs to grow in is occupied by the competing claim of riches. The word becomes unfruitful not because it was bad but because the internal soil was occupied by the deceptive attractiveness of wealth. The deceitfulness is the key: riches create a false inner perception of their own value and necessity.

**Mar 4:19 (P):** "but the cares of the world and the deceitfulness of riches (ploutos) and the desires for other things enter in and choke the word, and it proves unfruitful."
Mark's version adds "desires for other things" alongside riches. The deceitfulness of ploutos is the same as in Matthew. The inner-being content: three things enter and choke — cares, riches' deceitfulness, desires for other things. Ploutos is specifically named for its deceptive quality. Riches do not just compete with the word — they lie about their own value.

**Luk 8:14 (P):** "And as for what fell among the thorns, they are those who hear, but as they go on their way they are choked by the cares and riches (ploutos) and pleasures of life, and their fruit does not mature."
Luke's version: cares, ploutos, and pleasures (hēdonai) together choke. The riches here are paired with pleasures — both are things that consume the inner person's capacity for fruit. Inner-being content: ploutos, in the company of cares and pleasures, represents the material-enjoyment dimension that crowds out spiritual maturity. The fruit does not mature — it begins but never completes. The inner development is arrested by these competing claims.

**2Cor 8:2 (P):** "for in a severe test of affliction, their abundance of joy and their extreme poverty have overflowed in a wealth (ploutos) of generosity on their part."
The Macedonian churches: extreme poverty + overflowing joy = ploutos of generosity. This is a paradox: ploutos (wealth, riches) is the outcome of poverty and joy combined. The wealth is generosity — the abundance of giving, not of having. Inner-being content: genuine inner richness (ploutos) is not the outcome of material accumulation but of deep joy in the context of suffering and poverty. The Macedonians were materially poor; they were rich in generosity. The ploutos vocabulary is transferred from the material to the relational-moral domain to name what their giving produced.

**Heb 11:26 (P):** "He considered the reproach of Christ greater wealth (ploutos) than the treasures of Egypt, for he was looking to the reward."
Moses's inner calculation: he considered — esteemed, evaluated — the reproach of Christ as greater ploutos than Egypt's treasures. Inner-being content: ploutos used as the measure of comparative value. Moses performed an inner-being valuation: what is worth more — Egypt's treasure or the reproach of Christ? He chose the reproach as the greater ploutos because he was looking toward the reward. The verb "looking to" (apoblepō) names the inner orientation — his gaze was fixed on the future reward, which reordered his present valuation. This is inner-being reasoning in real time: the substitution of eternal ploutos for temporal ploutos through the discipline of forward-focused inner vision.

**1Ti 6:17 (P):** "As for the rich in this present age, charge them not to be haughty, nor to set their hopes on the uncertainty of riches (ploutos), but on God, who richly provides us with everything to enjoy."
Ploutos = riches, described as uncertain (adēlotēs — instability, unpredictability). Inner-being content: riches are uncertain — they can be lost, they shift, they are not a reliable foundation. Setting one's hope on ploutos is setting hope on something inherently unstable. The contrast: God, who richly (plousiōs) provides everything for enjoyment. The certainty of God's provision is the alternative ground for inner hope. Two foundations for inner hope — uncertain riches and certain God — and the charge is to anchor in the latter.

**Jam 5:2 (P):** "Your riches (ploutos) have rotted and your garments are moth-eaten."
Ploutos subject to decay — riches have rotted. Inner-being content: James addresses the rich who have accumulated unjustly (vv.3-5) — their riches are decaying even while they hold them. The decay of material wealth is the external sign of the inner moral condition. The riches rot — not because time has passed, but because they were accumulated through the oppression of workers (v.4). The inner-moral corruption of unjust accumulation is mirrored in the external corruption of the goods accumulated.

**Note on ploutos:** Reading every verse, ploutos in its relevant uses clusters around two patterns: (1) riches as the choking, deceiving inner force — the deceitfulness of ploutos that competes with the word (Mat, Mar, Luk); riches that rot from unjust accumulation (Jam 5:2); the uncertainty of ploutos as hope-object (1Ti 6:17); (2) ploutos as the measure of genuine value — the ploutos of generosity that exceeds material poverty (2Cor 8:2); the reproach of Christ as greater ploutos than Egypt's treasure (Heb 11:26). The first pattern names the problem: riches deceive and choke. The second pattern names the inversion: what is truly rich is not what the world calls rich.

## G4148 ploutizō — to enrich (mti_id=7580)
**Relevant P-status verses (1Cor 1:5 is SA):**

**2Cor 6:10 (P):** "as sorrowful, yet always rejoicing; as poor, yet making many rich (ploutizō); as having nothing, yet possessing everything."
The apostolic paradox: poor (ptōchos) yet making many rich (ploutizō). Inner-being content: the apostle in material poverty produces spiritual-relational enrichment in others. **Pro 8:18 (P):** "Riches (o.sher) and honor are with me, enduring wealth (hon) and righteousness."
Hon = enduring wealth. Wisdom's speech: hon is hers to give, as enduring wealth (hon atiq — accumulated, enduring). Inner-being content: the hon that Wisdom provides is enduring — unlike the transient hon that the stingy hastens after. Wisdom's hon is paired with righteousness. The wealth that comes through Wisdom is both morally ordered and lasting.

**Pro 10:15 (P):** "A rich man's wealth (hon) is his strong city; the poverty of the poor is their ruin."
Same as Pro 18:11 (without the "imagination" clause). The rich man's hon is his psychological stronghold. The poverty of the poor is their ruin. Inner-being content: the social-moral contrast — wealth provides (imagined) inner security, poverty produces inner-social ruin. The observation is not evaluative but descriptive of how the social-inner world operates.

**Pro 12:27 (P):** "Whoever is slothful will not roast his game, but the diligent man will get precious wealth (hon)."
Hon = precious wealth as the fruit of diligence. Inner-being content: the diligent inner character produces hon. The slothful person — who does not follow through — does not even roast the game they caught. The diligent person's inner follow-through produces material wealth. Character again as the determinant of material outcome.

**Pro 13:7 (P):** "One pretends (mit.a.sher) to be rich, yet has nothing; another pretends (mit.ra.shen) to be poor, yet has great wealth (hon)."
Hon = great wealth, concealed. The person with great hon presents themselves as poor. Inner-being content: the deliberate concealment of material wealth as an inner act. Two forms of pretence about material state: performing wealth without having it, and performing poverty while having hon. Both are inner acts of misrepresentation. The person who conceals their hon — what is the inner motivation? Possibly prudence, possibly deception, possibly modesty.

**Pro 13:11 (P):** "Wealth (hon) gained hastily will dwindle, but whoever gathers little by little will increase it."
Hon gained quickly disappears; hon gathered slowly grows. Inner-being content: the manner of hon-acquisition shapes its outcome. Hasty accumulation produces diminishment; patient, steady gathering produces growth. The inner character orientation — impatience vs. steadiness — produces different material trajectories. The inner quality of patience applied to wealth-gathering is what produces lasting hon.

**Pro 19:4 (P):** "Wealth (hon) brings many new friends, but a poor man is deserted by his friend."
Hon attracts friends; poverty loses friends. Inner-being content: hon produces a specific relational distortion — people are drawn to the wealthy person by their material condition, not by genuine relationship. The friendships that hon generates are conditional on its presence. The poor person is abandoned when poverty cannot provide what the wealthy person's hon could. Inner-being observation: wealth distorts authentic relationship, substituting material attraction for genuine relational commitment.

**Pro 19:14 (P):** "House and wealth (hon) are inherited from fathers, but a prudent wife is from the Lord."
Hon is inherited — it passes from generation to generation. The prudent wife comes from God. Inner-being content: the contrast between what human inheritance provides (material) and what divine gift provides (wisdom/prudence). Inherited hon is valuable but is a human transaction. The prudent wife — the wisdom that enables the household to flourish — is not inherited but divinely given. The inner person who recognises this distinction knows the limit of material inheritance.

**Pro 24:4 (P):** "by knowledge the rooms are filled with all precious and pleasant riches (hon)."
Knowledge fills rooms with hon. Inner-being content: inner knowledge (da.at) is named as the source that fills the house with hon. The intellectual-wisdom capacity produces material abundance as its fruit. This is the wisdom tradition's consistent claim: the ordered inner life (wisdom, knowledge, understanding) produces material flourishing as a natural consequence.

**Pro 28:8 (P):** "Whoever multiplies his wealth (hon) by interest and profit gathers it for him who is generous to the poor."
Hon multiplied by interest — unjust accumulation. The outcome: gathered for the generous person. Inner-being content: the person who accumulates hon through unjust means (interest, profit in the OT sense) does not ultimately benefit from it. The providence of God redirects it to the generous person. The inner moral failure of unjust accumulation does not produce lasting benefit; the inner moral quality of generosity is what ultimately receives the material abundance.

**Pro 30:15 (P):** "Three things are never satisfied; four never say, 'Enough (hon)': Sheol, the barren womb, the land never satisfied with water, and the fire..."
Hon = "Enough." The never-saying-enough is insatiability. The four things that never say hon — never acknowledge sufficiency — are named: Sheol, the barren womb, thirsty land, fire. These are forces of consuming demand. The inner-being content is present in the comparison: there is a condition of the inner person analogous to these insatiable forces — the inner person who cannot say "enough," who never reaches satisfaction.

**Note on hon:** Reading every verse carefully, hon operates in a complex moral-inner domain. It names material wealth in relation to: (1) the inner orientation of the person who holds it — whether it is the object of hasty grasping (Pro 28:22) or patient gathering (Pro 13:11), whether it is concealed or displayed (Pro 13:7), whether it attracts false friendship (Pro 19:4); (2) the inner character of the one who produces it — diligence (Pro 12:27), knowledge (Pro 24:4), generosity (Pro 11:25 via da.shen); (3) its limits as an inner-security foundation — the imagined stronghold (Pro 18:11), the uselessness in divine judgment (Pro 11:4), the inability to purchase love (Song 8:7); (4) its proper ordering when given to God (Pro 3:9) or received through Wisdom (Pro 8:18). Hon is the cluster's richest OT term — 26 verses, almost all in Proverbs, constituting the wisdom tradition's sustained engagement with the inner-being significance of material wealth.

## H6223 a.shir — rich (mti_id=7584)
**Relevant P-status verses:**

**2Sa 12:4 (P):** "Now there came a traveler to the rich man (a.shir), and he was unwilling (cha.mal — to spare, have pity) to take one of his own flock or herd to prepare for the guest who had come to him, but he took the poor man's lamb and prepared it for the man who had come to him."
The rich man — Nathan's parable to David. He has many flocks and herds (v.2) but is unwilling (cha.mal — specifically the failure of pity) to use his own. Inner-being content: the rich man's withholding is named through the word cha.mal — he fails to spare, fails to have pity. The inner act of the rich man is the refusal to give from abundance while taking from poverty. The failure is specifically one of pity — the capacity to feel and act on compassion toward another — which his wealth has not produced.

**Job 27:19 (P):** "He goes to bed rich (a.shir) and will do so no more; he opens his eyes, and his wealth is gone."
The wicked rich man goes to bed in prosperity and wakes to find it gone. Inner-being content: the suddenness of the loss — between sleeping and waking, the material condition reverses. The a.shir condition is revealed as radically unstable. The person who went to sleep as a.shir wakes as not-a.shir. The inner-being implication: anchoring the inner person in the condition of being-rich is anchoring in something that can vanish overnight.

**Pro 10:15 (P):** "A rich man's (a.shir) wealth is his strong city; the poverty of the poor is their ruin."
The rich man's wealth is his inner psychological fortress. Inner-being content: the a.shir person experiences their wealth as oz (strength, stronghold). This is an inner-being observation about how wealth functions in the psychology of the wealthy: it provides a felt sense of security, a protective city. The observation is neutral — it notes this is how it feels, not that it is actually protective.

**Pro 14:20 (P):** "The poor is disliked even by his neighbor, but the rich man (a.shir) has many friends."
The social reality: the a.shir attracts friends; the poor is disliked even by neighbors. Inner-being content: wealth creates social magnetism — people are drawn to the rich. The inner dimension of the rich person: they experience themselves as having many friends. But the next verse and the broader Proverbs context makes clear that these friendships are conditional on the wealth. The rich person's inner experience of being well-connected may be a function of their material condition, not their character.

**Pro 18:11 (P):** "A rich man's (a.shir) wealth is his strong city, and like a high wall in his imagination."
Same as Pro 10:15 plus "in his imagination." The high wall is described as mas.kit — a thought, an image in the mind. The inner stronghold of wealth is explicitly named as psychological — it exists in the person's inner imagination as a wall of security. Inner-being content: the security of wealth is partly a mental construct — the rich person experiences (imagines) themselves as protected by a high wall. This is not reality-denial but an inner-being observation: wealth produces a specific psychological sense of security that may or may not correspond to actual protection.

**Pro 18:23 (P):** "The poor man uses entreaties, but the rich man (a.shir) answers roughly."
The a.shir answers roughly — the inner posture of the wealthy toward the requests of the poor is roughness, harshness (az.zot — severity, hardness). Inner-being content: wealth produces a specific inner disposition toward petitioners — the capacity to be harsh, to answer without the softening that poverty-induced dependency produces. The poor entreat because they must; the rich answer roughly because they can. The inner-being condition of the a.shir includes this capacity for inner hardness toward the vulnerable.

**Pro 22:2 (P):** "The rich (a.shir) and the poor meet together; the Lord is the maker of them both."
A.shir and poor (rash) both made by God. Inner-being content: the shared creatureliness before God — both the a.shir and the rash are God's creation. The wealth differential does not create a different ontological standing before God. The inner-being implication for the a.shir: their material condition does not constitute a different quality of person before God. Both are equally made, equally accountable.

**Pro 22:7 (P):** "The rich (a.shir) rules over the poor, and the borrower is the slave of the lender."
A.shir exercises power over the poor — the social-structural reality of wealth as power. Inner-being content: the a.shir is in a position of dominion (ma.shal) over the poor. The wealth is not neutral — it constitutes social power. The inner dimension of the a.shir in this position: they are the ruler, the dominant party. The poor-person's inner condition (slave, borrower) is shaped by this structural relationship. The verse does not evaluate this but names the inner-structural reality of the wealth differential.

**Pro 28:6 (P):** "Better is a poor man who walks in his integrity than a rich man (a.shir) who is crooked in his ways."
A.shir who is crooked (ik.kesh — perverse, twisted) in his ways vs. the poor who walks in integrity. Inner-being content: the inner character comparison — integrity in poverty is better than material wealth with moral crookedness. The a.shir can be ik.kesh — the wealth does not produce inner moral uprightness and can coexist with its absence. The comparative better confirms: inner character (integrity) is the higher value.

**Pro 28:11 (P):** "A rich man (a.shir) is wise in his own eyes (cha.kham be.e.nav), but a poor man who has understanding will find him out."
The a.shir is wise in his own eyes — the inner self-perception of the wealthy person is that they are wise. Inner-being content: wealth produces a specific epistemic distortion — the wealthy person believes themselves to be wise on the basis of their material success. The poor person with genuine understanding (bi.nah) can see through this. The a.shir's inner self-perception (wise, knowledgeable, capable) is a function of their material success, not their actual wisdom. The poor person with understanding has access to a form of inner knowledge the a.shir lacks.

**Ecc 5:12 (P):** "Sweet is the sleep of a laborer, whether he eats little or much, but the full stomach of the rich (a.shir) will not let him sleep."
The a.shir's full stomach — their abundance — prevents sleep. Inner-being content: material abundance produces a specific physiological-inner restlessness. The laborer sleeps sweetly regardless of how much they ate (inner-physical peace); the rich person's material abundance — their full stomach — disturbs their rest. The irony: abundance, which should satisfy, produces a form of inner-physical unrest that poverty does not.

**Ecc 10:6 (P):** "Folly is set in many high places, and the rich (a.shir) sit in a low place."
The social inversion wisdom observes: the foolish are in high places, the rich are sitting low. Inner-being content: this is a wisdom observation about the disordering of proper social hierarchy — merit and character do not determine position. The a.shir finding themselves in a low place despite their wealth, the fool in high place — the inner experience of social inversion. The observation is Qohelet's — it is vanity that the social order is disordered this way.

**Mic 6:12 (P):** "Your rich men (a.shir) are full of violence (cha.mas); your inhabitants speak lies, and their tongue is deceitful in their mouth."
The rich men of Jerusalem: full of violence, speaking lies, deceitful tongue. Inner-being content: the a.shir of Jerusalem are characterised by cha.mas (violence, wrongdoing, injustice) as their inner condition. The material condition of being rich is accompanied by the inner moral condition of violence and deception. The rich men here are not merely wealthy — their wealth is paired with an inner orientation of violence and dishonesty that characterises their entire being.

**Note on a.shir:** Reading every verse, a.shir names the social category of the rich person and consistently reveals their inner-being condition through the context. The recurring pattern: the a.shir experiences imagined security (strong city, high wall in imagination — Pro 10:15; 18:11), attracts conditional friends (Pro 14:20), answers roughly to petitioners (Pro 18:23), and tends toward self-assessed wisdom (Pro 28:11). The term is also associated with violence (Mic 6:12), crookedness (Pro 28:6), withheld compassion (2Sa 12:4), and the inability to sleep despite abundance (Ecc 5:12). The wisdom tradition consistently observes the inner moral and psychological condition of the a.shir without romanticising poverty — the contrast "better a poor person with integrity than a rich person with crooked ways" (Pro 28:6) is the most explicit evaluative statement.

## H6238 a.shar — to enrich (mti_id=7585)
**Relevant P-status verses:**

**Gen 14:23 (P):** "that I would not take a thread or a sandal strap or anything that is yours, lest you should say, 'I have made Abram rich (a.shar).'"
Abram refuses enrichment from the king of Sodom. Inner-being content: Abram's deliberate refusal to be enriched is motivated by testimony — he does not want his material wealth attributed to a human source rather than to God alone. The inner act of not-being-enriched: refusing what was legitimately offered, sacrificing material advantage, in order to preserve the integrity of his dependence on God. This is one of the most specific inner-being acts in the cluster — the conscious decision not to accumulate.

**1Sa 2:7 (P):** "The Lord makes poor and makes rich (a.shar); he brings low and he exalts."
Hannah's song: God enriches (a.shar) and God makes poor. Inner-being content: the recognition that becoming rich is ultimately God's act. The one who becomes rich (a.shar) does so because God wills it. The inner-being implication: material prosperity is not self-generated but divinely given. The person who knows this receives wealth differently — with gratitude rather than pride, with openness rather than possessiveness.

**Job 15:29 (P):** "he will not be rich (a.shar), and his wealth will not endure, nor will his possessions spread over the earth."
Eliphaz speaking of the wicked: they will not become rich (a.shar), their wealth will not endure. Inner-being content: the wickedness of the person is named as the reason their a.shar — their enrichment — will fail. The inner moral condition (wicked) produces the material outcome (not becoming rich, not enduring). This is the reverse of the wisdom pattern — wicked inner character blocks the natural flourishing that comes to the righteous.

**Pro 10:4 (P):** "A slack hand causes poverty, but the hand of the diligent makes rich (a.shar)."
Diligence makes rich — a.shar as the outcome of diligent inner character. Inner-being content: the inner quality of diligence (cha.ruts — sharp, diligent, urgent) applied to work produces material enrichment. The slack hand — the inner quality of slackness — produces poverty. Character (diligent or slack) determines material outcome.

**Pro 10:22 (P):** "The blessing of the Lord makes rich (a.shar), and he adds no sorrow with it."
The Lord's blessing makes rich (a.shar) — divine enrichment. And "he adds no sorrow with it" — the wealth from divine blessing does not carry the inner cost that human toil-produced wealth carries. Inner-being content: there is a form of enrichment that comes from God's blessing that is qualitatively different — it does not bring the hidden sorrow or anxiety that self-accumulated wealth produces. The inner experience of divinely-given wealth is prosperity without grief.

**Pro 13:7 (P):** "One pretends (mit.a.sher) to be rich, yet has nothing..."
A.shar in hithpael: pretending to be rich. Inner-being content: the person who performs wealth they do not have — the act of presenting oneself as a.shar when actually poor. The inner dishonesty of the self-presentation, the gap between inner reality and outward display.

**Pro 21:17 (P):** "Whoever loves pleasure (sim.chah) will be a poor man; he who loves wine and oil will not be rich (a.shar)."
Love of pleasure prevents becoming rich. Inner-being content: the inner orientation toward pleasure (sim.chah — joy, pleasure) and toward sensory indulgence (wine, oil) is named as the inner condition that prevents material enrichment. The loves — the inner dispositions of desire — determine the material trajectory. The person whose inner desire is toward pleasure does not build material wealth.

**Pro 23:4 (P):** "Do not toil to acquire wealth (a.shar); be discerning enough to desist."
The explicit wisdom command: do not toil to become rich (a.shar). Be discerning (bi.nah) enough to stop. Inner-being content: the wisdom counsel is against the inner drive to accumulate. Bi.nah — understanding — is what enables the person to stop pursuing wealth. The discerning person knows when to desist from the wealth-seeking drive. This requires an inner act of restraint applied to the natural economic drive toward accumulation.

**Pro 28:20 (P):** "A faithful man will abound with blessings, but whoever hastens to be rich (a.shar) will not go unpunished."
Hastening to be rich (a.shar) brings punishment. Inner-being content: the inner urgency — the hastening — in the pursuit of wealth is named as the inner quality that produces punishment. It is not wealth itself that is punished but the urgent, hasty inner pursuit of it. The faithful person (e.mun — steady, reliable) receives blessings; the hasty wealth-seeker receives punishment. Inner character (faithful steadiness vs. hasty urgency) determines the outcome.

**Psa 49:16 (P):** "Be not afraid when a man becomes rich (a.shar), when the glory of his house increases."
The command not to fear when another person becomes rich. Inner-being content: the instruction addresses an inner-being response — fear — that is produced by watching another's material prosperity grow. The psalmist counsels: do not be internally disturbed by the prosperity of others. The fear is probably anxiety, envy, or unsettled comparison. The appropriate inner response to witnessing another's a.shar is composure, not fear.

**Hos 12:8 (P):** "Ephraim has said, 'Ah, but I am rich (a.shar); I have found wealth for myself; in all my labors they cannot find in me iniquity or sin.'"
The self-declaration of Ephraim: I am rich (a.shar), I have found wealth, therefore I am innocent. Inner-being content: the most explicit connection in the entire cluster between material prosperity and the inner self-assessment of moral innocence. Ephraim claims: because I am rich, I must be righteous — "they cannot find in me iniquity." The wealth is the inner evidence of divine favour and moral acceptability. This is precisely the inner distortion that wealth produces — the equation of material prosperity with moral status. God's response (v.9) is to expose this as delusion.

**Zec 11:5 (P):** "Those who buy them slaughter them and go unpunished, and those who sell them say, 'Blessed be the Lord, I have become rich (a.shar),' and their own shepherds have no pity on them."
The corrupt shepherds who sell and exploit the flock declare: "Blessed be the Lord, I have become rich." Inner-being content: the self-congratulatory attribution of wealth to divine blessing while exploiting others. The inner act of thanking God for wealth obtained through the exploitation of the flock is the height of the a.shar-distortion: theological self-justification of unjust accumulation. The declaration of blessing alongside the absence of pity (cha.mal — same word as 2Sa 12:4) reveals the inner condition: God's name invoked in gratitude while the inner compassion-capacity is absent.

**Note on a.shar:** Reading every verse, a.shar (to enrich, to become rich) reveals three consistent patterns: (1) enrichment as the fruit of inner character — diligence produces a.shar (Pro 10:4), steadiness receives blessing while hastiness brings punishment (Pro 28:20); (2) the drive to become rich as the inner problem — hastening to be rich brings punishment (Pro 28:20), do not toil to be rich (Pro 23:4), the wicked will not be rich (Job 15:29); (3) the self-declaration of richness as the ground of false moral assessment — Ephraim claims innocence because he is rich (Hos 12:8), corrupt shepherds thank God for wealth obtained through exploitation (Zec 11:5). The deliberate refusal of Abram (Gen 14:23) stands alone as the paradigmatic positive act — the inner discipline of not-being-enriched in order to preserve the integrity of divine dependence.

## READING COMPLETE — What the verses yield

### What emerged from reading — without pre-imposing categories

**Observation 1 — The vocabulary is not primarily about wealth as a material condition.**
Reading every verse, almost no term simply describes material wealth neutrally. Every term in this cluster operates in a relational or moral or inner-being frame. O.sher names riches in relation to trust, boasting, wisdom's gift, transience. Hon names wealth in relation to character, ordering toward God, imagined security, insatiability. A.shir names the rich person in terms of their inner psychological condition — imagined security, self-assessed wisdom, roughness toward others. A.shar names the act of becoming rich in terms of the inner character that produces it or distorts it. The vocabulary is not primarily economic — it is consistently inner-being.

**Observation 2 — The directional pattern is the dominant structural feature.**
Across the entire corpus, the most consistent pattern is direction: what the inner person does with wealth, toward whom they orient their material resources, what they trust, what they assess themselves to be. Wealth toward self (Luk 12:21 negative) vs. wealth toward God. Trusting in riches (Psa 49:6; 52:7; Pro 11:28) vs. trusting in God. Being rich through Babylon's exploitation vs. being rich toward God. The inner direction of the wealth-orientation is the primary moral question.

**Observation 3 — The rich/poor contrast is not primarily economic but inner-being and eschatological.**
The NT ptōchos/plousios contrast is not primarily social commentary. It is inner-being: the poor are blessed because poverty may be the condition of inner dependence and openness (Mat 5:3; Luk 4:18); the rich face a specific inner obstacle because wealth may be the condition of inner self-sufficiency that blocks reception (Rev 3:17; Luk 18:23). The eschatological reversal (Luk 6:24; 1:53; 16:22-23) exposes that material richness and actual inner richness are separable and may be inversely related.

**Observation 4 — The prosperity-apostasy sequence is an evidenced inner-being mechanism, not a generalisation.**
Deu 32:15 (sha.men verb), Hos 10:1 (rov), Neh 9:25 (a.dan, sha.men adj, sha.men verb, rov), Deu 31:20 (da.shen), Isa 6:10 (sha.men verb): the pattern is not a general observation but a specific inner-being mechanism with multiple independent witnesses. Material prosperity produces satiation; satiation produces the inner sense of no-longer-needing-God; that inner sense produces turning. The mechanism is not mechanical (prosperity always produces apostasy) but probabilistic — it is the systematic risk of abundance.

**Observation 5 — The ordered response is specific and evidenced, not merely aspirational.**
Autarkēs (Phil 4:11) — learned contentment. Moses (Heb 11:26) — forward vision that revalues. Zacchaeus (Luk 19:8) — spontaneous restitution as response to encounter. The Macedonian churches (2Cor 8:2) — wealth of generosity from poverty and joy. 2Cor 8:9 (Christ's poverty). Gen 14:23 (Abram's refusal). Pro 23:4 (wisdom counselling desistance from wealth-seeking). These are specific evidenced acts and patterns, not aspirational ideals.

**Observation 6 — What was not found.**
Generosity as a standalone characteristic is present in the verse evidence (Luk 19:8; 2Cor 8:2; 9:11; Pro 11:25 via da.shen) but is always instrumental — it flows from encounter, from being enriched for that purpose, from the natural outworking of right inner orientation. It is never named as a characteristic in itself by the M46 terms. The M46 vocabulary does not independently name generosity — it names the conditions from which generosity flows and toward which it flows.

**Observation 7 — The sub-group structure proposed earlier is substantially correct but needs refinement.**
M46-A (distortion) and M46-C (ordered response) are well-supported by the verse evidence. M46-B (moral category) is also supported but operates somewhat differently from A and C — it is less about the inner condition of the person toward wealth and more about the relationship between inner character and the quality of wealth. A possible refinement: M46-B is the sub-group where the biblical wisdom tradition treats wealth as a mirror of character — the wealth of the righteous is genuine; the income of the wicked brings trouble; diligence produces enrichment; wisdom carries wealth with righteousness. This is the character-revealing-through-wealth pattern.

### Step 1: What groupings emerge from the reading?

Sitting with all the verse readings without forcing a structure. What do the verses naturally cluster around?

**Cluster 1 — The inner person who has grown fat**

Deu 32:15 (sha.men verb — grew fat, stout, sleek → forsook God, scoffed)
Neh 9:25 (sha.men verb, a.dan — became fat, delighted in goodness → turned, v.26)
Deu 31:20 (da.shen — grown fat → turn to other gods, despise, break covenant)

What these share: material abundance or the condition of satiation produces a specific inner dulling. The person who has grown fat — fully satisfied, materially prosperous — loses something in the inner life. The capacity to perceive God, to feel the weight of the vulnerable, to maintain moral alertness. The fat condition is the condition of inner complacency, of the closed and self-sufficient inner person. The pattern: provision received → satiation → inner closure.

**Cluster 2 — The imagined stronghold**

Pro 10:15 (hon, a.shir — rich man's wealth is his strong city)

The wealth becomes the psychological fortress — felt as real, described as imagination by the text. The distortion: the inner person has placed their confidence, their self-assessment, and their sense of safety in something external and transient. This is not the same as the fat-cluster above — these verses are about where the inner person has put their trust and self-perception, not about what prosperity does to their moral alertness.

**Cluster 3 — Wealth as the self-justifying declaration**

Rev 3:17 (plousios, plouteō — "I am rich, I have prospered, I need nothing")
1Cor 4:8 (plouteō — "already you have become rich! you have become kings!")
Hos 12:8 (a.shar — "I am rich; I have found wealth; they cannot find iniquity in me")
Zec 11:5 (a.shar — "Blessed be the Lord, I have become rich" — while exploiting the flock)

What these share: wealth becomes the inner basis for a declaration about one's own status — moral, spiritual, royal. "I am rich" means "I am fine, I am validated, I am innocent, I need nothing." The material condition is read as inner-spiritual evidence. This is distinct from simply trusting in wealth (Cluster 2) — it is the active claim that wealth proves something about the person's inner standing before God. This is the most acute distortion: wealth as the ground of false inner assessment.

**Cluster 4 — Wealth producing relational distortion**

Pro 19:4 (hon — wealth brings many new friends; poor man deserted)
Pro 14:20 (a.shir — rich has many friends; poor disliked by neighbor)
Pro 18:23 (a.shir — poor uses entreaties; rich answers roughly)
Pro 22:7 (a.shir — rich rules over poor; borrower is slave to lender)
Mic 6:12 (a.shir — rich men full of violence; inhabitants speak lies)
Jam 2:2-3 (ptōchos — poor treated with contempt in the assembly)
Jam 2:6 (plousios — rich oppress and drag into court)
2Sa 12:4 (a.shir — unwilling to give from own flock; takes poor man's lamb)
Luk 16:19-21 (plousios — feasts daily; Lazarus desiring scraps)
Joh 12:6 (ptōchos — Judas uses the poor rhetorically while stealing)

What these share: the condition of wealth systematically distorts how the wealthy person relates to others — to the poor, to those who need them, to those beneath them socially. The rich attract false friends, answer harshly, rule domineeringly, acquire through violence, ignore the suffering neighbor, use the poor as rhetorical cover for self-interest. The relational domain is where wealth's inner distortion most visibly expresses itself.

**Cluster 5 — The deceitfulness of riches**

Mat 13:22 (ploutos — deceitfulness of riches chokes the word)
Mar 4:19 (ploutos — deceitfulness of riches chokes)
Luk 8:14 (ploutos — choked by riches and pleasures)
Jam 5:2 (ploutos — riches have rotted)
Ecc 4:8 (o.sher — eyes never satisfied with riches)
Ecc 6:2 (o.sher — wealth present but power to enjoy it absent)
Ecc 5:13-14 (o.sher — riches kept to owner's hurt, then lost)

What these share: wealth does not deliver what it promises. It chokes the word (cannot receive what God gives), it decays (rots, is lost), it cannot satisfy (never says enough), it cannot even be enjoyed when present (Ecc 6:2). The deceitfulness of wealth is not that it is evil but that it lies about its own nature — it presents itself as satisfying but produces the opposite, as securing but is uncertain, as valuable but perishes. This is the word apatē (deception) that Mat 13:22 uses.

**Cluster 6 — The transience of the rich and their wealth**

Jam 1:10-11 (plousios — rich fades away like grass and flower)
Job 27:19 (a.shir — goes to bed rich, wakes with wealth gone)
Pro 27:24 (cho.sen — riches do not last forever)
Pro 11:4 (hon — riches do not profit in the day of wrath)
Rev 6:15 (plousios — rich hide in caves at day of wrath)
Rev 18:15-19 (plouteō — merchants who grew rich weep at Babylon's fall)
Jam 5:2 (ploutos — riches rotted)
Psa 49:16 (a.shar — be not afraid when a man becomes rich)

What these share: wealth is impermanent. The rich person, and their wealth, passes away. The text returns again and again to this — the moment of reversal, the morning when wealth is gone, the day of divine judgment when material status provides no shelter. The inner-being implication: anchoring oneself in something transient is the foundation problem. Be not afraid when another becomes rich — it will not last.

**Cluster 7 — Poverty as the condition of kingdom openness**

Mat 5:3 (ptōchos — poor in spirit, theirs is the kingdom)
Luk 4:18 (ptōchos — gospel proclaimed to the poor)
Luk 6:20 (ptōchos — blessed are you who are poor)
Mat 11:5; Luk 7:22 (ptōchos — poor have good news preached to them)
Jam 2:5 (ptōchos — God chose the poor to be rich in faith)
Rev 2:9 (plousios — you are poor but you are rich)
Luk 1:53 (plouteō — rich sent away empty, hungry filled)

What these share: material poverty is not merely a social misfortune — in the biblical account it is the condition that correlates with openness to God. The poor are blessed, chosen, recipients of the gospel, heirs of the kingdom. God has chosen the ptōchos of this world to be rich in faith. The materially poor church (Smyrna) is actually rich. The hungry are filled while the rich are sent empty. This is not a romanticisation of poverty — it is an observation that the condition of dependence and need, which poverty creates, is the inner condition in which the kingdom most naturally arrives.

**Cluster 8 — The relinquishment that enriches**

2Cor 8:9 (ptōcheuō — Christ became poor so you might become rich)
2Cor 6:10 (ploutizō — as poor yet making many rich)
2Cor 9:11 (ploutizō — enriched in every way to be generous)
2Cor 8:2 (ploutos — extreme poverty overflowed in wealth of generosity)
Luk 19:8 (ptōchos — Zacchaeus gives half to poor)
Luk 21:3 (ptōchos — widow gave more than all)
Mar 10:21; Luk 18:22 (ptōchos — sell all, give to poor, treasure in heaven)
Mat 19:21 (ptōchos — give to poor; treasure in heaven)
Heb 11:26 (ploutos — Moses counted reproach greater wealth than Egypt's treasures)
Gen 14:23 (a.shar — Abram refused enrichment to preserve his witness)
Pro 23:4 (a.shar — do not toil to acquire wealth; desist)
Pro 11:25 (da.shen — whoever brings blessing will be enriched)

What these share: the act of releasing, giving, refusing, or relinquishing material wealth — whether voluntarily (Abram, Moses, the rich young man invited, Zacchaeus) or in poverty (the widow, the Macedonian churches, Paul) — produces a form of enrichment that exceeds what was released. This is not reciprocity but inversion: the act of giving from poverty produces wealth of generosity (2Cor 8:2); the act of refusing enrichment preserves the witness (Gen 14:23); the voluntary impoverishment of Christ produces the enrichment of many (2Cor 8:9). Wealth flows through relinquishment, not accumulation.

**Cluster 9 — Learned contentment independent of circumstance**

Phil 4:11 (autarkēs — learned, in whatever situation, to be content)
Isa 23:18 (cha.san negated — merchandise not stored or hoarded but given)
Pro 28:25 (da.shen — trust in the Lord produces enrichment; greed produces strife)
Neh 9:25 (a.dan — delighting in God's goodness — the ordered form of delight in abundance)
1Ti 6:17 (plousiōs — God who richly provides everything for enjoyment)
Rev 3:18 (plouteō — buy from me gold refined by fire, so that you may be truly rich)
1Ti 6:6 (autarkeia context — godliness with contentment is great gain)

What these share: the inner person who has learned not to anchor in material circumstance — neither destroyed by poverty nor corrupted by abundance. Contentment is a learned inner disposition (Phil 4:11), not a natural state. It is not acquired through having enough material goods but through a different kind of inner orientation — trust (Pro 28:25), delight in God's goodness rather than in goods (Neh 9:25), the recognition that true richness comes through refining rather than accumulation (Rev 3:18).

**Cluster 10 — Wealth as the test and mirror of character**

Pro 13:4 (da.shen — soul of diligent richly supplied; soul of sluggard craves)
Pro 12:27 (hon — diligent man gets precious wealth)
Pro 10:4 (a.shar — diligent hand makes rich)
Pro 10:22 (a.shar — Lord's blessing makes rich, adds no sorrow)
Pro 22:4 (o.sher — reward of humility and fear of the Lord is riches)
Pro 8:18 (hon, o.sher — Wisdom carries riches and righteousness)
Psa 112:3 (hon, o.sher — in house of righteous, wealth and righteousness together)
Pro 15:6 (cho.sen — in house of righteous, much treasure; trouble in wicked's income)
Pro 28:6 (a.shir — better poor with integrity than rich and crooked)
Pro 22:2 (a.shir — rich and poor equally made by God)
1Sa 2:7 (a.shar — the Lord makes poor and makes rich)
1Ch 29:12 (o.sher — both riches and honor come from you, God)

What these share: the wisdom tradition treats wealth as a domain that reveals and is shaped by character. The soul of the diligent is richly supplied — character produces material outcome. The house of the righteous has treasure; the income of the wicked brings trouble. Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together — they are not in conflict. The Lord himself makes rich and makes poor — material condition is within divine sovereignty. The person of integrity is better than the wealthy person who is crooked. Wealth is a domain that reveals what is in the person — it does not create character but it expresses and tests it.

### Step 2: Testing the ten clusters

Are these ten clusters actually distinct? Do any need combining?

**1 and 2 are distinct:** The fat-cluster (1) is about what prosperity does to inner moral alertness; the imagined-stronghold cluster (2) is about where the inner person places trust and self-assessment. Both are distortions but different mechanisms.

**3 is a subset of 2 but sharp enough to stand.** The self-justifying declaration ("I am rich therefore I am fine") is the extreme form of the imagined-stronghold. It might be captured within cluster 2 as the acute form, or kept separate as the most dangerous expression. Keeping separate for now.

**4 and 5 are related but distinct.** Cluster 4 is about what wealth does to relationships (person-to-person). Cluster 5 is about what wealth does to the inner person's capacity to receive God's word and find genuine satisfaction. Both are distortions but different registers.

**6 is the foundational observation that underlies clusters 2, 3, 4, and 5.** Everything built on what is transient will fail. Cluster 6 might be a sub-note to the others rather than a standalone group.

**7 and 8 form a pair.** Poverty as the condition of kingdom openness (7) and the relinquishment that enriches (8) are two faces of the same movement: giving/receiving in the kingdom register inverts the material register. They could be combined or kept separate.

**9 (contentment) is distinct.** It stands as the learned inner disposition — neither in the poverty/openness register nor in the relinquishment register but in the trained-through-circumstance register.

**10 (wealth as character-mirror) is distinct.** This is the wisdom tradition's specific contribution — the observation that character and material condition are connected, that wealth reveals and tests the inner person, that ordered character produces ordered wealth.

It may be the capstone — the inner-being resolution that stands after all seven groups have been described. The person who has worked through A-G has, perhaps, arrived at the capacity for autarkeia.

### Testing each cluster for its inner-being characteristic

Working through the seven clusters, asking: what is the inner-being characteristic this cluster names?

**Cluster A — The fat inner person**
Deu 32:15; Isa 6:10; Neh 9:25; Deu 31:20; Jer 5:28; Hos 10:1

What is happening in the inner being? The person who has received material abundance becomes inwardly dull. The capacity to perceive God, to feel the weight of moral demand, to remain alert to what conscience requires — this diminishes. The technical term is sha.men: the heart grows fat, thick, unresponsive. This is not a passive process — it is a progressive inner condition: satiated → dull → closed → turned.

Inner-being characteristic: **inner dullness produced by abundance** — the condition in which material satisfaction progressively closes the inner person's responsiveness to God and to moral demand.

**Cluster B — The imagined security and false self-assessment**
Pro 18:11; Rev 3:17; Hos 12:8; Psa 52:7; Psa 49:6; Pro 11:28; Pro 28:11; 1Ti 6:17

What is happening in the inner being? The person anchors their identity, their security, and their self-assessment in their material condition. The inner conviction: "I am rich, therefore I am safe; I am prosperous, therefore I am vindicated; I have much, therefore I need nothing." The wealthy person becomes wise in their own eyes, trusts their riches as their stronghold, and uses their material success as the evidence of their inner standing. This is a specific misdirection of the inner faculty of trust — trust (ba.tach) placed in material wealth rather than in God.

Inner-being characteristic: **misplaced trust and false self-sufficiency** — the condition in which material wealth becomes the foundation of inner security and self-assessment, displacing God as the ground of both.

**Cluster C — The relational distortion**
Luk 16:19-21; Jam 2:2-6; Mic 6:12; 2Sa 12:4; Pro 18:23; Pro 22:7; Pro 14:20; Joh 12:6

What is happening in the inner being? The person with material wealth develops a specific inner posture toward others that is shaped by the differential. Toward the poor: indifference, roughness, contempt, exploitation. Toward peers: attraction of those who want proximity to wealth, producing conditional relationships. The inner capacity for pity (cha.mal) is specifically what is absent in the rich man who takes the poor man's lamb and in the shepherds who grow rich while their flock suffers. The relational faculty — the capacity to feel with and toward the other — is distorted by the power differential wealth creates.

Inner-being characteristic: **hardness toward others** — the inner condition in which material wealth erodes the capacity for genuine compassion and produces indifference, exploitation, and contempt toward those with less.

**Cluster D — The deceitfulness and transience**
Mat 13:22; Ecc 4:8; Ecc 6:2; Ecc 5:13-14; Jam 5:2; Pro 27:24; Jam 1:10-11; Job 27:19

What is happening in the inner being? Wealth deceives the inner person about its own nature. It presents itself as satisfying but does not satisfy. It presents itself as secure but is transient. The eyes are never satisfied with riches; the person toils endlessly for more and cannot ask "for whom?" The person has wealth but lacks the power to enjoy it — the inner capacity for enjoyment is absent. And ultimately what was accumulated rots, is lost overnight, fades while the person is still pursuing it.

This cluster is less about a distinctive inner characteristic and more about the inner experience of wealth's failure to fulfil its promise. It names an inner condition: the insatiable-ness, the inability to enjoy, the grief of loss.

Inner-being characteristic: **insatiability and the inability to be satisfied** — the inner condition of the person for whom material wealth never reaches the point of enough, and the related condition of possessing wealth without the inner capacity to receive it as good.

**Observation:** Clusters A, B, C, D are not four separate inner-being characteristics of the same kind. A and B are more fundamental — they describe what happens to the inner person structurally (dulled, misplaced trust). C and D describe specific expressions or experiences within that structural condition. A and B might be the primary groupings; C and D might be secondary manifestations.

**Alternatively:** A, B, C, D could be kept distinct as four sub-faces of what happens to the inner person within the wealth domain, where wealth is not yet the subject of right ordering. Together they constitute the inner-being consequences of material wealth received without wisdom.

**Cluster E — Poverty as kingdom ground**
Mat 5:3; Luk 4:18; Luk 6:20; Jam 2:5; Rev 2:9; Luk 1:53

What is happening in the inner being? The condition of material poverty produces — or at least correlates with — a specific inner posture: the absence of material self-sufficiency creates an inner openness to receive from God. The poor in spirit (Mat 5:3) are those whose inner life is characterised by this posture of dependence and need — they know they have nothing to offer and are entirely receptive to what God gives. This is not idealising poverty but observing that the removal of material security may expose and create the inner condition most naturally receptive to the kingdom.

Inner-being characteristic: **inner poverty — the posture of dependence and openness before God** — the condition of the inner person who has nothing to offer, who is not self-sufficient, who is constitutively receptive to divine gift.

**Cluster F — The relinquishment that enriches**
2Cor 8:9; 2Cor 6:10; 2Cor 8:2; 2Cor 9:11; Gen 14:23; Luk 19:8; Luk 21:3; Heb 11:26; Pro 23:4; Pro 11:25 (via da.shen)

What is happening in the inner being? The person makes a voluntary inner choice to release, give, refuse, or relinquish material wealth — and this act of release produces a form of enrichment that exceeds what was released. This is not self-denial for its own sake but a specific inner-being act: the turning of material goods away from self-accumulation and toward others, producing the paradox of enrichment through impoverishment. Moses revalues: the reproach of Christ is greater wealth. Abram refuses: his integrity before God outweighs material gain. The Macedonians give from poverty: their generosity is a ploutos that exceeds their ptōcheia.

Inner-being characteristic: **the inner act of release** — the deliberate relinquishment of material wealth in response to a higher valuation; the inner orientation that gives wealth away rather than hoarding it, and finds enrichment in doing so.

**Cluster G — Wealth as character-mirror**
Pro 13:4; Pro 15:6; Pro 10:4; Pro 22:4; Pro 8:18; Psa 112:3; Isa 33:6; 1Sa 2:7; 1Ch 29:12

What is happening in the inner being? The wisdom tradition observes a consistent connection between inner character and material condition. The soul of the diligent is richly supplied. The house of the righteous has genuine treasure; the income of the wicked brings trouble. Humility and fear of the Lord produce wealth as their fruit. Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together. The material domain is not autonomous — it reflects and responds to the inner-being orientation of the person. God himself makes rich and makes poor — he governs the material domain.

Inner-being characteristic: **the ordering of character and material life** — the inner-being condition in which right character (diligence, trust, humility, wisdom, fear of God) produces a right relationship to material goods; wealth as the fruit of ordered inner life rather than its goal.

**The autarkeia question**
Phil 4:11 — "I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content."

Reading this again against all the clusters above: autarkeia is the inner-being condition that stands on the other side of clusters A-D. The person who has grown fat (A), who has misplaced trust (B), who has become hard toward others (C), who is insatiable (D) — these are the person who has not learned autarkeia. The person who has learned autarkeia has navigated all of this and arrived at an inner stability that is independent of material circumstance. It is neither the inner poverty of E (a condition of material lack) nor the release of F (an active giving) nor the character-wisdom of G (a fruit of virtue). It is specifically the learned disposition of inner settledness in the face of the entire wealth domain.

Autarkeia names an achieved inner-being state — contentment as the resolved inner condition of the person who is no longer governed by the wealth domain in either direction.

The seven clusters (A-G) plus autarkeia could be housed in several ways. Testing options:

**Option 1: Retain three sub-groups, map clusters in**

Looking at the clusters from an inner-being faculty perspective:
- One sub-group around what abundance does to *perception and trust* (A+B: dullness + misplaced trust)
- One sub-group around what abundance does to *relationship and satisfaction* (C+D: hardness + insatiability)
- One sub-group around *character as the determinant* (G: ordered inner life producing ordered wealth)
- One sub-group around *the inner posture that resolves the domain* (E+F+autarkeia: poverty of spirit, release, contentment)

**Option 4: Three sub-groups, but with different titles than before**
The reading suggests three primary inner-being movements, not seven discrete clusters:

- The wisdom that orders the wealth domain (G): character as the determinant of wealth's quality and trajectory
- The inner-being life that transcends the domain (E+F+autarkeia): poverty of spirit, release, contentment — the postures that move through or beyond the wealth domain toward something greater

This option keeps three sub-groups but with titles and content derived from the reading rather than from a prior message-frame.

### Sub-group 3 — Cluster G

**What the verses showed:**
The wisdom tradition consistently observes a connection between the inner life and material condition. The soul of the diligent is richly supplied (Pro 13:4 — ne.phesh + da.shen: the deepest inner self made fat by character). The house of the righteous has genuine treasure; the income of the wicked brings trouble (Pro 15:6). Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together (Pro 8:18 — they are not in conflict when wealth comes through Wisdom). The fear of the Lord is Zion's treasure (Isa 33:6 — cho.sen applied to the inner realities of salvation and knowledge). God himself makes poor and makes rich (1Sa 2:7; 1Ch 29:12).

**Testing a name:**
What is the inner-being characteristic? The inner person who has right character — diligence, trust, humility, fear of God, righteousness — stands in a different relationship to material goods. The wealth they have is different in quality from the wealth of the wicked. The soul itself can be richly supplied (da.shen on ne.phesh). The material domain is not separate from the inner-being domain — it responds to and reflects the inner condition of the person.

This is not a prosperity gospel. The wisdom tradition is making a more careful observation: inner character shapes how material goods are obtained, used, and experienced. And the goods themselves — when they come through wisdom — carry a different quality. The fear of the Lord is the true cho.sen. Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together. The material and the moral are not in opposition when the inner life is rightly ordered.

**Proposed sub-group label:** `M46-C` — *The ordering of character and wealth*

**Proposed description:**
"The inner-being observation of the wisdom tradition: character determines the quality and trajectory of material life. The soul of the diligent is richly supplied — inner character produces inner richness that includes but exceeds material wealth. The house of the righteous has genuine treasure; the income of the wicked brings trouble — the moral quality of the inner person shapes the moral quality of what they accumulate. Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together — they are not in conflict when wealth arrives through the ordered inner life. The fear of the Lord is Zion's treasure — the true cho.sen is not material but the inner reality of knowing God. And God governs the material domain entirely: he makes poor and makes rich according to his purposes. The inner person who knows this does not grasp for material goods but receives them as the fruit and gift of right inner orientation."

**Evidencing verses (anchor candidates):**

### Sub-group 4 — Clusters E, F, and autarkeia

**What the verses showed:**
Three related but distinct inner movements: poverty of spirit as the condition of openness to God's kingdom (E); the deliberate relinquishment of material goods that produces genuine enrichment (F); and the learned inner stability of contentment regardless of material circumstance (autarkeia). These are not the same inner-being characteristic but they share a direction: all three describe the inner person who has moved through or beyond the wealth domain's grip.

**Testing a name:**
E names an inner posture: dependence and openness — the person who has nothing to offer and is therefore fully receptive. F names an inner act: the choice to release, give, or refuse wealth in favour of a higher valuation. Autarkeia names an achieved inner state: the settled independence from material circumstance that has been learned through living in both conditions.

What holds these three together? The inner person who is poor in spirit, who gives from poverty, and who has learned contentment all share one thing: they are not governed by the wealth domain. Material abundance does not corrupt them (they either don't have it or have released it or are stable regardless). Material poverty does not destroy them (it becomes the ground of kingdom-belonging or the source of generous giving or the context of learned contentment). The common inner-being characteristic is **freedom from the wealth domain as a determining force**.

But is this one inner-being characteristic or three? Reading the verses again:
- Mat 5:3 — poor in spirit: this is a posture of dependence and receptivity before God
- 2Cor 8:9 — Christ became poor: this is a voluntary act of relinquishment for others
- Phil 4:11 — contentment: this is a learned inner stability regardless of circumstance

These are genuinely different inner-being characteristics. But they share the same direction of travel. Perhaps the sub-group description holds them together under the question: what does it look like when the inner person is no longer captive to the wealth domain?

**Proposed sub-group label:** `M46-D` — *Freedom from the wealth domain*

But "freedom" is somewhat abstract. What the verses actually show is more specific: these inner characteristics all involve the inner person having found a ground that transcends material circumstance. The poor in spirit are blessed because the kingdom is theirs — a possession that exceeds material goods. The one who gives from poverty enriches many — the act of release produces what accumulation could not. The contented person is stable in any circumstance — because they are anchored in something that material change cannot reach.

**Revised label:** `M46-D` — *The inner life beyond material circumstance*

**Proposed description:**
"The inner-being characteristics that emerge when the person is no longer governed by the wealth domain — three related but distinct postures. First, poverty of spirit: the inner condition of dependence and receptivity before God, the person who has nothing to offer and is therefore constitutively open to divine gift; God has chosen the ptōchos of this world to be rich in faith. Second, the inner act of release: the deliberate choice to give, relinquish, or refuse material wealth in response to a higher valuation — Moses counting the reproach of Christ as greater wealth than Egypt's treasure; Abram refusing enrichment to preserve his witness; the widow giving all her living; Christ becoming poor so that others might become rich. Third, learned contentment: the inner stability that Paul names as acquired through living in both abundance and scarcity — 'I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content' — an achieved inner independence from material circumstance that neither poverty destroys nor abundance corrupts."

**Evidencing verses (anchor candidates):**
- Mat 5:3 — "blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven"
- 2Cor 8:9 — "though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor"
- Phil 4:11 — "I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content"
- Heb 11:26 — "he considered the reproach of Christ greater wealth than the treasures of Egypt"
