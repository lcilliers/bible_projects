  → Ordered character-wealth and the inner life beyond material circumstance enable and expand relational capacity. M46-C recognises the relational equality before God that wealth does not create or destroy. M46-D actively extends relational capacity through giving, releasing, and the Christological pattern of self-impoverishment for others' enrichment.
- *T3.2.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?_
  → M46-D engages cognition through the revaluation act: Moses "considered" (hēgeomai, Heb 11:26) the reproach of Christ as greater wealth. The revaluation is a cognitive act — an explicit reckoning that places one value above another. The instruction "do not toil to acquire wealth; be discerning enough to desist" (Pro 23:4) requires the same cognitive stopping of the acquisition-drive.
- *T3.2.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?_
  → Requires and rewards: M46-C requires the active exercise of bi.nah (discernment) for the ordering of character and wealth. M46-D deepens cognition through the revaluation act — the person who has genuinely revalued (Moses, Heb 11:26) has performed a sophisticated cognitive act of comparative assessment across temporal and eternal registers.
- *T3.4.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?_
  → M46-D has the most positive affective content in the cluster. 2Cor 8:2: the Macedonian churches gave "with abundance of joy" (hyperbolē tēs charas) — even in extreme poverty, the act of giving is accompanied by overflowing joy. Phil 4:11-13: the learned contentment is not neutral settledness but a positive inner state. Mat 5:3: the blessing (makarioi) of poverty of spirit names a state of positive affective quality.
- *T3.4.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?_
  → M46-C and M46-D enable positive affect: ordered provision enables enjoyment (Ecc 5:19); M46-D's giving enables joy (2Cor 8:2); contentment enables peace (Phil 4:7-11 — the peace of God guards). These are affect-enabling patterns.
- *T3.6.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?_
  → Voluntary release is the cluster's most explicitly volitional content. Gen 14:23 (Abram's refusal — a clear volitional act), Luk 19:8 (Zacchaeus's spoken resolution — volitional commitment), 2Cor 8:9 (Christ's voluntary becoming-poor — ptōcheuō, a deliberate act). The entire M46-D release pattern depends on the volitional faculty choosing to give or refuse in response to a higher valuation.
- *T3.6.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with …_
  → M46-C and M46-D engage and exercise volition: the wisdom tradition commands specific volitional acts (stop wealth-seeking, Pro 23:4); M46-D evidences the highest exercises of volition in the cluster (deliberate release, deliberate refusal, deliberate choice of the reproach of Christ over Egypt's treasure).
- *T3.7.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?_
  → M46-D has the cluster's most deliberate agency: Abram refusing enrichment (Gen 14:23), Zacchaeus distributing half his goods (Luk 19:8), Moses choosing reproach over treasure (Heb 11:26), the widow giving all her living (Luk 21:3-4). The voluntary release pattern requires and exercises maximal agency — deliberate, costly, irreversible acts.
- *T3.7.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?_
  → The ordering of character and wealth and the inner life beyond material circumstance require and strengthen agency: diligence is sustained agency; voluntary release is decisive agency. These sub-groups are the agency-richest territory in the cluster.
- *T3.8.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — a…_
  → Moses's revaluation (Heb 11:26) is a moral evaluation act: he assessed the reproach of Christ as morally superior in value to Egypt's treasures. Abram's refusal (Gen 14:23) is grounded in a moral evaluation: he would not allow the king to claim moral credit for his wealth. Both are explicit moral evaluation acts applied to the wealth domain.
- *T3.8.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?_
  → Enables and sharpens: M46-C's wisdom tradition deepens the capacity for moral evaluation by establishing that inner character (not material condition) is the right criterion. M46-D's revaluation (Moses, Abram) exercises the moral evaluation faculty at its highest: comparing temporal and eternal values across registers.
- *T3.9.1* — _Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?_
  → M46-D implicitly engages conscience through the voluntary release: the act of giving to the poor (Luk 14:13; Luk 18:22) and Zacchaeus's restitution (Luk 19:8) suggest conscience's positive role — the awareness of obligation toward the poor and the duty to restore what was wrongly taken. The spontaneous restitution of Zacchaeus implies conscience's positive operation.
- *T3.9.2* — _Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?_
  → M46-D's voluntary release appears to operate through and deepen conscience: the awareness that one should give, should release, should restore — these are conscience-operations that M46-D's acts respond to.
