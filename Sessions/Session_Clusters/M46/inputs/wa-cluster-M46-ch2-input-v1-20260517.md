> The inner-being condition of the person whose abundance has produced two related failures of the inner life — hardness toward others and insatiability within. Hardness: the capacity for pity (cha.mal) erodes; the wealthy person answers roughly, ignores the suffering neighbour, honors the powerful and dishonors the poor, acquires through violence. Insatiability: the eyes are never satisfied with riches; wealth possessed cannot be enjoyed; what was accumulated rots; the person toils endlessly but cannot ask for whom. Both express the same inner contraction — unable to give to others, unable to receive from what they have.

> The inner-being observation of the wisdom tradition: character determines the quality and trajectory of material life. The soul of the diligent is richly supplied — inner character produces inner richness that includes but exceeds material wealth. The house of the righteous has genuine treasure; the income of the wicked brings trouble. Wisdom carries riches and righteousness together. The fear of the Lord is Zion's true treasure. And God governs the material domain entirely — he makes poor and makes rich. The inner person who knows this does not grasp for material goods but receives them as fruit and gift of right inner orientation.

**Sub-group description (from analytical record):**

> The inner-being characteristics that emerge when the person is no longer governed by the wealth domain — three related but distinct postures. First, poverty of spirit: the inner condition of dependence and receptivity before God, the person who has nothing to offer and is therefore constitutively open to divine gift; God has chosen the ptōchos of this world to be rich in faith. Second, the inner act of release: the deliberate choice to give, relinquish, or refuse material wealth in response to a higher valuation — Moses counting the reproach of Christ as greater wealth than Egypt's treasure; Abram refusing enrichment to preserve his witness; the widow giving all her living; Christ becoming poor so that others might become rich. Third, learned contentment: the inner stability that Paul names as acquired through living in both abundance and scarcity — an achieved inner independence from material circumstance that neither poverty destroys nor abundance corrupts.
