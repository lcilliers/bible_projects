## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The M03 corpus reveals a God who himself grieves, is moved by grief, records grief, and responds to it. God hears Israel's groaning under slavery and remembers his covenant (Exo 2:24, Exo 6:5 — M03-E-VCG-01). God sees Hezekiah's tears and responds with healing (2Ki 20:5 — M03-A-VCG-02). God collects the psalmist's tears in a bottle and records them in a book (Psa 56:8 — M03-A-VCG-02). In all their distress God himself was afflicted (Isa 63:9 — M03-D-VCG-03). In the New Testament, the Spirit's grief at sin (Eph 4:30 — M03-C-VCG-02) and Jesus's Gethsemane soul-sorrow (Mat 26:37-38, Mar 14:34 — M03-C-VCG-03) reveal that grief is not peripheral to divine nature but deeply embedded in it. The grief M03 describes in the human person therefore images a God who is moved by loss, responsive to suffering, and capable of entering into and participating in the grief of his creatures.

**[A, B, C, D, E, F, G]**
E — The same pattern is distributed across sub-groups. In M03-A, God hears the sound of weeping (Psa 6:8) and commands its cessation as promise (Isa 30:19; Jer 31:16). In M03-B, God governs mourning — commanding it, withholding it, and reversing it (M03-B-VCG-03). In M03-C, godly grief produces repentance (2Cor 7:9, 7:11) — a pattern that mirrors God's own grief over sin (Eph 4:30). In M03-D, the psalmist's cry from distress is met by God's answering voice from his temple (Psa 18:6), showing God as attentive to grief-distress. Across sub-groups, the characteristic is consistently shown as something God observes, responds to, participates in, and governs.

---

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Scripture does explicitly attribute grief to God. Three evidences stand out across the corpus. First, the Spirit's grief at sin is named directly: "grieve not the Holy Spirit" (Eph 4:30 — M03-C-VCG-02); the Spirit is personally affected by the community's choices, showing grief as an appropriate responsive inner-being state in the Spirit's person. Second, Jesus's Gethsemane grief is soul-located at maximum depth ("very sorrowful, even to death" — Mat 26:38, Mar 14:34 — M03-C-VCG-03); this is the characteristic in Jesus's own inner being as he faces the cross. Third, God's solidarity in distress: "in all their distress he was afflicted" (Isa 63:9 — M03-D-VCG-03). The divine attribution reveals that grief, sorrow, and anguish are not constitutional weaknesses to be overcome but appropriate relational responses to loss, sin, and suffering — which is why the human capacity for grief is not a mark of the fall alone but reflects the responsive relational nature God himself possesses.

**[C-VCG-02, C-VCG-03]**
E — The most explicit divine attributions are concentrated in M03-C-VCG-02 (Eph 4:30 — Spirit's grief) and M03-C-VCG-03 (Mat 26:37-38, Mar 14:34 — Jesus's grief in Gethsemane). These two VCGs carry the most theologically dense evidence for grief as a divine characteristic.

---

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[CLUSTER]**
S — The evidence is not silent; the corpus shows grief attributed to God in three distinct ways (Spirit's grief, Jesus's grief, divine solidarity in distress). However, some sub-groups within M03 contain no explicit divine attribution: M03-A (weeping and tears) does not attribute weeping to God directly, though God commands and promises to wipe away tears (Isa 25:8). M03-F (pain and inner ache) does not attribute bodily pain or ache to God. M03-G (bitterness) does not attribute bitterness to God. The absence of divine weeping (M03-A) and divine bodily pain (M03-F) may signal that the somatic-expressive registers of grief — tears, groaning, physical ache — are specifically creaturely modes of a grief that in God is real but expressed without the bodily limitations of mortal flesh.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The verse evidence across M03 indicates that grief equips the person for four primary purposes. First, grief drives prayer: the dominant pattern of M03-D-VCG-01 (tsar/tsa.rah corpus — 56 verses) is distress → cry to God → divine answer; grief is the engine of Godward petition. Second, grief enables moral responsiveness: godly grief produces repentance, earnestness, zeal, and moral reformation (2Cor 7:9-11 — M03-C-VCG-02; Eze 9:4 — M03-E-VCG-01). Third, grief enables empathic solidarity: weeping with those who weep is commanded as an act of will and love (Rom 12:15 — M03-A-VCG-05); Job's soul wept for the needy (Job 30:25 — M03-A-VCG-04). Fourth, grief orients the inner person toward eschatological hope: sighing as the mark of exile is paired with its future displacement by everlasting joy (Isa 35:10, 51:11 — M03-E-VCG-03), and the new creation's abolition of mourning (Rev 21:4 — M03-B-VCG-04) reveals that present grief is the condition of a not-yet-redeemed world, fitting for now, but not for the fullness.

**[A]**
E — Weeping equips the person for empathic solidarity (Rom 12:15), for intercessory prayer (1Sa 1:10; Joe 2:17), and for penitential honesty before God (2Ki 22:19; Ezr 10:1). In M03-A-VCG-02, tears function as the evidence that drives God to respond, making weeping the somatic form of genuine inner petition.

**[B]**
E — Mourning equips the person for socially structured grief expression (M03-B-VCG-01), for communal solidarity under shared catastrophe (M03-B-VCG-02), and for the orientation of the whole inner person toward God in repentance (Jam 4:9 — M03-B-VCG-04). The wise heart chooses the house of mourning (Ecc 7:2, 7:4) because mourning confronts the person with mortality and truth.

**[D]**
E — Distress equips the person for prayer and for covenantal discipline. M03-D-VCG-01 (56 verses) repeatedly names distress as the state that activates the divine-human exchange: "call upon me in the day of trouble" (Psa 50:15). M03-D-VCG-03 shows distress turning the inner will back toward God (Deu 4:30; 2Ch 15:4; Hos 5:15).

---

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The corpus gives evidence for both. The BOUNDARY sub-group includes the Genesis 3 toil-and-pain vocabulary (e.tsev H6089A at Gen 3:16; its.tsa.von H6093 at Gen 3:16, 3:17, 5:29 — M03-BOUNDARY-VCG-01), explicitly tying a dimension of the M03 register to the fall's consequences. The che.vel-B birth-pangs metaphor (Isa 13:8; Jer 13:21 etc. — M03-F-VCG-03) is embedded in the fall-curse frame of Gen 3. This argues that the acute pain and toil registers of M03 are at least partly responsive to the fallen condition. However, the core grief registers — mourning for the dead, distress that drives prayer, empathic weeping — are presented in the corpus without shame or judgment: Job's friends weep at his suffering (Job 2:12 — M03-A-VCG-01); Jesus is perilupos in Gethsemane (Mat 26:38 — M03-C-VCG-03); God himself grieves. The evidence suggests that the relational and responsive core of grief — the inner-being capacity to feel the weight of loss and to be moved by it — is part of the created design, while certain intensive and chronic expressions (groaning under slavery, the grinding toil of cursed existence) are responses to the fallen condition.

**[A-VCG-02, B-VCG-03, B-VCG-04, C-VCG-03, E-VCG-02]**
E — Penitential weeping (M03-A-VCG-02), God-governed mourning transformation (M03-B-VCG-03), NT mourning commanded as fitting inner response to sin (M03-B-VCG-04), Jesus's Gethsemane sorrow (M03-C-VCG-03), and the Spirit/creation groaning of Rom 8 (M03-E-VCG-02) together show grief operating within the fallen condition as a theologically proper and divinely honoured mode of the inner person. Grief is not the fall's product alone but the fitting response to living in a world where loss is real.

---

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes, and this is one of the most consistently signalled patterns across the corpus. The eschatological trajectory is explicit and recurring. Sighing flees as everlasting joy arrives (Isa 35:10, 51:11 — M03-E-VCG-03). God will wipe every tear from every face (Isa 25:8 — M03-A-VCG-02). Weeping will be silenced in the restored Jerusalem (Isa 65:19 — M03-A-VCG-05). Mourning shall be no more in the new creation (Rev 21:4 — M03-B-VCG-04). Pain shall be no more (Rev 21:4 — BOUNDARY). Creation's groan is oriented toward future redemption, not hopeless (Rom 8:22-26 — M03-E-VCG-02). The present experience of grief is consistently framed as the mark of the not-yet — the characteristic condition of those who live in exile, in fallenness, in the not-yet-redeemed order, whose grief is real and honoured but whose future is joy. In this sense, grief functions as an eschatological marker: its presence names where the person is; its future absence names where they are going.

**[A-VCG-05, B-VCG-04, E-VCG-02]**
E — These three VCGs carry the most concentrated eschatological orientation: judgment-weeping and end-of-tears promise (M03-A-VCG-05), NT mourning's abolition in the new creation (M03-B-VCG-04), and Spirit/creation groaning toward redemption (M03-E-VCG-02).

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The divine image expressed through M03 is primarily the image of a relational God who responds to loss with internal seriousness — a God who is moved by love for what he has bonded with, and whose inner being is affected when that bond is threatened or broken. Grief in the human person instantiates this relational responsiveness: it shows that the human person, like God, has genuine bonds of love that matter to the inner being, and that when those bonds are severed, the inner person is genuinely affected. The weeping of David for Absalom (2Sa 18:33 — M03-A-VCG-01), the inconsolable grief of Rachel (Jer 31:15 — M03-A-VCG-01), and the soul-melting grief of Psa 119:28 (tu.gah — M03-C-VCG-01) all display an inner seriousness about what is loved and lost that mirrors the seriousness with which God holds his bonds of love. The capacity to grieve is the inner evidence that one is a being who loves.

---

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The evidence indicates genuine sharing rather than mere analogy. The Spirit grieves (Eph 4:30 — M03-C-VCG-02); Jesus's soul is perilupos in Gethsemane (Mat 26:38 — M03-C-VCG-03); God was afflicted in all Israel's distress (Isa 63:9 — M03-D-VCG-03). These are not merely analogical descriptions; they name grief as a real inner-being state of the Spirit and of Jesus-as-human-person. The evidence suggests that grief is genuinely shared between God and the human person at the level of the characteristic's relational core (being moved by loss, responding to the suffering of the beloved). What differs is the mode: divine grief does not involve bodily exhaustion (groaning, physical ache, bodily deterioration from weeping), which are creaturely somatic expressions of a grief that in God is real but expressed without creaturely limitation.

---

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The corpus contains striking evidence on this point. The absence of grief where grief is morally appropriate is named as a mark of moral failure or hardened inner state. The Corinthians' arrogance where mourning was appropriate shows a hardened or indifferent inner state (1Cor 5:2 — M03-B-VCG-04). The self-indulgent are condemned for not being grieved over Joseph's ruin, marking their moral failure (Amo 6:6 — M03-BOUNDARY). Babylon's boast that she will never see mourning marks her pride and spiritual self-deception (Rev 18:7 — M03-B-VCG-04). Conversely, Josiah's tender, humbled heart weeping before God moves God to hear him (2Ki 22:19 — M03-A-VCG-02). The presence of grief, particularly grief over sin and loss, signals a relational inner life that is functioning as the image demands; its absence in contexts that call for it signals a hardening or deadening of the image's relational responsiveness.

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — The most concentrated evidence for grief as image-indicator is M03-C-VCG-02 (godly grief producing repentance): 2Cor 7:9-11 shows grief producing the full range of morally restorative inner states. The one who is genuinely grieved by sin is demonstrating an inner life properly calibrated to the moral and relational seriousness that the image requires.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes. The corpus contains several instances of grief functioning typologically. The most significant is Jesus's Gethsemane grief (Mat 26:37-38, Mar 14:34 — M03-C-VCG-03), which is the human person's grief at its absolute depth and which participates in the redemptive act. The mourning at beholding the pierced one (Zec 12:10-12 — M03-B-VCG-04) is eschatological and christological — the grief of recognition when Israel sees the one they pierced, pointing toward the cross. Rachel's weeping (Jer 31:15 — cited in Mat 2:18 — M03-A-VCG-05) functions typologically in Matthew: the grief of Israelite mothers over Herod's slaughter participates in the pattern of lamentation over the loss of Israel's children that reaches back to Rachel and forward to the cross. The Spirit/creation groaning of Rom 8:22-26 (M03-E-VCG-02) is eschatological — the groan of the not-yet-redeemed creation participating in the birth-pangs of the new age.

**[C-VCG-03, B-VCG-04]**
E — M03-C-VCG-03 (Gethsemane grief) and M03-B-VCG-04 (Zec 12:10, Rev 1:7 — mourning for the pierced one) are the primary sites of christological typology within the cluster.

---

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Both directions are present. The divine instance establishes the pattern for the human: Jesus's soul-sorrow in Gethsemane (M03-C-VCG-03) does not merely illustrate human grief — it carries the grief of fallen humanity into the redemptive act, establishing the depth of grief as the pathway through which salvation comes. The human instance also points toward the divine: Rachel's weeping (Jer 31:15) points forward to the fulfilment of grief in the Matthean context (Mat 2:18 — M03-A-VCG-05); the OT mourning for the dead and for national catastrophe points forward to the eschatological mourning when the tribes see the pierced one (Zec 12:10 — M03-B-VCG-04).

---

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[A-VCG-01, A-VCG-03, D-VCG-01, F-VCG-02, G-VCG-01]**
S — The bereavement weeping of M03-A-VCG-01, the communal national grief of M03-A-VCG-03, the psalmic distress-prayer pattern of M03-D-VCG-01, the unceasing inner pain of M03-F-VCG-02, and the bitterness vocabulary of M03-G-VCG-01 are not evidenced as carrying typological function. They operate as direct descriptions of the characteristic without explicit typological deployment.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The programme names this cluster Grief, Sorrow and Mourning. The name signals three distinct but overlapping realities. Grief names the inner state itself — the inner-being weight of loss and its effect on the person. Sorrow names the settled condition of one who carries that weight — grief as a sustained inner disposition rather than merely an acute event. Mourning names the structured outward form grief takes — the act, the ritual, the communal practice through which inner grief is given social shape. The conjunction of all three in the name signals that the cluster encompasses not just one of these but the full arc from inner state through to outward expression and social form.

---

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The Hebrew vocabulary reveals two primary registers. The weeping and tears register (ba.khah, be.khi, dim.ah — M03-A) names the somatic-vocal expression of grief: the act of weeping, its sound, its physical substance. The mourning register (sa.phad, mis.ped, a.val, e.vel, a.vel — M03-B) names the structured, socially recognised act of lamentation, often with embodied forms (tearing garments, sackcloth, fasting). The sorrow register (ya.gon, tu.gah, makh.ov — M03-C, F) names the inner phenomenology — grief as a weight carried in the heart, the soul's experienced suffering. The anguish/distress register (tsar, tsa.rah, me.tsu.qah — M03-D) names grief at its most acute and pressing, the crushing experience of overwhelming adversity. The groaning register (a.nach, a.na.chah, ne.a.qah — M03-E) names grief's involuntary vocal-somatic expression. The Greek vocabulary maps broadly: lupeō (to grieve), perilupos (deeply sorrowful), penthos/pen.the.o (mourning), klauthmos/klaiō (weeping/to weep), odunaō/odunē (anguish), sunochē (anguish seated in the heart), stenagmos/sustenazō (groaning). The Greek vocabulary is less formally structured around mourning rites and more focused on the inner state and its expression — consistent with NT's inward focus.

---

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes on all three counts. Directional: the mourning vocabulary consistently shows grief oriented upward toward God (distress driving prayer — M03-D-VCG-01) or toward another person in empathic solidarity (Job's soul weeping for the needy — M03-A-VCG-04; Rom 12:15 — M03-A-VCG-05). Relational: grief is almost always relational in the corpus — it arises from the loss of or damage to a relational bond (bereavement weeping, M03-A-VCG-01; anguish at relational loss, Act 20:38 — M03-D-VCG-05). Constitutional: grief consistently involves the soul and heart as its location (Jesus's soul sorrowful to death, Mat 26:38; sorrow lodged in the heart, Psa 13:2; anguish of my spirit, Job 7:11), signalling that it is a deep inner-being state rather than a surface emotional response.

---

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03's characteristic is primarily a condition — a state that the inner person is in rather than an act it performs. Grief settles into the heart (Psa 13:2 — M03-C-VCG-01), makes the soul melt (Psa 119:28 — M03-C-VCG-01), presses in as anguish (tsa.rah corpus — M03-D-VCG-01), and drains life (Psa 31:10 — M03-C-VCG-01). However, several sub-groups name grief as also an act: the act of weeping (ba.khah — M03-A), the act of mourning (sa.phad, a.val — M03-B), the act of groaning (a.nach — M03-E). The cluster therefore spans three kinds: condition (settled inner state of grief), quality (the phenomenological texture of sorrow — bitter, heavy, melting), and act (weeping, mourning, groaning as outward expressions of the inner condition). The condition is primary; the acts are the condition overflowing outward.

**[A]**
E — M03-A (weeping/tears) is primarily an act — the act of weeping — that arises from and expresses the inner condition of grief.

**[B]**
E — M03-B (mourning/lamentation) is primarily a socially structured act of mourning that externalizes the inner condition.

**[C, D, G]**
E — M03-C (sorrow/inner grief), M03-D (anguish/distress), and M03-G (bitterness) are primarily conditions — the characteristic as a settled inner state or quality of the person.

**[E, F]**
E — M03-E (groaning/sighing) is primarily an act (groaning, sighing) arising from the inner condition. M03-F (pain/inner ache) is primarily a condition — the phenomenological quality of grief as pain experienced in the body and inner being.

---

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03 is not simple. The corpus reveals several distinct constituent elements that together constitute the fuller phenomenon of grief, sorrow and mourning: (1) an inner state of sorrow/grief in the soul and heart (M03-C); (2) a pressing quality of anguish or distress (M03-D); (3) a somatic-expressive component — tears, weeping, groaning, sighing (M03-A, M03-E); (4) a structured social-outward form — mourning rites and practices (M03-B); (5) a qualitative dimension of bitterness — grief at its sharpest (M03-G); (6) a phenomenological pain dimension — what grief feels like from within (M03-F). These six constituent elements are distinct enough to form seven sub-groups but interrelated enough to form one cluster: they all name the inner-being reality of what it is to be a creature who has lost or is losing something that mattered.

---

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief, sorrow and mourning is the inner-being condition and its expressions arising from the experience of loss, separation, suffering, or devastation. It is constitutionally located in the soul and heart, where it is felt as a weight, a wound, a pressure, or a dissolution. It overflows outward into the body through tears, groaning, sighing, and the physical acts of mourning. It takes structured social form in mourning rites, communal lamentation, and prescribed grief practices. At its most acute it becomes anguish — an overwhelming pressing distress from which the inner person cries out to God. At its sharpest quality it becomes bitterness — grief that has acquired the taste of something harsh and without sweetness. At its most generative it produces repentance, prayer, and moral reformation. At its eschatological horizon it is the mark of the not-yet — the condition of those living between the fall and the fullness, whose grief is real and honoured but whose future is joy.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The corpus names multiple structural opposites of grief, each sub-group pointing toward its own specific opposite. Weeping's opposite is joy/rejoicing (Psa 30:5 — "weeping may endure for a night but joy comes in the morning" — M03-A-VCG-04). Mourning's opposite is dancing/gladness (Psa 30:11 — "you turned my mourning into dancing" — M03-B-VCG-03). Sorrow's opposite is joy — sorrow flees as everlasting joy arrives (Isa 35:10, 51:11 — M03-C-VCG-01). Distress's opposite is deliverance/rest — God delivers from all distress (Psa 34:17 — M03-D-VCG-01). Sighing's opposite is everlasting joy (Isa 35:10 — M03-E-VCG-03). Bitterness's opposite is sweetness — the scroll sweet in the mouth becomes bitter in the stomach (Rev 10:9-10 — M03-G-VCG-01). The cluster's master opposite is joy (M04), which is consistently named as what God gives in exchange for grief, mourning, and sorrow across the corpus.

---

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief resists and excludes: comfort (Rachel refusing comfort, Jer 31:15 — M03-A-VCG-01; Jerusalem's lovers offering no comfort, Lam 1:2 — M03-A-VCG-01); rest (sighing has replaced bread, Job 3:24 — M03-E-VCG-03; grief preventing rest, Jer 45:3 — M03-E-VCG-03); rejoicing (sorrow coexisting with joy but not yielding to it in C-VCG-01; godly grief displacing levity in M03-B-VCG-04; mourning commanded as the appropriate inner state displacing laughter, Jam 4:9); and eating/normal bodily function (Hannah's grief so consuming she cannot eat, 1Sa 1:7-8 — M03-A-VCG-01; sighing replacing bread, Job 3:24). Grief also resists the superficiality that refuses to mourn when mourning is appropriate — the Corinthians' arrogance where mourning was fitting is named as a deficiency (1Cor 5:2 — M03-B-VCG-04).

---

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Several distinctions emerge from the corpus and from the programme's earlier decisions. Grief is not fear (M01 — terror/dread), though distress and anguish can co-occur with fear; the grief register is characteristically oriented toward loss and relationship, while fear is characteristically oriented toward threat and danger. Grief is not anger (M02) — the bitterness vocabulary was split at Phase 3, with anger-bitterness (pikria, pikros) transferring to M02; M03's bitterness is grief-bitterness arising from suffering and loss, not anger-bitterness arising from hostile disposition. Grief is not guilt (M10) — though godly grief produces repentance and conscience-driven grief is evidenced (Eze 9:4; Joh 21:17 — M03-C-VCG-02), the cluster's characteristic is the felt weight of loss and suffering, not the moral assessment of fault. Grief is not despair (M20-Doubt) — though some grief verses touch despair (Psa 88:3 — BOUNDARY), the distinctive of M03 is movement and expression, while despair is characterised by paralysis and closure. Grief is not weakness (M24) — though the two overlap in the BOUNDARY terms, M03 grief is constitutively relational (it is grief over something or someone), while M24 weakness names the constitutional vulnerability and depletion of the person.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The corpus reveals five distinct modes. (1) Receptive-passive mode: grief arrives as an overwhelming inner condition from outside — anguish seizes the person (Jer 49:24 — M03-D-VCG-04), mourning falls upon the city (Isa 29:2 — M03-B-VCG-03), distress finds the psalmist (Psa 119:143 — M03-D-VCG-01). (2) Expressive-outward mode: grief overflows from the inner being into tears (M03-A), groaning (M03-E), and structured mourning acts (M03-B). (3) Generative mode: grief produces inner-being change — repentance (2Cor 7:9 — M03-C-VCG-02), prayer (Psa 18:6 — M03-D-VCG-01), zeal (2Cor 7:11), and moral reformation. (4) Sustained-condition mode: grief settles as a persistent inner weight that drains vitality over time — sorrow spending the psalmist's life (Psa 31:10 — M03-C-VCG-01); pain as a constant inward presence (Psa 38:17 — M03-F-VCG-01). (5) Social-ritual mode: grief takes organised communal form — mourning rites (M03-B-VCG-01), communal lamentation (M03-B-VCG-02), the house of mourning as a shared social space (Jer 16:5 — M03-B-VCG-03).

---

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes.
