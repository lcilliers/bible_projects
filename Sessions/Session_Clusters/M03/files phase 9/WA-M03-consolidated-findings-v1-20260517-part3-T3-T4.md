## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes. Several perceptive faculties are engaged. Sight: Elisha's weeping follows a fixed, penetrating gaze — the prophetic vision of coming catastrophe produces grief (2Ki 8:11 — M03-A-VCG-04). Jesus's grief in Mar 3:5 follows perceiving the Pharisees' hardness of heart — grief arising from spiritual discernment of moral stubbornness. Conscience as inner moral perception: God marks those whose sighing evidences genuine inward grief over witnessed wickedness (Eze 9:4 — M03-E-VCG-01) — conscience-driven perception of sin producing grief. Taste: bitterness in M03-G is explicitly a taste metaphor — the scroll bitter in the stomach (Rev 10:9-10 — M03-G-VCG-01), life made bitter (Gen 26:35 — M03-G-VCG-01). Hearing: God hears the sound of weeping (Psa 6:8 — M03-A-VCG-04); groaning is heard by others (Lam 1:21 — M03-E-VCG-01).

---

**T3.1.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief deepens moral perception. The conscience-driven sighing of Eze 9:4 (M03-E-VCG-01) shows grief as the sign of active inner perception — those who sigh and groan over wickedness are those whose moral perceptive faculty is functioning correctly. Prophetic grief arises from prophetic perception (Elisha weeping after foreknowledge — 2Ki 8:11 — M03-A-VCG-04; Jer 9:1 — M03-A-VCG-04). In this sense, grief can be an indicator that perception is functioning: the one who perceives the weight of sin, loss, and catastrophe genuinely will grieve. Grief does not bypass or impair perception; it is often the evidence that perception is operating.

---

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief is a perception-responsive characteristic: it arises when the inner person perceives something that matters being lost, harmed, or violated. The perceptive faculty is the trigger that activates grief — the person must perceive the loss or the sin before grief can arise. This means grief is not arbitrary or groundless; it is the appropriate inner-being response to perceived reality. The characteristic's perception-dependence also means that those whose perceptive faculty is dulled (by pride, self-deception, or moral hardening) will not grieve when grief is appropriate — confirming the T1.7.2 finding about inner conditions blocking reception.

---

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief engages cognition in two ways. First, cognitive recognition produces grief: the disciples' grief at Jesus's betrayal announcement (Mat 26:22 — M03-C-VCG-02) is triggered by understanding the implication of the announcement; Peter's grief at the third question (Joh 21:17 — M03-C-VCG-01) arises from cognitive recognition of his own past failure. Second, grief impairs cognition: the prophet's grief so total that the prophetic perception precedes cognitive processing (Elisha weeping before Hazael asks why — 2Ki 8:11-12 — M03-A-VCG-04); pain fills all days and prevents the heart from finding rest (Ecc 2:23 — M03-F-VCG-01), interrupting normal cognitive function.

---

**T3.2.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**[C-VCG-02, F-VCG-01]**
E — Godly grief deepens cognition: the Corinthians' grief produces a change of mind (metanoia — 2Cor 7:9, 7:11 — M03-C-VCG-02), showing grief enabling cognitive reformation. The cost of wisdom producing pain (Ecc 1:18 — M03-F-VCG-01) suggests that deeper cognitive engagement with reality produces grief — a bidirectional relationship. Sustained grief can impair cognition: pain preventing rest (Ecc 2:23 — M03-F-VCG-01), grief draining all vitality (Psa 31:10 — M03-C-VCG-01).

---

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief is not primarily a cognitive characteristic but it has cognitive consequences in both directions: accurate cognitive perception of reality (especially moral and relational reality) tends to produce grief; and grief tends to produce cognitive reformation (repentance as cognitive-moral revision). The relationship is bidirectional and significant, but the primary seat of grief remains affective, not cognitive.

---

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes, prominently. Memory is a recurring trigger for grief in M03-A. The exiles' communal grief sitting by Babylon's waters is triggered by remembering Zion (Psa 137:1 — M03-A-VCG-03): grief arises when the lost past is brought into present awareness through memory. Old men weeping when the new temple foundation is laid, because they remember the former one (Ezr 3:12 — M03-A-VCG-01): grief triggered by memory of what has been lost. Rachel's inconsolable weeping (Jer 31:15 — M03-A-VCG-01) arises from the knowledge of irreversible loss — memory of what was but is no longer. In M03-BOUNDARY, Joseph's naming his son Manasseh because God made him forget his hardship (Gen 41:51) shows grief diminishing when the painful memory is released.

---

**T3.3.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Memory deepens grief: the exile's grief is sustained by and deepened through the act of remembering (Psa 137:1). Grief also deepens memory's hold: the psalmist asks God to record tears in a bottle (Psa 56:8 — M03-A-VCG-02), presenting grief as something worthy of divine memory — implying that grief events constitute the memorial record of what was loved. Sustained grief can impair the capacity to move beyond the past: Rachel refusing all comfort (Jer 31:15 — M03-A-VCG-01) shows grief locking memory into an inconsolable present-of-the-past. Conversely, God's eschatological promise is that the former distresses will be completely forgotten (Isa 65:16 — M03-D-VCG-04), showing that eschatological restoration involves the transformation of grief-laden memory.

---

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief is memory-sensitive: it is frequently triggered, sustained, and deepened by memory. The characteristic is oriented toward the past (what has been lost) as much as toward the present situation of loss. This gives grief a temporal depth that other characteristics lack — it reaches backward into what was, and its resolution requires either the transformation of the memory's weight or the eschatological forgetting that God promises. The memory-grief relationship also illuminates why grief persists: as long as memory holds what was lost, grief can be renewed.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03 is by far the most affectively concentrated cluster in the programme. Every sub-group is primarily affective. The ya.gon and tu.gah corpus (M03-C-VCG-01) names grief as a settled affective condition of the heart. The weeping corpus (M03-A) names the somatic overflow of intense feeling. The distress corpus (M03-D) names the affective quality of being overwhelmed and pressed. The groaning/sighing corpus (M03-E) names the vocal-somatic expression of deep feeling. The bitterness corpus (M03-G) names the qualitative texture of felt inner experience. This is the cluster whose primary mode of inner-being reality is affective.

---

**T3.4.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief is itself a form of affect; it does not operate on affect from outside but is an affective state. However, the relationship within the affective domain is complex. Grief deepens affect by making the person's inner life more responsive and serious: the wise heart in the house of mourning (Ecc 7:2, 7:4 — M03-B-VCG-01) shows that grief deepens the affective-moral life. Grief also impairs other affects: grief displacing laughter (Jam 4:9 — M03-B-VCG-04), sorrow displacing dance (Lam 5:15 — M03-B-VCG-02), grief coloring all musical expression (Job 30:31 — M03-B-VCG-01). Sustained grief can saturate the affective life to the point of depletion (Psa 31:10; 1Sa 30:4 — M03-A-VCG-01).

---

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03 is constitutively affective — it is not a characteristic that engages the affective faculty from outside but is the affective faculty's primary content when the person is in the condition of grief. The characteristic reveals that the affective life of the human person is designed for seriousness and depth of response to real loss — the capacity for profound grief is the capacity for profound love expressed in the dimension of loss.

---

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1** — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty?

**[A-VCG-04, E-VCG-01]**
E — Grief engages the creative faculty in two sub-groups. In M03-A-VCG-04, the prophet's grief produces the lament form — a creative-literary expression of inner grief (Jer 9:1; Isa 16:9). In M03-E-VCG-01, the embodied sign of the prophet's groan (Eze 21:6) is a prophetic creative act produced by grief. The Lamentations corpus (M03-A-VCG-04) is perhaps the richest evidence of grief generating literary creativity — grief producing the most sustained poetic lament in Scripture.

---

**T3.5.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?

**[A-VCG-04, B-VCG-01]**
E — Grief can enable and channel creativity into lament expression — the prophet's weeping producing the lament poems (Jer 9:1 — M03-A-VCG-04) and the prophetic sign-act (Eze 21:6 — M03-E-VCG-01). However, grief can also impair ordinary creative expression: mourning so total it transforms music (Job 30:31 — M03-B-VCG-01) — grief colonising creative capacity rather than generating new forms.

---

**T3.5.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with creativity reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief generates a specific and distinctive creative form — lament — which is a creative expression of inner suffering rather than inner joy. The Lamentations and prophetic lament tradition shows grief as one of the most generative sources of Hebrew literary creativity. Grief does not bypass or impair the creative faculty but redirects it toward a specific register.

---

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes, and this is one of M03's most revealing patterns. Grief engages volition in multiple distinct ways.

**[C-VCG-01]**
E — Grief can fail to govern the will: Herod's grief is genuine but overcomes neither his oath nor his reputation (Mar 6:26 — M03-C-VCG-01). The rich young man departs sorrowful but his grief does not determine his choice (Mat 19:22; Mar 10:22 — M03-C-VCG-01). Grief is real but weak — a feeling that does not reshape the will's decision.

**[C-VCG-02, D-VCG-01, D-VCG-03]**
E — Grief can reshape the will: godly grief produces earnestness, indignation, fear, and zeal (2Cor 7:11 — M03-C-VCG-02). Distress as covenantal discipline turns the inner will back toward God (Deu 4:30; 2Ch 15:4 — M03-D-VCG-03). The psalmic distress drives prayer — the will directed toward God in petition (Psa 18:6 — M03-D-VCG-01).

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Mourning can be willed: mourning is commanded — laughter must yield to mourning (Jam 4:9 — M03-B-VCG-04). This shows that in certain registers, mourning is the result of a volitional act: the person chooses to let their inner state be calibrated toward mourning rather than levity.

---

**T3.6.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Four patterns: (1) grief bypassing the will (overwhelming grief where the will is disabled — Zion's hands fall helpless under distress, Jer 4:31 — BOUNDARY); (2) grief failing to govern the will (Herod, the rich young man — M03-C-VCG-01); (3) grief reshaping the will through repentance (godly grief, M03-C-VCG-02); (4) grief activating the will toward God in prayer (M03-D-VCG-01, D-VCG-03). The characteristic's relationship with volition is therefore not simple: grief can strengthen or weaken the will depending on whether it is godly or worldly, acute or chronic, received or resisted.

---

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with volition reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The volition-grief relationship is one of the cluster's most analytically significant findings. Grief does not automatically determine the will. Worldly grief (grief over loss of wealth or status) tends to fail to govern the will productively; godly grief (grief over sin and relational rupture) tends to reshape the will toward repentance and moral reformation. The decisive factor appears to be the object of the grief and the spiritual orientation of the person receiving it — not the intensity of the grief itself.

---

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes. Grief activates agency in the prayer-deliverance pattern (M03-D-VCG-01): distress drives the person to act — to cry out, to seek God, to make vows. Grief also activates agency in the mourning-and-action pattern: the morally motivated distress of servants leads directly to reporting injustice (Mat 18:31 — M03-C-VCG-02). Nehemiah's grief immediately drives him into sustained prayer and fasting (Neh 1:4 — M03-B-VCG-02).

---

**T3.7.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief can impair agency: anguish seizing the person so that hands fall helpless (Jer 4:31 — BOUNDARY; Jer 50:43 — M03-D-VCG-04). Overwhelming distress disabling the will to act (1Sa 13:6 — M03-D-VCG-04). Prolonged parental grief threatening to immobilise the prophet (1Sa 16:1 — M03-B-VCG-02). Grief can enable agency: Esther's grief-driven intercession (Est 8:3 — M03-A-VCG-02); Hannah's bitter weeping driving prayer (1Sa 1:10 — M03-A-VCG-02). The enabling pattern appears when grief is directed outward toward God or toward redemptive action; the impairing pattern appears when grief is overwhelming or misdirected.

---

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with agency reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief's relationship with agency mirrors its relationship with volition. The characteristic is not inherently enabling or impairing of agency — the outcome depends on the grief's orientation. Grief directed toward God in prayer activates agency; grief that overwhelms and paralyses disables it. The evidence suggests that grief functioning within the Godward prayer pattern (M03-D-VCG-01) is the context in which grief most consistently enables rather than impairs agency.

---

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes. Conscience-driven sighing (Eze 9:4 — M03-E-VCG-01) is the paradigmatic instance: grief arising from the moral evaluation of witnessed wickedness. Jesus's grief at the Pharisees' hardness of heart (Mar 3:5 — M03-C-VCG-02) is compassionate moral grief — grief arising from perceiving and evaluating the moral failure of others. Peter's grief (Joh 21:17 — M03-C-VCG-01) arises from conscience's moral evaluation of his own past denial. The entire godly grief passage (2Cor 7:9-11 — M03-C-VCG-02) shows grief as a morally evaluative response that leads to reformation.

---

**T3.8.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?

**[C-VCG-02, E-VCG-01]**
E — Grief deepens moral evaluation: godly grief produces "indignation" as one of its moral fruits (2Cor 7:11 — M03-C-VCG-02) — a sharpened moral sensitivity toward the wrong that produced the grief. Conscience-driven sighing (Eze 9:4 — M03-E-VCG-01) is itself evidence of moral evaluation operating in the inner being. The absence of grief where grief is morally appropriate signals that moral evaluation is not functioning (1Cor 5:2; Amo 6:6 — M03-B-VCG-04, BOUNDARY). Grief does not bypass or impair moral evaluation; it is one of the primary signs that moral evaluation is active.

---

**T3.8.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief functions as a moral indicator: its presence where loss or sin has occurred signals that moral evaluation is functioning correctly; its absence signals that it is not. This makes grief one of the programme's most significant moral-evaluative signals — not a characteristic separate from moral faculty engagement but one deeply intertwined with the functioning of conscience and moral awareness.

---

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Yes, prominently. Conscience is evidenced as both a trigger and a product of grief in M03. Eze 9:4 (M03-E-VCG-01): grief arising from conscience's response to witnessed wickedness — God marks those whose inner sighing expresses genuine conscience-driven grief. Joh 21:17 (M03-C-VCG-01): Peter's grief arises from conscience's recognition of his past failure. 2Pe 2:8 (M03-BOUNDARY): Lot's soul tormented day after day by witnessing lawless deeds — conscience-driven anguish.

---

**T3.9.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — Godly grief deepens conscience: the Corinthians' grief produces fear (reverent moral sensitivity — 2Cor 7:11 — M03-C-VCG-02) alongside other moral fruits. Grief that arises from conscience's perception deepens the conscience's sensitivity — the one who grieves over sin is more sensitised to its weight. Conversely, grief that is worldly or self-directed does not deepen conscience but may leave it unchanged (the rich young man's grief, M03-C-VCG-01).

---

**T3.9.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief and conscience have a reciprocal relationship in M03: conscience triggers grief (by perceiving sin, loss, or moral failure), and grief deepens conscience (by making the weight of sin more real and present to the person). This reciprocal loop — conscience → grief → deepened conscience — is one of the cluster's most significant structural findings and places M03 as closely related to M10 (Guilt, Conscience) in the programme's broader landscape.

---

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated moral response of awareness, volition, and action?

**[C-VCG-02, E-VCG-01]**
E — Yes. Godly grief produces the full conscientious response: earnestness, indignation, fear, longing, zeal, and readiness to see justice done (2Cor 7:11 — M03-C-VCG-02). This is the integrated outcome — grief as morally aware feeling → volitional reformation → action orientation. Conscience-driven sighing (Eze 9:4 — M03-E-VCG-01) is also an integrated conscientious response: moral perception of wickedness → inner grief as appropriate response → divine recognition and marking.

---

**T3.10.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — Godly grief enables and deepens conscientiousness. Worldly grief (grief that does not lead to repentance or action) does not produce conscientiousness — it leaves the person sorrowful but unchanged. The discriminating factor is whether the grief is directed toward God and oriented toward moral reformation, or whether it is self-directed and resolved in self-concern.

---

**T3.10.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03 reveals that grief is a potential gateway to full conscientious inner-being functioning — but only when grief is godly in quality. The cluster's evidence for grief-as-moral-catalyst is extensive in M03-C-VCG-02 and M03-D-VCG-03, establishing grief as one of the mechanisms through which the inner person's moral-volitional life is renewed and directed toward God.

---

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Grief is foundationally relational — it arises from and expresses genuine relational bonds. This is the cluster's most consistently evidenced faculty engagement. Bereavement weeping (M03-A-VCG-01) is grief over a broken relational bond. Empathic weeping (Rom 12:15 — M03-A-VCG-05) is grief exercised as an act of relational solidarity. The prophetic-compassion weeping (M03-A-VCG-04) is grief arising from relational identification with another's suffering. The anguish at relational loss (Act 20:38 — M03-D-VCG-05) names grief as the inner experience of the relational bond's severance. The Spirit's grief at sin (Eph 4:30 — M03-C-VCG-02) is grief arising from the Spirit's relational investment in the community.

---

**T3.11.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?

**[A-VCG-05, B-VCG-04, C-VCG-02]**
E — Grief deepens relational capacity: commanded weeping-with (Rom 12:15 — M03-A-VCG-05) shows that entering into another's grief expands relational capacity by extending empathic solidarity. The Corinthians' mourning becoming a sign of repentance and relational restoration (2Cor 7:7 — M03-B-VCG-04) shows grief restoring damaged relational bonds. Grief can also signal the depth of relational capacity — the depth of David's grief for Absalom (2Sa 18:33 — M03-A-VCG-01) reveals the depth of his relational bond. Where grief is absent in contexts requiring it, relational capacity may be impaired (1Cor 5:2 — M03-B-VCG-04; Amo 6:6 — BOUNDARY).

---

**T3.11.3** — What does the pattern of engagement with relational capacity reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — M03 reveals grief as simultaneously the evidence of relational capacity (one grieves because one has genuinely bonded), the expression of relational capacity (grief shared as solidarity deepens bonds), and the restorer of relational capacity (godly grief enabling repentance and restored relationship). The characteristic is among the most relational in the programme — it cannot exist without the relational foundation that generates it, and it reaches its full resolution only through relational restoration or eschatological reversal.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?

**[B-VCG-03, D-VCG-03, G-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, in three distinct modes. (1) God bestows grief as discipline: "sorrow in the soul as a languishing, which God gives as punishment" (Deu 28:65 — M03-C-VCG-01 anchor); God puts the servant to grief so that his soul-offering may justify many (Isa 53:10 — BOUNDARY); God adds sorrow to Baruch's existing pain (Jer 45:3 — M03-F-VCG-01). (2) God multiplies mourning as judgment: "mourning is something the Lord multiplies in Judah as an act of judgment" (Lam 2:5 — M03-B-VCG-02); God governs mourning's expression — commanding it, withholding it, and reversing it (M03-B-VCG-03). (3) God fills the inner being with bitterness: Job 9:18 and Lam 3:15 (M03-G-VCG-01 anchors) — God fills with bitterness and will not let the person breathe; God fills the person with bitterness to total saturation.

---

**T4.1.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The bases are varied. Disciplinary: God's use of distress and grief to press the inner will back toward him (M03-D-VCG-03). Judgmental: grief imposed as direct consequence of sin and covenant violation (Deu 28:65; Lam 2:5 — M03-B-VCG-02). Redemptive: God puts the servant to grief for salvific purpose (Isa 53:10 — BOUNDARY). Compassion-responsive: God hears the groaning of the oppressed and responds with covenantal faithfulness (Exo 2:24 — M03-E-VCG-01). The basis therefore includes both punitive and redemptive-compassionate modes — God extends grief-experiences toward the human person not uniformly but in ways conditioned by the person's covenantal situation, sin, and need.

---

**T4.1.3** — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — God's extension of grief toward the human person reveals a God who is seriously engaged with the inner life and its conditions. Two dispositions are evidenced simultaneously. First, seriousness about sin and covenant: God's grief-bestowal as discipline and judgment shows that he takes sin's inner consequences seriously — the heart must register what sin costs. Second, compassion and responsiveness: God hears the groan of the oppressed (Exo 2:24 — M03-E-VCG-01), sees and records tears (2Ki 20:5 — M03-A-VCG-02 anchor; Psa 56:8 — M03-A-VCG-02), wipes away tears eschatologically (Isa 25:8 — M03-A-VCG-02). God governs grief with the intention of its resolution: mourning is what God reverses (Psa 30:11 — M03-B-VCG-03 anchor), what he abolishes in the new creation (Rev 21:4 — M03-B-VCG-04). God's disposition is that grief is a real and serious inner-being state that he takes seriously, governs purposively, and intends ultimately to remove.

---

**T4.1.4** — If the evidence is silent on God-to-human operation, note this explicitly.

**[A-VCG-01, A-VCG-05, F-VCG-02, F-VCG-03]**
S — The bereavement weeping corpus (M03-A-VCG-01), the eschatological condemnation-weeping corpus (M03-A-VCG-05), the unceasing pain corpus (M03-F-VCG-02), and the birth-pangs metaphor corpus (M03-F-VCG-03) do not evidence God directly extending grief toward the person in these registers. They show grief arising from circumstance, judgment, or loss without explicit narration of God's direct bestowing act (though God's sovereign governance is implied throughout).

---

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God?

**[D-VCG-01, A-VCG-02, E-VCG-01]**
E — This is the most extensively evidenced relational direction in the entire cluster. M03-D-VCG-01 (56 verses) is almost entirely constituted by distress operating in the human person's movement toward God: "call upon me in the day of trouble" (Psa 50:15); "in my distress I cried to the Lord" (Psa 18:6 — anchor); God answers and delivers from all distress. M03-A-VCG-02 (36 verses) is the penitential weeping VCG — tears directed toward God as petition, intercession, and confession. M03-E-VCG-01 names groaning as the inner cry rising to God: Israel's groan under slavery rises to God (Exo 2:24 — anchor).

---

**T4.2.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required?

**[D-VCG-01, A-VCG-02]**
E — The inner posture evidenced for effective human-to-God grief movement is primarily humility and genuine inner orientation. Josiah's weeping before God is effective because of his "tender, humbled heart" (2Ki 22:19 — M03-A-VCG-02 anchor). Deu 1:45 (M03-A-VCG-02) shows weeping that goes unanswered because it follows disobedience without genuine repentance — the posture is absent. The inner conditions required appear to be: genuine inner state (not performance), Godward orientation, and — in the penitential context — humility. Distress directed to God through prayer appears to require simply the genuine experience of distress itself — no prerequisite of spiritual worthiness is named; the groaning of slaves reaches God without qualification (Exo 2:24 — M03-E-VCG-01).

---

**T4.2.3** — What does the human-to-God direction reveal about the person's relationship with God?

**[D-VCG-01]**
E — The consistent pattern of distress → cry → divine answer across M03-D-VCG-01 reveals a relationship in which grief is a constitutionally appropriate and effective form of approach to God. The pattern recurs with structural consistency: distress is the occasion that activates the covenantal exchange, and God's answering reveals that he regards the human person's grief as a valid appeal that he is disposed to answer. This is a relationship of asymmetric mutual engagement: the person brings the grief, and God brings the deliverance — grief is the form of the human person's dependence most directly addressed by God in the corpus.

---

**T4.2.4** — If the evidence is silent on human-to-God operation, note this explicitly.

**[A-VCG-01, A-VCG-03, B-VCG-01, G-VCG-01]**
