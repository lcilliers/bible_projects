## M03-F-VCG-01 — The Servant's Pain and Pain Before God — Identified Suffering

**Context description:** This VCG names pain that is defined, perceived, or held by God — pain that constitutes an identity or is acknowledged as real by the divine. The suffering servant is a man of sorrows, deeply familiar with grief; his inner life is marked by pain (Isa 53:3 makh.ov), and he bears the people's sorrows as his own burden (Isa 53:4). God declares he sees Israel's pain in Egypt and acknowledges it as the basis for his redemptive intervention (Exo 3:7 makh.ov); prayer directed toward the temple is driven by personal pain that each person carries inwardly (2Ch 6:29 makh.ov). Pain is a constant inward presence that does not leave the psalmist's awareness (Psa 38:17 makh.ov); enemies expose and mock the inner wound of God's wounded one (Psa 69:26 makh.ov). In Ecclesiastes, pain is the inner cost of increased wisdom (Ecc 1:18) and saturates all the days of the toiling person (Ecc 2:23). The king cries out in a tone of anguish over Daniel's fate, his anguish driven by urgent uncertainty and desperate hope (Dan 6:20 a.tsav). This VCG gathers the verses where pain is named, witnessed, or held — identified as a real inner reality by the one in pain or by God.

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 18585 — Isa 53:3 (H4341 makh.ov)  
*Rationale: Pain defines the servant's identity — "a man of sorrows, deeply familiar with grief" — making this the most definitional verse of pain as an identity-constituting inner experience, carried in the inner life and witnessed by others.*

## M03-F-VCG-02 — Unceasing Inner Pain — The Wound Without Healing

**Context description:** This VCG names pain characterised by its persistence, its resistance to remedy, and its erosive effect on the inner person and the divine relationship. Pain is unyielding regardless of whether Job speaks or stays silent (Job 16:6 ke.ev); it grew worse as the psalmist kept silent, showing pain as an inner pressure that silence cannot contain (Psa 39:2 ke.ev); the prophet's pain is unceasing like an incurable wound refusing to heal, threatening to erode trust in God (Jer 15:18 ke.ev). Pain is located in the heart, contrasted with gladness (Isa 65:14 ke.ev). Pain coexists with and underlies surface expressions of joy — the heart can ache beneath outward laughter (Pro 14:13 ka.av). Job's bodily pain after death is the sole remaining inner reality, confined to the self alone (Job 14:22 ka.av). God wounds but also heals — pain within a frame of purposive divine action (Job 5:18 ka.av). Pain from hostile neighbors is a wound inflicted from without but felt as an inner hurt (Eze 28:24 ka.av). The psalmist is in pain and appeals only to God's salvation as the means of being lifted out (Psa 69:29 ka.av). The magnitude of Job's suffering silenced all words from his friends (Job 2:13 ke.ev).

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 12659 — Jer 15:18 (H3511 ke.ev)  
*Rationale: Pain described as unceasing, like an incurable wound refusing to heal — with the prophet questioning whether God himself has become unreliable — directly names the persistence and relational corrosion that defines this VCG's register.*

## M03-F-VCG-03 — Birth-Pangs as Metaphor for Acute Inner Anguish

**Context description:** This VCG names pain vocabulary using the birth-pangs image (che.vel-B) to describe the acute, involuntary, sudden seizure of inner anguish that cannot be controlled or resisted. The meanings consistently use the birth-pain metaphor to name an overwhelming inner-and-physical grip: pain seizes the inner person like labour pangs gripping a woman in childbirth, producing visible outer symptoms (Isa 13:8); the community describes their anguish before God as the involuntary pangs of a woman in hard labour — overwhelming, physical, and inescapable (Isa 26:17); birth-pangs describe the sudden, overpowering grip of anguish that will seize Jerusalem (Jer 13:21), Lebanon (Jer 22:23), and Damascus (Jer 49:24). The miraculous sign in Isa 66:7 is precisely that Zion gave birth before the pain arrived — the birth-pain is the expected inner-physical anguish that normally cannot be skipped. Hosea uses the pangs of childbirth for Ephraim's critical moment of crisis — acute and imminent pain wasted through failure to respond rightly (Hos 13:13). The wicked receive pains distributed by God as divinely imposed consequences (Job 21:17 che.vel).

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 575 — Isa 13:8 (H2256B che.vel)  
*Rationale: Pain seizing the inner person like labour pangs — with visible outer symptoms revealing the depth of inner anguish — most directly names the birth-pangs-as-metaphor pattern: involuntary, sudden, total-body seizure of acute inner anguish.*
