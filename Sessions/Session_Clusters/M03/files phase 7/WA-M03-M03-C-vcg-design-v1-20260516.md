## M03-C-VCG-01 — Soul and Heart-Located Sorrow — The Inner Condition

**Context description:** This VCG names grief as a settled, located inner condition — sorrow that is felt in the soul, heart, and deepest inner being. The meanings consistently locate grief explicitly: sorrow is lodged in the heart all day long (Psa 13:2); grief is so deep the soul melts away (Psa 119:28); the sorrow of exile is a companion that flees only when everlasting joy arrives (Isa 35:10; 51:11). This is grief not primarily as an act performed or an outward event witnessed, but as a weight carried inwardly: Jacob anticipates sorrow so total it would drive him to Sheol (Gen 42:38; 44:31); the father's grief at a foolish son is a personal emotional burden with no accompanying joy (Pro 17:21); grief is what joy ultimately gives way to, lurking beneath even gladness (Pro 14:13). The New Testament lupeō corpus contributes the same pattern: the rich young man departs sorrowful (Mat 19:22; Mar 10:22); the disciples are greatly distressed upon hearing Jesus's announcement (Mat 17:23); Peter is grieved in his inner being (Joh 21:17); grief and joy coexist in the apostolic inner life simultaneously (2Cor 6:10; 1Pe 1:6). Sorrow here is the default inner condition of those who grieve — the state before or beneath the act.

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 848 — Psa 13:2 (H3015 ya.gon)  
*Rationale: Sorrow lodged in the heart all day long — sustained, inward, occupying the heart continually — is the most direct and definitional statement of inner-condition sorrow: located, persistent, and named as the heart's burden.*

## M03-C-VCG-02 — Godly Grief — Morally Generative Sorrow and Grief over Sin

**Context description:** This VCG names grief that is morally motivated and productive — sorrow that arises from witnessing or experiencing moral failure, sin, or relational rupture, and that leads toward moral action, repentance, or change. The meanings consistently show grief as a generative inner force: godly grief produces earnestness, indignation, fear, and zeal in the Corinthians (2Cor 7:11); the Corinthians were grieved into repentance (2Cor 7:9); Paul's grief inflicted by letter was temporary but purposeful — a transient inner wound in service of lasting good (2Cor 7:8). The fellow servants' morally motivated grief at merciless treatment leads directly to reporting the injustice (Mat 18:31). The disciples' grief at the betrayal announcement prompts each to self-examine (Mat 26:22; Mar 14:19). Grief also flows from conscience: Peter's grief when Jesus asks three times stirs painful self-awareness about his denial (Joh 21:17 — dual). Causing grief to the weaker conscience violates love (Rom 14:15). The Spirit can be grieved by moral failure in the community (Eph 4:30) — a uniquely important verse showing that the Spirit's responsive grief is the measure of sin's relational damage. James commands mourning and weeping as the fitting response of those convicted of sin (Jam 4:9 — secondary from M03-B).

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 12399 — 2Cor 7:11 (G3076 lupeō)  
*Rationale: Godly grief producing earnestness, indignation, fear, longing, zeal, and vindication directly names what morally generative grief looks like in the inner being — grief that reshapes will, conscience, and moral energy rather than producing damage.*

## M03-C-VCG-03 — Christ's Gethsemane Grief — Soul-Sorrow to the Depth of Death

**Context description:** This VCG names the unique inner-being grief of Jesus in Gethsemane — soul-located sorrow that reaches the extreme depth of threatening life itself. The meanings are clear and consistent: Jesus describes his soul as very sorrowful, even to death (Mat 26:38; perilupos); he begins to be sorrowful and troubled as he enters Gethsemane (Mat 26:37; lupeō); his soul is very sorrowful, even to death (Mar 14:34; perilupos). The explicit soul-location and the superlative depth — "even to death" — mark this as a distinct register within the sorrow sub-group. This is the maximum inner anguish of grief: grief that has penetrated to the deepest layer of the person and reached an extremity that threatens existence. It belongs in M03-C (inner-condition sorrow) rather than M03-D (anguish/distress) because the meanings locate it in the soul as a condition rather than as pressing distress driving prayer — it is the weight Jesus carries into the cross, held within rather than crying outward.

**Anchor verse:** vc_id 38148 — Mat 26:38 (G4036 perilupos)  
*Rationale: Jesus describes his soul as very sorrowful, even to death — the explicit soul-location and the superlative measure ("even to death") make this the most direct and definitional verse of soul-sorrow at its extreme depth.*
