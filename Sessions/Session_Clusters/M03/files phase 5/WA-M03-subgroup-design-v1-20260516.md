## M03-A — Weeping and Tears

**Core description:** This sub-group names the somatic and visible expression of grief through weeping and tears. The meanings consistently show weeping as an inner state that overflows outward into the body — tears that drench the bed through the night (Psa 6:6), exhaust the eyes (Lam 2:11), flow continuously on the face (Lam 1:2), or are so abundant the prophet longs for a fountain to sustain them (Jer 9:1). Across the corpus, tears function as the recognised evidence of inner grief: God sees them, hears the sound of them, and records them (2Ki 20:5; Psa 6:8; Psa 56:8). Weeping is communal as well as personal — whole peoples weep together in mourning (Deu 34:8; Num 20:29), families weep at reunion and death (Gen 45:14-15; Gen 50:1), and prophets weep for their nations (Jer 9:1; Isa 16:9). The sub-group also includes penitential weeping directed to God (Joe 2:12; Ezr 10:1; 2Ki 22:19) and eschatological weeping both as the condition of the condemned (Mat 8:12; Luk 13:28) and as something God will silence and ultimately wipe away (Isa 25:8; Isa 65:19; Rev 21:4 — shared with M03-B). The unifying characteristic is that these terms specifically name the act, sound, or substance of weeping and tears as the primary inner-grief indicator.

## M03-B — Mourning and Lamentation

**Core description:** This sub-group names the act and condition of mourning — the outward form that grief takes in its social, ritual, communal, and personal expression. The meanings in this sub-group consistently show mourning as the structured or habitual pattern of grief: mourning periods (Gen 50:10; Deu 34:8), mourning garments (2Sa 14:2), lament cries (1Ki 13:30), lamentation at gravesides (sa.phad at Gen 23:2; Gen 50:10), communal wailing (mis.ped at Amo 5:16-17; Mic 1:8). The sub-group includes both the personal act of mourning for the dead and for national disaster, and the divinely commanded or withheld mourning that marks the relationship between God and his people (mourning withheld as judgment at Jer 16:4-6; mourning commanded as the fitting response at Isa 22:12; Joe 1:13). Mourning is also the condition that God himself addresses and reverses: the mourner is the target of divine consolation (Isa 61:2-3), and mourning is what eschatological joy displaces (Jer 31:13; Rev 21:4). The New Testament vocabulary of pen.the.o and penthos carries the same double register — mourning as inner condition and as willed act — including Jesus's declaration that those who mourn are blessed (Mat 5:4) and the command to let laughter yield to mourning (Jam 4:9). The characteristic that unifies this sub-group is mourning understood as the human act and state of grief given outward, socially recognized form.

## M03-C — Sorrow and Inner Grief

**Core description:** This sub-group names grief as a settled inner emotional condition — the state of the person who is sorrowful, grieved, or weighted with sorrow in the soul and heart. The meanings consistently locate grief in the inner person directly: Jesus's soul is very sorrowful, even to death (Mat 26:38; perilupos); sorrow is lodged in the heart all day long (Psa 13:2; ya.gon); grief is so deep the soul melts away (Psa 119:28; tu.gah); the disciples are greatly distressed when they process Jesus's death announcement (Mat 17:23; lupeō). The distinguishing feature of this sub-group is the inner-state character of the grief — the sorrow is felt, located in the heart or soul, and experienced as an inner condition rather than primarily expressed outward as weeping, mourning ritual, groaning, or anguish-pressure. This register includes grief that is morally motivated (Jesus grieved at Pharisees' hardness — sullupeō; Eph 4:30 the Spirit grieves at sin), grief at relational loss (Act 20:38; odunē Rom 9:2), grief that coexists with joy (2Cor 6:10; lupeō), and godly grief that produces repentance (2Cor 7:9-11; lupeō). The New Testament lupeō group is the richest evidence of this sub-group — grief consistently portrayed as a personal, inner, soul-located state.

## M03-D — Anguish and Distress

**Core description:** This sub-group names grief's most intense, overwhelming form — anguish as a pressing, constricting, crushing inner state. The meanings consistently show distress as a force that presses in on the inner person, grips the soul and spirit, and drives the sufferer to cry out to God for deliverance. The dominant register is anguish that is located in the spirit (Job 7:11 — tsar: "from the anguish of my spirit I speak"), in the heart (Psa 25:17 — me.tsu.qah: "enlarged troubles of the heart"), and in the soul (Psa 31:7 — tsa.rah: "God has known it in my soul"). The two largest corpora in the cluster belong to this sub-group: tsa.rah (62 verses) and tsar (28 verses), both consistently showing distress as the inner-being crisis that drives prayer and from which God delivers. The sub-group also encompasses the Greek anguish vocabulary: sunochē (heart-seated anguish accompanied by many tears at 2Cor 2:4; nations' anguish at Luk 21:25), odunaō (anguish at relational loss at Act 20:38; afterlife anguish at Luk 16:24-25), and odunē (heart-located unceasing sorrow at Rom 9:2). The unifying characteristic is anguish as the most acute and pressing form of inner suffering — not primarily expressed as weeping or mourning or groaning, but felt as an overwhelming inner grip or weight.

## M03-E — Groaning and Sighing

**Core description:** This sub-group names the involuntary or deliberate vocal and bodily expression of grief through groaning, sighing, and travail-like utterance. The meanings consistently show groaning and sighing as the deep inner pressure of grief or suffering finding its outward voice — often inward, audible to God alone, and rising to him as a cry or appeal. Israel groans and sighs under slavery and their cry rises to God (Exo 2:23 — a.nach; Exo 2:24 — ne.a.qah), and God hears and responds covenantally. The prophet sighs from a breaking heart, his groaning grounded in bitter grief within (Eze 21:6 — a.nach). Sighing has replaced bread as Job's sustenance, so total is his grief (Job 3:24 — a.na.chah). Groaning rises from the tumult of the heart (Psa 38:8 — ne.ha.mah). The New Testament witnesses the Spirit groaning within the believer's spirit in intercession (Rom 8:26 — stenagmos) and all creation groaning together in birth-pain anguish oriented toward redemption (Rom 8:22 — sustenazō). The travail vocabulary also belongs here: ōdinō names the consuming, involuntary anguish of labour that Paul uses metaphorically for his apostolic inner travail of love for the Galatians (Gal 4:19), and for the woman's birth-cry at the extremity of distress (Rev 12:2). The unifying characteristic is grief expressed as the groan, sigh, or travail-cry — the involuntary or willed utterance of inner suffering that moves toward God.

## M03-F — Pain and Inner Ache

**Core description:** This sub-group names grief at the level of pain — the ache, wound, and soreness of the inner person. The meanings consistently describe a suffering that is felt as pain in the body and soul simultaneously, often persistent and inescapable. The servant of Isaiah is "a man of sorrows, deeply familiar with grief" (Isa 53:3 — makh.ov), and he bears the people's sorrows as a burden (Isa 53:4). Unceasing pain erodes trust in the divine relationship (Jer 15:18 — ke.ev). The heart can ache even beneath outward laughter (Pro 14:13 — ka.av). Pain has become a constant inward presence that does not leave the psalmist's awareness (Psa 38:17 — makh.ov). God wounds but also binds up — pain is seen within a frame of purposive divine action (Job 5:18 — ka.av). This sub-group also includes the birth-pangs vocabulary of che.vel-B (8 verses), where the meanings use labour-pain imagery consistently as a metaphor for the acute, involuntary seizure of inner anguish: pain seizes the inner person like labour pangs (Isa 13:8; 26:17; Jer 13:21; 22:23; 49:24). The unifying characteristic is pain as the primary phenomenological quality of grief — what grief feels like from within.

## M03-G — Bitterness of Soul

**Core description:** This sub-group names the specific inner quality of bitterness that arises from grief, suffering, loss, and divine dealings — the soul's experience of something that has become harsh, sharp, and without sweetness. The meanings consistently show bitterness as an intensification or colouring of grief: Naomi names herself Mara (bitterness) because the Lord's dealings have made her inner state one of total bitter affliction (Rut 1:20 — ma.rar); Job says God fills him with bitterness and will not let him catch his breath, showing bitterness as a suffocating inner state under divine pressure (Job 9:18 — mam.ror); God fills the person with bitterness as something total and saturating, leaving no room for relief (Lam 3:15 — ma.ror); bitterness is located in the heart as a private inner experience no outsider can share (Pro 14:10 — mor.rah); Esau's wives made life itself bitter for Isaac and Rebekah — a pervasive inner distress from relational friction (Gen 26:35 — mo.rah). The pikrainō corpus adds the dimension of bitterness produced by relational wounding (Col 3:19 — harshness producing bitterness in another's spirit) and the inner distress of receiving prophetic judgment-oracles that prove bitter within (Rev 10:9-10). The sub-group also includes me.mer, which names bitterness as the sharp inner pain a foolish son inflicts on his mother (Pro 17:25). What unifies these terms is bitterness as a specific inner quality of grief — the bitterness of the soul, not anger-bitterness (which transferred to M02), but the harsh, pungent inner experience of grief, loss, and suffering that has become like wormwood.
