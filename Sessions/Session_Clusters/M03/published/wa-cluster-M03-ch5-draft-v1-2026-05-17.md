## 5. How each characteristic works

How each characteristic works — how it moves between God and the person and between persons, how it is formed and deepened, how it sequences through the inner being, and what it leaves behind — is the subject of this chapter, taken one characteristic at a time.

---

### 5.1 Weeping and Tears

Weeping and tears moves in one primary direction: outward from within. The inner grief overflows through the body and becomes visible to others — and, as the evidence consistently shows, to God. Joseph's weeping is the paradigmatic instance of this overflow: "And he wept aloud, so that the Egyptians heard it, and the household of Pharaoh heard it" (Gen 45:2). Years of suppressed grief break through in a sound that cannot be contained. The weeping is an act — the act of grief becoming audible — but it arises from an inner condition that has long preceded it.

The temporal dimension of weeping is also evidenced. Gen 50:4 — "And when the days of weeping for him were past, Joseph spoke to the household of Pharaoh" — frames weeping as a bounded period: it has a beginning, it runs its course, and it concludes. After the days of weeping are complete, the will re-engages with practical life. This arc of weeping — from onset to completion — is the characteristic's most basic temporal movement.

**Movement toward God**

The most theologically significant movement of weeping and tears is upward. When tears are directed toward God in penitential honesty or prayer, they function as the somatic form of petition — a physical evidence of genuine inner state that God himself receives and responds to. God declares to Hezekiah: "I have heard your prayer; I have seen your tears. Behold, I will heal you" (2Ki 20:5). The tears are seen; they carry weight before God; they are answered. Josiah's weeping before the Lord is framed similarly: God hears him precisely because the weeping is the outward expression of a genuinely humbled and penitent heart (2Ki 22:19).

The evidence shows a limiting condition on this movement. Weeping that is performative — that covers the altar with tears without genuine inner repentance behind it (Mal 2:13) — does not produce the same divine response. Similarly, the weeping that follows disobedience without genuine repentance goes unanswered (Deu 1:45). The inner posture required for tears to reach God is not spiritual perfection but genuine Godward orientation — the absence of performance and the presence of real inner movement toward him.

**Movement between persons**

Weeping and tears also moves between persons in several modes. Within existing relational bonds, weeping is the most direct evidence of the bond's depth. David's grief for Absalom — "O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! Would I had died instead of you, O Absalom, my son, my son!" (2Sa 18:33) — reveals parental love at its most unguarded. The weeping discloses the inner life's real investment. Across relational distance, weeping requires a deliberate volitional act: "weep with those who weep" (Rom 12:15) is a command, not a description. Empathic weeping must be chosen; the will must extend itself toward another's grief, entering it as an act of love rather than as an automatic overflow.

Prophetic weeping moves in yet another register: the prophet weeps on behalf of a people or nation as an act of identification. Jeremiah's longing for inexhaustible tears — "Oh that my head were waters, and my eyes a fountain of tears, that I might weep day and night for the slain of the daughter of my people!" (Jer 9:1) — is grief extended beyond personal bond toward communal solidarity. The prophet's inner life is shaped by the grief of those he serves; his tears are given for others.

Grief also crosses covenantal boundaries. The Egyptians weep for Jacob; the prophet weeps for Moab. Weeping is a human phenomenon that does not respect the borders of the covenant community, though the most theologically generative weeping — penitential, Godward, and transformative — operates distinctively within the covenantal relationship between God and his people.

**Formation and deposit**

Bereavement weeping, communal national grief, and the weeping of exile do not produce evidenced transformation in the verse evidence. They describe grief experienced and expressed — real and legitimate, but not narrated as a force that reshapes the inner person. The penitential weeping of the Godward register does produce transformation, but this belongs primarily to the Sorrow and Inner Grief characteristic's evidence.

What weeping does leave is somatic deposit. Sustained weeping marks the body: Job's face reddened and eyes darkened (Job 16:16); the psalmist's bones wasted, bodily strength spent (Psa 31:10). The body carries the history of what the inner person has wept. This deposit is not permanent — God turns mourning into dancing, tears are wiped away — but while grief runs its course, the body accumulates its evidence.

### 5.2 Mourning and Lamentation

Mourning and lamentation is governed — in a way no other characteristic in this family is — from outside the person as well as from within. God commands it, withholds it, and reverses it. This governance is the defining feature of how mourning works: it is not simply what the person does in response to loss but a mode of inner and social life that God himself regulates.

The primary movement of mourning is willed and outward. Abraham "went in to mourn for Sarah and to weep for her" (Gen 23:2): the mourning is a deliberate entry into the grief-act, directed toward a specific person. Jacob "tore his garments and put sackcloth on his loins and mourned for his son many days" (Gen 37:34): the mourning is embodied, sustained, and directed against all comfort. Jacob then names his mourning as a permanent inner posture he intends to carry: "No, I shall go down to Sheol to my son, mourning" (Gen 37:35). The will has chosen to mourn indefinitely; mourning is not merely grief's expression but the form of the inner life going forward.

**Movement from God**

God extends mourning toward the person as discipline or judgment. Lamentations records God multiplying mourning in Judah as an act of judgment; God commands entire communities to mourn as a moral and penitential response; he forbids mourning when the relational context is broken — Jeremiah is told not to enter the house of mourning because God has withdrawn his peace from the people (Jer 16:5). God also reverses mourning by direct divine action: "You have turned for me my mourning into dancing; you have loosed my sackcloth and clothed me with gladness" (Psa 30:11). The reversal is not the natural expiry of grief but a divine transformation of the inner condition.

**Movement between persons and in community**

Mourning's most distinctive social dimension is communal mourning as a shared event. When Amos commands mourning to penetrate every square and street — "In all the squares there shall be wailing, and in all the streets they shall say, 'Alas! Alas!' They shall call the farmers to mourning and to wailing those who are skilled in lamentation" (Amo 5:16) — mourning is a whole-community act in which individual grief is given shared form. The house of mourning is a social institution; professional lament-makers are a social function; mourning garments and periods are social norms. Mourning is the characteristic that most fully extends grief into the communal and public life of a people.

In the New Testament, mourning narrows toward the individual's inner life. Jesus declares the mourning person blessed: "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted" (Mat 5:4). James commands mourning as the appropriate inner displacement of levity: laughter must yield to mourning when the situation morally demands it (Jam 4:9). The Corinthians' mourning is a sign of genuine repentance that restores damaged relational bonds (2Cor 7:7). In each case the mourning is an inner condition that God or the community can witness and that God responds to with comfort.

**Transformation and formation**

Mourning for the dead and communal national mourning do not produce evidenced inner transformation in their specific verse evidence — they describe grief expressed and endured rather than grief as a transforming force. The transformation evidence belongs to the penitential and NT registers. There, mourning participates in the formation of moral seriousness: the wise heart chooses the house of mourning over the house of feasting (Ecc 7:2, 7:4) because regular encounter with grief and mortality shapes wisdom. Mourning also has an eschatological formation trajectory: it is the mark of the not-yet, the condition that will be replaced by divine comfort when the fullness comes. Its presence names where the person is; its promised reversal names where they are going.

The failure modes of mourning name what the characteristic looks like when it is absent where it should be present. Arrogance, moral numbing, and self-deception are the three inner states Scripture names for those who do not receive the grief that their situation requires — the Corinthians' arrogance (1Cor 5:2), the complacency of those not grieved over Joseph's ruin (Amo 6:6), and Babylon's boast that she will never see mourning (Rev 18:7). Each is the inner alternative to genuine grief reception: the failure of the relational and moral responsiveness that mourning requires.

### 5.3 Supporting characteristics

The supporting terms gathered at the boundary of this study do not share a single working pattern — their diversity is precisely what places them at the edge of the grief vocabulary rather than its centre. What can be said of them collectively is how they arrive and what they do in their most concentrated instances.

The Gen 3:16 vocabulary establishes the governing frame for the terms at the fall-consequence edge of the group. "I will surely multiply your pain in childbearing; in pain you shall bring forth children" — the pain (*e.tsev*) is divinely multiplied and imposed at the most intimate bodily act. The second term in the same verse (*its.tsa.von*) names the same imposition as toil. Both move from God to the person as consequences of the fall; they are not sought or formed but received as the condition of life after Eden. The movement is entirely from outside: God multiplies them, the person bears them.

The hardship vocabulary moves differently. Joseph names the burden God has released: "God has made me forget all my hardship and all my father's house" (Gen 41:51). The hardship (*a.mal*) was a sustained inner burden carried over time — years of suffering and estrangement held in memory — and its release is also from outside: God acts, and the person is freed. The direction of movement here is God resolving what God had permitted.

The hostile-provocation terms move horizontally: "The archers bitterly attacked him, shot at him, and harassed him severely" (Gen 49:23). Here the movement is from adversary to person — the embittering pressure is externally imposed through violent hostility rather than arising from inner grief over loss. The term marks a different edge of the grief vocabulary.

These terms are held at the edge of this study precisely because each of them requires more examination before its inner home, movement pattern, and relational direction can be settled. They inform the study's understanding of grief's outer edges without yet yielding to full analysis.

### 5.4 Sorrow and Inner Grief

Sorrow and inner grief moves through the inner person in three distinct directions: from God to the person, from person to God, and from person to person. Of all the characteristics in this family, this one has the richest and most varied movement evidence — because it is the settled inner condition from which all other forms of grief arise.

**Movement from God**

God extends sorrow to the person as discipline, correction, and sovereign dealing. Deu 28:65 names the divine bestowal directly — the languishing soul and trembling heart are what "the Lord will give you there" in exile. Sorrow here is not a natural consequence of circumstances but a condition God gives. At the redemptive extreme, God puts his servant to grief so that his soul-offering may justify many (Isa 53:10 — the servant's grief is not incidental but appointed for redemptive purpose). The movement from God is purposive throughout: even the sorrow God gives as punishment is given with an end in view.

**Movement toward God**

The characteristic's movement toward God is most clearly evidenced in the penitential and pastoral registers. Godly sorrow moves in a sequence that the evidence describes with unusual precision: letter → grief → godly sorrow → repentance without regret → earnestness, indignation, fear, longing, zeal (2Cor 7:8–11). The grief is the catalyst for each stage that follows. It is worth noting that this sequence requires the grief to be directed toward God rather than toward self — godly grief produces reformation because it is oriented toward the moral and relational reality that God takes seriously; worldly grief, directed toward self, produces only the sorrow of regret that does not transform.

Jesus's grief in Gethsemane moves toward God in its own way: "My soul is very sorrowful, even to death; remain here, and watch with me" (Mat 26:38). The soul-sorrow is named before God; the request to watch alongside accompanies it; the prayer that follows (Mat 26:39 and following) is the Gethsemane movement toward the Father. Even at the most extreme inner depth, the soul's sorrow moves toward rather than away from God.

**Movement between persons**

Sorrow and inner grief also moves between persons: given through pastoral grief (Paul's apostolic grief over the Corinthians in 2Cor 2:4), received through relationship (the mother's grief from her foolish son, Pro 10:1; the disciples' grief from Jesus's announcement in Mat 26:22), and transmitted through word (the Corinthians receive grief through Paul's letter, 2Cor 7:8). The enabling condition for this person-to-person movement is genuine relational bond: the deeper the bond, the more directly the other's state produces grief in the person who loves them. The blocking conditions are attachment to something other than the relationship — wealth in the rich young man's case (Mat 19:22), arrogance in the Corinthians' case (1Cor 5:2).

**Modes**

Sorrow and inner grief operates in two structurally distinct modes. In its passive mode it is a condition the person is in — sorrow lodged in the heart all day long (Psa 13:2), grief melting the soul (Psa 119:28). In this mode the characteristic is received and carried rather than exercised. In its generative mode it becomes an active force that reshapes the inner life — godly grief producing earnestness and zeal (2Cor 7:11), sorrow producing the lament form (Jer 9:1). The distinction between these modes is morally significant: sorrow that remains passive and inward does not produce the same outcome as sorrow that is oriented toward God and allowed to move outward into reformed will and action.

**Formation**

Sorrow and inner grief participates in formation over the long arc in a way that the more acute characteristics do not. The recurring pattern of distress → prayer → deliverance across Israel's covenantal history (Deu 4:30; 2Ch 15:4; Hos 5:15) shows grief functioning as a formative instrument across generations — not once but repeatedly, pressing the will toward God in cycle after cycle of adversity and return. The wise heart's choice to dwell in the house of mourning (Ecc 7:2, 7:4) shows grief as a discipline chosen for its formative effect: regular encounter with grief and mortality produces the wisdom that the house of feasting does not.

### 5.5 Anguish and Distress

Anguish and distress moves most characteristically in a single direction: from the person toward God. Of all the characteristics in this family, this one has the largest evidenced body of movement — fifty-six verses in the tsar/tsa.rah corpus alone — and the movement is almost entirely constituted by the distress-drives-prayer pattern. "In my distress I called upon the Lord; to my God I cried for help. From his temple he heard my voice, and my cry to him reached his ears" (Psa 18:6). Distress arrives; the person cries; God hears from his temple and answers. This three-stage sequence is not merely repeated but is the structural form of the covenantal exchange at the level of grief.

What the evidence reveals about this movement is that distress is purposively designed: it is meant to move through to God rather than to remain as a closed inner condition. Job 15:24's image — "distress and anguish terrify him; they prevail against him, like a king ready for battle" — names distress at its most overwhelming: a force that defeats the will's autonomous resistance. But even this extreme form is not the end state: the evidence consistently frames distress as what activates the turn toward God.

**Movement from God**

God extends distress to the person as covenantal discipline. Deu 4:30 frames this with clarity: "When you are in tribulation... you will return to the Lord your God and obey his voice." The distress is not named as accidental but as the means by which God turns the will of the wandering person back toward himself. The movement from God is therefore purposive: God permits or sends distress in order that the will may be turned. 2Ch 15:4 and Hos 5:15 confirm the same pattern — divine-permitted distress producing Godward movement in Israel across the whole sweep of her covenantal history.

**Movement at spirit level**

Anguish and distress is the characteristic in this family that most clearly reaches spirit level — and spirit-level anguish has a distinctive movement pattern. Job 7:11 names it: "I will speak in the anguish of my spirit; I will complain in the bitterness of my soul." When distress reaches the spirit, it can no longer be restrained inwardly — it must express outwardly. The sequence is: anguish arrives at spirit level → restraint becomes impossible → outward speech erupts. Paul's anguish over Israel has a different movement at its depth: "I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart" (Rom 9:2) — sustained, unresolved, held in the heart as an ongoing internal state rather than erupting outward. The spirit-level and heart-level modes of anguish move differently: the spirit-level mode compels outward expression; the heart-level mode can be held within, sustained over time without resolution.

**Formation**

Anguish and distress participates in formation through the covenantal discipline pattern. The recurring sequence of adversity → distress → turning to God → seeking and finding (Deu 4:30; 2Ch 15:4; Hos 5:15) shapes the inner life over the long arc of covenant history. Each cycle of distress that drives the person toward God deepens the habit of Godward turning. Suffering deepens anguish: accumulated losses compound inner distress (Jer 45:3), and the covenantal discipline of divinely-governed suffering tests and produces Godward movement. The distress-prayer-deliverance sequence leaves the person not merely relieved but formed in the habit of bringing the inner life's most acute states to God.

Anguish and distress does not operate in the giving direction toward other persons in its primary evidence. It is a characteristic that is received and experienced, then directed toward God — not extended from person to person in the way that empathic grief is. The evidence is silent on person-to-person transmission for the anguish registers.

### 5.6 Groaning and Sighing

Groaning and sighing moves in the most direct path to God of any characteristic in this family: inner suffering → somatic groan → rises to God → divine hearing and response. This three-stage movement is established most clearly in the Exodus pattern: "the people of Israel groaned because of their slavery and cried out for help. Their cry for rescue from slavery came up to God" (Exo 2:23). The groan is involuntary — it escapes from the inner person under the weight of suffering — but it rises. And God receives it as covenantally significant: "And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob" (Exo 2:24). The groan activates the covenant memory; it is not a meaningless physiological event but a cry that reaches the covenant God.

The heart is the named origin of the groan in its most immediate expression. Psa 38:8 — "I am feeble and crushed; I groan because of the tumult of my heart" — gives the movement its most direct local form: the heart's turmoil produces the groan, which then rises outward and upward. The groan is what the tumultuous heart sounds like when it can no longer remain inward.

**The Spirit's groaning**

The most theologically significant instance of groaning and sighing moves in the reverse direction — from the Spirit within the believer toward God. Rom 8:26 — "the Spirit himself intercedes for us with groanings too deep for words" — names the Spirit joining the believer's spirit at the point where human language has failed. The movement is: believer's weakness → human prayer fails → Spirit steps in → Spirit's own groanings rise to God. The Spirit does not prompt the believer to groan on his own; he himself groans in the believer's place, carrying the inner life's most inexpressible cry to the Father. This is the Spirit's groaning, not the believer's — though it happens within the believer's inner life.

**The creation's groaning**

The whole creation's groaning in Rom 8:22 extends the movement to the cosmic scale: "For we know that the whole creation has been groaning together in the pains of childbirth until now." The groaning is universal and shared — the entire created order joins in the cry. But the direction is eschatological: the groaning is the labour-pain of a creation in travail toward its coming birth. The movement is forward as well as upward — toward the future redemption that the creation's groan anticipates. This is the one instance in this family of characteristics where the groaning itself is both pain and expectation: it is not a closed cry of hopelessness but a travail directed toward what is coming.

**Movement between persons**

Groaning and sighing moves between persons in the register of apostolic travail. Paul's groaning on behalf of the Galatians — "my little children, for whom I am again in the anguish of childbirth until Christ is formed in you!" (Gal 4:19) — is an extension of grief from person to community as an act of love. The travail-form of this movement is generative and directed toward an outcome: not merely grieving for the Galatians but labouring for their formation. Conscience-driven sighing also moves from within the person outward in a morally significant direction: those who sigh and groan over the abominations committed (Eze 9:4) are expressing, through their groan, a moral perception of what is happening around them. The groan marks them as morally alive.

**Formation and deposit**

Suffering produces groaning and deepens it: accumulated losses compound the inner anguish that the groan expresses. Sustained groaning leaves somatic deposit — bones wasting under prolonged sighing (Psa 102:5), bodily strength depleted (Psa 31:10) — and these deposits feed back into deeper inner depletion: the body's deterioration deepens the sense of inner exhaustion, which can deepen the groaning further. This negative feedback is the characteristic's most acute developmental pattern, and the evidence is clear that divine intervention — or the grief running its natural course — is required to interrupt it. Job captures the state of saturation: "For my sighing comes instead of my bread, and my groanings are poured out like water" (Job 3:24). When sighing has replaced food, grief has occupied every available capacity of the inner and outer person.

### 5.7 Pain and Inner Ache

Pain and inner ache works differently from the other characteristics in this family because its primary evidence for movement is silent in most of the directions that the other characteristics evidence clearly. The evidence is explicit about what pain is and what it does to the inner person; it is largely silent about pain as a vehicle for movement between persons or as a catalyst for transformation in the way that distress and sorrow are.

The characteristic's movement is primarily receptive: pain arrives and the person receives it. The servant in Isa 53:3 is "a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief" — the pain is the defining condition of his identity, not the result of his own action but the load he carries in his vocation. Dan 6:20 shows the king crying out "in a tone of anguish" as he approaches the den: the pain of uncertainty and fear is expressed outward as a vocal release, but the movement is not toward God as petition — it is toward Daniel as anxious question. Pain here is expressed rather than offered.

**God's purposive framing**

The evidence's clearest movement claim for pain is God's purposive relation to it. Job 5:18 — "He wounds, but he binds up; he shatters, but his hands heal" — frames pain as something God both permits and resolves, with the same hand doing both. Pain is not outside God's governance; it is within it. This means pain's movement is not only from circumstance to person but from God (who permits or sends it) through circumstance to person, and then — if the pain is held within the covenantal relationship — back toward God in the lament register.

Jer 15:18 shows this movement pressed to its limit: "Why is my pain unceasing, my wound incurable, refusing to be healed? Will you be to me like a deceitful brook, like waters that fail?" The lament is addressed directly to God — pain moving outward as speech to God. The direction is Godward, but the content is accusatory: the person demands to know why the one who should heal has apparently become unreliable. This is the honest form of pain's movement toward God — not suppressed but named, not resolved but spoken. The evidence affirms this movement as legitimate: the lament is addressed to God rather than away from him.

**Silences**

The unceasing pain register and the birth-pangs register do not produce evidenced transformation, do not describe a before-and-after sequence, and do not narrate mechanisms of change. They describe the condition of pain — what it is to carry it, what it does to the person who carries it — without narrating it as a transformative force. This silence is meaningful: not every form of pain in Scripture is presented as producing growth. Some pain is simply pain — endured, borne, and awaiting the promise of its future end. The servant's pain is redemptive, but that redemptive function is not extended in the evidence to pain generally. The unceasing pain of Jer 15:18 and the pain of Isa 13:8's birth-pangs are real and serious, but the evidence does not narrate them as formative or productive.

Somatic deposit is the one developmental consequence the evidence traces for this characteristic. Sustained grief leaves the body marked, and the body's marking feeds back into the inner person's sense of depletion. The purposive framing of Job 5:18 holds this deposit within a frame of divine intention: the hand that permits the wound also heals it.

### 5.8 Bitterness of Soul

Bitterness of soul is, among all the characteristics in this family, the one with the least evidenced movement. It arrives; it settles; it saturates. The verse evidence describes it as a sealed condition rather than a dynamic one — a state the inner person is in rather than one that moves in any particular direction.

**Movement from God**

The primary movement of bitterness in the evidence is from God to the person. Job 9:18 states it plainly: "he will not let me get my breath, but fills me with bitterness." God fills; the person is filled. Lam 3:15 confirms the same: "He has filled me with bitterness; he has sated me with wormwood." The divine filling is total — the person is saturated, with no room for relief. God is the active party; the person is the receiver. The bitterness comes from outside, from above, as a condition God imposes through his dealings with the person.

Esau's foreign wives make life bitter for Isaac and Rebekah — "and they made life bitter for Isaac and Rebekah" (Gen 26:35). The bitterness here comes from human relationships, not directly from God — but it results in the same condition: a pervasive inner quality that colours the whole experience of life. Bitterness can be inflicted relationally as well as divinely.

**The sealed condition**

Once bitterness is in place, the evidence does not describe it moving anywhere. Pro 14:10 — "The heart knows its own bitterness, and no stranger shares its joy" — is the defining statement of bitterness's characteristic posture: sealed within the heart, inaccessible to others, known only to the one who carries it. It does not rise toward God as a groan; it does not overflow outward as tears; it does not take social form as mourning. It sits in the heart.

This sealed quality is the characteristic's most significant feature in terms of working pattern. A foolish son is "a grief to his father and bitterness to her who bore him" (Pro 17:25) — the bitterness is the mother's inner condition as a result of her son's failure. It is given to her by his actions; she receives it; and there the narrative ends. The evidence does not describe what she does with it, how it resolves, or how it moves. The bitterness is named and the evidence closes.

**Silences**

The bitterness corpus is silent on transformation: no sequence of inner states is described, no mechanism of change narrated, no formation arc evidenced. It is silent on the giving direction: bitterness can be inflicted by one person on another (Gen 26:35; Pro 17:25; Col 3:19), but the primary evidence describes it as received rather than given. It is silent on the human-to-God movement: bitterness does not appear in the evidence as something the person brings to God in prayer in the way that distress drives prayer.

What the evidence leaves open is whether bitterness is always an endpoint or whether it can be a transitional state that the other expressions of grief eventually address. The verse evidence examined here describes the received form — bitterness as something that arrives and settles; what happens after bitterness has settled is a question the evidence in this particular corpus leaves without answer.

Col 3:19's warning — "do not be harsh with them" — carries a practical implication: bitterness can be prevented at its relational source. The one who would not embitter another withholds the harshness that produces it. This is the one instance in the evidence where movement around bitterness is possible — not resolving it once it has arrived, but not creating it in the first place.
