**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — **Hebrew primary:** H5545 **sa.lach** (verb, 45V) — to forgive, pardon; root meaning specifically "to forgive" (no concrete-physical etymology like kpr's "cover"). Used exclusively of divine forgiveness in OT. Cognates: H5546 **sal.lach** (adjective, "forgiving" — Psa 86:5 only); H5547 **se.li.chah** (noun, "forgivenesses" — Neh 9:17, Psa 130:4, Dan 9:9). **Greek primary:** G0863H **afiēmi** in forgive-sense (37V; from apo "from" + hiēmi "to send"). Root meaning: "to send away, let go, release"; the forgive-sense extends the release-meaning to sin-debt-release. **Root revelation:** the Hebrew lexicalizes forgiveness as its own distinct category (no concrete-cover etymology); the Greek frames forgiveness within the broader release-vocabulary, providing the conceptual continuity that ties M11-B to M11-C lexically.

---

**T7.1.2 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — sa.lach grammatical range: principally **Qal verbal** ("he forgives" — divine subject); occasional imperatival ("forgive!" — Exo 34:9, Num 14:19). Limited noun-derivatives (se.li.chah). Adjective-derivative (sal.lach) is rare and highly significant (Psa 86:5 names forgiveness-as-character). afiēmi grammatical range: Present, aorist, perfect — full verbal paradigm; passive constructions ("are forgiven" — Mat 9:2 et al.) common; the perfect-passive in 1Jo 2:12 ("have been forgiven") names the ongoing-state. **Revelation:** divine forgiveness is principally verbal (an act done) with limited but key adjective/noun forms naming the character-attribute and the abstract-state.

---

**T7.1.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — sa.lach's semantic range covers: (i) Levitical-priestly-outcome forgiveness (Lev 4–7); (ii) corporate-temple-prayer forgiveness (1Ki 8 corpus); (iii) intercessor-mediated forgiveness (Num 14:19–20); (iv) eschatological-comprehensive forgiveness (Jer 31:34); (v) character-attribute forgiveness (Psa 86:5 sal.lach; Neh 9:17). afiēmi-forgive ranges over: direct pronouncement, reciprocal-interpersonal, conditional, unconditional, eschatological — covering the full NT context. The integrated semantic range establishes divine forgiveness as operating across all OT-NT contexts of guilt-release.

---

**T7.1.4 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes — the vocabulary distinguishes aspects. (i) Disposition vs act: sal.lach (forgiving-as-character disposition; Psa 86:5) vs sa.lach (the act of forgiving). (ii) Received vs given: passive forms ("are forgiven" — Mat 9:2; Lev 4:20) vs active forms ("forgives" — sa.lach Qal). (iii) Specific vs comprehensive: sa.lach in specific cases (Lev 4–7) vs the comprehensive Jer 31:34 ("their sin I will remember no more"). (iv) Condition vs quality: the noun se.li.chah names forgiveness as divine-quality; the verb sa.lach is the act-and-condition.

---

**T7.1.5 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** S — No single antonym-noun for forgiveness in the M11-B corpus. The structural opposite operates through negation: "I will not forgive" (2Ki 24:4); "no forgiveness" (Mat 12:31–32); "blocks one's own forgiveness" (Mat 6:15); "this sin will not be forgiven" (Mat 12:31). The negative pole is grammatically-formed, lexically-absent — consistent with M11-A T7.1.5 finding and the structural reading that the opposite is the closed-state of the characteristic.

---

**T7.1.6 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Person-type terms: at the divine side, **sal.lach** itself (Psa 86:5) is *the forgiving one* — a person-type adjective applied to God. At the human side, the corpus does not provide a single noun for "habitual forgiver"; the Mat 18:21–35 parable describes the role functionally ("a forgiving servant") without lexical-naming. The recipient side has **the forgiven** (1Jo 2:12 "your sins are forgiven" → forgiven-identity) as descriptive role rather than discrete noun.

---

**T7.1.7 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Supplication-vocabulary surrounds divine forgiveness richly. (i) **Plead** — Dan 9:19 "urgent, repeated petition"; Psa 25:11 (specific appeal). (ii) **Pray-toward** — 1Ki 8 corpus uses *palal* (pray) directed at the dwelling. (iii) **Confess + ask** — 1Jo 1:9 confession; Lev 5:5 the seeking-offering pattern. (iv) **Cry out** — psalmic petitions across the corpus. The act of seeking forgiveness is expressed through prayer-verbs + content (confession + appeal), not by a single noun.

---

**T7.1.8 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — **OT-NT relationship reveals deep continuity with christological-distributional change**. (i) Continuity: sa.lach's outcome-position becomes afiēmi's outcome-position in the NT; the Septuagint translates sa.lach principally with afiēmi or its cognates. The concept-architecture carries. (ii) Distributional change: OT sa.lach was *exclusively divine*; NT afiēmi-forgive is *delegated to humans* in the reciprocal pattern (Mat 6:12, 18:21–35) and to the apostolic-ecclesial role (Joh 20:23). The development: the OT-private-divine privilege becomes the NT-shared-divine-and-human practice. (iii) Christological: Mat 9:6 inaugurates the on-earth divine-human-forgiver in Christ. The vocabulary tracks the theological development.

---

**T7.1.9 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — *aphesis* (G0859, M11-C-VCG-01) is a noun-form newly-foregrounded in the NT for forgiveness-of-sins as a salvation-content (Mat 26:28; Luk 1:77; Mar 1:4 et al.). While the word *aphesis* existed in classical Greek (release from prison, remission of debts), its NT deployment for salvation-of-sins is theologically-coined. **Revelation:** the NT period needed vocabulary capable of naming the *new-covenant once-for-all forgiveness* as a noun-concept (Christ as bringer-of-aphesis); this is the lexical correlate of the eschatological-completion noted at T0.4.

---

**T7.1.10 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Full vocabulary arc reveals divine forgiveness as **operating across every register of moral-relational standing-release**, from individual sin-offering outcome to cosmic eschatological resolution. The arc moves from (i) Levitical sa.lach (single sin forgiven); to (ii) corporate sa.lach (temple-prayer response); to (iii) attribute se.li.chah (divine forgiveness-as-character); to (iv) covenant-eschatological sa.lach (Jer 31:34 total + permanent); to (v) Christic afiēmi-forgive (Mat 9:6 earthly authority); to (vi) delegated afiēmi-forgive (Mat 6:12 reciprocal); to (vii) eschatological aphesis (universal availability). The vocabulary arc parallels and supports the theological-development arc.

**T7.2.1 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Primary anchor candidate Jer 31:34 ("their sin I will remember no more"): sa.lach functions as the **declarative-permanent verb** of the new-covenant resolution. The Hebrew word-order and grammar foreground the divine-act decisively; the parallel clause ("their iniquity I will forgive") and the rationale clause ("for I will be merciful") structure the verse as a covenant-promise statement. Argumentatively, this verse functions as the *new-covenant climax of forgiveness vocabulary* — what the recurring OT sa.lach acts could not finally produce, the new-covenant sa.lach act will produce permanently. In Lev 4:20 (the institutional anchor of M11-B-VCG-05), sa.lach functions as the *outcome-verb* of the priestly-atonement sequence: "and the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" — the operative-outcome formula.

---

**T7.2.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Multi-form: **Levitical-priestly instruction** (Lev 4–7, 19; Num 14–15, 30; ~16 verses); **psalm** (Psa 25, 86, 103, 130 — 4 verses); **historical-narrative** (Solomon temple corpus, 1Ki 8 / 2Ch 6 / 2Ch 7:14 — 11 verses); **prophecy** (Jer 5, 31, 33, 36, 50; Isa 55; Eze; Dan 9; Lam 3; Amo 7 — ~14 verses); **gospel-narrative** (Mat 6, 9, 12, 18; Mar 1–4, 11; Luk 1–11, 17, 23; Joh 20 — ~25 verses); **epistle** (Rom 4, 1Jo 1, 2, Jam 5 — 4 verses); **late-OT / historical** (Neh, Dan). **Responsible interpretation requires reading across forms** with their disciplines — narrative requires character-and-event tracking; psalm requires emotional-register attention; prophecy requires eschatological framing; gospel-narrative requires Christological framing; epistle requires doctrinal-argument tracking. The variety reflects M11-B's pervasive theological scope.

---

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Key argument-structures: (i) **Atonement → Forgiveness (Lev 4–7):** *Premise 1:* offerer commits sin. *Premise 2:* priest performs atonement. *Conclusion:* they are forgiven. (ii) **Reciprocal-binding (Mat 6:12, 14–15):** *Premise 1:* God forgives those who forgive. *Premise 2:* You must forgive others to be forgiven. *Conclusion:* failure to forgive forfeits being forgiven. (iii) **Repentance → Forgiveness (Isa 55:7):** *Premise 1:* the wicked forsake ways. *Premise 2:* they return to God. *Conclusion:* abundant pardon awaits. (iv) **Character-grounded forgiveness (Psa 86:5):** *Premise 1:* God is forgiving (character). *Premise 2:* The petitioner asks. *Conclusion:* God responds because of who God is. Each argument runs from condition (sin / hardness / turning / character) through divine response to standing-shift.

---

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Multi-context. **Liturgical-cultic** (Lev 4–7); **covenantal** (Lev 4–7 within Sinai; Jer 31:34 new-covenant); **communal** (Solomon temple corpus); **judicial** (negative pole, 2Ki 24:4); **pastoral / encounter** (Mat 9; Luk 7); **discipleship-training** (Mat 6, 18; Luk 11, 17). The pastoral-encounter and discipleship-training contexts are more developed in M11-B than in M11-A — reflecting forgiveness's status as the *declared-outcome* register that operates throughout the believer's lived experience, not just in institutional moments.

---

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Two strong candidates for primary anchor. (i) **Jer 31:34** — the new-covenant comprehensive-and-permanent forgiveness; theologically the most-developed and forward-pointing. (ii) **Psa 86:5** — forgiveness-as-divine-character ("you, Lord, are forgiving"); reveals the inner-disposition that grounds every operative act. For a *theological-teaching* anchor: Psa 86:5 (character anchor). For a *eschatological-trajectory* anchor: Jer 31:34. For a *NT-direct-encounter* anchor: Luk 7:48 or Mat 9:2. Like M11-A, M11-B has multiple legitimate anchors serving different analytical purposes; the synthesis batch should use Jer 31:34 as the principal headline anchor and Psa 86:5 as the character-anchor.

---

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Jer 31:34 reveals that **divine memory itself is shifted in the new covenant** — God commits not to remember the sin. No other M11-B verse states this so precisely. Psa 86:5 reveals that **forgiveness is God's character-attribute** — God IS forgiving — not a reluctant act. No other verse names this character-anchor so directly. The two anchors together reveal the dispositional source (Psa 86:5) and the eschatological-final outcome (Jer 31:34) of divine forgiveness; the verses between them describe the operative-life within this character and toward this outcome.

**T7.3.1 — Human Science Frameworks**
**[CHAR-2]** E — The most-useful human-science lens for divine forgiveness is **forgiveness psychology** (Worthington's REACH model; McCullough; Enright; the science-extract Section 1 framework). The REACH steps (Recall the hurt, Empathize, offer Altruistic gift, Commit publicly, Hold to forgiveness when doubted) map closely to the M11-B-VCG-02 reciprocal teaching at the human-extending end. McCullough's distinction between forgiveness-as-internal-process and reconciliation-as-relational-restoration corresponds to the M11-B / M11-E boundary. Adjacent useful frames: **moral psychology of guilt** (Tangney; Lewis on adaptive guilt vs maladaptive shame — relevant to T1.5/T1.6 immediate-and-sustained-response findings); **moral identity research** (Aquino & Reed — relevant to T0.3 image-bearer expression).

---

**T7.3.2 — Human Science Frameworks**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Illumination: (i) The **forgiveness-as-internal-process distinction** (McCullough) illuminates the Mat 18:35 "from-the-heart" requirement — the corpus is naming exactly the inner-process distinction that forgiveness research empirically maps. (ii) The **health-benefits of forgiveness** literature (reduced stress, better immune function, improved wellbeing) corroborates the Rom 4:7 "blessedness" finding — forgiveness produces measurable wellbeing in the forgiver. (iii) The **adaptive-guilt-motivates-repair distinction** illuminates the M11-B-T1.5 finding that the immediate response is gratitude + restored confidence, not paralyzing shame. (iv) The **enabling conditions for forgiveness** in the empirical literature (acknowledgement, remorse, empathy, prosocial values) align with the corpus's repentance-as-condition pattern (Mar 4:12; 2Ch 7:14).

---

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Divergences: (i) **Empirical forgiveness research focuses on the forgiver's process**; the corpus emphasizes the **received forgiveness** as primary and the forgiver's extension as derivative-imitation (Mat 18:21–35 — the master's forgiveness comes first, then the servant's is expected). The corpus theological-priority differs from the research empirical-focus. (ii) **Empirical research treats forgiveness as a human-relational process**; the corpus frames it principally as a **divine act** with human imitation. The divine-priority is theologically distinctive and not derivable from empirical frameworks. (iii) **Empirical research is silent on once-for-all eschatological-comprehensive forgiveness**; the corpus's Jer 31:34 / Mar 3:28 universal scope exceeds the research's individual-and-interpersonal frame. The divergences reveal forgiveness's theological excess over its psychological footprint.

---

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks**
**[CHAR-2]** E — Surfacing follow-up: (i) **Cognitive reframing techniques** in forgiveness research (mental reframing of the offender's perspective) — does the corpus address mental-reframing as part of forgiveness? Partially: Luk 23:34 "they know not what they do" is reframing-the-offender — worth Phase E exploration. (ii) **Self-forgiveness research** addresses the inner-experiential side of being-forgiven; the corpus is sparse on the experiential-felt-shape of receiving forgiveness (T1.5 silence-notes corroborate). **Investigation:** Phase E synthesis should attempt to characterize the lived inner-experience of receiving divine forgiveness more fully than M11-B's individual verses do. (iii) **Empirical research on forgiveness-as-skill** (Worthington's emphasis on practice and habit) corresponds to the corpus's Luk 17:3–4 repeated-practice pattern — the corpus expects forgiveness as cultivated discipline, which the empirical frame validates.
