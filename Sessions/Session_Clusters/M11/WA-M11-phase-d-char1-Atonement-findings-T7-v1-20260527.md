**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — **Primary Hebrew:** H3722A **kip.per** (כָּפַר, root kpr — to cover, to pacify, to atone, to ransom). The kpr root carries the underlying senses of (i) cover (Gen 6:14 pitch covering Noah's ark — *kaphar* in the literal sense of overlaying), (ii) pacify (Gen 32:20 Jacob's gift "covering Esau's face" — figurative for appeasement), and (iii) ransom (Exo 30:12 the half-shekel as a *kopher* — a ransom-price for one's life). All three senses operate within atonement-meaning, often simultaneously: the atoning act covers the guilt, pacifies the offended party, and serves as ransom-substitute. **Primary Greek (limited in M11-A):** G0863 **afiēmi** (ἀφίημι, from apo + hiēmi — to send away, let go) — but in M11-A only the permit-sense (G0863I) and only in 2 verses (Mat 27:50, Joh 18:8). The Septuagintal Greek for kip.per is *hilaskomai* (to propitiate), and its noun *hilastērion* (propitiation, mercy-seat) appears in Rom 3:25 / Heb 9:5 — but these terms are not directly in the M11-A verse evidence base. Root-meaning revelation: cover + pacify + ransom is the integrated semantic field; the act is layered.

---

**T7.1.2 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — **kip.per's grammatical range:** principally **verbal** (Piel stem overwhelmingly: "to make atonement, to cover, to atone for") — ~75 of 81 M11-A kip.per verses use the Piel verb-form. The verbal dominance reveals atonement as **an action performed**, not a state-noun or quality-adjective. **Noun-derivatives:** *kopher* (ransom, Exo 30:12), *kapporet* (mercy seat, Exo 25:17 et al. — not in M11-A but in adjacent inventory). **Participial use:** rare but significant (Pro 16:6's atoning-disposition could be read participially). **No adjectival range** — there is no kpr-form meaning "atoning" as an adjective applied to a person. The grammatical revelation: atonement is constitutively act-mode, with derivative noun forms for the *means* of the act (ransom) and the *place* of the act (mercy seat). This corroborates T1.2.1 (atonement is principally an act). The Greek G0863I in M11-A is verbal aorist active — "he released his spirit" (Mat 27:50) — same act-grammar.

**T7.1.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — kip.per's semantic range in M11-A spans: (i) **ritual covering by sacrificial blood** (Lev 4–7 corpus, dominant sense); (ii) **ransom-substitution** (Exo 30:15–16 half-shekel as life-ransom; Lev 17:11 life-for-life); (iii) **interpersonal appeasement** (Gen 32:20 Jacob's gift; Pro 16:14 wise person defusing wrath); (iv) **divine self-atonement** (Psa 65:3 God himself acts; Psa 78:38 divine compassion atones); (v) **eschatological cosmic resolution** (Dan 9:24); (vi) **moral-character disposition** (Pro 16:6 hesed + emet atone); (vii) **negative pole — denied / impossible** (1Sa 3:14; Isa 22:14). The range is broad — ritual to character, interpersonal to cosmic, divine-act to inner-disposition. The Greek G0863I in its M11-A use carries the **willed-surrender / self-substitution** end of the range (Mat 27:50, Joh 18:8). The integrated semantic range positions atonement as the inner-being mechanism for any kind of guilt-defilement-estrangement cover.

**T7.1.4 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes — the vocabulary differentiates aspects. (i) **Disposition vs act:** Pro 16:6 (hesed + emet as inner *disposition* that atones) vs the standard Levitical *act* of kip.per. The same Hebrew root operates across both registers but the grammatical and contextual features distinguish them. (ii) **Received vs given:** the priest's atoning *giving* (Lev 4:20 "the priest shall make atonement for them") vs the recipient's atoning *receiving* ("and they shall be forgiven" — passive forgiveness as received state). The active/passive grammar tracks the given/received distinction. (iii) **Condition vs quality:** *kopher* (ransom-substance — condition of the act) vs *kapporet* (mercy-seat — qualitative location of the act); the noun-derivatives differentiate the means from the place. (iv) **Mechanism vs outcome:** kip.per (act) and sa.lach (declared outcome) are different roots — the cluster's vocabulary architecture distinguishes the mechanism stage from the declared release stage (T6.5.3 finding).

**T7.1.5 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The Hebrew vocabulary does not provide a single antonym-term for kip.per. The structural opposite in M11-A operates through **negation-phrases**: "not be atoned for" (Isa 22:14 "until ye die"); "no atonement" (1Sa 3:14); "cannot be atoned for" (Isa 47:11); "let not their iniquity be blotted out" (Jer 18:23). The negative pole is expressed grammatically rather than lexically — through negated kip.per. This is structurally consistent with T1.3.1's finding: the structural opposite is the *closed state of atonement itself*, not a separate inner-being characteristic with its own vocabulary. The absence of an antonym-term reinforces the corpus's structural read.

---

**T7.1.6 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — a term for the one who habitually possesses or exercises this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes — the **kohen** (priest) is the person-type term for the habitual atoner in OT institutional context. While *kohen* is not lexically kpr-derived, the priestly office is constituted around the atoning function (Lev 16:32 "consecrated priest, clothed in holy garments, is the designated agent who performs atonement"). The kohen-term names the person whose office is to mediate atonement. In NT context, **Christ as the priest-after-Melchizedek** (Heb 5–10) extends the person-type term to the maximal-priestly atoner. **Intercessor / mediator** are functional-descriptor terms (Moses, Phinehas, prophets in M11-A-VCG-05) but not lexically a single noun. **Pro 16:6** suggests an inner-character person-type (the one possessed of hesed + emet) but this is not a single OT lexical term. Person-type vocabulary therefore operates at the *role* level (kohen) and at the *moral-character* level (descriptive phrase) rather than as a unified person-type noun.

---

**T7.1.7 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Supplication-vocabulary surrounds atonement-seeking but is not a single kpr-derived term. The act of seeking atonement is expressed through several locutions: (i) **Active seeking** — 2Sa 21:3 "the king seeks to know what atonement is required" (the Hebrew *baqash* or similar root, generic for seeking, applied to the atonement-context); (ii) **Intercessory plea** — Exo 32:30 Moses "rises to intercede" (the Hebrew *paga*, intervene/intercede); (iii) **Prayer + atonement** — 2Ch 30:18's "prayer for divine pardon"; the *palal* (pray/intercede) family of verbs; (iv) **Confession + offering** — Lev 5:5–6 (immediately preceding M11-A verses): the offerer confesses *and* brings the offering — the offering itself is the act of seeking. There is no single Hebrew noun for "supplication for atonement"; the act is expressed through prayer-verbs + offering-presentation combinations.

---

**T7.1.8 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The OT-NT vocabulary relationship reveals **deep continuity with christological transformation**. (i) **Continuity:** the Septuagint translates kip.per primarily with *hilaskomai* (to propitiate); the kpr conceptual field carries forward into NT theology through *hilastērion* (Rom 3:25 propitiation/mercy-seat) and the broader sacrificial language of Hebrews. The mechanism (life-substitution, mediator, cover) carries. (ii) **Christological transformation:** the M11-A NT verses themselves use afiēmi-permit (G0863I), not hilaskomai — Mat 27:50 / Joh 18:8 frame Christ's act as *willed release / surrender*, integrating the kpr-mechanism with the afiēmi-volitional vocabulary. The NT thus draws the Levitical sacrificial substance and frames the maximal-instance through a different verb-family, signaling that the Christic act both completes and transforms the OT pattern. (iii) **Outcome-vocabulary continuity:** sa.lach (OT divine-forgive) and aphesis (NT forgiveness, M11-C) carry the outcome-stage across the Testaments. The vocabulary arc therefore tracks the typological arc (T0.4): OT instances point toward the Christic fullness; the NT vocabulary integrates and transforms.

**T7.1.9 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes — *hilastērion* (propitiation, mercy-seat, Rom 3:25; Heb 9:5) is the NT-foregrounded term applying the LXX kapporet language to Christ. *Apolytrōsis* (redemption, Eph 1:7 — though M11-A does not directly carry these verses in its evidence base, the adjacent M11-C-VCG-01 corpus does) is a Greek noun-coinage extending the ransom-sense. **What the coinage reveals:** the NT period needed vocabulary capable of holding both the *Levitical-place* (mercy-seat) and the *christological-mechanism* (Christ as the propitiation) — *hilastērion* serves both. This is not strictly a NT coinage in the sense of new-word-not-in-prior-Greek (LXX uses *hilastērion* for kapporet), but it is a NT *deployment* of an existing term to a new theological referent. The mechanism: Christ is the *hilastērion* — the place / act of atonement-mercy combined. The vocabulary thereby compresses OT-place and OT-mechanism into one referent.

**T7.1.10 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The full vocabulary arc reveals atonement as **operative at every register of human-divine moral-relational repair**, with consistent core (life-substitution cover) and flexible form (ritual, character, christological). The arc moves from: (i) the literal *covering* sense (kpr root etymology); through (ii) ritual *blood substitution* (Lev 17:11 anchor); through (iii) *interpersonal appeasement* (Gen 32:20; Pro 16:14); through (iv) *divine self-atonement* (Psa 65:3); through (v) *inner-character disposition* (Pro 16:6); through (vi) *christological self-substitution* (Mat 27:50, Joh 18:8); to (vii) *eschatological cosmic resolution* (Dan 9:24; Col 1:20). The arc's coherence: every register operates by the substitution-mediation logic; every register is divinely-instituted; every register addresses an inner-being condition of guilt-defilement-estrangement. The full range positions atonement as the inner-being characteristic of moral-relational repair, the mechanism through which standing is restored across every scale.

**T7.2.1 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — In its primary verse (Lev 16:30, M11-A-VCG-01 anchor: "atonement on this day to cleanse you; from all your sins you will be clean before the Lord"), kip.per functions as the **operative verb** in the result-clause structure: the act (kip.per) produces the outcome (taher — cleansed) → state (lifne YHWH — before the Lord). The Hebrew sentence-structure (ki ba-yom ha-zeh yekapper aleichem letaher etchem) places kip.per as the mechanism-verb whose object is the recipient and whose result-purpose is the cleansing-before-God. Argumentatively, the verse functions as the *summary statement* of the entire Day of Atonement ritual — it tells the people what the day's elaborate ritual accomplishes inwardly. The verse compresses mechanism + recipient + outcome + relational-result into one sentence. In Lev 4:20 (the M11-A-VCG-02 anchor), kip.per similarly functions as the mediating verb whose subsequent declaration is "they shall be forgiven" — the verb is the pivot of the result-formula.

**T7.2.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — M11-A's primary verse evidence is dominated by **ritual-priestly instruction** (Lev 4–7, 12–17, 19, 23; Num 5, 6, 8, 15, 25, 28–31; Exo 29–30, 32 — ~70 of 83 verses). Secondary forms: **psalm** (Psa 65, 78, 79 — 3 verses; divine-self-atonement), **wisdom** (Pro 16:6, 16:14 — 2 verses; inner-character), **prophecy** (Isa 6, 22, 27, 28, 47; Jer 18; Eze 16, 45; Dan 9; Deu 32 — ~10 verses, mostly eschatological or negative-pole), **historical-narrative** (Gen 32; 1Sa 3; 2Sa 21; 1Ch 6; 2Ch 29, 30 — ~6 verses, interpersonal-or-institutional), **gospel-narrative** (Mat 27:50; Joh 18:8 — 2 verses, christological), **post-exilic** (Neh 10:33 — 1 verse). **Responsible-interpretation requirements:** the ritual-priestly dominance requires reading the texts within their cultic-system context (the priestly procedural detail matters); the prophetic verses require eschatological-framing; the wisdom verses require character-formation framing; the christological verses require typological-fulfillment framing. No single literary form covers atonement; the multi-form evidence base requires reading across forms with their respective interpretive disciplines.

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Three key argument-structures recur. (i) **The Levitical mechanism-argument** (Lev 4:20–6:7 paradigm): *Premise 1:* the offerer has committed sin / incurred guilt. *Premise 2:* the priest performs the prescribed atonement. *Conclusion:* the offerer is forgiven. The argument has the form "act → declared-outcome" and operates as institutional warrant: the priest's mediating act *suffices* to produce the forgiveness. (ii) **The blood-as-life argument (Lev 17:11):** *Premise 1:* the life of the flesh is in the blood. *Premise 2:* God has given blood on the altar to make atonement for the soul. *Premise 3:* the blood makes atonement by reason of the life. *Conclusion:* substitutionary blood-life covers the soul-guilt. This is the most explicit mechanism-explanation in the corpus and serves as theological foundation. (iii) **The atonement-character argument (Pro 16:6):** *Premise 1:* through hesed and emet, iniquity is atoned for. *Premise 2:* by the fear of the Lord, men depart from evil. *Conclusion:* moral character itself functions as atoning mechanism in the inner-being economy. This is the internalization-argument: atonement is not merely ritual but character. Each argument runs from a premise about the human condition through a divinely-given mechanism to a relational-restoration outcome.

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — M11-A's primary verses operate in **multiple overlapping settings**, with liturgical-covenantal as the dominant frame. (i) **Liturgical-cultic:** the Lev 4–7, 16, 12–15 corpus operates in the sanctuary-cult setting; the ritual is liturgical action. (ii) **Covenantal:** the entire Levitical apparatus presupposes and maintains the Sinai covenant; Exo 30:16 "keeps the people in God's remembrance" — atonement is covenant-maintenance. (iii) **Communal:** Num 15:25–26 communal atonement; Lev 16:33 the priestly act extends to the whole assembly; the people share the atoning provision. (iv) **Judicial:** the negative-pole verses (Isa 22:14, 1Sa 3:14, Jer 18:23) operate in judicial settings where guilt is being assessed and the impossibility of atonement is the verdict. (v) **Eschatological:** Dan 9:24 cosmic resolution; Eze 16:63 final-restoration; Mat 27:50 the eschatological-pivot event. **What this reveals:** atonement is not abstractable from its cultic-covenantal-communal home — the corpus consistently embeds it in lived religious institutional context. Even the eschatological and Christic verses derive their meaning from the covenantal-cultic substrate; without the OT framework, the NT atonement-verses would lose their theological weight.

---

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — **Lev 17:11** is the strongest candidate for primary anchor of M11-A, with Lev 16:30 as close secondary. Lev 17:11 is unique in providing the *mechanism-statement*: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it to you upon the altar to make atonement for your souls; for it is the blood that maketh an atonement by reason of the life." This verse alone among M11-A's evidence states *why* atonement operates by life-substitution, *what* it operates on (the soul), and *who* establishes it (God — "I have given"). Lev 16:30 is the canonical summary of Day of Atonement (atonement → cleanness → standing before God) and is the M11-A-VCG-01 anchor. Both verses are essential — Lev 17:11 supplies the mechanism's theological rationale; Lev 16:30 supplies the outcome-and-frame statement. For a single anchor, Lev 17:11 is preferable because it makes the operative principle explicit; for the characteristic-summary anchor, Lev 16:30 is preferable because it captures the institutional-frame.

---

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Lev 17:11 reveals the **theological ontology of the atonement mechanism** — why life-substitution operates as cover for soul-guilt. The verse provides the operative metaphysics: life-resides-in-blood; substitution operates by the substitute's-life replacing the offerer's-life under guilt; the mechanism is *given* (divine institution), not derived (human invention); the target is the *soul* (deepest seat of guilt). No other M11-A verse states this principle so directly. Other verses *operate* by this principle (Lev 4–7 corpus shows it in action); some verses *extend* it (Mat 27:50 christologically; Pro 16:6 internally); the negative-pole verses *fail* against it (Isa 28:18 false-covering exposed by lacking real life-substitute); but only Lev 17:11 articulates the principle as principle. This makes Lev 17:11 the *teaching-verse* of M11-A — the verse from which the characteristic's theological grammar is most clearly read. The corpus presupposes this teaching across many verses; the teaching itself is concentrated in this one.

**T7.3.1 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The most useful human-science lens for atonement is **restorative justice + clinical-moral psychology** (per the M11 science extract §3). (i) **Restorative justice frameworks** (Zehr; Johnstone & Van Ness's restorative-justice scholarship) address the structural question of how moral wrongs are repaired through processes that involve all parties: offender, victim, community. The Levitical-and-Christic atonement structures map closely onto restorative justice's offender-takes-responsibility / mediating-process / community-recognition / victim-restoration sequence. (ii) **Clinical-moral psychology of guilt and shame** (Lewis; Tangney & Dearing) distinguishes adaptive guilt (motivates repair) from maladaptive shame (motivates concealment); atonement-receiving in the M11-A corpus produces an adaptive humility-not-shame response (Eze 16:63) that maps onto this distinction. (iii) **Substitutionary moral repair** is less developed in human science than in theology — most secular frameworks don't have a category for one-party-substituting-for-another. This gap is itself analytically informative (T7.3.4). (iv) **Forgiveness research** (Worthington's REACH model) addresses adjacent territory but is more about the M11-B forgiveness side than M11-A's mediation-mechanism.

---

**T7.3.2 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The human-science frame illuminates three findings. (i) **Adaptive guilt vs maladaptive shame distinction** illuminates Eze 16:63's "confounded humility" — the corpus depicts post-atonement humility as the *adaptive-guilt* type (motivates ongoing right-relating) rather than the *maladaptive-shame* type (motivates concealment). This refines T1.5.1's "silenced humility" finding: the silencing is adaptive-self-knowledge, not self-condemnation-paralysis. (ii) **Restorative-justice mediation framework** illuminates the Levitical priest-role — the priest is the mediating-third-party that restorative justice recognizes as crucial for repair processes; the priestly office reflects the empirical reality that genuine moral repair often requires a qualified mediating agent rather than direct offender-victim engagement only. (iii) **Sacrificial-substitution as moral-repair mechanism** has empirical analogues in restitution-and-reparation studies — partial substitution (financial restitution) is a recognized mechanism; the M11-A theological-substitutional-substitute (the sacrificial life) extends this principle to the level the human-perpetrator cannot supply themselves. The science illuminates by validating the structural logic of mediation-and-substitution as genuine moral-repair mechanisms.

---

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The verse evidence diverges from human-science frameworks at three points, and the divergences reveal atonement's distinctive theological content. (i) **Substitutionary life-for-life:** human-science restorative-justice does not have a category for one entity substituting its life for another's guilt (Lev 17:11; Mat 27:50). Secular frameworks rest on the offender's own restitution or community-engaged repair. The divergence reveals that biblical atonement operates with a category that secular thought treats as theologically-special — not derivable from human-experience alone. (ii) **Divine-as-offended-party + Divine-as-source-of-cover:** human-science frameworks have not developed categories for the offended-party also being the originator and supplier of the repair mechanism — this is theologically distinctive (Psa 65:3 "God himself acts to atone"). The divergence reveals atonement's structural theological asymmetry (T3.7.3 — recipient cannot atone for self) and its christological-resolution in Mat 27:50. (iii) **Eschatological-cosmic scope:** human-science addresses individual-and-community repair; atonement's eschatological-cosmic scope (Dan 9:24; Col 1:20) exceeds the human-science frame's reach. The divergence reveals that biblical atonement is not finally a human-process but a divinely-mediated cosmic-relational mechanism.

---

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The human-science framework surfaces several aspects that warrant verse-investigation follow-up. (i) **Group-and-systemic repair dynamics:** restorative-justice literature attends to how systemic / institutional wrongs are addressed; M11-A's corporate atonement (Lev 16 DoA national; 1Ch 6:49 communal) gestures at this but the corpus operates more at the institutional-religious level than at the systemic-social-injustice level. **Further verse investigation:** does the broader programme address systemic-social atonement adequately? (likely M11-E reconciliation and M26 righteousness carry more weight here). (ii) **Restitution-as-component-of-atonement:** human-science treats restitution as central; M11-A includes restitution (Num 5:8) but not as central — the cover is principally substitutional, not restitutional. **Investigation:** how does the OT relationship between restitution and atonement work? This is a corpus-internal question worth Phase E exploration. (iii) **Therapeutic outcomes of being atoned-for:** human-science can measure psychological outcomes of forgiveness-received; M11-A's outcome-data is doctrinally-stated (forgiven, clean, restored) but the experiential-psychological texture (beyond Eze 16:63's humility) is underspecified. **Investigation:** Phase E synthesis should attempt to characterize the lived inner-experience of being atoned-for more fully than M11-A's individual verses do.
