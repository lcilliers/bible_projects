# M11 Phase D — Char 3 (Release) — T7 findings

**Characteristic:** M11-C Release (char_seq=3) — 20 prompts. Authored 2026-05-27.

---

**T7.1.1 Lexical and Semantic Analysis** **[CHAR-3]** E — Primary Greek terms: **G0859 afesis** (release/forgiveness, noun, 16V) from afiēmi-stem; **G0863 afiēmi** (verb, three sense-rows in M11-C: G0863G leave/abandon 32V, G0863I permit/allow 16V); **G1813 exaleifō** (verb, 4V) — to wipe out / erase. Root analysis: afiēmi (apo + hiēmi) = "to send away, let go"; exaleifō (ex + aleipsō) = "to wipe out from." The roots reveal release-as-separation (afiēmi) and release-as-erasure (exaleifō) — the spectrum from gentle-let-go to definitive-removal.

**T7.1.2** **[CHAR-3]** E — afiēmi: full verbal paradigm; afesis noun (release-as-state-or-event); exaleifō verbal across aorist / future. Range reveals release as principally-action with significant noun-state forms (the released-state itself nameable as afesis).

**T7.1.3** **[CHAR-3]** E — Semantic range spans: release-of-sin (Mat 26:28); release-of-attachment (Mat 4:20 leaving nets); release-as-permission (Mat 19:14 letting children come); release-as-restraint-of-self (Mat 5:40 non-resistance); release-as-erasure (Col 2:14 record cancelled); release-of-grief (Rev 21:4 every tear wiped). The breadth of semantic range is M11-C's defining feature.

**T7.1.4** **[CHAR-3]** E — Vocabulary distinguishes: act vs state (afiēmi verb vs afesis noun); transient vs final (afiēmi-leave is potentially-reversible action; exaleifō-erase is definitive); voluntary vs imposed (the disciples' leaving is voluntary; the eschatological wiping is divine-imposed).

**T7.1.5** **[CHAR-3]** S — No single antonym-term. Negation operates grammatically. Consistent with T1.3.1 finding.

**T7.1.6** **[CHAR-3]** S — Person-type terms are not lexically present for the release-faculty. Functional descriptors exist (the welcomed; the released; the disciple-who-has-left) but not as person-type nouns.

**T7.1.7** **[CHAR-3]** E — Supplication-vocabulary for sin-release receiving: Act 2:38 explicit linkage of repentance-and-baptism with reception; "ask"-vocabulary (1Jo 1:9 confession-and-receiving) — not lexically distinct from M11-B's supplication pattern.

**T7.1.8** **[CHAR-3]** E — OT-NT vocabulary relationship: OT does not principally use afiēmi-equivalent root for release. The Hebrew nasa (to lift/forgive — used in cross-register contexts) and the slch family (M11-B) cover OT release; the afiēmi-family is principally a NT-Greek development for the broader-release register. **Continuity:** the conceptual content is OT-rooted (Levitical release-of-sin via atonement; jubilee-year release via shemittah) even where the lexicon is new. **Development:** NT's afiēmi-family integrates the broader release-modes under one root.

**T7.1.9** **[CHAR-3]** E — afesis is principally a NT-foregrounded noun for forgiveness-of-sins as gospel content (Mar 1:4, Luk 1:77, Mat 26:28). Its noun-form makes the released-state thematizable in a way OT didn't quite achieve.

**T7.1.10** **[CHAR-3]** E — Full arc reveals release as semantic-bridge between cluster-internal characteristics (sin-release, atonement-outcome, eschatological-restoration) and cluster-external characteristics (volitional-detachment for following, eschatological-separation). The breadth makes M11-C the **lexical-bridge** of the cluster.

**T7.2.1 Verse and Literary Interpretation** **[CHAR-3]** E — Primary verse Mat 26:28 ("for the forgiveness of sins"): afesis (genitive case in Greek) functions as the *purpose-clause* — the blood is shed *for the purpose of* release-of-sins. The verse structures release as the *teleological-outcome* of the covenant-blood. Lev 4:20-style sequencing is christologically-compressed here: atonement and release are in one act.

**T7.2.2** **[CHAR-3]** E — Multi-form: gospel-narrative (Mat 26:28; Mat 4:20–22 disciples leaving); epistle (Eph 1:7; Col 1:14; Col 2:14; Heb 9:22, 10:18); apocalyptic (Rev 7:17, 21:4); prophetic (Isa 61:1 echoed in Luk 4:18); wisdom-narrative (Pro 16:14 cross-register); historical (1Cor 7:11–13 marital).

**T7.2.3** **[CHAR-3]** E — Argument structures: (i) **Atonement-for-release** (Mat 26:28; Heb 9:22): blood shed → forgiveness/release enabled. (ii) **Repentance-for-release** (Act 2:38; Mar 1:4): turn → be baptized → for release. (iii) **Discipleship-detachment** (Mat 19:29): leave → follow → eschatological reward. (iv) **Eschatological-consolation** (Rev 21:4): tribulation-completed → divine wipe → restored-state.

**T7.2.4** **[CHAR-3]** E — Contextual settings: Christological-covenantal (Mat 26:28); discipleship-calling (Mat 4:20); kingdom-welcome (Mar 10:14); eschatological-restoration (Rev 21:4); pastoral-instruction (1Cor 7:11–13); judicial-foreclosure (Mar 3:29 negative pole). Diverse contexts; the unifying frame is divine-and-imitative-let-go.

**T7.2.5** **[CHAR-3]** E — Primary anchor candidates: **Mat 26:28** (christological-blood-release foundational); **Rev 21:4** (eschatological-completion anchor); **Mat 4:20–22** (discipleship-paradigm). For the M11-primary core: Mat 26:28. For the full sub-group breadth: no single anchor covers all; multiple anchors are necessary.

**T7.2.6** **[CHAR-3]** E — Mat 26:28 reveals release as the **teleological-purpose** of the new-covenant blood; this is the verse where the OT atoning-release pattern is Christologically-integrated into the once-for-all act. Rev 21:4 reveals release reaching the **eschatological-totality** — release of grief itself, not just sin.

---

**T7.3.1 Human Science Frameworks** **[CHAR-3]** E — Multiple frames. (i) **Detachment psychology** (Buddhist-influenced contemporary clinical psychology; mindfulness-based therapies) addresses the discipleship-leaving register — letting-go-of-attachments is a recognized therapeutic-and-spiritual practice. (ii) **Forgiveness research** addresses the sin-release register (same as M11-B T7.3.1). (iii) **Bereavement and grief research** (Bonanno, Klass) addresses the tear-wiping register — though the eschatological-completion exceeds the empirical frame. (iv) **Restorative justice** for cancellation-of-record (Col 2:14 legal-debt nailed-to-cross has restorative-justice resonance).

**T7.3.2** **[CHAR-3]** E — Detachment psychology illuminates the Mat 19:29 leaving-for-following — the empirical literature shows that capacity-to-detach-from-secondary-attachments-for-primary-commitment is associated with mature spiritual / moral development. Restorative-justice illuminates the Col 2:14 record-cancellation. Grief-research illuminates the Rev 21:4 tear-wiping by mapping the inner topology of unprocessed grief, which the eschatological-act addresses comprehensively.

**T7.3.3** **[CHAR-3]** E — Divergences. (i) **Detachment psychology is neutral on what one detaches FOR**; the corpus is specifically detach-FOR-following-Christ — the theological telos differs. (ii) **Grief research treats grief-resolution as gradual-personal-process**; the eschatological tear-wiping is divinely-imposed, comprehensive, and final — exceeds the empirical frame. (iii) **Restorative justice operates between human parties**; the cosmic Christic-record-cancellation (Col 2:14 nailed-to-cross) is theologically-distinctive.

**T7.3.4** **[CHAR-3]** E — Surfacing follow-up: (i) The empirical literature on **letting-go as cultivable skill** (detachment psychology) suggests the corpus's discipleship-leaving may be enhanced by skill-development; M11-D shuv-recall practice may complement. (ii) Grief-research's **continuing-bonds vs detachment-from-deceased** distinction may illuminate eschatological-restoration's relationship to memory — does the redeemed remember pre-restoration grief? Worth Phase E reflection.
