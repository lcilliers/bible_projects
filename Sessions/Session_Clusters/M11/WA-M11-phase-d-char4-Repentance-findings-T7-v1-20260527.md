# M11 Phase D — Char 4 (Repentance and turning) — T7 findings

**Characteristic:** M11-D — 20 prompts. Authored 2026-05-27.
**Science extract:** Section 2 (Repentance, regret, and behavioural change)

---

**T7.1.1 Lexical Roots** **[CHAR-4]** E — **H5162H na.cham** (root nḥm) — be sorry / comfort / relent / change-of-mind. The root spans grief-comfort and change-of-mind senses (the same root produces nōḥam comforter at other contexts). **H7725N shuv** (root šwb) — to turn, return, restore — physical-and-figurative return; the most common Hebrew verb for repentance. **H5749A ud** — to return / sustain (rare). Root revelation: the dual root-architecture maps the dual-register of M11-D (na.cham = the affective-cognitive side; shuv = the volitional-directional side).

**T7.1.2** **[CHAR-4]** E — na.cham: Niphal "to be sorry / repent / relent" — middle-voice grammar suiting the inner-felt-state. shuv: Qal verb of physical-or-spiritual return. Both ranges principally verbal with limited noun-derivatives.

**T7.1.3** **[CHAR-4]** E — na.cham semantic range: divine grief over creation (Gen 6:6); divine relenting from judgment (Exo 32:14); human contrition (Job 42:6); the verb's range is wide. shuv range: physical return; covenantal-return; inner-heart-turning; the breadth covers many contexts.

**T7.1.4** **[CHAR-4]** E — Vocabulary distinguishes affective (na.cham) from volitional-directional (shuv). The two together capture turning's whole shape — felt and willed.

**T7.1.5** **[CHAR-4]** S — No single antonym-term. Negation operates grammatically.

**T7.1.6** **[CHAR-4]** S — No single person-type term for "the turning one."

**T7.1.7** **[CHAR-4]** E — Supplication-vocabulary integrates with turning: 1Ki 8:47–48 "turn... pray... confess" — the turning includes the prayer-supplication element. shuv frequently appears with verbs of prayer.

**T7.1.8** **[CHAR-4]** E — OT-NT continuity: shuv → epistrephō / metanoeō in the LXX/NT. The NT primary verbs for repentance are metanoeō (change-of-mind) and epistrephō (turn-back) — note these are NOT in M11-D's evidence base (M11-D is OT-dominant); NT-repentance vocabulary largely sits in adjacent clusters or in cross-register flags. **Significance:** the M11-D corpus is OT-anchored; NT-repentance has its own development but is conceptually-continuous.

**T7.1.9** **[CHAR-4]** E — *metanoia* (the noun "change of mind / repentance") is NT-foregrounded. Its emphasis on the inner-mental shift complements the OT directional emphasis (shuv).

**T7.1.10** **[CHAR-4]** E — Full vocabulary arc reveals turning as **cognitive-affective-volitional-directional** — the multi-faceted inner act of moral re-orientation. The OT-Hebrew bias is toward directional (shuv); the NT-Greek bias is toward cognitive (metanoia). Together they cover the full faculty.

---

**T7.2.1 Verse Function** **[CHAR-4]** E — Lam 3:21 "this I recall to my mind, therefore have I hope" — shuv functions as the *pivot-verb* turning grief into hope; the verse structures recall as the inner-act that reorients the felt-state. 1Ki 8:47–48 — shuv functions as the *first-verb in the sequence*: turn → consider → return → pray → confess. The turning-act anchors the sequence.

**T7.2.2** **[CHAR-4]** E — Multi-form: historical-narrative (1Ki 8 / 2Ch 6); psalm (Psa 106:45); prophecy (Jer; Joe; Jon; Amo; Eze; Isa); wisdom (Job 42:6); lamentation (Lam 3:21); covenantal-instruction (Deu 4, 30). Wide form range; responsible interpretation requires form-attention.

**T7.2.3** **[CHAR-4]** E — Argument structures: (i) **Turning-enables-mercy** (Jer 18:8; Joe 2:13–14): condition (turning) → response (relenting); (ii) **Memory-grounds-turning** (Deu 30:1; Lam 3:21): recall → turning → restoration; (iii) **Absence-of-turning-forecloses-mercy** (Jer 8:6; Mar 4:12 cross-evidence): no turning → no mercy.

**T7.2.4** **[CHAR-4]** E — Contexts: covenantal (Deu 30); exile / restoration (1Ki 8); prophetic-warning (Jer; Joe); narrative-judgment-and-mercy (Jon); personal-encounter-with-God (Job 42).

**T7.2.5** **[CHAR-4]** E — Anchor candidates: **Joe 2:13** (character-grounded relenting — the most-theologically-developed); **2Ch 7:14** (the humility-prayer-seeking-turning canonical sequence); **Lam 3:21** (the recall-to-hope pivot). For M11-D's principal anchor: Joe 2:13 (or 2Ch 7:14 alternate). For the recall-mechanism specifically: Lam 3:21.

**T7.2.6** **[CHAR-4]** E — Joe 2:13 reveals that **divine relenting is character-grounded** — turning produces relenting *because of who God is*, not as a transaction. This grounds the entire turning-mercy logic in God's hesed-character. No other M11-D verse states this so directly.

---

**T7.3.1 Human Science Frameworks** **[CHAR-4]** E — Most useful frames (per science extract Section 2): **regret psychology** (Zeelenberg, Pieters — regret as counterfactual emotion motivating change); **transtheoretical model of behavioural change** (Prochaska, DiClemente — precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance — maps closely to the inner-stages of repentance); **motivational interviewing** (Miller, Rollnick — relational conditions facilitating change). The shuv "about-face" structure is empirically validated by behavioural-change science.

**T7.3.2** **[CHAR-4]** E — Illumination: (i) the transtheoretical stages illuminate the multi-step inner sequence of M11-D's repentance (precontemplation = pre-recognition; contemplation = recognition; preparation = contrition; action = volitional turn; maintenance = sustained turned-orientation). (ii) Motivational-interviewing illuminates the prophetic-call pattern — the prophet's call works when the relational conditions allow the hearer to engage. (iii) Regret-research illuminates the na.cham contrition register — regret directed at changeable past actions motivates change, paralleling biblical contrition's productive-grief.

**T7.3.3** **[CHAR-4]** E — Divergences: (i) Behavioural-change science focuses on individual outcomes; biblical repentance is principally relational (toward God) and includes covenantal-corporate dimensions. (ii) Science treats motivation as psychological; biblical repentance includes divine-promp (Act 5:31 repentance-as-gift). (iii) Science is silent on divine-relenting-as-response; biblical content is principal at exactly this point.

**T7.3.4** **[CHAR-4]** E — Surfacing follow-up: (i) Behavioural-change literature on **sustaining change over time** parallels the corpus's recurring need for renewed turning (Luk 17:3–4 cross-evidence; the repeated character of repentance). (ii) **Habits-and-character** research (Aristotelian-renewal in contemporary moral psychology) parallels the corpus's character-formation through repeated turning.
