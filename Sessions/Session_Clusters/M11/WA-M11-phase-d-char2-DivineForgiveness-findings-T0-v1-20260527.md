# M11 Phase D — Char 2 (Divine forgiveness) — T0 findings

**Cluster:** M11 — Repentance, Forgiveness and Restoration
**Characteristic:** M11-B Divine forgiveness (char_seq=2)
**Tier:** T0 — Divine Image (12 prompts)

---

**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The M11-B corpus reveals divine forgiveness as constitutive of God's character itself — not merely an act God performs but a stable inner disposition. Psa 86:5 names this directly: "God's readiness to forgive is presented as a stable inner character trait — 'forgiving' is who God is, not just what he occasionally does." Neh 9:17 reinforces: "Despite Israel's persistent stiff-necked rebellion, God's nature as one 'ready to forgive' is his defining inner disposition — mercy not exhausted by repeated failure." Dan 9:9: "Forgiveness is acknowledged as belonging to God's very nature." Psa 103:3 lists forgiveness of all iniquity as a defining benefit of God's relationship to the person. Exo 34:9 (intercessory plea) appeals to gracious character as the basis of pardon. The character revealed: God IS forgiving, and the forgiveness extended in time is the temporal-expression of this character. M11-B reveals the inner disposition; M11-A reveals the mechanism by which it operates within history.

---

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Forgiveness is the most directly God-attributed of the M11 cluster characteristics. (i) **As character attribute:** Psa 86:5 ("forgiving"), Neh 9:17 ("ready to forgive"), Dan 9:9 ("forgivenesses are God's"), Psa 103:3 ("forgives all your iniquity"). The Hebrew vocabulary explicitly carries the attribute-form: *sal.lach* (H5546) is the adjectival "forgiving-one" character; *se.li.chah* (H5547) is the noun "forgivenesses" attributed to God. (ii) **As authoritative act:** the NT corpus (Mar 2:5/7/10; Luk 5:21/23/24) explicitly limits forgiveness-authority to God ("only God can forgive sins"); Mat 9:6 Jesus exercises this divine authority, demonstrating his identity. **Significance for the human person:** that God forgives establishes (a) the moral grammar of guilt-and-release as real (forgiveness assumes guilt as real); (b) the relational possibility of being-no-longer-held-against (the human person can stand not under what they have done); (c) the imitative imperative (Mat 6:12, 18:21–35 forgive as forgiven). The divine attribution gives the human characteristic both its ground and its model.

---

**T0.1.3 — Divine Nature Reflected — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The M11-B corpus is not silent on God's possession of forgiveness — it is the most-attributed-to-God of the cluster characteristics. The narrower silence worth naming: Scripture does not depict God as needing or receiving forgiveness from another party. The attribution is unidirectional — God forgives; God is not forgiven. This asymmetry confirms M11-B as a creaturely-directional characteristic in its directionality even where God is its agent. The act of forgiving belongs to divine character; the state of needing-forgiveness belongs to creaturely condition. This constrains Image-Bearer Expression (T0.3): humans bear the image as forgivers-of-others and as recipients-of-divine-forgiveness, not as those-whom-God-must-be-forgiven-by.

---

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Divine forgiveness equips the person to **be released from guilt's standing-weight and to enter restored covenant relating**. The verses repeatedly name the outcome-state: "released," "forgiven," "blessed are those whose lawless deeds are forgiven and sins covered" (Rom 4:7). At the human-extending level (M11-B-VCG-02), forgiveness equips the person to *forgive others* (Mat 6:14–15; Mat 18:35 "heart forgiving"; Luk 17:3–4 repeated forgiveness). At the relational-cleansing level (Mat 18:27 "compassion driving... debt released"), forgiveness equips the inner-person to release another's debt as a structural inner-act. Psa 25:11 and 1Jo 1:9 equip the person to live freely-petitioning under their own remaining failure. The created-purpose cluster: forgiveness equips the person to live unburdened-by-record, to extend the same to others, and to receive ongoing through honest confession.

---

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Divine forgiveness is **principally response-to-fallen-condition** in its operational form, but its **underlying mechanism (releasing-of-debt) participates in God's pre-fall character** (steadfast love, hesed). Every M11-B verse presupposes a guilt / sin / iniquity / transgression that requires release — there is no M11-B verse depicting forgiveness exercised in an unfallen condition. The Levitical pattern (VCG-05) addresses sin offerings; the Lord's Prayer (Mat 6:12) presupposes ongoing daily debts; the temple-prayer corpus (VCG-06) repeatedly addresses national sin. But Psa 86:5's "forgiving" character is presented as God's intrinsic disposition — not invented in response to the fall but pre-existing it; what changes after the fall is the *occasion* for the disposition to operate, not the disposition itself. So: M11-B's operational form is fall-response; its character-ground is pre-fall divine character. This mirrors M11-A's finding (T0.2.2 for char 1).

---

**T0.2.3 — Created Purpose — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes — M11-B's eschatological orientation is explicit. (i) **New covenant total forgiveness:** Jer 31:34 "God will forgive iniquity completely and remember sin no more — pardon is total, removing guilt from divine memory permanently"; Jer 50:20 "forgiveness here is so complete that iniquity and sin become unfindable... no trace of guilt remaining." These are future-fullness statements pointing to a coming state. (ii) **Already-forgiven assurance:** 1Jo 2:12 "sins are already forgiven through the name of Christ grounds the inner identity... guilt is already released, not conditionally pending." This is realized-eschatology — the future has broken into the present in Christ. (iii) **Cosmic scope:** Mar 3:28 "All sins and blasphemies of humanity fall within the scope of forgiveness — the verse asserts the breadth of divine release." (iv) **Limited present:** M11-B-VCG-09 (new covenant / total pardon) is the eschatological-anchor VCG. The trajectory points from present-recurring petitionary forgiveness to future-final-comprehensive forgiveness.

---

**T0.3.1 — Image-Bearer Expression — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Divine forgiveness expresses the divine image in the human person through **the capacity to release another from a debt against oneself**. Mat 6:12 / Mat 18:21–35 establish this directly: humans are called to forgive as they are forgiven; the parable of the unforgiving servant (Mat 18:27 "compassion driving... debt released") names the structural-imitation expected. The image-aspect instantiated is *imitatio Dei* — the human releasing of another's offense participates in the divine character of being-forgiving (Psa 86:5). When a person forgives, they image God; when they refuse to forgive (Mat 6:15 / Mat 18:35), they refuse the image. Pro 16:6's hesed-and-emet character (M11-A cross-evidence) extends this: the inner character of steadfast love + faithfulness *is* the image-bearing disposition that operates in the atoning-forgiving mode. The image is borne by capacity-to-release; the image is disabled by withholding-release.

---

**T0.3.2 — Image-Bearer Expression — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows forgiveness as **genuinely shared with structural asymmetry**. (i) **Shared:** humans are called and empowered to forgive (Mat 6:12; Luk 17:3–4; Mat 18:21–35); the human-forgive act is *real* forgiveness, not merely analogous. Joh 20:23 extends authority: "Forgiveness of sins is presented as an act that can be extended or withheld — a delegated moral authority over the release of guilt." Humans actually exercise a real moral authority that participates in the divine. (ii) **Asymmetric:** only God forgives sins at the level of absolute-moral-standing-before-God (Mar 2:7 "who can forgive sins but God alone"); humans forgive offenses-against-themselves at the level of interpersonal-standing. The two operate in different jurisdictions. (iii) **Christic resolution:** Mat 9:6 Christ exercises the divine authority on earth — the asymmetry is resolved in the incarnate Mediator. (iv) **Reciprocity built-in:** Mat 6:14–15 binds the two — human forgiveness is conditioned on divine; divine is conditioned on human (Mat 6:15 explicit). The shared-with-asymmetry structure makes forgiveness one of the most participational of the divine attributes.

---

**T0.3.3 — Image-Bearer Expression — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Presence and absence are both diagnostic. **Presence:** when a person forgives genuinely from the heart (Mat 18:35 "heart forgiving... inner disposition"), the divine image is functionally active — the person operates in the inner mode that mirrors God's character. The Mat 18:27 master shows that compassion driving release is the image active. **Absence:** when a person refuses to forgive (Mat 6:15 "Refusing to forgive others' trespasses blocks one's own forgiveness — a closed heart... produces a closed divine response, making inner withholding of pardon self-damaging"; Mat 18:35 "failure to forgive from the heart disqualifies one from receiving the Father's forgiveness"), the image is disabled or refused. The Mat 18 unforgiving-servant parable's verdict is sharp — the servant's refusal-to-extend after having received cancels the standing he had been granted. **Negative pole (VCG-04 foreclosed):** Mat 12:31–32 (blasphemy against Spirit) names an inner condition beyond which the image-bearing capacity has structurally closed. The divine image in a person is therefore directly read from their forgive-or-refuse-to-forgive operational state.

---

**T0.4.1 — Typological Significance — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate (covenantal, eschatological, christological)?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes — three typological deployments are evidenced. (i) **Covenantal:** the Solomon temple-prayer corpus (VCG-06; 1Ki 8:30–50; 2Ch 6:21–39) operates typologically — every act of divine forgiveness within the covenant points to the covenant's continuing operation; the temple is the typological place of divine response to repentance. (ii) **Eschatological:** Jer 31:34 (new covenant total forgiveness) is typologically future-pointing — present pardon anticipates the comprehensive new-covenant pardon; Jer 50:20 (sin unfindable) extends this; Mar 3:28 (universal scope of forgivable sin) anticipates the eschatological fullness. (iii) **Christological:** the NT-direct declarations (VCG-01) deploy forgiveness typologically — Christ's earthly authority to forgive (Mat 9:6) prefigures and inaugurates the cross-effected forgiveness (Mat 26:28 in M11-C; afesis through covenant blood). Rom 4:7 (Pauline-Davidic typology) makes this explicit: David's blessedness anticipates the gospel-pardoned blessedness. The typological use is dense.

---

**T0.4.2 — Typological Significance — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Both directions are evidenced, with **structural priority to the divine pattern**. (i) **Divine establishes pattern:** Psa 86:5's "forgiving" character is established as God's character; Mat 6:12 instructs humans to forgive *as we have been forgiven by God* — the divine pattern is the model. Mat 18:21–35 the unforgiving-servant parable explicitly contrasts the master's (God's) magnanimous forgiveness with the servant's (human's) failure — the divine pattern is the standard. (ii) **Human pattern points toward divine:** the Solomon temple-prayer corpus (1Ki 8:30 etc.) shows human petitioners directing prayer toward divine response — the human-side practice typologically anticipates and depends on the divine response. (iii) **Reciprocal binding:** Mat 6:14–15 bind the two directions structurally — human forgiveness becomes the visible-evidence of divine forgiveness received. The structural priority is unidirectional in source (God establishes; humans imitate) but bidirectional in temporal-typological flow (human practice points to divine; divine response makes human practice meaningful).

**[CHAR-2]** S — Typological use is explicitly evidenced (see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt registers S as catalogue fail-safe; the operative finding is at T0.4.1.
