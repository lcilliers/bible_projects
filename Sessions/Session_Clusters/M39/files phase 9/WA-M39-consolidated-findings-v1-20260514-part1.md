## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** God's character as gracious is constitutive, not episodic. The Exo 34:6 formula — "the Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness" — is not a description of what God does on occasion but of what God is by self-declaration. Group 2330-001 (all 13 chan.nun verses) sustains this portrait: the formula recurs in Psa 86:15; 103:8; 111:4; 116:5; 145:8; Joe 2:13; Jon 4:2, naming graciousness as the stable identity of God across narrative, prophetic, and psalmic contexts. Within M39-A, ba.rakh group 1299-001 (Gen 1:22; 1:28; 2:3) shows that blessing is the first outward act of this character — God blesses at creation before any human need arises. Charis group 888-001 (Eph 2:8; 2Cor 12:9) locates this character in its NT fullness: grace is sovereign, transforming, and sufficient in weakness. The character of God reflected here is: initiating, abundant, undeserved, personally engaged, and structurally prior to any human response.

**[B — Goodness]** The goodness characteristic also reflects divine character, but in its evaluative and moral register. The verse evidence in M39-B names goodness as what God affirms and approves: 1Ki 8:18 ("you did well that it was in your heart") shows God recognising and endorsing the quality of inner goodness before the act. The divine goodness-affirmation is morally precise — God evaluates the heart, not merely the outcome. The character reflected: God as moral evaluator whose own goodness sets the standard for what is affirmed as good.

**[CLUSTER]** Both sub-groups converge on a single underlying divine character truth: God is constitutively good and constitutively gracious. These are not two separate attributes but two aspects of the same inner divine character — the one gracious toward the lesser (M39-A) and the one who is and affirms the good (M39-B). The verse evidence does not separate them: Psa 116:5 pairs "gracious" and "righteous"; Psa 145:8 pairs "gracious" with "steadfast love"; Gen 4:7 connects doing well with being accepted.

The significance for the human person: because grace originates in God's character and not in human merit, the human person's reception of blessing is not an achievement but a constitutive gift. The human person exists as the recipient of what God initiates — the inner posture of dependence and supplication (the dominant pattern of cha.nan group 984-001) is the appropriate inner orientation precisely because the characteristic belongs first to God.

**[B — Goodness]** Yes — the attribution appears in the tov and ya.tav data most clearly in the divine affirmation-of-good pattern (1Ki 8:18; 2Ki 10:30; Deu 18:17) and in the divine goodness-giving (Eze 36:11 — "I will do more good to you than ever before"). The significance: goodness in the human person is not self-generated but derived — God is the source of good, and the human person's inner goodness (tov in the heart, ya.tav in moral orientation) participates in and reflects what God is. The person formed in goodness images the good God.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Ba.rakh group 1299-001 (Gen 1:22; 1:28; 2:3) establishes that blessing equips the creature for fruitfulness, multiplication, and dominion — the purposes of the creation mandate. The blessing at creation is not salvific (no fall has occurred); it is generative. Grace at the creation level equips the person to receive and inhabit what God gives. The grace-characteristic in its created-purpose form is the inner posture of receptivity — the creature constituted as recipient of divine generosity and thereby freed to be fruitful. The cha.nan supplication corpus (984-001) shows the sustained form of this: the person oriented toward God in dependence, which is the relational posture appropriate to the creature.

**[B — Goodness]** The goodness characteristic equips the person to recognise, embody, and extend what is genuinely good. Gen 4:7 is the foundational verse: doing well (ya.tav) leads to acceptance; not doing well opens the door to sin. The created purpose of goodness is the inner moral orientation from which right action flows — it equips the person to act in the world in a way that coheres with the divine evaluation of what is good. Pro 15:13 and 17:22 show the somatic consequence: the good/glad heart heals; it equips the person physically for sustained life. The created purpose: goodness equips the person to be morally coherent — to do what is genuinely beneficial — and to sustain the inner vitality that flows from that coherence.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Both, with temporal distinction. The ba.rakh blessing in Gen 1:22; 1:28; 2:3 is definitively pre-fall — God blesses creation and the Sabbath before any human failure. The creation-blessing is not remedial. However, much of the grace-vocabulary (particularly charis in the salvation register — Eph 2:8; Rom 3:24; Gal 2:21) is explicitly a response to the fallen condition: grace saves those who are dead in trespasses (Eph 2:5). The cha.nan supplication corpus (984-001) operates in contexts of affliction, distress, and need — overwhelmingly post-fall situations. The characteristic operates across both registers: blessing as created-design generativity (pre-fall), grace as saving response to guilt and need (post-fall), and ongoing favour as the continuing expression of the created relationship (both).

**[B — Goodness]** The goodness characteristic appears in both registers. Psa 119:71 ("it is good for me that I was afflicted") is explicitly post-fall: goodness-evaluating-affliction is a fallen-condition response. But the created-design dimension is also present: 1Ki 8:18 affirms the goodness of a heart-intention before the act — the inner goodness of orientation is a created-design quality. The volitional preference idiom (632-003 — "what seems good to you") operates as a created human faculty that precedes the fall conceptually, even if the examples are post-fall situations.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The grace/blessing characteristic expresses the image in two directions. First, the human capacity to receive grace without generating it — the creature is constituted as a recipient of undeserved generosity, which images the one who gives. Second, the propagating form: the grace-characteristic is transmitted through human agents. The cha.nan generosity texts (Psa 37:21; 37:26 — "he is ever lending generously, and his children become a blessing") and the charizō forgiveness corpus (Eph 4:32; Col 3:13) show that received grace generates extended grace. The image is expressed in this propagation: the human person who forgives as God forgives is imaging the gracious character of God. The priestly-blessing group (1299-003 — Num 6:24) is the most formalised form: the human mediating divine grace to others.

**[B — Goodness]** The goodness characteristic expresses the image through the capacity for moral evaluation and alignment with what is genuinely good. 1Ki 8:18 — God affirms David's heart-goodness — shows that the inner moral quality is recognisable to God and reflects his evaluative character. The person whose heart is genuinely good is imaging the divine evaluator. Pro 17:22 (the glad/good heart as good medicine) suggests the image is not merely formal but vital — the good inner state produces healing, which images the divine generativity of goodness.

**[CLUSTER]** The two sub-groups together instantiate complementary aspects of the divine image: the capacity to receive and extend grace (M39-A) and the capacity for inner moral goodness that coheres with the divine evaluation of what is good (M39-B). Together they express the image of a God who is both gracious toward the lesser and constitutively good.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Yes, extensively. **[B — Goodness]** Partial evidence. However, the M39-B verse set does not contain a developed christological typology for the goodness characteristic in the way M39-A does for blessing/grace.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster's dominant typological movement is christological, carried by M39-A. The Abrahamic blessing-to-nations pattern (1299-001) is the most extensively developed OT typology, fulfilled in the NT grace-gospel corpus.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Naming

**T1.1.1** — What name does the programme give this characteristic, and what does that name reveal about its nature?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The cluster is named "Blessing, Favour and Grace" (the cluster description). Within the programme, M39-A carries the sub-group label "Blessing and Grace." The name reveals: (1) "Blessing" names the comprehensive act — the sovereign declaration that constitutes flourishing (ba.rakh as the dominant OT term); (2) "Grace" names the inner disposition and relational mode — the freely given, unmerited quality of the characteristic (charis/chen/charitoō); (3) "Favour" bridges them — the relational standing that blessing produces and grace constitutes. **[B — Goodness]** The sub-group label is "Goodness." The name is a quality-word — it names what something or someone is, not what they do. Tov and ya.tav are the broadest positive evaluative terms in Biblical Hebrew; the English "goodness" captures the moral-quality register. The name reveals: the characteristic is constitutive of character, not merely episodic in action.

**T1.1.2** — What does the name signify in the original language vocabulary — what does the word family or root meaning add to the programmatic name?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Three root families converge in M39-A: (1) BARAK (H1288 ba.rakh) — the root is shared with "knee" (H1290 be.rekh), suggesting the bowing/kneeling associated with blessing; the act of blessing involves an orientation of deference or reverence even when God is the agent blessing the creature. (2) CHANAN/CHEN (H2603A/H2580) — root meaning: "to bend toward, to show partiality; the inclination of the greater toward the lesser." Grace as an inclination — a turning toward. (3) CHAR- (G5485 charis; G5483 charizō; G5487 charitoō) — root shared with chairo (to rejoice); grace and joy share a root, naming grace as joyful giving. The science extract (Section 1) confirms: the psychological intensity of gratitude is amplified when the benefactor acted at personal cost — the root meanings encode this structure.

**[B — Goodness]** Two terms: (1) TOV (H2895) — one of the most semantically broad terms in Biblical Hebrew; spans aesthetic goodness (pleasant, beautiful), moral goodness (right, excellent), relational goodness (well-off, content), and divine goodness (what God evaluates as good). The breadth is the theological point: goodness in the biblical frame is unified, not compartmentalised. (2) YA.TAV (H3190) — the verbal form of goodness — doing good, being made good, going well. The verb form makes explicit what tov implies: goodness is both a quality possessed and an action enacted.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is this — disposition, state, act, condition, orientation, or other?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** M39-A contains all four major kinds, distributed across its groups: (1) Disposition: the settled gracious character of God (2330-001 — chan.nun), chen as the gracious disposition toward another (889-001; 889-002), charis as God's sovereign disposition (888-001). (2) Act: ba.rakh as the enacted blessing-declaration (1299-001; 1299-003), charizō as the grace-giving act (5470-001; 5470-002), cha.nan as the act of being gracious (984-001). (3) Completed state/condition: charitoō (5471-001 — the kecharitōmenē standing in grace) and charis in 888-002 (the sphere of grace in which the believer stands). (4) Inner pleasure: ra.tsah (795-001; 795-002) as the divine inner delight and acceptance — closest to the affect register. The characteristic is multi-kind: grace is simultaneously what God is (disposition), what God does (act), and the sphere the recipient inhabits (condition).

**[B — Goodness]** Also multi-kind: (1) Quality/disposition: tov and ya.tav as the inner character quality of the person who is good (542-001; 632-002). (2) Affective state: the glad/merry heart (632-001) — an inner affective state. (3) Evaluative act: the approval and volitional-preference register (632-003; 542-001's moral-approval pole) — the inner judgment that finds something good. Goodness is what the person is (quality), what the person feels (affective state), and what the person evaluates (judgment act).

**[BOUNDARY]** Structural characterisation per §11.6: Three BOUNDARY terms serve distinct structural roles in the cluster economy: (1) G1435 dōron (gift/offering) — delivery mechanism: dōron names the material vehicle through which the grace-characteristic is enacted; the inner-being content is carried by the surrounding theological framework, not by dōron itself. (3) H2868 te.ev (be good — Aramaic) — affective gladness register: Dan 6:23 ("the king was exceedingly glad because [Daniel] had trusted in his God") names inner gladness at deliverance through trust. This is the gladness characteristic, not the goodness characteristic. OQ-003 remains: te.ev may belong in a gladness cluster. No term should be promoted from BOUNDARY to a full sub-group without researcher decision.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic primarily active or passive in its expression — something the person does, or something the person receives and is constituted by?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Both, with the receiving side primary for the human person. The M39-A verse landscape is dominated by: God blessing/gracing (active, God as agent) and the human receiving, seeking, or responding to grace (passive-receptive, human as recipient). **[B — Goodness]** Both registers are present with more even balance. Ya.tav group 632-002 (moral goodness) is active — it names the moral orientation from which right action proceeds. Group 632-003 (approval/volitional preference) is active-evaluative — the inner judgment that directs action. Group 632-001 (glad/merry heart) is more passive — the inner state that is produced by circumstances or the absence of inner discord. Tov group 542-001 is mixed: 1Ki 8:18 (God affirming what is in the heart) is receptive; Psa 119:71 (person evaluating affliction as good) is active.

**T1.2.3** — Is the characteristic primarily individual or communal in its expression — does it operate in the person alone, or is it constitutively relational?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Constitutively relational. No verse in M39-A evidences grace or blessing operating in isolation — every occurrence has a relational frame. Ba.rakh (divine blessing) always has a recipient; chen/charis (favour/grace) always has a giver and receiver; cha.nan (gracious) always describes a relationship between superior and suppliant; ra.tsah (acceptance) always describes a relational evaluation between offerer and receiver. The science extract (Section 1 — gift theory, Mauss) confirms: the gift is inherently a social act creating relational bonds. The grace-characteristic is constitutively two-directional.

**[B — Goodness]** More individual in primary expression, but not without communal dimension. Pro 15:13 and 17:22 (glad/good heart — good medicine) operate within the individual constitutional structure. Gen 4:7 (moral goodness and sin's crouching) is addressed to one person's inner orientation. However, 1Sa 2:26 (Samuel growing in favour), the communal approval texts (Jos 22:33), and Eze 36:11 (God doing good to Israel as a people) show that goodness has communal expression. The characteristic begins individually but does not stay there.

### T1.3 — Constitutional Location

**T1.3.1** — Where does this characteristic sit in the person — heart, mind, will, conscience, soul, spirit, body, or some combination?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Primarily at the relational-character level, engaging heart, soul, and will: (1) Heart: the ba.rakh group 1299-002 (human blessing of God in worship — Psa 16:7; 34:1; 103:1) and the cha.nan supplication corpus (Psa 31:9 — "be gracious to me... my soul and my body also") engage the heart as the seat of the inner orientation of worship and petition. **[B — Goodness]** Primarily in the heart, with somatic dimension: (1) Heart: 1Ki 8:18 ("it was in your heart") is the most direct location — goodness resides in the heart as the pre-actional inner quality. (2) Body: Pro 15:13 ("a glad heart makes a cheerful face") and 17:22 ("a joyful heart is good medicine, but a crushed spirit dries up the bones") show that the goodness characteristic produces visible and somatic effects — the face and the bones. The somatic dimension is the most unexpected finding in M39-B: inner goodness/gladness has physical consequences.

### T1.4 — Subject and Object

**T1.4.1** — Who or what is the primary subject of this characteristic — who enacts or embodies it?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** God is the primary subject across the verse landscape. The divine blessing (1299-001 — 89 verses, all God as subject), divine graciousness (2330-001 — 13 verses, all God), divine inner pleasure (795-001; 795-002 — ra.tsah with God as subject), divine grace (888-001 — charis, God as sovereign agent), divine goodwill (494-001 — eudokia, God as subject) collectively constitute the dominant subject-register. The human as subject (cha.nan generosity in 984-001; charizō human forgiveness in 5470-002; ba.rakh human blessing in 1299-002 and 1299-003) is the secondary and derivative register. God enacts; humans participate.

**[B — Goodness]** The human person is the primary subject — the one who is good, who evaluates as good, who does well. Tov group 542-001 has God as subject in the affirmation-form (1Ki 8:18; 2Ki 10:30 — God affirming human goodness) but the human is the agent of goodness being evaluated. Ya.tav group 632-002 (doing good) has the human as the moral actor. The goodness characteristic is more human-centred in subject than M39-A.

---

**T1.4.2** — Who or what is the primary object — who receives or is transformed by this characteristic?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The human person is the primary object — the recipient of divine grace, blessing, and favour. The entire structure of M39-A's dominant register is God-as-subject → human-as-object. The human community is also an object: the Abrahamic blessing-to-nations (Gen 12:3; 22:18), the Aaronic blessing of Israel (Num 6:24), and the grace-to-all-who-believe corpus (888-001) all name communities or all humanity as recipients. The secondary pattern: God is the object of human blessing in the worship register (1299-002) and the object of supplication (984-001 — the human directing the cha.nan-cry toward God).

**[B — Goodness]** The primary object is the person themselves and those around them. The good/glad heart heals the person (Pro 17:22). The communal approval texts (Jos 22:33; 2Sa 3:36) show the community receiving the consequence of another's goodness-expression.

---

**T1.4.3** — Does the subject and object of this characteristic shift across the verse evidence — and if so, what does that shift reveal?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Yes — the subject-object shift is M39-A's most structurally distinctive feature. The same terms function in both directions: ba.rakh has God as subject (1299-001) and human as subject (1299-002; 1299-003); cha.nan has God as the gracious one being petitioned and the human as the gracious-generous giver (Psa 37:21); chen operates from God toward humans (889-001) and between humans (889-002) and as a human character quality (889-003). The shift reveals: the grace-characteristic is structurally propagating — it does not remain fixed in one subject but flows from God through humans to others. The grace-characteristic creates a chain of transmission.

**[B — Goodness]** Subject-object shift is less prominent in M39-B but present: in the approval/volitional-preference register (632-003), both God (Deu 18:17) and humans (Neh 2:6; Est 5:14) serve as approving subjects. The shift here reveals that moral evaluation of the good is a shared capacity — both God and the human person exercise the faculty of recognising and approving the good.

---

### T1.5 — Characteristic Opposite

**T1.5.1** — What is the named or implied structural opposite of this characteristic?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Multiple structural opposites are named across M39-A's verse landscape: (1) Cursing (the inverse of blessing): ba.rakh group 1299-004 contains the clearest inversions — Job 1:11; 2:5; 2:9 (cursing God), Gen 12:3 (cursing vs. blessing). The anti-structure is lexically encoded: the same word is used euphemistically for its opposite. (2) Mercilessness/ungrace: Rom 11:6 ("if it is by grace, it is no longer on the basis of works — otherwise grace would no longer be grace") names the opposite as works-based merit-claim. (3) Rejection/non-acceptance: ra.tsah in the negative (Mal 1:10; Jer 14:10; Amo 5:22) — "I will not accept them" is the structural opposite of ra.tsah. (4) Corrupted grace: Nah 3:4 (chen as deadly charm); Psa 62:4 (ra.tsah directed at falsehood); the Acts political-favour texts (888-003) — grace weaponised or transactionalised.

**[B — Goodness]** The structural opposite is ra/evil — the antonym of tov and ya.tav. Gen 4:7 names it most directly: sin crouching at the door when goodness is absent. Psa 36:3 (ceasing to act wisely and do good) implies the opposite is active moral corruption. Pro 17:22 implies the opposite is the crushed spirit that dries up the bones — inner discord as the opposite of glad-goodness. The somatic dimension makes the opposite vivid: not merely moral failure but physical deterioration.

---

**T1.5.2** — Does the structural opposite appear in the verse evidence — or is it only implied?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Both — directly named (group 1299-004; Mal 1:10; Nah 3:4) and implied (the logical opposite of grace in Rom 11:6). The directly-named inversions are analytically significant: they show the grace-characteristic can be corrupted, withheld, and weaponised, not merely absent.

**[B — Goodness]** Directly named in Gen 4:7 (sin crouching as the opposite of doing well); implied in Pro 17:22 (the crushed spirit as the anti-state of the glad heart); present in Psa 36:3 (ceasing from goodness as a characteristic of the wicked).

---

**T1.5.3** — What does the structural opposite reveal about the contour and definition of the characteristic itself?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Three contour-revealing findings from the structural opposite: (1) Grace is genuine personal engagement, not mechanical — the fact that divine acceptance (ra.tsah) can be withheld (Mal 1:10) reveals that when it is given, it is truly given from a personal inner disposition, not automatically dispensed. (2) Grace is logically exclusive of merit — Rom 11:6 shows that the opposite of grace is not merely its absence but its categorical replacement by works; the two cannot coexist in the same soteriological frame. (3) The grace-characteristic can be corrupted from within — the same vocabulary serves both grace and its distortion (1299-004; Nah 3:4), revealing that the characteristic's corruption is not external attack but internal inversion.

**[B — Goodness]** The structural opposite of goodness (sin crouching, ceasing from good) reveals that goodness is not a neutral default state — it must be maintained by sustained inner orientation. Gen 4:7's structure ("if you do well... if you do not do well") presents goodness as a choice that must be actively held; its absence is not passive but opens actively to sin's entrance.

---

### T1.6 — Scope and Boundaries

**T1.6.1** — What is the scope of this characteristic within the person — does it operate across the whole inner being, or is it limited to particular faculties or locations?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Comprehensive scope. The verse evidence touches every constitutional level: soul-level (Isa 42:1 — "my soul delights"; Phili 2:13 — working in the human "willing"); heart-level (the supplication corpus; the worship-blessing corpus); character-level (chan.nun as settled identity; charitoō as permanent standing); body-adjacent (the kneeling/posture dimension of ba.rakh root; the somatic blessing-outcomes in 1299-001 — fruitfulness, multiplication). The grace-characteristic is not confined to one faculty; it addresses the whole person as recipient.

**[B — Goodness]** Scope is wide but with a clearer primary location: heart as the seat of the quality (1Ki 8:18), with somatic consequence (Pro 17:22), moral faculty expression (632-002), and volitional faculty expression (632-003). The characteristic is constitutional — it shapes across the whole inner person rather than operating through one faculty alone.

---

**T1.6.2** — Where does this characteristic touch, overlap with, or approach another inner-being characteristic — and what distinguishes it from that adjacent characteristic?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Three major adjacencies from M39-A's verse landscape: (1) Compassion/mercy (R111): the cha.nan terms share root family with the mercy vocabulary (H2603A appears in the mercy registry as XREF). The distinction: grace (M39-A) operates from the greater initiating toward the lesser regardless of the lesser's need; mercy (adjacent) is specifically responsive to guilt or need. Psa 51:1 names both in one verse: "according to your steadfast love; according to your abundant mercy" — they are distinct aspects of the same divine orientation. (2) Love (R103): Lam 3:22 pairs chesed and rachamim in parallel — the steadfast-love/compassion boundary overlaps. M39-A approaches this but grace is distinguished by the merit-exclusive logic (Rom 11:6): love may be given within covenant obligation; grace by definition cannot be owed. (3) Goodness (M39-B, adjacent within the cluster): Gen 4:7 (ya.tav/goodness → ra.tsah/acceptance) links them sequentially: goodness produces the condition for grace-acceptance. Distinct: goodness is the inner quality of the person; grace is the sovereign gift from the other.

**[B — Goodness]** Three adjacencies: (1) Blessing/Grace (M39-A, adjacent within cluster): see above. (2) Joy/gladness (the gladness registry): ya.tav group 632-001 (glad/merry heart) approaches the gladness characteristic. The distinction: goodness is constitutive character quality; gladness is the affective state that may or may not flow from it. Pro 15:13 shows them together but distinct: "a glad heart [gladness] makes a cheerful face" — the glad heart is an inner state, not identical with moral goodness. (3) Moral evaluation (adjacent to conscience): ya.tav's approval register (632-003) approaches the conscience characteristic. The distinction: goodness evaluates what is good; conscience specifically evaluates moral right and wrong, especially regarding obligation.

---

**T1.6.3** — What does the evidence reveal about the borders of this characteristic — where it ends and another begins?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The clearest borders: (1) Grace/mercy border: the cha.nan root generates both the grace vocabulary (chen, charis) and the supplication vocabulary (tachanum, techinah — seeking mercy). The border is functional: grace is the disposition of the giving side; mercy is the specific response to need/guilt on the receiving side. In many verses both are present simultaneously; the border is porous. (2) Grace/works border: Rom 11:6 draws the sharpest border in the NT — grace and works are mutually exclusive soteriological modes, even if they coexist in the same person experientially. (3) Favour/election border: chen/favour (889-001) and divine election sometimes overlap (Exo 33:12 — "I know you by name and you have found favour in my sight"). The favour is the form of the elect relationship, but not identical with the election itself.

**[B — Goodness]** The borders are less sharp in the verse evidence than in M39-A. The tov term's semantic breadth (aesthetic, moral, relational, material goodness) means that the characteristic borders on beauty/aesthetic, on wellbeing/peace, and on wisdom/moral evaluation simultaneously. The sharpest border from the evidence: ya.tav group 632-001 (gladness register) is where goodness approaches gladness — the verse evidence does not fully separate them, but the difference in grounding is analytical: gladness is the affective response; goodness is the constitutive quality from which gladness may flow.

### T1.7 — Acquisition and Conditions

**T1.7.1** — How does the person come to possess or experience this characteristic?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Exclusively through reception — the person cannot generate grace/blessing. This is the defining structural feature of M39-A: the characteristic is received or it is not possessed. Gen 6:8 ("Noah found favour") — found, not created. Eph 2:8 ("by grace you have been saved... not your own doing; it is the gift of God") — not produced by the recipient. Luk 1:28 (Mary kecharitōmenē — the completed passive voice: she has been graced by another). The cha.nan supplication corpus (984-001, 72 verses) confirms: the dominant human posture is petition — pleading for what cannot be self-generated. Rom 11:6 is definitive: grace that could be earned would not be grace.

**[B — Goodness]** Through both reception and formation. Gen 4:7 implies that goodness can be chosen ("if you do well") — it is a moral orientation the person can maintain or fail to maintain. 2Ki 10:30 ("you have done well in carrying out what is right in my eyes") implies goodness as practiced right action. Pro 17:22 implies that the inner state of gladness/good is cultivable. The science extract (Section 2 — virtue psychology, Aristotle) confirms: virtues are formed through practice; graciousness and goodness arise through habituated dispositions. However, Eze 36:11 ("I will do more good to you than ever before") shows that goodness can also be received as divine gift — both dimensions are present.

---

**T1.7.2** — What conditions in the person enable or block the reception or expression of this characteristic?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Enabling conditions for reception: (1) The supplication posture (984-001 — the cry from affliction, distress, loneliness); Psa 25:16 ("be gracious to me, for I am lonely and afflicted") — need is the ground of the petition. (2) The upright heart: 1Ch 29:17 ("you have pleasure in uprightness; in the uprightness of my heart I have freely offered") — ra.tsah is enabled by the inner quality of integrity. (3) Fear of God: Psa 147:11 ("the Lord takes pleasure in those who fear him") — the reverential orientation enables divine ra.tsah. Blocking conditions: (1) Corrupt offerings (Mal 1:10; Amos 5:22) — the absence of integrity blocks ra.tsah. (2) Merit-claim: Rom 11:6 (seeking grace through works closes the grace-frame). (3) Self-generated false satisfaction: the grace-characteristic requires acknowledgment of need; pride and self-sufficiency block its reception.

**[B — Goodness]** Enabling conditions: the sustained inner orientation toward what is genuinely right (632-002 — moral goodness as practice). Blocking conditions: Gen 4:7 is explicit — not doing well opens the door to sin; the absence of goodness is not neutral but actively hazardous. Psa 36:3 ("he has ceased to act wisely and do good") names cessation from goodness as a moral deterioration, implying that goodness must be actively maintained.

---

**T1.7.3** — What happens in the person when they encounter but do not receive this characteristic — what is the inner state of the person who resists or loses grace/goodness?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Two patterns in the verse evidence: (1) Loss of acceptance: Jer 14:10 ("the Lord does not accept them"); Psa 77:7 ("will the Lord spurn forever, and never again be favorable?") — the inner state of the person under withdrawn ra.tsah is anguish, uncertainty, and crisis of divine relationship. (2) Inversion: group 1299-004 (Job 2:9 — "curse God and die") shows the person who actively inverts the grace-characteristic — the inner state is one of rebellion and bitterness that turns the blessing-vocabulary into its opposite.

**[B — Goodness]** Gen 4:7 is the definitive verse: not doing well produces the condition in which sin crouches at the door. The inner state: vulnerability to the entry of what destroys. Pro 17:22 implies the crushed spirit as the anti-state — the drying of bones.

### T1.8 — Evidential Ground

**T1.8.1** — What verse or group functions as the primary analytical anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most directly and definitionally evidences it?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Primary anchors from the verse evidence: (1) **Exo 34:6** (2330-001) — the divine self-declaration as gracious; the foundational constitutional statement. (2) **Eph 2:8** (888-001) — the definitive NT grace-mode-of-salvation statement. (3) **Gen 12:2** (1299-001) — the paradigm covenantal sovereign blessing. Together these three anchors cover the three primary registers of M39-A: divine character (Exo 34:6), NT soteriology (Eph 2:8), OT covenantal blessing (Gen 12:2).

**[B — Goodness]** Primary anchor: **1Ki 8:18** (542-001) — "you did well that it was in your heart" — the most direct statement of goodness as a heart-prior quality evaluated by God. Secondary: **Pro 17:22** (632-001) — the somatic consequence statement, evidencing the dimensional depth of the characteristic.

---

**T1.8.2** — What is the most analytically significant verse in the evidence — not necessarily the anchor, but the verse that opens the most analytical depth?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** **Rom 11:6** — "But if it is by grace, it is no longer on the basis of works; otherwise grace would no longer be grace." This verse draws the sharpest analytical boundary of any in the cluster: it names the logical exclusivity of grace, the impossibility of hybrid grace-works, and the self-defeating nature of grace earned. No other verse so precisely captures what makes grace structurally distinctive as an inner-being characteristic. Second: **Phili 2:13** — "God who works in you, both to will and to work for his good pleasure" — grace operating inside the human volitional faculty is the deepest inner-being claim in the M39-A corpus.

**[B — Goodness]** **Gen 4:7** — "If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin is crouching at the door." This verse is analytically richest because it: (a) connects goodness (M39-B) to acceptance (M39-A's ra.tsah), establishing the inter-sub-group link; (b) names the consequence of goodness's absence (sin's entrance) with structural precision; (c) frames goodness as a maintained moral orientation with immediate inner consequences when it lapses.

---

**T1.8.3** — What is the verse that carries the most significant unresolved analytical question in the cluster?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** **Gen 33:10** (795-003) — "I have seen your face, which is like seeing the face of God, and you have accepted me." Jacob equates Esau's accepting face with the face of God. The analytical question: is this mere hyperbole or does the verse encode a structural claim — that genuine human acceptance of another is an imaging of divine ra.tsah? If the latter, the grace-characteristic at the human-horizontal level is not merely analogical but participatory in divine acceptance. This has implications for how M39-A's relational-favour register (889-002; 888-003) is understood.

**[B — Goodness]** **Num 24:1** (542-001) — "when Balaam saw that it pleased the Lord to bless Israel." Tov here names divine pleasure-at-blessing — operating in the M39-A register (divine pleasure = eudokia/ra.tsah) while being carried by the M39-B term (tov). The unresolved question: does tov's semantic breadth mean that the goodness and grace characteristics are ultimately facets of a single divine attribute (God is both good and gracious as one unified character), or are they genuinely distinct inner-being characteristics that tov happens to bridge?
