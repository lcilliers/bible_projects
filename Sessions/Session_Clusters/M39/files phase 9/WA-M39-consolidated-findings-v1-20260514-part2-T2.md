### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Exclusively received — never self-generated. This is the definitional structural claim of M39-A, confirmed across all 23 groups: (1) Bestowed by God: Eph 2:8 ("not your own doing; it is the gift of God"); Rom 11:5 ("chosen by grace"); Luk 1:28 (Mary graced by divine action). (2) Generationally carried: the Abrahamic blessing passes through offspring (Gen 12:3; 22:18). (3) Mediated by humans on God's behalf: the priestly-blessing group (1299-003 — Num 6:24) carries divine grace through human speech. Rom 11:6 is the definitive statement: grace that is generated by the recipient's merit would "no longer be grace."

**[B — Goodness]** Both sources: (1) Divine bestowal: Eze 36:11 ("I will do more good to you than ever before") — goodness given by God. 1Ki 8:18 — God affirms goodness in the heart, implying he has placed it there. (2) Human formation: ya.tav group 632-002 implies practice — "ceasing to act wisely and do good" (Psa 36:3) implies goodness as a sustained practice the person can maintain or abandon. The goodness characteristic has a formative dimension that grace lacks: it can be cultivated through sustained moral orientation.

---

**T2.9.2** — What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The origin is singular (God) but the mode of transmission is multiple: direct divine bestowal (Eph 2:8), generational inheritance (Gen 12:3), human priestly mediation (Num 6:24), and Spirit-distribution (1Cor 12:4 — charisma). The source is one; the channels are several.

**[B — Goodness]** The origin is dual: divine gift and human formation. The two are not separate: divine goodness is the standard against which human goodness is measured (Psa 119:68 — "you are good and do good; teach me your statutes"), suggesting that the formation of human goodness is oriented toward and enabled by the divine standard.

---

**T2.9.3** — Does the origin of this characteristic change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The origin (God) remains constant; the context shifts the mode. In the creation context (Gen 1:22) the mode is direct declaration; in the covenantal context (Gen 12:2) the mode is promise-and-fulfilment; in the cultic context (Num 6:24) the mode is priestly mediation; in the NT context (Eph 2:8) the mode is christological; in the Spirit context (1Cor 12:4) the mode is pneumatological distribution. Same source, shifting modes.

**[B — Goodness]** The relative weight of divine gift vs. human formation shifts by context: in the covenantal-judgment context (2Ki 10:30 — "you have done well"), human action is affirmed; in the eschatological context (Eze 36:11), divine action predominates; in the wisdom context (Pro 15:2), the formation of goodness through wisdom-practice is the frame.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Yes — a tri-level movement is evidenced: (1) From God's inner character (source-level) to the human soul through bestowal: Isa 42:1 (God's soul-level delight) → charizō (the act of giving) → the human as recipient. (2) From the inner character level to the heart (Phili 2:13 — eudokia working inside the human will at the heart level). (3) From the heart/inner level to the body: grace expressed in gracious speech (lips — Psa 45:2) and in the posture of blessing (kneeling — ba.rakh root). (4) From God through human mediators to community: the priestly blessing (1299-003 — Num 6:24) moves grace from God through the priest's speech to the assembled people. The characteristic is constitutionally mobile — it crosses from divine to human, from inner to expressed, from individual to communal.

**[B — Goodness]** Yes — a two-level movement: (1) From inner heart-quality to outer expression: the good heart (1Ki 8:18) produces good action (632-002) and good speech (632-002 — Pro 15:2). (2) From inner state to somatic consequence: the glad-good heart (632-001) produces the cheerful face (Pro 15:13) and the healed/healthy bones (Pro 17:22). The movement is inner-to-outer and inner-to-somatic.

---

**T2.10.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** The pattern is consistently outward and downward from God: God's gracious character (disposition-level) → the act of blessing/gracing → the human receiving → the human's inner transformation → the human's outward expression (in speech, in extended grace to others). The sequence is: character → act → reception → inner transformation → propagation. The propagating structure (charizō forgiveness — Eph 4:32 "as God in Christ forgave you") closes the chain: grace received generates grace extended.

**[B — Goodness]** The pattern is inner-to-outer: heart-quality → moral action → somatic consequence. The sequence is less directional than M39-A (goodness does not have a propagating chain in the same way grace does) but is more constitutive: goodness shapes the person from the inside out rather than passing from person to person.

---

**T2.10.3** — If no constitutional movement is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[A — Blessing and Grace]** Not applicable — constitutional movement is well-evidenced and structurally distinctive.

**[B — Goodness]** Not applicable — constitutional movement evidenced (inner-to-outer, inner-to-somatic).
