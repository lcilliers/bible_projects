## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The moral-inner defilement-state reveals God's character primarily by contradiction: the characteristic is what God is not, and its presence in the inner person marks the distance between the creature and the Creator. The most direct evidence is Isa 6:5 (M10c-C-VCG-01): confronted by divine holiness ("my eyes have seen the King, the Lord of hosts"), the prophet's immediate response is the recognition that his lips — his inner-moral organ — are unclean. The holiness of God is the revealing force; the defilement-state is what that holiness exposes. Similarly, 1Th 4:7 (M10c-C-VCG-02): "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness" — the divine calling is defined as holiness, and the moral-inner defilement-state is named as what the divine calling opposes and moves the person away from. God's character — as holy, as morally pure in all inner motivation — is the standard that makes the moral-inner defilement-state visible as a condition of the inner person.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The 26-verse corpus does not attribute the moral-inner defilement-state to God. No verse in §3 portrays God as possessing impurity, defiled conscience, corrupted will, or heart allegiance disorder. The characteristic is exclusively a condition of the human (and, in the Revelation apocalyptic register, of spiritually corrupted beings — Rev 16:13, 17:4, 18:2, VCG-04). The absolute absence of divine attribution confirms the significance: the moral-inner defilement-state is a departure from divine character, not an expression of it. Its significance for the human person is that it marks the inner person's failure to image God — the condition where the interior life has moved from conformity to divine holiness toward conformity to corrupted desire.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The silence confirms that the moral-inner defilement-state is the condition of the image-bearer when the image is disrupted or distorted. It is not a divine property the person is created to reflect; it is the condition that emerges when the person ceases to image God's holiness. Isa 64:6 (M10c-C-VCG-01) makes this most stark: "we have all become like one who is unclean, and all our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment" — the corporate confession of a community whose interior life no longer reflects divine moral purity. The silence about God's possession of this state positions the characteristic as the marker of image-disruption: the divine image in the person is in proportion to the absence of moral-inner defilement, and in inverse proportion to its presence.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The 26-verse corpus does not evidence a creational purpose for the moral-inner defilement-state. Unlike Characteristic 1 (Ritual defilement-state), which has structural provisions built into the created order (bodily management, cleansing remedies), Characteristic 2 is consistently framed in the evidence as a departure from the divine calling rather than as a creaturely feature designed for formation. The evidence is entirely about the condition's negative impact and the call to leave it (Rom 6:19, 1Th 4:7, 2Cor 7:1). No verse in the 26-verse corpus frames the moral-inner defilement-state as something God created for human flourishing or formation. Its purpose, if it has one, is revealed only indirectly through contrast: what the person is called away from clarifies what they are called toward.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence strongly indicates the moral-inner defilement-state is a consequence of the fallen condition, not part of the original created design. Job 14:4 (M10c-C-VCG-01) — "who can bring a clean thing out of an unclean? There is not one" — frames the condition as the constitutional reality of the human being as Job knows it: no clean thing emerges from this unclean origin. This is not a description of original design but of inherited human condition under sin. Rom 6:19 (M10c-C-VCG-02) describes the prior condition as "slaves to impurity and to lawlessness" — a slavery into which people fell, not a state they were created for. Rom 1:24 (M10c-C-VCG-02) frames the condition as God's judicial response to human rejection of him: "God gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity" — the defilement deepens as a consequence of moral defection. Taken together, the evidence locates the characteristic as a condition of the fallen human being, not the originally created one.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, through the positive-inverse register (M10c-C-VCG-05) and the call-to-cleansing register (M10c-C-VCG-03). Rev 3:4 (VCG-05): "people who have not soiled their garments... will walk with me in white, for they are worthy" — the future fullness is walking with Christ in white, which is the eschatological resolution of the moral-inner defilement's present struggle. Rev 14:4 (VCG-05): those who have not defiled themselves "follow the Lamb wherever he goes... redeemed from mankind as firstfruits for God and the Lamb" — the orientation is toward eschatological consummation with the Lamb. The call at 2Cor 7:1 (VCG-03): "bringing holiness to completion in the fear of God" — the directional goal is the completion of holiness, a future fullness that the active cleansing from defilement serves. 1Th 4:7 (VCG-02): the divine call is "in holiness" — the fullness is holiness; the defilement-state is what stands between the person and that fullness.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The moral-inner defilement-state does not positively express the divine image but reveals it negatively: its presence marks a specific dimension of the image's disruption. The most revealing evidence is Isa 6:5 (VCG-01): the prophet's recognition of his unclean lips at the sight of divine holiness shows that the faculty capable of recognising the defilement — the conscience — is itself a feature of the image-bearing capacity. The person who can name their moral-inner defilement is exercising an image-bearing capacity (moral self-awareness before God) that the defilement condition itself cannot eliminate. The evidence thus shows the divine image expressed negatively through this characteristic: the ability to recognise the condition (Isa 6:5), to feel its incompatibility with divine holiness (1Th 4:7: "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness"), and to be called to cleansing (2Cor 7:1) presupposes the image-bearing capacity for holiness.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely condition?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — Exclusively a creaturely condition, as evidenced by the silence at T0.1.2. The moral-inner defilement-state is not shared with God. There is no divine analogue to the corrupted conscience (1Cor 8:7), the enslaved will (Rom 6:19), or the heart allegiance disorder equated with idolatry (Eph 5:5). The evidence uniformly frames the condition as what God is not and as what the divine call moves the person away from (1Th 4:7). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — specifically, it is the condition of the fallen creature whose inner person has come under the dominion of desire, sin, and corrupted allegiance.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The presence of moral-inner defilement reveals disruption of the divine image at the most interior level — the conscience, will, and heart are not oriented toward God but toward corrupted desire and allegiance. The clearest evidence: Mat 23:27 (VCG-04) — "within are full of dead people's bones and all uncleanness" — the inner life that should reflect divine purity is instead marked by death and corruption. Eph 5:5 (VCG-04): the impure person is equated with an idolater — their interior has displaced God with corrupt desire, revealing the image's most fundamental disruption (misplaced allegiance). The absence of the characteristic — the positive-inverse state — reveals the image functioning toward its intended fullness: Rev 14:4 (VCG-05), those not defiled follow the Lamb, are called firstfruits for God — the image is expressed in the orientation of the whole inner person toward God.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, in two moves evidenced in the corpus. First, the Hebrew prophetic register provides typological scaffolding for the NT moral-inner register: Job 14:4 (VCG-01), Isa 6:5 (VCG-01), and Isa 64:6 (VCG-01) all express the constitutional moral-inner defilement of the human before God, which the NT then addresses directly in terms of the gospel and sanctification. The Hebrew voices point forward to a problem the NT claims is addressed in Christ. Second, the positive-inverse in Rev 14:4 (VCG-05) — "these have not defiled themselves... they follow the Lamb... redeemed from mankind as firstfruits" — is explicitly christological-eschatological: the undefiled inner condition is defined in relation to the Lamb, constituting a typological participation in the Lamb's purity. The few in Rev 3:4 (VCG-05) who have "not soiled their garments" will "walk with me in white" — the white garments are eschatological, pointing toward divine fullness.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The direction is from the human negative instance toward the divine-eschatological positive. The Hebrew moral-inner defilement descriptions (Job 14:4, Isa 6:5, Isa 64:6, VCG-01) name a human problem in need of divine resolution — they point forward to the NT announcement that the problem is addressed. The call at 1Th 4:7 (VCG-02) — "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness" — frames the divine calling as the telos that the person is moving toward. The typological direction is: human defilement (present) → divine call to holiness (active) → eschatological fullness of holiness (Rev 14:4) — human-to-eschatological. The divine does not establish defilement as a pattern for the human; the divine call to holiness establishes the goal the human defilement-state must be left behind.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Typological use is evidenced at T0.4.1 and T0.4.2. Not applicable.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic is named "Moral-inner defilement-state." Three elements of the name are analytically significant. *Moral*: the defilement is located in the moral domain — it concerns the inner person's orientation toward good, evil, right, and wrong, not merely a ritual or bodily condition. *Inner*: the locus is the interior of the person — conscience, will, heart — not external contact or authoritative verdict (that is Characteristic 1). *Defilement-state*: it is a condition, not an act — the person is in a state of corruption, not merely performing corrupt acts. The name signals that this characteristic is the inner-being condition of a morally compromised interior, which the evidence confirms: Mat 23:27 (VCG-04) — "within are full of dead people's bones and all uncleanness"; Eph 4:19 (VCG-02) — "greedy to practice every kind of impurity" arising from a silenced conscience.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Five terms carry this characteristic. H2931 (ta.me adj): "unclean" — appearing in its Hebrew metaphorical-inner register here (Job 14:4, Isa 6:5, Isa 35:8, Isa 52:11, Isa 64:6, Hag 2:14, VCG-01 and -03/05), pointing to the inner condition of moral uncleanness before God. G0167 (akatharsia): "impurity" — the primary NT noun, appearing 10 times (VCG-02 and -04), consistently naming the inner condition of moral corruption in the flesh-desire register. G3435 (molunō): "to defile, soil" — a verb for the act of defiling the inner person (1Cor 8:7, Rev 3:4, Rev 14:4, VCG-03 and -05). G3436 (molusmos): "defilement" — the noun from the molunō root (2Cor 7:1, VCG-03). G3394 (miasmos): "defilement" — from miainō, to stain or pollute (2Pe 2:10, VCG-03). Definitionally, these terms together reveal the characteristic as: an inner condition of contamination (akatharsia, molusmos, miasmos — state nouns), producible through actions of the will (molunō — verb), and affecting the whole constitution of the person.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, all three. Directional: "moral-inner" immediately orients the enquiry toward the interior of the person — the question is not what the person has touched or done outwardly but what has happened to the interior. The direction is inward, from the surface to the core. Relational: the defilement-state positions the person in a specific relational posture — away from divine holiness (1Th 4:7 — what God has not called the person for), away from Christ (Rev 3:4 — those not defiled walk with him), and in a corrupted relational posture toward God (Eph 5:5 — the impure person as idolater displacing God with corrupt desire). Constitutional: "state" signals that the enquiry is into the person's condition, not merely their behaviour — the characteristic is something the person is in, which has implications for the whole person's constitution (2Cor 7:1: "defilement of body and spirit").

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Primarily a condition, but with a strong dispositional dimension. The noun forms (G0167 akatharsia, G3436 molusmos, G3394 miasmos) all name states the person is in. Rom 6:19 (VCG-02) — "slaves to impurity" — frames it as a condition of slavery: the will is not free but bound. Eph 4:19 (VCG-02) — "given themselves up to sensuality, greedy to practice every kind of impurity" — shows a dispositional dimension: the person has developed a habitual appetite for impurity once conscience has been silenced. 2Cor 12:21 (VCG-02) — "impurity... that they have practiced" — shows the condition as persistently practiced, revealing a dispositional pattern. The characteristic is thus a condition (present state of the inner person) with an entrenched dispositional character (habitual orientation of will and desire toward corruption) — not a single act, but a sustained state generated by repeated acts of will.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Composite in structure. The 26-verse corpus distributes across five VCGs, each identifying a distinct inner-being facet: VCG-01 (constitutional-existential uncleanness before God — Job 14:4, Isa 6:5, Isa 64:6, Hag 2:14), VCG-02 (impurity as flesh-dominion and habitual corruption — Rom 1:24, 6:19, 2Cor 12:21, Gal 5:19, Eph 4:19, Col 3:5, 1Th 2:3/4:7), VCG-03 (conscience and will as locus of defilement / deliberate self-purification — 1Cor 8:7, 2Cor 6:17, 2Cor 7:1, 2Pe 2:10, Isa 52:11), VCG-04 (heart allegiance disorder and idolatrous impurity — Mat 23:27, Eph 5:3/5:5, Rev 16:13/17:4/18:2), VCG-05 (maintained moral purity and consecrated non-defilement — Isa 35:8, Rev 3:4, Rev 14:4). These five facets are constituent elements of one condition: the inner person's moral corruption, experienced from the existential (VCG-01), dispositional-habitual (VCG-02), conscience-will (VCG-03), heart-allegiance (VCG-04), and positive-inverse (VCG-05) dimensions.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Moral-inner defilement-state: the condition of the inner person — conscience, will, and heart — in a state of corruption arising from desire, habitual sin, and allegiance disorder. The defilement is located within the person (not through external contact), penetrates the full inner-outer constitution (2Cor 7:1: "defilement of body and spirit"), and functions as a prior master that directs the will and bodily members toward escalating moral disorder (Rom 6:19, VCG-02). It encompasses the constitutional human vulnerability to moral uncleanness (Job 14:4, VCG-01), the habitual flesh-impulse register (Gal 5:19, VCG-02), the conscience as locus of defilement (1Cor 8:7, VCG-03), and the heart's displacement of God with corrupt desire (Eph 5:5, VCG-04). Its structural opposite is M12 (purity/holiness of the inner person). Its primary cross-register relationships are M10 (sin-act producing the state), M11 (cleansing as the active response), M29 (desire as vehicle), M08 (pride as companion), M09 (consecration as positive orientation).

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — M12 (Purity/holiness of the inner person) is the structural opposite, confirmed throughout the corpus. The binary is explicit at 1Th 4:7 (VCG-02): "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness" — holiness (M12) and impurity (Characteristic 2) are named as antitheses within a single divine calling. 2Cor 7:1 (VCG-03): "cleanse ourselves from every defilement of body and spirit, bringing holiness to completion" — the goal of cleansing from defilement is the positive completion of holiness. Isa 35:8 (VCG-05): "the unclean shall not pass over" the Way of Holiness — the holy Way excludes the morally unclean. Rev 3:4 (VCG-05): those who have "not soiled their garments" walk with Christ "in white" — moral purity is the positive state. The structural opposite M12 is present implicitly in every verse and explicitly in several.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The moral-inner defilement-state explicitly excludes: (1) divine inheritance and kingdom membership — Eph 5:5 (VCG-04): "everyone who is... impure... has no inheritance in the kingdom of Christ and God"; (2) divine welcome — 2Cor 6:17 (VCG-03): "touch no unclean thing; then I will welcome you" — touching what is unclean is the barrier to divine welcome; (3) access to the eschatological Way of Holiness — Isa 35:8 (VCG-05): "the unclean shall not pass over it"; (4) the naming and communal identity of saints — Eph 5:3 (VCG-04): impurity "must not even be named among you, as is proper among saints"; (5) right motivation before God — 1Th 2:3 (VCG-02): the apostolic appeal does not spring "from impurity"; (6) worthiness for eschatological fellowship with Christ — Rev 3:4 (VCG-05): those not soiled are "worthy" to walk with Christ.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Characteristic 2 (Moral-inner defilement-state) is not: (1) Characteristic 1 (Ritual defilement-state) — the moral-inner register operates through the inner person's own volitional and dispositional corruption, not through bodily contact with defiling agents or authoritative verdict; (2) the sin-act itself (M10) — the moral-inner defilement-state is the condition produced by or accompanying the sin-act, not the act itself; at Rom 1:24 (VCG-02), God's giving over to impurity follows the sin-act of rejecting God (Rom 1:21-23), not simultaneous with it; (3) shame (M07) — the defilement-state is an interior moral condition, not the social-relational experience of disgrace; (4) Characteristic 3 (Corporate-covenantal defilement) — Characteristic 2 operates in the individual inner person's conscience, will, and heart, not in the corporate-sacral space; (5) Characteristic 4 (External spiritual agency defilement) — Characteristic 2 originates from within the person's own desires and choices, not from an inhabiting spiritual force. The boundary with Characteristic 1 is the most important: same vocabulary (ta.me, akathartos) but different locus (inner-volitional vs bodily-mechanical).

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The moral-inner defilement-state operates in at least four distinct ways within the inner person. (1) As constitutional ontological condition: Job 14:4 (VCG-01) — the impossibility of a clean thing from an unclean origin; the defilement is the person's constituted inner state before action. (2) As habitual flesh-dominion: Rom 6:19 (VCG-02) — "slaves to impurity" — the will's freedom is constrained by the defilement as a prior master; the person is not free but bound toward escalating moral disorder. (3) As silenced conscience leading to active greed: Eph 4:19 (VCG-02) — "become callous and have given themselves up to sensuality, greedy to practice every kind of impurity" — the conscience's silencing enables an active, appetitive pursuit of defilement. (4) As defiled conscience that makes the person's inner moral assessment unreliable: 1Cor 8:7 (VCG-03) — the weak person's conscience is defiled by eating food associated with idols; the defiled conscience cannot form correct moral assessment because the standard itself has been compromised.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, across three evident distinctions. By depth: the constitutional-existential register (VCG-01 — Job 14:4, Isa 64:6) describes the condition at the deepest constitutional level — the human person as inherently morally compromised before God; this is prior to specific acts. The habitual-dispositional register (VCG-02 — Rom 6:19, Eph 4:19, Col 3:5) describes the condition as it operates through sustained patterns of will and desire. The conscience-faculty register (VCG-03 — 1Cor 8:7) describes it operating in a specific faculty (conscience) through a specific act (eating food against conviction). By direction: the passive direction — defilement as a state the person is already in (Rom 6:19: "once presented as slaves"); and the active direction — the greedy pursuit of more defilement once the constraint of conscience is removed (Eph 4:19). By scope: individual conscience (1Cor 8:7) vs corporate moral state (Isa 64:6: "we have all become...") — the condition operates at both individual and corporate dimensions.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, at two evidenced points. First, Isa 6:5 (VCG-01): the prophet names his own defilement in speech — "I am a man of unclean lips" — the conscience's recognition of the moral-inner defilement-state is expressed in first-person verbal declaration. The speech is the moment the inner condition is named and acknowledged before God. Second, Mat 23:27 (VCG-04): the Woe pronouncement is the authoritative naming of the defilement that is hidden within — Jesus's speech act makes the inner defilement visible and declares its incompatibility with the name "holy." The communicative mode here is prophetic/judicial: the external word exposes what the inner condition has concealed. Third, by negative implication: Eph 5:3 (VCG-04) — impurity "must not even be named among you" — naming the condition within the community of saints is itself a violation; the word-act of naming defilement where it should not be named is forbidden.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering or recognising this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The most immediate inner response evidenced is the recognition of moral collapse in the presence of divine holiness. Isa 6:5 (VCG-01): upon seeing divine holiness the prophet's immediate response is "woe is me! For I am lost; for I am a man of unclean lips" — the encounter with holiness triggers immediate conscience-activated self-recognition of the defilement-state. The response is a cry of distress and lostness. A second immediate response is shown at 2Cor 7:1 (VCG-03): "let us cleanse ourselves from every defilement of body and spirit" — the recognition of defilement in the context of divine promises triggers an active first-response of self-purification. These two immediate responses (self-recognition in distress and active cleansing) represent the two poles of the characteristic's immediate register.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The immediate response varies significantly across the 26-verse corpus. Three distinct immediate response patterns are evidenced. (1) Conscience-activated distress and recognition (Isa 6:5, VCG-01 — "woe is me"); (2) Active self-purification (2Cor 7:1, VCG-03 — "let us cleanse ourselves"); (3) Hardened non-response — the opposite of recognition — in the cases where the defilement has advanced far enough that the conscience is silenced: Eph 4:19 (VCG-02) — "become callous," giving themselves up to impurity; 2Cor 12:21 (VCG-02) — those who "have not repented." The variation reveals that the characteristic does not produce a uniform response — it produces recognition and distress in those whose conscience remains active, and entrenched non-response in those whose conscience has been suppressed.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence is not uniformly silent — three patterns of immediate response are evidenced (T1.5.2 above). However, the majority of the 26 verses (particularly the legal-didactic VCG-02 corpus: Gal 5:19, Col 3:5, 1Th 2:3/4:7) describe the condition without narrating the person's immediate inner response to recognising it. In these cases the verse evidence is silent on the immediate response and focuses instead on the condition's nature and the calling away from it.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The verse evidence documents a specific trajectory of inner-being deterioration from sustained moral-inner defilement. Rom 1:24 (VCG-02): God "gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity, to the dishonoring of their bodies" — the impurity deepens and expands outward to bodily dishonour when unchecked. Rom 6:19 (VCG-02): "lawlessness leading to more lawlessness" — the defilement-state produces escalating moral disorder over time; the trajectory is intensification. Eph 4:19 (VCG-02): "become callous... greedy to practice every kind of impurity" — the sustained state produces a progressive hardening of the conscience (callousness) and an active appetite for more defilement (greed). The sequence evidenced by these three verses together: initial defilement → unchecked desire → God's judicial abandonment → bodily dishonour → moral escalation → conscience hardened → active seeking of defilement. The characteristic produces, over time, a person whose inner life is progressively oriented away from God and toward deepening corruption.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Sustained moral-inner defilement establishes: (1) callousness — the progressive suppression of conscience (Eph 4:19, VCG-02: "become callous"); (2) lawlessness as an inner orientation — the will directed toward "lawlessness leading to more lawlessness" (Rom 6:19, VCG-02); (3) active moral appetite — "greedy to practice every kind of impurity" (Eph 4:19, VCG-02); (4) hardened will resistant to repentance — 2Cor 12:21 (VCG-02): those who "have not repented of the impurity... that they have practiced"; (5) a defiled cognitive-moral faculty — Tit 1:15 is cross-referenced at VCG-03: "to the defiled and unbelieving, nothing is pure; both their minds and their consciences are defiled." Over time the characteristic establishes an inner person in whom the faculties meant to orient the person toward God (conscience, mind, moral evaluation) are all compromised.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, significantly. The immediate response (at the point of recognition — Isa 6:5: "woe is me!") is distress and awareness. The sustained effect (Rom 1:24 → Eph 4:19 trajectory) is the progressive elimination of that capacity for distress through the silencing of conscience. The immediate response requires a conscience that can still register the condition; the sustained effect produces a conscience that can no longer register it (callousness). This reveals a critical feature of the characteristic: the longer the condition persists without resolution, the more its own persistence suppresses the capacity for the immediate response that could initiate resolution. The sustained effect is thus increasingly self-reinforcing: moral-inner defilement suppresses the faculty (conscience) whose activation would begin the exit from defilement.

---

## T4 — Relational Interfaces (24 prompts)

**T4.1.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, in one specific and theologically significant mode: divine judicial abandonment. This is the characteristic operating from God toward the human: God does not cause the defilement but acts in a way that removes the restraint on it, allowing the condition to deepen as judicial consequence. This is the inverse of the divine remedial action of Characteristic 1 (where God provides standing remedy); here God's action is judicial withdrawal of the constraint that had limited the defilement's spread.

**T4.1.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Responsive and judicial. Rom 1:24 (VCG-02) frames the divine giving-over as a response to a prior human act — the rejection of God, the suppression of truth (Rom 1:18-23 — context). God's withdrawal of restraint is responsive to human moral defection. The basis is not unconditional or arbitrary; it is a judicial response proportioned to the human act of rejecting divine truth. The divine calling at 1Th 4:7 (VCG-02) — "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness" — frames the opposite direction: God's calling is inherently toward holiness, and the judicial giving-over is the consequence of refusing that calling.

**T4.1.3 — What does God's action regarding this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The judicial giving-over (Rom 1:24, VCG-02) reveals a disposition of serious moral seriousness: God does not treat moral defection as inconsequential. Alongside this: 1Th 4:7 (VCG-02) — "God has not called us for impurity, but in holiness" — reveals the divine disposition as fundamentally oriented toward the person's holiness, not toward their defilement. The judicial giving-over is therefore not God's desired outcome but his response to the human rejection of the better outcome he intended.
