## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The ritual defilement-state reflects, by structural contrast, the holiness and categorical purity of God. The characteristic operates as the condition that is incompatible with the divine presence, and this incompatibility is theologically explicit in the evidence. Lev 15:31 names the stakes directly: the people's uncleanness must be kept separate from them "lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle that is in their midst." The divine dwelling cannot coexist with the defiled state — the defilement-state reflects God's character by being precisely what God is not. Lev 11:44 makes the positive mirroring equally explicit: "I am the Lord your God. Consecrate yourselves therefore, and be holy, for I am holy. You shall not defile yourselves with any swarming thing." Ritual purity is the creaturely image of divine holiness; the defilement-state is its direct contradiction. The characteristic thus reveals that God's nature is one of absolute categorical distinction — holy/unclean are not merely labels but ontological realities before him.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The verse evidence does not attribute the ritual defilement-state to God. The 133 verses show the characteristic operating exclusively in the human person and in objects/spaces in relation to the human person. God does not become ritually unclean; he is the standard against which the clean/unclean distinction is measured (Lev 11:44-45). The significance of this absence: the defilement-state is a specifically creaturely vulnerability — a condition that arises from embodied life in contact with death, discharge, and disease. God, being without body and without limitation by death, cannot bear this condition. The absence thus locates the defilement-state within the creaturely domain of finitude and mortality, and signals that the human person's susceptibility to it is part of their created embodied constitution, not a moral failure of the image per se.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The silence confirms that the ritual defilement-state is not an image-bearer expression in the sense of resembling God, but rather it functions within the image-bearing vocation by defining the boundary of access to divine presence. Lev 10:10 frames the priestly vocation: "to distinguish between the holy and the common, and between the unclean and the clean." The priest's calling is to hold the distinction God himself embodies. The defilement-state, by being what God is not, creates the space in which the image-bearing vocation is exercised: the human person is called to manage the clean/unclean boundary in imitation of divine holiness. The silence about God's possession of defilement means the human engagement with this characteristic is participation in a structure God establishes and maintains, not a reflection of a divine property.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The ritual defilement-state in the created order equips the person to navigate the boundary between the sacred and the common, between death and life, with appropriate attention and discipline. Its purpose is formative: the discipline of managing the defilement-state — washing, waiting, presenting oneself for examination, maintaining the clean/unclean distinction (Lev 11:47, 10:10) — trains the inner person in watchful restraint, conscience-responsiveness, and sacral orientation. Lev 11:44 frames the purpose explicitly: the call not to defile oneself is the means by which the person participates in imitating divine holiness. The defilement-state, by being something that must be actively managed and resolved, equips the human person to be a creature whose bodily life is morally and sacrally ordered — not a merely biological entity but one whose bodily actions have inner and relational consequence.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence does not resolve this directly, but signals point to the defilement-state as a structural feature of embodied creaturely existence rather than exclusively a consequence of the fall. The physiological defilement register (M10c-A-VCG-05: Lev 12 childbirth, Lev 15 menstruation) attaches defilement to natural bodily processes — reproduction, discharge — not to sin. Similarly, contact with death (M10c-A-VCG-07) is the most severe defilement category; death is a consequence of the fall, but the defilement arising from death-contact is treated as a structural given requiring management, not as moral failure. The Num 9:7 men grieve that their corpse-contact impurity was involuntary — "we are unclean through touching a dead body" — showing no moral fault but a bodily condition. This suggests the defilement-state as a category inhabits both: some sub-registers (bodily discharge, menstruation) may reflect the created order of embodied creaturely vulnerability; others (death-defilement) intersect with the fallen condition. The evidence does not permit a single clean answer here.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence within Characteristic 1's 133 verses points forward primarily through the elaborate cleansing provisions that accompany every defilement state: washing, waiting, priestly examination and clearance (M10c-B-VCG-02), the water of impurity (M10c-A-VCG-07). These prescribed remedies signal that the defilement-state is not permanent — it has a designed resolution. The fullness orientation is most explicit in Num 19:9 (VCG-07), where the red-heifer ashes are a "standing remedy" designated for the congregation as a "sin offering" — indicating that defilement management is not merely ritual maintenance but participates in a larger sacrificial-atoning economy pointing toward ultimate purity. The NT akathartos verses in M10c-B-VCG-05 (Act 10:14/28) show the defilement category itself being revised by divine revelation, signalling eschatological movement: the categories formed in the ritual order are not the final word. The future fullness is the state of unrestricted access to divine presence, of which the prescribed cleansing is a present anticipation.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The ritual defilement-state expresses the divine image obliquely and structurally: by being the condition that opposes holiness, it defines the space in which the image-bearing vocation of maintaining sacral distinctions is exercised. The evidence shows this most clearly in M10c-B-VCG-01 (Lev 10:10, 11:47, 20:25): priests and all Israel are called to cultivate active inner discernment — "to distinguish between the holy and the common, and between the unclean and the clean" — which is a creaturely participation in the divine capacity to hold absolute distinctions. The image is expressed not through possessing the defilement-state but through the vocation of managing the clean/unclean boundary in light of divine holiness. The person who actively guards against defilement, who watches over bodily actions, who presents for examination, who manages the niddah cycle, is exercising a sacral stewardship that reflects the divine character.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The evidence confirms this characteristic is exclusively a creaturely condition, with no divine counterpart. As noted at T0.1.2, God is not a subject of this characteristic in the 133-verse corpus. The characteristic has a structural analogue in God: God's holiness is the positive reality against which the defilement-state is measured. But the defilement-state itself — as condition, as vulnerability, as something acquired and managed — belongs to the creaturely domain alone. It is not a shared property between Creator and creature.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The presence of the defilement-state does not, by itself, indicate damage to the divine image — because within Characteristic 1's register, defilement can be involuntary (Lev 5:2-3 VCG-01: hidden violation; Num 9:7 VCG-07: involuntary corpse-contact). The men at Num 9:7 who grieve their exclusion from Passover are fully image-bearing persons whose defilement was not moral fault. The image-condition is revealed more by the person's response to the defiled state: does conscience awaken (Lev 5:2-3), does the person seek cleansing (Num 19:19), does the will refuse to remain in the defilement (Num 19:20 — willful non-cleansing is the decisive moral fault)? The absence of the defilement-state — maintained purity — reveals a person actively ordered toward sacred access, exercising the sacral stewardship that the image-bearing vocation requires. The presence of defilement reveals vulnerability and finitude; the response to it reveals the condition of the image.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, at two points in the characteristic's 133-verse corpus. First, Num 19 (M10c-A-VCG-07): the red-heifer purification rite — the most elaborate response to the most severe defilement (corpse-contact) — is treated in the NT as a type of the blood of Christ (cited in Heb 9:13-14, though that verse is not in the M10c corpus). Within the corpus itself, the rite's structure — a sin offering preserved as a standing remedy for the entire congregation, administered through water — anticipates a purification economy beyond its own scope (Num 19:9: "it is a sin offering"). Second, the NT akathartos categorical revision (M10c-B-VCG-05: Act 10:14/28): the defilement category that shaped Peter's conscience is used by the divine vision to signal the covenantal transition — "what God has made clean, do not call unclean" (Act 10:28). The ritual-defilement-state category is deployed typologically as the boundary whose revision marks the new-covenant's extension to the nations.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The direction is bidirectional within the two typological instances identified at T0.4.1. In the Num 19 red-heifer rite, the human ritual instance (the most severe defilement state and its prescribed remedy) points forward and upward toward its divine antitype — the rite is the shadow; the cleansing power of Christ's sacrifice is the substance. In the Act 10 categorical revision, the divine initiative (God's voice declaring what is clean) establishes the pattern: the old human category (unclean persons/nations) is dissolved by divine speech, showing the divine instance as authoritative over the ritual category. In both cases, the ritual-defilement-state is the human creaturely structure that the divine economy uses as its scaffolding.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Typological use is evidenced (see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). No note required.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is named "Ritual defilement-state." The name signals three things. First, *ritual*: this is the register governed by cultic law and sacral practice, distinguishing this characteristic from the moral-inner defilement register (Characteristic 2) and the corporate-covenantal register (Characteristic 3). Second, *defilement*: the term names an active contamination — something has been transmitted, incurred, or declared; the person has not merely failed but has acquired a condition. Third, *state*: this is a condition of the person, not an act — the characteristic names what the person is in (a status), not what they do. The name therefore signals: a legally and ritually defined condition of contamination that attaches to the inner-being person through bodily mechanism or authoritative verdict.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Three terms carry this characteristic. H2930A (ta.me, verb): "to be/become unclean" — the dynamic acquisition of the defilement-state; the action of contracting or entering it. H2931 (ta.me, adjective): "unclean" — the condition itself, the static description of one who has the state. H5079 (nid.dah): "menstrual impurity / impurity of separation" — the paradigm case of a time-bound, physiologically-sourced defilement state that governs not only menstruation (Lev 15:19-33, M10c-A-VCG-05) but bodily discharge impurity more broadly, and becomes the term for the water of impurity used in corpse-defilement cleansing (Num 19:9, M10c-A-VCG-07). G0169 (akathartos): "unclean" — the NT categorical term, carrying the same clean/unclean distinction into conscience-level conviction (Act 10:14, M10c-B-VCG-05) and covenantal-status application (1Cor 7:14). At the definitional level, the Hebrew verb/adjective pair shows defilement as both a dynamic event (acquiring the state) and a static condition (being in it); nid.dah shows it as a paradigm case governing a whole category; akathartos shows the categorical conviction operative in the inner conscience.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, all three. Directional: the defilement-state is always moving toward something — toward restriction (from sacred participation), toward remedy (washing, waiting, examination), or toward communal re-entry. The corpus is structured around this directionality. Relational: the characteristic is inherently relational — it affects one's standing before God (access to the tabernacle, Lev 15:31 VCG-08), within community (social exclusion, Lev 13:46 VCG-03), and toward others (the duty not to transmit, Lev 15:11 VCG-06). Constitutional: the name "state" signals a condition of the whole person — not a faculty, not an act, but a status that bears on the person's constitutional integrity as a participant in the sacral community.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is a condition — a state the person is in, not an act they perform or a disposition they hold. The verse evidence consistently uses stative language: "he shall be unclean," "she is unclean," "his uncleanness is still on him" (Num 19:13, M10c-A-VCG-07). The condition has two acquisition mechanisms, both carrying their own structure: (1) the bodily-contact mechanism (M10c-A): the condition is incurred through specific physical interactions with defiling agents — touch, carry, proximity, ingestion; (2) the verdict mechanism (M10c-B): the condition is established through authoritative pronouncement — "the priest shall pronounce him unclean" (Lev 13:3 et al.). In both cases, the person is in the defilement-state; they did not choose it as a disposition and they do not enact it as an act — they are subject to it. However, within the condition, the will is engaged: one may seek remedy or refuse it (Num 19:20, M10c-A-VCG-07); one may volitionally avoid what causes it (Lev 11:43-44, M10c-A-VCG-04) or be warned to guard against it. The characteristic is a condition with strong volitional engagement at its perimeter.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic has constituent elements even within its ritual register. The SPLIT_DESIGN_RATIONALE (carry-forward observation) identifies two mechanisms — bodily-contact (M10c-A) and verdict/categorical (M10c-B) — as complementary registers of one inner state. Within M10c-A alone, the constituent sub-registers are: carcass-contact (VCG-02), object-contamination (VCG-03), volitional self-defilement (VCG-04), menstrual/physiological (VCG-05), discharge-contact (VCG-06), corpse-contact (VCG-07), and sacred-boundary framing (VCG-08). Within M10c-B: discernment discipline (VCG-01), priestly verdict (VCG-02), public declaration/stigma (VCG-03), disease in objects/structures (VCG-04), and NT categorical conscience (VCG-05). The characteristic is not simple — it is a unified condition with differentiated sub-registers that share the core inner-being reality (the state of ritual uncleanness bearing on sacred access and conscience) but differ in mechanism, severity, duration, and resolution.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Ritual defilement-state: a legally and sacrally defined condition of the whole person — acquired through bodily contact with a defiling agent (corpse, discharge, carcass, fluid) or established through authoritative priestly examination and verdict — that restricts the person's access to sacred participation and communal life for a defined duration. Constitutionally, the condition bears on the person as embodied being in sacral community: the body is the locus of acquisition; the conscience, will, and social-relational capacity are primarily engaged in its management and resolution. Immediate impact: exclusion from worship and communal participation; duty of self-declaration; social distancing. The primary faculties engaged are: conscience (awakening to contamination incurred — Lev 5:2-3 VCG-01; grief at exclusion — Num 9:7 VCG-07), will (active watchfulness over bodily actions; pursuit or refusal of cleansing — Num 19:20 VCG-07), discernment (the faculty required for clean/unclean distinction — Lev 10:10 VCG-01 M10c-B), and social conscience (the duty not to transmit one's state — Lev 15:11 VCG-06). Structural opposite: M12 (purity/holiness).

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The structural opposite is M12 (Purity). Every sub-group and VCG in the characteristic's 133-verse corpus names purity as the positive state that defilement opposes. The vocabulary of clean/unclean (tahor/tame) is a binary pair: to be clean is to be eligible for sacred access; to be unclean is to be excluded from it. Lev 10:10 (M10c-B-VCG-01) frames the opposition as the core priestly vocation: "to distinguish between the holy and the common, and between the unclean and the clean." Lev 15:31 (M10c-A-VCG-08) frames the stakes: defilement must be separated from the people "lest they die" by contaminating the divine dwelling. The Deu 12:15/22/15:22 (M10c-A-VCG-08) verses show the clean as the norm: those without defilement eat sacred food while the unclean are restricted to common eating. M12 (purity) is not simply the absence of defilement but an actively maintained positive condition of readiness for divine access.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The ritual defilement-state explicitly excludes the person from: (1) sacred eating — flesh touching an unclean thing is burned and may not be eaten (Lev 7:19 M10c-A-VCG-08); eating holy things while unclean is the grounds for being "cut off" (Lev 7:21 M10c-A-VCG-08; Lev 22:4 M10c-B-VCG-03); (2) communal worship participation — the Passover (Num 9:6-7 M10c-A-VCG-07); the Lord's offering; (3) residence within the community — those with discharge or skin disease are sent out of the camp (Num 5:2 M10c-A-VCG-07; Lev 13:46 M10c-B-VCG-03: "he shall live alone; his dwelling shall be outside the camp"); (4) sacred tithe-giving — Deu 26:14 (M10c-B-VCG-01) the worshiper must declare freedom from unclean state at tithe time; (5) proximity to the divine tabernacle — unchecked defilement risks defiling the tabernacle (Lev 15:31; Num 19:13 M10c-A-VCG-07/08).

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Characteristic 1 (Ritual defilement-state) is not: (1) moral guilt arising from sin-acts (that is M10 territory — the cross-register flags on H2930A point to M10 where the sin-act producing defilement is the focus); (2) moral-inner defilement of the conscience, will, and heart by desire or habitual corruption (that is Characteristic 2, M10c-C sub-group); (3) corporate-covenantal defilement of land, sanctuary, and divine name through apostasy (that is Characteristic 3, M10c-D sub-group); (4) defilement by external spiritual agency (Characteristic 4, M10c-E sub-group). Within Characteristic 1's domain, the defilement-state is not identical to shame (M07 cross-register): the men at Num 9:7 grieve but are not shamed — their state is involuntary and accommodated. The defilement-state ends where the verdict of clearance is pronounced (Lev 13 — priestly re-examination clears the disease), where the waiting period expires and washing completes (Lev 11:24: "shall be unclean until the evening"), or where the water of impurity is applied (Num 19:19-20).

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The ritual defilement-state operates in the inner person in at least four distinct ways evidenced across the 133 verses. (1) As a conscience-awakening signal: Lev 5:2-3 (M10c-A-VCG-01) — defilement may be incurred unknowingly, but when the person "comes to know it and realises his guilt," the conscience surfaces the condition. The defilement arrives before the inner recognition; recognition is its own distinct event. (2) As a duty-forming constraint on the will: the person in the defilement-state must not transmit it (Lev 15:11, M10c-A-VCG-06), must wash and wait, must present for examination, must not enter the tabernacle. The will operates under a structured set of obligations arising from the state. (3) As a grief-producing exclusion: Num 9:7 (M10c-A-VCG-07) — "why are we kept from bringing the Lord's offering?" The men's grief shows the defilement-state operating in the affective dimension — the desire for worship blocked by the condition they are in. (4) As a discernment-forming discipline: M10c-B-VCG-01 — the cultivation of the clean/unclean distinction is a perceptual and volitional discipline formed through sustained engagement with the characteristic's categorical structure.

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the characteristic operates with different severity, duration, and moral weight depending on the defiling agent and mechanism. In the bodily-contact register (M10c-A): carcass-contact and discharge-contact are typically one-day (until-evening) defilements; corpse-contact is seven-day with the prescribed remedy of water of impurity (Num 19:11, M10c-A-VCG-07) — the most severe and elaborate. Object contamination (M10c-A-VCG-03) is irreversible for earthenware (Lev 11:33 — must be broken). In the verdict/categorical register (M10c-B): disease in persons may be permanent for the duration (Lev 13:46 — "as long as he has the disease"); disease in objects and structures may culminate in demolition (Lev 14:44-45). At the constitutional level, the characteristic operates across the body (as the locus of acquisition), the conscience (as the site of awakening and guilt), and the will (as the site of volitional response). The NT register (M10c-B-VCG-05) shows the characteristic operating at the conscience-level conviction formed by the categorical system — Peter's resistance (Act 10:14) is a deeply formed inner conviction, not mere ritual compliance.

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, at two points. First, the public self-declaration of the unclean state: Lev 13:45 (M10c-B-VCG-03) — the person bearing skin disease "shall cry out 'Unclean, unclean.'" The inner reality of contamination is verbalized outward through ritual cry. The speech act is not merely announcement; it is the socially constitutive declaration that activates the community's protective response (maintaining distance). Second, the priestly pronouncement of the verdict: throughout M10c-B-VCG-02, "the priest shall pronounce him unclean" (Lev 13:3, 13:8, 13:11, 13:14, 13:15, 13:20, 13:22, 13:25, 13:27, 13:30, 13:44). The priestly speech act establishes the condition officially — it is not merely descriptive but performative. The condition exists objectively but is socially activated by the authoritative pronouncement.

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The most immediate response evidenced in the corpus is conscience awakening — the moment of recognition that the condition has been incurred. Lev 5:2-3 (M10c-A-VCG-01) describes this precisely: "it is hidden from him and he has become unclean, and he realises his guilt." The defilement precedes awareness; the awakening of guilt is the immediate inner response when the person "comes to know it." A second immediate response is grief at the prospect of exclusion: Num 9:7 (M10c-A-VCG-07) — the men approach Moses immediately upon recognising their condition and express the grief of being "kept from bringing the Lord's offering." The immediacy is notable — they do not wait; the exclusion motivates immediate approach to leadership. These two immediate responses — conscience awakening and grief-driven seeking — characterise the well-formed inner response to the defilement-state in the evidence.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The immediate response varies, and the variation itself is significant. The well-formed response is conscience awakening, grief at exclusion, and active pursuit of remedy — as at Lev 5:2-3 and Num 9:7. But Num 19:20 (M10c-A-VCG-07) shows the deviant response: "if the man who is unclean does not cleanse himself, that person shall be cut off from the midst of the assembly." The will can refuse remedy rather than pursue it. The willful non-cleansing is treated as the decisive moral fault — not the defilement itself but the failure to respond to it through the appointed remedy. This contrast between the grief-driven seeking of Num 9:7 and the willful refusal of Num 19:20 shows the characteristic as a diagnostic of the inner person: the same condition produces either conscience-awakened pursuit or will-hardened resistance, revealing the inner orientation of the person.

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence is not silent. Both varieties of immediate response (conscience-awakening/grief-seeking and willful non-response) are directly evidenced. However, the large majority of the 133 verses describe the condition and its prescribed remedy without narrating the person's inner response — they are normative/legal rather than narrative. Immediate response is directly evidenced at Lev 5:2-3, Num 9:6-7, Num 19:20, and Lev 13:45 (the self-declaration cry). The remaining verses provide structural information about the condition but leave the inner response implicit.

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Sustained engagement with the ritual defilement-state and its management produces two documented inner-being effects over time. First, a formed conscience shaped by the clean/unclean categorical system. M10c-B-VCG-01 (Lev 10:10, 11:47, 14:57, 20:25, Deu 26:14) shows this most clearly: the purity laws exist to cultivate "active moral discernment" — distinguishing clean from unclean becomes "a perceptual and volitional discipline central to covenant life" (Lev 11:47). Second, a vigilant bodily discipline — the duty of watchfulness over bodily actions near defiling agents (Lev 11:24-40, M10c-A-VCG-02; Lev 15:11, M10c-A-VCG-06) becomes an ongoing orientation of the person toward their bodily life and its sacral consequence.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Sustained engagement with the ritual defilement-state establishes: (1) the orientation of sacral vigilance — the person habituated to the clean/unclean boundary becomes watchful over bodily actions, over what they touch, eat, and carry; (2) the capacity for discernment — the clean/unclean distinction is the base capacity on which more complex moral-spiritual perception builds (Lev 10:10, M10c-B-VCG-01); (3) the orientation of communal responsibility — the duty not to transmit one's state (Lev 15:11 M10c-A-VCG-06; the Lev 13:45 self-declaration requirement) forms the person in awareness of their relational impact; (4) humility before the body's creaturely vulnerability — the physiological defilement register (M10c-A-VCG-05: Lev 12 and Lev 15 menstrual) attaches defilement to natural processes, forming the person in acceptance of embodied limitation without moral shame; (5) dependence on prescribed remedy — the standing availability of cleansing (water of impurity, Num 19:9) forms the person in an orientation of ongoing reliance on divinely provided resolution.
