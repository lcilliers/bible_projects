**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic is named Dignity. ---

**T1.1.2 — What does the primary Greek term reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — G4587 semnotēs — noun from semnos (venerable, worthy of reverence, august, serious). The root connects to sebomai (to reverence, to worship) — the same family as the godliness vocabulary. The term names a quality of serious, weighty character that naturally commands respect rather than demanding it: the person of semnotēs does not need to assert their authority or worth because the settled gravity of their character does this without performance. At the definitional level, the term reveals dignity as *reverence-worthiness from the inside* — the quality that makes a person naturally worthy of reverent regard through their settled moral seriousness rather than through claimed status.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — All three. *Relational:* semnotēs is always visible in relation — 1Ti2:2 in public life, 1Ti3:4 in household governance, Tit2:7 in teaching. The characteristic shapes how the person inhabits their social relationships and roles. *Constitutional:* the characteristic is named as an inner quality (Pass A meanings: "inner gravity," "sober weighty seriousness of character," "inner quality") that expresses itself outwardly. *Directional:* the characteristic's direction is inward-first (settled inner moral gravity) expressing outward (in public life, household, teaching) — from the grounded inner person outward into social existence, not from social expectation inward.
