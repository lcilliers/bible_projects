## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus reveals God as one who both calls for and restores the freely-moved willing spirit. Exo35:5 — "take from among you a contribution to the Lord" — God calls for an offering that must originate in a willing heart: he does not require coerced contribution but freely-given offering from the heart's genuine motivation. 2Ch29:31 — "all who were of a willing heart brought burnt offerings" — the context of restored worship after Hezekiah's reformation images God as delighted in freely-offered devotion. Most significantly, Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit" — evidences God as the one who restores and sustains the willing spirit after moral failure: God is the giver and sustainer of the very inner quality he calls for. The characteristic thus images a God who desires genuine, uncoerced inner offering rather than external compliance, and who actively works to restore the inner capacity for freely-given devotion.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The five-verse corpus does not explicitly attribute willing-heartedness to God. In all five verses, the characteristic is a quality of human persons (the willing-hearted givers in Exo35; the willing craftsmen in 1Ch28:21; the penitent psalmist in Psa51:12). God is the recipient and restorer of the characteristic rather than its primary bearer. The implicit direction of attribution is that God values and can restore the characteristic, but is not described as having it himself.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The silence on divine possession suggests that willing-heartedness in its creaturely form is specifically the inner posture of the creature *toward God* — the freely-moved heart offering to the one who calls. The characteristic presupposes an asymmetry: there is one who calls (God) and one who freely offers (the creature). God cannot be willing-hearted toward himself in the same structural sense; the characteristic is constitutively creaturely in its directional structure. However, the divine calling for free offering rather than coerced contribution (Exo35:5) and God's own free giving (Psa51:12 — upholding with a willing spirit) suggest that God's own liberality is the divine ground from which the creaturely willing-heartedness derives and which it reflects: the freely-giving God calls for freely-giving creatures.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus evidences three purposes. First, willing-heartedness equips the person for *genuine worship and offering*: Exo35:5 and 35:22 — the freely-moved heart is the inner condition that produces genuine contribution to the Lord's service rather than compelled donation. Second, it equips the person for *wholehearted skilled service*: 1Ch28:21 — willingness coupled with skill enables wholehearted participation in the temple work. Third, Psa51:12 — the willing spirit as the condition for sustaining "joyful, freely-offered devotion" — equips the person for sustained devoted engagement with God after moral failure. Together: the characteristic equips the person to give and serve genuinely rather than under compulsion, and to sustain that giving orientation over time.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of original design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence suggests both registers. Exo35:5/22 and 2Ch29:31 — the freely-moving willing heart producing generous offering and worship — evidence the characteristic as a positive, creation-consistent orientation of the human will toward God; there is no explicit fallen-condition framing in these verses. 1Ch28:21 — willingness enabling skilled service — similarly addresses the characteristic as a positive given rather than a remedial response. However, Psa51:12 is explicitly post-failure: "uphold me with a willing spirit" is the prayer of the penitent who has lost the willing orientation through moral failure. This evidences the characteristic as both an original-design orientation (the freely-given heart) and one that must be restored after the fallen condition's effects (Psa51:12 — restoration sought after failure).

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The five-verse corpus does not directly evidence an eschatological orientation for willing-heartedness. The Exodus and Chronicles contexts address present community worship and service; Psa51:12 addresses present penitential restoration. No explicit eschatological framing appears in §3.E.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Willing-heartedness expresses the divine image through the *freely-giving dimension* — the creature's uncoerced, internally-motivated generosity toward God reflects the divine freedom and liberality. God does not give because compelled but freely; the willing-hearted creature similarly gives freely rather than under constraint. Exo35:5/22 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart" — the non-compelled offering images the divine liberality in creaturely form. Psa51:12 adds the dimension of willing spirit as what enables joyful freely-offered devotion — the image-bearing creature at its most expressive of the divine generous character is the one whose spirit is willing rather than dragged.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The characteristic has a creaturely form (the creature's freely-moved will toward God) that images the divine liberality but is not directly shared in the same structural way. God's liberality is his own essential character; the creature's willing-heartedness is the responsive imaging of that liberality in the creaturely direction (from creature toward God). The structural asymmetry (God as recipient and sustainer; creature as willing giver) confirms the characteristic as primarily creaturely in its specific form, imaging a divine quality without being structurally identical to it.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The presence of willing-heartedness reveals an image-bearer whose will is genuinely oriented toward God in free, uncoerced offering — the most authentic expression of the creature's responsive imaging of divine liberality. The VCG-01 evidence (Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31) shows willing-hearted givers as those whose image-bearing is fully expressive in the offering-to-God register. Psa51:12 reveals by contrast: the loss of willing-heartedness through moral failure is a diminishment of the image's expressive capacity — the person who has lost the willing spirit cannot freely offer what the image calls for. The restoration sought in Psa51:12 is the restoration of the image's offering-capacity.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The Exodus tabernacle-building context (Exo35:5/22) carries typological resonance. The willing-hearted offering for the construction of the tabernacle (God's dwelling place among Israel) anticipates the NT community as the temple of the Spirit in which willing-hearted devotion is given. The pattern of free-will offering for God's dwelling place in the midst of his people is the OT type of the NT willing-hearted Spirit-dwelling in believers. Psa51:12's willing spirit as divine gift anticipates the NT giving of the Spirit who enables willing devotion (connected to the new covenant gift of the Spirit enabling genuine heartfelt obedience — Jer31:31–34 type/antitype, though outside corpus). The typological dimension is present in resonance rather than explicit deployment within §3.E alone.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The direction is *human-to-divine*: the human instances of willing-hearted offering for God's tabernacle (Exo35) and temple service (1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31) are types pointing toward the fullness of willing Spirit-given devotion. The human pattern of willing-heartedness in the OT tabernacle/temple register points toward its NT Christological and pneumatological fulfillment. Psa51:12's prayer for a willing spirit points toward the Spirit-given willing orientation that the new covenant grants — the type (prayer for a willing spirit) anticipates the antitype (Spirit-given willing spirit).

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Typological resonance is present in both the tabernacle-offering register (Exo35) and the willing-spirit-prayer register (Psa51:12) — see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2. The typological use is present through resonance and structural anticipation rather than explicit deployment in §3.E.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

*Re-reading §3.E fresh for T1's lens.*

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The characteristic is named Willing-heartedness. The compound name signals both its constitutional location (the heart) and its constitutive quality (willing — freely-moved, uncoerced). The name points immediately toward the inner source of the characteristic: it is not willing-ness in general but willing-ness that originates in the heart as the integrating centre of the person. The name also signals the absence of compulsion as constitutive: willing-heartedness is specifically the quality of the heart that moves freely rather than under external pressure. Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart" — and Psa51:12 — "a willing spirit" — together show the two constitutional locations (heart, spirit) that the compound name encompasses.

---

**T1.1.2 — What does the primary Hebrew term reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — H5081G na.div — adjective/noun from a root related to nadab (to give voluntarily, to offer freely). Na.div names one who is noble-hearted, generous, willing — specifically in the sense of freely-given inner readiness. The root nadab carries the sense of the will that springs forward freely rather than being pushed from behind. At the definitional level, the term reveals willing-heartedness as the inner posture of the will that is self-initiated and uncoerced — the will that moves toward giving and service from within rather than from external compulsion. The na.div is thus the freely-springing inner movement of the will toward God and others.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — All three. *Directional:* the freely-moved heart/spirit moves *toward* God and toward his service — Exo35:5 (toward the Lord's contribution), 2Ch29:31 (toward burnt offerings), Psa51:12 (toward joyful devotion). The direction is outward-Godward from the inner person. *Relational:* the characteristic is constitutively relational toward God — the free offering is always directed toward the one who calls for it (Exo35:5 — "a contribution to the Lord"). *Constitutional:* the characteristic is located in the heart (Exo35:5 — "of a generous/willing heart"; Exo35:22; 2Ch29:31 — "willing heart") and in the spirit (Psa51:12 — "a willing spirit"). Both the heart and spirit are named as constitutional locations.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus evidences willing-heartedness primarily as a *disposition and quality* of the heart and spirit. Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart" — naming the person by the disposition of their heart; Exo35:22 — "all who were of a willing heart" — the characterological description; 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man" — person-type vocabulary. Psa51:12 — "a willing spirit" — the spirit as a characterological disposition. The characteristic is the settled inner orientation of the will rather than a momentary act — it is what the heart and spirit are, not merely what they do at a given moment. However, it also produces acts (bringing offerings) — so it is a disposition that issues in acts.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The two VCGs evidence two constituent registers. VCG-01 (Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31): *freely-moved generosity* — the willing heart as the inner source of spontaneous, uncoerced giving and service toward God; the positive, active, outward-giving dimension. VCG-02 (Psa51:12): *divine-gift willing spirit* — willingness as something that can be lost and must be divinely restored; the vulnerability and restoration dimension. Together the two registers constitute the full characteristic: freely-given inner readiness (VCG-01) and the same readiness as divine gift that requires sustaining (VCG-02). The constituent elements are: spontaneous self-generated willingness (VCG-01) and received/restored willingness (VCG-02).

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The current best working description from the evidence: *Willing-heartedness (na.div) is the inner disposition of the heart and spirit in which the will moves freely and gladly toward giving and service to God — uncoerced, spontaneously motivated from within (Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31). This freely-moved inner readiness is the source of genuine offering and wholehearted service rather than compelled contribution. It is also a disposition that can be lost through moral failure and requires divine sustaining to persist (Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit"), positioning it as both a natural inner posture of the genuinely committed person and a gift of God to be sought and maintained.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The structural opposite is implied throughout the corpus as *reluctance, compulsion, and unwilling compliance* — the giving that happens under external pressure or obligation rather than from internal free movement. Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart let him bring" — implies that those whose hearts are not willing do not bring, or bring under compulsion. Psa51:12's request for a willing spirit implies the opposite state: the spirit that is not willing — dragged, heavy, unable to freely offer. Additionally, the structural opposite connects to M08 (Pride/self-assertion) and to the broader resistant will that self-protects rather than freely gives. Psa51:12's context of moral failure suggests the opposite state is the spirit burdened by guilt that cannot freely give.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a willing heart" — explicitly structures the offering around the inner condition rather than external compulsion: the characteristic excludes giving that originates in external pressure, social obligation, or duty-compliance rather than inner free movement. 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man who has skill" — the willingness is paired with skill but distinguished from compulsion: the characteristic excludes skilled service done under force or fear. Psa51:12's request for upholding implies the characteristic excludes the self-generated will that has collapsed — it is not mere willpower but the freely-springing inner readiness that can only be divinely sustained after failure.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three boundaries are supported. First, willing-heartedness is not *mere willpower or determination* — Psa51:12 ("uphold me with a willing spirit") evidences that genuine willingness cannot be sustained by sheer self-determination after moral failure; it requires divine upholding. The characteristic ends where it becomes self-reliant willpower. Second, willing-heartedness is not the same as *submission (CHAR-2)* — submission is the will's yielding to a specific authority; willing-heartedness is the will's spontaneous movement toward giving and service. The difference: submission is responsive to a command; willing-heartedness is self-initiated from inner motivation. Third, willing-heartedness is not *compelled compliance* — the corpus consistently names it as the non-compelled, freely-generated inner movement (Exo35:5/22 — not commanded contributions but voluntary).

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two distinct modes are evidenced corresponding to the two VCGs. First, *as spontaneous inner generosity* (VCG-01 — Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31): the characteristic operates as the freely-moving heart/spirit that generates the motivation for offering and service from within — no external push is required; the inner movement is the source. Second, *as a divine-sustained orientation* (VCG-02 — Psa51:12): the characteristic operates as a disposition that must be received and upheld by God — the penitent cannot self-generate the willing spirit after failure; it must be divinely gifted. Both modes operate in the same constitutional location (heart/spirit) but with different constitutional sources (self-generated vs divinely-upheld).

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. In the tabernacle/temple worship contexts (Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31), the characteristic operates as the positive freely-motivated orientation of the community toward God's service — the spontaneous generosity of those who are in good standing with God. In the penitential context (Psa51:12), the characteristic operates as a sought-restoration after moral failure — the person who has lost the spontaneous willing orientation prays for it to be divinely restored. The context thus determines whether the characteristic operates as a natural inner expression (VCG-01) or as a divinely-restored gift (VCG-02).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The five-verse corpus does not directly evidence a speech-based mode of operation for willing-heartedness. The characteristic is evidenced through physical acts of bringing offerings (Exo35; 2Ch29:31) and skilled service (1Ch28:21) rather than through verbal expression. Psa51:12's prayer is verbal but addresses the restoration of the characteristic rather than its exercise through speech.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The most immediate response evidenced is *the act of bringing/giving* — the freely-moved heart immediately issues in the outward act of offering. Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous heart, let him bring" — the willing heart and the bringing are directly connected without intervening friction. Exo35:22 — "all who were of a willing heart brought" — the aorist verb confirms the immediacy: the willing-hearted ones brought. 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man who has skill for any kind of service" — the willingness directly enables participation. The characteristic is its own immediate activation — no further inner preparation is required; the willing heart simply brings.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Consistent in VCG-01: in all four offering/service verses (Exo35:5/22; 1Ch28:21; 2Ch29:31), the immediate response is the outward act of bringing or serving — the willing heart produces the action without recorded inner conflict or delay. In VCG-02 (Psa51:12), the dynamic is different: the psalmist's immediate response is the prayer for restoration of the willing spirit, not the act of free giving. The VCG-01 pattern (immediate active giving) and VCG-02 pattern (immediate prayerful seeking) are consistent within their respective registers but different between registers.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The inner phenomenology of the person exercising the characteristic (what willing-heartedness feels like from the inside) is not directly addressed. The evidence describes the outer acts produced by the inner disposition rather than the felt inner experience of the person who is willing-hearted.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — 2Ch29:31 — "all who were of a willing heart brought burnt offerings" — this is within Hezekiah's reform context, suggesting the restoration of worship produces renewed willing-heartedness in the community; sustained worship and willing offering are mutually reinforcing. Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit" — implicitly names what sustained possession of the characteristic would produce: the ability to sustain joyful, freely-offered devotion over time. The sustained production is *habitual generous offering and wholehearted service* — the willing-hearted person becomes characterologically generous and devoted over time.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained possession establish?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus implies that sustained willing-heartedness establishes: *habitual generosity* (Exo35:5/22 — repeated free-will offering as the pattern of the willing-hearted community); *wholehearted skilled service* (1Ch28:21 — willingness enabling full engagement of skill); *sustained joyful devotion* (Psa51:12 — the willing spirit as the condition for joy-of-salvation and freely-offered devotion). Together: a stable orientation of the whole person toward freely-given service and worship without the friction of reluctance or compulsion.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. The immediate response (T1.5.1) is the specific act of bringing or serving prompted by the freely-moved inner orientation. The sustained effect is the characterological formation of a generous, devout person who habitually brings and serves from an inner that is consistently freely-moved. The immediate response is event-level; the sustained effect is character-level — the person becomes the willing-hearted person through repeated free-offering rather than merely performing willing-hearted acts on specific occasions.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus evidences two enabling conditions. First, *genuine inner-motivated orientation toward God*: Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart" — the heart's own genuine movement toward God rather than external compliance. The enabling condition is that the inner movement originates genuinely in the person's own heart rather than being externally produced. Second, *reception as divine gift* (Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit"): the enabling condition for the restoration of willing-heartedness after failure is divine upholding — the person must receive it from God rather than self-generate it. The enabling condition varies by register: in VCG-01, the enabling is genuine inner freedom; in VCG-02, the enabling is divine gift-reception.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus implies several blocking conditions. *Compulsion and external pressure*: the free-offering register (Exo35:5/22) implies that external compulsion blocks genuine willing-heartedness — commanded giving cannot produce willing-heartedness, only compliance. *Moral failure*: Psa51:12 — the request for restoration implies that moral failure disrupts or destroys the willing orientation, leaving the person unable to freely offer until the spirit is divinely restored. *Guilt and spiritual heaviness*: Psa51:12's penitential context implies that the burden of unresolved failure blocks the free movement of the spirit.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The corpus does not directly name the inner state of the person who encounters the occasion for willing-heartedness but whose heart is not willing. Exo35:5 implies that some in the community are not of a willing heart (only "whoever is of a willing heart" is called to bring) but does not describe those whose hearts are not willing. Psa51:12 indirectly names the unwilling-spirit state as the condition requiring divine restoration — but this is specifically post-failure, not a general description of the non-willing person.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary dimension is *volitional-affective* — the freely-moving inner orientation of the will combined with the generous, joyful inner quality of the movement. The characteristic is primarily about how the will moves (freely, without compulsion) and the inner quality of that movement (generous, willing, gladly — not dragged). Both elements are present: the volitional (the will's free movement) and the affective (the generous, joyful quality of the movement — Psa51:12 pairs the willing spirit with the joy of salvation). The two together constitute the primary dimension: the will that gladly and freely moves toward God and service.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Volitional support: Exo35:5 — "whoever is of a generous/willing heart let him bring" — the will's free movement toward the act; 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man" — the person characterised by their will's orientation; Psa51:12 — "a willing spirit" — the spirit's volitional quality. Affective support: 2Ch29:31 — "willing heart" in the context of festive worship restoration (joyful context); Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit" immediately following "restore to me the joy of your salvation" — the willing spirit is paired directly with joy, confirming the affective dimension.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Secondary dimensions are present. *Relational dimension (secondary):* the characteristic is constitutively relational-Godward — the freely-moved heart moves toward God's service (Exo35:5 — "contribution to the Lord"; 2Ch29:31 — temple worship). *Constitutional-location dimension (secondary):* both heart (VCG-01) and spirit (VCG-02) are named as constitutional locations — the characteristic operates at multiple constitutional levels. These do not compete with the volitional-affective primary but confirm the characteristic's constitutional and relational depth.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

*Re-reading §3.E fresh for T2's lens.*

**T2.1.1 — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, explicitly in Psa51:12 — "uphold me with a willing spirit" (ruach nedivah — a willing/free-spirited spirit). Ruach (spirit) is the explicit constitutional location in this verse. The willing spirit is the spirit characterised by free, uncoerced inner readiness for God — the spirit-level being the deepest constitutional location named for the characteristic in this corpus.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Psa51:12 evidences the spirit as the constitutional level at which willing-heartedness resides and from which it must be divinely restored after failure. The spirit is the origin point in the VCG-02 register — God upholds the spirit and the spirit's willing quality is thereby restored. In VCG-01, the heart is the primary constitutional location (Exo35:5/22; 2Ch29:31 — "willing heart"). Whether the spirit or heart is primary origin is register-dependent: in VCG-01, the heart; in VCG-02, the spirit.

---

**T2.1.3 — What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Psa51:12's spirit-level location reveals that willing-heartedness operates at the deepest constitutional level of the person — the spirit as the innermost faculty. When the spirit is burdened by moral failure (Psa51 context), the willing quality of the spirit is lost and cannot be self-restored; it requires divine upholding. This depth of constitutional location explains why willing-heartedness cannot be coerced: external compulsion cannot reach the spirit level to produce genuine willingness — it can only produce outer compliance while the spirit remains unwilling or burdened.

---

**T2.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence is not silent; spirit-level location is explicitly evidenced in Psa51:12 (see T2.1.1–T2.1.3).

---

**T2.2.1 — Is this characteristic identified as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The heart (lev) is explicitly named as a constitutional location: Exo35:5 — "generous/willing heart"; Exo35:22 — "willing heart"; 2Ch29:31 — "willing heart." In Hebrew anthropology, lev (heart) encompasses the soul's integrating functions (knowing, willing, feeling). The heart as constitutional location confirms soul-level engagement — the character is located in the soul's integrating centre.

---

**T2.2.2 — What does soul-level location reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The heart-location (Exo35:5/22; 2Ch29:31) reveals that willing-heartedness operates at the soul's integrating centre — not in a single faculty (will alone, or affect alone) but in the heart that integrates knowing, willing, and feeling together. The willing heart is one whose integrating centre is freely-moved toward God — the whole person's inner integration is oriented toward free giving rather than reluctant compliance. The soul-level location confirms that the characteristic is a whole-person orientation rather than a single-faculty operation.

---

**T2.2.3 — If the evidence is silent on soul-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence is not silent; soul-level location is explicitly evidenced through the heart-location in VCG-01 (see T2.2.1 and T2.2.2).

---
