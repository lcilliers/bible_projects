## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Both verses locate God's response to contrition as nearness, saving action, and indwelling presence: Psa34:18 — "the Lord is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit"; Isa57:15 — "I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." God is revealed as one who is drawn toward the crushed inner state rather than repelled by it — his nearness and dwelling are characteristically responsive to contrition. The character of God evidenced is that of one who inhabits both transcendence ("the high and holy place") and the broken interior of the contrite person — suggesting that contrition is the inner state most aligned with God's dwelling-presence.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The corpus does not explicitly attribute contrition to God. In both verses, God is the responsive agent — the one who draws near, saves, and revives — rather than the one who is contrite. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely in its direct attribution within this corpus: contrition is the inner state of the person; God is the one who responds to it with saving nearness.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The silence on God's own contrition is analytically significant. Contrition is structurally the inner state of one who has confronted their own failure or the holiness of another — a posture that presupposes moral limitation or transgression. God's possession of this characteristic in its creaturely form would be constitutionally incoherent. The silence therefore reveals that contrition is an exclusively creaturely characteristic — uniquely belonging to the moral creature who exists in the tension between their own failure and God's holiness. It does not image God by mirroring a divine quality but by being the inner state through which the creature's moral reality is honestly confronted before God.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Both verses evidence contrition as the inner condition that positions the person for divine encounter and restoration. Psa34:18 — God's nearness and saving action is the direct response to the crushed spirit; the purpose of contrition is to create the interior openness through which saving action enters. Isa57:15 — God's dwelling with and reviving of the contrite spirit identifies the characteristic's purpose as enabling divine indwelling and the reviving of spirit and heart. The characteristic equips the person to receive what God gives — saving nearness, reviving presence — by creating the inner poverty that does not resist or deflect divine action. Its purpose is receptive: making the person capable of receiving what pride and self-sufficiency prevent.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of original design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus strongly evidences contrition as a response to the fallen condition rather than part of original design. Both verses operate in the context of moral failure, limitation, and the recognition of one's true standing before God's holiness: Psa34:18 — "the brokenhearted" and "crushed in spirit" — language that presupposes a condition of inner breaking. Isa57:15 — "contrite" paired with "lowly" — the characteristic arises from genuine confrontation with failure or with divine holiness. There is no evidence in this corpus that contrition exists in the absence of the moral condition (failure, sin, guilt) that occasions it. The characteristic appears structurally responsive to the fallen condition: it is the right inner response to one's own moral reality before a holy God.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, indirectly. Isa57:15's "revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite" points toward a renewal and restoration — the contrite state is not a permanent condition but one through which God moves toward reviving. The reviving suggests a future state of greater vitality, not permanent brokenness. However, whether this reviving itself represents an eschatological orientation or a this-life restoration is not specified in the two-verse corpus. The evidence is limited to the trajectory of God's reviving response to contrition rather than an explicit eschatological telos.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Contrition expresses the divine image not by mirroring a divine quality but by enabling the human person's genuine relational encounter with God at the point of moral reality. Both verses evidence God's nearness to the contrite person (Psa34:18; Isa57:15) — the characteristic is the inner state that makes genuine divine-human encounter possible at the deepest level of the person's moral condition. The image is expressed through the person's capacity for honest self-confrontation before God's holiness — a capacity that only the being made in God's image can exercise. The characteristic thus expresses the image-bearing capacity for genuine moral self-knowledge and genuine relation with the holy God.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence positions contrition as exclusively creaturely (see T0.1.2 and T0.1.3). There is no divine possession of the characteristic in this corpus — only divine responsiveness to it. Contrition is a uniquely creaturely phenomenon that presupposes the moral structure of a being capable of failure before a holy God. It is not a creaturely analogue to something in God but a uniquely creaturely inner-being state that God responds to with nearness, saving action, and reviving.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The presence of genuine contrition reveals an image-bearer who has maintained the capacity for moral self-knowledge before God — the person who can be genuinely broken in spirit by their own failure is one whose conscience and moral awareness are functioning genuinely. The absence of contrition before genuine moral failure (its mirror being hardness, denial, or self-justification) reveals an image in distortion — one that has closed itself to the moral reality of its own condition. Isa57:15's pairing of contrition with lowliness suggests that the characteristic is the acute expression of the same receptive orientation toward God that lowliness describes dispositionally; its absence marks the person who is constitutionally closed to God's reviving work.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The Isa57:15 verse carries typological potential. The high-and-holy God who dwells with the contrite anticipates the Incarnation — the divine taking up residence with those who are broken, which the NT reads as fulfilled in Christ's presence among the poor, the sinners, and the brokenhearted. Psa34:18 in the Psalms context is part of the wisdom-sufferer tradition that NT authors read Christologically (Psa34 is cited in 1Pe3:10–12 in its broader context). However, the direct typological deployment of contrition as a type pointing to a Christological reality is not explicitly evidenced within the two-verse corpus of §3.B alone. The typological resonances are present in the broader canonical context but not directly evidenced in these two verses.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — To the extent typological resonance is present (T0.4.1), the direction appears to be human-to-divine: the contrite person's experience of God's drawing near (Psa34:18) and dwelling with them (Isa57:15) is a human instance that anticipates and points toward the fuller divine coming-to-the-broken in the Incarnation and the Spirit's indwelling of believers. The human instance of contrition opening to divine presence is a type of the fuller relational reality the gospel brings.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Typological resonance is present in the broader canonical context but not directly and explicitly deployed in the two-verse corpus (see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). The evidence is suggestive rather than demonstrative within this corpus alone.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

*Re-reading §3.B fresh for T1's lens.*

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic is named Contrition. The name derives from the Latin conterere (to grind, crush, wear down) — signalling that this is the inner state of being ground down or broken, not merely softened. The name signals an acute, physically-resonant inner reality: the crushed spirit (Psa34:18 — "crushed in spirit"), the broken heart (Psa34:18 — "brokenhearted"), the contrite spirit (Isa57:15). The name thus immediately conveys the severity and intensity of the inner state — not mild regret but genuine interior breaking before moral reality.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The sole term is H1793A dak.ka — an adjective/noun meaning crushed, pulverised, reduced to dust. The root dkk conveys thoroughgoing reduction to a lower state — not merely bent or lowered but ground down. In Psa34:18 it modifies the spirit ("the crushed/dak.ka in spirit"); in Isa57:15 it describes the "contrite and lowly spirit" alongside anaw/lowly. At the definitional level, the term reveals that contrition is the most acute and thoroughgoing form of interior diminishment — not a mild self-assessment but a crushing of the spirit's self-sufficiency and pride. The term's root in grinding/pulverising also suggests the process character of the state — it is the result of a crushing experience rather than a chosen posture.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — All three. *Constitutional:* Psa34:18 locates the characteristic in spirit ("crushed in spirit") and heart ("brokenhearted") — two explicit constitutional locations. *Relational:* both verses are constitutively Godward — contrition is the inner state before God specifically; Isa57:15 names God's dwelling with the contrite as the relational orientation. The characteristic does not exist in isolation but always in relation to the divine holy presence that occasions or responds to it. *Directional:* the crushing is downward — from self-sufficiency and pride toward genuine interior brokenness. The direction is the opposite of self-assertion; the crushed spirit is the one that no longer stands in self-sufficiency before God.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences contrition primarily as a *condition and state* rather than an act or disposition. The passive/resultant quality of dak.ka (crushed — the result of a grinding process) frames it as something the person finds themselves in rather than something they choose or generate. Psa34:18 ("brokenhearted," "crushed in spirit") names states of being. Isa57:15 ("who is of a contrite and lowly spirit") names a characterological state. However, since genuine contrition requires the person's genuine moral self-knowledge and openness to it, there is a volitional element: the person who genuinely confronts their failure rather than defending against it participates volitionally in the state's formation. The characteristic is primarily a *condition that contains volitional elements* — the person's willingness to remain in the crushed state rather than flee from it.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Within the two-verse corpus, the characteristic appears to have two constituent elements. First, the *brokenness/crushing element*: the spirit crushed, the heart broken — the interior collapse of self-sufficiency (Psa34:18). Second, the *lowliness element*: Isa57:15 pairs contrition with lowliness ("contrite and lowly in spirit") — the crushed state has a lowliness dimension that relates it to CHAR-1 Humility. The two elements are distinguishable: brokenness is the acute experiential condition; lowliness is the resulting relational posture before God. Together they constitute contrition as an acute crushing that produces a lowered relational posture before God.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The current best working description from the evidence: *Contrition is the acute, grief-laden inner state of being crushed and broken in spirit — the interior collapse that follows genuine confrontation with one's own moral failure or with the holiness of God. It is located in spirit and heart (Psa34:18; Isa57:15), paired with lowliness as its relational posture (Isa57:15), and is constitutively oriented toward God who responds to it with nearness, saving action, and reviving presence. It is the most intensive register of interior self-knowledge before God — not mild regret but genuine crushing of the inner person's self-sufficiency before divine holiness.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus does not name the structural opposite directly, but the contrast structure of Isa57:15 implies it: God dwells with "the contrite and lowly spirit" — implying he does not dwell in the same way with the non-contrite, self-sufficient, proud spirit. The structural opposite is the *hardened, proud, or self-sufficient spirit* that does not register or respond to its own moral condition before God. The dak.ka root (crushed) contrasts structurally with the hard or elevated — the spirit that has not been brought down or crushed by genuine moral confrontation.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic excludes *self-sufficiency and pride* as inner orientations: the crushed spirit (Psa34:18) is by definition one that no longer stands in proud self-sufficiency. It excludes *self-justification* — genuine contrition requires honest confrontation with moral failure rather than denial or defence. It excludes *detachment from one's own moral condition* — the broken heart (Psa34:18) is one that genuinely registers the weight of what has occurred rather than remaining analytically distant from it. The characteristic is incompatible with the self-protective deflection of genuine moral reality.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Two boundary distinctions are supported by the evidence. First, contrition is not the same as *humility* (CHAR-1) — Isa57:15 explicitly pairs them as distinct ("contrite and lowly in spirit") — they co-occur but are distinguishable. Humility is the stable dispositional posture; contrition is the acute experiential state specifically occasioned by moral failure or divine-holiness encounter. Second, contrition is not *despair* — both verses evidence God's responsive nearness and reviving as the outcome (Psa34:18 — saves; Isa57:15 — revives). The characteristic is acute inner breaking that opens toward God's restoration, not closing-down despair that excludes God. Where the opening toward God's reviving is absent, the inner state is not contrition but something else (despair, shame, or hardening).

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Two distinct modes are evidenced. First, as *acute experiential breaking*: the spirit is crushed, the heart is broken (Psa34:18) — the characteristic operates as an interior collapse that the person undergoes rather than controls. Second, as *an inner posture that opens toward God*: Isa57:15 — God "dwells with" the contrite — the characteristic operates as the inner state that makes divine indwelling possible; it is the condition that does not resist God's reviving presence but opens to receive it. The first mode is the experiential (what happens to the person); the second is the relational-receptive (what the state enables in the person's relation to God).

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The two-verse corpus does not directly evidence context-dependent variation. Both verses evidence the characteristic at the same constitutional levels (spirit and heart) and in the same relational direction (Godward). The Psa34:18 instance may be more crisis-precipitated (brokenhearted, crushed — acute states), while Isa57:15 presents the characteristic more as a sustained posture that God dwells with. The difference is between the acute moment of brokenness (Psa34:18 — crisis-precipitated) and the ongoing state that characterises the person (Isa57:15 — the one "who is of a contrite and lowly spirit"). Both registers are within the same characteristic; the variation is in temporal form (acute event vs ongoing condition) rather than direction or constitutional level.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The two-verse corpus does not directly evidence a speech-based mode of operation for contrition. The characteristic is evidenced as an inner state that God responds to, not as a verbal expression. Psalm 34 in its broader context involves praise and testimony, but within the two-verse evidence scope (Psa34:18 alone), the speech dimension is not addressed.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The immediate inner-being response evidenced is God's response to the contrite state: Psa34:18 — "the Lord is near" and "saves the crushed in spirit"; Isa57:15 — "I dwell with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." The immediate divine response is drawing near, saving, dwelling, and reviving. From the human side, the immediate response that the corpus evidences is the opening of the inner person toward God's action — the crushed spirit is one that does not resist God's nearness but is open to receive it. The corpus does not evidence an immediate human-experienced feeling that precedes or follows the state; the focus is on the divine response to the state.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The two-verse corpus is consistent: in both verses, the immediate divine response to contrition is presence and restoration (nearness + saving in Psa34:18; dwelling + reviving in Isa57:15). The direction and quality of divine response is consistent: God moves toward the contrite rather than away. The verbs vary (near, saves, dwells, revives) but all describe a consistent divine orientation of drawing near and restoring.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus addresses the divine immediate response extensively (see T1.5.1 and T1.5.2) but is silent on the human-experienced immediate inner response *to* the state of contrition — what it feels like to be in the state, or what immediately precedes or accompanies it from the human experiential side. The two-verse corpus focuses on what God does in response rather than on the human experiential phenomenology of the state itself.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The sustained production evidenced is: *reviving of spirit and heart* (Isa57:15 — "to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite"). The characteristic produces, through God's reviving response, a renewal of the inner person that the crushed state itself cannot produce — God's dwelling with the contrite is what generates the reviving. The long-term product of genuine contrition (as God responds to it) is thus the renewal and restoration of the spirit and heart that were crushed. Saving action (Psa34:18) implies a delivered state that follows the crushed state through God's intervention.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained possession establish?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The two-verse corpus is thin on sustained states beyond the immediate reviving. Isa57:15 — the revived spirit and heart — names restored vitality as the state that contrition occasions through God's response. The pairing of contrition with lowliness (Isa57:15) suggests that the sustained orientation following genuine contrition includes the lowly posture of CHAR-1 — the person who has been genuinely broken does not return to prideful self-sufficiency but retains a lowered relational posture toward God. However, this is inferential from the pairing rather than directly stated.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The immediate divine response is drawing near and saving (Psa34:18 — present-tense action). The sustained effect is the reviving of spirit and heart (Isa57:15 — a deeper renewal that goes beyond the moment of nearness to a thoroughgoing restoration of inner vitality). The immediate response is relational presence; the sustained effect is constitutional renewal. The characteristic thus opens the person to God's presence (immediate) which then produces the deeper work of reviving (sustained).

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences one primary enabling condition: the openness to genuine moral self-confrontation rather than self-defence. Both verses evidence the person who genuinely registers their own broken condition — "the brokenhearted" (Psa34:18) and "the contrite" (Isa57:15) are presented as those who have not fled from the reality of their inner state. The enabling condition is thus the absence of the self-protective deflection that would prevent the characteristic from taking hold. Additionally, Isa57:15's pairing with "lowly" suggests that the lowly posture (CHAR-1 — openness to God's authority and word) is a pre-condition for genuine contrition to emerge rather than hardened self-justification.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The structural opposites evidenced by implication are: *pride and hardness* — the elevated, self-sufficient spirit that does not register the weight of moral failure; *self-justification* — the inner defence that interprets the person's own condition as not-requiring-breaking; *hardening* — the refusal to allow the inner collapse that genuine contrition requires. The God who "dwells with the contrite" (Isa57:15) does not similarly dwell with the hardened or proud — implying that hardness and pride block the characteristic's reception by blocking the inner-collapse it requires.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The two-verse corpus does not directly name the inner state of the person who encounters the occasion for contrition but does not enter it. The evidence describes those who are contrite (the recipients of God's nearness and reviving) without describing those who refuse contrition. The structural implication is hardness, self-sufficiency, or unrepentance — but this is inferred from the contrast structure rather than directly evidenced in §3.B.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary dimension is *affective-existential*: the characteristic is primarily an intense inner experience of brokenness — the heart broken, the spirit crushed (Psa34:18). This is not primarily a volitional act (the person does not choose to be crushed in the way they choose to humble themselves), nor primarily a cognitive evaluation, but an acute inner suffering before God — an existential condition of the inner person's collapse. The affective-existential dimension — the state of being inwardly broken and crushed — is the primary constitutional character of contrition.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Supporting evidence: Psa34:18 — "brokenhearted" (nishbar-lev — the heart broken/shattered) and "crushed in spirit" (dak.ka-ruach — the spirit crushed/ground); these are acute experiential-constitutional states, not cognitive-volitional acts. Isa57:15 — "contrite and lowly spirit" (dak.ka-ve-shpal-ruach) — the spirit characterised by crushing and lowness as its state. The physical-resonance vocabulary (crushed, broken, pulverised) confirms that the primary dimension is the affective-experiential condition of intense inner suffering before God, not the volitional posture (CHAR-1 Humility) or the cognitive-evaluative act.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — do they compete with the primary?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Secondary dimensions are present. *Relational dimension (secondary):* the characteristic is constitutively Godward — it exists before God and draws God's response. *Volitional element (secondary):* the person who remains in the contrite state rather than fleeing into hardness exercises a passive-receptive volitional openness. These do not compete with the affective-existential primary but are the relational orientation and volitional element that enable the characteristic to be genuine contrition rather than mere emotional breakdown or despair. The affective-existential dimension is primary; the relational and volitional are enabling secondary dimensions.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

*Re-reading §3.B fresh for T2's lens.*

**T2.1.1 — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, explicitly in both verses. Psa34:18 — "the crushed in spirit" (dak.ka-ruach) — ruach is the Hebrew word for spirit; the spirit is the explicit constitutional location. Isa57:15 — "with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit" (dak.ka-ve-shpal-ruach) and "to revive the spirit of the lowly" — the spirit is named twice, establishing it as the primary constitutional site of the characteristic.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The primary vocabulary in both verses is ruach (spirit) — the spirit is the constitutional level most directly named as the location of the crushed/contrite state. The heart is also named (Psa34:18 — brokenhearted; Isa57:15 — "revive the heart of the contrite") but the spirit is the first-named and dominant location. The origin-point of the characteristic is the spirit-level — the crushing reaches the spirit first; the heart is the secondary constitutional level named in relation to it.

---

**T2.1.3 — What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Spirit-level location reveals that contrition is the deepest possible inner-being state — it reaches the innermost constitutional level of the person. A breaking that is only at the surface (emotion, behaviour) without reaching the spirit would not be genuine contrition in the biblical sense. Isa57:15's framing — the high-and-holy God dwelling with the contrite spirit — implies that the spirit-level location makes the characteristic the point of closest divine-human contact: where the person's spirit is genuinely crushed, God's spirit revives. The constitutional depth of the characteristic (spirit-level) is what makes it the site of the deepest divine-human encounter.

---

**T2.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence is not silent; spirit-level location is explicit and primary (see T2.1.1–T2.1.3).

---

**T2.2.1 — Is this characteristic identified as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The heart (lev/lebab in Hebrew) is explicitly named: Psa34:18 — "brokenhearted" (nishbar-lev). In Hebrew anthropology, lev (heart) encompasses what the programme framework would treat as soul-level functions (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness). The heart as constitutional location confirms soul-level operation alongside the spirit-level. Isa57:15 — "revive the heart of the contrite" — the heart's reviving as the outcome of God's dwelling with the contrite evidences the soul-level engagement.

---

**T2.2.2 — What does soul-level location reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Psa34:18's "brokenhearted" reveals that contrition operates at the soul's integrating centre — the heart as the seat of knowing, willing, feeling, and moral awareness is broken. This depth of soul-level operation confirms that contrition is not a surface emotional state but a condition that reaches the inner person's integrating faculty. Isa57:15's "revive the heart of the contrite" confirms that the soul-level operation requires divine reviving — the characteristic at soul-level depth cannot restore itself but requires God's reviving action.

---

**T2.2.3 — If the evidence is silent on soul-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence is not silent; soul-level location is explicitly evidenced through the heart-location in both verses (see T2.2.1 and T2.2.2).

---

**T2.3.1 — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, explicitly. Psa34:18 — "brokenhearted" (nishbar-lev — the heart/lev broken/shattered). Isa57:15 — "revive the heart of the contrite" — the heart is the constitutional level that receives God's reviving response to the contrite state. Heart-location is explicit in both verses, confirming it as a primary constitutional site alongside the spirit.

---

**T2.3.2 — What does the heart-location reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The heart's breaking (Psa34:18) reveals that contrition engages the heart's *integrating function comprehensively*: the heart as the centre of knowing, willing, feeling, and moral awareness is broken — not one function only but the integrating centre itself. When the heart is broken, the integrating function that holds the person together before God is genuinely disrupted. Isa57:15's "revive the heart" indicates that the restoration God provides is equally comprehensive — reaching the heart's integrating function to restore it. The heart-location confirms that contrition is a whole-person inner event, not a compartmentalised emotional state.

---

**T2.3.3 — If the evidence is silent on heart-location, note this explicitly.**
