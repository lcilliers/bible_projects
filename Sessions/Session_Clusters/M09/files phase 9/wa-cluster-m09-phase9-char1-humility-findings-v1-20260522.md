## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus reveals that God himself is the perceiver and responder to humility in the inner person, suggesting that humility corresponds to something in the divine nature that recognises and honours it. In the ka.na evidence, God repeatedly observes the inner act of humbling (2Ch12:7 — "they have humbled themselves; I will not destroy them"), responds to it with mercy (1Ki21:29; 2Ch12:12; 2Ch34:27), and explicitly contrasts it with proud self-assertion as the posture he opposes (Jam4:6 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble"; 1Pe5:5). Most significantly, Christ's self-humbling (Phili2:8 — "he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death") and his own self-description as "gentle and lowly in heart" (Mat11:29) place humility within the divine character itself. The characteristic thus reflects a God who, in the Incarnation, voluntarily takes the downward path — suggesting humility is not merely a creaturely virtue but an expression of the divine self-giving nature.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Scripture attributes humility to the incarnate Son explicitly and personally: Jesus names himself "gentle and lowly in heart" (Mat11:29) and the Phili2:8 passage depicts his self-humbling as a willed, downward movement of the self taking on creaturely form and accepting death. This attribution is remarkable: the characteristic most often associated with creaturely dependence is owned by the one who has no need of dependence. That the Son takes on humility voluntarily rather than by necessity elevates the characteristic's significance — in the human person, humility is not merely an appropriate response to creatureliness but a participation in the self-giving movement that characterises the Son's own inner life. The Phili2 context frames the human call to humility (v.3 — "count others more significant") explicitly as imitation of Christ's pattern (v.5–8), establishing a direct connexion between the divine attribution and the human calling.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence is not silent on this matter (see T0.1.2). However, where the corpus addresses humility in the Father rather than the Son, it is consistently indirect: God *responds to* humility, *requires* humility (Mic6:8 — "what does the Lord require of you but… to walk humbly with your God"), and *produces* humility in the person. The Father's own possessing of the characteristic is not directly asserted in the corpus — only the Son's. This asymmetry suggests the characteristic, while present in the divine life, takes its definitively creaturely form in the Incarnation: the form in which a being descends below its natural standing, which is structurally unavailable to the Father in the same way. The silence at the Father level is analytically significant: humility in the human person is imago Dei not by simple reflection of the Father's nature but by participation in the Son's kenotic movement.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences several purposes humility serves in the human person. First, it equips the person for genuine covenant relation with God: across the ka.na evidence (2Ch7:14; 2Ki22:19; 2Ch34:27), humbling oneself is the inner act that restores or maintains the covenantal relationship — it positions the person receptively before God's word and action. Second, it equips the person for wisdom: Pro11:2 — "with the humble is wisdom" — establishes humility as the inner disposition that opens the person to true understanding rather than closing them in self-sufficiency. Third, it equips the person for genuine community: Eph4:2 places humility as foundational to the corporate harmony of the body; Phili2:3 defines it as the cognitive reordering that enables genuine regard for others. Fourth, it is the inner posture through which God's reversal-work operates: Luk18:14 (the tax collector justified) and the exaltation-of-the-humble pattern evidence humility as the condition through which divine vindication moves.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence suggests humility operates in both registers, though the corpus is weighted toward the response-to-fallen-condition side. The ka.na evidence is almost entirely set within contexts of moral failure, covenant violation, and divine judgment — humbling before God is the required inner response when the people have sinned (2Ch7:14; 2Ch12:6; 2Ch33:12; Lev26:41). Similarly, the tapeinoō inversion pattern (Mat23:12; Luk18:14) addresses those who have exalted themselves wrongly. This strongly positions humility as the corrective posture of the fallen inner person. However, Mic6:8 ("walk humbly with your God") and the portrait of Christ's self-humbling (Phili2:8) point toward humility as a structural feature of right creaturely-to-Creator relation — not merely corrective but constitutive of what it means to be a creature before God. Phili2:3's call to count others more significant, framed as the character-garment of the renewed person (Col3:12), suggests humility belongs to the created design that the fall disrupts and renewal restores.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The exaltation-pattern provides the primary eschatological orientation: Mat18:4, Mat23:12, Luk14:11, Luk18:14, Jam4:10, 1Pe5:6 all link the present willed self-lowering to a future divine exaltation ("he will exalt you" — 1Pe5:6; "at the proper time he may exalt you" — 1Pe5:6). The present humility is explicitly the condition for a future vindication not yet realised. Phili3:21 adds a creaturely-eschatological dimension: the present "lowly body" (tapeinōsis) awaits transformation into conformity with Christ's glorious body — the experienced lowliness of the creaturely state is itself oriented toward a future glorification. The characteristic is thus doubly eschatological: the voluntary self-lowering of M09-A points toward divine exaltation, and the experienced creaturely lowliness of M09-B points toward bodily transformation.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Humility expresses the divine image most directly through the imitation of the Son's kenotic self-giving. The Phili2 passage uses Christ's self-humbling (v.8) as the explicit ground for the human call to humility (v.3 — "in humility count others more significant"), making the connexion between the divine image-prototype and the human image-bearer structural. The characteristic instantiates the aspect of the divine likeness that involves self-giving rather than self-assertion: God-in-Christ moves downward for the sake of others; the image-bearer reflects this in counting others more significant and voluntarily taking the lower place (Mat18:4; Phili2:3). More broadly, humility instantiates the creaturely capacity for right ordered relation to the Creator — the openness to God's word, authority, and reversal that constitutes the image-bearer as genuinely responsive rather than self-enclosed.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence positions humility as partially shared and partially creaturely-analogue. The shared dimension: Christ's self-humbling (Phili2:8; Mat11:29) establishes that the divine Son takes on the characteristic genuinely, not merely analogically. The creaturely-analogue dimension: the specific form of humility as response to one's own sin, limitation, or dependence (the dominant register in the ka.na corpus and the inversion pattern) is structurally unavailable to God. What is shared is the self-giving, downward movement — what the Father enacts in sending the Son and what the Son enacts in his kenosis. What is uniquely creaturely is the form of humility that names one's own failure, dependence, and need before a greater power. The characteristic thus has both a genuinely shared dimension (the kenotic movement) and a creaturely-analogue dimension (the creature's appropriate posture before Creator).

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus is explicit: the absence of humility is consistently linked to inner deterioration, moral failure, and divine opposition. 2Ch33:23 frames Amon's failure to humble himself as directly linked to increasing guilt; 2Ch36:12 identifies Zedekiah's refusal as the inner root of his evil; Psa107:12 evidences God actively bowing hearts down when the voluntary posture is absent — divine imposition replacing what the person will not offer. The structural contrast in Pro11:2 ("pride comes → disgrace; with the humble → wisdom") shows the absence of humility as the presence of pride — the distortion of the image most directly opposed to the divine self-giving nature. Conversely, the presence of humility (Luk18:14 — the tax collector who humbled himself goes home justified; 1Ki21:29 — Ahab's humbling delays judgment) reveals the image capable of right ordered response to God — the relational openness that constitutes the image-bearer as genuinely personal before God.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The OT ka.na evidence carries typological weight in two directions. First, the repeated pattern of Israel's kings humbling before God and receiving mercy (1Ki21:29; 2Ch12:7; 2Ch33:12; 2Ch34:27) functions as the narrative type of which Christ's self-humbling (Phili2:8) is the antitype — human figures humbling themselves before divine judgment prefigure the one who humbles himself definitively and without sin. Second, the tapeinōsis of Mary (Luk1:48 — "he has looked on the humble estate of his servant") carries typological Christological resonance: her lowly estate becomes the condition for the Incarnation, so that her tapeinōsis participates in the broader movement of God choosing the low and despised things (1Cor1:28 — outside this corpus but structurally related). Act8:33 (Christ's humiliation — "in his humiliation justice was denied him") explicitly interprets Isa53 as Christological fulfillment of the suffering-servant type.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The typology runs in both directions, and the evidence is explicit about this. In the Phili2 passage, the divine instance (Christ's self-humbling, v.8) explicitly establishes the pattern for the human (v.3 — "in humility count others more significant"), making the direction divine-to-human. However, in the OT ka.na narrative (1Ki21:29; 2Ch7:14; 2Ch33:12), the human instances of humbling before God point forward toward the one who will do so definitively — the human-to-divine direction. The dual directionality is itself significant: Christ's humbling is simultaneously the antitype of OT human humbling patterns and the prototype of NT Christian humility. The typological structure thus frames humility as the characteristic through which the divine and human meet — the Son condescends to take on the creaturely form of humility, and in doing so validates and redeems it as the primary posture of the creature before God.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Typological use is evidenced (see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is answered by those findings.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

*Re-reading §3 verse-meanings fresh for T1's lens: what kind of phenomenon is humility, how does it operate, what are its modes, responses, effects, conditions, and dimensional classification?*

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is named Humility. The name signals a fundamentally relational orientation: to be humble is to be in a certain posture relative to God and to others — not a self-enclosed state but a positioned one. The name encompasses both the willed act of self-lowering (M09-A — the voluntary disposition) and the experienced state of being low (M09-B — the condition register), indicating that humility in Scripture names both what the person does and what the person finds themselves to be. The Hebrew terms tsa.na and tsa.nu.a (walking humbly, the humble) and ka.na (humbling, being humbled) carry relational direction built into their structure — humbling is always humbling *before* someone. The Greek tapeinos/tapeinofrosunē/tapeinoō family's root (tapeinos — low, base, not elevated) signals that the characteristic is defined by position: the person places themselves or finds themselves below their natural or claimed standing.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — At the definitional level, the Hebrew ka.na reveals humility as primarily an act: the verb form dominates the corpus, and almost every ka.na instance is the person *humbling* themselves or being humbled — a dynamic, event-like characteristic rather than a static trait. The Greek tapein- root reveals humility as primarily a disposition and quality: tapeinos (the adjective — "lowly in heart," Mat11:29) and tapeinofrosunē (the noun of quality — "with all humility," Eph4:2; Act20:19) name what the person *is*, while tapeinoō (the verb) names what the person *does* (Phili2:8; Jam4:10). Tapeinōsis names the *state* of being in lowliness (Luk1:48; Phili3:21). Together the vocabulary presents humility as simultaneously act (the willed event), disposition (the settled character), and condition (the state in which one finds oneself) — three registers that the volume-split between M09-A and M09-B captures structurally.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, all three. *Directional:* humility is consistently downward — toward God (Jam4:10; 1Pe5:6; 2Ch7:14), toward others (Phili2:3; Eph4:2; 1Pe5:5), and away from self-elevation (Mat23:12; Luk14:11). The direction is built into the name. *Relational:* ka.na and the Greek tapein- terms are almost never reflexive-only; they name a posture before another (before God, before elders, before one another). The relational target is structural to the characteristic, not incidental. *Constitutional:* Mat11:29 locates lowliness explicitly "in the heart" — the innermost personal centre. Lev26:41 names the "uncircumcised heart" that must be broken for humility to emerge. The characteristic is constitutionally interior: the relational posture originates from the heart's orientation, not from external behaviour alone.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences all four. As *act*: the ka.na verbal corpus presents humbling as a single datable event — Manasseh humbled himself greatly in distress (2Ch33:12), Josiah humbled himself when he heard God's words (2Ch34:27), Jam4:10 commands "humble yourselves before the Lord." As *disposition*: tapeinofrosunē in Act20:19 names Paul's pervading manner of ministry; in Eph4:2 and Col3:12 it is a character-garment worn continuously. As *condition*: tapeinōsis in Luk1:48 names Mary's estate; Phili3:21 names the lowly body as the present creaturely condition. As *quality*: Mat11:29 names lowliness in the heart as a character quality Jesus invites others to learn. The characteristic is thus genuinely multi-modal — it operates as an event, a character-disposition, a condition one inhabits, and a quality of inner being, with the corpus distributing examples across all four.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Humility has constituent elements visible at first encounter. The volume-split between M09-A (willed self-lowering) and M09-B (experienced/imposed lowliness) represents the primary structural distinction: the voluntary-dispositional register and the condition-state register are genuinely different in structure even when they name the same faculty. Within M09-A, further constituent elements appear: (a) the cognitive dimension — Phili2:3 defines humility as *counting others more significant* (a volitional cognitive act); (b) the affective dimension — 2Ki22:19 pairs humbling with weeping as its expression; (c) the volitional dimension — tapeinoō throughout is the deliberate act of the will bowing down. The Colossian material (Col2:18; Col2:23) adds a fourth: the authenticity dimension — the distinction between performed humility and genuine inner transformation. The characteristic is thus structured rather than simple, with cognitive, affective, volitional, and authenticity constituents.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The current best working description, drawn from the evidence corpus: *Humility is the inner disposition in which the person voluntarily relinquishes the claim to their natural or assumed standing — before God, in response to his word and authority, and before others, in recognition of their worth — while also naming the state in which the person finds themselves lowered by circumstance, affliction, or divine action. It is constitutionally located in the heart (Mat11:29; Lev26:41), engages the will (Jam4:10; Phili2:8), cognitive evaluation (Phili2:3), and affect (2Ki22:19; 2Ch34:27), and is characterised by an openness to God and others that self-assertion closes.* The M09-A register (willed self-lowering) represents the primary characteristic; the M09-B register (experienced lowliness) represents the same faculty operating under external pressure or as the state in which the person awaits God's reversal.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus names the structural opposite explicitly and repeatedly: pride (Hebrew ga.on / Greek hyperēphanos). Pro11:2 sets the direct antithesis: "when pride comes, then comes disgrace; with the humble is wisdom." Jam4:6 and 1Pe5:5 both cite the same formula: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble" — structurally pairing the proud and the humble as the two positions before God. Mat23:12 and its parallels (Luk14:11; Luk18:14) oppose the one who "exalts himself" against the one who "humbles himself." 2Ch32:26 identifies Hezekiah's humbling as explicitly "for the pride of his heart" — pride is the inner condition humility addresses and replaces. The structural opposite is pride as the inner posture of self-assertion, self-elevation, and refusal to acknowledge one's true standing before God and others.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus reveals several explicit exclusions. (a) *Self-exaltation:* Mat23:12 — the self-exalting and the self-humbling are direct opposites; the humble do not assert their standing. (b) *Conceit and selfish ambition:* Phili2:3 — humility is defined as the direct counter to conceit (kenodoxia) and selfish ambition (eritheia). (c) *Performed or externally-driven self-lowering:* Col2:18 and Col2:23 expose humility that is outward performance masking inner pride — the characteristic explicitly excludes pseudo-humility that is self-focused. (d) *Haughtiness and social hierarchy:* Rom12:16 — associating with the lowly is contrasted with being "haughty" and being "wise in your own sight." (e) *The refusal to hear God's word:* 2Ch36:12 — Zedekiah's refusal to humble himself before the prophet is his failure to receive divine address; humility includes openness to being spoken to and corrected by God.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The Colossian material makes the clearest boundary statement: Col2:18 and Col2:23 distinguish humility from *performed asceticism and self-denial* — outward severity to the body that has the *appearance* of wisdom and humility but cannot touch the inner disposition. Humility is not the same as self-abasement for its own sake or as social display. Additionally, the volume-split itself defines a boundary: humility (CHAR-1) is distinct from *contrition* (CHAR-3 in M09 — dak.ka), which is the acute, grief-laden form specifically following moral failure. Contrition is the crisis-precipitated acute form; humility is the stable disposition and its experiential states. A further boundary: humility is not the same as *submission to authority* (CHAR-2 — the M09-C/D register), though the two overlap and interact. Submission is the will's yielding to a specific authority; humility is the inner posture of the whole person before God and others generally.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences at least four distinct modes of inner operation. (a) *As an initiating act of the will:* humbling oneself (tapeinoō in Jam4:10; 1Pe5:6; 2Ch7:14) is a deliberate volitional event that initiates a posture before God. (b) *As a pervading disposition of character:* tapeinofrosunē in Act20:19 pervades Paul's whole manner of ministry; in Eph4:2 it characterises every relational interaction — the characteristic colours the whole inner person rather than emerging only on occasion. (c) *As a cognitive reordering:* Phili2:3 — humility is the act of *counting others more significant than oneself* — a deliberate cognitive re-evaluation of relative worth. (d) *As an inner response to divine address:* in the ka.na corpus, humbling is the inner movement of the heart when confronted with God's word, judgment, or claim (2Ch34:27; 2Ch36:12 negative instance). The four modes are distinguishable: volitional, dispositional, cognitive, and responsive.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the evidence shows context-dependent variation. *Direction-vertical (human-to-God):* the ka.na corpus is almost entirely vertical — humbling *before* God in response to his word or judgment. The Godward direction dominates the Hebrew register. *Direction-horizontal (human-to-human):* Phili2:3 (counting others more significant), Eph4:2 (foundational to corporate harmony), 1Pe5:5 (clothing oneself with humility toward one another) — the Greek register frequently operates in the horizontal direction. *Constitutional level variation:* the act of humbling (volitional, initiating — ka.na verbal) operates at the will level; the pervading disposition (tapeinofrosunē as character-garment) operates as a deeper character-level orientation; the condition of lowliness (tapeinōsis) operates as a state the person inhabits at multiple constitutional levels simultaneously. The characteristic is thus genuinely multi-directional and multi-level in its operation.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows a limited but real speech-adjacent mode. In the ka.na corpus, the outward expression of the inner humbling is often paired with speech-acts: 2Ch12:6 — the princes "humble themselves and said, 'The Lord is righteous'" — verbal acknowledgment of God's rightness is the expression of the inner act. 2Ki22:19 and 2Ch34:27 pair the humbling with weeping and tearing clothes (embodied expression) but also with the act of receiving God's spoken word as addressed to oneself. The characteristic does not primarily operate through speech but it regularly issues in speech-acts of acknowledgment (confession, praise, verbal self-lowering) and is itself awakened by the reception of spoken word (God's address through the prophets). 2Cor10:1 (Paul's self-effacement in personal presence) suggests the characteristic also shapes the manner and register of speech rather than its content alone.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Where the corpus describes an immediate response, it is typically a relational opening toward God: in the ka.na narrative, the immediate correlate of humbling is God *hearing* the person — "I also have heard you" (2Ki22:19; 2Ch34:27). The inner movement is toward receptivity: the heart that was hardened or self-asserting becomes open to God's word, mercy, and address. In the NT register, the immediate relational opening is toward others: Eph4:2 positions humility as the disposition that enables bearing with one another; the tax collector in Luk18:14 goes home justified immediately following his posture of self-lowering before God. The characteristic thus produces an immediate relational opening — toward God in the OT register, toward divine justification/mercy in the NT. There is no evidence of delayed or deferred immediate response; the opening appears to be structurally immediate.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The immediate response is broadly consistent in direction (relational opening and receptivity) but varies in target and register. In the ka.na corpus, the immediate correlate is consistently divine perception and responsive mercy — God sees the humbling and responds (1Ki21:29; 2Ch12:7; 2Ch33:12). In the tapeinoō corpus, the immediate correlate is divine exaltation or justification (Luk18:14; Jam4:10; 1Pe5:6). In the tapeinofrosunē corpus, the immediate relational effect is community harmony and genuine regard for others (Phili2:3; Eph4:2). The variation is in the *target* of the opening (God's mercy, divine exaltation, or human regard) rather than in the structural character of the response (relational opening). The consistency across the variation is the directional movement from self-enclosure to openness.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence addresses immediate response extensively. The M09-B register (experienced lowliness — 2Cor7:6; 2Ch28:19; Psa107:12) is the partial exception: the imposed-lowering verses describe the state of being brought low but do not always record an immediate inner response from the person who is lowered. 2Cor7:6 ("God who comforts the downcast, comforted us by the coming of Titus") names God's response to the state but the person's immediate inner response is implicit rather than named. This partial silence is analytically significant: in the imposed-lowering register, the inner response is not automatically generated — the person may experience the lowered state without the relational opening that characterises the voluntary register. The two registers thus differ in their immediate response profile.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sustained operation of humility produces several inner-being outcomes across the corpus. (a) *Wisdom:* Pro11:2 — "with the humble is wisdom" — sustained humility as the inner disposition that opens the person to true understanding. (b) *Contentment:* Phili4:12 — Paul has "learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger" through repeated experience of being brought low (tapeinoō); the sustained navigating of lowliness produces a cultivated stability. (c) *Corporate harmony:* Eph4:2 — humility as the foundational disposition enabling the sustained bearing of one another in love. (d) *Divine favour:* the sustained pattern across the ka.na corpus is that sustained humility before God is the inner condition that maintains the covenantal relationship and averts judgment. (e) *Character formation:* Col3:12 — humility is a garment deliberately put on as part of the character-wardrobe of the renewed person; sustained wearing shapes the whole character.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences the following established states and qualities. *Relational openness:* sustained humility before God (the ka.na pattern) establishes and maintains the openness that characterises genuine covenant relation — the person who habitually humbles is consistently accessible to God's word and responsive to his address. *Self-knowledge:* 2Ch32:26 (Hezekiah humbling in response to "the pride of his heart") evidences that humility produces accurate inner self-assessment — the person who humbles genuinely is one who can identify their own fault. *Security:* Mat11:29 — Christ's own lowliness-in-heart is what makes his yoke restful ("you will find rest for your souls"); sustained possession of the characteristic produces rest rather than the anxiety of self-maintenance and status-protection. *Teachability:* Act20:19 — Paul's sustained humility through trials maintains his ministry posture; sustained humility sustains openness to correction and formation.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The immediate response (T1.5.1) is primarily relational opening — the moment of humbling produces immediate access to God's mercy or divine exaltation. The sustained effect differs in kind: it is characterological rather than eventive. Phili4:12 ("I have *learned* the secret of facing… being brought low") evidences that sustained navigation of the lowliness condition produces a *learned* stability — a cultivated inner quality rather than a moment of opening.
