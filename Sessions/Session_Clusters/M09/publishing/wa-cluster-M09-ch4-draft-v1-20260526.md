## 4. Where each characteristic lives in the person

Each of the six characteristics in this study inhabits the inner person differently — in different locations, engaging different inner capacities. This chapter traces each one to its primary home in the inner life and examines which of the person's capacities it brings into play.


### 4.1 Humility — willed self-lowering

Humility lives primarily in the heart — the seat, in Scripture's account of the inner person, of will, orientation, and the deepest dispositions. The most direct locating statement comes from Jesus himself: "I am gentle and lowly in heart" (Mat 11:29). The heart is named explicitly as the home of lowliness; not the mind alone, not outward conduct, not reputation — the heart. This is consistent with the Old Testament evidence: when 2 Chronicles 7:14 calls the people to humble themselves and pray and seek God's face, the humbling is not primarily a public act but an inner reorientation of the whole person toward God, and the heart is its centre. The heart as the inner location of humility means that humility is not accessible to the surface of the person — it cannot be performed from the outside in.

**Will.** The will is the primary inner capacity humility engages, and this is confirmed across both the Hebrew and Greek evidence. The voluntary humbling instances — 2 Chronicles 7:14 with its "humble themselves," Luke 18:14 with "the one who humbles himself" — all position humility as a willed act. Philippians 2:8 presents the clearest case in the divine register: "he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." The self-humbling is not a passive receiving but a chosen downward movement. The will's engagement is constitutive of humility: without the willing, it becomes something else — lowliness perhaps, or externally imposed condition, but not humility in this form.

**Cognition.** The cognitive capacity is also directly engaged. Philippians 2:3 establishes this: "in humility count others more significant than yourselves." The verb is cognitive — "count," "reckon," "estimate" — and it points to a deliberate inner assessment of relative worth. Humility requires the person to think something about themselves and about others, and to act from that thinking. This is not mere feeling; it is an inner act of re-ordering the self's self-estimate in relation to others. The cognitive dimension distinguishes genuine humility from performed lowliness: genuine humility believes the counting it enacts.

**Affect.** The affective capacity is engaged in the Hebrew evidence: the instances of weeping that accompany genuine humbling — kings who humble themselves in 2 Kings 22:19 do so with tears — show that the inner movement of humility is not cold calculation but involves genuine feeling. The heart that is tender toward God's word is also the heart that is moved, that responds with something beyond detachment. The affective engagement is not primary (as it is in contrition) but it is present, confirming that humility engages the whole person.

**The divine-versus-human distinction.** The evidence shows a significant difference between the divine and human registers. When the incarnate Son is named as "lowly in heart," the characteristic is genuinely his — it belongs to the inner person of the Son who has taken on creaturely existence. When the same characteristic is addressed to the believer — "humble yourselves" (Jam 4:10), "clothe yourselves with humility" (1Pe 5:5) — the language is imperative, directional: this is something to be taken on, put on, actively moved toward. The human engagement with humility is always an act of formation and will; the divine-incarnate instance is not the same process, though the inner state it produces is recognisably the same. The evidence does not address humility as an attribute of the Father or the Spirit in the same mode.

**The authenticity dimension.** The evidence also introduces an important internal distinction: Colossians 2:18 and 2:23 name the problem of performed pseudo-humility — a humility that has the appearance but not the inner reality. This marks out the inner location as the decisive one: humility that does not originate in the heart and will is not the characteristic named in this study. The inner location is not merely one of several possible sites; it is the site that distinguishes genuine humility from its imitation.

Taken together, the inner topography of humility — willed self-lowering is that of a heart-located, will-led, cognitively-enacted, affectively-accompanied movement downward: not a single-faculty event but an engagement of the whole inner person toward a voluntary lowering of the self.

### 4.2 Lowliness — experienced and imposed state

Lowliness — experienced and imposed state — also lives in the heart, but the mode of inhabitation is different from humility. In the voluntary form, the will places itself low; in the state-form, the heart is the site where the lowering is received and experienced, not necessarily the initiating agent of it. Psalm 107:12 is the most direct locating statement: "he bowed their hearts down with hard labor; they fell down, with none to help." The heart is the named inner site — and the bowing of it is done from outside. What occupies the heart here is not the act of self-lowering but the condition of having been lowered. The heart is the place where the imposed state lands, where it is felt and endured and navigated.

**The receiving capacities.** The inner capacities most clearly engaged by lowliness as experienced state are those of feeling, endurance, and — distinctively — the formation of contentment. Luke 1:48 presents Mary's tapeinōsis — her humble estate — as the outer condition God looked upon; the inner response implied is one of faithful waiting in the condition given. Paul's account in Philippians 4:12 makes the formation of inner capacity explicit: "I know how to be brought low, and I know how to abound. In any and every circumstance, I have learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger, abundance and need." The inner capacity formed through the experience of being brought low is contentment — a settled equanimity that the condition has produced rather than the person having generated it independently.

**Agency and will.** The will is engaged differently here than in humility. In lowliness the will does not initiate the downward movement; it responds to a condition already present. The willed act available to the person in lowliness is not "humble myself" but rather "endure this, receive it, do not resist God's hand in it." This is still volitional — Paul's "I have learned" shows active inner engagement — but the agency is responsive rather than initiating.

**The transformation dynamic.** The evidence shows that lowliness as a received state has its own inner transformation pattern. The person who is brought low and endures — who receives the imposed state with a heart oriented toward God — moves from the state of lowliness toward a stability (Phili 4:12) or toward divine lifting (Luk 1:48, 1:52). The inner capacity that enduring lowliness forms is thus not identical to voluntary humility but is the creaturely analogue of it shaped by circumstance rather than by choice.

**The verse evidence is thin.** The three key verses for this characteristic — Psalm 107:12, Luke 1:48, and Philippians 4:12 — are the primary evidence base, and they address the heart as location, the condition as received rather than chosen, and the formation of contentment as the characteristic inner response. Broader inner topography — specific engagement of conscience, memory, or perception — is not directly evidenced in this corpus. The inner picture this evidence supports is therefore limited to what these three verses carry, and claims beyond them are not warranted by the evidence available.

The inner home of lowliness — experienced and imposed state is the heart as the receiving place of an externally placed condition: a site not of initiating movement but of the formation of patient endurance and, where God acts, the starting point for his lifting.

_Meaning of the term here:_ Being brought low (tapeinoō) is a circumstantial, experiential state Paul has passed through and learned to navigate; here the inner quality is a cultivated contentment enabling him to endure lowness without loss of peace or stability.


### 4.3 Submission — inner disposition of will

Submission — inner disposition of will — lives most explicitly in the heart and the conscience, and the verse evidence locates it there with notable precision. Romans 6:17 establishes the primary location: "you have become obedient from the heart to the standard of teaching to which you were committed." The heart is named as the origin and seat of the obedience; the movement of the will outward into compliance begins there. This is not submission as an external behaviour but as an inner orientation — the heart that has been genuinely aligned with a pattern of authority, not merely conforming to it under external pressure.

**Will.** The will is the primary inner capacity that submission — inner disposition engages, and this is confirmed by both the directness of the locating language and by the contexts of the evidence. Hebrews 5:8 shows what inner-submission looks like at the most extreme level: "although he was a son, he learned obedience through what he suffered." The Son's submission is a willed yielding under pressure, a disposition of the will shaped through the experience of costly engagement with the Father's call. The will's engagement in submission is not passive; it is active even when it is receptive — the will that submits chooses to place itself under.

**Conscience.** The conscience is also directly engaged. Philippians 2:12 shows this clearly: "as you have always obeyed, so now, not only as in my presence but much more in my absence, work out your own salvation with fear and trembling." The obedience that continues in the absence of external accountability is obedience rooted in conscience — the inner moral sense that recognises the authority of the claim even when no one is watching. The fear and trembling that accompanies it is not terror but the conscience's active engagement with the reality of being before God. Submission that has no root in conscience is not the characteristic named here; it is eye-service, which the evidence explicitly excludes.

**Mind.** Second Corinthians 10:5 extends the inner topography to include the cognitive level: "we destroy arguments and every lofty opinion raised against the knowledge of God, and take every thought captive to obey Christ." The obedience here is specifically cognitive — it is the submission of the thinking self, the placing of every intellectual construct under Christ's authority. This is perhaps the most demanding inner-topographic claim in the evidence: not only the will and conscience but the entire thought-life is the territory of submission. The inner capacity of mind is not exempt from the characteristic's reach; on the contrary, the cognitive level is where the most active resistance to submission tends to arise, and it is named explicitly as the site of the battle.

**The learned quality.** The evidence at Hebrews 5:8 introduces a dimension specific to the inner topography of this characteristic: it is learned. The inner disposition of submission is not naturally given but formed through the will's costly engagement with hardship under authority. This formation dynamic places submission in the context of inner development — the conscience and will are not simply switched on in the mode of submission; they are shaped into it over time. This has implications for where submission lives: it is not a surface-level inner event but a deep disposition formed by sustained engagement with legitimate authority's claims.

**The divine-versus-human distinction.** In the evidence, submission as inner disposition is explicitly evidenced in Christ as the one who "learned obedience through what he suffered" — the disposition is genuinely his, formed through his incarnate engagement with the Father's will. For the believer, it is evidenced as something that has been formed through the gospel's transformation of the heart (Rom 6:17) and through sustained practice under authority. The inner topography is shared between the divine-incarnate and the human, though the mode of formation differs.

The inner home of submission — inner disposition of will is the heart-conscience-mind complex: a characteristic that occupies the deepest inner territory and engages the will, conscience, and cognitive life together in a yielding that, where genuine, is held in the absence of any external enforcement.

### 4.4 Submission — relational pattern of obedience

Submission as a relational pattern of obedience occupies much of the same inner ground as the inner disposition form — the will, conscience, and mind are all engaged — but the evidence for this form foregrounds the way the inner state extends outward into recognisable patterns of life in relationship. The inner topography here is best understood as the inside of the relational expression: the obedience-of-faith named in Romans 1:5 is the inner-relational event behind the observable pattern of people from all nations being brought under Christ's authority.

**Will directed outward.** The distinctive inner feature of this form is that the will's submission is directed toward a specific relational structure — a named authority, a recognised community pattern, a concrete relationship. Ephesians 6:1 grounds it clearly: "Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right." The will here is not simply yielding to a general claim of authority but to a specific relational instance of it. The phrase "in the Lord" — grounding the relational submission in divine right — shows that the inner capacity engaged is conscience recognising divine authority mediated through a human relationship.

**The eschatological horizon.** Genesis 49:10 opens the widest inner-topographic vista in the evidence for this characteristic: "and to him shall be the obedience of the peoples." This is not a description of individual inner life but of the inner posture of whole peoples directed toward a coming ruler. The inner capacity named here is the capacity for allegiance — the orientation of a people's will toward a sovereign. The eschatological dimension does not remove the inner topography but places it in the largest possible frame: submission as inner disposition, writ across human history.

**The limits of the evidence.** The evidence for this form — four key verses — addresses the relational pattern more than it maps detailed inner topography. The inner location is consistently the will and conscience, directed specifically toward a concrete authority relationship. Memory, perception, and the detailed engagement of cognitive faculties are not directly evidenced. The inner picture is real but not elaborated beyond what these verses carry.

The inner home of submission — relational pattern of obedience is the will-and-conscience as they engage specific relational structures of authority: from the family, to the community of faith, to the gospel's reach among all nations, and ultimately to the eschatological submission of peoples to Christ.


**Key verses for Submission — relational pattern of obedience:**

_Meaning of the term here:_ Obedience of faith is the inner-relational response to the gospel that Paul's apostleship aims to produce among the nations; it is directed toward God's name and involves volitional submission to the proclaimed word.

**Heb 5:9** (G5219 hupakouō) [M09-D-VCG-02]
> Heb 5:9 And being made perfect , he became the source of eternal salvation to all who obey him ,
_Meaning of the term here:_ Christ becomes salvation's source only to those who obey him; obedience is the ongoing inner orientation of trust and submission that connects the believer to salvation, making it a condition of the will inseparable from receiving Christ's benefit.

> Eph 6:1 Children , obey your parents in the Lord , for this is right .
_Meaning of the term here:_ Children's obedience to parents is grounded in the Lord—making it a theologically motivated act of the will, not mere social compliance; the phrase 'in the Lord' frames it as an inner orientation of conscience toward divine right.

_Meaning of the term here:_ The peoples' obedience is a willing, inner yielding of allegiance directed toward Judah's coming ruler, framed as a future gathering of nations under his sovereign authority.


### 4.5 Contrition — crushed and broken spirit

Contrition lives in the spirit and the heart together — the two inner named locations that Isaiah 57:15 identifies with unusual precision: "I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." The verse places contrition simultaneously in the spirit and in the heart, and it is both locations that God addresses in his reviving response. The spirit — the innermost depth of the person's life before God — and the heart — the seat of will, feeling, and orientation — are named as the twin home of this characteristic.

**The spirit as primary location.** Of the two named locations, the spirit carries the more concentrated description. The term for contrition — dak.ka — means crushed, ground fine; it applies to the spirit in the first instance. This places contrition at the deepest inner level: not the surface of feeling, not the outer conduct, not even the decision-making layer of the will — but the spirit, the innermost mode of the person's existence before God. A crushed spirit is not a bad mood or a passing wave of regret; it is the inner person's most fundamental orientation broken open by the weight of what God is and what the person is before him.

**The heart as companion location.** The heart appears alongside the spirit in Isaiah 57:15, and God's reviving response addresses both: "to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." This co-location suggests that contrition in its full form is not confined to the spirit alone; it penetrates to the heart — the will and feeling and orientation-centre of the person — and it is this penetration that makes contrition what it is rather than a merely intellectual acknowledgment of failure. A contrite heart is a heart that has genuinely felt the weight of the moral reality, not merely registered it cognitively.

**The affective capacity.** Of all the inner capacities, the affective one is most clearly central to contrition. The spirit that is crushed is the spirit that has been moved — profoundly, acutely, beyond management. Psalm 34:18, which names the same territory ("the Lord is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit"), uses brokenhearted alongside crushed in spirit, confirming the affective depth of the inner location. Contrition is not cold assessment; it is the inner person shaken at its roots by what it has encountered.

**What is silent.** The evidence for contrition in this study rests on two verses — Isaiah 57:15 and Psalm 34:18 — and the inner topography they provide, while clear on spirit, heart, and affect, does not extend to detailed descriptions of how cognition, will, conscience, or perception are engaged. That the will is involved is implied — genuine contrition is not merely passive, but the evidence does not elaborate a volitional dimension. That the conscience is implicated is analytically plausible given the nature of the characteristic, but the named verses do not make this explicit. The inner topography of contrition, in this evidence base, is mapped on two coordinates only: the spirit-heart complex as the location, and the affective depth as the primary inner-capacity engagement.

The inner home of contrition — crushed and broken spirit is the spirit and heart together, at the greatest depth of inner-person existence: the place where God chooses to dwell and where his reviving response begins.


**Key verses for Contrition — crushed and broken spirit:**

**Isa 57:15** (H1793A dak.ka) [M09-E-VCG-01]
> Isa 57:15 For thus says the One who is high and lifted up, who inhabits eternity , whose name is Holy : "I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit , to revive the spirit of the lowly , and to revive the heart of the contrite .
_Meaning of the term here:_ Contrition is paired with lowliness and located in the spirit; God dwells with the contrite and lowly spirit and revives both spirit and heart, showing contrition as the inner condition that draws God's reviving presence.


### 4.6 Meekness — calibrated restraint and gentleness

Meekness lives primarily in the inner capacity for dealing-with — the inner quality that governs the quality of engagement with others rather than naming a specific anatomical location in the inner person. The verse evidence for this characteristic is concentrated in a single key verse, and it is a verse of relational function: "He can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward, since he himself is beset with weakness" (Heb 5:2). The inner home of meekness, as this verse presents it, is not a named inner location — heart, spirit, or mind — but the capacity itself: the inner ability to hold one's response within bounds when engaging another in their weakness.

**The capacity as location.** Metriopatheō — the Greek term — names a quality of dealing-with, a measured and bounded feeling-response to another's condition. It is a capacity, not a fixed inner location. The most precise description of where it lives is therefore the intersection between the inner person and the relational encounter: meekness exists in the person but is visible only in the moment of engagement with another. This makes its inner topography relational by nature — it is an inner-capacity-in-use rather than an inner-state-at-rest.

**The grounding: shared weakness.** The critical inner-topographic feature of meekness is its ground. The high priest "can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward, since he himself is beset with weakness." The capacity for measured restraint is not generated independently; it is grounded in the inner person's genuine awareness of their own creaturely weakness. The inner home of meekness therefore includes this: a settled awareness of shared limitation that sits in the background of every encounter with the erring. The person who has not genuinely reckoned with their own weakness cannot exercise metriopatheō in its full form — they lack its inner ground.

**The absence of hesitation.** Acts 9:38, the second term in the evidence for this characteristic, carries the okneō-absence dimension: the prompt, unimpeded readiness that accompanies meekness. The inner capacity here is a kind of frictionlessness in response — the meek person is not only measured in their engagement but ready to engage without self-protective delay. This volitional readiness is an inner-capacity feature that the evidence names without locating it in a specific inner site.

**The limits of the evidence.** The single key verse for this characteristic — Hebrews 5:2 — does not elaborate inner location in the way the humility or submission evidence does. The inner topography is functional rather than anatomical: meekness is a capacity, grounded in the awareness of shared weakness, that operates at the interface between person and encounter. Claims about whether meekness sits specifically in heart, will, or conscience are not supported by the evidence available.

The inner home of meekness — calibrated restraint and gentleness is the inner capacity for bounded, compassionate engagement — not a fixed location but the way the whole inner person, grounded in the awareness of its own weakness, meets another in theirs.


**Key verses for Meekness — calibrated restraint and gentleness:**

**Heb 5:2** (G3356 metriopatheō) [M09-F-VCG-01]
> Heb 5:2 He can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward , since he himself is beset with weakness .
_Meaning of the term here:_ The high priest can deal gently with the erring because he himself shares human weakness; the gentleness is an inner capacity for measured, compassionate restraint grounded in shared creaturely vulnerability rather than detachment or severity.


### 4.7 Dignity — grounded moral gravity

Dignity lives across the whole inner person rather than in any single named location — it is a character quality, the settled property of the whole person rather than of one particular inner faculty. The Greek term semnotēs carries this comprehensive character: it names a quality of being, not a capacity of engagement or an act of the will. The three verses in the evidence — 1 Timothy 2:2, 1 Timothy 3:4, and Titus 2:7 — present dignity as the quality from which orderly public life, orderly household, and credible teaching all flow. As 1 Timothy 2:2 places it: "that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way." The phrase "in every way" is the inner-topographic signal: dignity is not located in one dimension of the inner person but suffuses the whole.

**The stable character quality.** Because dignity is a character quality rather than an act, disposition, or capacity, it inhabits the inner person differently from the other characteristics in this study. It is not something the inner person does (like humility's willed self-lowering); it is something the inner person is, as a settled property. The inner capacities most relevant to it are those of character formation — whatever has shaped the person into the settled, non-self-promoting gravity that semnotēs names. The character formation dimension is not directly evidenced in the three verses, but it is implied by the noun-of-quality form that the term takes: qualities are not instantaneous events but formed over time.

**The social sphere as the site of expression.** The three verses consistently show dignity expressing itself in relational and social spheres — the believer's public life, the household under governance, the teaching situation. The inner quality is expressed outward into the social world; the person's inner gravity becomes the visible ordering force in communities. First Timothy 3:4, which names the character from which orderly household governance flows, shows that the inner quality is the source of a social effect: dignity makes order without coercion.

**What is silent.** The evidence does not address which specific inner faculties — heart, will, conscience, mind — are most closely associated with dignity. The tone of semnotēs in its contexts is calm, settled, unhurried — suggesting an inner life not in conflict but ordered, which implies engagement of the will and conscience in a mode of stability rather than crisis. But these are inferences from the character of the term and its contexts, not direct inner-topographic claims the evidence makes. The three verses locate dignity in the person's overall character and in its social expressions, not in the detailed inner anatomy.

The inner home of dignity — grounded moral gravity is the whole inner person as settled character: not one faculty's engagement but the quality of the entire inner life as it has been shaped into ordered, unperforming gravity, visible in the peace it produces around itself.


**Key verses for Dignity — grounded moral gravity:**

**1Ti 2:2** (G4587 semnotēs) [M09-G-VCG-01]
> 1Ti 2:2 for kings and all who are in high positions , that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life , godly and dignified in every way.
_Meaning of the term here:_ Dignity (semnotēs) is paired with godliness as the inner quality characterizing a peaceful, quiet life under good governance; it names a sober, weighty seriousness of character that shapes how believers carry themselves in public life.


### 4.8 Willing-heartedness — the freely-moved spirit

Willing-heartedness lives in the heart and the spirit — both named explicitly in the two key verses that anchor this characteristic. Exodus 35:5 grounds it in the heart: "Whoever is of a generous heart, let him bring the Lord's contribution: gold, silver, and bronze." Psalm 51:12 grounds it in the spirit: "uphold me with a willing spirit." The two named inner locations together form the inner home of willing-heartedness — heart and spirit as the twin sites of the freely-moving will toward God.

**Heart and spirit as the double location.** The pairing of heart and spirit is not incidental. In Scripture's account of the inner person, the heart is the seat of will, orientation, and disposition; the spirit is the innermost mode of the person's life before God. That willing-heartedness is located in both is consistent with its character as the freely-moved will toward God — it engages both the volitional-dispositional centre (heart) and the deepest inner-person-before-God level (spirit). A willing heart without a willing spirit would be something less than what the evidence names; the characteristic reaches both depths.

**The volitional capacity.** The will is the primary inner capacity that willing-heartedness engages, and the evidence makes this explicit. The "generous heart" of Exodus 35:5 is the inner condition that produces the act of bringing — the willing heart generates the outward act without friction. The volitional movement is self-initiated: not commanded in its specific instance but springing from within. This is what distinguishes willing-heartedness from submission: both engage the will, but submission's will is responsive to a command from outside; willing-heartedness's will moves from an inner motivation before the command arrives.

**The affective capacity.** The pairing of willing spirit with the joy of salvation in Psalm 51:12 — "Restore to me the joy of your salvation, and uphold me with a willing spirit" — shows that the willing spirit and joy travel together. The freely-moving will toward God has an affective dimension: the willing heart is also the joyful heart. This suggests that the affective capacity is engaged alongside the volitional in willing-heartedness — the characteristic is not cold duty but gladly-moving devotion.

**The two registers and the inner-location consequence.** The two VCG-level registers within this characteristic — the positive form (Exo 35 — the freely-generated willingness of the offering) and the restored form (Psa 51:12 — the willingness sought from God after moral failure) — both locate the characteristic in the same inner sites but from different conditions. In the positive register, the heart and spirit are actively and freely generating the movement. In the restored register, the heart and spirit are seeking the capacity that moral failure has diminished. The inner location is the same; the mode of inhabitation differs. This means willing-heartedness is not only the inner experience of the spiritually healthy person but is also the prayer of the person who knows they have lost what the characteristic names.

The inner home of willing-heartedness — the freely-moved spirit is the heart-and-spirit complex: the deepest voluntary and dispositional inner capacities operating in their most self-initiated, freely-given mode, whether in the full possession of the characteristic or in the prayer for its restoration.

**Exo 35:5** (H5081G na.div) [M09-H-VCG-01]

_Meaning of the term here:_ The willing inner disposition originates in the heart; a generous, freely-moved heart is the inner condition that produces the outward act of bringing an offering.

> Psa 51:12 Restore to me the joy of your salvation , and uphold me with a willing spirit .
_Meaning of the term here:_ The psalmist asks God to uphold him with a willing spirit; willingness is located in the spirit and is presented as a divine gift needed to sustain joyful, freely-offered devotion after moral failure.
