## 5. How each characteristic works

Each characteristic in this study moves in its own way — how it is received, how it is formed, how it is expressed, and how it deepens in the inner person over time. This chapter traces the working life of each characteristic in turn.


### 5.1 Humility — willed self-lowering

The dominant movement of humility — willed self-lowering is downward, and it is a movement the person initiates. As 2 Chronicles 7:14 frames it, the call is "if my people who are called by my name humble themselves, and pray and seek my face and turn from their wicked ways, then I will hear from heaven and will forgive their sin and heal their land." Humility is the first act in a sequence of inner movements toward God; it is not the passive result of circumstances but the opening volitional act. The person who humbles themselves sets in motion a chain of engagement — prayer, seeking God's face, turning — that together constitute the inner reorientation God requires before he forgives. Humility's downward movement is the precondition for every subsequent movement toward God.

**Movement: downward before God and others.** The evidence shows humility operating in both the vertical and horizontal registers of movement. In the vertical register, the person moves inwardly downward before God — as the tax collector in Luke 18:14 who "would not even lift up his eyes to heaven" and beat his breast in an act of inner abasement. This vertical movement toward God is what enables justification — "this man went down to his house justified." In the horizontal register, Philippians 2:3 names the movement outward toward others: "in humility count others more significant than yourselves." The cognitive-volitional act of re-ordering the self's estimate is the horizontal form of the same downward movement.

**Movement: the Christological paradigm.** Philippians 2:8 is the paradigmatic statement of humility's movement from the divine side: "he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." Here the downward movement is maximal — from the form of God to the form of a servant, to death, to the cross. This establishes not only the pattern (divine-to-human direction) but also the claim that humility's downward movement is not loss but the way through which exaltation comes. The movement from humility to exaltation — established in Christ and extended to the believer in 1 Peter 5:6 ("humble yourselves under the mighty hand of God, so that at the proper time he may exalt you") — is the structural arc of humility's movement over time.

**Formation: how humility is shaped.** Humility is formed through sustained engagement with God's word as personally claiming. The Hebrew evidence shows that the humbling consistently accompanies the hearing and receiving of God's word — 2 Kings 22:19 shows a king's heart is tender because the word has struck it. Formation also occurs through the counter-pressure of circumstances: the kings who do not humble themselves accumulate guilt (2 Chronicles 33:23); the contrast shows that humility is not the default but the response to God's initiative, whether through word, circumstance, or the explicit call to bow down. The New Testament adds the dimension of the community of faith as the context of formation: Philippians 2:3 addresses the communal context — "count others more significant than yourselves" — placing the formation of humility in the texture of daily relational life.

**Opposition.** The evidence explicitly names what humility resists: pride (the spirit that self-elevates), conceit (the inflated self-estimate), and performed pseudo-humility (Colossians 2:18/23 — the appearance of humility without its inner reality). These operate against humility's formation and are themselves its structural opposite. The person who is self-enclosed is not available for humility's movement; the person who performs humility without its inner engagement has the form but not the characteristic.

**Sustained working.** Over time, voluntary acts of humility form the character quality of tapeinofrosunē — the mind of lowliness that becomes stable rather than episodic. The movement that begins as an act becomes a disposition, and the disposition becomes the settled orientation from which wisdom (Pro 11:2), contentment, corporate harmony (Eph 4:2), and divine favour follow. The characteristic works in the inner person by deepening from event into stable inner character.

Matthew 11:29 captures the telos of this working: "Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls." The rest offered is not idleness but the internal peace of the person whose inner life is aligned with the posture that Jesus himself inhabits — the lowly heart as the source of genuine rest.

### 5.2 Lowliness — experienced and imposed state

Lowliness moves differently from humility because the initiating movement is not the person's own. The characteristic's working life begins with an external force — God, circumstances, affliction — that brings the person low, rather than with the person's own volitional descent. Psalm 107:12 shows the movement plainly: "he bowed their hearts down with hard labor; they fell down, with none to help." The bowing is God's act; the falling is its consequence; the inner person is the site of the landing. The working life of lowliness begins not with an act the person takes but with a condition the person receives.

**Movement: the pattern of divine lowering and lifting.** The evidence shows a consistent arc: God lowers, and the person who receives the lowering rightly finds themselves in the position from which God lifts. Luke 1:48 shows the pattern from the receiving side — Mary's lowly estate is the condition from which "from now on all generations will call me blessed." The lowliness is real, not performed; and it is the context for divine favour. The movement is not circular but purposive: the lowering is not the end but the preparation for the lifting.

**Formation: learning contentment through imposed state.** The formative dimension of lowliness appears most clearly in Philippians 4:12: "I know how to be brought low, and I know how to abound. In any and every circumstance, I have learned the secret of facing plenty and hunger, abundance and need." The passive voice — "to be brought low" — confirms that this is an imposed state. The formation that results is contentment — a learned capacity that Paul names as a "secret": something that is not obvious, that does not arise naturally, but that is formed through actual passage through the condition. Lowliness works as a formative agent: the person who has been brought low and has endured it with an open rather than resistant inner life comes out with a capacity for contentment that the person who has only known abundance does not have.

**Opposition.** What opposes the formative working of lowliness is inner resistance to the condition — the person who experiences lowliness but refuses it, who fights against it, who interprets it as injustice to be overcome rather than as a condition to be received. The evidence does not directly name this resistance, but it is implied by the contrast between the externally-imposed humbling that leads to dependence and the externally-imposed humbling that leads to no change (the kings who are lowered but do not genuinely receive it — 2 Chronicles 28:19 and the comparative cases — find no transformation in the condition).

**What is silent.** The formation of inner capacities through lowliness beyond contentment is not directly evidenced in the three key verses for this characteristic. The inner working that the evidence supports is: imposed condition → received with open inner life → formation of contentment and capacity for both-registers equanimity. The three verses do not trace a richer inner-working map.

Lowliness works in the inner person primarily as a formative pressure from outside that, when received rather than resisted, produces the inner capacity for endurance, contentment, and the quiet waiting that positions the person for God's lifting.

### 5.3 Submission — inner disposition of will

Submission — inner disposition of will — moves in two directions simultaneously: it is received from above (from the authority whose claim the person is placed under) and expressed outward through the pattern of life that willing obedience generates. The dominant movement in the evidence is the downward-inward movement of the will placing itself under: Romans 6:17 describes those who "have become obedient from the heart to the standard of teaching to which you were committed" — the teaching comes from outside; the heart receives it and places itself under it. The movement of submission is the will's placing of itself under what comes from above.

**Movement: hearing and placing-under.** The etymology of hupakoē — "under-hearing" — encodes the movement: the person receives what is spoken by authority and places themselves under it. This is the foundational movement of submission as inner disposition. Second Corinthians 10:5 shows the same movement at the cognitive level: "we destroy arguments and every lofty opinion raised against the knowledge of God, and take every thought captive to obey Christ." The cognitive movement is active but in the direction of submission — taking thoughts captive is an act that results in placing those thoughts under. The movement of submission is always this: the person's inner life brought into alignment with what is claimed over it from a legitimate authority.

**Formation: learned through suffering.** The formative dimension of submission is one of the most striking features of its working. Hebrews 5:8 places it at the centre: "although he was a son, he learned obedience through what he suffered." The inner disposition of will-yielding is not naturally given; it is formed through the will's costly engagement with hardship under authority. For the Son, this was the formation of incarnate obedience through suffering; for the believer, the analogous formation is the sustained practice of will-yielding in the face of what costs something. Philippians 2:12 shows the sustained character of this formation: "as you have always obeyed, so now, not only as in my presence but much more in my absence, work out your own salvation with fear and trembling." The obedience that holds in the absence of external accountability is obedience that has been genuinely formed.

**Opposition.** What opposes submission is the proud self-assertion that places personal judgment over the claim of legitimate authority — the inner refusal that sets what the person thinks or wants above what the authority requires. The evidence names this in Romans 6:12–16, where the person who yields to sin is named as sin's servant. What one yields to determines whose servant one is: the inner movement of submission defines the person's deepest allegiance. The opposition to genuine submission is not difficulty but the will's preference for its own authority over the authority of another.

**Modes: fear and trembling as the inner register.** Philippians 2:12's "fear and trembling" names the inner register in which submission works: not the terror of punishment but the sober, active, engaged conscience that recognises the gravity of what it is placed under. This is the affective tone of submission at its deepest — not casual compliance but engaged, serious inner alignment.

**Production.** Over time, submission as an inner disposition forms the person who is trustworthy to God in the absence of external accountability — the person whose yielded will has become the stable ground of reliable character. First Peter 1:22 shows what sustained obedience produces: "obedience to the truth" has "purified your souls for a sincere brotherly love." The inner formation of submission produces the purity of inner life from which genuine love of others grows.

### 5.4 Submission — relational pattern of obedience

Submission as a relational pattern of obedience works outward from the inner disposition into the actual structures of human life and history. The movement here is the inner disposition's extension into the world of relationships — households, communities of faith, the gospel's reach among nations, and ultimately the eschatological submission of peoples to Christ. Romans 1:5 names the widest horizon: "to bring about the obedience of faith for the sake of his name among all the nations." The apostolic mission is oriented toward producing a relational pattern of obedience in communities and peoples as the outward expression of what the gospel works inwardly.

**Movement: from inner to relational.** The characteristic works by bringing the inner disposition of will-yielding into expression in specific relational structures. Ephesians 6:1 names the most basic of these: "Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right." The relational pattern is concrete — a child, a parent, a specific relationship — and the inner disposition finds its expression there. The "in the Lord" grounds it theologically: the relational pattern of obedience is not sociological conformity but the inner disposition's expression through a divinely-ordered relational structure.

**The gospel as the generative movement.** The most expansive instance of this characteristic's working is the gospel's movement through the nations. Romans 1:5 places the obedience of faith at the centre of the apostolic mission: the gospel proclaimed produces, in those who receive it, an inner-relational response that is named as obedience. This is not law-keeping but the whole-person response of faith that submits to the claim of the proclaimed Christ. The working of submission in this form is the movement of the gospel itself through human communities.

**Formation: the eschatological horizon.** Genesis 49:10 — "and to him shall be the obedience of the peoples" — shows that the relational pattern of obedience has an eschatological form: the full expression of this characteristic is the willing, inner yielding of allegiance by the nations to the coming ruler. This is the telos of the characteristic's working over time — not merely individual obedience in discrete relationships but the comprehensive relational submission of humanity to Christ. Hebrews 5:9 names the salvific ground of this: "he became the source of eternal salvation to all who obey him." Obedience in its relational form connects the person to salvation not as a meritorious act but as the ongoing posture of the will that receives what Christ offers.

**Opposition.** What opposes the relational pattern is the refusal to extend the inner disposition into actual relational structures — the person who has a nominal inner commitment but whose conduct in relationships does not express it. Second Thessalonians 1:8 names the most extreme form: those who "do not obey the gospel" — the relational pattern withheld from the most fundamental authority claim. The evidence also names the counter-case: Galatians 2:5, where submission is deliberately withheld from false teachers to protect the gospel. The relational pattern of obedience is not unconditional; it is directed toward legitimate authority, and the evidence shows it can and should be withheld where authority oversteps its bounds.

Over time, the relational pattern of obedience works at two scales simultaneously: in the particularity of individual relationships (household, community, teaching) and in the sweep of the gospel's movement through human history toward the eschatological submission of the peoples.

### 5.5 Contrition — crushed and broken spirit

Contrition moves differently from all other characteristics in this study because its primary movement is not one the person initiates but one they undergo. The evidence rests on a single key verse, and it is addressed from the outside — from the God who declares where he dwells: "I dwell in the high and holy place, and also with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit, to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite" (Isa 57:15). The movement in this verse is from God toward the contrite person: it is God who draws near, who revives, who initiates the encounter with the crushed spirit.

**Movement: God toward the broken.** The characteristic's most fundamental movement is divine-toward-creaturely. Contrition does not move toward God in the same initiating way that humility does — it is the state that draws God to the person. Psalm 34:18 confirms the pattern: "the Lord is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit." The movement is consistently in this direction — the broken condition is the attractor; God's nearness and saving response follow. Contrition works in the inner person by making it the place where God comes.

**Formation: genuine confrontation as the origin.** What produces contrition — what causes the spirit to be crushed — is genuine confrontation with moral failure or with the holiness of God. The evidence does not address the mechanics of this confrontation in detail, but the nature of the terms is clear: dak.ka (crushed, ground fine) names the result of a force applied to the inner person. The crushing comes from the encounter with what is real — God's holiness, the gravity of moral failure — breaking through the inner person's self-sufficiency. Contrition cannot be manufactured; it is the result of genuine reckoning.

**The reviving response.** What contrition produces, via God's response, is reviving: "to revive the spirit of the lowly, and to revive the heart of the contrite." The characteristic works in the inner person not as an end state but as the condition that opens the person to God's restoring work. The crushed spirit is not the telos of contrition; the revived spirit-and-heart is. Contrition is the opening, not the arrival.

**What is silent.** The single verse base for this characteristic means that a fuller account of how contrition is formed, how long it lasts, how it deepens, or how it relates to other inner states over time is not available from the direct evidence. The inner working that the evidence supports is: genuine moral confrontation → crushed spirit and heart → draws God's nearness → reviving. Beyond this arc, the evidence does not go.

Contrition works in the inner person by receiving a force from outside that breaks through self-sufficiency, and by drawing to itself the reviving presence of the God who chooses the broken spirit as his dwelling place.

### 5.6 Meekness — calibrated restraint and gentleness

Meekness works at the interface between person and encounter — it is the inner quality that governs the quality of engagement rather than a state that operates apart from relational contact. Its working life is therefore visible primarily in the moment of encounter with another person in their weakness or failure. The evidence concentrates this in a single key verse: "He can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward, since he himself is beset with weakness" (Heb 5:2). The working of meekness begins in that "can" — it is a capacity that the inner person possesses, drawn upon in the moment of engagement.

**Movement: outward into encounter.** The movement of meekness is outward — from the inner capacity into the encounter with another. The metriopatheō terminology names a quality of dealing-with, not a state-at-rest. Meekness works by shaping the quality of outward engagement: measured rather than excessive, compassionate rather than severe, appropriately responsive rather than self-protective. The working is relational in its expression even though the capacity is inner in its seat.

**The grounding that makes the movement possible.** The inner working of meekness depends on its ground: the awareness of shared creaturely weakness. The high priest "can deal gently" because "he himself is beset with weakness." The meekness works through this awareness — the person who has genuinely reckoned with their own limitation finds, in that reckoning, the inner resource for compassionate engagement with another's limitation. The inner movement that produces outward gentleness is the inward acknowledgment of shared weakness.

**Volitional readiness.** The okneō-absence dimension — the absence of self-protective hesitation — shows that meekness works not only in the quality of engagement but in its promptness. The meek person does not delay when engagement is called for. The inner working here is a readiness that does not wait for assurance that the encounter is safe before moving toward the other. This promptness is itself evidence of an inner freedom from self-protection: the person who is genuinely meek is not calculating risk before responding.

**Formation and opposition.** How meekness is formed is not directly addressed in the evidence. The ground it rests on — awareness of shared weakness — is itself a formed inner awareness, suggesting that the formation of meekness is bound up with the formation of genuine self-knowledge. What opposes meekness is the two opposite distortions named in the evidence: disproportionate severity (engaging the erring with excessive force) and self-protective hesitation (delaying or withdrawing when engagement is called for). Both are failures of the inner capacity that meekness names.

Meekness works in the inner person by drawing on a grounded awareness of shared weakness and translating that awareness into measured, compassionate, prompt engagement with those who err or are weak.

### 5.7 Dignity — grounded moral gravity

Dignity works differently from the other characteristics in this study because it does not have an obvious initiating movement or a named formation process in the evidence. It is the settled inner quality that the three verses present as already operative in the person — the quality from which peaceful public life, orderly household, and credible teaching flow. The working of dignity is therefore the working of a character quality: it does not so much move as it is — and in being what it is, it produces the order around itself that the evidence names.

**Movement: from inner quality to social order.** The working of dignity is from inside outward — not as an act but as a steady expression of character into the social sphere. First Timothy 2:2 shows the outcome: "a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way." The dignity that characterises the inner person produces a particular quality of public life — peaceful, ordered, non-agitated. First Timothy 3:4 shows the same outward expression in the household: the person of dignity has "children who are submissive and respectful in every way." The inner quality creates a social environment that reflects it. Titus 2:7 shows it in the teaching context: "show integrity, dignity" — the teaching is authenticated by the character of the teacher.

**No direct formation account.** The evidence for dignity does not address how it is formed — what produces semnotēs in a person, through what disciplines or experiences it develops. The three verses present it as a present quality to be exhibited, prayed for, and taught in, not as something traced through its formation. This is a genuine limit of the evidence base and should be acknowledged as such rather than filled with inference.

**Opposition.** What opposes dignity is not directly named in the three-verse corpus, but the evidence places it in contrast with proud self-display (its structural opposite in the wider cluster) and with moral frivolousness — the absence of the seriousness and gravity that semnotēs names. The working of dignity excludes the self-promoting, the lightweight, and the unstable self-presentation that would undermine the ordered social sphere its presence creates.

**Modes.** Dignity works in three modes according to the evidence: as the quality of the whole life (1Ti 2:2 — "in every way"), as the source of household order (1Ti 3:4), and as the authenticating character of teaching (Tit 2:7). These are not sequential but simultaneous: the person of dignity inhabits all three spheres at once.

Dignity works in the inner person as a stable character quality that expresses itself consistently into the social world — creating peace, order, and credibility not through effort but through the settled gravity of what the inner person is.

### 5.8 Willing-heartedness — the freely-moved spirit

The most distinctive feature of willing-heartedness's working is that it moves first — before the command, before the calculation, before the requirement. Exodus 35:5 establishes the pattern: "Whoever is of a generous heart, let him bring the Lord's contribution: gold, silver, and bronze." The call is an invitation, not a compulsion; what determines the response is the condition of the heart. The willing heart brings because it is willing — the inner movement generates the outward act without being dragged into it.

**Movement: self-initiated outward.** The characteristic works by a movement from within outward that is self-initiated. The willing-hearted person does not wait to be compelled; their inner disposition moves them first. The evidence in Exodus 35 — "every willing-hearted person came" (35:22); the willing-hearted brought skilled labour and offerings (35:21–22) — shows a community-wide pattern where the inner orientation of multiple people generates a shared contribution without coercion. The working of willing-heartedness at the community level is the aggregated movement of many freely-moved spirits toward a shared goal.

**Formation: the two-register dynamic.** Willing-heartedness works in two modes that the evidence distinguishes. In the first mode, the willing spirit is simply operative: the person whose heart is freely oriented toward God brings, comes, and serves without friction. In the second mode, the willing spirit has been diminished by moral failure and must be sought from God as a restorative gift: "Restore to me the joy of your salvation, and uphold me with a willing spirit" (Psa 51:12). The formation of willing-heartedness is therefore not only a matter of its original possession but of its restoration — the person who has lost the freely-moving spirit through moral failure can seek its return from God. This makes willing-heartedness one of the few characteristics in this study where the evidence directly names both the experience of having it and the prayer for its restoration.

**The joy connection.** Psalm 51:12 pairs the willing spirit with the joy of salvation: "Restore to me the joy of your salvation, and uphold me with a willing spirit." The two are companion gifts sought together, suggesting that willing-heartedness and joy are functionally linked in their working — the freely-moving spirit is also the joyful spirit. Where joy has been lost, the willing spirit has also been diminished; where both are restored, the person is again capable of freely-given devotion.

**Opposition.** What opposes willing-heartedness is compulsion and moral failure. Compulsion produces the appearance of giving without its inner reality; the person who brings because they must is not in the mode that Exodus 35 describes. Moral failure diminishes the willing capacity — Psalm 51:12 implies that David's sin has cost him something of the freely-moving spirit. The inner working of willing-heartedness is therefore fragile in one register: it can be diminished by what the person does with their moral freedom.

**Production.** Over time, the willing spirit generates the pattern of habitual generous offering, wholehearted skilled service, and sustained joyful devotion. The tabernacle-building context of Exodus 35 shows the productive output: a community whose collective willing-heartedness generates a dwelling for God. This is the most expansive sustained production of the characteristic — not merely individual acts of generosity but the construction of the place where God dwells among his people.

Willing-heartedness works in the inner person as the most self-initiated of the cluster's characteristics — the movement from within that does not wait to be pushed but springs from the heart's own freely-moved orientation toward God, and which must be restored when moral failure has diminished its spring.
