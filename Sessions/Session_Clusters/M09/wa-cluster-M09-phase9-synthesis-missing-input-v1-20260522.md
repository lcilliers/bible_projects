#### T3.2.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?

_Tier: T3_  _Component: T3.2 Cognition_

**[CHAR-1] Humility** (finding):
**[CHAR-1]** E — Humility *enables and deepens* genuine cognition. Pro11:2 establishes the enabling connexion: the humble possess wisdom, the proud gain disgrace — the cognitive capacity for genuine understanding is linked to the posture of humility. This is consistent with the perceptive pattern (T3.1.2): the self-enclosure of pride distorts cognition, while the openness of humility enables accurate perception and therefore accurate understanding. The Colossian critique of false humility (Col2:18) shows that distorted self-assessment (cognitive pride operating within an outwardly humble practice) impairs rather than enables genuine understanding. Genuine humility enables cognition by removing the self-protective filter that keeps the person from accurately knowing themselves, God, and others.

**[CHAR-2] Submission** (finding):
**[CHAR-2]** E — Submission *rightly orders and deepens* genuine cognition. Taking every thought captive to Christ (2Cor10:5) is not the suppression of thinking but its reorientation under a legitimate authority — it enables more accurate cognition by placing it under a reliable epistemic authority (Christ) rather than leaving it to self-generated "lofty opinions." Rom16:19 — Paul redirects the Romans' obedient disposition toward "wisdom as to what is good" — submission enables the discernment that pure self-generated reasoning cannot produce. The absence of genuine submission is linked to cognitive self-deception: the "lofty opinions" of 2Cor10:5 are the cognitive dysfunction that resists the knowledge of God.

**[CHAR-3] Contrition** (finding):
**[CHAR-3]** E — Contrition *enables accurate cognition* of one's moral condition. The person in genuine contrition is one who has arrived at a truthful cognitive assessment of their own failure — the breaking is, in part, the cognitive-experiential event of genuine self-knowledge before God. The characteristic deepens cognitive honesty by validating it: the person who genuinely confronts their failure rather than defending against it is engaging the cognitive faculty most accurately. The structural opposite (self-justification, pride, hardening) impairs accurate moral cognition by distorting the person's self-assessment.

**[CHAR-4] Meekness and gentleness** (finding):
**[CHAR-4]** E — Meekness/gentleness *enables more accurate cognition* of others' conditions by removing the distorting lens of assumed superiority. The high priest who genuinely knows his own weakness (Heb5:2) cognises the erring other's condition more accurately — without the distortions that self-protective pride introduces. The characteristic thus deepens the cognitive capacity for accurate social and moral perception by grounding it in genuine self-knowledge.

**[CHAR-5] Dignity** (finding):
**[CHAR-5]** E — Semnotēs *deepens credible cognition* in the teaching and leadership registers. Tit2:7 — teaching authenticated by dignity — enables hearers to receive the instruction as genuinely credible, enhancing their cognitive reception of the content. The characteristic enables the cognitive function of teaching to be received as weighty rather than dismissed as ungrounded performance.

**[CHAR-6] Willing-heartedness** (finding):
**[CHAR-6]** E — Willing-heartedness *enables wholehearted cognitive engagement*. 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man who has skill" — willingness enables the full deployment of skill; the unwilling would bring their skill under compulsion without genuine engagement. The characteristic enables the cognitive faculty to be applied with genuine investment rather than with the distracted half-engagement of reluctant compliance.

#### T6.5.2 — Where the evidence shows apparent overlap, what is the precise boundary?

_Tier: T6_  _Component: T6.5 Distinctions_

**[CHAR-1] Humility** (finding):
**[CHAR-1]** E — The overlap is clearest in Isa57:15 (CHAR-3 evidence) which pairs contrition with lowliness: "with him who is contrite and lowly in spirit." This verse sits at the boundary — the contrite person is also lowly, and the humble person who has genuinely failed will also experience contrition. The boundary is: humility is the *stable posture* that does not require the acute triggering of moral failure; contrition is the *acute response* to the specific recognition of failure. A person may be genuinely humble without being in a state of acute contrition; a person in contrition is in the most acute and specific form of the humility register. Contrition implies humility but humility does not always imply contrition.

**[CHAR-2] Submission** (finding):
**[CHAR-2]** E — The most significant overlap is at Phili2:8 (Christ "humbled himself by becoming obedient") and Luk17:10 (the servant's self-assessment of unworthiness as the inner fruit of having submitted). The boundary: humility at Phili2:8 is the inner disposition (self-emptying, downward movement of the self); the obedience at Phili2:8 is the specific form that humbling takes in relation to the Father's authority. Luk17:10 shows submission producing humility (the servant's inner non-merit assessment flows from having done all that was commanded) — submission occasions the humble self-assessment rather than being identical to it. The boundary is: submission is the directional yielding to authority; humility is the general self-regarding posture that the submission both expresses and reinforces.

**[CHAR-3] Contrition** (finding):
**[CHAR-3]** E — The precise boundary is: the lowly spirit of Isa57:15 (CHAR-1 register) can be present without the contrite spirit (CHAR-3 register) — a person can be genuinely humble without currently being in the acute state of contrition. But the contrite spirit (CHAR-3) is always also lowly (CHAR-1) — contrition implies and includes lowliness. Contrition is thus a subset of the humility register: specifically the acute, grief-laden, crisis-precipitated form that humility takes at the moment of genuine moral confrontation. The boundary is: not all humility is contrition, but genuine contrition always includes lowliness.

**[CHAR-4] Meekness and gentleness** (finding):
**[CHAR-4]** E — The precise boundary is evidenced at Heb5:2: the shared-weakness self-awareness that grounds metriopatheō is also the self-knowledge that characterises genuine humility (CHAR-1). The overlap is at this enabling self-awareness — both characteristics require genuine self-knowledge of one's creaturely limitation. The boundary: humility is what that self-knowledge produces as an inner posture (self-lowering before God and others); meekness/gentleness is what that self-knowledge produces as a relational quality in engagement with the erring and needy. One can have the inner posture (humility) without currently being in the act of engagement with the erring; one exercises meekness/gentleness specifically in the act of engagement.

**[CHAR-5] Dignity** (finding):
**[CHAR-5]** E — The overlap between CHAR-1 and CHAR-5 is in the shared non-self-assertive register. The boundary: humility involves the person actively choosing to take the lower place (Phili2:3 — count others more significant; Jam4:10 — humble yourselves); dignity involves the person inhabiting their place and roles with settled gravity without needing to promote or defend their standing (1Ti2:2; 1Ti3:4; Tit2:7 — no active lowering but settled non-promotional inhabiting). The boundary is: humility is the active downward movement; dignity is the stable settled position that does not need to assert upward.

**[CHAR-6] Willing-heartedness** (finding):
**[CHAR-6]** E — The overlap is at 1Ch28:21 — "every willing man… wholly at your command" — willing-heartedness and submission to command coexist in the same verse: the willing craftsmen are also submissively available ("at your command"). The boundary: the willing-heartedness is what motivates the prior to any command (the inner movement toward service); the submission is the responsive availability when direction is given. Willing-heartedness provides the motivational ground; submission provides the responsive structure. The two are complementary in the service context without being identical.

#### T6.5.3 — Is the distinction between this characteristic and its nearest neighbour one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?

_Tier: T6_  _Component: T6.5 Distinctions_

**[CHAR-1] Humility** (finding):
**[CHAR-1]** E — The distinction between Humility and Contrition is primarily one of *temporal form and affective intensity* — which is close to *degree* combined with *constitutional level*. Both operate at the heart and spirit level; both are relational toward God; both involve the person's posture before God's authority and rightness. The distinction is in the *intensity and occasion* (acute crisis-response versus stable disposition) and in the *affective loading* (contrition carries grief, crushed-spirit affect as primary; humility may or may not be accompanied by weeping at any given moment). It is not a distinction of *kind* (both are inner postures before God) or primarily of *direction* (both are Godward in their primary orientation).

**[CHAR-2] Submission** (finding):
**[CHAR-2]** E — The distinction is primarily one of *direction and relational structure*. Humility has no necessary specific authority-target (one can be humble in one's own inner posture without being in an active submission-relationship); submission is constitutively directed toward a specific authority. The distinction is not primarily of constitutional level (both operate at heart/will level) nor of degree (both can be deep or shallow). It is structural: humility is the inner posture relative to self; submission is the inner posture relative to a specific other-in-authority.

**[CHAR-3] Contrition** (finding):
**[CHAR-3]** E — The distinction is primarily one of *intensity and temporal form* — which is close to *degree* combined with *occasion*. Both characteristics operate at the same constitutional levels (spirit and heart); both are Godward in direction; both are in the same semantic domain of inner lowering before God. What differs is the intensity (contrition is acute; humility is stable) and the specific occasion (contrition requires a moral-failure trigger or holiness-encounter; humility is the stable ongoing posture). The distinction is not of kind (both are inner postures before God) or of constitutional level, but of intensity-form and occasion.

**[CHAR-4] Meekness and gentleness** (finding):
**[CHAR-4]** E — The distinction is primarily one of *register and occasion*. Both operate at the same constitutional level (will, self-knowledge, heart), both are enabled by the same inner self-awareness, and both are Godward and other-directed. The difference is: humility is the inner posture that the self-awareness produces as a stable orientation; meekness/gentleness is the specific quality of outward relational engagement that the same self-awareness enables. The distinction is contextual-relational rather than of constitutional level or kind.

**[CHAR-5] Dignity** (finding):
**[CHAR-5]** E — The distinction is primarily one of *kind and stance*. Both operate at the character-level and both are non-self-assertive. But humility is dynamic (the act of self-lowering) while dignity is settled (the stable character quality of grounded non-assertion). The distinction in kind: humility is a movement; dignity is a resting-place. The distinction in direction: humility points downward (active self-lowering); dignity does not primarily move in any direction but rests in settled gravity.

**[CHAR-6] Willing-heartedness** (finding):
**[CHAR-6]** E — The distinction is primarily one of *source and occasion*. Both characteristics operate at the same constitutional level (will/heart) and both are Godward in their ultimate direction. The distinction: submission is occasioned by a command from a specific authority (responsive); willing-heartedness is generated from within the person's own freely-moving heart (spontaneous). The distinction in source (external occasion vs internal generation) is the primary characterising difference — not degree, kind, or constitutional level.

#### T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?

#### T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?

_Tier: T7_  _Component: T7.2 Verse and Literary Interpretation_

**[CHAR-1] Humility** (finding):
**[CHAR-1]** E — Key logical structures: *2Ch7:14:* Premise 1 — God's people are called by his name (covenantal standing). Premise 2 — They are to humble themselves, pray, seek, and turn. Conclusion — God will hear, forgive, and heal. The argument is conditional-covenantal. *Phili2:3–8:* Premise 1 — The community is to do nothing from selfish ambition. Premise 2 — In humility each is to count others more significant. Premise 3 — Christ humbled himself to the point of death in obedience to the Father. Conclusion (implicit) — the community's humility participates in the pattern of the Son. *Jam4:6–10:* Premise 1 — God gives more grace. Premise 2 — God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble. Conclusion — Submit to God; humble yourselves before the Lord; and he will exalt you. The argument is wisdom-principle → covenantal application → eschatological promise.

**[CHAR-2] Submission** (finding):
**[CHAR-2]** E — Key logical structures: *Rom6:16–17:* Premise 1 — you present yourselves to whoever you obey as slaves. Premise 2 — you were slaves to sin; you have become obedient from the heart to the teaching. Conclusion: your master has changed; you are now slaves of righteousness (transitional premise → conclusion). *1Pe1:22:* Premise — you have purified your souls by obedience to the truth. Conclusion — love one another earnestly from a pure heart (purification → affective-fruit). *Heb5:8–9:* Premise — the Son learned obedience through suffering. Conclusion — being made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation to all who obey him (formation → salvific enablement).

**[CHAR-3] Contrition** (finding):
**[CHAR-3]** E — *Psa34:18:* Premise — the Lord is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit. This is a generalising wisdom-principle: the Lord's characterological disposition is nearness to the broken. The logical structure is a simple divine-character statement with an implied promise-application. *Isa57:15:* Premise 1 — I (God) dwell in the high and holy place. Premise 2 — I also dwell with him who is of a contrite and lowly spirit. Premise 3 — in order to revive the spirit of the lowly and the heart of the contrite. Conclusion (implied) — therefore the contrite person is where God's reviving is found. The logical structure is a divine self-description of double dwelling (transcendent and immanent) with the purpose of the immanent dwelling explicitly named.

**[CHAR-4] Meekness and gentleness** (finding):
**[CHAR-4]** E — *Heb5:2:* Premise 1 — the high priest is appointed to act on behalf of humans before God (v.1 — implied). Premise 2 — he can deal gently with the ignorant and wayward. Premise 3 — he himself is beset with weakness. Conclusion: his shared weakness is the ground that enables his measured-compassionate function. The argument structure is: shared-weakness-as-enabling-ground → calibrated-priestly-capacity. This is a surprising inversion of the expected logic (weakness → capacity rather than strength → capacity), which is the key rhetorical move. *Act9:38:* implicit argument: urgent genuine need → expected response is immediate presence without hesitation.

**[CHAR-5] Dignity** (finding):
**[CHAR-5]** E — *1Ti2:2:* Premise — prayer for rulers enables peaceful public existence. Premise — peaceful existence enables godly, dignified life. Premise — managing well includes maintaining dignity and producing submissive children. Conclusion — dignity and produced-submission are the tests of effective household leadership. *Tit2:7:* Premise — the teacher must be a model of good works. Premise — in teaching, integrity and dignity are required qualities. Conclusion — these qualities are the character-authentication of genuine teaching.

**[CHAR-6] Willing-heartedness** (finding):
**[CHAR-6]** E — *Exo35:5:* Premise — the Lord's contribution is needed for the tabernacle. Premise — God calls for it from those of willing heart. Conclusion — the appropriate giver is the one whose inner condition is genuinely free. The argument identifies inner condition as the discriminating criterion for genuine worship-contribution. *Psa51:12:* Premise — God restores the joy of salvation (the preceding plea). Premise — the psalmist needs a willing spirit to sustain freely-offered devotion. Conclusion — seek God to uphold the willing spirit. The argument positions the willing spirit as both the necessary condition for ongoing devotion and a gift requiring divine sustaining.
