## Cluster overview

M26 spans a dense semantic field of righteousness, justice, and their legal-judicial counterparts across both Testaments. The Hebrew tsedaqah/tsedeq family and the Greek dik- word family form two interlocking centres: the first emphasising covenant-relational moral standing, the second emphasising legally grounded rightness and its judicial execution. The cluster divides into at least six distinguishable inner-being phenomena — a state of righteous character, a disposition of moral uprightness, an act of justification/vindication, retributive avenging justice, condemnatory judicial sentence, and an evaluative reckoning faculty — plus two structural opposite terms that define the cluster's boundary from within.

**Onion observation:** The border between M26-A (core righteousness as state) and M26-B (uprightness as straightness) is the cluster's most porous seam, since tsedaqah and yosher can both describe the same person's moral character; similarly, M26-C (justification) and M26-E (condemnation) morph into one another because both are verdicts issuing from the same judicial faculty, differing only in direction.

## M26-A — Core Righteousness: State of Being Right

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the character and covenantal standing of the person (or in God's own nature); engages the moral-judicial faculty by which conformity to a standard is recognised and expressed; produces a recognised status before God and in community conduct; structurally opposed by wickedness (rasha) and injustice (aval, M26-G); directed both from God toward the person (imputed/bestowed righteousness) and from the person outward toward God and neighbour (lived righteousness).

**Rationale for grouping:**

These terms form the definitional core of the cluster, each naming the state or quality of 'being right' in a covenantal-moral sense rather than a procedure or act. The Hebrew tsedaqah/tsedeq/tsaddiq group and the Greek dikaios/dikaiosunē group converge on the same concept across testaments; endikos is placed here because its LSJ meaning 'according to right, just, legitimate' and Mounce's 'just, deserved' function as qualifying descriptors of the righteous standard rather than an independent action.

## M26-B — Moral Uprightness: Inner Disposition of Straightness

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the will and conduct of the person; engages the moral-volitional faculty through the governing metaphor of straightness versus crookedness; produces directness, equity, and reliability in action and relationship; structurally opposed by perverseness and crooked dealing; directed from the person outward in social and relational conduct rather than primarily from God, distinguishing it from the covenantal emphasis of M26-A.

**Rationale for grouping:**

The Hebrew yosher/meshar/nekhokhah group all share the root idea of being straight or level — moral rectitude conceived geometrically — and Greek euthutēs (Mounce: 'a figurative extension of a straight object') is its explicit NT equivalent. This sub-group is distinguishable from M26-A because the emphasis falls on directness and reliability of inner disposition rather than on covenant status or forensic standing.

## M26-C — Justification: Declaring or Making Righteous

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the judicial act directed at a person's standing before God or a human court; engages the declarative-judicial faculty; produces a change in moral-legal status from condemned to vindicated or righteous; structurally opposed by condemnation (M26-E); directed from the judging party (usually God) toward the judged person, making this distinct from M26-A which names the state rather than the act of conferring it.

**Rationale for grouping:**

These four terms all name the judicial act of declaring or effecting righteous standing: the verbal root (dikaioō, tsadeq), the resultant process (dikaiōsis), and the concrete requirement or righteous act that grounds the verdict (dikaiōma). Together they form the procedural dimension of righteousness — what happens when a verdict of 'righteous' is pronounced.

| H6663 | *tsa.deq* | to justify | Gloss 'to justify' — the Hebrew verbal root (Qal/Hiphil) for declaring or being righteous, the OT semantic anchor of NT dikaioō. |

## M26-D — Avenging Justice: Retribution for Wrongdoing

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the will and zeal of the avenger (divine or commissioned human agent); engages the retributive-judicial faculty in response to perceived injustice or wrong done; produces punishment, requital, or restoration of the violated right; structurally opposed by impunity and the toleration of wrong; directed from the avenger toward the wrongdoer, distinguishing it from M26-C (directed toward the innocent/accused) and M26-E (a verdict rather than an enacted response).

**Rationale for grouping:**

The ekdik- word group (ekdikeō, ekdikēsis, ekdikos) and antapodidōmi share the semantic core of paying back and taking up a cause against wrongdoing; dikaiokrisia (Mounce: 'righteous judgment justice') names the quality of righteous judgment as it is enacted retributively, fitting the sub-group's emphasis on justice carried out rather than status declared.

## M26-E — Condemnation: Judicial Sentence Against

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the judicial pronouncement (divine or human) directed against a person found guilty; engages the judicial-evaluative faculty in its negative verdict; produces a declared state of guilt, penalty, or sentence; structurally opposed by justification (M26-C); directed from the judge toward the defendant — and reflexively inward in the case of self-condemnation (autokatakritos), which reveals the inner-being dimension of the group.

**Rationale for grouping:**

The kata-krin- group (katakrinō, katadikazō, katakrisis) plus dikē ('condemnation' in NT usage) all name the act or state of a negative judicial verdict; autokatakritos is particularly significant for inner-being analysis because it shows the condemnatory faculty turned reflexively on the self.

## M26-F — Moral Reckoning: Evaluative Faculty of Worth

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the cognitive-judicial faculty of the mind; engages the evaluative-reckoning capacity that assigns worth, credits righteousness, or determines accountability; produces a credited status or recognised liability; structurally opposed by partial, arbitrary, or prejudiced assessment (adiakritos names the absence of that opposite); directed from the evaluating subject (God, self, or judge) toward the person or act being assessed.

**Rationale for grouping:**

These terms share an evaluative or accounting logic: logizomai (credit/count), axioō (deem worthy), arakh (to value/assess), hupodikos (standing under accountability), and adiakritos (free from the prejudice that distorts such assessment). Together they describe the inner faculty of moral accounting that underlies both justification and condemnation.

## M26-G — Structural Opposites: Wickedness and Injustice

**Characteristic definition (T1-shaped):**

Located in the character and will of the person; engages the moral-volitional faculty in the direction of perversity and covenant violation; produces conduct and inner status directly opposed to tsedaqah and dikaiosunē; these terms define the cluster's inner boundary by naming what righteousness is not; directed from the person away from the divine standard, making them the polar opposite of M26-A in causal direction.

**Rationale for grouping:**

Both terms are the acknowledged structural opposites of core righteousness vocabulary in the Hebrew OT: rasha is the opposite of tsaddiq (righteous person), and aval names the act of perverting justice — the opposite of tsedaqah-based conduct. Their presence in M26 anchors the T1 requirement that the structural opposite appear in the cluster.
