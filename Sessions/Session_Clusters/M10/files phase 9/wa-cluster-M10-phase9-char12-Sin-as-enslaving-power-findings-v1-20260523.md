## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Sin as enslaving power reveals God's character primarily through the liberation it requires. (1) *God as the liberator from sin's mastery*: Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — Jesus names the slavery as the condition requiring the freedom that the Son can give (the broader Joh 8:36 context: "if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed" — implied by the slavery statement). (2) *God as the source of the indwelling seed that prevents sin's dominion*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him" — the divine birth and the indwelling seed are the counter-powers to sin's enslaving function; these are God-given. (3) *God as protector against sin's dominion*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him" — God actively protects the born-of-God person from sin's re-enslavement; the protecting agent (born of God — Christ) is the divine counter to the enslaving power.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute sin as enslaving power to God?**

**[CHAR-12]** S — No verse in the 9-verse corpus attributes sin's enslaving power to God. God appears as the liberator (1Jo 3:9 — God's seed; 1Jo 5:18 — the born-of-God protects) and as the one against whom the enslaved person sins (the broader biblical framework), but not as the enslaving power itself. The characteristic is exclusively a fallen-condition creaturely reality.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The silence on God possessing sin's enslaving power is absolute and structurally significant: the characteristic presupposes a *master-slave relationship* in which sin is the master and the creature is the slave (Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin"). God cannot be enslaved — God is sovereign; and God does not enslave through sin — his purpose is liberation (1Jo 3:9; 1Jo 5:18). The silence reveals that the enslaving-power characteristic is constitutively the anti-God power structure: sin enslaves by claiming the governing role that belongs to God. 1Jo 3:8 — "the devil has been sinning from the beginning" — names the adversarial spiritual ground of the enslaving power; it is not God's power but the devil's originary sinning that grounds the enslaving structure.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose sin as enslaving power serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-12]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for sin's enslaving power. The 9 verses present it as: enslavement requiring freedom (Joh 8:34), universal imprisonment requiring faith-promise (Gal 3:22), a condition that born-of-God persons are liberated from (1Jo 3:9; 5:18), and a danger that anger can lead to if unchecked (Eph 4:26). No verse presents the enslaving power as serving any designed function in the created person.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether sin as enslaving power is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The evidence points entirely to the fallen condition. 1Jo 3:8 — "the devil has been sinning from the beginning" — the origin of the enslaving power is in the devil's originary sinning, not in the creation's design. Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin" — the universal imprisonment is something that Scripture (God's word) has disclosed; it is the fallen condition declared and enclosed. 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning" — the new birth as the counter to the enslaving power implies that without the new birth, the power holds; the new birth addresses a fallen-condition reality, not a design feature.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that sin as enslaving power is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes — in two opposed eschatological directions. (1) *Negative trajectory — sin unto death*: 1Jo 5:16 — "there is sin that leads to death" — the enslaving power, when unchecked, moves toward an eschatological terminus of death; the trajectory is toward total and final severance of life. (2) *Positive trajectory — liberation through birth and protection*: 1Jo 5:18 — "everyone who has been born of God does not keep on sinning, but he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him" — the born-of-God trajectory is toward a condition where sin's mastery is permanently broken; the eschatological direction for the liberated person is toward freedom from the enslaving power's reach.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does sin as enslaving power express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Sin as enslaving power does not express the divine image positively but perverts the image's designed governance capacity. The image-bearer is created to govern (the broader Genesis mandate), but under sin's enslaving power, what should be the image's governing self becomes a slave to what it should govern. Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — the practice-to-slavery mechanism inverts the image's design: the image-bearer was designed to exercise governing authority (ruling the creation), but habitual sinning reverses this — the image-bearer is ruled by sin rather than exercising rule over it (cf. Gen 4:7 — "you must rule over it" — the contrast to the enslavement state).

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest sin as enslaving power is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-12]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God cannot be enslaved by sin; his nature is sovereign and holy. The enslaving power operates on created persons (Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin"; Gal 3:22 — "everything" imprisoned under sin). The characteristic is inapplicable to the divine nature.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of sin as enslaving power reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The presence of sin's enslaving power reveals the divine image in a condition of *reversed governance*: instead of the image-bearer governing rightly (the created design), the image-bearer is governed by sin as its master. Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin" — the slave-to-sin condition names the image in its most degraded constitutional form: the governance capacity has been captured. 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil" — the deepest revealing consequence: the image-bearer under sin's enslaving power has an allegiance-marker that declares its belonging not to God but to the devil. The image is present (the person still exists and wills) but its allegiance-orientation and governing-structure have been wholly captured.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use sin as enslaving power typologically?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin, so that the promise by faith in Jesus Christ might be given to those who believe" — the universal imprisonment under sin functions typologically: the condition of universal imprisonment is the necessary precondition and backdrop against which the promise by faith operates. The imprisonment-type is not an end in itself but points toward the faith-promise as its resolution. The negative type (imprisonment under sin) points toward the positive antitype (liberation through faith in Jesus Christ). The typological use is explicitly purposive: "so that the promise... might be given."

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The direction is: universal imprisonment under sin (negative type) → faith-promise in Jesus Christ (positive antitype fulfillment). Gal 3:22 makes the purposive structure explicit: the imprisonment is not accidental but was *so that* the promise could be given to those who believe. The human condition of being enslaved under sin is the necessary historical-covenantal context against which the gospel's promise-fulfillment becomes intelligible. The direction is from the human enslaved condition (type) toward the divine liberating provision (antitype).

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Typological use is evidenced in Gal 3:22 (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Sin as enslaving power." Three signals. (1) *Sin*: the moral-spiritual reality at stake is sin in its power-register — not a specific act but sin operating as a force. (2) *Enslaving*: the characteristic's defining mark is that sin is not merely present but actively captures and holds the will; Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — slavery names the constitutive inner relationship. The enslaving signal distinguishes CHAR-12 from CHAR-11 (universal condition — under sin) by naming sin's *active governing function over the will* specifically. (3) *Power*: the term signals that sin operates as an active agent, not merely a state; Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin" — sin is the imprisoning force; it has the power to confine. The name thus encodes: sin operating as an active governing master that holds the will captive.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The primary NT Greek terms are: (1) **hamartia (G0266)** — noun — deployed in the enslaving-power register: Joh 8:34 — "slave to sin" (hamartias — genitive, naming sin as the master); Rom 6:1/2 — "continue in sin... live in it" (singular noun — sin as domain); Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin" (hamartian — accusative, sin as the imprisoning power). The noun form names sin as an entity with governing authority. (2) **hamartanō (G0264)** — verb — in the practice/habit register: Eph 4:26 — "do not sin"; 1Jo 3:8/9 — "makes a practice of sinning"; 1Jo 5:16/18 — "sinning... does not keep on sinning." The verb's present continuous form in 1Jo 3:8/9/5:18 — the present participle *hamartanōn* — names habitual ongoing sinning as the condition of enslavement. No OT Hebrew vocabulary appears; the characteristic is exclusively NT Greek.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: the enslaving power moves inward — from sin's external pull to the internal capture of the will; Joh 8:34's practice (outward acts) → slavery (inner constitutional captivity) reveals the direction. *Relational*: the characteristic is constitutively relational — a master-slave relationship between sin (master) and the person (slave — Joh 8:34). *Constitutional*: 1Jo 3:9 — "God's seed abides in him... he cannot keep on sinning" — the cannot-keep-sinning is a constitutional impossibility grounded in the new birth; the liberation from the enslaving power is equally constitutional. The name thus implies: the will's constitutional direction, the relational master-slave structure, and the inner-directional movement of capture.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is sin as enslaving power?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The corpus reveals a *power-dynamic condition* — sin operating as an active governing force over the inner person. Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin" — the slave is in a condition, not performing an act; the slavery is a constitutional state produced by practice. Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin" — the imprisonment is a structural condition, not a moment-by-moment act. 1Jo 3:8 — "of the devil" — the enslaving power reaches the level of constituted allegiance: the person's deepest identity-marker is shaped by the master to whom they are enslaved. The characteristic is thus primarily a *dispositional power-condition* — the state of being under sin's governing authority — rather than a specific act or momentary event.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The corpus reveals two constituent elements. (1) *The practice-to-slavery mechanism*: Joh 8:34 — the mechanism is the practice of sinning producing the enslaved state; repeated sinning generates the structural captivity. (2) *The universal imprisonment dimension*: Gal 3:22 — the Scripture's imprisoning of everything under sin is a distinct element — not individual practice-to-slavery but a universal structural confinement that applies to all regardless of individual sinning patterns. These two elements address different aspects: the individual's practice producing their personal enslavement, and the cosmic/universal imprisonment that confines all under sin as the presupposition of the gospel.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Working description: *Sin as enslaving power (CHAR-12) is the characteristic in which sin operates as an active governing force that masters the inner person's will — specifically through the mechanism of habitual practice producing constitutional slavery (Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin") and through the universal structural imprisonment that Scripture discloses (Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin"). The enslaved person is constitutionally captive: their will is no longer free toward God. The deepest marker of the enslaved condition is allegiance-direction: 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil." The liberation from enslavement occurs through new birth (1Jo 3:9 — "God's seed abides in him... he cannot keep on sinning") and through Christ's protective intervention (1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him"). The characteristic is differentiated by eschatological consequence: 1Jo 5:16 names a form of sin that leads to death as the enslaving power's terminal outcome.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of sin as enslaving power?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The structural opposite is *freedom from sin's mastery through new birth and faith*. (1) *New birth as counter-power*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him" — the abiding seed is the structural opposite of the abiding sin-master; the born-of-God condition structurally prevents the enslaving power's hold. (2) *Christ's protection*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him" — the protective guardianship of Christ is the structural opposite of sin's enslaving mastery. (3) *Faith-promise*: Gal 3:22 — "the promise by faith in Jesus Christ... given to those who believe" — faith as the mechanism of receiving the promise is the structural opposite of the imprisonment that universal sin-condition constitutes.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does sin as enslaving power explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — The enslaving power explicitly excludes: (1) *Freedom of will toward God*: Joh 8:34 — the slave to sin cannot freely choose toward God; the will's God-directed freedom is what the enslavement eliminates; (2) *The practice of righteousness*: Rom 6:1/2 — "continue in sin... live in it" — living in sin excludes living in righteousness by the same logic (one cannot simultaneously live in two opposed domains); (3) *The born-of-God non-sinning pattern*: 1Jo 3:9 — "he cannot keep on sinning, because he has been born of God" — the enslaving power excludes this constitutional sinlessness-pattern; those still under sin's mastery cannot produce the born-of-God pattern.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is sin as enslaving power not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-11 (Sin as universal condition)*: CHAR-11 is the universal structural condition all persons stand in; CHAR-12 is the specific active governing function of sin as a master over the will. CHAR-11 is the broader category; CHAR-12 names the power-mechanism within it. (2) *Not CHAR-6 (Habitual defection)*: CHAR-6 is the settled dispositional direction of the will toward sin; CHAR-12 is the active power-structure that governs the will and prevents free choice otherwise. CHAR-6 describes the will's direction; CHAR-12 describes the external-to-internal governing force that has captured the will. (3) *Not Eph 4:26's anger becoming sin*: Eph 4:26 — "be angry and do not sin" — is at the boundary of the corpus but addresses the prevention of anger becoming sin, rather than the state of being under sin's enslaving mastery; it is the weakest connection to the characteristic's core definition.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does sin as enslaving power operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Three distinct inner modes. (1) *Practice-generated captivity*: Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — sin enslaves through the repetition of sinning; each practice-act deepens the captivity; (2) *Universal structural imprisonment*: Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin" — sin functions as a universal enclosure that Scripture declares, confining all persons under its power regardless of individual practice patterns; (3) *Allegiance-capture*: 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil" — the enslaving power reaches the deepest constitutional level — the person's allegiance is captured and redirected toward the devil's identity rather than God's.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does sin as enslaving power operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. (1) *Individual-practice context*: Joh 8:34 — the enslavement operates through individual habitual practice (the one who practices sin); the mechanism is personal and accumulative. (2) *Universal-cosmic context*: Gal 3:22 — the imprisonment operates at the universal level — everything confined under sin; the mechanism is structural and comprehensive, not dependent on individual practice. (3) *Community-protection context*: 1Jo 5:16/18 — the enslaving power's reach is addressed within the community context of intercessory prayer and the protective role of the born-of-God; the mechanism operates against a community within which some may still be under sin's reach (5:16 — the sinning brother).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does sin as enslaving power have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-12]** S — The 9-verse corpus does not evidence a communicative or speech-based mode for sin's enslaving power. The characteristic operates through practice-to-slavery (Joh 8:34), universal imprisonment (Gal 3:22), and allegiance-capture (1Jo 3:8) — all structural rather than verbal-communicative modes. The enslaving power does not speak or communicate; it governs.

**[CHAR-12]** E — Two response patterns. (1) *The enslaved person's non-response*: the enslaved person continues in sin — Rom 6:1 — "continue in sin" is the rhetorical form of the question about what the enslaved might do; the enslaved state is characterised by continued sinning. (2) *The born-of-God person's structural non-response to the enslavement*: 1Jo 3:9 — "he cannot keep on sinning, because he has been born of God" — the born-of-God person encounters the enslaving power but the God's-seed-indwelling constitutionally prevents it from gaining its typical hold. The protective dimension (1Jo 5:18 — "the evil one does not touch him") names the specific absence of the enslaving power's grip for the born-of-God person.

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Variable depending on the person's constitutional state. For the un-born-again person: the enslaving power holds and the response is continued sinning (Joh 8:34 — slavery through practice; Rom 6:2 — "live in it"). For the born-of-God person: the enslaving power is structurally resisted (1Jo 3:9 — "cannot keep on sinning"; 1Jo 5:18 — "does not keep on sinning"). For the endangered person within the community: 1Jo 5:16 — prayer and life given through intercession — the response available is communal-intercessory.

The corpus describes the structural state (slavery — Joh 8:34) and its prevention (new birth — 1Jo 3:9) but not the subjective experience of the enslavement process itself.

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained sin-enslavement establish?**

(1) *Slave-identity*: Joh 8:34 — the enslaved person's identity is now constituted around the master-slave relationship with sin; (2) *Devil-allegiance*: 1Jo 3:8 — "of the devil" — the identity-marker declares the person's deepest belonging; (3) *Death-trajectory*: 1Jo 5:16 — "sin that leads to death" — the most severe form of sin-enslavement establishes a trajectory toward eschatological death; the sustained enslavement moves the person toward the condition where intercessory prayer is unavailing.

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. The immediate response (continued sinning — Rom 6:1/2; the practice that produces the slavery — Joh 8:34) is the act-level. The sustained effect is constitutional: the person who has continued sinning is now a slave (a constituted identity), the allegiance is now to the devil (a deepened identity-marker), and the trajectory is toward death-through-sin (an eschatological consequence). The sustained effect moves from the behavioural to the constitutional and eschatological levels.

The liberated state is thus: constitutionally new (born of God), internally reinforced (God's seed abiding), and externally protected (Christ's guardianship). These secondary dimensions describe what the will's captivity produces beyond the captivity itself.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is sin as enslaving power explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him" — the new birth and God's seed are spirit-level realities; the fact that they prevent the enslaving power confirms that the enslaving power operates at the spirit level where it competes with the new-birth-constitution. 1Jo 3:8 — "of the devil" — the allegiance-capture at the deepest level ("of the devil") is a spirit-level identity claim; the person's spirit-level belonging has been redirected from God to the devil's domain. The enslaving power thus operates at the spirit level by competing with and displacing the God-relationship at the person's deepest constitutional identity-level.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does sin as enslaving power originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Partly. 1Jo 3:8 — "the devil has been sinning from the beginning" — the originary sinning is named as the devil's primordial activity; the devil's sinning from the beginning is the spirit-level source of the enslaving power's authority over those who practice sin. The enslaving power thus originates in the devil's originary spirit-level sinning, not in the creature's own spirit-level.

---

**T2.1.3 — What does spirit-level location reveal?**

**[CHAR-12]** E — Spirit-level location (1Jo 3:8/9 — devil's sinning as origin; new birth as counter at same level) reveals that the enslaving power is not merely a behavioural pattern but an identity-belonging claim at the deepest constitutional level. The enslaving power's spirit-level operation explains why new birth (a spirit-level transformation — 1Jo 3:9) is required for liberation: the power must be countered at the same constitutional depth at which it operates. Behavioural modification alone cannot address a spirit-level enslavement.

---

**T2.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**
