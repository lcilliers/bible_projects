## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The 162-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as moral auditor who measures and tracks iniquity to completion*: Gen 15:16 — "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete" — God tracks iniquity as a measurable quantity with a threshold; Hos 13:12 — "the iniquity of Ephraim is bound up; his sin is kept in store" — God maintains a sealed record; Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you, our secret sins in the light of your presence" — nothing is hidden from his omniscient gaze. (2) *God as just judge who acts when iniquity reaches its measure*: Isa 26:21 — "the Lord is coming out from his place to punish the inhabitants of the earth for their iniquity"; Jer 25:12 — after seventy years God punishes Babylon for their iniquity. (3) *God as sovereign pardoner who removes iniquity by his own act*: Isa 40:2 — "her iniquity is pardoned"; Mic 7:18/19 — "pardoning iniquity... he will tread our iniquities underfoot"; Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity." (4) *God as the one wearied by accumulated iniquity*: Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — the accumulation has a relational weight that strains even divine forbearance.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute iniquity as accumulated moral crime to God?**

**[CHAR-18]** S — No verse in the 162-verse corpus attributes the iniquity-as-crime condition to God. God appears as the one who measures iniquity (Gen 15:16), remembers it (Hos 13:12; Eze 21:24), punishes it (Isa 26:21), bears its weight vicariously through the servant (Isa 53:6 — "the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all"), and removes it (Zec 3:4). God does not bear iniquity as a personal moral crime; the characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The silence confirms iniquity-as-accumulated-crime is constitutively creaturely: it is the human moral account of wrongdoing accumulated before the one who measures and judges it. Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — positions God as the one upon whom iniquity's weight falls relationally; he is not the one who carries it as personal crime but the one who absorbs its relational burden. The silence is structurally necessary: God cannot commit moral crimes against himself or a higher authority, nor can he accumulate iniquity within himself. The very act of God pardoning iniquity (Mic 7:18) presupposes his total absence of it.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose iniquity as accumulated moral crime serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-18]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of a positive created purpose for iniquity-as-accumulated-crime. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition category: the accumulation of moral wrongdoing before God. No verse presents iniquity-as-crime as a designed feature of the human person. Its nearest functional dimension is its measurability (Gen 15:16 — "not yet complete"), which implies a divinely calibrated accounting system, but this accounting system responds to wrongdoing rather than being its purpose.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. Psa 51:5 — "I was brought forth in iniquity, and in sin did my mother conceive me" — iniquity characterises the womb-context of human existence, locating it at the constitutional origin of the fallen human condition but not at the original creation. Isa 53:6 — "all we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned — every one — to his own way; and the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all" — the universal iniquity arises from the self-directed turning of every person; the characteristic is the outcome of the fall's universal operation. No verse in the corpus situates iniquity-as-crime within the pre-fall created design.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that iniquity as accumulated moral crime is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — two eschatological orientations. (1) *Judgment endpoint*: Gen 15:16 — "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete" — the accumulation moves toward a divinely appointed threshold at which judgment falls; Jer 25:12 — after seventy years, Babylon's iniquity reaches its appointed reckoning; Isa 22:14 — "this iniquity will not be atoned for you until you die"; (2) *Removal endpoint*: Jer 50:20 — "in those days and in that time... iniquity shall be sought in Israel, and there shall be none" — the eschatological fullness is the complete dissolution of the iniquity-record; Zec 3:9 — "I will remove the iniquity of this land in a single day"; Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more" — the new covenant fullness is iniquity entirely removed from divine memory.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does iniquity as accumulated moral crime express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Iniquity-as-accumulated-crime expresses the image through its *inverted moral accounting*: the image-bearer is constituted as a moral being whose acts are weighed and measured before God (Gen 15:16 — iniquity measurable; Hos 13:12 — bound up in divine record). Only a being made in God's image can accumulate a moral account before him — only the image-bearer stands in the morally accountable relationship that makes iniquity possible. Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you" — the "before you" is the relational positioning of the image-bearer before God; the capacity to accumulate iniquity in God's sight belongs to the image's constituted God-relationship.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is iniquity as accumulated moral crime shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-18]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 — God does not bear iniquity as personal moral crime). The characteristic requires the position of moral offender accumulating crime before a higher moral authority; God occupies no such position.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of iniquity as accumulated moral crime reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The presence of iniquity-as-accumulated-crime reveals the image in its *morally corrupted and accumulating fallen condition*: the image-bearer not only commits individual sinning acts but has accumulated a store of moral wrongdoing that constitutes a standing crime before God. Isa 1:4 — "a people laden with iniquity" — the weight of the accumulation is the image's fallen condition expressed as moral burden; Ezr 9:6 — "our iniquities have risen higher than our heads" — the accumulation has exceeded the person's capacity to address it through self-effort. The image is not destroyed but its moral account before God is deeply defiled and growing.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use iniquity as accumulated moral crime typologically?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — through the servant-bearing passages (VCG-06 Isa 53 sub-set). Isa 53:5 — "he was crushed for our iniquities" — the servant's bearing of the people's accumulated iniquity is typological: the priestly scapegoat bearing (Lev 16:21/22 — VCG-01) is the OT type; the servant's bearing is the eschatological fulfilment-type pointing toward Christ. Isa 53:6 — "the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all" — the universal scope of the iniquity-bearing is the typological pinnacle: all accumulated human iniquity placed upon the one servant. Zec 3:4 — "I have taken your iniquity away from you" — Joshua's single-person iniquity-removal prefigures the land's total removal (Zec 3:9) and the new covenant's complete forgiveness (Jer 31:34).

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — From OT priestly institution and prophetic enactment toward NT Christological fulfilment. The scapegoat bearing Israel's iniquities and being sent to the wilderness (Lev 16:21/22 — VCG-01) is the cultic type; the servant who bears "the iniquity of us all" (Isa 53:6) is the prophetic type pointing toward Christ. Both types share the same structure: the iniquity of the many placed on the one bearer who removes it by carrying it away. The direction runs from human cultic institution (Lev 16) through prophetic prototype (Isa 53) toward divine fulfilment — from the human representative (Aaron; the scapegoat; the servant-figure) to the divine one who bears the iniquity of all.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Typological use is extensively evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Iniquity as accumulated moral crime." Three signals. (1) *Iniquity*: the English rendering of a.von (H5771G) — the OT's comprehensive term for moral wrongdoing at its deepest register; not merely a sinning act but the moral weight of that act accumulated before God; (2) *As accumulated*: the defining modifier — iniquity here is not the single act but the store of wrongdoing built up over time and generations; Gen 15:16 — "not yet complete" — the accumulation is quantifiable and progressive; (3) *Moral crime*: the "crime" dimension signals the forensic register — iniquity-as-crime stands before a judge (God) as an offence requiring adjudication. The name signals: the person's moral account of wrongdoing before God, built up over time, constituting a standing moral-legal liability.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Single primary term throughout the entire 162-verse corpus: **a.von (H5771G)** — the crime/moral-wrong sub-entry of the a.von root. The root (*ʿāwôn*) spans three distinct senses within M10's framework: H5771G (crime/moral-wrong — CHAR-18), H5771H (guilt/liability — CHAR-17 VCG-03), and H5771I (punishment/consequence — CHAR-17 VCG-04). The H5771G crime-sense names the wrongdoing itself as a moral reality before God — what Job calls "my iniquity" in the sense of "my moral crime" (Job 7:21 — "take away my iniquity"; Job 13:23 — "how many are my iniquities?"). No NT Greek vocabulary appears in this corpus; the characteristic is exclusively OT Hebrew, reflecting the a.von root's primary OT-framework operation.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — All three. *Directional*: iniquity has a trajectory of accumulation — Gen 15:16 — "not yet complete" — moving toward fullness; Hos 14:1 — "you have stumbled because of your iniquity" — the iniquity operates as a directional force pulling the person away from God. *Relational*: iniquity is always iniquity-before-God — Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you" — the relational frame is the moral account in God's presence; Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — iniquity is a relational burden between the person and God. *Constitutional*: Psa 51:5 — "I was brought forth in iniquity" — the iniquity-condition is constitutional, prior to individual acts; Eze 18:20 — "the wickedness of the wicked shall be upon himself" — it attaches constitutionally to the person who commits it.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is iniquity as accumulated moral crime?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The corpus reveals CHAR-18 as an *objective moral-legal condition* before God — not primarily a subjective feeling (that is CHAR-17) but the actual crime-weight the person carries in God's moral ledger. The definition identifies it as "the inner dimension of accumulated moral wrongdoing — crime in its moral-spiritual sense." The characteristic's five dominant registers illuminate its nature: (1) personal moral crime individually owned (VCG-04 — Eze 18:20 — "upon himself"); (2) accumulated weight that overwhelms (VCG-05 — Psa 38:4 — "gone over my head"); (3) generational crime transmitted across family lines (VCG-02 — Exo 20:5); (4) iniquity in divine memory (VCG-03 — Hos 13:12 — "bound up"); (5) iniquity that ruins from within (VCG-07 — Pro 5:22 — "ensnare him").

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The corpus reveals eight constituent elements corresponding to the eight VCGs. (1) *Priestly cultic bearing* (VCG-01 — 17V): the institutional mechanism for iniquity's ritual address; (2) *Generational transmission* (VCG-02 — 18V): iniquity crossing family lines; (3) *Divine memory* (VCG-03 — 28V): iniquity held in God's sight and record; (4) *Personal individual ownership* (VCG-04 — 29V): each person's own crime; (5) *Overwhelming accumulated weight* (VCG-05 — 30V): the mounting pressure that exceeds capacity; (6) *Pardon and removal* (VCG-06 — 21V): divine forgiveness resolving the moral debt; (7) *Self-destructive force* (VCG-07 — 15V): iniquity ruining from within; (8) *Heart-seated origin* (VCG-08 — 4V): iniquity originating in the will.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Working description: *Iniquity as accumulated moral crime (CHAR-18) is the inner dimension of accumulated moral wrongdoing — crime in its moral-spiritual sense, the store of inner wrongdoing before God as a measured, generational, heart-seated reality. The corpus evidences eight registers: (1) Priestly bearing (VCG-01 — 17V): Aaron confessing Israel's iniquities over the scapegoat (Lev 16:21/22); (2) Generational transmission (VCG-02 — 18V): iniquity of fathers visited on children to the third and fourth generation (Exo 20:5); (3) Divine memory (VCG-03 — 28V): God's omniscient awareness — "iniquity of Ephraim is bound up; his sin is kept in store" (Hos 13:12); (4) Personal ownership (VCG-04 — 29V): each soul dies for its own crime — Eze 18:20; (5) Overwhelming weight (VCG-05 — 30V): "gone over my head; like a heavy burden, they are too heavy for me" (Psa 38:4); (6) Pardon and removal (VCG-06 — 21V): servant bears/is crushed for iniquities of all (Isa 53:5/6/11); (7) Self-destructive force (VCG-07 — 15V): "ensnare him, held fast in cords of his sin" (Pro 5:22); (8) Heart-seated origin (VCG-08 — 4V): "taken their idols into their hearts... stumbling block of their iniquity" (Eze 14:3/4/7). Distinguished from CHAR-17 (the subjective experience of guilt) by being the objective moral-crime reality that subsists before God whether felt or not.*

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of iniquity as accumulated moral crime?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The structural opposite is *moral blamelessness — the state of no iniquity upon the person*. Psa 32:2 — "blessed is the man against whom the Lord counts no iniquity, and in whose spirit there is no deceit" — the not-counted state is the explicit opposite; Jer 50:20 — "iniquity shall be sought in Israel, and there shall be none" — the eschatological opposite is iniquity's total absence; 2Sa 22:24 — "I was blameless before him, and I kept myself from guilt" — blamelessness is the active moral opposite, sustained through self-restraint. Eze 18:20 — "the righteousness of the righteous shall be upon himself" — the righteous person's moral account is the direct structural opposite of the wicked person's iniquity upon himself.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does iniquity as accumulated moral crime explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Divine acceptance*: Lev 7:18 — offering eaten on the third day is "tainted" and the person "shall bear his iniquity" — iniquity nullifies the acceptance that the offering would have granted; (2) *Covenant standing*: Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God" — iniquity excludes unimpeded God-relationship; (3) *Answered prayer*: Isa 59:2 — "your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear" — iniquity excludes divine responsiveness; (4) *Standing before God*: Psa 130:3 — "if you, O Lord, should mark iniquities, O Lord, who could stand?" — iniquity, if fully accounted, excludes every person from standing before God.

**T1.3.3 — What is iniquity as accumulated moral crime not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-17 (Guilt as inner-being state)*: CHAR-17 is the subjective inner experience of moral liability — the conscience's felt recognition; CHAR-18 is the objective moral-crime reality before God that subsists whether the conscience registers it or not. Hos 12:8 — Ephraim denies iniquity can be found in him — but the iniquity is real (God knows — Hos 13:12); the denial is a CHAR-17 conscience-failure, not a CHAR-18 non-existence. (2) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the discrete sinning act; CHAR-18 is the accumulated store of moral crime that sinning acts have produced; (3) *Not CHAR-15 (Generational sin)*: CHAR-15 is the transmission mechanism through generations; CHAR-18 includes the generational dimension (VCG-02) but is not limited to it — it also encompasses individual personal crime (VCG-04 — Eze 18:20).

**T1.4.3 — Does iniquity as accumulated moral crime have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — in three speech-modes. **T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response to the onset of iniquity's weight?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — The immediate responses vary across the corpus. (1) *Conscience-strike*: 2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him after he had numbered the people" — the immediate inner response to the iniquity is conscience activation; (2) *Shame and face-avoidance*: Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God, for our iniquities have risen higher than our heads" — shame and the inability to face God are immediate; (3) *Request for removal*: 2Sa 24:10 — David immediately says "please take away the iniquity of your servant" — the immediate movement is toward plea for divine removal; (4) *Despair*: Psa 40:12 — "my iniquities have overtaken me, and I cannot see; they are more than the hairs of my head; my heart fails me" — the crushing weight produces immediate inner collapse.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Variable. The conscience-engaged person responds with shame and plea (Ezr 9:6; 2Sa 24:10); the overwhelmed person responds with despair (Psa 40:12; Psa 38:4 — "too heavy for me"); the self-deceived person shows no immediate response at all — Hos 12:8 — "they cannot find in me iniquity" — the iniquity is present but produces no internal response because the conscience is suppressed. Amo 9:10's sinners (CHAR-16) — presuming no disaster will come — illustrate the response-less extreme. The immediate response thus ranges from acute conscience-awareness (2Sa 24:10) through overwhelming despair (Psa 40:12) to complete denial (Hos 12:8).

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained iniquity establish?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Three established states. (1) *Separation from God*: Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear" — sustained iniquity establishes structural relational distance from God; (2) *Moral self-deception*: Psa 36:2 — "he flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated" — sustained iniquity establishes a false inner self-assessment; Hos 12:8 — the wealthy Ephraim's claim of clean hands alongside real iniquity; (3) *Ruinous self-destruction*: Eze 18:30 — "lest iniquity be your ruin" — the sustained iniquity establishes the ruin-trajectory as the person's inner direction.

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes. The immediate response (shame — Ezr 9:6; conscience-strike — 2Sa 24:10; despair — Psa 40:12) is episodic. The sustained effects (bodily decay — Psa 31:10; separation from God — Isa 59:2; moral self-deception — Psa 36:2; entrapment — Pro 5:22) are constitutional states established over time by unaddressed iniquity. The progression is: immediate shock of recognition → sustained accumulated weight → constitutional corruption of the inner person → ruination.

**[CHAR-18]** E — The perceptive faculty is engaged on both sides: the person's perception of their own iniquity and God's perception of all iniquity. ---

**T3.1.2 — Does iniquity as accumulated moral crime enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-18]** E — Iniquity *impairs* the perceptive faculty through its self-protective distortion mechanism. **[CHAR-18]** E — The perceptive pattern reveals an inverse relationship between the depth of iniquity's entrenchment and the accuracy of the person's self-perception: the more entrenched the iniquity (Psa 36:2 — the hardened wicked; Hos 12:8 — self-deceived Ephraim), the less accurately the person perceives it.
