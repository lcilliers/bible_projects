## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The 72-verse corpus reveals God's character primarily through explicit negation of injustice. (1) *God structurally defined as without injustice*: Deu 32:4 — "a God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he"; 2Ch 19:7 — "there is no injustice with the Lord our God, or partiality or taking bribes"; Psa 92:15 — "there is no unrighteousness in him"; Job 34:10 — "far be it from God that he should do wickedness, and from the Almighty that he should do wrong"; Zep 3:5 — "the Lord within her is righteous; he does no injustice"; (2) *God as the one who hates injustice*: Isa 61:8 — "I the Lord love justice; I hate robbery and wrong"; (3) *God as the righteous judge of injustice*: Rev 18:5 — God has remembered Babylon's crimes; 2Pe 2:9 — God keeps the unrighteous under punishment; (4) *God as the faithful rewarder against injustice*: Heb 6:10 — "God is not unjust so as to overlook your work."

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute injustice as moral failure of right conduct to God?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The corpus explicitly tests and denies this: Rom 3:5 — "That God is unrighteous to inflict wrath on us? (I speak in a human way.)" — the suggestion of divine injustice is raised as a rhetorical foil and immediately repudiated; Job 36:23 — "who can say, 'You have done wrong'?" — the question is rhetorical, implying the answer is no one. God's absolute freedom from injustice is one of the most densely evidenced theological claims in this corpus: five verses directly assert it (Deu 32:4; 2Ch 19:7; Psa 92:15; Job 34:10; Zep 3:5). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The corpus is not silent — it is emphatically declarative. God's freedom from injustice is the defining standard against which all human injustice is measured. Deu 32:4's "without iniquity, just and upright is he" establishes the divine character as the ontological baseline for justice; Eze 18's detailed Ezekiel-chapters ground individual moral accountability in God's own justice. The repeated explicit denials confirm: injustice requires a moral order to violate, and God is that moral order's author and embodiment — he cannot violate what he constitutes.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose injustice as moral failure of right conduct serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-22]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for injustice. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition phenomenon — a failure of the right-conduct faculty. No verse presents injustice as a designed feature of the image-bearer.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. Eze 28:15 — "you were blameless in your ways from the day you were created, till unrighteousness was found in you" — the cherub's pre-fallen blamelessness establishes that injustice was not original to the created being; it was "found in" the being as an emergence from within, not as a design-feature. Eze 18:24 — "when a righteous person turns away from his righteousness and does injustice" — injustice enters when the person turns from the prior righteousness; the turn is from, not toward, the designed state.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that injustice as moral failure of right conduct is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — two eschatological orientations. (1) *Judgment of the unjust*: 2Pe 2:9 — "the Lord knows how to... keep the unrighteous under punishment until the day of judgment" — the unrighteous's current condition anticipates the definitive judgment; Rev 18:5 — "God has remembered her iniquities" — the accumulated record awaits final reckoning; (2) *Eschatological elimination of injustice*: Zep 3:13 — "those who are left in Israel... shall do no injustice and speak no lies" — the purified remnant's future is characterised by injustice's total absence; the eschatological fullness is the restored right-conduct community.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does injustice as moral failure of right conduct express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Injustice expresses the image through its *inverted exercise of the moral-conduct faculty*: the image-bearer is constituted with the capacity for just conduct — to judge rightly, act fairly, and treat persons according to their worth (the M26 righteousness the cross-register flag tracks). Injustice is this same capacity deployed against its designed purpose. Psa 58:2 — "in your hearts you devise wrongs" — the heart's deliberating capacity (a mark of the image's rational design) is deployed toward unjust schemes. The very faculty of moral reasoning that enables justice also enables injustice.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is injustice as moral failure of right conduct shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-22]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). God is not only free from injustice but constitutes its standard-opposite. The characteristic has no divine analogue.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of injustice as moral failure of right conduct reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The presence of injustice reveals the image's *moral-conduct faculty in its perverted expression*: the image-bearer designed for just conduct (Lev 19:15 — "in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor") has turned their judicial, commercial, and interpersonal conduct away from God's standard. Psa 53:1 — "the fool says in his heart, 'There is no God.' They are corrupt, doing abominable iniquity" — the godless heart produces unjust conduct; the image's knowledge of God is what sustains just conduct, and its denial produces injustice. Job 18:21 — the unjust person "knows not God" — the absence of God-knowledge is the diagnostic marker of the unjust character.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use injustice as moral failure of right conduct typologically?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — through the Christological substitution type. 1Pe 3:18 — "Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God" — the unjust (*adikoi*) are the precise moral category for whom the righteous Christ substitutes; the unrighteous/unjust person-type is the direct object of Christ's redemptive action. The unjust person-type (VCG-03 — *adikos*) is deployed typologically: the human category of the unjust points toward the divine provision of the righteous One who takes their place.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The typological direction is from human moral condition (*adikos* — the unjust) toward divine redemptive provision (the righteous Christ dying for them). The human instance (the unjust person) does not point toward the divine; rather the divine action (the righteous dying for the unjust) defines the human category's need and resolution. Direction: human injustice-condition → divine righteous-provision as its resolution.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Injustice as moral failure of right conduct." Three signals. (1) *Injustice*: the antonym of justice (*mishpat*/dikaiosynē) — what violates the right standard in dealings with persons; (2) *As moral failure*: not merely legal breach but the inner-moral dimension — the heart's orientation toward wrong conduct against others; (3) *Of right conduct*: the failure is specifically of right-conduct (*tsedaqah*/*dikaiosynē*) — the M26 cross-register cluster. The name signals: M10's most explicitly other-directed characteristic — injustice is done *toward* persons and *against* right order; it cannot exist without a victim and a violated standard.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Five primary term-families. (1) **a.vel / av.lah (H5766A/H5766B)** — noun/verb "injustice, wrong, wickedness, violence" — OT dominant (VCG-01/02); root *ʿāwal* — "to act unjustly, to be crooked"; (2) **av.val (H5767)** — "the unrighteous, the unjust person" — agent-noun (VCG-03 OT); (3) **a.vil (H5760)** — "ungodly, morally lawless" — VCG-03 OT (Job 16:11; Job 18:21); (4) **adikos (G0094)** — NT "unjust, unrighteous" — agent-noun/adjective (VCG-03 NT dominant); (5) **adikēma (G0092)** — NT "wrongdoing, crime, injustice" — the act-noun (Act 18:14; Rev 18:5). The terms cluster around two axes: the wrong-act dimension (a.vel/av.lah/adikēma — what is done) and the wrong-person dimension (av.val/a.vil/adikos — who the person is).

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — All three. *Directional*: injustice is always directed toward someone — Psa 58:2 — "your hands deal out violence on earth" — the violence is toward others; Eze 3:20 — "commits injustice" as an act against the righteousness-standard; *Relational*: injustice damages persons — Psa 43:1 — "from the deceitful and unjust man deliver me"; Job 6:29 — "let no injustice be done" to Job; *Constitutional*: Luk 16:10 — "one who is dishonest in a very little is also dishonest in much" — injustice is a settled character trait scaling across all contexts; Zep 3:5 — "the unjust knows no shame" — the constitutional insensibility to their own wrongdoing.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is injustice as moral failure of right conduct?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The corpus reveals CHAR-22 as simultaneously a *dispositional character trait* and a *volitional act*. (1) *Dispositional*: Luk 16:10 — "dishonest in a very little is also dishonest in much" — a fixed inner character property that scales; Job 15:16 — "drinks injustice like water" — a thoroughly internalized disposition; Zep 3:5 — "the unjust knows no shame" — shamelessness as a stable character-trait; (2) *Volitional act*: Psa 58:2 — "in your hearts you devise wrongs; your hands deal out violence" — a deliberate inner devising; Eze 18:24 — "a righteous person... does injustice" — a specific act committed; Job 34:32 — "if I have done iniquity, I will do it no more" — injustice as a named doable-and-stoppable act.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Three constituent elements corresponding to the three VCGs. (1) *Judicial and commercial injustice* (VCG-01 — 20V): the corruption of right process — partial judging, fraudulent weighing, commercial deceit; (2) *Injustice as oppression and harm to persons* (VCG-02 — 33V): the doing of wrong against vulnerable persons — violence, deceit, verbal wrongdoing; (3) *Character of the unjust person* (VCG-03 — 19V): the person-type constituted by injustice — adikos, av.val, a.vil — whose character is the inner condition from which the acts proceed.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Working description: *Injustice as moral failure of right conduct (CHAR-22) is the inner state/disposition of doing wrong toward persons — unjust conduct, unjust character, the moral faculty bent toward wrongdoing against others and against right order. Three registers: (1) Judicial and commercial injustice (VCG-01 — 20V): "you shall do no injustice in court" (Lev 19:15); "till unrighteousness was found in you" (Eze 28:15); (2) Injustice as oppression and harm to persons (VCG-02 — 33V): "in your hearts you devise wrongs; your hands deal out violence on earth" (Psa 58:2); "the unjust knows no shame" (Zep 3:5); "drinks injustice like water" (Job 15:16); (3) Character of the unjust person (VCG-03 — 19V): "dishonest in a very little is also dishonest in much" (Luk 16:10); "the righteous for the unrighteous" (1Pe 3:18). Cross-register flag: M26 (Righteousness/Justice) on all terms — CHAR-22 is M10's entry-point into the justice/righteousness cluster. Distinguished from CHAR-21 (perversion as inner inversion) by its other-directed focus: injustice is always toward persons and against right-order.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of injustice as moral failure of right conduct?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The structural opposite is *righteousness/justice as right conduct* (*tsedaqah*/*dikaiosynē*) — named explicitly in multiple verses. Lev 19:15 — "in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor" — the impartial righteousness; Deu 32:4 — "just and upright is he" — the divine character as the standard; Zep 3:5 — "the Lord within her is righteous; he does no injustice" — God's righteousness as injustice's opposite; Psa 119:3 — "who also do no wrong, but walk in his ways" — the upright walk in God's ways as the structural opposite of doing wrong.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does injustice as moral failure of right conduct explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Inheritance of God's kingdom*: 1Cor 6:9 — "the unrighteous will not inherit the kingdom of God" — the unjust character disqualifies from the kingdom; (2) *Reliable trustworthiness*: Luk 16:11 — "if you have not been faithful in the unrighteous wealth, who will entrust to you the true riches?" — injustice forfeits the trust that enables greater stewardship; (3) *Standing before the righteous*: Pro 29:27 — "an unjust man is an abomination to the righteous" — injustice makes the person morally repugnant to those who value right conduct; (4) *Functioning as a judge*: 1Cor 6:1 — "does he dare go to law before the unrighteous?" — the unjust are unsuitable arbiters.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is injustice as moral failure of right conduct not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-21 (Perversion as inner inversion)*: CHAR-21 is the inner structural distortion of the moral faculty; CHAR-22 is that distortion specifically expressed as wrong-conduct toward others; CHAR-21 is constitutive, CHAR-22 is other-directed and conduct-specific; (2) *Not CHAR-19 (Transgression)*: CHAR-19 is the knowing boundary-crossing; CHAR-22 adds the specific other-victim dimension — injustice is done *toward* persons, not merely *past* a known boundary; (3) *Not mere carelessness*: Psa 58:2 — "in your hearts you devise wrongs" — injustice is a deliberate devising; Psa 64:6 — "they search out injustice, saying, 'We have accomplished a diligent search'" — the active, purposeful character distinguishes CHAR-22 from inadvertent wrong.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does injustice as moral failure of right conduct operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Four distinct inner modes. (1) *As a heart-devised scheme*: Psa 58:2 — "in your hearts you devise wrongs" — injustice is planned from the heart's deliberating centre; Psa 64:6 — "they search out injustice" with "diligent search" — deliberately planned; (2) *As a thoroughly internalised appetite*: Job 15:16 — "a man who drinks injustice like water" — the characteristic has become the person's natural sustenance; (3) *As shameless character*: Zep 3:5 — "the unjust knows no shame" — the inner moral-response faculty is insensible; (4) *As a fixed scalar trait*: Luk 16:10 — "dishonest in very little is also dishonest in much" — the inner character is consistent at every scale.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does injustice as moral failure of right conduct operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes across the three VCG registers. (1) *Judicial context* (VCG-01): injustice operates as partiality and bribery — Psa 82:2 — "judge unjustly and show partiality to the wicked" — the relational-authority context shapes the judicial mode; Lev 19:15 — "you shall do no injustice in court" — the court context defines this mode; (2) *Oppression context* (VCG-02): injustice operates as violent harm against persons — Psa 58:2 — "your hands deal out violence on earth"; Job 15:16 — thoroughly internalised appetite for wrong; (3) *Character context* (VCG-03): injustice operates as a stable identity-level disposition — Luk 16:10 — fixed across small and large; shameless insensibility (Zep 3:5).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does injustice as moral failure of right conduct have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — through the verbal-wrongdoing dimension. Isa 59:3 — "your tongue mutters wickedness" — injustice is uttered; Job 6:30 — "is there any injustice on my tongue?" — the tongue as the potential organ of verbal injustice; Job 13:7 — "will you speak falsely for God and speak deceitfully for him?" — injustice as theological-speech falsehood; Zep 3:13 — "they shall do no injustice and speak no lies" — injustice and lying speech paired as the two modes being eliminated in the remnant. Speech is CHAR-22's secondary expressive mode after conduct.

**[CHAR-22]** E — Varied across positions. (1) *Response of the one suffering injustice*: Job 6:29 — "please turn; let no injustice be done. Turn now; my vindication is at stake" — urgent plea for relief; Psa 43:1 — "vindicate me, O God, and defend my cause against an ungodly people, from the deceitful and unjust man deliver me" — immediate appeal to God for deliverance; (2) *Response of the righteous observer*: Psa 37:1 — "fret not yourself because of evildoers; be not envious of wrongdoers" — the warning against fretful envy; (3) *Non-response of the unjust*: Zep 3:5 — "the unjust knows no shame" — no inner response at all.

The unjust person's inner response is shameless absence of response (Zep 3:5).
