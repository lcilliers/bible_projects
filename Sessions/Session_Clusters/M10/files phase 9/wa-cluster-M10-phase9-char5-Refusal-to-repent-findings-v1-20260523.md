## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent reveals God's character primarily through the expectation it violates and the response it draws. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back to your law. Yet they acted presumptuously and did not obey" — reveals God as a God who warns before judging, who desires turning, and who regards the refusal to turn as a presumptuous overriding of his patient initiative. The God who warns is a God of moral patience and persistent invitation; the human refusal to turn is the creature's rejection of that invitation. Heb 10:26 — "if we go on sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth, there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins" — reveals that God's provision of sacrifice presupposes the creature's willingness to engage it; refusal to turn after knowing the truth closes off the divine provision. The characteristic reveals God as one whose moral provisions (sacrifice, warning, the turn) are refused by the creature that should receive them.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute refusal to repent to God?**

**[CHAR-5]** S — No verse in the 18-verse corpus attributes refusal to repent to God. God appears throughout as the one whose law is not followed (2Ki 10:31), whose warnings are not heeded (Neh 9:29), and whose sacrifice is closed off by deliberate refusal (Heb 10:26). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely: it presupposes a will that can choose to turn but chooses not to — a freedom God possesses but in the opposite direction (God always acts in accordance with his own moral nature). God cannot refuse to repent because God does not sin and therefore has no turning to withhold.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The absence of any divine refusal-to-repent in the corpus (T0.1.2) combined with the evidence of God's persistent moral invitation (Neh 9:29 — warnings given to turn them back) reveals that refusal to repent belongs entirely to the fallen creaturely condition. The divine image includes the *capacity* to turn (genuine moral agency, genuine will) but the fallen creature's use of that capacity in the direction of refusal is not a divine image expression but its negation. The silence confirms: the image provides the capacity for moral turning, and refusal to repent is what the will does with that capacity when it is captured by sin.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose refusal to repent serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-5]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that refusal to repent serves any positive created purpose. The evidence uniformly presents it as a moral failure: 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord with all his heart" (failure of alignment); Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" (hardened resistance to God's word); Heb 10:26 — sinning deliberately after knowing the truth (closing off divine remedy). None of these evidence a created purpose; all evidence a failure of the created design.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether refusal to repent is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The evidence points exclusively to refusal to repent as belonging to the fallen condition. The corpus's dominant pattern — kings who "did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam" across generations — is a portrait of a lineage of fallen wills persisting in inherited sinfulness. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back" — the act of warning presupposes a departure from the original design that God wishes to restore. Heb 10:26 — "sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth" — the knowledge received presupposes an original or restored moral clarity that the deliberate sinning then works against. Refusal to repent is not a designed capacity but a fallen use of the designed capacity for turning.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that refusal to repent is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus evidences a negative future orientation: the trajectory of refusal to repent points toward judgment and loss, not toward fullness. Heb 10:26 — "there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins" — the future state toward which deliberate persistent sinning moves is the closing off of divine remedy: a state of irreversible moral liability. Eze 18:24 — "the sin he has committed, for them he shall die" — the future of the righteous person who turns to persistent sin is death. The characteristic is not oriented toward a future fullness but toward a future of closing-off: the progressively foreclosed possibility of restoration.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does refusal to repent express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes the image's central feature: genuine moral agency — a will that can choose. The corpus's language is uniformly volitional: "he did not depart," "he clung to," "they would not obey" (Neh 9:29), "we go on sinning deliberately" (Heb 10:26). These formulations presuppose that departure was available and choosing not to depart is a genuine exercise of will. The image's volitional capacity — the freedom to turn or not turn — is what makes refusal to repent possible. The characteristic perverts the image's most distinctive feature (moral freedom to turn toward God) by using it to remain away from him.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest refusal to repent is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-5]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 and T0.1.3). God does not sin and therefore has no turning to withhold. The characteristic is structurally inapplicable to God.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of refusal to repent in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The presence of refusal to repent reveals the divine image in a condition of *persistent volitional capture*: the will that was designed to turn toward God is entrenched in a sinful direction and choosing not to reverse. 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord... with all his heart" — the divided or entirely un-aligned heart is the image-condition. Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" — the image's bodily and volitional faculties are set in resistance rather than responsive turning. The image is present (the will exists, agency exists) but has been persistently directed away from God rather than toward him. The image's capacity for turning — and the sustained refusal to use it — is what makes refusal to repent a particularly revealing indicator of the image's compromised condition.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use refusal to repent typologically?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Limited typological deployment. The Kings Jeroboam-pattern corpus (1Ki 16:31; 2Ki 3:3; 10:29/31; 13:2/6/11; 14:24; 15:9/18/24/28) functions as a *cumulative typological warning*: each king who "did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam" both perpetuates the sin and adds to the typological portrait of a leadership class whose refusal to turn models — and transmits — a pattern of sustained refusal. This cumulative pattern typologically anticipates the NT's sharpest formulation in Heb 10:26: the persistent, deliberate sinning after knowing the truth as the ultimate form of refusal to repent. The OT pattern points toward the NT's eschatological intensification.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The direction is *human instance pointing forward to eschatological reckoning*. The OT Kings pattern (each king who did not depart) accumulates into a witness that points toward the eschatological consequence of sustained refusal that Heb 10:26 articulates: the closing off of the sacrificial remedy. The OT instances are the type — human leaders who refused to turn — and Heb 10:26's announcement is the antitype consequence: when refusal to repent persists after full knowledge of the truth, the divine provision for sin is no longer available. The typological direction is escalating: from individual kings refusing → to the community refusing (Neh 9:29) → to the NT warning that persistent refusal has eschatological finality.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Refusal to repent." The name encodes three claims: (1) *Refusal* — this is an active act of the will, not a passive failure. The verb "refuse" implies that turning was available and the will said no. This is confirmed throughout the corpus: "he did not depart from" (2Ki 3:3; 13:2/11; 14:24; 15:9/28), "he did not turn aside from" (2Ki 10:29), "they would not obey" (Neh 9:29), "sinning deliberately" (Heb 10:26). (2) *To repent* — the thing refused is a moral turning, a departure from the sinful course. Repentance is the available alternative that the will refuses. (3) The name implies a *known alternative*: one can only refuse what one is called to do. The definition confirms this: "turning actively withheld." The name signals: the will possesses the capacity to turn, is called to turn, and chooses not to.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398)** in the phrase construction "did not depart from the sins of..." — the characteristic is not expressed by a single dedicated term but by the combination of cha.ta (sins) with verbs of non-departure: *lo sar min* (did not turn aside from — 2Ki 10:29), *lo sar min* (did not depart from — 2Ki 13:2/11), *lo sur* (did not turn away — 2Ki 15:24), "clung to" (*davaq* — 2Ki 3:3). The secondary OT term is in Neh 9:29 where sinning (*chatanu*) is accompanied by the physical metaphor "stubborn shoulder" (*natan kateph sorerah* — gave a stubborn shoulder) and "stiffened their neck" (*qashu et-orpam*). The primary NT term is **hamartanō (G0264)** in its persistent/deliberate mode: 1Ti 5:20 — "persist in sin" (*hamartanontōn*); Heb 10:26 — "go on sinning deliberately" (*hekousiōs hamartanontōn*). The definitional revelation: the characteristic has no single dedicated term but is expressed through the combination of sinning-vocabulary with non-departure language.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: the refusal to repent is a refusal to turn — the will is held in a direction away from God and away from righteousness; the turning that is refused would be a directional change. "He did not turn aside from... he did not depart from" (2Ki 10:29; 13:2) — the language is directional. *Relational*: the refusal is against God's law (2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord") and against his warnings (Neh 9:29 — "you warned them... yet they would not obey"). The refusal is exercised in the context of a relational claim. *Constitutional*: Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" — these bodily-metaphor terms describe a constitutional posture of resistance, not a single act. 2Ki 10:31 — "not with all his heart" — the constitutional incompleteness of the inner commitment.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is refusal to repent?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent is primarily a *sustained volitional disposition* — not a single act but a settled direction of the will maintained over time. The corpus's dominant form — the Kings formula repeated across 10+ kings ("did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam") — presents refusal to repent as a characteristic persisted in across reigns, even lifetimes: 2Ki 15:18 — "he did not depart all his *days* from all the sins of Jeroboam" — a lifetime's direction named explicitly. 1Ti 5:20 — "those who *persist* in sin" — the present participle naming ongoing dispositional stance. The characteristic is not one act of not turning but the settled dispositional commitment to remain on the sinful course.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus reveals two constituent elements. (1) *Passive non-departure*: the majority of the Kings corpus — "did not depart," "did not turn aside" — presents the characteristic as a failure to move, a remaining-in-place. The will does not actively turn toward sin but fails to turn away from it. (2) *Active clinging and hardening*: 2Ki 3:3 — "he *clung* to the sin of Jeroboam" — the will actively adheres to the sinful course; Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder... stiffened their neck... *would not* obey" — active wilful resistance to turning. The two constituent elements (passive non-departure vs active clinging/resistance) represent different intensities of the same refusal: from staying put (passive) to gripping and resisting (active).

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Working description: *Refusal to repent is the sustained volitional condition in which the will persistently declines to turn from a sinful course that the person knows they are called to forsake. The characteristic is constituted by a series of non-departures — each moment the will could turn and does not — accumulating into a settled inner disposition of remaining on the sinful path. It ranges from passive non-departure (the Kings who "did not depart from" the sins of Jeroboam — 2Ki 13:2/11/14:24) to active clinging (2Ki 3:3 — "clung to the sin") to bodily-metaphor hardening (Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder... stiffened neck"). In the NT, it reaches its sharpest form as deliberate ongoing sinning after knowledge of truth (Heb 10:26). The primary faculty is the will; the primary location is the heart in its incomplete-commitment mode (2Ki 10:31 — "not with all his heart").*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of refusal to repent?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The structural opposite is *repentance* — the turning of the will from the sinful course toward God. The corpus names this opposite through its own negation: "did not turn aside from" (2Ki 10:29) implies that turning aside would be the positive; "would not obey" (Neh 9:29) implies that obeying would be the opposite. Eze 33:12 — "the wickedness of the wicked, he shall not fall by it when he *turns from* his wickedness" — turning from wickedness is explicitly named as the alternative to the sinning that overrides prior righteousness. The structural opposite is the turning/departure that the will refuses — the repentance that, once performed, reverses the condition.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does refusal to repent explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes and resists: (1) *Obedience to God's law*: 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord, the God of Israel" — law-walking is what refusal to repent excludes. (2) *Complete heart-commitment*: 2Ki 10:31 — "with all his heart" — wholehearted inner alignment is what the divided/non-turned will resists. (3) *Response to warning*: Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back... yet they acted presumptuously and did not obey" — responsive turning to prophetic warning is what the stubborn will resists. (4) *The sacrifice*: Heb 10:26 — deliberate sinning after knowing truth closes off sacrifice — refusal to repent excludes the use of the divine remedy.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is refusal to repent not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Three clear boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression)*: conscience suppression is where the person does not recognise they have sinned or need to turn; refusal to repent is where the person knows (Heb 10:26 — "after receiving the knowledge of the truth") and refuses. The boundary is at conscience-registration: CHAR-4 = conscience not registering; CHAR-5 = conscience registers (or knowledge is present) but the will refuses to act on it. (2) *Not CHAR-6 (Habitual defection)*: habitual defection is the sustained pattern of sinning without the specific will-act of refusing; refusal to repent implies a decision-point where turning was available and withheld. (3) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: wilful sinning is the single act of committing the known wrong; refusal to repent is the ongoing decision not to stop and reverse.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does refusal to repent operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Three modes. (1) *Passive continuation*: the will remains on the sinful course by default — the Kings pattern "did not depart from" (2Ki 13:2; 14:24; 15:9) presents an apparently passive mode: the will simply continues without turning. (2) *Active clinging*: 2Ki 3:3 — "he *clung* to the sin of Jeroboam" — the will actively holds the sinful course, making the refusal an act of attachment. (3) *Active hardening against correction*: Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck and would not obey" — the will actively resists the call to turn; the refusal is not passive but a stiffening against God's word. These three modes share the common feature of the will not departing, but differ in the will's active contribution to the non-departure.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does refusal to repent operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Yes. (1) *Leadership context*: the Kings pattern (1Ki 16:31; 2Ki 3:3; 10:29–31; 13:2/6/11; 14:24; 15:9/18/24/28) shows refusal to repent operating at a leadership level where the leader's refusal transmits the sinful course to the people ("which he made Israel to sin"). The leader's inner refusal has communal scope. (2) *Communal context*: 2Ki 13:6; Neh 9:29 — the whole community "did not depart from" or "would not obey" — collective refusal operating through communal formation. (3) *Individual post-truth context*: Heb 10:26 — a single person who has received knowledge of truth and deliberately continues sinning — individual refusal in a context of maximum available information. (4) *Formerly-righteous context*: Eze 18:24 / 33:12 — the person who has been righteous and then turns away, sinning against their prior standing.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does refusal to repent have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Limited speech-based evidence. 1Ki 16:31 — Ahab treating Jeroboam's sins "as a light thing" implies an internal evaluative speech-act: the characterisation of established wickedness as minor is a verbal-cognitive minimisation. The corpus does not evidence refusal to repent primarily through speech-acts but through *action/non-action*: the will's refusal is expressed through what it does (clings, remains, walks in) and does not do (does not depart, does not turn). Rom 6:15 — "are we to sin because we are not under law but under grace?" — the hypothetical question implies that refusal to repent can be voiced as a theological argument, which Paul emphatically rejects ("By no means!"). The characteristic can be articulated as a position, but its primary mode is dispositional, not verbal.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when refusal to repent is confronted?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus shows two patterns of response when refusal to repent is confronted externally. (1) *Continued non-departure*: the Kings corpus shows that prophetic and covenantal warnings did not break the pattern — the formula "did not depart from" applies even to kings who received divine word. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them... yet they acted presumptuously" — the warning met by presumptuousness is the inner response. (2) *Public rebuke required*: 1Ti 5:20 — "those who persist in sin, rebuke them in the presence of all, so that the rest may stand in fear" — the persistent refuser's response is not changed by normal correction; only public confrontation aimed at producing communal fear addresses it. The immediate inner response of the person who refuses to repent is continued refusal — the characteristic is by definition one that does not yield to normal correction.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The immediate response is consistent: the corpus shows no instance where the refusal-to-repent pattern is broken within the evidence presented. Every king formula ends with "did not depart from" — the confrontation with the available turn never produces the turn within these verse-portraits. Neh 9:29's "warned them... yet they acted presumptuously" — the warning is met by increased presumptuousness, not by turning. The consistency suggests that refusal to repent as a settled volitional disposition is highly resistant to immediate correction; its resolution, when it occurs, is not evidenced within the corpus of these 18 verses.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus is not silent on immediate response — the response is consistently continued refusal (T1.5.1 and T1.5.2). However, the inner phenomenological experience of the person who is refusing to repent — what it feels like from within — is not evidenced. The corpus describes the condition and its external expression (non-departure) but not the person's inner experience of the moment of confrontation.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does refusal to repent produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Two sustained inner-being effects are evidenced. (1) *Constitutional hardening*: Neh 9:29's "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" is the end-state of sustained refusal — the bodily-metaphor names a constitutional posture of resistance that has become characteristic. The stiffening is a formed disposition, not a single act. (2) *Loss of prior standing*: Eze 18:24 — "none of the righteous deeds that he has done shall be remembered" — sustained refusal to repent (turning from righteousness to sin) produces the erasure of the prior moral record. Eze 33:12 — "the righteous shall not be able to live by his righteousness when he sins" — prior righteousness cannot sustain the person who has entered persistent sinning.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained refusal establish?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Sustained refusal to repent establishes: (1) *Inherited moral orientation received and kept*: 2Ki 15:9 — "as his fathers had done. He did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam" — the inherited sinful pattern is accepted and sustained, becoming one's own moral orientation through continued non-departure. (2) *Lifelong directional commitment*: 2Ki 15:18 — "all his days" — refusal to repent produces a whole-life orientation. (3) *Increasing entrenching of the will in the sinful direction*: 2Ki 3:3's "clung to" describes an active inner relationship with the sin — sustained refusal creates a bond between the will and the sinful course. (4) *Closed-off divine remedy*: Heb 10:26 — sustained deliberate sinning after knowing truth forecloses the sacrifice — the most severe consequence of refusal to repent.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The immediate response is continued non-departure (the same refusal maintained). The sustained effect adds to this: the will becomes increasingly entrenched (from "did not depart" → "clung to" → "stubborn shoulder"), and the external consequences accumulate (loss of standing — Eze 18:24; foreclosure of remedy — Heb 10:26). The sustained effect thus shows a *directional deepening*: not only does the will continue in its refusal, but the refusal becomes more entrenched, the will more captured, and the consequences more severe. What begins as non-departure can deepen into active clinging and constitutional hardening.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable refusal to repent?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Three enabling conditions are evidenced. (1) *Inherited sinful orientation already in place*: the Kings pattern shows that each king "followed the sins of Jeroboam" — the inherited orientation is already established, and refusal to repent is simply its continuation. 2Ki 15:9 — "as his fathers had done" — the enabling condition is an inherited moral legacy that has been received and not rejected. (2) *Minimisation of established wickedness*: 1Ki 16:31 — "as if it had been a light thing for him to walk in the sins of Jeroboam" — moral minimisation of the gravity of the sinful course is the enabling condition for not departing from it. (3) *Partial obedience as substitute for turning*: 2Ki 10:29/31 — Jehu reformed in some areas but "did not turn from" in others — partial obedience enables continued refusal by providing the inner sense of having done enough.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent refusal to repent?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus names the blocking conditions through contrast. (1) *Walking in the law of the Lord with all the heart*: 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord... with all his heart" — the positive blocking condition is whole-heart commitment to God's law. (2) *Fear of God*: 1Ti 5:20 — "so that the rest may stand in fear" — the fear of God (or fear of judgment) that public rebuke aims to produce in the community is the deterrent against persistent refusal. (3) *Receiving and acting on the knowledge of truth*: Heb 10:26 — the person who has received knowledge of truth and acts on it rather than continuing to sin.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters the call to repent but does not refuse — who turns?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus evidences the turning person most directly through Eze 33:12 — "the wickedness of the wicked, he shall not fall by it when he turns from his wickedness" — the turning person is the one whose prior wickedness does not condemn them because the will has moved. The turning person's inner state is the opposite of "did not depart" — it is active departure, movement, change of direction. The corpus (18 verses all describing refusal) does not portrait the inner state of the turning person extensively, but Eze 33:12's "turns from his wickedness" is the positive counter-portrait: the will that moves in the direction of departure.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of refusal to repent?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The primary inner-being dimension is **volition / will** — the characteristic is constitutively a will-act (refusal) sustained over time. The definitional language throughout is volitional: "did not depart," "clung to," "would not obey," "sinning deliberately" (Heb 10:26 — *hekousiōs* = willingly/voluntarily). The will is both the faculty that could turn and the faculty that is refusing the turn. The characteristic cannot exist without an operative will — there must be a will that is capable of turning for there to be a refusal to turn.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports the will-primary classification?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — (1) 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk... with all his heart" — the heart as the integrating volitional centre is the faculty engaged; "all his heart" = complete volitional alignment; its absence = volitional partial-refusal. (2) Neh 9:29 — "would not obey" — the "would not" is a volitional statement. (3) Heb 10:26 — "sinning deliberately" (*hekousiōs*) — the adverb of willing/deliberateness names the will directly. (4) 2Ki 3:3 — "clung to" (*davaq*) — a volitional attachment. (5) Rom 6:15 — the hypothetical of sinning "because" of grace — the "because" implies a reasoned volitional choice about whether to sin.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does refusal to repent carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Moral evaluation*: 1Ki 16:31 — Ahab's minimisation of the sin as "a light thing" involves a moral evaluation that is wrong — the moral evaluation faculty producing a false verdict that enables the refusal. (2) *Knowledge*: Heb 10:26 — "after receiving the knowledge of the truth" — the cognitive dimension of knowing the truth is the background against which the deliberate sinning is measured. The will acts against available knowledge. (3) *Constitutional bodily-metaphor*: Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" — the body as the expression of the will's resistance. These secondary dimensions describe what the will works through and what shapes its continued refusal; they do not compete with the will-primary classification.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is refusal to repent explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-5]** S — The 18-verse corpus contains no explicit spirit-level (ruach/pneuma) location for refusal to repent. ## T3 — Inner Faculties (33 prompts)

**T3.1.1 — Does refusal to repent engage the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The perceptive faculty is engaged in the minimisation mode. 1Ki 16:31 — "as if it had been a light thing for him to walk in the sins of Jeroboam" — the inner perceptive/evaluative assessment of the sin as "light" or trivial is a perceptive act that shapes refusal to repent: if the sin is perceived as minor, the urgency of turning from it is diminished. The perceptive faculty has assessed the moral gravity incorrectly. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them" — the warnings were delivered (perceptive input provided) but the stubborn neck and shoulder show the warnings were not correctly received despite being present.

---

**T3.1.2 — Does refusal to repent enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent *impairs* the perceptive faculty in its moral-gravity-assessment function: 1Ki 16:31 — the minimisation of the sin's gravity shows a perceptive faculty producing false assessments. Sustained refusal to repent (the Kings pattern across a lifetime — 2Ki 15:18) further impairs the perceptive faculty by habituating the person to perceiving the sinful course as normal and acceptable. The more consistently the turn is refused, the more the perceptive faculty adjusts to the sinful course as the default, making the gravity of the non-departure increasingly imperceptible.

---

**T3.1.3 — What does the perceptive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The perceptive pattern reveals that refusal to repent is sustained partly through a *perceptive distortion of moral gravity*: the sinful course is perceived as lighter than it is (1Ki 16:31) or as normal by habituation (the Kings pattern). Correcting refusal to repent therefore requires not only volitional change but perceptive re-calibration — the person must come to perceive the sin's gravity accurately before the will is sufficiently motivated to turn. Neh 9:29's warnings were an attempt to re-calibrate perceptive assessment from outside; their failure shows that external warning alone cannot correct the perceptive distortion when the will is entrenched.

---

**T3.2.1 — Does refusal to repent engage the cognitive faculty?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The cognitive faculty is centrally engaged in Heb 10:26 — "sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth" — the person has the relevant knowledge cognitively (the truth has been received) and the will acts against it. The cognitive faculty holds the truth but the will refuses to act in accordance with it. 1Ki 16:31 — the minimisation of Jeroboam's sins ("a light thing") is a cognitive assessment. The cognitive faculty is engaged in both the knowing-the-truth mode (Heb 10:26) and the false-evaluation mode (1Ki 16:31).

---

**T3.2.2 — Does refusal to repent enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent *bypasses* cognition in the Heb 10:26 mode: the knowledge is present but the will acts against it. In the minimisation mode (1Ki 16:31), it *distorts* cognition by producing a false moral evaluation that enables continued refusal. Over time, sustained refusal may impair cognition by entrenching the false evaluation (minimisation) as the habitual cognitive framework, making accurate moral assessment progressively harder.

---

**T3.2.3 — What does the cognitive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The cognitive pattern reveals a critical distinction within CHAR-5: the Heb 10:26 mode shows the will acting against correct knowledge (knowledge present, will bypasses it), while the 1Ki 16:31 mode shows the will enabled by distorted knowledge (knowledge corrupted by minimisation). In both cases the will refuses, but the cognitive configuration differs: in one, the problem is purely volitional (know but don't turn); in the other, the problem combines volitional and cognitive failure (don't turn partly because know wrongly).

**T3.3.1 — Does refusal to repent engage the memory faculty?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Memory is implicitly engaged through the inherited-pattern dimension. 2Ki 15:9 — "as his fathers had done" — the son who follows his fathers in not departing from Jeroboam's sins carries the memory of his fathers' pattern as the inherited moral framework. The memory of "this is how things have been done" provides the cognitive-memorial basis for the inherited refusal. Neh 9:29 — the corporate prayer recites the history of Israel's stubborn refusal over generations — the memory faculty holds this history as the community's self-understanding.

**T3.3.2 — Does refusal to repent enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent *selectively activates* the memory of inherited patterns (the fathers' example — 2Ki 15:9) while potentially suppressing or not accessing the memory of what the sinful course has cost. The person who "did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam" holds the memory of that pattern as the natural path while not engaging the memory of the consequences (loss of standing, Eze 18:24; foreclosure of remedy, Heb 10:26). Memory is engaged in the reinforcement direction (reinforcing the inherited pattern) but not in the corrective direction (recalling the costs of the sinful course).

**T3.3.3 — What does the memory pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The memory pattern reveals that refusal to repent is sustained partly by *selective memory*: the memory of the inherited sinful pattern is operative (providing precedent and norm), while the memory of its moral gravity and consequences is not operative. Neh 9:29's communal prayer recites the history precisely to break this selective memory — by naming what the community has repeatedly done despite warnings, the prayer aims to make the suppressed moral-memory accessible. The corrective of refusal to repent requires making the memory of the sinful course's costs as vivid as the memory of the course itself.

**T3.6.1 — Does refusal to repent engage the volitional faculty?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The volitional faculty is the *primary* faculty engaged — refusal to repent is constitutively a will-act. The entire corpus is volitional language: "did not depart," "did not turn," "would not obey" (Neh 9:29), "sinning deliberately" (Heb 10:26 — *hekousiōs* = willingly). The will is the faculty that has the capacity to turn and is refusing to exercise it in the direction of turning. The characteristic is impossible without an operative will — the will must be functioning for there to be a refusal; an absent or non-functioning will would be CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression) territory.

**T3.6.2 — Does refusal to repent enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent *misdirects* volition while keeping it fully operative. The will remains fully functioning — it is making choices, clinging to the sinful course, resisting correction (Neh 9:29 — "would not obey" is an act of will). But the direction of volitional operation is away from God and away from the available turn. Over time, sustained refusal *constrains* volition: Heb 10:26 — "there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins" — the persistent-refusal will progressively forecloses the options available to it (the sacrifice is closed off). The will is not impaired in its operational capacity but progressively constrained in its available options through sustained refusal.

**T3.6.3 — What does the volitional pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The volitional pattern reveals that refusal to repent is a *sustained volitional commitment to non-departure*: each moment of "did not depart from" is itself a volitional act (or sustained non-act) that constitutes the characteristic. The cumulative effect of these sustained volitional non-departures is: the will becomes increasingly bound to the sinful course (2Ki 3:3 — "clung to"), the volitional options progressively narrow (Heb 10:26), and the will's default orientation is shaped toward continued refusal. Refusal to repent is thus a self-propagating volitional condition: each act of volitional non-departure makes the next one more likely.

**T3.7.1 — Does refusal to repent engage the agency faculty?**

Over time, the accumulated agentive outputs of the sinful course (the actions performed while not departing) constitute the person's life-trajectory, making departure increasingly costly: 2Ki 15:18 — "all his days" — an entire lifetime's agency invested in the sinful course.

This is why long-established refusal to repent (lifetime — 2Ki 15:18; generational — 2Ki 15:9) is harder to reverse than recent refusal: more of the person's agency-history is invested in the sinful course.

**T3.8.3 — What does the moral evaluation pattern reveal?**

## T7 — Lexical, Literary, and Human Science (20 prompts)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — No single dedicated Hebrew or Greek term names refusal to repent; the characteristic is expressed through *compound constructions*. The primary OT construction is: **lo sar min** ("did not turn aside from") or **lo sur min** ("did not depart from") + **chat.tat** (the sins of Jeroboam). Sur (H5493) means to turn aside, depart, remove; its negated form (*lo sur*) names the refusal-direction explicitly. The complementary construction is **davaq** (H1692 — 2Ki 3:3: "clung to") meaning to cling, adhere, be joined — revealing the active dimension of the refusal. The OT bodily-metaphor construction is **qashu et-orpam** (Neh 9:29 — "stiffened their neck" — H7185 qasha: to be hard, stiff) + **natan kateph sorerah** ("gave a stubborn shoulder" — H5637 sarar: to be stubborn/rebellious). The primary NT construction is **hekousiōs hamartanontōn** (Heb 10:26: sinning willingly/deliberately) and **hamartanontōn** in the present continuous (1Ti 5:20: those persisting in sin).

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary terms?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The grammatical range of the primary construction: (1) *Negated movement verb + preposition + sin-noun* (lo sur min + chat.tat): this is a predicate negation construction — it names what did not happen (turning/departing) relative to a specified object (the sins). (2) *Perfect/imperfect verb* of clinging (davaq — 2Ki 3:3): the perfect aspect of "clung to" names a completed act of attachment with ongoing effect. (3) *Hiphil stem* of the hardening verbs (Neh 9:29 — qasha, sarar): the Hiphil makes these causative — they actively caused their neck to stiffen, their shoulder to be stubborn. (4) *NT present participle* (hamartanontōn — 1Ti 5:20; Heb 10:26): ongoing action, active voice — sinning that continues. The grammatical range reveals: refusal to repent is expressed as both *completed adherence* (perfect davaq) and *continued ongoing action* (present participle hamartanō) — the will's past commitment to non-departure and its present continuation.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary terms?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The sur/lo sur construction's semantic range covers: (1) *physical turning aside* (a person turning from a path); (2) *moral turning from a practice* (departing from a sinful course — the characteristic's primary sense); (3) *complete severance* (removing oneself entirely from a course). The Jeroboam-sin formula is the covenantal-political standard. (2) *Corporate prayer-lament* (Neh 9:29): the characteristic confronted within a communal act of confession and prayer — the refusal is acknowledged as part of the community's history before God. (3) *NT theological argument* (Rom 6:15; Heb 10:26): refusal to repent addressed within doctrinal argument about the relationship between grace/law and moral accountability. **T7.2.5 — Does any verse function as the primary anchor?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — 2Ki 3:3 functions as the primary anchor: "Nevertheless, he clung to the sin of Jeroboam the son of Nebat, which he made Israel to sin; he did not depart from it." This verse most fully expresses CHAR-5's essential character because: (1) it contains both the active clinging (*davaq*) and the negated departure formula, capturing the characteristic's two modes; (2) the "nevertheless" signals the contrast with available reform — the refusal to repent is present even where other improvements occur; (3) the communal transmission dimension is named ("which he made Israel to sin"); (4) it represents the most intense single OT expression of the characteristic within the corpus.

**T7.2.6 — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — 2Ki 3:3 uniquely reveals the *co-existence of partial reform and sustained refusal*: "Nevertheless, he clung to..." — the "nevertheless" is the critical marker. Jehoram had removed the Baal pillar (2Ki 3:2) — a genuine reform — and yet continued to cling to Jeroboam's sins. No other verse in the corpus so clearly names the phenomenon of partial obedience alongside continued refusal: the reform in one area does not constitute turning in another. This reveals that refusal to repent can persist and coexist with genuine partial reforms — the characteristic is domain-specific in its operation. A person may genuinely turn from some sinful practices while continuing to cling to others, and the clinging in the unreformed area remains a full instance of CHAR-5.

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — **Motivational psychology** (specifically self-determination theory, reactance theory, and addiction research) provides the most useful framework. Reactance theory (Brehm) illuminates how perceived threats to freedom produce increased valuing of the threatened behaviour — Neh 9:29's stiffened neck and stubborn shoulder in response to God's warnings is a classic reactance pattern: the warning produces increased resistance rather than compliance. Self-determination theory illuminates the *why* of sustained non-departure: the person's motivational system has internalised the sinful course as identity-defining, making departure feel like self-loss. Addiction research illuminates the *clung to* dimension (2Ki 3:3 — davaq): the attachment of the will to the sinful course shows features of addictive bonding — the sinful course has become the source of psychological reinforcement that departure would remove.

**T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Reactance theory illuminates 1Ki 16:31's "as if it had been a light thing" — Ahab's minimisation of the prior sinful course before going further may represent a reactance pattern: the warnings against Jeroboam's sins (not evidenced within these verses but implied by the covenantal context) had been treated as threats to freedom, producing increased investment in the sinful course (going further to Baal worship). Self-determination theory illuminates 2Ki 15:18 ("all his days") — the lifelong refusal suggests that the sinful course had become identity-constitutive for Menahem; departure would have required a reorganisation of his entire self-understanding as king and as Israel. The "as his fathers did" pattern (2Ki 15:9) illuminates how inherited orientation becomes identity-defining through familial-cultural formation.

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The primary divergence: motivational psychology treats the refusal to change as a function of motivational factors that could in principle be reconfigured — change the reinforcement structure, the identity framing, or the reactance dynamic, and the person could turn. The verse evidence treats the refusal as a *moral act before God* that carries accountability regardless of its motivational origins: Heb 10:26 — "sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth" — names the volitional quality as the morally decisive factor, not the person's motivational configuration. God holds the kings accountable for not departing from Jeroboam's sins (the regnal formulas always include "he did evil in the sight of the Lord"), not because their motivational structure was defective but because their will made a moral choice. The divergence reveals that the verse evidence treats refusal to repent as a *free moral act* in a way that psychological frameworks that emphasise motivational determinism cannot fully capture.

---

**T7.3.4 — Does the framework surface any aspect not yet addressed by the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — Addiction research surfaces the question of the *withdrawal cost* of departure — the phenomenon where the person cannot easily stop the sinful behaviour because departure involves real experiential cost (the removal of psychological reinforcement). This is not named in the verse evidence (which assumes departure is always available and the refusal is volitional) but illuminates why the *davaq* (clinging) mode of CHAR-5 is more resistant to correction than the passive non-departure mode: the person who clings has a stronger psychological bond to the sinful course than the person who merely has not departed. The framework does not require further verse investigation — the corpus does not address the subjective phenomenology of departure-cost; the framework enriches the reading of 2Ki 3:3 without requiring new data.
