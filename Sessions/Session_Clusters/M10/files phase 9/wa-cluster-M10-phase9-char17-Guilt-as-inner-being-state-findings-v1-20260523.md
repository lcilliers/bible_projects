## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The 97-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who sees guilt when the person cannot hide it*: Jer 2:22 — "though you wash yourself with lye and use much soap, the stain of your guilt is still before me, declares the Lord God" — divine omniscience makes guilt permanently visible regardless of the person's concealment effort; Psa 69:5 — "the wrongs I have done are not hidden from you" — the divine gaze penetrates guilt's concealment. (2) *God as the one who withholds to provoke guilt-recognition*: Hos 5:15 — "I will return again to my place, until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God strategically withdraws until guilt is acknowledged; the withdrawal is purposeful. (3) *God as the one who removes guilt by sovereign act*: Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for" — God removes the guilt-burden through the seraph's action; (4) *God as the one who exacts less than guilt deserves*: Job 11:6 — "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" — divine mercy moderates the full punitive weight.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute guilt as inner-being state to God?**

**[CHAR-17]** S — No verse in the 97-verse corpus attributes the inner experience of guilt to God. God appears as the one who sees guilt (Jer 2:22), responds to it with judgment or mercy, removes it (Isa 6:7), and withholds until it is acknowledged (Hos 5:15) — but does not bear guilt as a subjective inner state. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of guilt as inner state, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The silence confirms that guilt-as-inner-state is constitutively creaturely: it is the conscience's recognition of moral liability *before* God — a relational inner state that presupposes both an offender (the person) and the one before whom the offense exists (God). God cannot be guilty before another, having no superior moral authority. Ezr 9:15 — "we are before you in our guilt, for none can stand before you because of this" — guilt is the condition of standing before God with an unresolved moral debt; God stands in no such relational position before another. The silence is structurally necessary.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose guilt as inner-being state serves in the human person?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The characteristic serves the purpose of *conscience-activation toward remedial action*. Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he has committed" — guilt-recognition triggers confession; Lev 6:4 — "if he has sinned and has realized his guilt and will restore what he took" — guilt-realization drives restitution; Num 5:7 — "he shall confess his sin that he has committed. And he shall make full restitution" — guilt produces the double movement of confession and restitution. The characteristic thus equips the person to recognise moral failure and initiate repair of what the failure broke.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether guilt as inner-being state is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Primarily a response to the fallen condition, but with a design-function dimension. (1) *Response to fall*: Gen 4:13 — Cain's guilt ("my punishment is greater than I can bear") presupposes the fall and the sinning act; Lev 5:2-5's guilt-recognition sequence presupposes prior violation. (2) *Design-function dimension*: the conscience's capacity to recognise guilt (Lev 4:13 — "they realize their guilt"; Num 5:6 — "that person realizes his guilt") is itself a created faculty; the guilt-recognition capacity belongs to the image-bearer's constituted conscience. Guilt-as-inner-state is the fallen-condition activation of a conscience-capacity that belongs to the created design.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that guilt as inner-being state is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — toward both removal and reckoning. (1) *Positive orientation — removal*: Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" — the eschatological fullness is the complete removal of the guilt-burden; Jer 33:8 — "I will cleanse them from all the guilt of their sin against me" — the future fullness is total cleansing from accumulated guilt; (2) *Negative orientation — final reckoning*: Eze 21:25 — "the time of your final punishment" — the guilt-burden accumulates toward its eschatological appointed day; Isa 24:6 — inhabitants suffer for their guilt in the earth's final desolation. Both orientations are evidenced: the guilt-state points either toward its removal (Isa 6:7; Jer 33:8) or its eschatological enforcement (Eze 21:25).

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does guilt as inner-being state express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Guilt-as-inner-state expresses the image through the image-bearer's *moral accountability before God*: only a being made in God's image stands before God in a morally accountable relationship. Ezr 9:15 — "we are before you in our guilt, for none can stand before you because of this" — the guilt is experienced as a barrier to standing before God; only a being designed to stand before God (the image-bearer) experiences this specific form of conscience-burden. Gen 42:21 — the brothers' conscience connects past cruelty to present suffering, demonstrating the moral-causal awareness that belongs to the image-bearing conscience.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is guilt as inner-being state shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-17]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 — God does not bear guilt as a subjective inner state). The characteristic requires the position of moral debtor before a higher moral authority, which God does not occupy.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of guilt as inner-being state reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The presence of guilt as inner-being state reveals the image in its *morally accountable fallen condition*: the conscience is functional enough to recognise guilt (Lev 4:13/22/27 — "realizes his guilt") but the recognition occurs after the violation rather than preventing it. The guilt-awareness reveals a conscience operating in its reactive mode: the image-bearer's moral self-assessment is post-hoc. Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the false heart is the image's condition that generates guilt; the guilt-bearing confirms the image's fallen state rather than the absence of conscience.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use guilt as inner-being state typologically?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — through the transference dimension (VCG-03). Exo 28:38 — "Aaron shall bear any guilt from the holy things that the people of Israel consecrate" — the high priest's guilt-bearing on his forehead is typological: Aaron as the one who bears the community's guilt before God prefigures the NT sin-bearer. Eze 4:4/5/6 — the prophet Ezekiel is commanded to bear Israel's and Judah's punishment symbolically by lying on his side — the enacted guilt-bearing is typological of the servant-bearing of iniquity (Isa 53:12 — "he bore the sin of many" — in CHAR-13's corpus).

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — OT institution/prophetic enactment → NT Christological fulfilment. Aaron's guilt-bearing on his forehead (Exo 28:38 — the high priest physically bearing Israel's cultic guilt) is the type pointing toward Christ's high-priestly guilt-bearing. Ezekiel's enacted punishment-bearing (Eze 4:4/5/6 — lying on his side for 390 + 40 days) is the prophetic type pointing toward the servant who bears iniquity. The direction is from human institution/prophetic act (OT) to divine fulfilment (NT). Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" — through the seraph's coal (OT) points toward the NT's once-for-all guilt-removal through Christ's atoning work.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Guilt as inner-being state." Three signals. (1) *Guilt*: the moral condition of having violated a standard and bearing its weight — not merely the legal verdict but the lived inner experience of that verdict; (2) *As inner-being state*: the characteristic is specifically the subjective inner experience, not the objective record (CHAR-13 — sin as divine record) or the act (CHAR-1). The "state" naming signals duration: guilt is not a momentary sensation but a condition the person inhabits; (3) *Inner-being*: the guilt operates within — it is the conscience's recognition of moral liability before God. The name signals: a sustained inner-experiential condition of moral-liability awareness, distinguishable from the act that produced it and the divine record that holds it.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Four Hebrew term-groups across the 97 verses. (1) **a.sham (H0816)** — verb "to be guilty, to incur guilt, to realise one's guilt" (VCG-01 dominant): Lev 4:13/22/27; 5:2/3/4/5/17/19 — the guilt-realisation sequence; the verb form emphasises the moment of coming-to-awareness. (2) **a.shem (H0818)** — noun form "a guilty one, guilt" (VCG-01): Gen 42:21 — "in truth we are guilty"; Ezr 10:19 — "their guilt offering was a ram." (3) **ash.mah (H0819)** — noun "guilt, guiltiness" (VCG-02 dominant): Ezr 9:6 — "our guilt has mounted up to the heavens"; the noun names the accumulated condition. (4) **a.von (H5771H/I)** — "iniquity, guilt, punishment" (VCG-03/04): H5771H names the guilt as transferable burden (Gen 44:16; Exo 28:38); H5771I names the punitive consequence (Gen 4:13 — "my punishment"; Eze 4:4/5/6). (5) **pu.qah (H6330)** — "stumbling, staggering of conscience" (1Sa 25:31 — single verse, M03 cross-register): names the destabilising inner effect of guilt-awareness on the conscience.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — All three. *Directional*: guilt is directed toward God — Ezr 9:15 — "we are before you in our guilt" — the guilt-state faces toward the divine judge; guilt is always guilt-before-someone. *Relational*: guilt is constitutively relational — it requires both the guilty person and the one wronged; Num 5:6 — "any of the sins that people commit by breaking faith with the Lord" — guilt arises from a broken relationship; Gen 42:21 — "we are guilty concerning our brother" — guilt arises from wrong against another person. *Constitutional*: Ezr 9:6 — guilt as a condition that has mounted up over time; Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the guilt is a constitutional weight the false-hearted person must carry.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is guilt as inner-being state?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The corpus reveals CHAR-17 as a *sustained inner condition of the conscience engaged in moral-liability awareness*. The definition names it: "the conscience's recognition of moral liability before God — the subjective inner experience of being guilty." Three distinct inner registers within the condition: (1) *Guilt-recognition* (VCG-01): the moment of conscience-awareness arising when a violation "becomes known" — Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt"; (2) *Guilt-as-accumulated-weight* (VCG-02): the sustained condition of carrying moral liability — Ezr 9:6 — guilt mounted to the heavens; (3) *Guilt-as-consciousness-of-punitive-liability* (VCG-04 — a.von H5771I): Gen 4:13 — Cain's felt unbearability of the punishment-dimension of his guilt.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The corpus reveals five constituent elements corresponding to the five VCGs. (1) *Conscience-recognition moment* (VCG-01): the specific inner event of guilt-realisation after violation; (2) *Accumulation dynamic* (VCG-02): guilt mounting as more violations add to the weight; (3) *Transferability* (VCG-03): guilt as a burden that can be assumed by or redirected to another; (4) *Punitive-consequence dimension* (VCG-04): the specific weight of the penalty-dimension of guilt; (5) *Indelibility and divine perspective* (VCG-05): Jer 2:22 — guilt visible to God despite self-cleansing attempts; Isa 6:7 — the removal of guilt through divine action.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Working description: *Guilt as inner-being state (CHAR-17) is the conscience's recognition of moral liability before God — the subjective inner experience of being guilty, of bearing a wrong that cannot be hidden. The corpus evidences five registers: (1) The conscience-recognition moment (VCG-01 — 18 verses): guilt-realization arising when a violation becomes known — "when he realizes his guilt" (Lev 4:13/22/27; 5:2/3/4/5) — the inner trigger for confession and restitution; (2) Guilt as accumulated weight (VCG-02 — 19 verses): the mounting, compounding condition — Ezr 9:6 "our guilt has mounted up to the heavens" — guilt accumulating beyond the head; (3) Guilt as transferable burden (VCG-03 — 16 verses): the guilt-weight that can be borne by another — Aaron on his forehead (Exo 28:38); Abigail's self-substitution (1Sa 25:24); (4) Guilt's punitive-consequence dimension (VCG-04 — 18 verses): the a.von/punishment register — Cain's "my punishment is greater than I can bear" (Gen 4:13); Ezekiel's enacted bearing (Eze 4:4/5/6); (5) Guilt exposed, indelible, and removed (VCG-05 — 26 verses): Jer 2:22 — indelible despite soap; Isa 6:7 — taken away by divine act. The characteristic is distinguished from CHAR-13 (the objective divine record) by being the subjective inner experience of what that record means to the conscience.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of guilt as inner-being state?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The structural opposite is *the state of being acquitted / not guilty*. Psa 34:22 — "none of those who take refuge in him will be condemned" — the not-condemned state is the direct opposite of the guilty state; Psa 34:21 — the wicked are condemned (guilty); Psa 34:22 — those who take refuge in God are not condemned (not guilty). Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" — the removed-guilt state is the opposite of the guilt-state. Lev 5:19 — "he has indeed incurred guilt before the Lord" — its opposite is the cleared-of-guilt standing. Ezr 9:15 — "none can stand before you because of this" — the opposite is the standing-before-God-freely state that cleared guilt enables.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does guilt as inner-being state explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Standing before God*: Ezr 9:15 — "none can stand before you because of this" — guilt-state excludes the capacity to stand in God's presence; (2) *Self-concealment*: Jer 2:22 — "the stain of your guilt is still before me" — guilt cannot be hidden from God by any human self-cleansing effort; (3) *Self-exculpation*: Gen 44:16 — "how can we clear ourselves? God has found out the guilt of your servants" — the divine discovery of guilt forecloses self-justification; (4) *Acquittal without address*: Lev 5:19 — guilt is incurred before the Lord — it cannot simply evaporate; it requires addressing through the prescribed remedy.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is guilt as inner-being state not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-13 (Sin as divine record)*: CHAR-13 is the objective moral account before God — the record exists whether the person feels it or not; CHAR-17 is the subjective inner experience of that record — the conscience's awareness of what CHAR-13 holds. CHAR-13 is objective; CHAR-17 is subjective. (2) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the act that creates the guilt; CHAR-17 is the subsequent inner state that follows the act; (3) *Not CHAR-16 (Sinner as moral character)*: CHAR-16 is the constituted identity of the person defined by sin; CHAR-17 is the inner experiential state of carrying guilt at any given moment — a person can bear CHAR-17 guilt without yet being CHAR-16's constituted sinner-type.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does guilt as inner-being state operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Five distinct inner modes corresponding to the five VCGs. (1) *As conscience-recognition event*: Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt... and confesses" — the guilt operates as a moment of dawning inner recognition; (2) *As mounting accumulation*: Ezr 9:6 — "guilt has mounted up to the heavens" — guilt operates as a growing weight that progressively overwhelms; (3) *As unbearable burden*: Gen 4:13 — "my punishment is greater than I can bear" — guilt-as-punitive-weight operates as an inner load exceeding capacity; (4) *As conscience-stagger*: 1Sa 25:31 (pu.qah) — "pangs of conscience" — guilt operates as an inner stumbling that destabilises moral footing; (5) *As indelible stain*: Jer 2:22 — "the stain of your guilt is still before me" — guilt operates as an inner defilement that no self-generated effort removes.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does guilt as inner-being state operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes across the five VCGs. (1) *Individual-unintentional context* (VCG-01): guilt operates through conscience-recognition after a violation; the recognition is the key moment — Lev 4:13 — "they realize their guilt"; (2) *Communal-accumulated context* (VCG-02): guilt operates as a generational and corporate weight — Ezr 9:7 — "from the days of our fathers... we have been in great guilt"; (3) *Priestly-liturgical context* (VCG-03): guilt operates as a transferable burden borne representationally — Exo 28:38 — Aaron bears the people's cultic guilt; (4) *Punitive-judicial context* (VCG-04): guilt operates through the weight of earned consequence — Gen 4:13 — Cain's punishment; Eze 4:4/5/6 — enacted punishment-bearing; (5) *Prophetic-judgment context* (VCG-05): guilt operates through its indelibility before God (Jer 2:22) and its exposure at the time of divine reckoning (Eze 21:23/24).

---

**T1.4.3 — Does guilt as inner-being state have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — in two directions. (1) *Confession*: Lev 5:5 — "confesses the sin he has committed" — guilt's natural speech-mode is confession; the inner guilt-recognition moves into verbal acknowledgment before God; Num 5:7 — "he shall confess his sin" — the guilt is spoken outward; (2) *Acknowledgment of guilt before another*: Gen 42:21 — "in truth we are guilty concerning our brother" — the brothers' verbal acknowledgment of guilt to one another; Gen 44:16 — Judah's "God has found out the guilt of your servants" — verbal acknowledgment of guilt before Joseph. The guilt-state thus has a speech-mode of honest naming — both before God (confession) and before others (acknowledgment).

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response to guilt's onset?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The immediate response pattern across VCG-01 is: recognition → shame or distress → remedial action. Gen 42:21 — "in truth we are guilty concerning our brother, in that we saw the distress of his soul... that is why this distress has come upon us" — the immediate inner response is interpretive: the brothers connect their current suffering to their past guilty act; Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you" — shame is the immediate inner-affective response to guilt-recognition; Gen 4:13 — Cain's "my punishment is greater than I can bear" — the immediate inner response to the punitive guilt-dimension is despair.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Variable. Shame (Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush"), despair (Gen 4:13 — "greater than I can bear"), interpretive retrospection (Gen 42:21 — connecting past guilt to present suffering), and remedial initiative (Lev 6:4 — "realized his guilt and will restore") are all immediate inner responses evidenced. The variable response reflects the three guilt-registers: guilt-recognition in the Lev 4/5/6 priestly context produces remedial initiative; guilt-accumulation in Ezr 9 produces shame; guilt-as-punitive-weight in Gen 4 produces despair. The response type varies with the guilt-register experienced.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-17]** E — For the communal accumulated guilt contexts (VCG-02 — 2Ch 24:18; 2Ch 28:10/13; 1Ch 21:3), the immediate inner response of the individual community-members to the collectively incurred guilt is not described. The corpus names the guilt and its communal consequences (divine wrath) but not the individual's moment-by-moment inner experience of bearing the community's accumulated guilt-weight.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does guilt as inner-being state produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Four sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Increasing accumulation*: 2Ch 33:23 — "Amon incurred guilt more and more" — the sustained guilt-state grows over time without address; (2) *Physical incapacitation*: Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you" — the posture of guilt cannot lift its face before God; Eze 33:10 (CHAR-13 context) — rotting away; (3) *False-heart-deepening*: Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the sustained guilt-state accompanies and deepens a false inner disposition; (4) *Inner stumbling*: Hos 5:5 — "Israel and Ephraim shall stumble in his guilt" — the sustained guilt-weight causes the person to lose moral footing progressively.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained guilt establish?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Three established states. (1) *Shame-posture*: Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God" — sustained guilt establishes a bodily-inner posture of shame and face-hiding before God; (2) *Inability to stand*: Ezr 9:15 — "none can stand before you because of this" — sustained guilt establishes a standing-disability before God; (3) *Punitive expectation*: 2Ki 7:9 — "punishment will overtake us" — sustained guilt-consciousness establishes an inner orientation of expecting punishment; the conscience is attuned to the punitive consequences of the guilt-burden.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes. The immediate response (shame — Ezr 9:6; despair — Gen 4:13; interpretive retrospection — Gen 42:21) is episodic. The sustained effects (accumulation — 2Ch 33:23; shame-posture — Ezr 9:6; inability to stand — Ezr 9:15; stumbling — Hos 5:5) are constitutional conditions that develop as the guilt-state persists without resolution. The sustained condition is more debilitating than the initial response: Ezr 9:6's shame produces Ezr 9:15's standing-disability, which is the accumulated inner effect of guilt that has been borne for generations (Ezr 9:7 — "from the days of our fathers").

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine guilt-recognition?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Knowledge of the violation*: Lev 5:2/3/4 — "when it is hidden from him... when he comes to know it" — guilt-recognition requires the relevant knowledge; the recognition is blocked when the violation is hidden; (2) *Honest conscience*: Gen 42:21 — "in truth we are guilty" — the word "in truth" (*ʾăbāl*) names honest acknowledgment as the enabling inner posture; (3) *Divine withdrawal provoking seeking*: Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God's strategic withdrawal creates the inner pressure that enables guilt-acknowledgment; the absence of God's presence is itself an enabling condition for guilt-recognition.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort guilt-recognition?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Three blocking conditions. (1) *Hardened false heart*: Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the false heart does not accurately register guilt even while bearing it; (2) *Pride*: Eze 16:49 — "this was the guilt of your sister Sodom: she and her daughters had pride" — pride is the inner disposition that prevents guilt-recognition by generating a false sense of self-sufficiency; (3) *Self-generated concealment*: Jer 2:22 — the attempt to wash away guilt with lye and soap is itself a blocking mechanism — the effort at self-cleansing is a form of guilt-avoidance rather than guilt-recognition.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who refuses to acknowledge guilt?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Eze 21:23/24 — "they have sworn solemn oaths, but he brings their guilt to remembrance" — the person who suppresses guilt-acknowledgment exists in a state of false oath-confidence that is overridden by God's recall of the guilt-record; Amo 9:10 (CHAR-16 context) — "disaster shall not overtake or meet us" — the guilt-denier's inner state is presumptuous self-assurance. Jer 2:22 names the guilt-denier's activity: attempting self-cleansing while the stain remains before God. The inner state of guilt-refusal is thus false confidence sustained against an objective guilt-reality that God sees and will eventually expose.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — The primary dimension is **conscience in its guilt-awareness mode** — the definition's own identification. The conscience is the faculty that registers guilt (Lev 4:13/22/27 — "realizes his guilt"), experiences its weight (Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed"), staggered by it (1Sa 25:31 — pu.qah — pangs of conscience), and initiates the remedial movement (Lev 6:4 — "realized his guilt and will restore"). Guilt-as-inner-state is constitutively a conscience-phenomenon; it cannot exist without conscience engagement.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — (1) Lev 4:13/22/27 — "they realize their guilt" — the realisation is conscience's act; (2) Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses" — conscience-realisation produces confession; (3) Gen 42:21 — the brothers' conscience connects their past cruelty to their present distress; (4) 1Sa 25:31 — pu.qah (staggering of conscience) names the conscience-specific faculty directly; (5) Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt" — the acknowledgment is a conscience act.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does guilt as inner-being state carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — three secondary dimensions. (1) *Affective dimension*: Ezr 9:6 — shame; Gen 4:13 — despair/unbearability — guilt produces sustained negative affect; (2) *Social-relational dimension*: Gen 42:21 — guilt concerning the brother; 2Sa 19:19 — guilt as moral debt owed to the king — guilt is relational, owing something to someone; (3) *Punitive-consequence dimension* (VCG-04): Gen 4:13 — the punishment-sense of a.von — the guilt-state carries the weight of earned consequence; the punitive dimension is secondary to the primary conscience-recognition dimension.
