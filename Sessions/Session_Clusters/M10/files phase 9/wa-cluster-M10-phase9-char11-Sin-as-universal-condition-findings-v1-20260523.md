## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The 112-verse corpus reveals God's character through five distinct dimensions. (1) *God as the one whose glory constitutes the standard from which sin's universality is measured*: Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — God's glory is the normative standard; sin is defined as the universal human falling-short of that glory, revealing God as the one in whose image humanity was made to live but from whose likeness all have departed. (2) *God as the one who alone can forgive sins*: the VCG-02 forgiveness register — Mar 2:7; Luk 5:21 — "who can forgive sins but God alone?" — God's exclusive authority to forgive is what makes Jesus's forgiveness-declarations a claim to divine identity. The character of God as the exclusive forgiver is the foundation on which the entire VCG-02 corpus rests. (3) *God as the one who condemns sin in the flesh through the Son*: Rom 8:3 — "he condemned sin in the flesh" — God's active judicial movement against sin through the incarnation reveals his moral seriousness and salvific intent simultaneously. (4) *God as the one whose holiness requires propitiation and purification*: Heb 2:17; Heb 1:3 — sin creates an offense requiring propitiation and a defilement requiring purification; both reveal God as holy in a way that cannot simply absorb sin's presence. (5) *God as the one whose new-covenant promise is specifically the removal of sin*: Rom 11:27 — "when I take away their sins" — God's covenantal commitment is defined by what he will do about sin.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute sin as universal condition to God?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Partially — in an inverse form. Joh 8:46 — "Which of you convicts me of sin?" — and Heb 4:15 — "tempted as we are, yet without sin" — and 2Cor 5:21 — "him who knew no sin" — all confirm that the sinless state is attributable to God (in Christ), which is the structural opposite of the universal sinful condition. God does not possess the condition; God's sinlessness is the standard against which the universality of human sin is measured and from which all humanity has fallen (Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God"). The explicit divine sinlessness functions as the positive ground of the characteristic's definition.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The silence on God possessing sin as universal condition is absolute and explicit — reinforced positively by the sinlessness of Christ (Heb 4:15; 2Cor 5:21; Joh 8:46) and the requirement for propitiation and purification that implies God's own holiness is not under sin's dominion (Heb 1:3; 2:17). The silence suggests that sin as universal condition is precisely the condition that separates the creature from the Creator: it is the defining gap between the human being as made and the God in whose image the human was made. Gen 4:7 — sin "crouching at the door" — presupposes a moral agency that can resist (unlike God who does not face such a force); the addressability of the command ("you must rule over it") presupposes a creaturely confrontation with sin that is categorically absent from the divine nature.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose sin as universal condition serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-11]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that sin as universal condition serves any positive created purpose. The entire corpus presents it as the condition requiring salvation (VCG-02), the enslaving power requiring liberation (VCG-03), and the defining quality of the wicked requiring judgment (VCG-01). Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God" — frames sin as a deficiency from the creature's intended end, not a feature of the created design.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether sin as universal condition is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The evidence is clear: sin as universal condition belongs entirely to the fallen condition. Rom 5:12 — "sin came into the world through one man" — sin entered the world; it was not part of the original world as created. Gen 4:7 — sin "crouching at the door" with its "desire for you" — the scene presupposes that sin as a power is already present and threatening (post-fall), not the original creation state. Rom 3:23's "fall short" presupposes an original intended end (the glory of God) from which humanity has departed — the falling is the fallen condition; the glory was the original direction. Heb 4:15 — Christ "without sin" yet fully human — establishes that true humanity does not require sin; sin is the abnormality, not the norm.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that sin as universal condition is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes — in two directions. (1) *The negative eschatological trajectory*: VCG-01 — Mal 4:1 — "all evildoers will be stubble. The day that is coming shall set them ablaze" — sin as universal condition among the wicked is oriented toward eschatological judgment; (2) *The positive eschatological resolution*: VCG-02 — Luk 24:47 — "repentance for the forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed in his name to all nations" — the proclamation of sin's forgiveness is itself eschatologically oriented; the removal of sin is the content of the redemptive future. Rom 11:27 — "when I take away their sins" — the new-covenant removal of sin is a future-oriented promise. 1Th 2:16 — "filling up the measure of their sins" — sin accumulates toward a full measure that triggers final judgment.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does sin as universal condition express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Sin as universal condition does not express the divine image positively but reveals its fallen state. Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — the "glory of God" that human beings fall short of is the standard of what the image should reflect; the universal falling-short reveals the image in its failed state. The characteristic is the negative diagnostic of the image's condition: the image was designed to reflect God's glory (the positive); sin as universal condition is the universal failure to do so. Gen 4:7 — sin crouching at the door, desiring to master — shows the image-bearer (Cain) facing a force that seeks to capture the image's ruling capacity; the image-bearer was created to rule (Gen 1:28) but sin seeks to reverse that rule by mastering the image-bearer.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest sin as universal condition is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Exclusively creaturely — and universally so among creatures. Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned" — the "all" encompasses every human being without exception. The contrast with Christ (Heb 4:15 — "without sin"; 2Cor 5:21 — "knew no sin") confirms that the one fully divine-human person did not share the universal condition; his sinlessness is what qualified him to bear what others could not bear (2Cor 5:21 — "made him to be sin who knew no sin").

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the universal presence of sin as condition reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The universal presence of sin as condition reveals that the divine image is comprehensively fallen: Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — the "all" without exception names a total condition affecting every image-bearer without remainder. Rom 7:14 — "sold under sin" — the slave-market metaphor reveals the image's condition as ownership-by-another: the image is held under a master it was not designed to serve. Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — the image in sin-conditioned humanity is not merely damaged but spiritually dead; the image remains (humanity still exists) but its life-capacity before God has been extinguished. The universality of the condition — including the "righteous" (Rom 3:10 context — no one righteous) — means the image cannot correct itself from within; only the divine provision (VCG-02 — forgiveness; VCG-03 redemption) can address it.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use sin as universal condition typologically?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes — in two directions. (1) *Adam as type*: Rom 5:12/14 — "just as sin came into the world through one man... Adam, who was a type of the one who was to come" — Adam's original sin-entry is the negative type that establishes the universal condition; Christ as the antitype reverses what Adam established. The typological use of sin's universal entry (through one man) points toward sin's universal removal (through one man — the last Adam). (2) *Israel's trespass as type*: Rom 11:11/12 — "through their trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles" — Israel's corporate participation in the universal sinful condition becomes the occasion for the redemptive purpose to extend to all nations; the trespass is deployed typologically as the instrument of unexpected global blessing.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Two directions. (1) *Adam → Christ*: human universal sin-condition (Adam's type) → universal sin-removal (Christ as antitype). Rom 5:12 — "sin came into the world through one man" names the type; the broader Rom 5 argument names Christ as the one through whom the reversal comes. The direction is negative type (one man's sinning as the entry-point of universal condition) → positive antitype (one man's righteousness as the entry-point of universal justification). (2) *Israel's trespass → Gentile inclusion*: the corporate trespass (Israel's participation in the universal sin-condition) becomes the occasion for the redemptive purpose to reach all nations — a providential typological deployment of the universal condition's consequences.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Typological use is clearly evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Sin as universal condition." Three signals: (1) *Sin*: the moral-spiritual reality that separates the creature from God — not a single act but the comprehensive inner state. (2) *Universal*: the scope is every human being without exception — Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — and Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned" — and Heb 5:3 — even the high priest must sacrifice for his own sins. The universality distinguishes this characteristic from sin-as-specific-act (CHAR-1) or sin-as-habitual-pattern (CHAR-6): CHAR-11 is the bedrock condition underlying all other sinning characteristics. (3) *Condition*: the characteristic is a structural state rather than a discrete event — "under sin" (Rom 3:9), "dead in trespasses and sins" (Eph 2:1), "sold under sin" (Rom 7:14), "die in your sin" (Joh 8:21). The name signals: every person stands in this state regardless of whether they have committed a specific sinful act, because the condition is ontological — part of their fallen human constitution.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Three term-groups across the corpus. (1) *OT Hebrew terms (VCG-01)*: **rish.ah (H7564)** — wickedness, moral-evil as a defining quality (Isa 9:18; Mal 1:4; 3:15; 4:1) — the root names the condition of being wicked as a settled identity; **al.vah (H5932)** — iniquity as a defining moral characterisation (Hos 10:9) — the unjust as a constituted type. (2) *NT noun*: **hamartia (G0266)** — the dominant term (majority of the 112 verses) — miss the mark, fall short. The noun form (as distinct from the verb) is what VCG-03 deploys to name sin as a power: "sin reigned" (Rom 5:21), "under sin" (Rom 3:9), "sin that dwells within me" (Rom 7:17). The noun form names the condition rather than the act. (3) *NT noun*: **paraptōma (G3900)** — trespass, false step, falling aside (Rom 4:25; 5:16; 11:11/12; Eph 2:1/2:5) — specifically the relational-fall dimension, naming sin as an offense/stumble rather than just a condition. (4) *NT verb*: **hamartanō (G0264)** — to sin (Rom 2:12; 3:23; 5:12/14/16; 1Cor 7:28/36) — used in the universal-condition context to name the completed-act dimension of the universal state.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — All three implications are present at characteristic scope. *Directional*: Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God" — the direction is downward (falling short, not reaching the standard) and inward (the condition is in the person, not merely their acts). *Relational*: the condition is always measured against God — "under sin" (Rom 3:9) is a relational position relative to a power; Joh 8:21 — "die in your sin" where Jesus is the one departing — the relational consequence of dying in sin is separation from where Jesus goes. Heb 5:1 — sin creates the need for priestly mediation between human and divine — the relational distance is the characteristic's relational implication. *Constitutional*: the condition is a structural feature of every human person — Joh 9:34 — "born in utter sin" — the birth-origin framing; Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — a constituted inner state, not an external label.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is sin as universal condition?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The corpus establishes CHAR-11 as a *structural condition* — a state of being — rather than an act, disposition, or quality in isolation. The three VCG registers name it differently: (1) VCG-01 — a *defining inner quality* (rish.ah — wickedness as the person's settled moral character: Mal 1:4 — "the wicked country"); (2) VCG-02 — a *standing moral debt* before God (requiring forgiveness — Mat 9:2; requiring blotting out — Act 3:19; requiring covering — Rom 4:7); (3) VCG-03 — an *enslaving power* (hamartia as a ruling master: Rom 5:21 — "sin reigned"; Rom 6:23 — "sin pays wages"; Rom 7:17 — "sin that dwells within me"). The characteristic thus operates simultaneously as quality (VCG-01), as debt (VCG-02), and as power (VCG-03) — three faces of the same underlying structural condition.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The corpus reveals a multi-dimensional condition with at least four constituent elements. (1) *Universal scope*: every person without exception is under the condition — Rom 3:9/23; Rom 2:12 — Jew and Gentile alike; Heb 5:3 — even the priest. (2) *Death-consequence*: the condition produces death — Rom 5:12 ("death through sin"); Rom 6:23 ("wages of sin is death"); Eph 2:1 ("dead in trespasses and sins"). (3) *Enslavement*: the condition functions as a master relationship — Rom 7:14 ("sold under sin"); Rom 6:16-20 (slavery to sin); Gen 4:7 ("its desire is for you"). (4) *Deficiency from intended glory*: Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God" — the condition is not neutral but a falling below what human existence was designed to be.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Working description: *Sin as universal condition (CHAR-11) is the structural inner-being state in which every human person stands — not primarily an act (CHAR-1) or a habitual pattern (CHAR-6) but the ontological condition of being under sin's dominion, falling short of God's glory, carrying a standing moral debt before God, and subject to death as the condition's consequence. The corpus evidences three registers of this condition: (1) Wickedness as constituted moral character — the settled inner quality of evil as a defining identity (VCG-01 — rish.ah, Mal 1:4; al.vah, Hos 10:9); (2) Sin as moral debt requiring divine forgiveness and removal — the standing obligation that the redemptive acts address (VCG-02 — hamartia in the forgiveness register); (3) Sin as an enslaving, indwelling power that rules over the person and produces death — the master relationship that resists the person's own will to good (VCG-03 — hamartia in the power register, especially Romans 5-8). Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — is the definitional summary: universal scope + structural deficiency from the created end.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of sin as universal condition?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The structural opposite is named across the corpus in three forms matching the three VCG registers. (1) *Against VCG-01 wickedness*: righteousness as a settled identity — the implicit opposite of "the wicked country" (Mal 1:4) is the righteous nation. (2) *Against VCG-02 standing debt*: forgiveness and justification — the removal of the debt condition: Rom 4:7/8 — "lawless deeds are forgiven... the Lord will not count his sin" — the non-counting state is the opposite of the condition of sin counted against the person. (3) *Against VCG-03 sin-power*: the state of being "under grace" rather than "under sin" (Rom 6:14), "freed from sin" (Rom 6:18/22), "dead to sin and alive to God" (Rom 6:11). The fullest opposite statement: Rom 6:22 — "set free from sin and become slaves of God... fruit leading to sanctification... eternal life."

---

**T1.3.2 — What does sin as universal condition explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The condition explicitly excludes: (1) *Justification by works*: Rom 3:20 — "by works of the law no human being will be justified in his sight, since through the law comes knowledge of sin" — the condition is too total for law-works to remedy; (2) *Self-rescue*: Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — the dead person cannot self-revive; (3) *Righteousness*: Rom 6:20 — "when you were slaves of sin, you were free in regard to righteousness" — slavery to sin is a total condition that excludes even the possibility of righteousness from within; (4) *Sanctification and eternal life*: Rom 6:22 — freedom from sin is the precondition for the fruit of sanctification and eternal life; under sin, these are unavailable.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is sin as universal condition not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Four boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-11 is the structural condition; CHAR-1 is the discrete act. Every wilful sinning act (CHAR-1) expresses the universal condition (CHAR-11), but CHAR-11 is not reducible to any specific act. (2) *Not CHAR-12 (Sin as enslaving power)*: while VCG-03 overlaps conceptually with CHAR-12, CHAR-11's defining scope is the universal condition affecting all persons; CHAR-12 addresses the enslaving power as a distinct characteristic. (3) *Not the specific sinful act whose forgiveness VCG-02 announces*: VCG-02 names the forgiveness-remedy, not the specific acts being forgiven; it addresses the condition at the level of its removal. (4) *Not guilt as inner-state*: 1Cor 7:28/36 — "you have not sinned" — uses the term negatively to define what the condition is not in specific cases; these negative uses clarify the characteristic's scope by showing what falls outside it.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does sin as universal condition operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Four distinct inner modes from the corpus. (1) *As indwelling power hijacking agency*: Rom 7:17/20 — "sin that dwells within me" — sin operates as an internal occupying force that produces actions the conscious will does not choose; (2) *As a competing law in the members*: Rom 7:23 — "the law of sin that dwells in my members" — sin operates as a counter-authority within the body, waging war against the mind's law; (3) *As a self-activating force exploiting the commandment*: Rom 7:8/9 — "sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, produced in me all kinds of covetousness" — sin operates by using God's good law as its instrument of arousal; (4) *As a predatory force seeking mastery*: Gen 4:7 — "sin is crouching at the door. Its desire is for you, and you must rule over it" — sin operates as an external-becoming-internal predator seeking to dominate the will.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does sin as universal condition operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes. (1) *Under law (VCG-03 — Romans 7)*: sin exploits the commandment as an opportunity — "sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment" (Rom 7:8) — the law context activates sin's power within the person who has the law; (2) *Without law*: Rom 5:13 — "sin indeed was in the world before the law was given" — the condition exists before law, though it is not legally counted without law; the condition's reality precedes its legal articulation; (3) *In unbelief*: Joh 16:9 — "concerning sin, because they do not believe in me" — the Spirit convicts of the sin that is constituted specifically by unbelief; (4) *In the mortal body*: Rom 6:12 — "let not sin therefore reign in your mortal body, to make you obey its passions" — the mortal body is the site in which the condition seeks to operate through passions.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does sin as universal condition have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Limited speech evidence. The primary mode of CHAR-11 is structural (condition) and power-dynamic (enslaving force) rather than verbal. However: (1) *Speech of confession*: VCG-02 — Mat 3:6; Mar 1:5 — "confessing their sins" at baptism — the condition is publicly acknowledged through verbal confession as part of repentance; (2) *Speech of declaration*: VCG-02 — Jesus's verbal forgiveness declarations (Mat 9:2 — "your sins are forgiven") — the condition is addressed and removed through authoritative verbal declaration; (3) *The Spirit's convicting speech*: Joh 16:8/9 — the Spirit "will convict the world concerning sin" — the condition requires an external verbal-divine agent to make it known to the person who does not self-recognise it.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when the universal sin-condition is encountered?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Different responses across the VCG registers. (1) *VCG-01 response*: the condition of wickedness as identity produces judgment — Mal 4:1 — "shall set them ablaze"; Mal 1:4 — "I will tear down" — the response of God to the condition of settled wickedness is judicial destruction. (2) *VCG-02 response*: the condition encountered in the context of Jesus's ministry produces wonder and resistance — Luk 7:49 — "who is this, who even forgives sins?" — bystanders cannot categorise the one who addresses the condition; Mar 2:7 — "who can forgive sins but God alone?" — the scribes resist the implication of Jesus's authority over the condition. (3) *VCG-03 response*: awareness of the condition under the law produces inner conflict — Rom 7:9 — "when the commandment came, sin came alive and I died" — the inner response is a death-experience as the condition's full weight becomes known.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Variable across VCG registers and contexts. Judgment (VCG-01 — wickedness meets divine response), wonder and resistance (VCG-02 — who can forgive?), and inner death-experience (VCG-03 — the law's arrival activates the condition's lethal force) are all distinct immediate responses. Within VCG-02, even the immediate response to forgiveness-declaration varies: some receive it with faith (Mat 9:2 — the paralytic's faith context), others question Jesus's authority (Mar 2:7), others wonder at his identity (Luk 7:49). The characteristic's universality means it is encountered differently depending on whether the person is under law, in unbelief, or in the context of the gospel's offer.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The inner phenomenological experience of the person who has not yet encountered the good news of the condition's remedy — what it feels like to live under sin's dominion without awareness of the gospel — is not directly described in the corpus. The corpus describes the condition (VCG-01/03), its remedy-announcement (VCG-02), and specific responses to the remedy-announcement, but not the raw subjective experience of living under the condition without reference to its remedy.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does sin as universal condition produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Four sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Death*: Rom 6:23 — "the wages of sin is death" — the condition's sustained operation produces death as its final output; Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — the sustained state is spiritual death. (2) *Hardening*: Heb 3:13 — "hardened by the deceitfulness of sin" — sustained operation of the universal condition through sin's deceitfulness progressively hardens the inner person against correction. (3) *Accumulation to full measure*: 1Th 2:16 — "filling up the measure of their sins" — the condition's sustained operation produces an accumulating offense before God that eventually reaches its full measure and triggers judgment. (4) *Insatiable appetite*: 2Ti 3:6 — "burdened with sins and led astray by various passions" — the condition produces a burdened inner person susceptible to multiple passions.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained sin-condition establish?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — The condition establishes: (1) *Spiritual deadness*: Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — the condition as a settled state of inner spiritual non-life; (2) *Total enslavement*: Rom 6:20 — "when you were slaves of sin, you were free in regard to righteousness" — the condition's total orientation away from righteousness; (3) *The law of sin*: Rom 7:23 — "the law of sin that dwells in my members" — the condition as an established counter-authority within the person; (4) *Separation from the glory of God*: Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God" — the condition as a settled deficiency from the person's intended end.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes. The immediate response to encountering the condition (wonder at Jesus — Luk 7:49; inner death-experience — Rom 7:9; divine judgment against the wicked — Mal 4:1) produces discrete events. The sustained effect is constitutional: the progressive hardening (Heb 3:13), the accumulating measure (1Th 2:16), the established deadness (Eph 2:1), the total enslavement (Rom 6:20) — these are the long-term constitutional outcomes of the condition's sustained operation. The immediate response is event-like; the sustained effect is structural.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable the universal sin-condition?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — By definition, no inner enabling condition is required — the condition is universal and structural, not conditional: Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — no pre-qualifying condition is named. However, two enabling pathways through which the condition becomes active are evidenced. (1) *Unbelief*: Joh 16:9 — "concerning sin, because they do not believe in me" — unbelief is the specific activating condition for the form of sin that the Spirit convicts of; (2) *The law*: Rom 7:8/9 — "when the commandment came, sin came alive" — the law's arrival activates the condition's full force within the one who has the law; Joh 15:22/24 — Jesus's coming and speaking removes excuse, making the condition's accountability operative.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent the universal sin-condition?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Nothing within the fallen person can block the universal condition — it is by definition universal: Heb 4:15 — "tempted as we are, yet without sin" — Christ's sinlessness is the single exception and it is rooted in his divine nature, not in human achievement. Within the condition's ongoing operation, what blocks its dominion is: (1) *Forgiveness received* (VCG-02): the condition's debt-dimension is addressed by divine forgiveness — "your sins are forgiven" (Mat 9:2); (2) *Death to sin through Christ*: Rom 6:11 — "consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus" — the condition's enslaving power is blocked within the believer by their co-death with Christ; (3) *The Spirit's law*: Rom 8:2 — "the law of the Spirit of life has set you free... from the law of sin and death" — the Spirit's operation blocks the law of sin.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who has been freed from the universal condition's dominion?**

**[CHAR-11]** E — Rom 6:22 — "you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the fruit you get leads to sanctification and its end, eternal life" — the freed person is: no longer under sin's dominion, now under God's authority (slave of God), producing the fruit of sanctification, and oriented toward eternal life. Rom 6:18 — "having been set free from sin, have become slaves of righteousness" — the freed person's inner allegiance has been relocated from sin to righteousness. The inner state is not the absence of sin's presence but the absence of sin's dominion: Rom 6:11 — "dead to sin and alive to God" — the condition persists as a reality to reckon oneself dead to, not as a master with living authority over the person.

---
