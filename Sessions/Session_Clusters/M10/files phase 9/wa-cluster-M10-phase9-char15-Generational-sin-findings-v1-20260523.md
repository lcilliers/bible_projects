## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The 8-verse corpus reveals God's character across three dimensions. (1) *God as the one who holds generations accountable to the ancestral record*: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — God addresses present Israel through the lens of ancestral failure; the present generation is addressed as the inheritors of what the first father began. (2) *God as the one who responds to prolonged corporate sin with judgment*: Jer 8:14 — "the Lord our God has doomed us to perish... because we have sinned against the Lord" — the corporate sinning of the present generation in continuity with the past produces divine doom; (3) *God as the redeemer who saves from ancestral backsliding*: Eze 37:23 — "I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — God's redemptive purpose addresses the inherited sinning pattern from outside it.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute generational sin to God?**

**[CHAR-15]** S — No verse in the 8-verse corpus attributes the inherited sin-condition of generational sin to God. God appears as the one who addresses the condition (Isa 43:27 — naming the ancestral origin; Eze 37:23 — promising redemption from the backslidings) but not as the source or bearer of the condition. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The silence on God possessing the generational sin condition confirms its entirely fallen-creaturely nature: generational sin is the moral weight that accumulates through creaturely failure across generations. God cannot inherit sin because he has no predecessors whose sinning he would receive. Isa 43:27's structure — "your first father sinned" — presupposes a beginning-point of sinning in the first human ancestor, which God by definition does not share. The silence reveals that generational sin is the specific form of moral inheritance that belongs to finite creatures embedded in lineages, families, and nations — not to the eternal God who stands outside such transmission chains.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose generational sin serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-15]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for generational sin. The characteristic is presented entirely as a fallen-condition burden: Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the prolonged state of sin is experienced as a problem requiring divine resolution, not as a serving-a-purpose feature. Eze 37:23's divine redemption-from-backsliding confirms the condition has no created-design function.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether generational sin is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the origin is in the first father's sinning, which is the entrance of sin into the lineage; the mechanism requires a prior sinning-origin before transmission is possible. The creation-design had no first father sinning; the generational sin-condition begins with that sinning and propagates from it. The 8-verse corpus describes the pattern's sustained operation (Psa 78:17/32 — continuing to sin in the wilderness) and corporate confession of it (1Ki 8:47) — all post-fall dynamics.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that generational sin is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — toward two eschatological endpoints. (1) *Positive — divine cleansing*: Eze 37:23 — "I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — the eschatological fullness of the covenant is the cleansing of the generationally accumulated sinning and the restored God-people relationship; (2) *Negative — curse*: Isa 65:20 — "the sinner a hundred years old shall be accursed" — the sinner whose characteristic is defined by sin faces eschatological curse even within the new-creation context of the surrounding prophecy.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does generational sin express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Generational sin reveals the divine image through its *transmission capacity*: only image-bearers who stand in lineages and covenant communities can transmit a morally weighted sinning-history to their descendants. The image was designed for community and continuity (the creation mandate involves generations — "be fruitful and multiply"); generational sin is the fallen-condition distortion of this design: what should transmit blessing, calling, and covenant transmits instead the sin-pattern and its moral weight. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — presupposes the image-bearer's capacity for a sinning act that can become the starting-point of a generational chain.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest generational sin is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-15]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God has no ancestors whose sinning he could inherit; the transmission mechanism (lineage, family, national belonging) presupposes finite creatures embedded in historical succession. The characteristic is inapplicable to the divine nature.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of generational sin reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The presence of generational sin reveals that the image-bearer's moral condition is not individually self-contained but socially and historically embedded: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the present image-bearer carries a moral liability whose origin precedes their individual existence. The image is thus not merely individually fallen (through personal sinning acts) but corporately and historically fallen through the accumulated sinning of predecessors. Psa 78:17/32 — the wilderness generation's persistent sinning despite wonders reveals an image whose sinning patterns are formed and reinforced across communal experience; the generational dimension deepens the image's fallen condition beyond what individual sinning alone would produce.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use generational sin typologically?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — through Isa 43:27's structure. "Your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the naming of the "first father" as the origin-point of Israel's sinning functions as the typological ground: the ancestral sinning is the type that the present generation's condition fulfils. The first father's sin becomes the pattern that the mediators continued and the present nation inherits — a typological escalation-chain where the ancestral type is expanded and confirmed in each subsequent generation.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The direction runs from ancestral origin to present condition: the first father's sin (Isa 43:27) is the originating type whose consequences are now the present reality. The typological movement is temporal — from the founding sinning act forward through generations to the present corporate condition. The type (first father's sin) explains the current condition (Israel's moral liability before God). Eze 37:23's reversal — divine cleansing of the backslidings — points toward the antitype: the eschatological cleansing that breaks the generational chain.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Typological use is evidenced in Isa 43:27 (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Generational sin." Two signals. (1) *Generational*: the defining mark is the transmission-across-generations dimension — the sin-condition is not merely the individual's own accumulated acts but enters through lineage, family, or national belonging; Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the present generation is addressed through the ancestral chain. (2) *Sin*: the transmitted condition is specifically a moral condition — guilt or moral liability, not merely a cultural or behavioural tendency. The name signals: a sin-condition whose origin and weight extends beyond the individual to encompass ancestors and the communities that shaped them.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The single primary term across all 8 verses is **cha.ta (H2398)** — the verb "to sin, to miss the mark, to incur guilt." The verb form throughout this corpus is significant: generational sin is evidenced through the *act of sinning* as an inherited pattern, not through a distinct noun for the inherited condition itself. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" (*cha.ta*) — the past-tense verb names the ancestral sinning act that initiated the generational chain; Psa 78:17 — "they sinned still more" — the repeated sinning reinforces the pattern. No specialised vocabulary for the inherited-condition dimension exists in this 8-verse corpus; the inherited condition is described through the repeated sinning-verb applied to successive generations.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — All three. *Directional*: the condition moves backward (from ancestors to present) and forward (from present to descendants); Isa 43:27's "first father sinned" is the backward direction; the ongoing pattern (Psa 78:17/32 — still sinning) is the forward continuation. *Relational*: the condition is constitutively relational — it operates within lineages, families, and covenant communities; it cannot be inherited without the relational bond of ancestry. *Constitutional*: Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the prolonged duration suggests the condition has become constitutional, not merely episodic.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is generational sin?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The corpus reveals a *corporate moral condition sustained across generations* — not primarily a discrete act but an embedded sinning-pattern that communities carry. Psa 78:17/32 — the wilderness generation sinned persistently in a pattern; Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the prolonged state confirms this is a condition, not a momentary act. However, the corpus also reveals individual participation in the corporate condition: 1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned" — each member of the exiled community confesses within the corporate "we." The characteristic is thus a *corporate-condition in which individuals participate* rather than a purely individual condition.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The corpus reveals two constituent elements. (1) *The originating ancestral sinning act*: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — there is a founding sinful act from which the generational condition begins; (2) *The sustained corporate sinning pattern*: Psa 78:17/32; Isa 64:5 — successive generations continue in the sinning-pattern, deepening and extending the condition. The two elements are distinct: the origin (first father's act) and the continuation (each generation's own sinning within the inherited pattern) together constitute the full characteristic.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Working description: *Generational sin (CHAR-15) is the corporate moral condition that originates in an ancestral sinning act (Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned") and is sustained across generations through the ongoing sinning of successive communities who inherit and continue the pattern. The inner person carries a moral liability not only for their own acts but for the accumulated sinning history of those before them. The corpus (8 verses, all cha.ta — VCG-01) evidences three dimensions: (1) The originating ancestral act (Isa 43:27); (2) The persistent corporate sinning pattern sustained across generations (Psa 78:17/32; Isa 64:5 — "a long time in our sins"); (3) The corporate confession that names the condition before God (1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned"; Jer 8:14 — "we have sinned against the Lord"). God's promise to cleanse the accumulated backsliding (Eze 37:23) names the eschatological resolution.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of generational sin?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The structural opposite is *generational faithfulness* — a lineage characterised by covenant-keeping rather than sinning. Eze 37:23's positive — "I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — names the restored covenant-community as the opposite condition: cleansed from the accumulated pattern, restored to God-people relationship. The opposite of the "long time in our sins" (Isa 64:5) is the community that remembers God's ways and works righteousness (Isa 64:5 — "you meet him who joyfully works righteousness, those who remember you in your ways" — the positive foil stated in the same verse).

---

**T1.3.2 — What does generational sin explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Belief in God's wonders*: Psa 78:32 — "they still sinned; despite his wonders, they did not believe" — the persistent sinning is rooted in unbelief; belief in God's demonstrated power is what the sinning-pattern resists; (2) *Response to God's provision*: Psa 78:17 — "sinned still more against him, rebelling against the Most High in the desert" — the rebellion is against God's own desert provision; the sinning-pattern resists gratitude and appropriate response; (3) *Learning from the ancestral warning*: Isa 43:27 — the first father's sinning named as Israel's starting-point implies the warning that the pattern carries and which each generation resists receiving.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is generational sin not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the discrete individual wilful act; CHAR-15 is the inherited corporate condition within which individual sinning acts occur. Individual wilful sinning (CHAR-1) contributes to and expresses the generational condition (CHAR-15) but is not identical with it. (2) *Not CHAR-11 (Sin as universal condition)*: CHAR-11 is the structural ontological condition all persons share; CHAR-15 is the specific lineage-transmission mechanism. CHAR-11 is broader; CHAR-15 is a specific mode of entry into the condition. (3) *Not CHAR-13 (Sin as divine record)*: CHAR-13 is the accumulated moral account before God; CHAR-15 is the specific generational mechanism through which some of that account is inherited rather than personally accumulated.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does generational sin operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Three inner modes evidenced. (1) *As an inherited unbelief disposition*: Psa 78:32 — "despite his wonders, they did not believe" — the sinning pattern is rooted in an inherited capacity for unbelief that does not yield to experienced divine power; (2) *As a prolonged inner condition*: Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the condition is not episodic but characterises the inner-being state over an extended time; (3) *As a pattern recognised and confessed corporately*: 1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly" — the corporate inner recognition of the generational pattern is the conscience's response.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does generational sin operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes. (1) *Wilderness context* (Psa 78:17/32): the generational pattern operates through continued rebellion despite miraculous provision — God's visible acts are the context in which the inherited sinning persists; (2) *Exile context* (1Ki 8:47; Jer 8:14): the generational pattern has produced the condition of captivity; the confession occurs in exile, where the corporate consequences of the accumulated sin are most visible; (3) *Prophetic-covenantal context* (Isa 43:27; Isa 64:5): the pattern is named in its originating dimension (first father) and its prolonged-duration dimension ("a long time"), allowing both the origin and the extent to be addressed.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does generational sin have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — the corporate confession is the speech-mode. 1Ki 8:47 — "saying, 'We have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly'" — the speech-act of corporate confession is the verbal form through which the generational pattern is acknowledged; Jer 8:14 — "because we have sinned against the Lord" — the corporate speech-act of naming the sin before God and the community. The corporate confessional "we" is the characteristic's distinctive communicative form: it is not individual private confession but communal speech that acknowledges the shared inherited condition.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when generational sin is encountered?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Corporate confession. 1Ki 8:47 — "they turn their heart in the land... and repent and plead with you... saying, 'We have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly'" — the immediate inner response to encountering the generational condition in exile is heart-turning, repentance, and verbal confession. Jer 8:14 — "we have sinned against the Lord" — the corporate recognition produces a speech-act of acknowledgment. The most immediate response is thus the collective recognition and naming of the condition before God.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Variable. 1Ki 8:47 and Jer 8:14 both show corporate confession as the immediate response in the exile-context. But Psa 78:17/32 shows the opposite: the immediate response to God's continued provision is *more sinning and unbelief*, not confession. The response thus depends on the moment in the generational cycle: in exile (where consequences are obvious), confession is the immediate response; in desert-provision (where consequences are not yet visible), persistent sinning is the immediate response.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-15]** E — For the prophetic passages (Isa 43:27; Isa 64:5; Isa 65:20; Eze 37:23), the immediate inner response of the person who hears the naming of the generational pattern is not directly described. The naming of the first father's sin (Isa 43:27) and the prolonged-sin condition (Isa 64:5) describe the condition without specifying the hearer's immediate inner response.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does generational sin produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Three sustained inner effects. (1) *Prolonged condition of sinning*: Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the sustained inner state is one of persistent sin-conditionedness over an extended period; (2) *Unbelief resistant to evidence*: Psa 78:32 — "in spite of all this, they still sinned; despite his wonders, they did not believe" — sustained generational sinning produces a hardened unbelief that does not yield to divine demonstration; (3) *Corporate doom*: Jer 8:14 — "the Lord our God has doomed us to perish... because we have sinned" — the sustained sinning produces an inner sense of hopelessness and doom within the corporate community.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained generational sin establish?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Three established states. (1) *Prolonged sin-conditionedness*: Isa 64:5 — "a long time in our sins" — the settled inner state of being-in-sin across generations; (2) *Rebellion-oriented inner disposition*: Psa 78:17 — "rebelling against the Most High" — the sustained sinning establishes a rebellious inner orientation toward God rather than trust; (3) *Defilement*: Eze 37:23 — "they shall not defile themselves anymore" — the sustained backslidings have produced a state of inner defilement that requires divine cleansing before it can be reversed.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes. The immediate response (corporate confession in exile — 1Ki 8:47; Jer 8:14) is an episodic event. The sustained effects (prolonged sinning — Isa 64:5; hardened unbelief — Psa 78:32; defilement — Eze 37:23) are constitutional conditions that develop over the entire generational span. The immediate confession (acute) gives way to — or coexists with — the sustained condition that requires divine redemption to address (Eze 37:23).

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable the reception of generational sin's moral liability?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Corporate-community belonging*: membership in the lineage, family, or nation is the enabling condition for receiving the generational liability; Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the "your" addresses those who stand in the lineage of the first father; one cannot inherit what one is not lineally connected to; (2) *Inherited unbelief disposition*: Psa 78:32 — "they did not believe" — the unbelief that drives the generational pattern is itself enabled by the community's sustained failure to receive God's demonstrated faithfulness as a reason for trust.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or resist the generational sin's hold?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The opposite of the generational pattern's unbelief: Isa 64:5 — "you meet him who joyfully works righteousness, those who remember you in your ways" — joyful righteousness-working and remembering God's ways are the inner conditions that resist the generational sinning pattern. These are the alternative inner orientations that break the cycle within a generation. Eze 37:23 — God's cleansing and saving — names the divine intervention that is ultimately what blocks the pattern when human inner conditions alone cannot.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who recognises the generational pattern but does not receive its liability?**

**[CHAR-15]** S — The corpus does not describe the inner-being state of the individual within the community who recognises the generational sin-pattern but stands apart from it. Isa 43:27 names the first father's sin; Eze 37:23 names the promise to cleanse; neither describes the inner experience of the individual who breaks the pattern within their generation.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The primary dimension is **corporate moral identity** — the person's constitutive belonging to a community whose accumulated sinning history shapes their inner moral condition. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the present-tense "your" makes the ancestral sinning part of the current person's moral identity through their lineage. 1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned" — the "we" is not merely a grammatical convenience but names the corporate moral subject as the primary dimension.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — (1) Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the ancestral sin is addressed to the present community as their inherited condition; (2) 1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned" — the corporate "we" is the locus of the characteristic; (3) Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the possessive "our" names the shared corporate condition; (4) Jer 8:14 — "we have sinned against the Lord" — the corporate acknowledgment. All uses are corporate rather than individually-bounded.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does generational sin carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Two secondary dimensions. (1) *Temporal/historical dimension*: the condition extends across time — "a long time in our sins" (Isa 64:5); the temporal reach backward (to the first father) and forward (to the present and potentially future generations) is a secondary dimension that distinguishes CHAR-15 from merely corporate sin; (2) *Will/unbelief dimension*: Psa 78:32 — "they did not believe" — the unbelief that drives the sustained sinning is a secondary will-level dimension; the generational pattern is not merely structural but is sustained by each generation's own volitional unbelief.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is generational sin explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — through the defilement and cleansing language. Eze 37:23 — "they shall not defile themselves anymore with their idols and their detestable things, or with any of their transgressions. But I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them" — the defilement and cleansing operate at the spirit level: idolatry defiles the inner person's spirit-orientation toward God; the divine cleansing restores it. The "shall be my people, and I will be their God" restoration names the spirit-level relational restoration that follows cleansing.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does generational sin originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Partially. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the originating act of the first father is a will-level sinning act rather than exclusively a spirit-level phenomenon. However, Psa 78:32 — "despite his wonders, they did not believe" — the unbelief that drives the persistent sinning is a spirit-level failure: the failure to trust God's power and faithfulness at the deepest level of the person's God-orientation. The generational pattern thus has a spirit-level dimension in its sustaining mechanism (unbelief) even if its origin is in a specific volitional act.

---

**T2.1.3 — What does spirit-level location reveal?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Spirit-level location (Eze 37:23 — defilement through idolatry; unbelief — Psa 78:32) reveals that generational sin operates at the deepest constitutional level of the community's God-relationship: the idolatry that defiles (Eze 37:23) is a spirit-level misorientation of the community's allegiance from God toward idols; the unbelief (Psa 78:32) is the spirit-level failure of trust that perpetuates the sinning across generations. The divine cleansing required (Eze 37:23) is therefore spirit-level restoration, not merely behavioural correction.

---

**T2.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Spirit-level location (defilement — Eze 37:23; unbelief — Psa 78:32) is evidenced. This prompt is superseded.

---

**T2.10.1 — Does generational sin move across constitutional levels?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes. The characteristic moves from inner to outer and from outer-consequence back to inner. (1) *Inner-to-outer*: the ancestral sinning acts (Isa 43:27 — inner volitional acts) produce outer national consequences (Jer 8:14 — "doomed us to perish... has given us poisoned water to drink"); the inner sinning generates the outer catastrophic condition; (2) *Outer-consequence to inner-recognition*: the outer exile-condition (1Ki 8:47 — "the land to which they have been carried captive") produces the inner heart-turning and confession ("turn their heart... repent and plead"); the outer consequence drives the inner recognition.

---

**T2.10.2 — What is the sequence of that movement?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — The movement sequence evidenced in 1Ki 8:47 and Jer 8:14: (1) ancestral sinning acts (inner) → accumulated moral condition across generations → outer covenant-judgment (exile, doom, poisoned water); (2) outer consequence (captivity, doom) → inner recognition and heart-turning → corporate confession ("we have sinned"). The sequence is: inner-sinning-acts → outer-judgment-consequences → inner-repentance-recognition → (potential) divine-restoration. The outer catastrophe serves as the mechanism that drives the inner sequence from acknowledgment to potential restoration.

---

**T2.10.3 — If no constitutional movement is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Constitutional movement (inner → outer → inner) is evidenced (T2.10.1 and T2.10.2). This prompt is superseded.

---

**T2.2.1 — Is generational sin identified as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes. 1Ki 8:47 — "if they turn their heart in the land to which they have been carried captive, and repent and plead with you" — the heart-turning is a soul-level act; the repentance is the will's movement; the pleading is the whole-person's appeal to God. The corporate "we" of the confession (1Ki 8:47; Jer 8:14) names each member of the community as a soul-level moral subject participating in the corporate condition.

---

**T2.2.2 — What does soul-level location reveal?**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Soul-level location (1Ki 8:47 — heart-turning; Jer 8:14 — "we have sinned") reveals that generational sin, though corporate in origin, engages the individual soul-level moral subject: each person who confesses "we have sinned" participates personally in the corporate condition's acknowledgment. The characteristic is not merely an external legal-status imputed from outside but is genuinely carried at the soul level of each community member who stands within the lineage.

---

**T2.2.3 — If the evidence is silent on soul-level location, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-15]** E — Soul-level location is evidenced (1Ki 8:47; Jer 8:14). This prompt is superseded.

---
