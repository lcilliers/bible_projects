## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession reveals God's character in two directions. (1) *God as the one to whom confession is rightly directed*: throughout the corpus, genuine confession is oriented toward God as the primary offended party — "I have sinned against the Lord" (1Sa 7:6; Jer 14:20; Dan 9:11; Luk 15:18/21). God's moral authority as the standard against which sin is measured makes him the proper recipient of confession — which reveals him as *holy and morally sovereign*, the reference point of all moral reality. (2) *God as the one who cancels sin upon genuine confession*: Eze 33:16 — "None of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him" — reveals God as a God who holds a moral record but is prepared to erase it when the person turns. The characteristic exists in a landscape where confession is heard and responded to by a God who both takes sin seriously and provides for its cancellation.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The corpus contains no attribution of confession to God. God does not confess sin because God does not sin. The entire 34-verse corpus is structured around human persons (individual and corporate) confessing before God — with God as the consistent recipient, never the confessor. The absence is constitutively significant: confession is exclusively a creaturely act, presupposing both the capacity to sin (which the creature has and God does not) and the relational standing before a holy God that requires acknowledgment of sin.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The structural silence on God confessing (T0.1.2) reveals that confession belongs to the *fallen creature's response to God* rather than to the original divine image. It is not a capacity the creature exercises because it shares it with God, but a capacity the creature requires because it has sinned against God. The corpus does evidence that confession is *divinely designed for* — God hears it (Neh 1:6 — Nehemiah prays "day and night" and God's ear is invoked as "attentive"), provides for it (Eze 33:16 — sins cancelled upon turning), and responds to it (1Sa 7:6 — corporate confession at Mizpah precedes Samuel's judgeship and renewal). The silence on divine confession and the presence of divine response together suggest that confession expresses the image not by sharing a divine quality but by enabling the image-bearer's restoration toward the God in whose image they were made.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose confession serves in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences confession serving three distinct purposes. (1) *Relational restoration*: Luk 15:18/21 — the prodigal's planned and enacted confession is explicitly oriented toward restoring the father-son relationship: "I will arise and go to my father." 1Sa 7:6 — corporate confession at Mizpah marks the people's turning back toward God and precedes Samuel's renewal of the community. (2) *Removal of guilt's weight*: Dan 9:5 — Daniel's multi-layered confession ("we have sinned and done wrong and acted wickedly and rebelled") is a comprehensive naming of what has been done, providing the inner person with clarity about what they carry. 2Sa 24:10 — David's confession "please take away the iniquity of your servant" shows confession as the instrument of guilt-removal. (3) *Alignment of inner reality with moral truth*: Num 21:7 — "we have sinned, for we have spoken against the Lord and against you" — the confession names the inner rebellious attitude as the root of the wrongdoing. Confession equips the person to be a morally honest creature before God and to seek restored relationship.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether confession is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence points clearly to confession as a *response to the fallen condition* — it presupposes sin and exists to address its relational and moral consequences. No verse in the corpus presents confession as a pre-fall capacity or as part of what the unfallen creature was designed to express. The entire 34-verse corpus is structured around sin already committed: Exo 9:27 ("this time I have sinned"), 2Sa 24:10 ("I have sinned greatly in what I have done"), Dan 9:5 ("we have sinned"). The characteristic is responsive rather than original — it is what the image-bearer needs to be equipped for because of the fallen condition, not what they were created to express in an unfallen state.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that confession is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Limited forward-orientation evidence. Eze 33:16 — "None of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him. He has done what is just and right; he shall surely live" — points beyond confession to a future state of living in which past sins no longer define the person. The promised future is one in which the record of sin is abolished. Mic 7:9 — "until he pleads my cause and executes judgment for me. He will bring me out to the light; I shall look upon his vindication" — Micah's confession of sin is accompanied by confident eschatological expectation: enduring divine indignation now, looking to vindication ahead. The confessional mode is not the final state but the posture that positions the person toward a promised future of light and vindication.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does confession express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession expresses the divine image through the capacity for *moral self-knowledge and honest self-naming*. The image-bearer who confesses demonstrates: (1) *Moral cognition*: the ability to assess their own act against a moral standard and name it correctly as sin (2Sa 24:10 — "I have sinned greatly"; Dan 9:5 — the full cascade of named failures). (2) *Relational accountability*: the capacity to stand before God and speak truthfully about what was done — "Against you, you only, have I sinned" (Psa 51:4, the paradigmatic confession-verse contextually). (3) *Moral integrity of the will*: the willingness to own what was done rather than deny or displace it (1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned... do not let the plague be on your people"). The image is expressed not because God confesses but because God's image-bearer is made for moral transparency — honesty about the self before God.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — Confession is exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). No divine analogue is evidenced. The characteristic has no shared dimension with the divine nature — it is grounded entirely in the creature's sinfulness and the relational structure of the creature's accountability before God.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of confession reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus provides evidence at both ends. (1) *Presence of genuine confession*: signals an image functioning with active moral conscience, honest self-knowledge, and relational accountability before God. David's conscience-struck confession (2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him") before any external word of judgment represents the image at near-full functioning in this mode: conscience registers sin, will names it, mouth speaks it to God, plea for mercy follows. Luk 15:21 (the prodigal's enacted confession: "I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son") shows total self-knowledge and relational honesty. (2) *Hollow or externally-coerced confession* (VCG-03: Deu 1:41; VCG-01: Exo 9:27/10:16 — Pharaoh; 1Sa 15:30 — Saul): the verbal form is present but the will does not follow. These cases reveal an image that can produce the *form* of confession without the *substance* — the moral self-knowledge is present (they know what they did) but the will has not turned. The image is partially functioning: cognition and speech operate but the volitional transformation is absent.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use confession typologically?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, in two directions. (1) *The corporate confessional sequence as typological pattern*: the Judges-Samuel pattern (Judg 10:10/15; 1Sa 7:6; 12:10) — confession leading to divine intervention and leadership renewal — is a recurring pattern that typologically prefigures the deeper restoration in which the new covenant provides a once-for-all resolution of what corporate confession could only address episodically. (2) *Daniel's prayer (Dan 9:5/8/11/15)* functions typologically: a righteous man praying on behalf of a sinful community, including himself in the confession, points forward to the intercessory representative who identifies with the sinners he confesses for. The sustained, comprehensive character of Daniel's confession — multiple specific dimensions named — anticipates the total coverage of sin that the new covenant promises.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The direction is *human instance pointing forward to divine resolution*. The human confessional acts (Judges, Samuel, Daniel) establish the pattern and the need — repeated corporate confession that must be renewed repeatedly reveals the inadequacy of episodic confession to resolve the deep root of sin. The forward pointing is toward a covenant arrangement in which the sin that requires confession is comprehensively addressed rather than repeatedly named. Eze 33:16 ("None of the sins shall be remembered against him") points toward a state of total non-remembrance that the episodic confessional system could not achieve permanently — the type is the human confession; the antitype is the divine forgetting of sin in its totality.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Confession." The name signals three things. (1) *Speech-act*: confession is inherently verbal — it is a naming, a speaking, an act that moves from inner to outer through language. (2) *Past-reference*: confession names what has *already been done* — it is retrospective, not prospective. Every instance in the corpus names a committed sin: "this time I have sinned" (Exo 9:27), "I have sinned greatly in what I have done" (2Sa 24:10), "I have sinned by betraying innocent blood" (Mat 27:4). (3) *Relational direction*: confession is always addressed to someone — God (Luk 15:18 — "against heaven and before you"), persons (Exo 10:16 — "against the Lord your God, and against you"), or a public audience (Job 33:27 — "he sings before men"). The name "Confession" thus encodes: a speech-act directed at a relational recipient, naming a past wrong as wrong.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398)** in its perfect-tense form: "cha.ta.ti" — "I have sinned." This is the same root as CHAR-1 and CHAR-2, but in CHAR-3 the characteristic is not the sinning itself but the *naming of the sinning* — the confessional form. The definitive grammatical marker is the first-person perfect (or its corporate equivalent "sinned we/our fathers"): the act is complete, and the confession names it as such. The NT primary term is **hamartanō (G0264)** in the aorist/perfect confession form: Mat 27:4 ("I have sinned"), Luk 15:18/21 ("I have sinned against heaven and before you"). At the definitional level, both terms reveal that confession deploys the sinning-vocabulary in the self-application mode — not describing someone else's sin but naming one's own.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: confession moves from inner (the conscientious recognition) to outer (the spoken word) — 2Sa 24:10 shows the sequence: "David's heart struck him" (inner) → "David said to the Lord" (outer direction, God-ward). *Relational*: every confession is addressed to a party: God (VCG-01 predominant), persons (Exo 10:16 — "against you"), or a public community (VCG-02 corporate confessions). The relational direction is constitutive — confession is not self-addressed monologue but a speech-act directed at a relational other. *Constitutional*: confession engages the conscience (the inner witness), the will (which must agree to name the act as wrong), and the mouth (through which the naming is enacted). The constitutional depth runs from conscience to will to speech.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does confession appear to be?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession is a *speech-act that enacts an inner orientation* — it is simultaneously an outer verbal event and an inner moral event. The verbal act (speaking "I have sinned") is not confession without the inner movement of conscience and will that it expresses; the inner movement is not complete confession without the verbal enactment. This dual nature is evidenced in the contrast between genuine and hollow confession: Deu 1:41 (VCG-03) — the words "we have sinned" are spoken, but the will immediately proceeds on its own — showing that the verbal act without inner transformation is definitionally hollow. Conversely, 2Sa 24:10 shows the inner conscience-movement ("heart struck him") as the primary event that the verbal confession ("I have sinned greatly") completes. Confession is thus a *conscience-to-speech event* — an inner recognition externalised through deliberate verbal naming.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The VCG structure reveals three constituent elements at first encounter. (1) *Individual first-person confession* (VCG-01, 23V): the personal conscience naming personal sin — from the single-sentence formula ("I have sinned," Exo 9:27) to the extended multi-dimensional Daniel confession (Dan 9:5/8/11/15). (2) *Corporate confession* (VCG-02, 10V): the community naming shared sin — "We have sinned" (Judg 10:10; 1Sa 7:6; Psa 106:6). (3) *Hollow acknowledgment* (VCG-03, 1V): the verbal form present without inner transformation (Deu 1:41). The three VCGs reveal that confession is not a simple uniform act but a spectrum: from heart-generated genuine individual naming (VCG-01 ideal: 2Sa 24:10), through communal corporate ownership (VCG-02), to the degenerate form where the words are spoken but the will does not follow (VCG-03).

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Working description: *Confession is the conscience-to-speech event in which the inner person names a committed sin as sin — directed toward God, persons, or community, and expressing a genuine or partial inner recognition of moral wrong. It is constitutively a speech-act rooted in conscience: conscience identifies the wrong (2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him"), the will agrees to name it (1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned"), and the mouth speaks it (Luk 15:21 — "I have sinned against heaven and before you"). Genuine confession is followed by a plea for forgiveness, a reorientation of the will, or both (Num 21:7 — confession + intercession request; 2Sa 24:10 — confession + "please take away the iniquity"). Hollow confession (VCG-03) produces the verbal form without this inner reorientation (Deu 1:41).*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of confession?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The structural opposite is *silence in the face of recognised sin* — the person who knows they have sinned but does not name it. The corpus evidences this opposite through contrast: 2Sa 19:20 (Shimei — "your servant knows that I have sinned... I have come... the first") — the one who comes forward stands in contrast to those who stay silent. The corpus does not explicitly name the opposite within its own 34 verses, but CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression) is the adjacent characteristic that names what happens when the conscience registers sin and is refused rather than voiced. The structural opposite is: sin recognised, conscience engaged, but the speaking withheld — the un-confessing person before a known sin.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does confession explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession explicitly excludes and resists three things. (1) *Denial*: confession names the act as sin, which excludes the alternative of denying it occurred or denying it was wrong. The hollow-confession verse (Deu 1:41) shows denial's proximate alternative — not denying the sin verbally but denying it in action by proceeding autonomously. (2) *Self-justification*: the corpus's genuine confessions are notable for their absence of excuse — 1Ch 21:17 ("It is I who have sinned and done great evil. But these sheep, what have they done?") is self-accusatory without self-justification; Luk 15:21 ("I am no longer worthy to be called your son") accepts the full moral consequence without appealing to mitigating circumstances. (3) *Displacement*: Num 12:11 (Aaron — "we have done foolishly and have sinned") and Num 21:7 both own the community's sin rather than displacing it onto others.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is confession not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Three clear boundaries. (1) *Not repentance itself*: confession is the naming of sin; repentance (CHAR-5's opposite) is the turning of the will away from it. They are contiguous: Num 21:7 shows confession followed by intercession request (the turning); Deu 1:41 (VCG-03) shows confession without the turning — the hollow form. Confession can occur without repentance, which is what makes VCG-03's hollow acknowledgment analytically possible. (2) *Not forgiveness*: Eze 33:16 — the cancellation of sin — is the adjacent characteristic (CHAR-14) that follows confession; the verse evidences what God does in response to confession, not confession itself. (3) *Not mere recognition of guilt*: CHAR-17 (Guilt as inner-being state) is the inner condition that precedes or accompanies confession; confession is the externalisation of that recognition in speech. Guilt is the inner state; confession is its voiced expression.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does confession operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Three distinct modes. (1) *Conscience-initiated, spontaneous confession*: 2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him" — conscience moves inward before any external prompt, and confession follows. This is confession in its purest inner-being form: the moral witness within the person activates unprompted and drives the speech-act. (2) *Externally-prompted confession*: Exo 9:27/10:16 (Pharaoh — confession under plague judgment), Num 21:7 (people — confession under serpent judgment), 1Sa 26:21 (Saul — confession after David's mercy). These operate through external events triggering recognition and naming. (3) *Sustained, extended intercession-confession*: Neh 1:6 ("confessing the sins... day and night"), Dan 9:5/8/11/15 — sustained comprehensive naming across multiple dimensions. This mode is not a single moment but a prolonged practice of naming before God. The three modes differ in origin (inner/external) and duration (single moment/sustained) but share the naming structure.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does confession operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. *By context*: individual confession (VCG-01) operates at the personal conscience level and produces personal plea (2Sa 24:10 — "please take away the iniquity of your servant"); corporate confession (VCG-02) operates at community level and produces collective appeal (1Sa 7:6 — corporate fasting + confession precedes Samuel's mediation). *By direction*: God-directed confession (predominant in VCG-01: "I have sinned against the Lord," Dan 9:11) and person-directed acknowledgment (2Sa 19:20 — Shimei confessing before the returning king; Mat 27:4 — Judas confessing to the priests). *By depth*: Daniel's extended multi-dimensional confession (Dan 9:5 — "sinned and done wrong and acted wickedly and rebelled") represents constitutional-depth engagement; Pharaoh's single-sentence formula (Exo 9:27) represents surface engagement. The constitutional depth of the confession correlates with the genuineness of the inner-being transformation it produces.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does confession have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession is constitutively speech-based: the characteristic is defined by the verbal naming act. The corpus evidences three specific speech-modes. (1) *Direct verbal declaration*: "I have sinned" / "We have sinned" — the formulaic spoken confession that names the act (Exo 9:27; Judg 10:10; Dan 9:5; Luk 15:21). (2) *Written/prayed confession*: Neh 1:6 — "confessing the sins of the people of Israel... that I now pray before you day and night"; Dan 9:5 — an extended prayer-confession. The written/prayed form extends the speech-act into sustained verbal engagement with God. (3) *Public declaration*: Job 33:27 — "He sings before men and says: 'I sinned and perverted what was right'" — confession offered publicly to a human audience. The speech-based mode is the defining operation: without the speaking, the inner recognition remains conscience and guilt (CHAR-17) but does not become confession (CHAR-3).

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering confession in another?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences varied immediate responses when one person confesses to another. (1) *Forgiveness/mercy offered*: Luk 15:20 (context — the father runs and embraces before the prodigal has completed his confession speech); 1Sa 26:21 — Saul promises "I will no more do you harm" in response to David's mercy that prompted his confession. (2) *Indifference or dismissal*: Mat 27:4 — the priests' response to Judas's confession is "What is that to us? See to it yourself" — the confession is heard but the recipients are unmoved. (3) *God's response*: Eze 33:16 — sins not remembered. The immediate response from the human side is not uniform; from the divine side the corpus points toward reception and cancellation.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The immediate response varies substantially depending on whether confession is genuine or hollow, and who receives it. Genuine individual confession to God (2Sa 24:10; Dan 9:5; Mic 7:9) is met with the expectation of divine response — Mic 7:9 ("until he pleads my cause") implies confident awaiting. Hollow confession (Deu 1:41) produces no external divine response — the people proceed on their own. Corporate confession (Judg 10:10/15; 1Sa 7:6/12:10) consistently triggers communal intercession and divine engagement. Person-directed confession (Mat 27:4) can be dismissed. The variation reveals that the immediate response is shaped by: (a) the genuineness of the confession (genuine vs hollow), (b) the direction of the confession (God vs persons), and (c) the recipient's own moral orientation.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus is not globally silent on immediate response (T1.5.1 and T1.5.2 show the range). The specific silence is on the confessor's immediate inner-being experience at the moment of speaking the confession — the corpus describes what comes before (conscience activated) and what comes after (plea, forgiveness, dismissal), but the moment of speaking itself is not described phenomenologically. The inner experience of the act of naming one's own sin is present in 2Sa 24:10 (heart-struck) but the moment of actual speech is not described separately from the surrounding conscience-activation.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does confession produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences two trajectories from sustained confession. (1) *Moral clarity and self-knowledge*: Dan 9:5's cascade — "sinned and done wrong and acted wickedly and rebelled, turning aside from your commandments" — is itself a sustained differentiation of the multiple dimensions of moral failure. Extended confession produces greater inner articulation of what the sin was and how it operated. (2) *Expectation of vindication*: Mic 7:9 — "I will bear the indignation of the Lord because I have sinned against him, until he pleads my cause" — the sustained posture of confession opens toward a confident future orientation. Job 33:27 — "I sinned and perverted what was right, and it was not repaid to me" — the restored man who has confessed publicly now bears the memory of sin that was not punished: a grace-shaped self-knowledge.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained confession establish?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Sustained genuine confession establishes: (1) *Moral transparency before God*: Neh 1:6 — "confessing... day and night" — a posture of ongoing moral openness toward God that refuses accumulation of unacknowledged sin. (2) *Capacity for intercession*: Daniel's confession (Dan 9:5/8/11/15) is simultaneously a personal and an intercessory act — he names his own sin and his community's, establishing the intercessor's posture of identifying with those for whom he pleads. (3) *Humility of self-assessment*: 1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned" — sustained ownership of personal sin rather than displacement builds the inner quality of honest self-accountability.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The immediate response of confession (heart-struck conscience, verbal naming, plea) is episodic. The sustained effect is formative: a person who habitually confesses sin promptly (2Sa 24:10 shows David's pattern — "heart struck him" → immediate confession to God) develops a conscience that fires readily and a will that responds to it without long delay. The sustained effect thus shapes the inner person's moral responsiveness — confession practiced over time forms a more sensitive conscience and a more ready will. The immediate response is a single moral event; the sustained effect is character formation in moral transparency.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine confession?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences four enabling conditions. (1) *Active conscience*: 2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him" — the conscience must fire for confession to arise from within. Shimei (2Sa 19:20) — "your servant knows that I have sinned" — explicit inner knowledge driving the confession. (2) *Will's agreement to name the act as wrong*: 1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned and done great evil" — the will accepts responsibility without deflection. (3) *Awareness of a relational recipient*: confession is directional — the person must be oriented toward a recipient (God or person) to whom the naming is addressed (Luk 15:18 — the prodigal's first inner movement is toward his father). (4) *The absence of self-protective concern outweighing truthfulness*: genuine confession is distinguished from Saul's (1Sa 15:30) by the absence of self-interest driving the naming — David confesses without condition (2Sa 24:10/17).

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort confession?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Three evidenced blocking conditions. (1) *Self-interest overriding conscience*: 1Sa 15:30 — Saul's confession is immediately followed by "yet honor me now before the elders" — the need for social standing distorts the confession into a tool for self-preservation rather than genuine moral naming. (2) *External pressure as the sole motivator*: Exo 9:27/10:16 — Pharaoh's confessions are plague-prompted; once the plague ceases, he hardens (Exo 10:20 implied by context). Externally motivated confession without inner conscience-engagement is the hollow form. (3) *Will proceeding autonomously*: Deu 1:41 (VCG-03) — confession + immediate autonomous action = will not aligned with the confession's content. The blocking condition here is a will that remains self-directed despite the verbal acknowledgment.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters the opportunity for confession but does not confess?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus does not directly evidence the non-confessing person's inner state within CHAR-3's 34 verses (this belongs primarily to CHAR-4 — Conscience suppression). However, VCG-03 (Deu 1:41) and the Pharaoh pattern (Exo 9:27/10:16 — confesses under pressure, hardens when pressure lifts) evidence the *partial* version: the person who produces the verbal form but whose inner state remains unchanged. This partial state is characterised by: the conscience may have registered (enough to produce the words) but the will has not been reoriented. The full non-confessing person (who would not even produce the words) is outside this corpus's scope — their state belongs to CHAR-4.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of confession?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary inner-being dimension of confession is **conscience** — conscience in its confessional mode, as the brief's definition states. 2Sa 24:10 establishes this most clearly: "David's heart struck him" — the heart/conscience is the initiating faculty; the verbal confession follows. The conscience here functions not as a deterrent (as in CHAR-1's VCG-03) but as a *retrospective witness that drives speech*. It is the faculty that fires post-act, identifies the wrong, and refuses to remain silent — driving the will and mouth toward naming. Neh 1:6 ("confessing the sins... day and night") shows conscience in sustained confessional mode rather than as a single episode.

Hollow confession (Deu 1:41) shows that verbal confession without volitional alignment does not enable the will — the will remains on its own course.

**T3.6.3 — What does the volitional pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The volitional pattern is the key diagnostic of genuine vs hollow confession: the will must agree with and enact what the mouth says. 1Ch 21:17 shows this alignment at full depth — the will claims personal ownership, distinguishes itself from the innocent, and invites divine judgment on itself alone. This is confession as a full volitional act, not merely a verbal formula. The characteristic thus tests the will at its deepest level: will it own what it has done, or will it use the verbal form while directing the will elsewhere?

**T3.7.1 — Does confession engage the agency faculty?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. Agency is engaged in two ways. (1) *Claiming agency for the sin*: 1Ch 21:17 — "Was it not I who gave command to number the people? **T3.7.2 — Does confession enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Genuine confession *restores and clarifies* agency: by owning the sin as one's own act, the confessor names themselves as the responsible agent, which is the foundation for seeking remedy and making different choices going forward. 2Sa 24:10/17 — David's agency claimed → petition for mercy → the will now oriented toward remedy. **T3.7.3 — What does the agency pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The agency pattern reveals that confession is an *agency-claiming event*: it requires the person to identify themselves as the agent of the sin rather than displacing responsibility. The moral demand is proportional to how clearly the agency-claim is owned. Hollow confession (Deu 1:41) shows the failure: the verbal claim of agency for the sin does not produce ownership of agency for the remedy.

**T3.8.1 — Does confession engage the moral evaluation faculty?**

**T3.8.3 — What does the pattern reveal?**

## T5 — Transformation, Sequence, Mechanism (21 prompts)

**T5.1.1 — Does confession produce transformation in the person — condition, orientation, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Genuine confession produces transformation in both condition and orientation. The orientation change is the more visible transformation in the corpus — confession produces a reorientation of the will toward God and away from the sinning direction. **T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for confession — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398) in its first-person perfect confessional form**: *chatati* ("I have sinned") and *chatanu* ("we have sinned"). The root (miss the mark, offend, incur guilt) applied in the first-person perfect creates the confessional form: the act of sinning (marked by the root) is now named as completed by the speaker themselves. No dedicated confession-term appears; the sinning vocabulary is self-applied.

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — In the confession-mode, cha.ta operates almost exclusively as a **first-person perfect verb**: *chatati* (I have sinned) or *chatanu* (we have sinned). The grammatical precision of the confessional form thus encodes the moral precision of genuine confession: it names a specific completed act by a specific identified person. **T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Tavuchis's analysis illuminates why Pharaoh's confession (Exo 9:27/10:16) is hollow while David's (2Sa 24:10) is genuine: genuine apology/confession in Tavuchis's framework requires the acknowledgment of *unique, non-transferable obligation* — the offender names what they specifically did and accepts that only they bear this specific moral responsibility. 1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned and done great evil. **T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary divergence: moral repair frameworks (Tavuchis, Worthington) treat confession/apology as fundamentally a human relational transaction — its effect is measured by whether the offended party accepts it and whether the relationship is repaired. The verse evidence treats confession as primarily a *vertical transaction*: confession is made to God (even when it simultaneously addresses persons), and the criterion of success is not whether the human recipient accepts it but whether God receives and cancels the sin (Eze 33:16). Judas's confession (Mat 27:4) is rejected by the priests yet the moral act of naming the sin is still internally genuine (his conscience drove it). The human science framework cannot account for a confession that is moral and valid even when the human recipient dismisses it — the vertical dimension exceeds what the relational-transaction framework can contain.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The social-psychology research on self-disclosure surfaces the question of *audience effect*: confession before a community (VCG-02 — Judg 10:10; 1Sa 7:6; Psa 106:6) differs psychologically from private confession to God (VCG-01 — 2Sa 24:10) — public confession creates social accountability that private confession does not. The verse corpus treats both as equally valid confession forms without differentiating their social-psychological dynamics. No further verse investigation is required — the corpus does not raise these questions; the framework surfaces them for potential future analytical attention.
