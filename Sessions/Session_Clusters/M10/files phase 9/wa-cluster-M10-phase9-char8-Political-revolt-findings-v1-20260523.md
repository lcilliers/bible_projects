## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt reveals God's character primarily through one distinctive verse: 2Ch 21:10 — "So Edom revolted from the rule of Judah to this day... At that time Libnah also revolted from his rule, *because he had forsaken the Lord, the God of his fathers*." The causal connection — Jehoram forsaking the Lord producing Libnah's revolt — reveals God as the constitutive source of covenantal-political order. When the king abandons God, the political coherence that God's covenantal relationship sustained begins to fragment. God's character is revealed as the foundation of ordered authority: his being forsaken produces political disorder; his presence maintains the relational-political structure. The remaining verses address political revolt at the human-authority level without explicit God-reference, but 2Ch 21:10 establishes that God is the ultimate ground of the authority structure from which revolt breaks.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute political revolt to God?**

**[CHAR-8]** S — No verse in the 9-verse corpus attributes political revolt (pa.sha — breaking submission) to God. God does not revolt against authority; God is the source and ground of authority. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it is what subordinate parties do when they break submission to governing authority.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The silence on God revolting combined with 2Ch 21:10's causal link (God forsaken → revolts occur) reveals that political revolt is the characteristic that emerges when the God-grounded authority structure is abandoned. God himself does not revolt because he is the source and legitimising ground of all authority — revolt against God is the primordial rebellion that the pa.sha register of the H6586 root names in other M10 contexts. The political-revolt register of pa.sha in this corpus applies the same root to the human-political level: just as theological revolt breaks the God-human covenantal relationship, political revolt breaks the authority-subject governing relationship. The silence on divine political revolt confirms: God is on the receiving end of revolt (covenantal-theological), not its practitioner.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose political revolt serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-8]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for political revolt. The 9 verses uniformly present it as a negative event (rebellion, revolt, defection from governing relationships). The "to this day" markers (1Ki 12:19; 2Ki 8:22; 2Ch 10:19; 2Ch 21:10) name persistent political fracture as the ongoing consequence. No verse presents revolt as serving the created person's designed function.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether political revolt is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt belongs to the fallen condition. 2Ch 21:10 — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — locates the origin of the revolt sequence in Jehoram's personal spiritual defection (forsaking God), which is itself a fallen-condition event. The revolt follows the forsaking as its structural consequence. 1Ki 12:19 — Israel's rebellion against the house of David "to this day" — is presented as a persistent broken reality traced to a decisive act of broken loyalty (the 1Ki 12:1–19 Rehoboam narrative), not as part of the original design of the authority structure.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that political revolt is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The corpus orients political revolt toward sustained fracture rather than future fullness. 1Ki 12:19 and 2Ch 10:19 — "to this day" — name the ongoing persistence of the broken political relationship, with no resolution in view. 2Ki 8:22 and 2Ch 21:10 — "to this day" — similarly name Edom's revolt as a permanent fracture without restoration. The trajectory evidenced is toward continued division, not toward eschatological reunion. The characteristic does not point toward a positive future; it points toward an enduring political rift.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does political revolt express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt does not express the divine image positively but perverts one of the image's core capacities: the created person's capacity for covenantal relationship (including political authority structures) and for loyalty within those relationships. The image-bearer is designed for relationships of ordered loyalty and covenant fidelity; political revolt is the decisive breaking of that loyalty. 2Ch 21:10's causal chain — Jehoram forsaking the Lord → Libnah revolting — shows the image's capacity for loyalty operating in inversion: when the image-bearer breaks loyalty to God, the political-relational structures dependent on that loyalty fragment.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest political revolt is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-8]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The characteristic requires a subordinate party breaking submission to a governing authority — a relationship God does not occupy as a subordinate.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of political revolt in a person or community reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt reveals the divine image in a condition of broken covenantal loyalty. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day" — the ongoing rebellion names a condition in which the image-bearing community refuses the authority structure that God established. 2Ch 21:10's causal chain reveals the deepest dimension: the image-condition that produces political revolt is first a condition of having forsaken the Lord — the vertical loyalty broken before the horizontal political loyalty fractures. Political revolt is thus a surface manifestation of a deeper image-condition of broken loyalty, both toward God and toward his established order.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use political revolt typologically?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Limited typological evidence. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day" — the narrator's summary functions as a theological-historical marker that points forward: Israel's persistent political revolt from the Davidic house is an ongoing wound in the covenant history that ultimately points toward the eschatological restoration of the Davidic covenant (the hope of the restored Davidic king). The political revolt marks a fracture in the covenant history that requires future divine resolution. The typological direction is: the present fracture anticipating future restoration.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The direction is *present rupture pointing toward future restoration*. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19's "to this day" marks the ongoing broken state that the covenant history is still carrying. The fracture in the Davidic authority structure (Israel's revolt from the house of David) is the historical wound that the covenant expectation of a restored Davidic king addresses. The human political revolt is the type; the eschatological restoration of Davidic covenant authority is the anticipated antitype.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Limited typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Political revolt." The name encodes three things. (1) *Political*: the characteristic operates in the political-social domain — the relationships of governing authority between peoples, nations, and kingdoms. This distinguishes it from other pa.sha registers that address theological-covenantal revolt (CHAR-19). The corpus is uniformly political: Moab against Israel (2Ki 1:1; 3:5/7), Edom and Libnah against Judah (2Ki 8:20/22; 2Ch 21:8/10), Israel against the house of David (1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19). (2) *Revolt*: the characteristic is an active breaking-away — not passive non-compliance but a decisive act of defection from the governing relationship. 2Ki 8:20 — "Edom revolted from the rule of Judah and *set up a king of their own*" — the positive act of establishing independent authority is part of the revolt. (3) The name implies both a *prior relationship* (submission to authority) and a *decisive rupture* — revolt can only occur where prior submission existed.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The primary term is **pa.sha (H6586)** in its political-revolt register. The root meaning of pa.sha is *to break away from, to revolt, to transgress* — it names the action of decisive separation from an established binding relationship. In CHAR-8's political register, pa.sha is used exclusively of the breaking of governing authority relationships: "Moab rebelled against Israel" (2Ki 1:1), "Edom revolted from the rule of Judah" (2Ki 8:20), "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David" (1Ki 12:19). At the definitional level, pa.sha reveals that political revolt is not merely passive non-compliance but an active transgressive breaking-away — the same root that names sin-as-transgression in CHAR-19 is here applied to the political-authority domain, confirming that political revolt carries the full moral weight of wilful transgression. No NT Greek vocabulary appears in the 9-verse corpus — the characteristic is exclusively OT Hebrew.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: revolt is a movement away from — breaking out of the governing relationship, moving from submission to independence. 2Ki 8:20 — "revolted *from* the rule of Judah" — the directional preposition (*min* — from) names the movement away from authority. *Relational*: revolt requires a pre-existing governing relationship; the revolt breaks that relationship. 2Ki 3:7 — "the king of Moab has rebelled against me" — the revolt is understood within a prior suzerain-vassal relationship. *Constitutional*: 2Ch 21:10's causal link — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — implies that the inner constitutional condition of forsaking God is what produces the outward political revolt. The political revolt is the constitutional expression of the inner spiritual abandonment.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is political revolt?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt is a *decisional volitional act* — it is not a gradual drift or a habitual condition but a specific decisive action. 2Ki 8:20 — "Edom revolted from the rule of Judah and set up a king of their own" — the revolt is an event with a specific act (setting up their own king). 2Ki 1:1 — "After the death of Ahab, Moab rebelled" — the revolt has a triggering moment (Ahab's death). The characteristic is act-like rather than dispositional: it is the specific moment of breaking submission rather than the ongoing habitual pattern of non-compliance (which would be CHAR-5/6 territory). The 9-verse corpus focuses entirely on the act and its persistent consequences, not on the ongoing disposition of the revolting party.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The corpus reveals two constituent elements. (1) *The decisive act of breaking submission*: the pa.sha revolt itself — "rebelled against" (2Ki 1:1; 3:5), "revolted from the rule of" (2Ki 8:20; 2Ch 21:8/10), "in rebellion against" (1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19). (2) *The constructive assertion of independent authority*: 2Ki 8:20 / 2Ch 21:8 — "and set up a king of their own" — the revolt is not merely negative (breaking from) but positive (establishing alternative authority). The two elements together constitute the full act: breaking submission + asserting independence. The 2Ch 21:10 verse adds a third element: *the inner spiritual ground* — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — the political revolt is grounded in the revolter-instigator's own inner abandonment of God.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Working description: *Political revolt (CHAR-8) is the decisive volitional act of breaking submission to an established governing authority, expressed through the pa.sha (H6586) political-revolt register. The act involves both negative departure from governing submission (rebelling from, revolting from — 2Ki 8:20/22; 2Ch 21:8/10) and positive assertion of independent authority (setting up a king of their own — 2Ki 8:20). The characteristic operates in the political domain — governing relationships between peoples and kingdoms — rather than the personal moral domain. Its most distinctive analytical feature is the causal connection to the inner spiritual condition: 2Ch 21:10 links Libnah's revolt directly to Jehoram's forsaking of God, revealing that the inner abandonment of God produces the outer fragmentation of political-covenantal order. The revolt carries the moral weight of wilful transgression (as the pa.sha root confirms) while being expressed in the political register.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of political revolt?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The structural opposite is *loyal submission to governing authority* — the prior relationship that revolt breaks. The corpus names this opposite through the negation: "Edom revolted from the rule of Judah" (2Ki 8:20) implies that prior to the revolt, Edom was under Judah's rule in a submission relationship. The "to this day" markers (1Ki 12:19; 2Ki 8:22) name the ongoing revolt as the negation of what the governing relationship would have been. The structural opposite — submission — is the original condition from which the revolt departs. No dedicated positive vocabulary for submission appears in the corpus, but the revolt-language itself implies the prior submission as its structural precondition.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does political revolt explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt explicitly excludes: (1) *Governing submission*: 2Ki 8:20 — "revolted from the rule of" — the ruling authority's governance is what revolt refuses; (2) *Covenantal political loyalty*: 1Ki 12:19 — "rebellion against the house of David" — the Davidic covenantal authority structure is what Israel's revolt resists; (3) *The divine order that political authority structures express*: 2Ch 21:10 — the revolt is linked to the ruler's forsaking of God — revolt against political authority mirrors and is linked to the inner revolt against God's authority.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is political revolt not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-19 (Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing)*: the same root pa.sha operates in both, but CHAR-8 uses it in the political-military submission-breaking register (peoples and kingdoms), while CHAR-19 uses it in the personal-covenantal moral register (individuals crossing God's moral boundaries). (2) *Not CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent)*: political revolt is a decisive break-away action, not the sustained volitional refusal to turn from sinning; CHAR-5 requires a recognised sinning course that the will refuses to exit. (3) *Not mere political non-compliance*: political revolt involves a positive assertion of independence (2Ki 8:20 — "set up a king of their own") and a decisive relational rupture, not merely passive non-performance of obligations.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does political revolt operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The corpus evidences two inner modes. (1) *Opportunistic-volitional mode*: 2Ki 1:1 and 2Ki 3:5 — both Moab revolts occur "after the death of Ahab" — the death of the governing authority creates an opportunity that the subject-people's will seizes for revolt. The inner mode is: assessing the weakened authority position and choosing to break submission at the opportune moment. (2) *Spiritually-grounded mode*: 2Ch 21:10 — Libnah's revolt linked to Jehoram forsaking the Lord — the inner ground for revolt is the ruler's own spiritual abandonment; the revolt appears to arise from the vacuum of legitimate spiritual authority that Jehoram's forsaking created. These two modes represent different inner dynamics: opportunistic calculation vs response to spiritual-authority vacuum.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does political revolt operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Yes, across two clear contexts. (1) *Vassal-state political context*: 2Ki 1:1; 3:5/7 (Moab), 2Ki 8:20/22; 2Ch 21:8/10 (Edom) — smaller nations breaking from the control of Israel or Judah; the revolt is a bid for national independence. (2) *Intra-covenantal political context*: 1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19 — the northern tribes of Israel breaking from the house of David; the revolt is within the covenant community, breaking the Davidic governing structure. The intra-covenantal revolt is theologically weightier because it breaks not merely a political authority but a covenantally grounded one.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does political revolt have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Limited speech evidence. 2Ki 3:7 — Jehoram "sent word to Jehoshaphat king of Judah: 'The king of Moab has rebelled against me'" — the revolt is communicated verbally in the diplomatic-military context; the king names the revolt in requesting military alliance. The speech here is not the revolt itself but its public articulation in the political-diplomatic register. The revolt itself is expressed through action (setting up a king — 2Ki 8:20) rather than primarily through speech.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when political revolt occurs?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Two response patterns. (1) *Military response sought*: 2Ki 3:7 — Jehoram's immediate response to Moab's revolt is to summon Jehoshaphat for military alliance against Moab; the response is military-political engagement. (2) *Permanent fracture accepted*: 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "to this day" — the narrator's assessment implies that the response to Israel's revolt from the Davidic house was not immediate restoration but the acceptance of a permanent division. The responses differ: revolt against a vassal nation triggers military response; revolt against the governing authority within the covenant community produces lasting fracture.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Variable. Military response (2Ki 3:7), permanent fracture (1Ki 12:19; 2Ki 8:22), and the implicit context of 2Ch 21:10 (revolt as consequence of the ruler's spiritual state, without an immediate human response named) represent three different response patterns. The corpus shows no consistent single response: the specific authority relationship and political context shapes the response. What is consistent is that the revolt produces a recognisable break that is named and remembered — in every case the revolt is recorded as a significant political event with lasting consequence.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The inner phenomenological experience of the revolting party — what the inner person experiences in the moment of revolt — is not described in the corpus. The corpus describes the political-military facts and consequences of revolt, not the inner subjective experience of the decision to revolt.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does political revolt produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The corpus evidences *persistent political fracture* as the sustained consequence. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day" — the revolt produces an ongoing relational rupture that persists indefinitely. 2Ki 8:22 / 2Ch 21:10 — "Edom revolted from the rule of Judah to this day" — similarly permanent. The sustained inner-being consequence for the revolting community is: a settled condition of political independence from the prior governing relationship, which has become a permanent identity marker ("to this day").

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained political revolt establish?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Sustained political revolt establishes: (1) *Settled political independence*: 2Ki 8:20 — "set up a king of their own" — the revolting community establishes its own governing structure as an ongoing reality; (2) *Permanent relational fracture*: the "to this day" markers (1Ki 12:19; 2Ki 8:22) name a fracture that has become the permanent state of the relationship; (3) *In 2Ch 21:10's causal context*: the fracture is embedded in a spiritual-moral framework — the ruler's forsaking of God has produced a political environment in which loyalty structures break down.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Yes. The immediate response may include military engagement (2Ki 3:7 — seeking alliance against Moab). The sustained effect is the permanent fracture — the "to this day" condition. What begins as a decisive act of revolt becomes a settled political reality: Edom is no longer under Judah's rule; Israel is no longer under the Davidic house. The immediate action produces a permanent condition; the sustained effect is the institutionalisation of the break.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions in the revolting party enable political revolt?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Two evidenced enabling conditions. (1) *Perceived weakness of governing authority*: 2Ki 1:1 and 3:5 — both Moab revolts occur "after the death of Ahab" — the death of the strong authority figure creates the enabling condition for revolt; the perceived weakness of the successor authority makes revolt viable. (2) *Spiritual-authority vacuum in the governing authority*: 2Ch 21:10 — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — Jehoram's forsaking of God creates a spiritual-moral vacancy that enables the revolts of Edom and Libnah; when the governing authority has abandoned the spiritual foundation of legitimate rule, the subject peoples revolt.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent political revolt?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The blocking conditions appear by implication from the enabling conditions. (1) *Perceived strength and legitimacy of governing authority*: Moab's revolts occurring "after the death of Ahab" (2Ki 1:1; 3:5) imply that during Ahab's reign, Moab did not revolt — the strong authority blocked revolt. (2) *The ruler's faithfulness to God*: 2Ch 21:10's causal link implies the converse: a ruler who does not forsake the Lord maintains the spiritual ground of legitimate authority that prevents the subject peoples from revolting. Fidelity to God in the governing authority is the primary blocking condition.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the community that does not revolt against authority?**

**[CHAR-8]** S — The corpus does not describe the inner-being state of communities that maintain submission to governing authority. The corpus is entirely focused on the revolting parties; the non-revolting community's inner state is not addressed.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of political revolt?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — The primary inner-being dimension is **will/volition** — the decisive act of breaking submission requires a will that chooses the break. The pa.sha root itself names a deliberate wilful transgression-breaking: 2Ki 8:20 — "Edom revolted... and set up a king of their own" — the constructive act of setting up their own king is a volitional assertion of independence. 2Ch 21:10's causal ground — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — shows that forsaking (a volitional act) is the inner precondition for the political revolt. The characteristic is constitutively volitional: revolt requires a will that decides to break submission.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — (1) Pa.sha root meaning — deliberate transgressive breaking-away (not accidental); (2) 2Ki 8:20 — "set up a king of their own" — positive volitional assertion of independence alongside the breaking; (3) 2Ch 21:10 — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — *forsook* (*azav*) is a volitional act of abandonment; the political revolt follows from the volitional inner spiritual forsaking; (4) 2Ki 1:1 / 3:5 — "after the death of Ahab, Moab rebelled" — the temporal marker implies a decision taken at a specific moment.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does political revolt carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-8]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Relational*: revolt is constitutively a relational act — it breaks a governing relationship; without the prior relationship, there is no revolt; (2) *Political-social*: the characteristic operates at the communal-political level (nations, peoples, kingdoms) rather than the individual-personal level; (3) *Spiritual*: 2Ch 21:10 — the link to forsaking God introduces a spiritual dimension — the political revolt mirrors and is causally linked to the inner spiritual revolt against God. These secondary dimensions describe the relational-social context within which the will's revolting act operates; they do not compete with the will-primary classification.
