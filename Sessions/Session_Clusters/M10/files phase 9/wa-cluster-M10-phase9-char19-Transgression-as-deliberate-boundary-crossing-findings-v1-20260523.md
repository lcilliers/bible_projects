## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The 147-verse corpus reveals God's character across five dimensions. (1) *God as the one who establishes boundaries and commands*: Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — the transgression is possible only because God has established a knowable order; transgression presupposes divine command; (2) *God as the one who counts and tracks transgressions*: Amos 1:3 — "for three transgressions of Damascus, and for four, I will not revoke the punishment" — God keeps a precise count toward a threshold; Dan 9:24 — "seventy weeks are decreed... to finish the transgression" — a divine timeline governs transgression's end; (3) *God as the one who does not pardon certain transgressions*: Exo 23:21 — "he will not pardon your transgression, for my name is in him" — some transgressions exceed the reach of pardon; Jos 24:19 — "he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins"; (4) *God as the one who pardons through steadfast love*: Mic 7:18 — "pardoning iniquity and passing over transgression for the remnant of his inheritance"; Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake"; (5) *God as the one who is pierced by transgression through the servant*: Isa 53:5 — "he was pierced for our transgressions" — the transgression's cost falls on the servant whom God appoints.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing to God?**

**[CHAR-19]** S — No verse in the 147-verse corpus attributes transgression to God. God appears as the one who establishes the boundaries crossed (Rom 4:15), counts transgressions (Amo 1:3), withholds or grants pardon (Exo 23:21; Mic 7:18), and absorbs their cost through the servant (Isa 53:5/8). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The silence is structurally necessary: transgression requires a higher boundary to cross, and God stands as the boundary-setter. Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — God is the law-giver whose command makes transgression possible; God cannot transgress his own commands. The silence reveals that transgression is constitutively creaturely — it can only exist where a creature stands under divine authority and chooses to cross the known line. God's absolute holiness (Exo 23:21 — "my name is in him" as ground for no pardon) is what makes the crossing possible as transgression.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-19]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of a positive created purpose for transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition response. Rom 4:15 — the boundary must exist before transgression can occur; Rom 5:20 — "the law came in to increase the trespass" — the law's purpose relative to transgression is to expose and define it, not to legitimise it. No verse presents transgression as a designed feature of the image-bearer.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition, with one notable precedent-setting moment. Rom 5:14 — "the transgression of Adam, who was a type of the one who was to come" — Adam's transgression is the paradigmatic first instance; VCG-05's paraptōma passages (Rom 5:15/16/17/18) name the trespass as the historical entry-point of condemnation. The Amos threshold-formula (Amo 1:3/6/9/11/13/2:1/4/6) assumes accumulated transgression as the fallen condition across nations. The characteristic is not a designed feature but arises from the image-bearer's will choosing to cross the known boundary God has set.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — two eschatological orientations. (1) *Judgment and finish*: Dan 9:24 — "seventy weeks are decreed about your people and your holy city, to finish the transgression, to put an end to sin" — the eschatological fullness is the divinely decreed completion and termination of transgression; Dan 8:23 — "when the transgressors have reached their limit" — a maximum is appointed; (2) *Pardon and removal*: Isa 43:25 — "I will not remember your sins"; Psa 103:12 — transgressions removed to the furthest distance; Eze 18:22 — "none of the transgressions he has committed shall be remembered against him" — forgiven transgressions have no future hold.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Transgression expresses the image through its *inverted exercise of the will*: the image-bearer is constituted with a will capable of obedience to God's known commands — only a being made in God's image can knowingly receive a command and choose to cross it. Rom 5:14 — Adam's transgression is the paradigmatic knowing-violation; Rom 4:15 — transgression requires prior knowledge of the law. The capacity to know the boundary and choose to cross it belongs to the image's rational-moral constitution. Isa 1:2 — "children have I reared and brought up, but they have rebelled against me" — the parental image reveals that the capacity for known defiance is what makes the rebellion a moral reality rather than mere animal deviation.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-19]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The characteristic requires a known boundary to cross and a higher authority to transgress against; God occupies no such position. The characteristic is constitutively relational in a downward direction (the creature under the Creator).

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The presence of transgression-as-deliberate-boundary-crossing reveals the image in its *wilfully defiant fallen condition*: the image-bearer's rational capacity to know God's command has been deployed against God rather than for him. Isa 48:8 — "from of old your ear has not been opened... from before birth you were called a rebel" — the image is so thoroughly characterised by transgression that it has become a defining label. Eze 2:3 — "nations of rebels who have transgressed against me... from their fathers to this very day" — the sustained multi-generational character of the transgression reveals an image whose volitional capacity for knowing defiance has become its dominant orientation.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing typologically?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — through two typological axes. (1) *Adam-paradigm transgression* (VCG-05): Rom 5:14 — "Adam... a type of the one who was to come" — Adam's transgression is explicitly named as a type; the trespass's death-consequence (VCG-05 — Rom 5:15/16/17) is the negative type whose positive antitype is Christ's one act of righteousness; (2) *Servant-bearing* (VCG-07 cross-reference; VCG-06): Isa 53:5 — "he was pierced for our transgressions"; Isa 53:8 — "stricken for the transgression of my people" — the servant's bearing of transgression's cost is the OT type pointing toward Christ. Isa 53:12 — "numbered with the transgressors; yet he bore the sin of many, and makes intercession for the transgressors" — the intercession-for-transgressors is the typological bridge.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Two directions. (1) *Negative to positive type*: Adam's trespass (negative — VCG-05; Rom 5:14) is the type that Christ's obedience reverses (positive antitype); the direction is from fallen-human boundary-crossing toward divine-human righteousness-constituting. (2) *OT prophetic to NT Christological*: Isa 53:5/8's servant-bearing-transgression is the OT prophetic type pointing toward Christ's cross; the direction is from Isaiah's prophetic portrait (human servant) to Christ's actual vicarious suffering (divine fulfilment). Both directions: human type → divine antitype.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing." Three signals. (1) *Transgression*: the English rendering of the primary vocabulary family (pe.sha/pa.sha; parabasis); the word carries both the legal sense of crossing a line and the relational sense of betrayal; (2) *As deliberate*: the defining modifier — not inadvertent error (CHAR-2) but knowing, wilful choice; Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — a known boundary is required; (3) *Boundary-crossing*: the spatial-moral image that names the core act — the will steps over a line it knows to exist. The name signals: the most distinctively volitional category in M10 — the will that knows and chooses to defy.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Four primary term-groups. (1) **pe.sha (H6588)** — noun "transgression, rebellion, breach" — OT dominant (most VCGs); root sense of breaking away from, revolting against; (2) **pa.sha (H6586)** — verb "to transgress, to rebel, to break away" — action-form of the same root; Eze 2:3 — "nations of rebels who have rebelled (*pa.sha*) against me"; (3) **sa.rah (H5627)** — noun "revolt, apostasy, turning aside" — VCG-02; Isa 1:5 — continued revolt despite punishment; Jer 28:16 — "spoken rebellion against the Lord"; (4) **parabasis (G3847) / parabatēs (G3848) / parabainō (G3845)** — NT "transgression/transgressor/to transgress" — VCG-04; Rom 4:15 — definitional: "where there is no law there is no transgression"; (5) **paraptōma (G3900)** — "trespass, false step" — VCG-05 — Adam's paradigm (Rom 5:15/16/17/18/20). The term-families converge on the same semantic core: the will choosing to step past a known boundary.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — All three. *Directional*: transgression is movement across a boundary — the will moves from the permitted side to the forbidden side; Isa 59:20 — "those who turn from transgression" — the turn-from presupposes a direction-toward; Isa 31:6 — "turn to him from whom people have deeply revolted" — the revolt is a directional departure. *Relational*: transgression is always against someone — Jer 2:29 — "you have all transgressed against me"; Eze 2:3 — "rebelled against me"; Gen 31:36 — "what is my offense against you?" — the relational frame is constitutive. *Constitutional*: Isa 48:8 — "from before birth you were called a rebel" — transgression as a pre-personal constitutional label; Isa 57:4 — "children of transgression" — a hereditary identity.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The corpus reveals CHAR-19 as a *volitional act-and-disposition complex*: primarily a wilful act of the will (the specific crossing) but also a dispositional orientation of the will that has become constitutive. (1) *Act dimension*: Amos 1:3/6/9 — specific counted acts that cross the threshold; Rom 4:15 — the law-violation as a specific act; Eze 18:28 — "all the transgressions that he had committed" — enumerable past acts; (2) *Dispositional dimension*: Psa 36:1 — "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart" — an inner orientation that has become a voice; Isa 57:4 — "children of transgression" — transgression as identity; Isa 48:8 — "from before birth called a rebel" — a constitutive inner character.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The corpus reveals nine constituent elements corresponding to the nine VCGs. (1) *Against God in the covenant relationship* (VCG-01 — 27V): rebellion as relational betrayal; (2) *Personal moral breach — Job/Psalms/wisdom* (VCG-09 — 34V): inward-facing honest assessment; (3) *Interpersonal breach between persons* (VCG-08 — 19V): horizontal wrongdoing; (4) *Revolt — inner defiance speaking and acting* (VCG-02 — 7V): sa.rah's vocal and active inner rebellion; (5) *Oppressive weight crushing and enslaving* (VCG-07 — 14V): transgression as a burden-force; (6) *Pardoned and erased by God* (VCG-06 — 13V): divine forgiveness of rebellion; (7) *Accumulated crossing of divine tolerance threshold* (VCG-03 — 13V): the Amos count-formula; (8) *Law-violation — NT parabasis* (VCG-04 — 15V): the knowing-law-crossing register; (9) *Adam-paradigm foundational crossing* (VCG-05 — 5V): the paraptōma first-trespass.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Working description: *Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing (CHAR-19) is the wilful defiance of known moral or covenantal order — the will that knowingly crosses a boundary or breaches a covenant. The corpus evidences nine registers: (1) Against God in covenant (VCG-01 — 27V): "children have I reared... but they rebelled" (Isa 1:2) — relational betrayal; (2) Personal moral breach (VCG-09 — 34V): "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart" (Psa 36:1); (3) Interpersonal breach (VCG-08 — 19V): "love covers all offenses" (Pro 10:12); (4) Revolt (VCG-02 — 7V): "whole head is sick and whole heart faint" (Isa 1:5) — sa.rah's systemic collapse; (5) Oppressive weight (VCG-07 — 14V): "bound into a yoke... set upon my neck" (Lam 1:14); (6) Pardoned and erased (VCG-06 — 13V): "as far as east from west" (Psa 103:12); (7) Threshold crossing (VCG-03 — 13V): "for three transgressions... and for four" (Amo 1:3); (8) Law-violation (VCG-04 — 15V): "where there is no law there is no transgression" (Rom 4:15); (9) Adam-paradigm (VCG-05 — 5V): "one man's trespass" (Rom 5:15) — the foundational boundary-crossing. Distinguished from CHAR-1 (wilful sinning as act) by the emphasis on the knowing crossing of a known boundary, and from CHAR-18 (iniquity as accumulated crime) by being specifically the act-of-defiance rather than the accumulated moral-crime weight.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The structural opposite is *faithful covenant-keeping — the will that remains within the known boundary*. Hos 14:9 — "the upright walk in them, but transgressors stumble in them" — the upright walker is the structural opposite; Psa 32:1/2 — "blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered... in whose spirit there is no deceit" — the no-deceit, no-transgression state; Rom 2:25 — "circumcision indeed is of value if you obey the law" — obedience to the law is the structural opposite of parabasis (transgression of the law); 2Sa 22:24 / Psa 18:23 — "I was blameless before him, and I kept myself from guilt" — the blamelessness of deliberate self-restraint within the boundary.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Covenant membership*: Eze 20:38 — "I will purge out the rebels from among you... they shall not enter the land of Israel" — transgression excludes from the restored community; Eze 37:23 — no longer defiling themselves with transgressions is the condition for becoming "my people"; (2) *Divine pardon in certain cases*: Exo 23:21 — "he will not pardon your transgression"; Jos 24:19 — "he will not forgive your transgressions"; (3) *Wisdom and understanding*: Hos 14:9 — transgressors stumble in the Lord's right ways — the capacity to walk in wisdom is excluded by transgression; (4) *Divine prosper*: Pro 28:13 — "whoever conceals his transgressions will not prosper."

---

**T1.3.3 — What is transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-2 (Unintentional sinning)*: CHAR-2 lacks the knowing-deliberate dimension; Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — requires knowledge of the boundary; 1Ti 2:14 — "the woman was deceived and became a transgressor" — deception diminishes the deliberate dimension; (2) *Not CHAR-18 (Iniquity as accumulated moral crime)*: CHAR-18 is the accumulated crime-weight before God; CHAR-19 is the specific act of boundary-crossing that generates that accumulation; the two overlap (Exo 34:7 names both) but CHAR-19 is the act while CHAR-18 is the account; (3) *Not mere social offence*: Gen 50:17 — the brothers' transgression required forgiveness; but CHAR-19 is specifically the *knowing* crossing of a moral line, not merely offensive behaviour without moral definition.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Five distinct inner modes. (1) *As an inner voice*: Psa 36:1 — "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart" — transgression operates as an internal counselling voice that drives out the fear of God; (2) *As a threshold-crossing act*: Amos 1:3/6/9 — the counted transgressions that reach the threshold; Rom 4:15 — the law-violation as specific crossing; (3) *As a dominating power*: Psa 19:13 — "great transgression" exercising dominion; Pro 29:6 — transgression ensnaring the evil man; (4) *As identity-level character*: Isa 48:8 — "called a rebel from birth"; Isa 57:4 — "children of transgression"; (5) *As relational rupture*: Isa 59:13 — "transgressing, and denying the Lord, and turning back from following our God" — the inner act of turning away is constitutive.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes across the nine VCG registers. (1) *Divine-covenant context* (VCG-01): transgression operates as relational betrayal of the nurturing God (Isa 1:2) — the relational betrayal dimension dominates; (2) *Legal context* (VCG-04): transgression operates as law-violation with judicial consequences (Rom 2:23 — "dishonor God by breaking the law"); (3) *Interpersonal context* (VCG-08): transgression operates as breach of trust or relational duty (Exo 22:9 — breach of custodial trust; Pro 17:9 — covering an offense seeks love); (4) *Threshold context* (VCG-03): transgression operates as a countable accumulation (Amos 1:3 — "for three... and for four") toward a divine tolerance limit; (5) *Revolt context* (VCG-02): transgression operates as vocal, active inner defiance that destroys the whole person (Isa 1:5 — "whole head sick and whole heart faint").

---

**T1.4.3 — Does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — in three speech-modes. (1) *Transgression as inner speech*: Psa 36:1 — "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart" — the characteristic has an inner-voice dimension; (2) *Revolt expressed through verbal rebellion*: Isa 59:13 — "speaking oppression and revolt, conceiving and uttering from the heart lying words"; Jer 28:16 — "you have uttered rebellion against the Lord" — the spoken word is the revolt's expression; (3) *Confession of transgression*: Psa 32:5 — "I will confess my transgressions to the Lord" — the voluntary speech-act of naming and owning the transgression; Psa 38:18 — "I confess my iniquity"; Pro 28:13 — "he who confesses and forsakes them will obtain mercy."

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response to transgression's onset?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The immediate responses vary significantly. (1) *Shame and exposure*: Lam 1:22 — "because of all my transgressions; for my groans are many, and my heart is faint" — the immediate response is grief and inner collapse; Ezr 10:13 — "we have greatly transgressed in this matter" — corporate acknowledgment; (2) *Dominion over the will*: Psa 19:13 — "let them not have dominion over me!" — the immediate threat is enslavement; (3) *Self-justification and denial*: Pro 28:24 — "Whoever robs his father... says, 'That is no transgression'" — the immediate response in hardened conscience is denial; (4) *Despairing awareness*: Eze 33:10 — "surely our transgressions and our sins are upon us, and we rot away because of them. How then can we live?" — despairing recognition.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Variable across the VCG registers. In the personal-awareness register (VCG-09 — Psa 51:3 — "I know my transgressions, and my sin is ever before me") the immediate response is honest self-recognition. In the revolt register (VCG-02 — Isa 1:5 — the people continue to rebel despite being struck down) the immediate response is defiant persistence. In the oppressive-weight register (VCG-07 — Eze 33:10 — "we rot away") the immediate response is despair. In the interpersonal register (VCG-08 — Pro 28:24 — "that is no transgression") the immediate response can be conscience-suppression. The response ranges from honest recognition (Psa 51:3) through defiance (Isa 1:5) to denial (Pro 28:24).

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-19]** E — For the threshold-accumulation register (VCG-03 — Amos 1:3/6/9 — nations whose transgressions are counted toward judgment), the immediate inner response of the transgressing nations to their own accumulation is not described. The corpus names the transgressions and the divine judgment-threshold but not the moment-by-moment inner experience of Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Moab, Ammon, Judah, and Israel as they commit and accumulate their counted transgressions.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Four sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Systemic inner collapse*: Isa 1:5 — "the whole head is sick, and the whole heart faint" — sustained revolt produces comprehensive inner-being deterioration; (2) *Enslaving bondage*: Pro 29:6 — "an evil man is ensnared in his transgression" — the accumulated transgression becomes the trap; Lam 1:14 — "bound into a yoke... set upon my neck... my strength to fail" — the sustained burden produces physical and volitional incapacitation; (3) *Identity-level characterisation*: Isa 48:8 — "from before birth called a rebel" — sustained transgression becomes the defining identity label; Isa 57:4 — "children of transgression" — generationally transmitted; (4) *Moral blindness toward God*: Jer 2:29 — "you have all transgressed against me" yet they "contend" with God — the sustained transgressor has lost accurate moral self-assessment.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained transgression establish?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three established states. (1) *Settled inner rebellion as orientation*: Eze 2:3 — "nations of rebels who have transgressed against me... from their fathers to this very day" — sustained transgression establishes multigenerational rebellion as the default orientation; (2) *Self-justifying posture*: Jer 2:29 — "why do you contend with me?" — the sustained transgressor adopts a self-justifying inner stance toward God; Pro 28:24 — "that is no transgression" — the sustained sinner develops an inner narrative that suppresses conscience; (3) *Separation from God's ways*: Hos 14:9 — "transgressors stumble in them" — sustained transgression establishes a constitutive incompatibility with God's right ways.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes. The immediate response (shame — Lam 1:22; awareness — Psa 51:3; defiance — Isa 1:5) is episodic. The sustained effects (inner collapse — Isa 1:5; enslaving bondage — Lam 1:14; identity-characterisation — Isa 48:8; moral blindness — Jer 2:29) are constitutional developments of the will over time. The progression is: wilful act → repeated acts → habit → constitutive inner orientation of rebellion → identity-level rebel label (Isa 48:8) → stumbling in God's right ways as settled pattern (Hos 14:9).

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Knowledge of the boundary*: Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — knowledge of the law is the prerequisite; transgression is definitionally impossible without prior awareness of the boundary being crossed; (2) *Inner absence of God-fear*: Psa 36:1 — "transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart; there is no fear of God before his eyes" — the absence of the fear of God is the enabling inner condition for transgression to take hold; (3) *Appetite of the will*: Pro 28:21 — "for a piece of bread a man will do wrong" — the will's desire is what transgression exploits; Pro 17:19 — "whoever loves transgression loves strife" — a positively-oriented inner affection toward transgression.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort recognition of one's own transgression?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three blocking conditions. (1) *Self-flattery and pride*: Pro 28:24 — "that is no transgression" — the wilful verbal denial; Jer 2:29 — "why do you contend with me?" — the self-justifying contention against God; (2) *Love of transgression itself*: Pro 17:19 — "whoever loves transgression loves strife" — the positive inner affection toward transgression blocks recognition of it as wrongdoing; (3) *Deception*: 1Ti 2:14 — "the woman was deceived and became a transgressor" — external deception can partially diminish the deliberate dimension by distorting perception of the boundary.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who persists in transgression?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — Isa 1:5 names it comprehensively: "why will you still be struck down? Why will you continue to rebel? The whole head is sick, and the whole heart faint" — the persistent transgressor's inner state is one of comprehensive moral-spiritual illness in both cognitive (head) and volitional (heart) faculties. Jer 2:29 — "why do you contend with me? You have all transgressed against me" — the inner state is self-righteous contention against God despite guilt. The persistent transgressor's inner state is thus a combination of structural inner deterioration (Isa 1:5 — head and heart incapacitated) and self-justifying posture (Jer 2:29) that produces inability to stand in God's right ways (Hos 14:9 — stumbling).

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — The primary dimension is **the will's volitional act of knowing defiance** — the definition's "wilful defiance of known moral or covenantal order." The will is the primary faculty: it knows the boundary (Rom 4:15 — law required), it chooses to cross (Isa 1:2 — "they have rebelled"), it sustains the direction (Eze 2:3 — "to this very day"). Every other dimension (cognitive distortion, affective collapse, conscience suppression) is secondary to the will's primary volitional choice.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-19]** E — (1) Rom 4:15 — transgression requires knowledge of the law → will can only transgress what it knows; (2) Isa 1:2 — "children have I reared... but they have rebelled" — the rebellion is wilful choice against received care; (3) Eze 18:30 — "repent and turn from all your transgressions" — the remedial call is to the will to turn; (4) Isa 31:6 — "turn to him from whom people have deeply revolted" — the revolt and its reversal are both volitional movements; (5) Pro 28:13 — "he who confesses and forsakes them" — the forsaking is a volitional act confirming that the committing was volitional.

---
