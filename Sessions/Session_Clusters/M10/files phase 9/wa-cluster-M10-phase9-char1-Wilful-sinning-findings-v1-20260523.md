## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning reveals the character of God negatively: it is the one thing the verse evidence consistently shows God holding the person morally accountable for, which illuminates God as the moral reference point against whom all wilful sin is ultimately directed. Psa 51:4 states the paradigmatic formula — "Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight" — locating God as the primary party offended by every act of wilful sinning, even sins with human victims (David's sin against Bathsheba and Uriah). Gen 39:9 (Joseph) frames the refusal to sin as inseparable from reverence for God: "How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" — implying that God's moral character defines the boundary the sinning will crosses. The characteristic thus reflects God's holiness by negation: wilful sinning is the inner act that violates what God is.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The verse corpus for CHAR-1 contains no attribution of wilful sinning to God. This absence is analytically significant: the entire 98-verse corpus is structured around human sinning against God (VCG-01, 32V), human sinning against persons (VCG-02, 18V), and human sinning requiring restraint or remedy. God appears throughout as the offended party, the restraining agent (Gen 20:6 — "it was I who kept you from sinning against me"), and the judge (Eze 18:4 — "the soul who sins shall die"), but never as a sinning subject. The silence establishes wilful sinning as an exclusively creaturely phenomenon — something that arises from the constitution of the creature, not from the nature of God.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The structural silence on God possessing wilful sinning (see T0.1.2) combined with the corpus's consistent framing of God as moral standard and judge (Eze 18:4; Eze 18:20; Gen 20:6; Eze 3:21) suggests that wilful sinning belongs to the fallen condition of the image-bearer rather than to the original divine image. The capacity for moral choice — the will — is part of the image; sinning is what happens when that will is turned against its Maker. 1Jo 2:1 ("I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin") presupposes that sinning is avoidable — that believers retain moral agency — but it is something Scripture explicitly aims to prevent rather than express. The imago Dei includes the will; wilful sinning represents the will's defection from the direction the image was designed to express.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The verse corpus offers no evidence that wilful sinning serves a positive created purpose. No verse in the 98-verse corpus describes sinning as enabling, equipping, or fulfilling the person. On the contrary, the evidence uniformly presents sinning as producing rupture (national defeat, drought, exile — 1Ki 8:33/35), death (Eze 18:4/20), and damage to the one sinned against (Gen 20:9 — "you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin"). The evidence is silent on any positive created purpose for wilful sinning — which analytically confirms that it belongs to the fallen condition, not to a positive dimension of the created design.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidence points to wilful sinning as arising from the fallen condition, not from the original created design. Gen 39:9 (Joseph naming sinning as "great wickedness") and Eze 28:16 ("you sinned; so I cast you as a profane thing from the mountain of God") both treat sinning as a departure from an original blameless state. The cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04: Lev 4–5 sin-offering system) presupposes that sin is real and requires remedy — a remedial structure that would be unnecessary if sinning were part of the original design. The deterrence register (VCG-03: Gen 20:6, Exo 20:20, Psa 4:4, 1Jo 2:1) likewise frames sinning as something to be prevented, guarded against, and resisted — not something that belongs to what humans were made to be. The evidence collectively locates wilful sinning in the fallen condition.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The verse corpus contains limited but present evidence of future-orientation. Eze 3:21 ("if you warn the righteous person not to sin, and he does not sin, he shall surely live") frames non-sinning as the path toward life — implying that the person is moving away from wilful sinning toward a future of sustained righteousness. Joh 5:14 ("sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you") and Joh 8:11 ("go, and from now on sin no more") both present Jesus issuing a future-directed command after forgiveness — pointing to a trajectory beyond sinning rather than entrapment within it. 1Jo 2:1 explicitly names the telos: "I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin." The trajectory evidenced is away from wilful sinning — not deeper into it — and the cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04) establishes that each instance of sinning has a prescribed remedy pointing back toward restored standing. The evidence is oriented toward the cessation and transcending of wilful sinning.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning does not express the divine image positively, but it does presuppose the image's most distinctive feature: genuine moral agency. The corpus consistently treats sinning as a personal, accountable, wilful act. Eze 18:4 declares "the soul who sins shall die" — with no mitigation for ignorance or compulsion in the wilful register. Eze 18:20 underscores personal ownership: "the soul who sins alone shall die; the son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father." Gen 43:9 (Judah) and Gen 44:32 both show a human person voluntarily assuming moral accountability for another — an act only possible because genuine moral agency exists. The capacity for wilful sinning thus presupposes the image-dimension of moral agency: the person who can wilfully sin is the same person who can wilfully choose righteousness. The image provides the capacity; sinning is its misuse.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence establishes wilful sinning as exclusively creaturely with no divine analogue. Every instance of sinning in the corpus is human or creaturely: human persons sin against God (VCG-01), against other persons (VCG-02), or even angels sin (2Pe 2:4 — "God did not spare angels when they sinned"). God appears uniformly as the offended party, the restrainer, or the judge — never as a sinning subject. The closest the corpus comes to a divine-side parallel is Gen 20:6, where God actively restrains Abimelech from sinning — positioning God as the external check on creaturely sinning. There is no divine analogue to wilful sinning evidenced in this corpus.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The presence of wilful sinning reveals a will that has turned against its Maker — a damaged but not destroyed divine image. The evidence treats sinning as a real inner act carrying personal moral consequence (Eze 18:4/20), not as something that annihilates the person's moral agency or their status as an image-bearer. Eze 3:21 presupposes that a sinning person can still be warned and can still choose not to sin — moral agency persists. 1Jo 1:10 ("if we say we have not sinned, we make him a liar") treats sinning as a universal creaturely condition whose honest acknowledgment itself requires a functioning conscience. The pattern suggests: the divine image remains (the will is present, moral agency persists), but wilful sinning represents that image operating in defection from its designed orientation toward God.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus contains clear typological deployment of wilful sinning in two directions. First, Eze 28:16 ("you sinned; so I cast you as a profane thing from the mountain of God, O guardian cherub") uses the cherub's sinning as a paradigmatic instance that illuminates the inner dynamic of how sinning produces expulsion from the divine presence — a pattern that maps onto human sinning and its consequences. Second, the Levitical sin-offering system (VCG-04: Lev 4:14, 4:22, 4:23, 4:27, 4:28, 4:35, 5:6–13) is typological throughout — the sacrificial remedy for wilful sinning points forward to the substitutionary atonement that resolves sin's debt. The Lev 5:13 formula ("and he shall be forgiven") and Lev 4:35 ("the priest shall make atonement for him for the sin which he has committed, and he shall be forgiven") foreshadow a forgiving transaction that the NT corpus (VCG-05) casts as fulfilled in Christ's advocacy (1Jo 2:1: "we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous").

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The typological direction is bi-directional. In the Levitical sin-offering corpus (VCG-04), the human instance of sinning points forward to a divine resolution: the sacrificial system is a human-facing institution that anticipates the divine act of atonement. In the Eze 28:16 cherub passage, the creaturely (angelic) instance of sinning establishes the pattern — falling from the presence of God — that the human instance participates in and illuminates. The NT corpus (VCG-05) shows the reverse movement: 1Jo 2:1's advocacy language presupposes that Christ (the divine) addresses the human instance of sinning, making the divine-to-human direction primary in the NT resolution of the type.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Typological use is evidenced (see T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded by the positive finding above.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Wilful sinning." The name encodes a binary claim: (1) that sinning is a *wilful* act — not accidental, not compelled, but chosen by the inner person with awareness of what is being done; and (2) that this is *sinning* — a relational act that violates a standard. The name signals that the defining feature is not the category of wrong committed but the quality of the inner act: the will's knowing engagement with the wrong. This is confirmed by the definitional separation from CHAR-2 (Unintentional sinning) — that characteristic occupies its own sub-group precisely because the corpus distinguishes deliberate from inadvertent wrongdoing. The name "Wilful sinning" thus foregrounds volition as the constitutive element: what makes this characteristic analytically distinct is not the content of the sin but the inner quality of the will that commits it.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary Hebrew term is cha.ta (H2398), present in all 86 OT verses of the corpus. At the definitional level, cha.ta is a verb — an action-term — meaning to miss the mark, to offend, to incur guilt. The NT primary term is hamartanō (G0264), present in the NT VCG-05 corpus and also the interpersonal VCG-02 instances (Mat 18:15, Luk 17:3/4). Hamartanō is likewise a verb, a direct Greek counterpart to cha.ta in semantic range. The paraptōma (G3900) appears once in VCG-05 (Gal 6:1 — "caught in any transgression"). The definitional-level reading of both primary terms reveals that sinning is expressed verbally — as an event, an act, a thing done or committed — rather than as a static condition or enduring state. Both terms foreground the act of sinning over the accumulated condition of sin.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The name "Wilful sinning" carries strong relational and directional implications. The corpus structures sinning along three directional axes: (1) vertical — sinning against God (VCG-01: "sin against God" Gen 39:9; "against you, you only" Psa 51:4; "sinned against me" Eze 14:13); (2) horizontal — sinning against persons (VCG-02: "sin against his neighbor" 1Ki 8:31; "sins against you" Mat 18:15); and (3) reflexive — sinning against oneself (1Cor 6:18: "the sexually immoral person sins against his own body"; Pro 8:36: sinning injures oneself). These three directions are constitutive: the name implies that wilful sinning is always sinning *against* someone — it is irreducibly relational. The constitutional implication is that sinning engages the will in a movement *away from* a relational bond that was owed, making the will the primary constitutional faculty at stake.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The verse corpus establishes wilful sinning primarily as an *act* — a discrete event of the will. The dominant grammatical form throughout is the cha.ta verb in the perfect or imperfect (committed, commits, sinned), naming an act rather than a state. "The soul who sins shall die" (Eze 18:4) and "Israel has sinned" (Jos 7:11) both describe a completed act with consequences. The cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04) further confirms this: "the sin which he has committed" (Lev 4:28) triggers a prescribed response — an act-to-remedy structure that makes sense only if sinning is an event rather than a standing condition. However, the corpus also contains dispositional evidence: Hos 4:7 ("the more they increased, the more they sinned against me") and Hos 13:2 ("they sin more and more") describe sinning as an escalating pattern that moves beyond single acts toward an entrenched orientation. The characteristic is primarily an act but contains a dispositional trajectory when sustained.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — At first encounter the characteristic has constituent elements. The VCG structure of M10-J itself reveals four distinct registers: VCG-01 (God-directed), VCG-02 (interpersonal), VCG-03 (deterred/restrained — the conscience-and-will register), VCG-04 (requiring ritual remedy — the cultic-legal register). Within each register the inner-being structure differs: in VCG-01, the act involves the will turning against God's covenant claim; in VCG-02, the act involves a relational harm directed at a person; in VCG-03, the act is averted or protested (conscience-active resistance); in VCG-04, the act has occurred and the cultic machinery responds. Even at this early encounter, wilful sinning is not a simple undivided act but a constellation of related inner events sharing the core of a deliberate will engaging wrongdoing.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Working description: *Wilful sinning is a volitional act of the inner person — primarily located in the will — in which the person knowingly commits a wrong directed against God, another person, or the self. It engages the will as the primary faculty, the conscience as the witness (whether active in restraint, as in VCG-03, or present as the moral register that recognises the act as wrong), and moral evaluation as the standard against which the act is measured. Its impact is rupture: it damages the vertical relationship with God (producing guilt, divine anger, and the need for atonement), horizontal relationships with persons (producing harm and requiring forgiveness), and the person's own moral integrity (self-damage, Psa 51:4; Pro 8:36; 1Cor 6:18). The act creates a debt — moral, relational, and (in the cultic corpus) ritual — that requires resolution.* Evidence: Gen 39:9 (will knowingly refuses); Psa 51:4 (vertical rupture named); Mat 18:15 (horizontal harm requiring confrontation); Lev 4:28/35 (cultic debt and remedy); 1Jo 2:1 (advocate for the sinning believer).

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The structural opposite is evidenced in two registers. (1) *Integrity of heart / blamelessness*: Gen 20:6 — God says Abimelech "acted from integrity of heart" and intervened to prevent sinning. The opposite of wilful sinning is the will aligned with integrity, acting in correspondence with moral truth rather than against it. (2) *Righteousness / not sinning*: Eze 3:21 — "if you warn the righteous person not to sin, and he does not sin, he shall surely live" places non-sinning and righteousness as a linked opposite pair. Eze 18:4/20 contrast the sinning soul (who dies) with the righteous person. Psa 119:11 ("I have stored up your word in my heart, that I might not sin against you") presents the word-saturated will as the inner condition that opposes sinning. The structural opposites are integrity, righteousness, and will-alignment with God's word.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning explicitly excludes several inner conditions. (1) *Divine restraint / protection*: Gen 20:6 — God's external intervention is what prevented sinning; sinning is what happens when that protection is not operative or is refused. (2) *Intercession*: 1Sa 12:23 — Samuel identifies ceasing to pray for Israel as itself a sin; sinning and faithful intercession stand in opposition. (3) *Covenant faithfulness*: Jos 7:11 — "Israel has sinned; they have transgressed my covenant" — sinning is the act of covenant-breaking. (4) *God's word dwelling in the heart*: Psa 119:11 — the stored word guards against sinning. (5) *Self-restraint of speech*: Psa 39:1 — guarding the mouth resists sinning with the tongue. The characteristic resists and excludes every inner condition that aligns the will with God: covenant faithfulness, stored word, intercession, restraint, and divine protection.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence marks three clear boundaries. (1) *Not identical to unintentional sinning*: the cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04) includes Lev 4:22 ("when a leader sins, doing unintentionally") and Lev 5:15 ("commits a breach of faith and sins unintentionally") — this category (CHAR-2 in the programme) is present within VCG-04 as the cultic-remedy context, but the wilful register proper is marked by the knowing, deliberate quality. (2) *Not guilt as an inner state*: the corpus distinguishes the act of sinning (CHAR-1) from the experience of guilt that follows it (CHAR-17 — Guilt as inner-being state). 2Sa 12:13 shows sinning acknowledged, then guilt removed by God; the sequence distinguishes act from experienced condition. (3) *Not refusal to repent*: Hos 4:7 and 13:2 (escalating sinning) border on CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent), but the wilful-sinning register remains at the level of the act committed, not the subsequent refusal of the will to turn from it. The boundary between CHAR-1 and CHAR-5 is temporal: the act of sinning precedes the question of whether to repent.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus reveals four distinct modes of operation. (1) *As a willed act of transgression directed vertically against God* (VCG-01): the inner person knowingly crosses a boundary God has set — Gen 39:9 (Joseph's refusal names the act's relational target); Eze 18:4 (the individual soul's sinning carries mortal consequence). (2) *As a willed harm directed horizontally against another person* (VCG-02): the inner wrongdoing is enacted in the relational sphere — "sin against his neighbor" (1Ki 8:31), "sins against you" (Mat 18:15). (3) *As a reflexive act of self-damage* (Pro 8:36; 1Cor 6:18): sinning against wisdom or against one's own body shows the will turning destructively inward. (4) *As an act that grows in intensity and compulsion over time* (Hos 4:7; 13:2): "the more they sinned against me" — a mode where the single act opens toward a pattern. These four modes share the will as the operative faculty but differ in directionality.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows clear contextual differentiation. In the God-directed register (VCG-01), sinning is primarily a covenantal and relational act with communal consequence: 1Ki 8:33/35 show national defeat and drought following the people's sinning against God; the scope is corporate. In the interpersonal register (VCG-02), sinning is personal and remedial: Mat 18:15 specifies a repair process (confrontation → listening → forgiveness) that is individual. In the cultic register (VCG-04), sinning operates as an objective cultic fact requiring prescribed remediation regardless of the person's subjective emotional state — the sin offering is brought on realisation, not on feeling. The constitutional level also differs: in Gen 20:6, God restrains sinning from outside the person; in Psa 119:11 and Psa 4:4, the internal resources of stored word and reflective pondering are the operative mechanisms. The characteristic operates at both the social/communal and individual constitutional levels.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Speech-based sinning is explicitly evidenced. Psa 39:1 ("I will guard my ways, that I may not sin with my tongue") locates sinning's communicative mode in unguarded speech — the tongue as the instrument. Job 2:10 ("Job did not sin with his lips") confirms that under suffering the lips are the point of potential sinning. Job 31:30 ("I have not let my mouth sin by asking for his life with a curse") shows speech as a vehicle for directing sinful aggression against an enemy. Ecc 5:6 ("let not your mouth lead you into sin") frames the mouth as an organ that leads the whole person into sin through rash speech before the divine messenger. Isa 29:21 shows sinning through false verbal accusation in public proceedings. The communicative mode operates in the outward direction (speech expressing inner sinful orientation) and the self-implicating direction (rash speech creating liability before God).

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus reveals two divergent immediate responses to encountering wilful sinning. (1) *Conscience-activation and moral recognition*: Gen 42:22 — Reuben's immediate response to the brothers' sinning against Joseph is to invoke the reckoning that follows: "so now there comes a reckoning for his blood." The guilty party's immediate response is awareness of consequence. Gen 20:9 — Abimelech's immediate response to learning of the sin brought upon him is protest and confrontation: "you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin." (2) *Moral refusal and naming*: Gen 39:9 — Joseph's immediate response to the temptation is to name it as "great wickedness and sin against God" — conscience-active refusal before the act is committed. The pattern suggests that the most immediate inner-being response to encountering wilful sinning (whether in oneself or from another) is moral recognition — the conscience registering the moral quality of the act.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The immediate response varies across the corpus. In VCG-03 (deterrence/restraint), the immediate response is refusal and resistance (Gen 39:9, Job 1:22, Psa 4:4, Psa 39:1). In VCG-01 (God-directed sinning), the immediate response evidenced is often external consequence rather than internal registration — drought (1Ki 8:35), defeat (1Ki 8:33), expulsion (Eze 28:16). In VCG-02 (interpersonal), the immediate response includes protest (Gen 20:9), accountability-assumption (Gen 43:9, Gen 44:32), and confrontation-call (Mat 18:15). In VCG-04 (cultic), the immediate response is the realisation of guilt triggering the obligation to bring an offering (Lev 4:23 — "the sin which he has committed is made known to him"). The variation suggests that immediate response is shaped by the directional mode (God-directed vs interpersonal vs reflexive) and by whether the person is the agent or the recipient of the sinning.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence is not globally silent on immediate response (see T1.5.1 and T1.5.2), but the corpus is notably sparse on the immediate inner response of the sinning agent in the God-directed register (VCG-01). The verses in VCG-01 primarily record the fact of sinning and its consequences (national disaster, exile) rather than the sinning person's immediate inner experience of committing the act. Eze 18:4/20 declare outcome (death) without describing the sinner's immediate inner response at the moment of sinning. This silence may reflect the prophetic genre's interest in consequence over phenomenology.

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Sustained wilful sinning produces three evidenced trajectories in the inner being. (1) *Escalating compulsion*: Hos 4:7 ("the more they increased, the more they sinned against me") and Hos 13:2 ("they sin more and more, and make for themselves metal images") show sinning intensifying with each repetition — a self-reinforcing dynamic where the will's engagement with wrongdoing makes subsequent sinning more likely and more elaborate. (2) *Corporate moral deformation*: Isa 1:4 describes a "sinful nation, a people laden with iniquity" — collective identity reshaped around sinning, with estrangement from God as the settled condition. (3) *Accumulation of debt and consequence*: 1Ki 8:33/35/50 show sinning accumulating into a record that triggers defeat, drought, and captivity — sustained sinning produces a weight of consequence that falls on communities and individuals.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sustained-effect evidence points to: (1) *Vulnerability to temptation*: Neh 13:26 — Solomon's sustained exposure to foreign women produced a sinning that "even him" — the beloved and gifted king — could not resist, suggesting that sustained sinning erodes the will's resistance capacity. (2) *Estrangement from God*: Isa 1:4 ("they have forsaken the Lord, they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged") describes a settled orientation of alienation as the outcome of sustained national sinning. (3) *Increased elaborateness of idolatry*: Hos 13:2 — sinning "more and more" produces "metal images, idols skillfully made" — an escalating investment in what replaces God. (4) *Moral weight on subsequent generations*: Neh 13:26 (Solomon's foreign-women vulnerability), the cultic-atonement system's communal dimension (Lev 4:3 — "if it is the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people") suggest that sustained sinning by leaders creates a moral weight borne by communities.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sustained effect differs markedly from the immediate response. The immediate response (T1.5.1) is moral recognition — conscience registering the act. The sustained effect is compound: escalating compulsion (Hos 4:7; 13:2), estrangement (Isa 1:4), and consequence-accumulation (1Ki 8:33–50). The critical difference is directional: the immediate response includes conscience-active elements that resist or protest sinning, whereas the sustained effect shows conscience being progressively overridden — the will habituates to sinning and the moral recognition becomes less operative. This difference is the evidence base for the programme's distinction between CHAR-1 (wilful sinning as act) and CHAR-5 (refusal to repent) / CHAR-6 (habitual defection) — sustained wilful sinning without repentance migrates toward the habitual-defection register.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — "Reception" requires clarification for this characteristic: wilful sinning is not received from outside in the way a gift or grace is received; it is generated by the person's own will. The inner conditions that enable its occurrence (rather than reception) are evidenced as: (1) *Absence of divine restraint*: Gen 20:6 — Abimelech sinned only in ignorance; God's external intervention prevented him. The absence of that restraint enables the will to proceed. (2) *Social pressure overriding conscience*: 1Sa 15:24 — Saul sinned because "I feared the people and obeyed their voice" — social pressure as the enabling condition that overrode the will's alignment with God's command. (3) *Vulnerability through exposure*: Neh 13:26 — foreign women as the corrupting influence that enabled Solomon's sinning. (4) *Temptation presenting the opportunity*: Neh 6:13 — enemies constructed a trap designed to produce sinning. These are conditions that the will then responds to by sinning.

## T5 — Transformation, Sequence, Mechanism (21 prompts)

**T5.1.1 — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, orientation, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Wilful sinning produces transformation in both condition and orientation. *Condition change*: the person who sins moves from a state of innocence or standing to a state of guilt and moral liability — Eze 18:4/20 ("the soul who sins shall die") declares a condition change: the sinning soul is now under mortal judgment. The cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04) presupposes this: the sin offering is required precisely because the person's condition has changed through sinning — Lev 4:35 ("the sin which he has committed"). *Orientation change*: Hos 4:7/13:2 (escalating sinning — "the more they sinned against me") shows the sinner's orientation progressively locked into the sinning direction. Isa 1:4 ("utterly estranged") names the orientation change as an outcome: estrangement from God as the directional result of sustained sinning. Both condition and orientation are transformed.

---

**T5.1.2 — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The transformation is primarily evidenced as reversible through divine action. 2Sa 12:13 — Nathan declares: "The Lord also has put away your sin; you shall not die." The condition change (guilt → forgiven) is reversed by God's act of putting away. 1Ki 8:33/35 — Solomon's prayer structure presupposes reversibility: if the people "turn again to you and acknowledge your name and pray," God will forgive (1Ki 8:50). The cultic-atonement corpus (VCG-04: Lev 4:35/5:10/5:13 — "and he shall be forgiven") shows that the condition change from sinning is reversed through the prescribed remedy. Eze 3:21 confirms reversibility at the orientation level: the righteous person warned not to sin "does not sin" — the trajectory can be held or altered. However, the escalation pattern (Hos 4:7/13:2) suggests that sustained sinning without reversal progressively narrows the window for reversal — not closing it by divine decree, but by the hardening of the inner person.

**T5.2.1 — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The corpus evidences a recurring sequence. (1) *Pre-act state*: the person in a condition of temptation or pressure — the will is not yet committed (Gen 39:9: Potiphar's wife's appeal; 1Sa 15:24: the people's voice pressing; Neh 6:13: enemies' trap set). (2) *Decision point*: the will either resists (VCG-03: Joseph's refusal) or capitulates (1Sa 15:24: Saul yields). (3) *Post-act state A — guilt/liability incurred*: the sinning has occurred and a new moral condition exists — Lev 4:23 ("the sin which he has committed is made known to him"), Gen 42:22 (reckoning arrives). (4) *Post-act state B — consequences manifesting*: 1Ki 8:33/35 (defeat, drought). (5) *Post-act state C — remedy sought and granted*: Lev 4:35 (sin offering → forgiven), 2Sa 12:13 (confession → forgiveness declared).

**T5.2.2 — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sequence (pressure/temptation → decision point → guilt-incurred → consequence → remedy) reveals that wilful sinning is not a terminal event but a *relational episode* with a prescribed arc. The Levitical system (VCG-04) codifies this arc into a structured remedial process: each sinning act opens a relational deficit that the prescribed ritual closes. The Solomon prayer corpus (1Ki 8:33–50) maps this arc at national scale: sinning → defeat/disaster → turning + prayer → forgiveness + restoration. The sequence also reveals that the will is operative at the decision point and at the remedy point: sinning is a decision and so is repentance. The characteristic is reversible precisely because both ends of the arc are volitional.

**T5.3.1 — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning produces change through two primary mechanisms. (2) *Habituation/escalation*: Hos 4:7 ("the more they increased, the more they sinned") and Hos 13:2 ("they sin more and more") show that repeated sinning forms a habit that progressively shapes the will's default orientation — a formation mechanism that gradually changes the inner person's constituted direction.

**T5.3.2 — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The relational-rupture mechanism operates immediately and universally: every act of sinning produces the rupture regardless of context or repetition (Eze 18:4; Lev 4:14). The habituation/escalation mechanism is a sustained-pattern phenomenon: it requires repeated sinning over time (Hos 4:7/13:2; Isa 1:4's national pattern). A single act of sinning ruptures the relation; sustained sinning without remedy forms the person in the sinning direction. The cultic-atonement system (VCG-04) addresses the immediate rupture mechanism with an immediate remedy. The habituation mechanism requires a more comprehensive inner transformation than a single sin offering.

## T7 — Lexical, Literary, and Human Science (20 prompts)

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398)** — a verb whose root meaning is "to miss (a target), to go wrong, to offend, to incur guilt." The root imagery of missing a mark (the physical sense appears in Judg 20:16 — slingers who would not "miss," same root) applied to the moral realm names sin as a *directional failure*: the person aimed elsewhere than the moral mark. The primary NT term is **hamartanō (G0264)** — from hamartia, likewise meaning "to miss, to err, to sin." The semantic convergence of Hebrew and Greek roots around the "missing the mark" image is analytically significant: both Testaments anchor wilful sinning in the concept of a directional failure — not a neutral act but one that misses what was aimed at. Paraptōma (G3900, once in VCG-05: Gal 6:1) means "a false step, a trespass" — accidental entanglement rather than directed missing.

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Cha.ta (H2398) operates as: (1) **Qal verb** — the active sinning act ("the soul who sins," Eze 18:4; "I have sinned," 2Sa 12:13; "they sinned against me," Eze 14:13). (2) **Piel verb** (intensive/causative) — "making/causing to sin" (not in this corpus directly but underlying the causative sense in 1Ki 14:16 — Jeroboam who "made Israel to sin," which belongs to CHAR-7). (3) **Hiphil** — causative. In VCG-04 the Lev 4/5 corpus uses the noun form chat.tat alongside the verb. Hamartanō (G0264) is similarly verbal throughout — an action-term not a state-term. The grammatical range reveals that sinning is *event-language* not *condition-language*: the primary grammatical form names a discrete act, not a standing state. This confirms that CHAR-1 is constitutively an act rather than a condition.

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?**

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary divergence: moral psychology treats moral disengagement as a process of psychological self-protection — the person avoids cognitive dissonance by adjusting their moral standards.
