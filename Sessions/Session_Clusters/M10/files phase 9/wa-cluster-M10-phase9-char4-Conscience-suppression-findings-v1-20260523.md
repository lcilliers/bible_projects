## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression reveals the character of God primarily through contrast and consequence. Jer 2:35 — God declares: "Behold, I will bring you to judgment for saying, 'I have not sinned'" — God's response to the false innocence claim reveals him as a God who sees through self-deception and judges not only the sin but the denial of it. God's moral omniscience is the implicit premise: the person may suppress awareness of their sin, but God's awareness is undiminished. Jer 16:10 — the people ask "what sin have we committed?" in the face of declared judgment — God's announcement of judgment presupposes his accurate moral knowledge even when the persons themselves lack it. The characteristic thus reveals God as the one whose perception cannot be suppressed: what the conscience suppresses in the human, God sees clearly and will bring to judgment.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute conscience suppression to God?**

**[CHAR-4]** S — No verse in the 8-verse corpus attributes conscience suppression to God. God appears throughout as the one who sees, knows, and judges the moral reality that the human conscience fails to register. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes the creaturely capacity to fail to perceive moral reality that God perceives fully. God cannot suppress awareness of moral reality because he is its author and standard. The absence is constitutive.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The silence on God possessing conscience suppression (T0.1.2) indicates that the characteristic belongs entirely to the fallen creature rather than to any dimension of the divine image. Conscience itself is an image-bearing faculty — the moral witness within the person that reflects God's own moral awareness. Conscience suppression is the malfunction of that image-bearing faculty — the image's moral-witness capacity turned off or refused. The silence on God's possession confirms: the image includes conscience; conscience suppression is what happens to the image when it is damaged by sin and sustained rebellion. The characteristic is not a reflection of the divine image but its negation.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose conscience suppression serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-4]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that conscience suppression serves any positive created purpose. No verse presents the absence of moral self-awareness as an equipping or enabling capacity. The corpus presents conscience suppression uniformly as a moral deficit: Jer 2:35 (denial of sin brings judgment), Jer 16:10 (failure to recognise sin despite facing judgment), Ecc 8:12 (sinning without the fear of God — the absence of the right inner orientation). The characteristic serves no created purpose; it is a failure of the moral-witness faculty that belongs to the image.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether conscience suppression is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence points to conscience suppression as belonging entirely to the fallen condition. Ecc 8:12 — the sinner who "does evil a hundred times yet prolongs his life" operates in a state where the fear of God is absent from within — the contrasting phrase "those who fear God, because they fear before him" implies that the fear of God is the design-intended inner orientation, of which the sinner's conscience-absent state is the falling away. Jer 2:35 — the active claiming of innocence where guilt is real represents a distortion of the moral-witness faculty from its proper operation. Conscience suppression is not original creation; it is what the fall does to the image-bearer's moral self-awareness.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that conscience suppression is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus contains one eschatological-orientation evidence: Jer 2:35 — "Behold, I will bring you to judgment for saying, 'I have not sinned'" — the suppressed conscience does not avoid judgment but accumulates toward it. The future toward which conscience suppression is oriented is *divine judgment that cannot be suppressed*, not a positive fullness. Tit 3:11 — "he is self-condemned" — the self-condemnation implied by the divisive person's conscience (even where the person does not acknowledge it) anticipates an outer verdict that will confirm what the inner witness already declared. The trajectory is not toward flourishing but toward judgment: conscience suppression orients the person toward a reckoning they are not prepared for.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does conscience suppression express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression does not express the divine image positively; it reveals the image's degraded condition. However, even within the suppressed state, the image is not wholly absent. Tit 3:11 — "he is self-condemned" — the self-condemnation is the conscience's witness operating implicitly against the person, even though not acknowledged. The image-bearing conscience is still present, still functioning as a moral witness — but its witness is suppressed and refused rather than voiced and acted on. The characteristic thus reveals the image's *persistence under suppression*: the moral faculty is still active (hence self-condemnation is possible) but its output is refused (hence the suppression). The image is present but its proper operation is blocked.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest conscience suppression is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-4]** S — Exclusively creaturely (see T0.1.2). God cannot suppress awareness of moral reality; the characteristic is structurally impossible for God. The absence of any divine analogue is complete.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of conscience suppression in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The presence of conscience suppression reveals the divine image in a state of active malfunction at its moral-witness faculty. Jer 2:35 — the person who says "I have not sinned" is not merely ignorant; they are actively claiming a false moral verdict — the image's moral-evaluation capacity is producing incorrect output and refusing correction. Ecc 8:12 — the sinner who "does evil a hundred times" without the fear of God reveals an image from which the God-orientation has been removed at the most fundamental level: "the absence of the fear of God within" (Pass A meaning) names a constitutional God-orientation gap. Jer 16:10 — the people's "what sin have we committed?" reveals an image whose capacity for self-moral-assessment has been so degraded that severe external judgment cannot trigger self-recognition. The image is present (the person is still a moral creature, still accountable) but its operational integrity is profoundly compromised.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Conscience suppression." The name encodes a specific inner-being claim: (1) *Conscience* — the moral-witness faculty that registers sin, guilt, and moral reality — is the faculty at stake; (2) *Suppression* — this faculty is not absent by original design but is being held down, refused, or prevented from operating. The name implies an active dimension: something that could function is being prevented from functioning. This is evidenced in the spectrum of the corpus: Jer 2:35 (active verbal denial — "I have not sinned" — is active suppression), Jer 16:10 (failure to recognise — passive suppression), Ecc 8:12 (ongoing sinning without the fear of God — conscience-absent by habituation), 1Cor 15:34 (ongoing sinning linked to ignorance of God — stupor rather than alert), Tit 3:11 (self-condemned but not self-aware — implicit conscience operating under explicit suppression).

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary term is **cha.ta (H2398)** and its participial/person-type forms, used here not in the confessional mode (CHAR-3) but in the *unacknowledged* mode — sinning that is not named as such by the sinner. Jer 2:35 — "I have not sinned" (*lo chatati*) — the negative first-person perfect is the definitive evidence: the sinning vocabulary is explicitly denied. Jer 16:10 — "what is the sin that we have committed?" — the question-form of *chatanu* (we sinned) applied without self-recognition. The NT terms are **hamartanō (G0264)** (1Cor 15:34 — "do not go on sinning") applied to those in a stupor, and the adjectival *hamartōlos* equivalent in Tit 3:11 ("sinful"). At the definitional level: the sinning vocabulary is present throughout, but conscience's normal response (recognition, confession) is absent. The characteristic is defined by the gap between the objective sinning fact and the person's absent or refused inner recognition.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The name carries primarily *constitutional* implications: the faculty of conscience is a constituted inner capacity, and suppression names what is done to that capacity. The constitutional implication is a malfunction at the conscience-level of the inner person. *Relational* implications are secondary but present: Jer 2:35 — the denial of sin is directed at God ("I am innocent; surely his anger has turned from me") — the suppression operates in the vertical relational context, claiming a standing before God that does not exist. The directional implication runs *inward*: conscience suppression prevents the inward moral recognition that should move toward confession and repentance. It is a blockage of the inward-to-outward moral movement that CHAR-3 (Confession) represents.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus reveals conscience suppression as operating across a spectrum of three kinds. (1) *A condition* (settled inner state): Ecc 8:12 — the sinner "does evil a hundred times" while the fear of God is absent — this is a habituated settled condition, not a single act. Tit 3:11 — "warped and sinful" — a constituted inner character deformation. (2) *An active denial* (speech-act): Jer 2:35 — "I have not sinned" — an explicitly voiced claim; this is more act-like (the person actively asserts innocence). (3) *An absence/blindness* (epistemic gap): Jer 16:10 — the people genuinely do not recognise their sin — this is closer to an epistemic condition (absence of moral self-knowledge) than a deliberate act. The characteristic is thus not one kind of inner-being phenomenon but three related expressions: an active denial, a habituated condition, and an epistemic blindness — all sharing the common feature of the conscience not functioning in its proper registering mode.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The VCG-01 corpus (8 verses, single VCG) has distinct constituent elements. (1) *Active false innocence claim*: Jer 2:35 — the person verbally asserts "I have not sinned" / "I am innocent" — the most active and self-aware mode of suppression. (2) *Genuine self-blindness*: Jer 16:10 — the people do not know what sin they committed — a perceptual blindness rather than a deliberate denial. (3) *Habitual sinning without recognition*: Ecc 8:12 — sin repeated a hundred times without the fear of God — conscience-absent by habit. (4) *Knowledge-absence enabling ongoing sinning*: 1Cor 15:34 — "some have no knowledge of God" — cognitive/spiritual ignorance as the conscience-suppression mechanism. (5) *Implicit self-condemnation not consciously held*: Tit 3:11 — "self-condemned" — the conscience operates but its verdict is not consciously received. (6) *Literal non-moral use*: Judg 20:16 — physical missing of target — no inner-being content. These elements represent a spectrum from active denial to passive absence.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Working description: *Conscience suppression is the inner condition in which the person's moral-witness faculty (conscience) fails to register, actively denies, or refuses to acknowledge their own sinful state. The suppression operates in three modes: active verbal denial of sin (Jer 2:35 — "I have not sinned"), genuine epistemic self-blindness (Jer 16:10 — "what sin have we committed?"), and habituated absence of the fear of God producing persistent sinning without inner recognition (Ecc 8:12; 1Cor 15:34). The person may be objectively sinful (Jer 2:35 — God will bring them to judgment; Tit 3:11 — self-condemned) while subjectively claiming innocence or remaining unaware of their condition. The characteristic is the inverse of CHAR-3 (Confession): where confession voices the conscience's verdict, conscience suppression silences or falsifies it.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The structural opposite is *active conscience* — the inner faculty functioning in its proper moral-witness mode. The corpus names this opposite through contrast: Ecc 8:12 — "those who fear God, because they fear before him" is the structural opposite of the sinner who does evil repeatedly without that fear. 1Cor 15:34 — "wake up from your drunken stupor, as is right" — the command to wake implies the opposite state of wakeful moral alertness. Jer 2:35 — the structural opposite of "I have not sinned" is the confessional "I have sinned" (CHAR-3). The structural opposite is thus: the conscience awake, functioning, and voicing its verdict — which is CHAR-3 (Confession) in its most active form and CHAR-17 (Guilt as inner-being state) in its felt-condition form.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does conscience suppression explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression excludes and resists: (1) *Accurate moral self-knowledge*: Jer 16:10 — the people's failure to recognise their sin excludes the self-knowledge that would name it correctly. (2) *The fear of God*: Ecc 8:12 — "those who fear God" is explicitly contrasted with the sinner who lacks this inner orientation — the fear of God as an operative inner state is excluded by conscience suppression. (3) *Confession*: Jer 2:35 — the active "I have not sinned" is the verbal exclusion of what confession would say; the denial explicitly resists the confessional naming. (4) *Shame and awakening*: 1Cor 15:34 — "I say this to your shame" — Paul's appeal to shame presupposes that the shame-response is what the stupor resists; the continued sinning excludes the shame-and-awakening that right moral awareness would produce.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is conscience suppression not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Three clear boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-2 (Unintentional sinning)*: unintentional sinning involves genuine ignorance of the prohibition at the moment of the act; conscience suppression involves ongoing non-recognition of sin despite available moral knowledge (Jer 16:10 — the people have been told God's words; their ignorance is not innocent). (2) *Not CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent)*: CHAR-5 is where the person recognises sin but refuses to turn from it; CHAR-4 is where the recognition itself is absent or denied. The boundary is at the conscience's registration: CHAR-4 ends and CHAR-5 begins when the person knows what they did and is refusing to turn — not when they are denying they did it. (3) *Not simple ignorance*: Jer 16:10's people are not without moral instruction; Jer 2:35's person is actively claiming innocence. Both cases involve a failure of conscience that goes beyond mere lack of information.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does conscience suppression operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Three evidenced modes. (1) *Active verbal denial*: Jer 2:35 — "I have not sinned" / "I am innocent" — conscience suppression externalised in a speech-act that falsely claims moral standing. This is the most active mode: the person speaks against the conscience's verdict even where it dimly exists. (2) *Perceptual self-blindness*: Jer 16:10 — the people's "what sin have we committed?" — the conscience is not firing at all; the question is genuine. This is the most passive mode: the moral-witness faculty simply does not produce the signal. (3) *Habituation producing moral stupor*: Ecc 8:12; 1Cor 15:34 — repeated sinning or continued sinning under ignorance of God. This is the developmental mode: the conscience has been progressively suppressed through habit and lack of God-knowledge until it operates at minimal capacity.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does conscience suppression operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes. By *context*: the Jeremiah passages (Jer 2:35; 16:10) show conscience suppression operating within the covenantal relationship — Israel claiming innocence before the Lord who has declared judgment. The Ecclesiastes passages (Ecc 2:26; 8:12) show it within the wisdom register — the sinner as a general human type defined by absence of God-orientation. The NT passages (1Cor 15:34; Tit 3:11) show it within the community-of-faith context — believers warned against or described as exhibiting conscience suppression. By *constitutional level*: the active-denial mode (Jer 2:35) operates at the speech/will level; the perceptual blindness mode (Jer 16:10) operates at the cognitive-perceptive level; the habituated absence (Ecc 8:12; 1Cor 15:34) operates at the dispositional/formational level.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does conscience suppression have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes — the most vivid form of conscience suppression is speech-based. Jer 2:35 — "you say, 'I am innocent; surely his anger has turned from me'" and "I have not sinned" — the characteristic is enacted in these spoken claims. The speech-mode operates as a *false declaration of moral standing*: where CHAR-3 (Confession) uses speech to name sin truthfully, conscience suppression uses speech to falsely deny it. The speech-based mode of CHAR-4 is the inverted mirror of CHAR-3's confessional speech: same grammatical form (first-person declaration about one's own moral state), opposite content (denial vs naming).

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when another person exhibits conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus evidences two divine responses to conscience suppression in others. (1) *Prophetic declaration of judgment*: Jer 2:35 — God responds to Israel's false innocence claim with "Behold, I will bring you to judgment" — the immediate response to the denial of sin is the announcement of accountability. (2) *Call to awakening and shame*: 1Cor 15:34 — "Wake up from your drunken stupor, as is right, and do not go on sinning... I say this to your shame" — Paul's response to conscience suppression in the community is an appeal to shame and alertness. Both responses treat conscience suppression as a condition requiring external correction, since the person's inner conscience is not performing its corrective function.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus shows consistent external responses of *divine or prophetic confrontation* with conscience suppression: Jer 2:35 (God announces judgment for the denial), Jer 16:10 (God has already declared judgment via the prophetic message that prompts the question), 1Cor 15:34 (Paul rebukes and calls to shame), Tit 3:11 (the divisive person is to be warned). The consistent response is that the external moral authority (God, prophet, apostle) supplies what the suppressed conscience cannot: the accurate moral verdict that the person's inner faculty is failing to produce.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The Ecclesiastes verses (Ecc 2:26; 8:12) and Job 35:6 do not evidence immediate response scenarios — they describe the state of conscience suppression as an observed condition (the sinner defined by not-pleasing-God, doing evil a hundred times) rather than as an encounter. The corpus is silent on the inner immediate response of the conscience-suppressed person themselves — they do not appear to experience an immediate internal response because the conscience that would generate it is suppressed.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does conscience suppression produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus evidences two sustained trajectories. (1) *Accumulated judgment*: Jer 2:35 — God will bring judgment; the sinning that has gone unacknowledged accumulates as a growing liability before God. Jer 16:10 — the people face "all this great evil" as the consequence of the sin they did not recognise. The inner-being consequence is not felt within the conscience-suppressed state but accumulates externally as judgment. (2) *Moral warping*: Tit 3:11 — "warped and sinful" — sustained conscience suppression produces a constitutional deformation of character. The person who habitually refuses the conscience's verdict becomes progressively distorted inwardly — the warping is the long-term inner-being consequence of sustained suppression.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained conscience suppression establish?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Sustained conscience suppression establishes: (1) *Self-deception as a settled inner orientation*: Jer 2:35 — claiming "I am innocent" moves from a single false claim to a settled self-understanding that has lost contact with moral reality. (2) *Constitutional warping*: Tit 3:11 — "warped" names a deformation of the inner person's basic moral structure — the continued refusal of conscience's witness reshapes the inner person away from moral accuracy. (3) *Spiritual stupor*: 1Cor 15:34 — "drunken stupor" as the sustained state — moral alertness is progressively diminished until the person is in a state analogous to intoxication. (4) *Knowledge-absence*: 1Cor 15:34 — "some have no knowledge of God" — sustained suppression of the conscience and of God-knowledge reinforce each other, producing a settled epistemic blindness about God and self.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The distinction is significant. There is effectively no immediate internal response within the conscience-suppressed person (the conscience is not producing the alerting signal). The sustained effect, by contrast, is substantial: accumulated judgment (Jer 2:35), constitutional warping (Tit 3:11), spiritual stupor (1Cor 15:34), and the progressive loss of moral self-knowledge (Jer 16:10 — a condition apparently reached over generational time). The sustained effect is what conscience suppression *does to the person* from the outside-in (judgment accumulates) and from the inside-out (the person is progressively deformed).

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Three evidenced enabling conditions. (1) *Absence of the fear of God*: Ecc 8:12 — the sinner does evil a hundred times without the fear of God within — the fear of God's absence is the primary enabling condition for conscience-absent sinning. (2) *Self-interest in innocence*: Jer 2:35 — the person claims "I am innocent; surely his anger has turned from me" — the desire to believe oneself innocent (and free from divine anger) is an enabling condition for the active denial. (3) *Ignorance of God*: 1Cor 15:34 — "some have no knowledge of God" — the cognitive-spiritual absence of God-knowledge enables and sustains the stupor of continued sinning. These three (absence of fear of God, self-interested innocence-claim, ignorance of God) are the inner conditions that produce or sustain conscience suppression.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus evidences the preventive conditions by contrast. (1) *Fear of God*: Ecc 8:12 — those who "fear God, because they fear before him" — the fear of God as an operative inner orientation is the primary preventive against conscience-absent sinning. (2) *Knowledge of God*: 1Cor 15:34 — "some have no knowledge of God; I say this to your shame" — the call to "wake up" implies that knowledge of God, restored, is the preventive against the stupor. (3) *Responsive awakening to shame*: 1Cor 15:34 — Paul's appeal to shame as a corrective implies that the capacity for shame, if activated, can break the stupor. The blocking conditions are the positive counterparts: God-knowledge, fear of God, and moral shame-responsiveness.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters conscience suppression in themselves but does not resolve it?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The 8-verse corpus does not directly evidence this scenario — the persons exhibiting conscience suppression in the corpus are not described as encountering and then refusing to resolve it (that would be closer to CHAR-5 — Refusal to repent). The corpus describes conscience suppression as an *already-settled* inner condition: Jer 2:35 (already claiming innocence), Jer 16:10 (already failing to recognise), Ecc 8:12 (already sinning a hundred times), Tit 3:11 (already warped). The inner-being state of the person within conscience suppression is: moral self-perception that does not accurately register one's own sinfulness — a state characterised by false peace (Jer 2:35 — "his anger has turned from me") or genuine puzzlement (Jer 16:10 — "what sin?").

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of conscience suppression?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary inner-being dimension is **conscience** — specifically conscience in its *malfunctioning, absent, or refused mode*. The characteristic is constitutively a conscience-failure: the moral-witness faculty (conscience) that should register sin, produce guilt, and drive confession either does not register (Jer 16:10; Ecc 8:12; 1Cor 15:34) or is explicitly refused/denied (Jer 2:35; Tit 3:11 implicit). The characteristic is named for this faculty precisely because conscience is what is suppressed.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The conscience-primary classification is supported by: (1) Jer 2:35 — the explicit denial of sin is an anti-confessional conscience-act: the conscience that should say "I have sinned" is made to say "I have not sinned"; (2) Jer 16:10 — "what sin have we committed?" — the question reveals a conscience that is not firing its sin-recognition signal; (3) Ecc 8:12 — sinning "a hundred times" without the fear of God reveals conscience-absent or conscience-numbed habituation; (4) 1Cor 15:34 — the "drunken stupor" is explicitly a conscience-alertness failure; (5) Tit 3:11 — "self-condemned" — the person's own conscience implicitly witnesses against them (the self-condemnation is a conscience-verdict they do not consciously receive).

---

**T1.8.3 — Does conscience suppression carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Cognition/moral knowledge*: Jer 16:10 — the failure to recognise sin involves a cognitive-perceptual failure (the person genuinely does not know). 1Cor 15:34 — "no knowledge of God" — cognitive dimension. (2) *Will*: Jer 2:35 — the active denial requires a will that chooses the false claim over honest acknowledgment. The will is engaged in the suppression-as-active-denial mode. (3) *Moral evaluation*: Ecc 8:12 — the sinner who evaluates his long life as evidence that all is well has a moral evaluation faculty producing incorrect assessments. These secondary dimensions do not compete with the conscience-primary classification; they describe the channels through which conscience suppression operates.

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — **Social psychology's self-serving bias and moral disengagement theory** (Bandura) provide the most useful lens. Bandura's moral disengagement mechanisms — especially *moral justification*, *advantageous comparison*, and *attribution of blame to others* — illuminate how persons suppress conscience by cognitively reconstructing their acts as acceptable or by attributing fault elsewhere. 1Cor 15:34's "knowledge of God" absence maps onto the framework's finding that moral disengagement is sustained by the absence of the internalised moral reference point.

**T7.3.2 — Where the framework illuminates the verse evidence — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Bandura's moral disengagement illuminates Jer 2:35's two-step structure: (1) "I am innocent" — moral justification: the person reconstructs their moral status as blameless; (2) "surely his anger has turned from me" — attribution: the person interprets current circumstances (absence of visible punishment) as confirming the innocence claim. Together these are a classic moral disengagement sequence: reconstruct the self as innocent → interpret the environment as confirming the reconstruction. The self-serving bias framework illuminates Ecc 8:12's prosperity observation: the sinner interprets prolonged life as moral vindication — a classic self-serving outcome-attribution pattern.

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary divergence: moral disengagement theory (Bandura) treats conscience suppression as a *cognitive self-regulatory process* — the person manages their self-image through cognitive mechanisms. The verse evidence treats it as a *relational-moral condition*: Jer 2:35 — the false innocence claim is not merely a cognitive self-management strategy but a claim made *before God*, who will bring judgment *for the specific claim*. The framework sees the suppression as self-directed (managing one's own moral self-concept); the verse evidence sees it as God-directed (making a false claim in a relational context where God is the witness and judge). The divergence reveals that the verse evidence treats conscience suppression as *theologically constituted* — its gravity is determined by the relational reality before God, not by the person's inner cognitive economy.
