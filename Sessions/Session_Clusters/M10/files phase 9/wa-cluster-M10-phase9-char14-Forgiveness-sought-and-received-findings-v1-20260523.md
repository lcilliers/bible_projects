## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The 34-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who provides the means of atonement*: Exo 29:36 — the daily sin offering is God's prescribed institution; Lev 23:27/28 — the Day of Atonement is God's appointed holy convocation; he has established the framework within which forgiveness is available. (2) *God as the one who does not count trespasses*: 2Cor 5:19 — "in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses against them" — the divine disposition is toward non-imputation, not toward accumulating the record against the person. (3) *God as the one who provides blood-atonement*: Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — God himself provides the atoning blood rather than requiring the creature to supply it. (4) *God as the one whose forgiveness has eschatological scope*: Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man" — God's forgiveness has universal potential scope, with only one exception (Mar 3:29 — blasphemy against the Holy Spirit).

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute forgiveness sought and received to God?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — on the giving side. God is the one who forgives: 2Cor 5:19 — "God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses"; Col 2:13 — "God made alive together with him, having forgiven us all our trespasses"; Eph 1:7 — "the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace." The seeking side is human (the person seeks forgiveness); the granting side is divine. The characteristic thus involves both divine action (granting) and human action (seeking); Scripture attributes the granting explicitly to God.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's seeking forgiveness, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — God never seeks forgiveness — he has no offense-record requiring another's pardon. The silence confirms the asymmetry at the characteristic's core: the seeking is constitutively creaturely (the fallen creature's movement toward God), while the granting is constitutively divine. This asymmetry reveals that the characteristic cannot be abstracted from the God-creature relationship: it names a specific direction of moral movement (creature → God in seeking; God → creature in granting) that requires both parties to be what they are.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose forgiveness sought and received serves in the human person?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic serves the purpose of *restoring the God-human relationship ruptured by sin*. Lev 16:30 — "you shall be clean before the Lord from all your sins" — the purpose is restored purity before God; Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... God made alive" — the purpose is life where death held sway; Act 2:38 — repentance and baptism → forgiveness → receipt of the Holy Spirit — the purpose is access to the Spirit who was blocked by the sin-record. The characteristic thus serves the restoration of the person's full relational capacity before God.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. The priestly atonement system (VCG-01/03) presupposes accumulated sin; the forgiveness declarations (VCG-02) address the specific sin-record requiring removal. Lev 4:3 — "if it is the anointed priest who sins... he shall offer... for the sin that he has committed" — the whole framework is triggered by committed sin, which belongs to the fallen condition. The kip.pu.rim atonement system is God's provision for the fallen condition, not a designed feature of unfallen creation.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the OT atonement system itself is eschatologically oriented. VCG-01's kip.pu.rim sacrifices are annual, repeated, provisional — Exo 30:10 — "once in the year throughout your generations" — each annual cycle points forward to a definitive resolution not yet accomplished. Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — the new covenant's blood-forgiveness is the eschatological fulfillment the OT system anticipated: a once-for-all atonement that permanently resolves the annual cycle's provisional character.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does forgiveness sought and received express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic expresses the divine image through the image-bearer's capacity for *repentant return to God*: only a being made for God-relationship can seek forgiveness from God. Exo 32:30 — Moses "will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin" — the intercessory seeking is the image-bearer exercising the mediatorial capacity that belongs to the image's vocation. Act 2:38 — the capacity to repent, be baptised, and receive the Holy Spirit names the image-bearer's full moral-and-relational restoration capacity, which only makes sense for a being whose image-bearing orientation is toward God.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is this characteristic shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The seeking dimension is exclusively creaturely; the granting dimension is divine. The characteristic is thus relationally shared: God grants (2Cor 5:19; Col 2:13; Eph 1:7) and the creature seeks (Exo 32:30 — Moses; Act 2:38 — repentance; Act 10:43 — belief). The characteristic requires both poles: without the creature's seeking it is not enacted; without God's granting it is not completed. It is the most explicitly bi-directional characteristic in M10.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The presence of the seeking dimension reveals the image in its *fallen-but-recoverable* condition: the image bears the sin-record (CHAR-13) that requires forgiveness, is capable of recognising this (conscience), and has the volitional capacity to turn toward God in seeking (Act 2:38 — "repent"). Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... God made alive" — the image's condition before forgiveness is spiritual death; the forgiveness-received transforms the condition to life. The characteristic's presence thus reveals both the depth of the image's fallen condition (requiring forgiveness) and the fullness of its recovery capacity (able to seek, receive, and be made alive).

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the most concentrated typological evidence in M10. The entire VCG-01 kip.pu.rim system (12 verses) is typological: the Day of Atonement (Lev 23:27/28; 25:9), the daily sin offerings (Exo 29:36; 30:10), and the priestly bearing of iniquity (Lev 10:17) all function as institutional types pointing toward the antitypical atoning work of Christ. Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — is the antitype declaration: the covenant blood replaces the animal blood; the permanent forgiveness replaces the annual provisional covering.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — OT institutional type → NT Christological antitype. The direction is: the annual kip.pu.rim cycle (OT type) → the once-for-all blood of the new covenant (NT antitype — Mat 26:28). The priestly mediation (Lev 10:17 — priest bears the congregation's iniquity) → Christ's high-priestly mediation (the broader NT theological context, evidenced in this corpus by 2Cor 5:19 — God in Christ reconciling). The atonement money bringing Israel to remembrance (Exo 30:16) → Christ's name through which everyone who believes receives forgiveness (Act 10:43). The typological direction is consistently from OT ritual institution to NT Christological fulfillment.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Typological use is extensively evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Forgiveness sought and received." Three signals. (1) *Forgiveness*: the moral-relational transaction at stake — not merely guilt-relief but the objective removal of the sin-record that separates the person from God; (2) *Sought*: the inner movement originates in the person — a volitional turning toward God to appeal for what only God can grant; Act 2:38 — "repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness of your sins" — the seeking is a commanded human movement; (3) *Received*: the characteristic names completion — not merely seeking but the divine response of granting; Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven" — the declaration closes the transaction. The name's compound form ("sought and received") signals the characteristic's bi-directional structure: the human movement of seeking and the divine act of granting are both necessary constituents.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Four term-groups. (1) **kip.pu.rim (H3725)** (VCG-01 — 12 verses): the noun-plural of kāpar — "coverings/atonements." The root kāpar means "to cover, to atone, to make expiation" — the mechanism is concealment/covering of the sin-record. The plural form names not a single act but the institution: the Day of Atonement as a structured series of covering acts. (2) **chat.tat (H2403B/H2403H)** (VCG-01/03): the sin-offering as the ritual vehicle of seeking forgiveness — the animal bears the sin that the person brings. (3) **cha.ta.ah (H2401)** (VCG-02): the sin named before God in the seeking-context (Exo 32:30/31 — Moses naming the great sin). (4) **hamartia (G0266) / hamartēma (G0265) / paraptōma (G3900)** (VCG-02): NT terms for the sin/trespasses whose forgiveness is declared through Christ's blood (Mat 26:28; Mar 3:28/29; Eph 1:7; Col 2:13).

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — All three. *Directional*: "sought" names a movement from the person toward God; "received" names the return movement from God to the person. The directional structure is toward-God then from-God — a bilateral movement that constitutes the full forgiveness-event. *Relational*: the characteristic is constitutively relational — it requires the person-God relationship as its operative field; forgiveness cannot be sought from or granted by anyone else (Mar 2:7 context — "who can forgive sins but God alone?" — referenced in CHAR-11 but structurally operative here). *Constitutional*: Act 2:38 — repentance + baptism → forgiveness → Spirit-reception — the constitutional change (receiving the Spirit) is the consequence of the characteristic's completion.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is forgiveness sought and received?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The seeking dimension is a *volitional-relational act*: the will's turn toward God in appeal. Exo 32:30 — "I will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin" — Moses's going up is a volitional movement; Act 2:38 — "repent and be baptized" — the repentance is an inner volitional turn. The receiving dimension is a *relational-condition change*: the person's standing before God is altered by the divine declaration. Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven" — the declaration changes the woman's relational condition before God. Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... made alive" — the received forgiveness is a constitutional condition change. The characteristic is thus a *volitional act (seeking) producing a relational-condition change (receiving)*.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The corpus reveals four constituent elements. (1) *The inner recognition*: Lev 4:14 — "when the sin which they have committed becomes known" — the sin must be recognised before it can be sought-for; (2) *The seeking act*: Exo 32:30 — Moses going up; Act 2:38 — repenting; Act 10:43 — believing; (3) *The mediatorial mechanism*: VCG-01/03 — the priestly/sacrificial system; VCG-02 — Christ's blood and name; (4) *The divine declaration or grant*: Lev 16:30 — "you shall be clean before the Lord"; Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven"; Col 2:13 — God "having forgiven us all our trespasses."

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Working description: *Forgiveness sought and received (CHAR-14) is the bi-directional characteristic in which the inner person moves volitionally toward God in appeal for the removal of the sin-record (the seeking dimension), and God responds by declaring, granting, or enacting the covering/removal of that record (the receiving dimension). The corpus evidences this through three registers: (1) The Levitical kip.pu.rim atonement system (VCG-01 — 12 verses): institutionalised annual and occasional seeking through the prescribed sacrifice and priestly mediation; (2) The Levitical sin-offering (VCG-03 — 10 verses): individual and communal seeking through the chat.tat offering system when specific sins become known; (3) Christological forgiveness declaration (VCG-02 — 12 verses): seeking through repentance and faith and receiving through Christ's atoning blood and authoritative declaration. The characteristic's primary anchor is Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — naming the definitive once-for-all mechanism that replaces the annual kip.pu.rim cycle.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of forgiveness sought and received?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The structural opposite is the *unforgiven sin-record remaining in force*. On the human side: the failure to seek — Lev 4:14 implies that when sin "becomes known" and no remedy is sought, the record remains; Jos 24:19 (CHAR-13 corpus) — God "will not forgive your transgressions or your sins" names the condition of unforgiveness. On the divine side: Mar 3:29 — "whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit never has forgiveness, but is guilty of an eternal sin" — the single case in the corpus where the seeking-and-receiving is permanently foreclosed: the eternal sin that has no forgiveness names the absolute structural opposite.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does forgiveness sought and received explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes: (1) *Self-generated forgiveness*: the priestly mediation (Lev 10:17 — priest bears the congregation's iniquity) and Christ's blood (Mat 26:28) both establish that forgiveness requires an external mediatorial mechanism; self-forgiveness is excluded as an option; (2) *Forgiveness without seeking*: Act 2:38 — "repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness" — the grammatical structure makes repentance the condition of forgiveness-reception; passive receipt without seeking is not evidenced; (3) *Forgiveness for the eternal sin*: Mar 3:29 — the blasphemy against the Holy Spirit resists all seeking; the characteristic cannot operate in that direction.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is forgiveness sought and received not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-3 (Confession)*: confession is one of the seeking-acts that may accompany forgiveness-seeking (Num 5:7 in CHAR-13's context; Psa 32:5), but it is the speech-act of acknowledgment rather than the full characteristic of seeking-and-receiving forgiveness; CHAR-3 is a component that may precede CHAR-14. (2) *Not CHAR-13 (Sin as divine record)*: CHAR-13 is the record that requires forgiveness; CHAR-14 is the seeking and receiving of that record's removal — CHAR-13 is what CHAR-14 addresses. (3) *Not CHAR-11 (Sin as universal condition)*: CHAR-11 is the structural condition all persons stand in; CHAR-14 is the specific seeking-and-receiving event that addresses one dimension of that condition.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does forgiveness sought and received operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Three distinct inner modes. (1) *As volitional turning toward God*: Act 2:38 — "repent" (metanoeō — a change of mind and direction); Exo 32:30 — "I will go up to the Lord" — the will's movement toward God as the source of forgiveness is the primary inner mode; (2) *As self-affliction in seeking*: Lev 23:27 — "you shall afflict yourselves" — the Day of Atonement requires inner self-affliction as the seeking posture; (3) *As faith receiving the declaration*: Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through his name" — belief is the inner receptive mode in the NT register; Luk 7:47 — "her sins... are forgiven — for she loved much" — love is the inner receptive disposition evidencing that forgiveness has been received.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does forgiveness sought and received operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — significantly. (1) *Levitical priestly context* (VCG-01): the seeking operates through prescribed ritual (bringing the animal, laying hands, priestly action); the individual's inner seeking is mediated through the institutional framework; (2) *Individual-sin context* (VCG-03 — Lev 4:3/14/5:6/10): the seeking is triggered when the sin "becomes known" — an individual recognition moment generates the seeking-event; (3) *Intercessory context* (Exo 32:30/31 — VCG-02/03): Moses seeks forgiveness on behalf of others — the seeking is not for the seeker's own record but for another's; (4) *Christological-declarative context* (VCG-02 NT): seeking is through repentance/faith and receiving is through direct declaration (Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven").

---

**T1.4.3 — Does forgiveness sought and received have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — on both sides. (1) *Human-side speech*: Exo 32:31 — Moses verbally names the sin before God ("Alas, this people has sinned a great sin"); the speech-act of naming the sin before God is part of the seeking; (2) *Divine-side speech*: Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven" — the forgiveness declaration is a speech-act; Mat 26:28 — "for this is my blood of the covenant... poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — the institution-declaration at the Last Supper is speech announcing the mechanism of forgiveness; Act 10:43 — "to him all the prophets bear witness" — the prophetic testimony to forgiveness is a speech-tradition.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response to receiving forgiveness?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Luk 7:47 — "she loved much" — the woman's love is the most immediate inner-being response to received forgiveness: the magnitude of forgiveness received produces a proportional love-response. Col 2:13 — "made alive" — the immediate inner response to forgiveness received is new life displacing prior death. Lev 16:30 — "you shall be clean before the Lord from all your sins" — the purity-status is the immediate declared condition following the atonement's completion. The immediate inner response thus ranges from the affective (love — Luk 7:47), through the vital (alive — Col 2:13), to the relational-status (clean — Lev 16:30).

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Variable. Love (Luk 7:47), life (Col 2:13), cleanness (Lev 16:30), and Spirit-reception (Act 2:38 — "you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit") are all immediate responses named in the corpus. The variation reflects the different registers: the Levitical register names ritual purity-status (clean); the NT personal register names the affective and vital responses (love; alive); the Pentecost register names Spirit-reception. The consistent underlying element is relational restoration: forgiveness received restores the person to full standing before God, but this restoration is experienced differently across the corpus's registers.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-14]** E — For the kip.pu.rim atonement passages (VCG-01 — Exo 29:36; 30:10/16; Lev 23:27/28; 25:9; Num 5:8; 29:11; 2Ch 29:23/24), the immediate inner response of the individual receiving the atonement's benefit is not described. The corpus characterises the ritual mechanism and its purpose but not the individual worshipper's moment-by-moment inner experience of the atonement's completion.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does forgiveness sought and received produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Three sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Love as sustained orientation*: Luk 7:47 — "he who is forgiven little, loves little" — the magnitude of sustained love is proportional to the magnitude of forgiveness received; forgiveness produces love as an ongoing inner orientation; (2) *Life replacing death*: Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... made alive" — the life produced by forgiveness received is a sustained constitutional change, not a momentary affect; (3) *Spirit indwelling*: Act 2:38 — "you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" — the Spirit's indwelling following forgiveness is the most significant sustained inner-being consequence in the NT register.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained forgiveness-reception establish?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — (1) *Cleanness before God*: Lev 16:30 — "clean before the Lord from all your sins" — the established condition of ritual-relational purity; (2) *Love-orientation*: Luk 7:47 — love as the sustained inner disposition of the forgiven person; (3) *Life-orientation*: Col 2:13 — made alive as the constitutional orientation replacing the death-in-trespasses state; (4) *Redemption-identity*: Col 1:14 — "in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins" — redemption as the established identity of the forgiven person; Eph 1:7 — "in him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace."

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes. The immediate response is the declaration or initial condition change (Lev 16:30 — clean; Luk 7:48 — sins forgiven; Col 2:13 — made alive). The sustained effect is the ongoing inner orientation that forgiveness produces over time: love (Luk 7:47 — the "much-love" that flows from much-forgiveness), Spirit indwelling (Act 2:38 — the permanent gift), and redemption-identity (Col 1:14; Eph 1:7 — the "we have redemption" that is a present permanent possession). The sustained effect deepens and extends what the immediate response initiates.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine forgiveness-seeking?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Recognition that sin has been committed*: Lev 4:14 — "when the sin which they have committed becomes known" — the recognition moment is the prerequisite; (2) *Repentance*: Act 2:38 — "repent... for the forgiveness of your sins" — the inner volitional turn is the explicit enabling condition in the NT; (3) *Faith*: Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins" — belief/faith is the specific inner condition through which forgiveness is received in the NT. Additionally: Lev 23:27 — self-affliction as the inner-posture of seeking on the Day of Atonement — the humbling of the self is an enabling condition for the ritual's efficacy.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent reception of forgiveness?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Two blocking conditions. (1) *The unrecognised sin*: Lev 4:14 — sin "becomes known" only when it is recognised; while unrecognised, the seeking cannot begin (the sin must first be "made known" — T1.7.1); (2) *The eternal-sin condition*: Mar 3:29 — "whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit never has forgiveness, but is guilty of an eternal sin" — the eternal-sin condition is the one inner condition in the corpus that permanently forecloses forgiveness-reception. Lev 10:19 — Aaron's question about whether eating the sin offering while personally stricken with grief "would the Lord have approved" implies that a wrong inner state in the officiant can block the ritual's efficacy.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who seeks forgiveness but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Mar 3:29 — "guilty of an eternal sin" — the person guilty of the eternal sin is in a state of permanent unresolved guilt before God; the seeking cannot break through the condition. For the broader case (seeking without receiving), the corpus is largely silent: the pattern evidenced is that genuine seeking (repentance + faith — Act 2:38/10:43) produces reception. The case of seeking without receiving is not directly evidenced beyond the eternal-sin exception of Mar 3:29.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The primary dimension is **will/volition in its God-directed turning mode** — the will's movement toward God in seeking forgiveness. Act 2:38 — "repent" (metanoeō — change of mind + direction); Exo 32:30 — "I will go up to the Lord" — the volitional ascent; Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives" — faith as the will's trust-act directed toward Christ. The seeking dimension is constitutively volitional; the receiving dimension is relational-condition change that follows the volitional seeking. The will's God-directed movement is the primary inner-being faculty engaged.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — (1) Act 2:38 — "repent" — repentance is a volitional act named first in the seeking-sequence; (2) Exo 32:30 — "I will go up to the Lord" — the first-person future volitional statement; (3) Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives" — belief as the volitional trust-act; (4) Lev 23:27 — "you shall afflict yourselves" — the deliberate inner self-affliction is a volitional posture of seeking; (5) 2Ch 29:23 — "they laid their hands on them" — the hand-laying is the volitional act of identification with the offering's purpose.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes. (1) *Affective dimension*: Luk 7:47 — love as the seeking-dimension's inner disposition and the receiving-dimension's inner response; Lev 23:27 — self-affliction as the affective-penitential posture; (2) *Relational-standing dimension*: Lev 16:30 — cleanness before God; Col 1:14/2:13 — redemption identity and made-alive state; (3) *Spiritual-faculty dimension*: Act 2:38 — the Spirit's reception as the consequence of forgiveness received — a secondary inner-being consequence that opens a new dimension of the person's constitution.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is forgiveness sought and received explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — through the Spirit's reception as the consequence of forgiveness. Act 2:38 — "repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" — the Spirit's indwelling follows forgiveness and is its most direct spirit-level consequence. The forgiveness operates at the spirit level precisely because it opens the person to the Spirit who was previously blocked by the sin-record. Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... God made alive" — the death-to-life movement is a spirit-level restoration; the spiritual death produced by sin is reversed by forgiveness at the same constitutional level.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does forgiveness sought and received originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-14]** E — The seeking dimension does not primarily originate at the spirit level: it originates in the will's volitional turning (Act 2:38 — "repent"), in the conscience's recognition (Lev 4:14 — "becomes known"), and in the community's ritual response. The receiving dimension, however, has a spirit-level consequence (Act 2:38 — Spirit received) and is enacted by God's sovereign act (2Cor 5:19 — "God was reconciling the world to himself"). The divine granting of forgiveness thus has a spirit-level source (God) even if the human seeking does not originate there.

---

**T3.1.1 — Does forgiveness sought and received engage the perceptive faculty?**
