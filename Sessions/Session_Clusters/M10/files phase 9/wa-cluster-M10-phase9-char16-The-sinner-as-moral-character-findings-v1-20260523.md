## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The 64-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who names and categorises the sinner*: 1Sa 15:18 — "Go, devote to destruction the sinners, the Amalekites" — God designates the Amalekites as "sinners" as the basis for their destruction; the divine naming is the authoritative moral verdict; Amo 9:8 — "the eyes of the Lord God are upon the sinful kingdom" — God's omniscient gaze identifies the sinful character of a people. (2) *God as the one whose holiness defines the sinner's incompatibility*: Isa 33:14 — "who among us can dwell with the consuming fire?" — the sinner's dread reveals God as a consuming holiness that the sinner-identity cannot survive; Psa 1:5 — sinners excluded from the congregation of the righteous under God's ordering. (3) *God as the one who pursues sinners for redemption*: Psa 25:8 — "he instructs sinners in the way"; Mat 9:13 — "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners"; 1Ti 1:15 — "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners." (4) *God as the one who constitutes sinner-status through Adam*: Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners" — God's judgment of Adam's act imputes the sinner-identity to all.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute the sinner-as-moral-character to God?**

**[CHAR-16]** S — No verse in the 64-verse corpus attributes the sinner-identity to God. God appears as the one who names sinners (1Sa 15:18; Amo 9:8), instructs them (Psa 25:8), came to save them (1Ti 1:15), and is separated from them (Heb 7:26 — "separated from sinners"). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The silence on God possessing the sinner-identity is absolute and defining: Heb 7:26 — "holy, innocent, unstained, separated from sinners, and exalted above the heavens" — Christ's high-priestly character is defined precisely by his total separation from and non-participation in the sinner-identity. The silence reveals that the sinner-as-moral-character is constitutively the anti-image: it is the condition that arises when the image-bearer's moral character becomes defined by what God is not (sinless, holy, righteous). The characteristic exists only in the contrast with God's character.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose the sinner-identity serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-16]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for the sinner-as-moral-character identity. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition category: Psa 1:5 — excluded from the congregation of the righteous; Isa 33:14 — seized with dread before God's holiness. No verse presents the sinner-identity as a designed feature.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design or a response to the fallen condition?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners" — the sinner-identity entered through Adam's transgression; it presupposes the fall. Luk 5:32 — "I have not come to call the righteous but sinners to repentance" — the need for calling presupposes the fallen condition in which the sinner-identity is the default. Isa 13:9 — the day of the Lord's destruction of sinners presupposes the accumulated sinful condition that preceded it.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that the sinner-as-moral-character is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — two eschatological directions. (1) *Positive — repentance and return*: Luk 15:7 — "there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons" — the sinner-identity is oriented toward its own reversal through repentance; Psa 51:13 — "sinners will return to you" — the eschatological direction for the sinner is return to God; (2) *Negative — judgment and destruction*: Isa 13:9 — sinners destroyed on the day of the Lord; Psa 104:35 — "let sinners be consumed from the earth"; Jude 15 — "to execute judgment on all... ungodly sinners."

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does the sinner-as-moral-character express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-as-moral-character expresses the divine image through its *inverted moral characterisation*: the image-bearer is designed for a constituted moral identity oriented toward God; the sinner-identity is that constituted moral identity oriented away from God and toward sin. Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man, O Lord" — Peter's recognition of his sinner-identity in the presence of the Holy One is the clearest image-expression: only a being made for God-relationship can stand in the relational field of that holiness and recognise itself as a "sinful man." The capacity for moral self-identification (whether as sinner or as righteous) belongs to the image.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is this characteristic shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Exclusively creaturely. Heb 7:26 — Christ is "separated from sinners" — the sinner-identity is constitutively absent from Christ and therefore from God. Rom 5:19 — "the many were made sinners" through Adam's disobedience — the making-sinners applies to humanity in Adam, not to God. The sinner-identity is the fallen creature's constituted moral character, not shared with the divine nature.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of the sinner-identity reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The presence of the sinner-identity reveals the image in its *characterologically defined fallen state*: the image-bearer is not merely doing sinful acts (CHAR-1) or under a sin-condition (CHAR-11) but has become a recognisable moral type — "the sinner." Gen 13:13 — the men of Sodom "were wicked, great sinners against the Lord" — the image-bearer has been constituted as a recognisable moral category of wrongdoing. This is the image's deepest fallen characterological expression: the person has been formed into what they do — sinning has become who they are.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use the sinner-as-moral-character typologically?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes. Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous" — the sinner-identity constituted through Adam is the type that Christ's righteousness reverses; Adam as the type of the sinner-constituting one, Christ as the antitype of the righteous-constituting one. 1Ti 1:15/16 — Paul as the "foremost sinner" functions paradigmatically: the mercy shown to him (the worst case) becomes a demonstration for all future believers that Christ's saving mission extends to the extreme sinner-type.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The direction is from Adamic sinner-constitution (negative type) to Christological righteous-constitution (positive antitype). Rom 5:19's parallel structure — "made sinners... will be made righteous" — establishes the typological reversal: the sinner-identity constituted through Adam's disobedience is the type that Christ's obedience reverses into righteousness-identity. 1Ti 1:15/16's Paul-as-foremost-sinner is an individual typological demonstration: Paul's transformation from worst sinner to apostle is the pattern for all future sinners' reception of mercy.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The programme names this characteristic "The sinner as moral character." Three signals. (1) *The sinner*: the noun-form — not "sinning" (the act) or "sinfulness" (the condition) but "the sinner" as a person-type; the definite article signals a recognisable category. (2) *As moral character*: the characteristic is a constituted inner moral identity — who the person *is* at their moral core, not what they have done or stand in. (3) *Moral*: the characterisation is specifically moral, not social or cultural — though the social dimension (VCG-01's exclusion and stigma) is a consequence. The name signals: sin has become a defining feature of the person's moral identity, not merely their behaviour or legal standing.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Two term-groups. (1) **chat.ta (H2400)** — noun form (OT — 14 verses in VCG-03): the agent-noun "one who sins habitually, a sinful person." The noun form distinguishes from the verb (cha.ta — the act): chat.ta names the person constituted by sinning rather than the sinning act. Gen 13:13 — "great sinners" (*chat.ta.im gĕdōlîm*) — the plural noun names a category of persons; 1Sa 15:18 — "the sinners, the Amalekites" — chat.ta is used as an identity descriptor applied to a people. (2) **hamartōlos (G0268)** — noun/adjective (NT — 50 verses across all VCGs): "sinful person, sinner." The Greek term functions both as a noun (the sinner — Luk 7:37) and as an adjective (sinful — Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man"; Mar 8:38 — "sinful generation"). Both terms are agent-nouns naming the person constituted by sin rather than the act itself.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — All three. *Directional*: Jam 5:20 — "whoever brings back a sinner from his wandering" — the sinner is characterized by directional drift away from God; the direction of the sinner's moral character is away-from-God. *Relational*: Psa 1:1 — "the way of sinners" as a social-moral class whose company shapes inner orientation; Luk 15:2 — Pharisees' complaint that Jesus "receives sinners and eats with them" — the relational dimension is constitutive. *Constitutional*: Rom 5:19 — "the many were made sinners" — the constitutional imputation of the sinner-identity through Adam's disobedience names this as a constituted inner status, not merely a behavioural descriptor.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is the sinner as moral character?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The corpus reveals a *constituted moral identity* — the person not merely performing sinful acts but having become a recognisable moral type. The three VCG registers name it differently: (1) VCG-03 — extreme habitual wrongdoers (Gen 13:13 — Sodom; 1Sa 15:18 — Amalekites) — the sinner-identity as totality of moral corruption; (2) VCG-01 — social-moral category carrying exclusion and stigma (Psa 1:5 — excluded from congregation; Mat 9:10 — social category at table) — the sinner-identity as publicly recognised status; (3) VCG-02 — constituted/imputed status (Rom 5:19 — made sinners through Adam) — the sinner-identity as formally constituted moral standing. Across all three registers: a recognisable moral category, not merely a description of acts.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The corpus reveals three constituent elements corresponding to the three VCGs. (1) *The inner moral disposition* (VCG-03): the extreme sinner's total moral character is oriented toward sin — Gen 13:13's "great sinners against the Lord" names an inner orientation of persistent rebellion; (2) *The social-relational status* (VCG-01): the sinner-identity is publicly recognised and carries social exclusion — Psa 1:5; Mat 9:10/11; Luk 7:37; (3) *The constituted/imputed standing* (VCG-02): Rom 5:19 — "made sinners" — the status is formally constituted through Adam's representative act, operating independently of individual moral character formation.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Working description: *The sinner as moral character (CHAR-16) is the constitutive inner moral identity of the person defined by sin — not primarily the act (CHAR-1) or the structural condition (CHAR-11) but what kind of person they are at their moral core. The corpus evidences three registers: (1) The extreme moral type (VCG-03 — 14 verses): persons whose moral character is thoroughly defined by sin — Sodom's "great sinners against the Lord" (Gen 13:13), the Amalekites devoted to destruction (1Sa 15:18); (2) The social-moral category (VCG-01 — 30 verses): the sinner as a publicly recognised class whose status carries exclusion from community and stigma — "tax collectors and sinners" as social pairing (Mat 9:10/11); (3) The constituted/imputed identity (VCG-02 — 20 verses): the sinner-identity as formally established status — "many were made sinners" through Adam (Rom 5:19); "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners" (1Ti 1:15). The primary anchor is Rom 5:19 for the constituted dimension and Gen 13:13 for the extreme-type dimension.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of the sinner as moral character?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The structural opposite is the *righteous person* as the contrasting moral-character type. Psa 1:1/5 — the righteous man who walks not in the way of sinners; excluded sinners stand opposite to "the congregation of the righteous"; Pro 13:21 — "disaster pursues sinners, but the righteous are rewarded with good" — the righteous/sinner opposition is explicit. In the NT: Mat 9:13 — "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" — the righteous and the sinner are the two poles; Rom 5:19 — "the many were made sinners... the many will be made righteous" — the constituted sinner-identity finds its direct opposite in the constituted righteous-identity.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does the sinner-identity explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-identity explicitly excludes: (1) *Standing in judgment*: Psa 1:5 — "sinners will not stand in the judgment" — the sinner-identity is incompatible with standing under divine scrutiny; (2) *Membership in the righteous congregation*: Psa 1:5 — "nor sinners in the congregation of the righteous"; (3) *Divine responsiveness*: Joh 9:31 — "God does not listen to sinners" — the sinner-identity cuts off divine hearing; (4) *Association with Christ's holiness*: Heb 7:26 — Christ "separated from sinners" — the sinner-identity is structurally incompatible with the high priestly holiness.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is the sinner as moral character not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the discrete sinning act; CHAR-16 is the constituted moral identity produced through and expressed in such acts; a person may commit sinning acts (CHAR-1) without yet being constituted as "the sinner" as a recognisable moral type. (2) *Not CHAR-11 (Sin as universal condition)*: CHAR-11 is the structural ontological condition all persons share; CHAR-16 is the characterological identity that some persons have been constituted into or recognised as — not all persons are called "sinners" in the technical VCG sense. (3) *Not CHAR-13 (Sin as divine record)*: CHAR-13 is the accumulated moral account before God; CHAR-16 is the person-type that account has produced — the sinner is the person whose accumulated acts have constituted them as a recognisable moral category.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does the sinner-identity operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three distinct inner modes. (1) *As self-recognised identity*: Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man, O Lord" — Peter's spontaneous inner recognition of his sinner-identity in the presence of holiness; Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me, a sinner" — the tax collector's self-confession of the sinner-identity; 1Ti 1:15 — Paul's "of whom I am the foremost" — the inward claim of the worst-sinner identity; (2) *As externally assigned identity*: Mat 9:11 — "why does your teacher eat with tax collectors and sinners?" — others name the category; Joh 9:24 — "we know that this man is a sinner" — authoritative external declaration; (3) *As constituted imputed standing*: Rom 5:19 — "made sinners through Adam's disobedience" — the identity is constituted from outside the person.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does the sinner-identity operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — three contextual modes. (1) *Extreme-type context* (VCG-03): the identity is totalising — Gen 13:13 — "great sinners against the Lord" — the sinful character defines the entire person or people; (2) *Social-stigma context* (VCG-01): the identity operates through social exclusion and contamination-fear — Mat 9:11; Luk 7:37/39 — the social category carries contamination risk that the observant avoid; (3) *Gospel context* (VCG-02 NT): the identity is the basis of Christ's saving mission — 1Ti 1:15 — "came into the world to save sinners" — the worst sinner-identity is the target of grace rather than the disqualifier from it.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does the sinner-identity have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — in both directions. (1) *Self-confession*: Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man, O Lord"; Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me, a sinner"; 1Ti 1:15 — "of whom I am the foremost" — the inward identity expressed through verbal self-declaration; (2) *External declaration*: Joh 9:24 — "we know that this man is a sinner" — authoritative community verdict spoken aloud; Gal 2:15 — "Gentile sinners" — ethnic-religious category named in speech; (3) *Hostile speech as identity-expression*: Jude 15 — "the harsh things that ungodly sinners have spoken against him" — the sinner's identity is expressed through their verbal opposition to God.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when the sinner-identity is recognised?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Two contrasting immediate responses. (1) *Prostration and plea*: Luk 5:8 — "he fell down at Jesus' knees, saying, 'Depart from me, for I am a sinful man'" — the immediate inner response to recognising the sinner-identity in the presence of holiness is prostration, awe-dread, and the impulse to distance Jesus from the defiled self; Luk 18:13 — "stood far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner'" — the immediate inner response is physical self-abasement and penitential appeal; (2) *Dread*: Isa 33:14 — "sinners in Zion are afraid; trembling has seized the godless" — the sinner's immediate inner response to God's consuming presence is terror.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Variable across the three VCG registers. VCG-01/02 authentic self-recognition produces prostration and plea (Luk 5:8; Luk 18:13). VCG-03 extreme sinner in judgment context produces dread and trembling (Isa 33:14). The extreme-type sinners of VCG-03 may have no recognition response at all during their sinning: Amo 9:10 — "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — the immediate response is presumptuous self-confidence, not recognition. The response thus varies from authentic self-abasement (Luk 5:8) through penitential appeal (Luk 18:13) to eschatological dread (Isa 33:14) to defiant denial (Amo 9:10).

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-16]** E — For the social-stigma sinner-category (VCG-01 — Mat 9:10; Luk 15:1/2; Luk 7:37) — the sinners who draw near to Jesus, recline at table, or approach with ointment — the immediate inner response of receiving the sinner-label from others is not directly described. The corpus characterises their behaviour (drawing near, reclining, approaching) but not the moment-by-moment inner experience of bearing the externally assigned sinner-identity.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does the sinner-identity produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Moral totality*: Gen 13:13 — "great sinners against the Lord" — sustained sinfulness produces a totality of moral character defined by sin; the individual identity becomes comprehensively sinful rather than partially; (2) *Self-deceived presumption*: Amo 9:10 — "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — sustained sinner-identity produces a false inner sense of security; the habitual sinner stops perceiving the threat that their condition poses; (3) *Wandering from truth*: Jam 5:20 — "whoever brings back a sinner from his wandering" — the sustained sinner-identity is characterised as an ongoing directional drift away from truth toward death.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained sinner-identity establish?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three established states. (1) *Constituted social exclusion*: Psa 1:5 — "sinners will not stand in the congregation of the righteous" — the sustained identity produces structural exclusion from the covenant community; (2) *Transactional inner orientation*: Luk 6:32/33/34 — sinners love only those who love them, do good only to those who do good, lend only expecting return — the sustained identity establishes a calculating, self-centred inner orientation; (3) *Divine-hearing cut-off*: Joh 9:31 — "God does not listen to sinners" — the sustained identity severs the communication channel with God.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes. The immediate response (prostration — Luk 5:8; dread — Isa 33:14; defiant denial — Amo 9:10) is episodic. The sustained effects (moral totality — Gen 13:13; social exclusion — Psa 1:5; wandering — Jam 5:20; divine-hearing cut-off — Joh 9:31) are constitutional outcomes of the sustained sinner-identity over time. The immediate dread (episodic) gives way to either the soul-conversion of repentance (Luk 15:7 — joy over one sinner who repents) or the deepened presumption of the habitual extreme sinner (Amo 9:10 — denying disaster will come).

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable authentic reception of the sinner-identity?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Proximity to holiness*: Luk 5:8 — Peter's recognition occurs specifically when Jesus demonstrates divine power over the fish; proximity to holiness triggers the conscience's recognition; (2) *Genuine humility*: Luk 18:13 — "would not even lift up his eyes to heaven" — the tax collector's self-abasement is the inner posture that enables honest self-recognition; (3) *Spiritual self-knowledge*: 1Ti 1:15 — Paul's "of whom I am the foremost" — the apostolic self-assessment is a considered inner knowledge of one's own worst state, not merely a moment of emotional distress.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort reception of the sinner-identity?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Two blocking conditions. (1) *Self-righteousness*: Mat 9:13 — "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" — those who perceive themselves as righteous cannot receive the sinner-identity they bear; the Pharisees (implied) block recognition through self-evaluation as righteous; (2) *Presumptuous self-confidence*: Amo 9:10 — "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — the habitual extreme sinner's inner conviction of security from consequence blocks the recognition of the sinner-identity's implications.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who resists the sinner-identity?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Amo 9:10 names the resistant state: "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — the inner state is confident self-deception about the consequences of the sinner-identity. Joh 9:24/25 — the Pharisees who declare Jesus "a sinner" with certainty while the healed man refuses the label — the resisters are in a state of inverted moral judgement: applying the sinner-label to the holy and denying it to themselves. The inner state of resistance is thus characterised by moral inversion: others are sinners; the self is not.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The primary dimension is **constituted moral identity** — the person's moral character as a whole, not a specific faculty. Rom 5:19 — "were made sinners" — the making is a total-person status; Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me, a sinner" — the sinner-identity is a whole-person self-designation. The characteristic does not primarily engage a specific inner faculty (will, conscience, affect) but constitutes the person's *moral self as a whole* as a sinner.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — (1) Rom 5:19 — "made sinners" — the passive voice confirms the total-person constitution; (2) Gen 13:13 — "great sinners against the Lord" — the identity is the totality of the Sodomites' moral character; (3) Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man" — the whole-person self-declaration; (4) 1Ti 1:15 — "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners" — the salvific mission targets the whole person, not a specific faculty; (5) Heb 7:26 — "separated from sinners" — the separation is total-person, not faculty-specific.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does the sinner-identity carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — three secondary dimensions. (1) *Social-relational*: VCG-01 — the sinner-identity is expressed and maintained through social categorisation and exclusion (Psa 1:5; Mat 9:10/11; Luk 7:37); (2) *Volitional*: Luk 6:32/33/34 — the sinner's inner orientation is fundamentally transactional and self-directed; the will operates within a self-interest radius; (3) *Eschatological*: Isa 13:9; Psa 104:35; Jude 15 — the sinner-identity carries an eschatological judgment-destination; the person-type is oriented toward divine destruction.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is the sinner-identity explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — through the defilement and separation language. Heb 7:26 — "holy, innocent, unstained, separated from sinners, and exalted above the heavens" — the sinner's moral character is the polar opposite of holiness, innocence, and unstained purity; these are spirit-level characterisations. Isa 33:14 — "who among us can dwell with the consuming fire?" — the sinners' dread before God's holy presence reveals that their spirit-level incompatibility with God is the issue; the consuming fire is God's holiness that the sinner's spirit-level character cannot withstand. Jam 4:8 — "cleanse your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded" — the heart-purification called for alongside the sinner's hand-cleansing names the spirit-level inner defilement.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does the sinner-identity originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Partly. The constituted sinner-identity through Adam (Rom 5:19 — "made sinners through one man's disobedience") operates at the spirit-level of the person's fundamental God-standing. The extreme-type sinner (VCG-03) whose moral character is thoroughly defined by sin has a spirit-level orientation against God: Gen 13:13 — "great sinners against the Lord" — the "against the Lord" names the spirit-level directional opposition. ## T3 — Inner Faculties (33 prompts)

**T3.1.1 — Does the sinner-identity engage the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The perceptive faculty is engaged on both sides: in the sinner's perception of their own condition and in others' perception of the sinner. (1) *Sinner self-perception*: Luk 5:8 — Peter's perception of his own sinfulness is triggered by witnessing divine power; Luk 18:13 — the tax collector perceives himself as "a sinner" clearly enough to name it before God; (2) *Others' perception of the sinner*: Luk 7:39 — the Pharisee perceives the woman "is a sinner" from her reputation; Joh 9:25 — the healed man perceives that he cannot determine whether Jesus "is a sinner" by observing his act; Luk 13:2 — Jesus challenges the perceptive inference that suffering signals greater sinfulness.

---

**T3.1.2 — Does the sinner-identity enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-identity *impairs* the perceptive faculty in both the extreme-type sinner and the self-righteous observer. Amo 9:10 — "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — the habitual sinner's perceptive faculty fails to register the danger their identity creates; the perceptive capacity is impaired by self-deception. Joh 9:24 — the Pharisees' "we know that this man is a sinner" — the observer's perceptive faculty is impaired by prejudice: they perceive sinfulness where there is none (Jesus). Luk 13:2 — Jesus corrects the perceptive inference that suffering indicates greater sinfulness, revealing a general perceptive-faculty impairment in the population.

---

**T3.1.3 — What does the perceptive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The perceptive pattern reveals that the sinner-identity creates two characteristic perceptive distortions: (1) the sinner's impaired self-perception (Amo 9:10 — seeing no danger; extreme-type sinners not recognising their condition); and (2) the observer's impaired perception of others (Joh 9:24 — wrongly labelling Jesus a sinner; Luk 7:39 — perceiving the woman's sinner-label as contagion). The corrective perceptive acts — Luk 5:8 (accurate self-perception), Luk 13:2 (Jesus correcting misperception), Joh 9:25 (refusing the false verdict) — are the exceptions that mark the impairment as the norm.

---

**T3.2.1 — Does the sinner-identity engage the cognitive faculty?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — through the self-assessment and category-application dimensions. Joh 9:24/25 — "we know that this man is a sinner" / "whether he is a sinner I do not know" — the cognitive act of determining who qualifies as a sinner is explicitly contested; Gal 2:15 — "Gentile sinners" is a cognitive-ethnic categorisation; Rom 7:13 — "that sin might be shown to be sin, and through the commandment might become sinful beyond measure" — the commandment's function is cognitive: it reveals and magnifies sin's character so that the mind can know it accurately.

---

**T3.2.2 — Does the sinner-identity enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-identity *distorts* cognition at the self-assessment level. Amo 9:10 — "who say, 'Disaster shall not overtake or meet us'" — the habitual sinner's cognitive self-assessment is wrong; the sinner-identity produces a false cognitive conclusion about the self's safety. Joh 9:24 — "we know that this man is a sinner" — the authoritative religious establishment's cognitive verdict is wrong; the sinner-identity-label is misapplied with cognitive certainty. Conversely, Rom 7:13 — the commandment deepens cognitive understanding of sin's true nature: the cognitive faculty is sharpened by the law's disclosure of what is "sinful beyond measure."

---

**T3.2.3 — What does the cognitive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-16]** E — The cognitive pattern reveals an asymmetry in the sinner-identity's cognitive effects: applied to the self by the honest conscience, the sinner-label is accurate (Luk 5:8; Luk 18:13; 1Ti 1:15 — Paul's "foremost sinner"); applied to others by the self-righteous, it is a tool of false categorisation (Joh 9:24 — Jesus wrongly labelled). The cognitive faculty is most accurate when the sinner recognises their own condition (self-directed cognition) and least accurate when it is deployed to identify and exclude others (other-directed cognition from a position of self-righteousness).
