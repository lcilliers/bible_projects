## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The 205-verse corpus reveals God's character across five dimensions. (1) *God as the one who sees and records*: Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God maintains a divine register; Gen 18:20 — "the outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is great and their sin is very grave" — the sin has reached God as a measurable, audible reality. (2) *God as the one who remembers sin precisely*: Jer 14:10 — "he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins"; Hos 13:12 — "his sin is kept in store" — the divine memory is reliable and permanent. (3) *God as the one who searches for sin*: Job 10:6 — "that you seek out my iniquity and search for my sin" (VCG-03) — God actively investigates and scrutinises the person. (4) *God as the one who blots out the record*: Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake, and I will not remember your sins" — God can erase the divine record as a sovereign act. (5) *God as the one who bears the burden of his people's sin*: Isa 43:24 — "you have burdened me with your sins; you have wearied me with your iniquities" — the accumulated record is experienced by God as a burden on the divine-human relationship.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute sin as divine record to God?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Partially — in the positive keeping-record mode. God is the keeper of the book from which sinners are blotted (Exo 32:32/33) and the one who searches for sin (Job 10:6; 14:16 — VCG-03). God maintains the record; he does not bear the sinful-record status. Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God is the author of the record, not a participant in the condition recorded in it. The characteristic thus reveals God as the moral-record keeper rather than as the one who carries a sin-record before another.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — God does not carry a sin-record before anyone else. He is the moral record-keeper, the one before whom the human sin-record exists. The silence on God possessing the record-as-burden reveals: (1) the characteristic is constitutively relational — it is the condition of a creature standing before a perfectly righteous Creator whose memory is complete; (2) Isa 43:24 — "you have burdened me with your sins" — God is burdened by human sin but as the one receiving the offense, not as the one who has an offense-record of his own. The asymmetry is structural: God keeps the record; the creature is the subject of it.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose sin as divine record serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-13]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for sin as divine record. The characteristic is entirely fallen-condition: it is the record of offenses against God that accumulates in the post-fall human condition. No verse presents the divine-sin-record as a designed feature of the created person; it presupposes the fall.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether sin as divine record is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Entirely a response to the fallen condition. Psa 51:5 — "in sin did my mother conceive me" (VCG-01) — the record's origin is in the condition inherited from birth; it is not a creation-design feature. Exo 32:32/33 — the divine book from which sinners are blotted presupposes sin's prior entry into the world; the record exists because sin does. The Jeroboam-sin legacy (VCG-04, 46 verses) shows sin as a transmissible recorded pattern accumulating across generations — entirely post-fall dynamics.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that sin as divine record is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — in two eschatological directions. (1) *Positive — the new-covenant promise of divine forgetting*: Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more" — the eschatological fullness of the new covenant is the permanent removal of the sin-record from divine memory; (2) *Final divine reckoning*: Exo 32:34 — "in the day when I visit, I will visit their sin upon them" — the deferred record has an eschatological appointment of judgment. Dan 9:24 — "seventy weeks are decreed... to put an end to sin" — the divine record of sin has an eschatological terminus in which it will be completely resolved.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does sin as divine record express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The divine image's moral accountability before God is the constitutive dimension of CHAR-13: only an image-bearer who stands in moral relationship with God can have a sin-record before God. Animals do not carry a divine sin-record. Psa 51:5 — "in sin did my mother conceive me" — the conceived state is that of an image-bearer accountable to God. The image-bearer's creation for moral relationship with God is what makes the divine-record characteristic possible: the record exists because the person stands before God as a morally accountable subject.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest sin as divine record is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Exclusively creaturely. God is the keeper of the record, not its subject. Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God is the record-keeper; the humans are the entries. Amo 5:12 — "for I know how many are your transgressions" — God knows the record; he does not have one. The asymmetry is absolute: God's perfect moral record is the standard against which the human's sin-record is measured (Rom 3:23 in CHAR-11 — falling short of the glory of God).

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of sin as divine record reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The presence of a sin-record reveals the image in its primary condition of fallen moral accountability. Jer 17:1 — "the sin of Judah is written with a pen of iron; with a point of diamond it is engraved on the tablet of their heart" (VCG-02) — the record is not external only but engraved internally on the heart; the image-bearer carries the record within as well as before God. Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you" — the divine record is the relational barrier; the image designed for God-relationship now stands behind a wall of recorded sin. The depth of the record's internal engraving (Jer 17:1 — iron pen, diamond point) reveals how thoroughly the fallen condition has shaped the image from within.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use sin as divine record typologically?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes. (1) *Day of Atonement typology*: Lev 16:21 — "Aaron shall lay both his hands on the head of the live goat, and confess over it all the iniquities of the people of Israel, and all their transgressions, all their sins. And he shall put them on the head of the goat and send it away into the wilderness" — the annual ritual of sin-record being placed on the scapegoat and sent to the wilderness typologically points toward the Lamb of God (Joh 1:29 — "who takes away the sin of the world"). (2) *Moses's divine-book intercession*: Exo 32:32 — Moses's offer to be blotted from God's book if the people's sin is not forgiven typologically prefigures Christ's bearing the sin-record of the people.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The direction is from human-institution type to divine-act antitype. The Day of Atonement ritual (Lev 16:21 — scapegoat bearing the confession of sins and sent to the wilderness) points forward to the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world (Joh 1:29). The type is the annual human ritual that temporarily displaced the sin-record; the antitype is the once-for-all divine act that permanently removes it (Dan 9:24 — "to put an end to sin"; Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more").

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Sin as divine record." Three signals. (1) *Sin*: the moral reality being tracked is sin — not a neutral human limitation but moral offense against God. (2) *Divine record*: the characteristic is not the subjective experience of guilt (which would be CHAR-17) but the objective moral weight existing before God — whether the person feels it or not. The record exists independently of the person's awareness. (3) *Record*: the metaphor signals documentary retention — Hos 13:12 — "his sin is kept in store"; Exo 32:33 — "I will blot out of my book"; Jer 17:1 — "written with a pen of iron... engraved on the tablet of their heart." The name thus signals: an objective, retained, divine-witnessed moral account that is not destroyed by the person's ignorance, self-deception, or concealment from others.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Five term-groups across the 205 verses. (1) **chat.tat (H2403B)** — the dominant term (VCG-05 — 113 verses): sin as moral fact, wrong committed, the act that enters the record. The noun form names the sin-entity as a reality requiring priestly atonement (Lev 4:23/26/28/35) or divine forgiveness. (2) **cha.ta.ah (H2401/H2403A)** — sin as objective moral category requiring covering (VCG-02 — 9 verses): Psa 32:1 — "whose sin is covered"; Jer 17:1 — "sin of Judah is written... engraved on the tablet of their heart." (3) **chet (H2399)** — personal moral liability (VCG-01 — 32 verses): Num 9:13 — "that man shall bear his sin"; Deu 24:16 — "each one shall be put to death for his own sin." The bearer-of-sin framing is distinctive: chet names the personal carrying of moral liability. (4) **cha.ta (H2398)** — the verb (numerous VCG-05 verses): "whoever has sinned against me" (Exo 32:33) — the act of sinning that creates the record entry. (5) **hamartia/hamartēma (G0266/G0265)** — NT terms: Joh 1:29 — "the sin of the world" as a collective burden lifted by the Lamb; Rom 3:25 — "former sins" passed over in divine forbearance.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — All three. *Directional*: the record accumulates in one direction — from the person upward toward God; Isa 59:12 — "our sins testify against us" — the testimony runs from the person toward divine judgment. *Relational*: the record is constitutively relational — it exists before God (Lev 4:26 — "the priest shall make atonement for him for his sin, and he shall be forgiven" — the atonement addresses a relational deficit). *Constitutional*: Jer 17:1 — "engraved on the tablet of their heart" — the record is also internally inscribed; Psa 51:5 — "in sin did my mother conceive me" — the record begins constitutionally, at birth. The directional, relational, and constitutional dimensions are all evidenced in the 205-verse corpus.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is sin as divine record?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The corpus reveals CHAR-13 as a *standing objective moral condition* — not primarily an act (CHAR-1) or a feeling (CHAR-17) but the accumulated moral weight that sits before God as a fact. Hos 13:12 — "his sin is kept in store" — the record is a stored reality; Exo 32:34 — "in the day when I visit, I will visit their sin upon them" — the record is a deferred moral account. The characteristic operates at the objective rather than subjective level: the record exists whether the person feels its weight or not (definition: "the person carries this record whether they feel it or not"). It is thus a *condition of standing* before God rather than an inner experience.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The corpus reveals four constituent elements. (1) *Individual liability*: chet (VCG-01) — "each one shall be put to death for his own sin" (Deu 24:16) — personal accountable record; (2) *Collective/cumulative dimension*: 1Sa 12:19 — "we have added to all our sins this evil" — the record accumulates across acts and across the community; (3) *Named institutional pattern*: the Jeroboam-sin legacy (VCG-04) — 46 verses of a named recorded sin-pattern transmitted across generations; (4) *Divine-surveillance register*: VCG-03 — Job's testimony of God actively searching and watching for sin (Job 10:6; 14:16) — the record is under active divine monitoring.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Working description: *Sin as divine record (CHAR-13) is the standing objective moral condition in which every human person's sin exists before God as a retained, accumulated account — recorded, named, not hidden from divine memory, and not erased by the person's ignorance or concealment from others. The corpus evidences five sub-registers: (1) Individual moral liability (chet — VCG-01): each person bears their own sin-record; (2) Broad chat.tat record (VCG-05 — 113 verses): sin as an act that creates a moral fact requiring priestly resolution; (3) Cha.ta.ah as objective moral category requiring covering (VCG-02): the record requires a covering act; (4) The Jeroboam-sin legacy (VCG-04 — 46 verses): a named institutional sin-pattern transmitted across royal generations as a defined recorded legacy; (5) The Job surveillance register (VCG-03): God actively searching and monitoring for sin as a divine investigator. The characteristic's definitive image is Jer 17:1 — "the sin of Judah is written with a pen of iron; with a point of diamond it is engraved on the tablet of their heart" — the record is both divinely maintained and internally inscribed.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of sin as divine record?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The structural opposite is *sin forgiven and removed from divine memory*. Multiple metaphors name it: (1) *Blotting out*: Isa 43:25 — "I will not remember your sins"; Eze 33:16 — "none of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him"; (2) *Casting behind the back*: Isa 38:17 — "you have cast all my sins behind your back" — removed from the divine sight; (3) *Casting into the sea*: Mic 7:19 — "you will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea" — the record disposed of permanently; (4) *Covering*: Psa 85:2 — "you covered all their sin" — the record concealed from divine judgment; (5) *The new-covenant promise*: Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more" — the ultimate eschatological opposite of the divine record.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does sin as divine record explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The divine record explicitly excludes: (1) *Concealment from God*: Num 32:23 — "your sin will find you out" — the record resists the person's attempt to hide it; Job 10:14 — "if I sin, you watch me and do not acquit me" — the record precludes acquittal through divine surveillance; (2) *Transfer of liability to another*: Deu 24:16 — "each one shall be put to death for his own sin" — the record resists being assigned to someone else; (3) *Self-exculpation through prosperity*: Hos 12:8 — "in all my labors they cannot find in me iniquity or sin" — Ephraim's wealth-based self-exculpation is explicitly unfounded; the record persists regardless of material standing.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is sin as divine record not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-17 (Guilt as inner-being state)*: CHAR-13 is the objective record before God — the moral fact existing independently of the person's subjective feeling; CHAR-17 is the inner experience of guilt as a felt burden. The record exists whether felt or not; guilt (CHAR-17) is the subjective experience of the record. (2) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the discrete act of sinning; CHAR-13 is what that act becomes once recorded — the accumulated moral account. (3) *Not CHAR-11 (Sin as universal condition)*: CHAR-11 is the structural condition all persons stand in; CHAR-13 is the specific accumulated record of particular acts within that condition.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does sin as divine record operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Four distinct inner modes. (1) *As an internal inscription*: Jer 17:1 — "engraved on the tablet of their heart" — the record operates internally, inscribed on the heart's structure; (2) *As a persistent inner accusation*: Psa 51:3 — "my sin is ever before me" (VCG-02) — the record operates as an inner presence that occupies conscience continually; (3) *As a weight producing physical effects*: Psa 38:3 — "there is no health in my bones because of my sin" — the record operates on the physical body through its moral weight; (4) *As an invisible moral account affecting life-quality*: Jer 5:25 — "your sins have kept good from you" — the record operates by blocking divine blessing without the person necessarily knowing the mechanism.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does sin as divine record operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes. (1) *Individual context*: chet (VCG-01) — each person bears their own sin personally; Deu 24:16 — individual personal liability; (2) *Corporate-national context*: VCG-04 Jeroboam-sin legacy — the record operates at the national level, accumulated across royal generations, with the whole people affected by the leader's named sin-pattern; (3) *Divine-surveillance context*: VCG-03 — Job perceives God as actively searching for and watching his sin, creating a condition of personal surveillance; (4) *Covenantal context*: VCG-05 sevenfold discipline passages (Lev 26:18/21/24/28) — the record triggers covenant consequences at specific thresholds.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does sin as divine record have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — in two registers. (1) *Prophetic declaration of the record*: Isa 58:1 — "declare to my people their transgression, to the house of Jacob their sins" — the prophet's speech makes the record known to those who have suppressed it; Mic 3:8 — "to declare to Jacob his transgression and to Israel his sin" — the Spirit-empowered prophet is specifically commissioned to voice the record. (2) *Testifying record*: Isa 59:12 — "our sins testify against us" — the record itself speaks as a witness against the person in a judicial-communicative mode.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when the divine record is encountered?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Multiple response patterns across the corpus. (1) *Confession*: Num 5:7 — "he shall confess his sin that he has committed" — the encounter with the record that "is made known" (Lev 4:23/28) triggers the confession-response; Psa 32:5 — "I acknowledged my sin to you... I said, 'I will confess my transgressions to the Lord'" — the immediate response to facing the record is acknowledgment. (2) *Sorrow*: Psa 38:18 — "I am sorry for my sin" — the encounter produces grief as an immediate affective response. (3) *Plea for pardon*: Exo 10:17 — Pharaoh's "forgive my sin, please, only this once" — the record encountered produces an urgent appeal for its cancellation.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Variable. Confession (Num 5:7; Psa 32:5), sorrow (Psa 38:18), and plea for pardon (Exo 10:17; Num 12:11) are the positive responses. But several verses show non-response or resistance: Jer 16:10 — the people cannot identify their sin when under judgment — the immediate response to the prophet's declaration is incomprehension rather than acknowledgment; Hos 12:8 — Ephraim denies the record ("they cannot find in me iniquity or sin") — self-deception is the immediate response. The response to the divine record depends on whether the conscience is engaged (confession/sorrow) or suppressed (denial/incomprehension).

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-13]** E — For the many persons described as carrying the Jeroboam-sin legacy (VCG-04 — 46 verses of kings who "walked in the sins of Jeroboam and did not depart"), the immediate inner response to the record is not described. The corpus characterises their conduct and its consequences but not the moment-by-moment subjective experience of carrying the recorded sin-pattern.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does sin as divine record produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Four sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Separation from God*: Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you" — accumulated record produces relational estrangement; (2) *Rotting and decay*: Eze 33:10 — "our sins are upon us, and we rot away because of them" — the accumulated record produces inner moral decay; (3) *Physical deterioration*: Psa 38:3 — "there is no soundness in my flesh... there is no health in my bones because of my sin" — the weight of the record produces bodily consequences over time; (4) *Loss of blessing*: Jer 5:25 — "your sins have kept good from you" — sustained accumulation of the record progressively closes off the positive divine provision.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does a sustained unaddressed sin-record establish?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Three established states. (1) *Accuser-testimony state*: Isa 59:12 — "our sins testify against us" — the accumulated record becomes a self-generating accusation that cannot be silenced; (2) *Hardened-face state*: Isa 3:9 — "they proclaim their sin like Sodom; they do not hide it" — when the record has grown extensive enough, conscience no longer restrains its display; the face itself bears witness to the interior record; (3) *Divine-memory vigilance state*: Jer 14:10 — "he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins" — the sustained record places the person in a state of pending divine reckoning; the record awaits its appointed day of visitation (Exo 32:34).

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes. The immediate response is often confessional or grief-oriented (Num 5:7; Psa 38:18) — an acute response to the moment of recognition. The sustained effect is the gradual accumulation of separation from God (Isa 59:2), physical deterioration (Psa 38:3), and moral rotting (Eze 33:10). The sustained effect also involves the hardening of the face (Isa 3:9 — openly proclaimed without shame), suggesting that immediate conscience-engagement (acute response) gives way to diminished conscience over time if the record is not addressed.

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of the divine-record's reality?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Four enabling conditions. (1) *Awareness when sin is made known*: Lev 4:23 — "the sin which he has committed is made known to him" — the conscience's ability to receive the disclosure of the record is the primary enabling condition; (2) *Conscience engagement*: Num 5:6 — "that person realizes his guilt" — the internal realization triggers the response mechanism; (3) *Humility before God*: Psa 32:5 — "I acknowledged my sin... I did not cover my iniquity" — the person's willingness not to cover their sin enables reception; (4) *Memory of prior failure*: Gen 41:9 — "I remember my offenses today" — the cupbearer's memory of past failure enables current acknowledgment.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort reception of the divine record?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Three blocking conditions. (1) *Self-deception through prosperity*: Hos 12:8 — "in all my labors they cannot find in me iniquity or sin" — material success produces a false sense of moral clearance; (2) *Suppression of the record*: Psa 32:5 — "I did not cover my iniquity" (the positive) implies that covering is the blocking condition; Jer 16:10 — the people's inability to identify their sin shows a suppression that blocks reception; (3) *Hardened face*: Isa 3:9 — "they proclaim their sin like Sodom; they do not hide it" — conscience has been so thoroughly suppressed that the record no longer produces shame; the blocking condition has become permanent display without awareness of its gravity.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters the divine record and does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Jer 16:10 — the people under judgment ask "What is our iniquity? What is the sin that we have committed against the Lord our God?" — the non-receiving state is incomprehension: the record is there (God sees it; judgment has come) but the person cannot identify it in themselves. Isa 3:9 — "they proclaim their sin like Sodom; they do not hide it" — the alternative non-receiving state: the record has produced such conscience-deadness that the person exhibits their sin publicly without awareness of its gravity. Both states involve a failure of the inner conscience to correctly interpret the moral reality of the record before God.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of sin as divine record?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The primary dimension is **moral-accountability standing before God** — not a specific faculty but the person's total relational-moral position. The characteristic is defined by its standing before God, not by its engagement of a specific inner faculty. Amo 5:12 — "for I know how many are your transgressions and how great are your sins" — God's knowledge of the record is the defining dimension; the record exists before divine omniscience. Hos 13:12 — "his sin is kept in store" — the storage is in the divine memory. The primary dimension is thus the person's standing in God's moral memory.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — (1) Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — the person's standing in the divine book is the operative moral reality; (2) Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more" — the erasure of divine memory is the defining liberation; (3) Job 10:6 — "seek out my iniquity and search for my sin" — divine scrutiny of the person's standing is the characteristic's primary mode; (4) Gen 18:20 — "their sin is very grave" — the gravity is measured by what has reached God as an objective reality, not by the person's own internal assessment.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does sin as divine record carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — three secondary dimensions. (1) *Internal-inscription dimension*: Jer 17:1 — "engraved on the tablet of their heart" — the record has a secondary heart-level constitutional dimension; the sin is written within as well as before God; (2) *Physical-consequence dimension*: Psa 38:3 — "no health in my bones because of my sin" — the record produces bodily consequences as a secondary dimension; (3) *Conscience-awareness dimension*: Psa 51:3 — "my sin is ever before me" — the record's persistent presence in the conscience is a secondary inner-dimension, distinct from the primary objective-standing-before-God dimension.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is sin as divine record explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — through the separation from God that the record produces. ## T3 — Inner Faculties (33 prompts)

**T3.1.1 — Does sin as divine record engage the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The perceptive faculty is engaged through its failure and its restoration in relation to the record. Job 10:6 — "that you seek out my iniquity and search for my sin" (VCG-03) — Job perceives God as actively scrutinising him; the perception of divine surveillance is the operative inner dynamic. Num 32:23 — "your sin will find you out" — the sin's eventual self-revelation engages the perceptive faculty: what was hidden will become visible. Isa 3:9 — "the look on their faces bears witness against them; they proclaim their sin like Sodom" — the sinners' own faces have become perceptible testimony to their record; the perceptive faculty registers what the conscience no longer conceals.

---

**T3.1.2 — Does sin as divine record enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The record *distorts and eventually impairs* the perceptive faculty. Jer 16:10 — "they say to you, 'Why has the Lord pronounced all this great evil against us? What is our iniquity? What is the sin?'" — the people cannot perceive their own record even under judgment; the perceptive faculty is impaired by suppression of the conscience. Hos 12:8 — "in all my labors they cannot find in me iniquity or sin" — Ephraim's prosperity distorts perception of the record: wealth is mistaken for moral clearance. Conversely, when the record is made known (Lev 4:23 — "made known to him"), the perceptive faculty is restored to accurate moral self-assessment.

---

**T3.1.3 — What does the perceptive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The perceptive pattern reveals that the divine record operates partly through perceptive concealment: the record exists before God regardless of whether the person perceives it, but the person's perceptive engagement with it is variable and distortable. Jer 16:10 (incomprehension under judgment), Hos 12:8 (prosperity-induced blindness), and Isa 3:9 (sin publicly displayed without shame) represent the full perceptive failure spectrum — from genuine incomprehension to wealth-induced blindness to conscience-deadened display. The perceptive faculty's restoration is the first step toward the record being addressed (Lev 4:23 — "made known to him" → brings offering).

---

**T3.2.1 — Does sin as divine record engage the cognitive faculty?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The cognitive faculty is engaged through the law's disclosure function and through accusation. Rom 3:20 (CHAR-11 corpus, but relevant principle) — the law provides the cognitive framework for recognising the record. Directly in CHAR-13: Pro 24:9 — "the devising of folly is sin" — the cognitive act of deliberate scheming creates a record entry; the mind's devising is itself sinful. Job 13:23 — "how many are my iniquities and my sins? Make me know my transgression and my sin" — Job's cognitive faculty cannot self-enumerate the record; he requires divine cognitive disclosure to know what the record contains.

---

**T3.2.2 — Does sin as divine record enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The record *impairs accurate self-cognition* while being fully known to God. Job 13:23 — Job cannot know the extent of his own record without divine disclosure; the cognitive faculty's self-assessment is inherently limited. Jer 16:10 — the people's cognitive question ("What is our iniquity?") under judgment shows the record has accumulated beyond their cognitive self-awareness. Pro 24:9 — conversely, the cognitive act of devising folly creates the record: active scheming is morally registered even if the schemer does not cognise it as sin.

---

**T3.2.3 — What does the cognitive pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The cognitive pattern reveals an asymmetry between divine and human cognition of the record: God knows it fully (Amo 5:12 — "for I know how many are your transgressions and how great are your sins") while the human's cognitive access to their own record is partial and often distorted. This asymmetry is the characteristic's epistemic structure: the record is fully known to God and partially (or wrongly) known to the person. Isa 58:1 — the prophetic function is precisely to bridge this asymmetry: "declare to my people their transgression, to the house of Jacob their sins."

---

**T3.3.1 — Does sin as divine record engage the memory faculty?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The memory faculty is the primary faculty engaged — on both the divine and human sides. (1) *Divine memory*: Jer 14:10 — "he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins"; Hos 8:13 — "now he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins" — the divine memory retains the record and triggers judgment from it. The liberation metaphors are memory-erasure metaphors: Isa 43:25 — "I will not remember your sins"; Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more." (2) *Human memory*: Gen 41:9 — "I remember my offenses today" — the cupbearer's memory of past failure resurfaces; Psa 25:7 — "Remember not the sins of my youth" — the psalmist pleads for God not to hold the past record in memory against him.

---

**T3.3.2 — Does sin as divine record enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The divine-record characteristic *is constituted by divine memory*: the record exists as God's memorial of every sin committed. It deepens divine memory's function (every sin is retained — Hos 13:12 — "kept in store") while the characteristic's resolution is precisely divine memory-erasure. For human memory: the record can deepen human memory of past failures (Psa 51:3 — "my sin is ever before me"; Gen 41:9 — "I remember my offenses today") or be suppressed through wilful forgetting (Jer 16:10 — people cannot identify their sin). The characteristic thus operates through, and in some cases against, the human memory faculty.

---

**T3.3.3 — What does the memory pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The memory pattern reveals that CHAR-13 is uniquely memory-constituted among M10 characteristics: the divine record is divine memory made judicial — every sin is retained in God's memory as a standing moral account. Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more" — the new-covenant liberation is defined as divine memory-cancellation, not merely divine forgiveness-declaration. The characteristic thus reveals: sin's ultimate hold over the person is not primarily in inner experience or constitutional damage but in divine memory's retention of the moral account — and liberation is the permanent cancellation of that retention.

**T3.4.1 — Does sin as divine record engage the affective faculty?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — the record produces affective responses when conscience-accessible. Eze 33:10 — "our sins are upon us, and we rot away because of them. How then can we live?" — the affective dimension is despair: the weight of the record produces hopelessness.

---

**T3.4.2 — Does sin as divine record enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The record *deepens negative affect* when conscience is engaged: Psa 38:18 — sorrow; Eze 33:10 — despair. But when conscience is suppressed, the record *bypasses affect*: Isa 3:9 — sin proclaimed without shame — the affect of shame is absent despite the record's presence. The record exists independently of the affective state; affect is the conscience's variable response to the record, not a constitutive feature of the record itself.

---

**T3.4.3 — What does the affective pattern reveal?**

**[CHAR-13]** E — The affective pattern reveals that the divine record does not require affective engagement to exist — the record is objective (the definition: "whether they feel it or not"). Affect is the optional inner-being register of the record when conscience is functional: sorrow (Psa 38:18), despair (Eze 33:10), and hopelessness (Eze 33:10 — "how then can we live?") are the primary negative affects the record produces. The positive resolution of the record (forgiveness and erasure) produces the corresponding positive affect: Psa 32:1 — "blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven" — blessedness is the positive affective state of having the record addressed.

**[CHAR-13]** E — The creative pattern reveals that the divine record is not only generated by obvious moral failures but also by the active cognitive-creative deployment of the person's planning and devising capacities toward ends contrary to God (Pro 24:9; Isa 30:1). The record captures what the mind devises and what the will plans, not only what the body does overtly. This confirms the record's comprehensive scope: it is sensitive to inner creative-cognitive movements as well as outer acts.

**T3.6.1 — Does sin as divine record engage the volitional faculty?**

The will's decisive turn enables the record's erasure (Eze 18:21; 33:14/16). The record thus tracks the will's directional history precisely: VCG-04's "he walked in" and "he did not depart" language is the volitional vocabulary of record accumulation.
