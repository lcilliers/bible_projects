## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin reveals the character of God through three lenses. (1) *God as the one who warns in advance*: Exo 23:33 and Deu 20:18 are preventive commands — God specifies before entry into Canaan that foreign nations will "make Israel sin" and that being taught their abominable practices will result in sinning against the Lord. God's foreknowledge of the contagion mechanism and his preventive instruction reveal him as a God who sees the transmission pathways of sin before they operate. (2) *God as the one who holds leaders accountable for what they transmitted*: 1Ki 16:2 — God directly charges the king: "you have made my people Israel to sin, provoking me to anger" — naming the contagious transmission as the specific indictment. 1Ki 21:22 — God declares Ahab's house will be destroyed "because you have made Israel to sin." (3) *God as the one who is provoked through transmitted sin*: 1Ki 14:22; 1Ki 16:13/26 — Israel's sins (transmitted from the leader) provoke God to anger through their idolatrous objects. The communal sin that leaders generate reaches God as a relational offence from the community.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute contagious sin to God?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — No verse in the 19-verse corpus attributes contagious sin to God. God appears throughout as the one who warns against it (Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18), is provoked by it (1Ki 14:22; 16:13), and holds leaders accountable for it (1Ki 16:2; 21:22). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it requires a human leader with a sinful inner commitment and a community that receives the transmitted direction. God does not sin, therefore cannot make others sin in the morally culpable sense the corpus describes.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The silence on God possessing contagious sin combined with his direct indictment of leaders who practise it (1Ki 16:2; 21:22) reveals that the characteristic represents an abuse of the God-given authority structure. Leaders are given authority so that their inner moral direction shapes the community toward God; contagious sin is what happens when that authority structure operates in the opposite direction — transmitting sin rather than righteousness. The silence confirms that the characteristic is the *perversion* of the image's authority-bearing capacity, not an expression of it. God-given authority is designed for moral formation toward God; contagious sin corrupts it into moral formation away from God.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose contagious sin serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that contagious sin serves any positive created purpose. Every verse presents it as a moral failure with severe consequences: God abandoning Israel (1Ki 14:16), destruction of royal houses (1Ki 21:22), driving Israel from following the Lord (2Ki 17:21), causing child sacrifice (Jer 32:35). The characteristic serves no positive created purpose; it is the corruption of the created authority structure.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether contagious sin is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin belongs entirely to the fallen condition. Exo 23:33 and Deu 20:18 frame it as a danger God is warning against *before* it occurs — the warning presupposes that it is not the designed operation of authority but a corrupted one that becomes possible when leaders are unfaithful. 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — the driving-away implies an original condition of following the Lord from which Israel was driven; the defection from that original orientation is the fallen condition within which contagious sin operates. The capacity for human leadership to form communities (morally formative authority) is a created design feature; contagious sin is what that capacity does under sin's influence.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that contagious sin is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The corpus orients contagious sin toward judgment and structural dismantling as its future trajectory. 1Ki 21:22 — "I will make your house like the house of Jeroboam... because you have made Israel to sin" — the future judgment falls on the leader who transmitted sin. 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's pulling down of Jeroboam's altar — represents the future-facing dismantling of contagious sin's structural deposit: the altar built for contagious sinning is physically destroyed. The trajectory is toward accountability (leadership judged — 1Ki 16:2; 21:22) and structural reversal (the sin-transmitting institution demolished — 2Ki 23:15), not toward fullness in any positive sense.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does contagious sin express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin does not express the divine image positively but perverts one of its core capacities: the image-bearer's designed capacity to shape and form others through moral authority. 1Ki 16:2 — "I exalted you out of the dust and made you leader over my people Israel" — God explicitly names the elevation to leadership as his own action, confirming that leadership authority is image-related (the image-bearer is created to bear authority and responsibility within God's creation). Contagious sin is what happens when this image-capacity is captured by sin: instead of the leader's inner commitment to God shaping the community toward God, the leader's inner commitment to sin shapes the community toward sin.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest contagious sin is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God is the one against whom contagious sin is directed and who holds the sinning leaders accountable. No divine analogue exists; the characteristic is the corruption of God-given authority, not an expression of God's own character.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of contagious sin in a leader reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The presence of contagious sin in a leader reveals the divine image in a condition of *inverted authority*: the image's authority-bearing capacity is operating in the opposite direction to its design. 1Ki 16:2 — "I exalted you... and you have walked in the way of Jeroboam and have made my people Israel to sin" — the contrast is stark: God elevated the person (image-bearing design) for their people's benefit; the person used the elevation to transmit sin (image-bearing capacity inverted). 2Ki 21:11 — Manasseh "committed abominations... more evil than all that the Amorites did... and has made Judah also to sin" — the image's capacity for moral formation of others has been deployed not merely neutrally but actively against the image's designed function. The more clearly the leader's authority is God-given (1Ki 16:2 — explicit elevation by God), the more severe the inverted use of that authority appears.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use contagious sin typologically?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. The Jeroboam-formula ("who made Israel to sin") operates as a *negative typological benchmark* — Jeroboam is the established type of the sin-transmitting leader against whom every subsequent king is measured. 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's destruction of Jeroboam's altar — is typologically significant: the dismantling of the physical monument to Jeroboam's contagious sin points toward the eschatological dismantling of all sin-transmitting structures. The altar at Bethel was the enduring structural legacy of Jeroboam's contagious sin; its demolition under Josiah prefigures the complete removal of the structures that sin-transmitting leadership builds. The OT pattern thus points forward to a leadership that transmits righteousness rather than sin — the antitype of Jeroboam is the leader through whom God's people are formed toward God rather than away from him.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The direction is *negative type pointing toward positive antitype*. Jeroboam's contagious sin is the established negative benchmark (repeatedly named in 12+ Kings verses as "Jeroboam... who made Israel to sin"). The positive antitype is the leader whose inner commitment to God shapes the community toward God — implied by the corpus's structure (God-given authority designed for moral formation toward God). Josiah's demolition of the Bethel altar (2Ki 23:15) is the typological act of reversal: the physical dismantling of Jeroboam's legacy anticipates the comprehensive undoing of sin-transmitting leadership that the eschatological expectation envisions.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Contagious sin." The name signals three things. (1) *Contagious*: the characteristic is not self-contained in the leader but spreads outward — as a disease passes from one person to another, the leader's sinful inner commitment is transmitted to the community. The contagion metaphor names the mechanism: proximity to the source (the leader) and the medium (authority relationship) combine to transmit the moral direction. (2) *Sin*: the transmitted content is specifically the leader's sinful direction, not merely their moral influence. What spreads is the wrong thing — the defection from God. (3) The name implies a *source and a recipient*: unlike other sinning characteristics that name the internal state of a single person, contagious sin requires a transmission structure — a leader (source) and a community (recipient). The name thus encodes the relational-authority framework within which the characteristic uniquely operates.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The defining Hebrew term is the **Hiphil stem of cha.ta (H2398)**: *heheti* — "caused to sin," "made to sin." This is the causative form of the standard sinning verb: where the Qal (basic) stem names the person who sins themselves (CHAR-1/2/4/5/6), the Hiphil names the person who causes or enables another's sinning. The Hiphil construction appears across the corpus in the formula *heheti et-Israel* ("he made Israel to sin") and its Judah equivalent. This grammatical distinction — Qal personal sinning vs Hiphil caused-sinning — is the vocabulary-level marker that separates CHAR-7 from all adjacent characteristics. The NT does not contribute distinct terms in the 19-verse corpus; the characteristic is an OT-Hebrew phenomenon expressed through the Hiphil causative. The definitional revelation: CHAR-7 is the characteristic that the Hiphil of cha.ta uniquely names — sinning that operates through causing another to sin.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: contagious sin moves from inner (the leader's sinful commitment) to outer (the community's sinning) — the direction is source-to-recipient, inner-to-community. 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — the directional movement is explicitly outward from the leader's act to the community's redirection. *Relational*: the transmission requires the authority relationship — 1Ki 16:2 — "I made you leader over my people Israel" — the leader-people relationship is the medium through which sin travels. Without the authority relationship, there is no contagious sin, only personal sinning. *Constitutional*: the community's inner moral orientation is reshaped by the leader's transmitted sinning — what was oriented toward God is driven away (2Ki 17:21) and turned toward idols (2Ki 21:11). The recipients' constitutional inner direction is altered.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is contagious sin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin is a *relational-causal inner-being phenomenon* — it requires two inner-being realities in a causal relationship. (1) The *leader's inner commitment to the sinful course*: 1Ki 16:19 — "walking in the way of Jeroboam" — the leader's personal inner direction is the source. The sinning is not accidental but reflects the leader's own settled will. (2) The *community's redirected inner orientation*: 2Ki 17:21 — Jeroboam "made them commit great sin" — the community's inner direction has been redirected by the leader's influence. The characteristic is not purely an inner-being state (it requires two parties) nor purely a social phenomenon (it originates in and produces inner-being states in both parties). It is uniquely a *transmitted inner moral direction* — the leader's inner sinful orientation flowing through authority to reshape the community's inner orientation.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The corpus reveals three constituent elements. (1) *The leader's personal sinning as the source*: 1Ki 14:16 — "the sins of Jeroboam, which he sinned" — the personal Qal sinning is always present alongside the Hiphil causative; the leader is themselves sinning. (2) *The causative transmission through authority*: "and made Israel to sin" — the Hiphil causative is the transmission mechanism; the leader's authority is the channel. (3) *The structural embedding*: 2Ki 23:15 — Jeroboam's altar at Bethel — contagious sin builds physical-institutional structures (altars, high places) that outlast the individual leader and continue to transmit the sinning orientation to subsequent generations. These three elements (personal source + authority transmission + structural deposit) constitute the characteristic's complete form.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Working description: *Contagious sin is the inner moral dynamic in which a leader's personal sinful commitment is transmitted to the community under their authority through the exercise of that authority. The transmission mechanism is the Hiphil causative: the leader does not merely sin themselves (Qal) but causes others to sin (Hiphil). The source is always the leader's inner will committed to the sinful course (Jeroboam "walked in the way" — 1Ki 16:26; kings "walked in his sin" — 1Ki 15:26); the channel is the authority relationship ("I exalted you... leader over my people" — 1Ki 16:2); the result is the community's redirected inner orientation away from God (2Ki 17:21 — "drove them from following the Lord"). The characteristic operates through three stages: inner source (leader's sinful will) → authority transmission (Hiphil causative) → structural deposit (altars, institutions — 2Ki 23:15 — that embed and perpetuate the transmitted orientation).*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of contagious sin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The structural opposite is the leader whose inner commitment to God is transmitted to the community through the same authority relationship, forming them toward God. The corpus names this opposite through contrast: 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's demolition of Jeroboam's altar — Josiah is the implicit structural opposite of Jeroboam: a leader whose inner commitment to God drives the dismantling of the sin-transmitting structures. Exo 23:33 — "they shall not dwell in your land, lest they make you sin against me" — the implicit opposite is the community that remains uninfluenced by the foreign sinning direction, maintaining its God-orientation. The structural opposite of contagious sin is contagious righteousness — leadership whose inner commitment transmits righteous orientation to the community.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does contagious sin explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin explicitly excludes: (1) *The community following the Lord*: 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — following the Lord is what contagious sin excludes from the community's orientation. (2) *The uninterrupted God-human authority chain*: 1Ki 16:2 — God elevated the leader to form his people; contagious sin breaks the intended chain (God → leader → people toward God) and replaces it with leader → people away from God. (3) *The community's moral independence from the leader's sin*: Exo 23:33 — the divine command to prevent foreign nations from dwelling in the land is precisely to prevent the loss of moral independence; contagious sin excludes the community's capacity to maintain their own God-orientation in the face of the leader's transmitted sin.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is contagious sin not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Three boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent)*: contagious sin is the Hiphil-caused transmission of sin through authority; CHAR-5 is the individual will refusing to turn. The community members who receive the contagious sin may themselves exhibit CHAR-5, but the contagious sin itself is the transmission event, not the recipient's volitional response. (2) *Not CHAR-6 (Habitual defection)*: habitual defection is the formed dispositional pattern in an individual over time; contagious sin is the causal mechanism through which a leader transmits their sinful direction. The community's habitual defection may result from sustained contagious sin, but the contagious sin is the transmission, not the resulting disposition. (3) *Not mere bad example*: contagious sin involves the authority relationship as the transmission channel — the leader's sin reaches the community specifically because the leader has authority over them. Peer influence without authority is not contagious sin in the CHAR-7 sense; the characteristic requires the Hiphil causative authority structure.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does contagious sin operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Three modes within the *leader* (source): (1) *Walking in the sinful course as the leader's own inner commitment*: 1Ki 16:26 — "walked in all the way of Jeroboam" — the leader's personal inner alignment to the sinful path is the prerequisite for transmission; (2) *Misusing the authority position*: 1Ki 16:2 — "I... made you leader... and you have walked in the way of Jeroboam" — God names the misuse of the given authority as the indictment; (3) *Building structures that transmit the sinning*: Jer 32:35 — "they built the high places of Baal... to cause Judah to sin" — the constructive agentive act embeds the transmission into a permanent structure. Within the *community* (recipient): the inner moral direction is bent — 2Ki 17:21 — "drove Israel from following the Lord" — the community's inner God-orientation is replaced by the sinning direction transmitted through the leader's authority.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does contagious sin operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. (1) *Pre-emptive/warning context*: Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18 — contagious sin is anticipated before it occurs; the warning names the mechanism in advance (foreign nations will cause Israel to sin through proximity and teaching). (2) *Monarchic leadership context*: the Kings corpus (1Ki 14:16 through 2Ki 23:15) — contagious sin operates through the formal authority of kingship; the king's inner commitment is transmitted to Israel through royal authority. (3) *Prophetic accusation context*: Jer 32:35 — specific leaders building specific sinning structures (high places) that cause specific abominations (child sacrifice). (4) *Historical retrospect/judgment context*: 2Ki 17:21 — the narrator explaining Israel's exile as the consequence of Jeroboam's contagious sin across generations.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does contagious sin have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Limited speech-based evidence. Deu 20:18 — "that they may not *teach* you to do according to all their abominable practices" — the teaching mode is explicitly named: contagious sin can operate through verbal instruction in the sinning practices. The foreign nations teach Israel; the teaching is a communicative mode through which the sinning orientation is transmitted. 1Ki 16:2 — God's indictment is verbal/prophetic — but this is God's speech about contagious sin, not the mechanism of contagious sin itself. The primary mode of contagious sin is structural-behavioural (walking in the sinful path, building altars) rather than verbal, but the teaching-mode (Deu 20:18) is a secondary speech-based mechanism.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when contagious sin is encountered?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The corpus evidences two responses when the community encounters the leader's transmitted sin. (1) *Compliance and adoption*: the dominant pattern in the Kings corpus — Israel/Judah follows the leader's sinning path; 1Ki 15:26 — "walked in the way of his father, and in his sin which he made Israel to sin" — the community walks in the transmitted path. The immediate response is alignment with the leader's sinning direction. (2) *Escalation*: 1Ki 14:22 — "Judah did what was evil... more than all that their fathers had done" — the community does not merely replicate the transmitted sin but escalates beyond it. The immediate response to contagious sin from a prior generation can be to exceed the transmitted level.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The Kings corpus shows consistent compliance as the dominant response. Across 12+ king-formula repetitions, the pattern is: leader transmits the sinning direction → subsequent leaders and people follow. The exception is 2Ki 23:15's Josiah — who responds to the transmitted sinning legacy by demolishing its structural deposit rather than complying. Josiah's response is the outlier that confirms the pattern's consistency: every other response in the corpus is compliance or escalation; Josiah's demolition of the altar is the singular reversal. The variable is whether the response is compliance (dominant) or reversal (exceptional).

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The inner phenomenological experience of the community members receiving the transmitted sin — what it feels like from within to be formed toward sin by a leader's authority — is not described in the corpus. The corpus describes the outcomes (Israel sinning, following the Lord being abandoned) but not the subjective inner experience of the transmission.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does contagious sin produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Two sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Progressively diverted allegiance*: 2Ki 17:21 — "drove Israel from following the Lord" — the sustained effect is the progressive movement of the community's inner allegiance away from God. Over generations of leadership transmitting the same sinning direction, the community's inner orientation is increasingly shaped away from God-following. (2) *Escalating provocation of God*: 1Ki 14:22 — "more than all that their fathers had done" — sustained contagious sin across generations produces not stable sinning but escalating sinning; each generation receiving the transmitted sin adds to it, producing an escalating relational offence against God.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained contagious sin establish?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Sustained contagious sin establishes: (1) *Institutional structures that become self-perpetuating*: 2Ki 23:15 — Jeroboam's altar at Bethel is still standing generations after Jeroboam's death, still transmitting the sinning orientation; (2) *Communal identity shaped around the sinning direction*: 2Ki 21:11 — Manasseh's abominations have shaped Judah's communal moral identity — "has made Judah also to sin with his idols"; (3) *Generational transmission*: 1Ki 22:52 — Ahaziah follows "the way of his father and... his mother and... Jeroboam the son of Nebat, who made Israel to sin" — three generations of transmitted sinning orientation each contributing to the received pattern.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. The immediate response is compliance or escalation (walking in the transmitted sinning path). The sustained effect is structural: the sinning orientation becomes embedded in institutions (Jeroboam's altar — 2Ki 23:15) that outlast any individual leader and continue to transmit the sinning direction without requiring a new leader's active causative intent. The sustained effect transforms contagious sin from a leader-driven transmission event into a self-perpetuating structural reality. This structural persistence is why Josiah must physically demolish the altar (2Ki 23:15) rather than merely counter the leader's personal sinning — the structure has become the ongoing transmission mechanism.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions in the leader enable contagious sin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Three evidenced enabling conditions in the leader. (1) *Personal inner commitment to the sinful course*: 1Ki 16:26 — "walked in all the way of Jeroboam" — the leader must be internally committed to the sinning direction; they cannot transmit what they do not themselves hold. (2) *Occupation of the authority position*: 1Ki 16:2 — "I exalted you out of the dust and made you leader over my people Israel" — the authority position is what makes the inner commitment's transmission effective. The same inner sinning commitment in a non-leader produces personal sinning (CHAR-1/5/6) rather than contagious sin. (3) *Active exercise of leadership toward the sinning direction*: 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — the active exercise of leadership energy in the sinning direction is the causative mechanism. Passive personal sinning without leadership-directed transmission does not produce CHAR-7.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions in the community enable or block reception of contagious sin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Dependence on the authority figure*: the community's reception of the transmitted sin is enabled by their structural position under the leader's authority — they receive what the leader transmits because the leader is their authority; (2) *Absence of independent God-orientation strong enough to resist*: Exo 23:33 — the warning implies that without the protective boundary (removing foreign nations), the community will be vulnerable to sin-transmission through influence. Reception-blocking conditions: 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's response shows that a leader whose inner commitment is to God can reverse the transmission by dismantling the structures. The blocking requires either a leader whose own inner commitment to God overrides the transmitted sinning pattern, or the community's own independent resistance.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters contagious sin from a leader but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — The corpus does not evidence the inner-being state of a community member who resists the leader's transmitted sinning direction. The corpus shows compliance (dominant Kings pattern) and structural reversal (Josiah). The individual community member who privately maintains their God-orientation while the leader transmits the sinning direction is not addressed in the 19-verse corpus. This is a gap in the evidence.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of contagious sin?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The primary inner-being dimension is **will/volition** — specifically the leader's will operating in its *causative* mode. The Hiphil causative of cha.ta — the grammatical marker that distinguishes CHAR-7 from all other sinning characteristics — is precisely the form that names the will acting to cause another's sinning, not merely one's own. 1Ki 16:2 — "you have walked in the way of Jeroboam and have made my people Israel to sin" — the will is the agent ("walked") and the causative force ("made... to sin"). The will is primary at both ends: the leader's will is committed to the sinning direction and exercises its authority to transmit it; the community's will is redirected by the transmitted influence.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — (1) 1Ki 16:19 — "walking in the way of Jeroboam, and for his sin which he committed, making Israel to sin" — the will expressed through walking (personal commitment) and the Hiphil causative (transmitted to others); (2) 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — drove (*niddah* — to push away, banish) names the causative volitional force; (3) 1Ki 16:2 — God holds the leader accountable for "making my people... to sin" — the accountability is volitional (you chose to walk, you chose to make); (4) Jer 32:35 — "they built the high places... to cause Judah to sin" — the building is an agentive volitional act with causative intent.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does contagious sin carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Relational*: the authority relationship is the medium through which contagious sin operates — without the leader-people relational bond, there is no transmission; (2) *Institutional/structural*: 2Ki 23:15 — the altar at Bethel — contagious sin produces structural deposits that become secondary transmission mechanisms; (3) *Moral identity of the community*: 2Ki 21:11 — Judah's corporate moral identity is shaped by Manasseh's transmitted sinning; the community's inner orientation becomes defined by the transmitted sin. These secondary dimensions describe the channels and consequences of the will's causative operation; they do not compete with the will-primary classification.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is contagious sin explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — The 19-verse corpus contains no explicit spirit-level (ruach/pneuma) location for contagious sin. The characteristic operates through the will (leader's committed direction), the authority relationship (institutional), and the community's redirected orientation — without explicit spirit-level vocabulary.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does contagious sin originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-7]** S — No evidence locates the origin at the spirit level. The origin evidence points to the leader's personal inner will (walking in the sinning path — 1Ki 16:26) and their authority position (1Ki 16:2) rather than to spirit-level phenomena.
