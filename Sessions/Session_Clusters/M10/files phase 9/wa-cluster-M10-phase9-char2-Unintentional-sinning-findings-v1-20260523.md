## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning reveals the character of God primarily through the remedial system God established to address it — a system that reflects God's moral seriousness about sin at every intentionality level, and his provision for the person who has sinned without knowing. Lev 4:2 — "if anyone sins unintentionally in any of the Lord's commandments about things not to be done" — presupposes a God who holds commandments whose violation is real even without deliberate defiance. The prescribed remedy (Num 15:28 — "the priest shall make atonement before the Lord for the person who makes a mistake, when he sins unintentionally... and he shall be forgiven") reveals a God who is both morally exacting (even inadvertent violation requires remedy) and graciously provident (a remedy is prescribed and forgiveness follows). The character of God visible here is *holy and merciful simultaneously*: the holiness requires that inadvertent sin be addressed; the mercy ensures that address is available.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The corpus contains no attribution of unintentional sinning to God. God appears in the CHAR-2 corpus as the standard-setter whose commandments are violated (Lev 4:2), the one whose altar requires purification (Exo 29:36; Eze 43:20), and the one whose forgiveness resolves the condition (Num 15:28; Lev 19:22). The absence is constitutive: God cannot sin unintentionally because he cannot be without knowledge of the moral reality of any act. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes the limitation of creaturely knowledge that God does not share.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The silence on God's possession of unintentional sinning (T0.1.2) reveals that this characteristic is grounded in *creaturely epistemic limitation* — the capacity to act without full knowledge of the moral implications of the act. God's omniscience excludes this capacity entirely: no act of God can be unintentional in the sense of proceeding without knowledge of its moral reality. The silence confirms that unintentional sinning belongs to the human condition as a *creature* (limited knowledge, limited perception) rather than to the divine image as such. The human capacity for inadvertent sin is the negative imprint of the creature's finitude, not a reflection of the divine image directly.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The verse evidence does not suggest that unintentional sinning serves a positive created purpose. Like CHAR-1, it is not something the created design equipped the person to express; rather, the prescriptions for addressing it (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) are remedial responses to a condition that requires correction. The cultic-purification corpus (VCG-02: Exo 29:36; Eze 43:20/22/45:18) similarly addresses contamination of sacred spaces, not a positive created function. The evidence is uniformly remedial — unintentional sinning creates a condition requiring resolution, not a capacity serving flourishing.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence points clearly to unintentional sinning as belonging to the fallen condition, with one important nuance. The Levitical system (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) presupposes that unintentional sinning is a real and recurring feature of human moral life requiring a standing remedial institution — not an exceptional aberration. Balaam's case (Num 22:34 — "I have sinned, for I did not know that you stood in the road against me") shows the characteristic operating in a context of limited creaturely perception: the sin arose because the angel was hidden from Balaam. This epistemic limitation (not seeing the angel) is a feature of creaturely existence that may be part of created finitude; however, the *moral weight* that attached to the act (requiring confession and offer to reverse course) belongs to the fallen condition. The evidence does not resolve the question of whether inadvertent violation would have existed in an unfallen state, but the need for remedy and the moral liability it creates belong to the fallen order.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Limited evidence. The Ezekiel cultic-purification corpus (Eze 43:20/22; 45:18) belongs to Ezekiel's vision of the restored sanctuary — a future-oriented eschatological context. The annual sanctuary purification (Eze 45:18 — "in the first month, on the first day of the month, you shall take a bull from the herd without blemish and purify the sanctuary") within the eschatological temple vision suggests that even in the restored order, a purification mechanism remains in view. Whether this is purely visionary-symbolic or implies the continuation of sin-addressing ritual in the eschaton is not resolved by the verse evidence itself. The orientation is toward a purified sacred space — the future fullness is a sanctuary fully cleansed, not a condition of unintentional sinning continued.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes two image-related features. (1) *Moral creaturely accountability*: Lev 4:2 ("if anyone sins unintentionally... and does any one of them") establishes that even inadvertent violation creates real moral liability — the person bears responsibility regardless of intent. This moral accountability presupposes the image-dimension of being a morally serious creature before God. (2) *Conscience-engagement post-awareness*: Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he has committed") shows conscience activating when the person comes to know what they did. The conscience that fires upon realisation is an image-bearing feature: it is the moral witness that marks the person as accountable to God. Unintentional sinning thus reveals the image's moral-seriousness feature by showing that even its inadvertent violation carries weight.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — Unintentional sinning is exclusively creaturely (see T0.1.2 and T0.1.3). The corpus contains no divine analogue and the concept is constitutively inapplicable to God. This characteristic has no shared element with the divine nature — it is grounded entirely in creaturely epistemic limitation and fallen moral liability.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The presence of unintentional sinning in a person reveals that they are a morally accountable creature operating within the bounds of limited knowledge — the image is present (they are accountable before God) but the creature is finite (they can act without full moral knowledge). The *response* to unintentional sinning is more revealing of image-condition than the sinning itself: Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses") shows the image working correctly — conscience engaged, acknowledgment made, remedy sought. Num 22:34 (Balaam — "I have sinned... if it is evil in your sight, I will turn back") shows the will responding rightly to the realisation of inadvertent sin. The person who responds to unintentional sinning with confession and remedy demonstrates an image functioning in its conscience-and-accountability dimension. The person who does not — who suppresses the realisation — would be approaching CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression), not CHAR-2.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The cultic-purification corpus (VCG-02) carries clear typological freight. The daily sin offering for the altar (Exo 29:36 — "every day you shall offer a bull as a sin offering for atonement... you shall purify the altar... and anoint it to consecrate it") is part of a larger typological system: the repeated daily and annual cleansing of the altar and sanctuary (Eze 45:18) points toward a final, comprehensive atonement that the sacrificial system cannot itself achieve but only anticipates. The individual unintentional-sin offering (Num 15:27/28 — goat offered, priest makes atonement, forgiven) similarly functions typologically: the external remedy for an inadvertent moral failure anticipates a remedy that addresses the inner moral condition more completely than a prescribed animal sacrifice can. Lev 4:3 (priestly sin implicating the community) prefigures the priest-as-representative who mediates between the community and God.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The direction is *human instance pointing forward to divine resolution*. The unintentional-sin offerings (Lev 4/5; Num 15:27/28) are human-institution responses to human moral liability that point forward to a divine act of atonement more complete than any animal offering can achieve. The ritual establishes the *shape* of atonement (substitutionary offering + priestly intercession + forgiveness declared) without being the substance. The direction runs from the repeated human cultic act toward the once-for-all divine provision. The cultic-purification VCG-02 (altar purification in Eze 43/45) points toward the eschatological sanctuary where God himself purifies the sacred space comprehensively.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Typological use is evidenced in T0.4.1 and T0.4.2. This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The programme name "Unintentional sinning" contains a qualifier ("unintentional") that is definitionally load-bearing: the name signals that the sinning act occurred without the inner person's deliberate defiance of a known command. The qualifier distinguishes this from CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning) not by minimising the moral weight of the act but by specifying the inner quality of the will at the moment of commission. Lev 4:2 uses the adverb *bishgagah* ("unintentionally / in error / by mistake") — embedded in the name is the Hebrew concept of *shegagah* (inadvertence, mistake, error), which carries the sense of wandering from the path without intending to. The name thus signals: this is real sinning (the same cha.ta term applies), but the inner-being state at the moment of the act lacked the knowing quality that characterises CHAR-1.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398)** accompanied by **bishgagah** (the adverbial qualifier, "unintentionally / in error") — the combination names the act (cha.ta = sin, miss the mark) and its volitional quality (bishgagah = without conscious defiance). Lev 4:2 and Num 15:27/28 both use this construction: *ki yecheta bishgagah* — "when he sins in error." The VCG-02 verses use cha.ta in the Piel stem as a denominative verb meaning "to de-sin / purify" (the altar, sanctuary) — a distinct usage that does not name a personal moral act at all but the ritual removal of impurity from a sacred object. At the definitional level, the moral-unintentional register (VCG-01) involves a person's act; the cultic-purification register (VCG-02) involves a process applied to a place or object.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The name carries primarily *constitutional* implications: unintentional sinning locates the characteristic within the inner person's epistemic constitution — the capacity to act without full knowledge. The relational implication is present but muted compared to CHAR-1: Lev 4:2 ("in any of the Lord's commandments about things not to be done") locates the standard relationally (God's commands), but the violation occurs without the relational *defiance* that characterises wilful sinning. The directional implication is toward God: even uninstructed violation is "unintentional in any of the Lord's commandments" — God remains the reference point. But the direction lacks the volitional turning-against that VCG-01 of CHAR-1 carries. Constitutional epistemic limitation is the defining implication.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning is primarily an *act* — a specific deed committed — but one whose inner-being content is distinguished by what is absent at the moment of commission: deliberate knowledge that the act is wrong. Lev 4:2 ("if anyone sins unintentionally... and does any one of them") is act-language: the person did something. Num 15:27 ("if one person sins unintentionally") confirms the act-character. The inner-being dimension that makes it distinctive is not the act itself but the epistemological condition under which it was committed: the conscience was not engaged at the point of action because the moral reality of the act had not yet surfaced to awareness. The act-character is primary; the inner-epistemic condition is what distinguishes it from CHAR-1.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — At first encounter the characteristic has two constituent elements visible in the VCG structure: (1) the *moral-unintentional act* (VCG-01: Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28; Num 22:34; 1Sa 14:33/34) — a person commits a real sin without deliberate knowledge, then conscience engages upon realisation; and (2) the *cultic-purification act* (VCG-02: Exo 29:36; Lev 8:15; Eze 43:20/22/45:18) — cha.ta used for the ritual cleansing of sacred objects, where no personal moral act by the sinner is in view. These two registers share vocabulary but differ in inner-being content. The characteristic's structure is two-register: personal moral (with conscience-engagement post-act) and ritual-cultic (with minimal personal inner-being content).

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Working description: *Unintentional sinning is a real moral act committed by a person in the absence of deliberate defiance of a known command — the will acts but not against a consciously registered moral prohibition. The act creates objective moral liability before God (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) regardless of intent. When the sin surfaces to awareness — through realisation, disclosure, or confrontation — conscience engages (Lev 5:5: "when he realizes his guilt... and confesses"; Num 22:34: "I have sinned, for I did not know"). The prescribed remedy (sin offering, priestly atonement, forgiveness declared) addresses the objective moral liability and restores the person's standing. The cultic-purification register (VCG-02) uses the same vocabulary for the cleansing of sacred spaces from cultic defilement, which carries minimal personal inner-being content.* Primary evidence: Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28; Lev 5:5; Num 22:34.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The structural opposite operates at two levels. (1) At the *intentionality* level: the opposite of unintentional sinning is CHAR-1 (wilful sinning) — the sin committed with knowledge and deliberate will. Num 15:30 (not in scope but contextually adjacent) contrasts the unintentional offering with the "high-handed" sinner who sins defiantly — this explicit contrast in Numbers 15 establishes wilful defiance as the structural opposite. (2) At the *sin/no-sin* level: the opposite is sinless integrity — the person who neither wilfully nor inadvertently violates God's commands. The corpus does not evidence this positive opposite directly within its 18 verses, but Lev 5:5's confession-and-remedy structure implies that the resolved state (forgiven, standing restored) is the positive terminus the characteristic points toward.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning explicitly excludes *deliberate defiance*: the bishgagah qualifier (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) specifically marks the act as not arising from a will consciously set against God's command. The Lev 5:1 verse (willful silence of a witness who "knows the matter yet does not speak") sits at the boundary — it involves a person who *knows* but withholds testimony, which approaches deliberate omission. The Pass A meaning marks it as "willful silence" — conscience is engaged because knowledge is present. This borderline verse reveals that CHAR-2 excludes acts where the person's knowledge of the moral reality was operative at the moment of the act. The characteristic resists: deliberateness, defiance, and conscience-active commission.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Three clear boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: the boundary is at the point of inner knowledge — CHAR-1 requires the person to know the act is wrong and proceed; CHAR-2 occurs when that knowledge is absent. (2) *Not CHAR-3 (Confession)* as such: Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses") shows confession as the *response* to unintentional sinning once awareness arrives — confession is the adjacent characteristic that follows, not the characteristic itself. (3) *Not the cultic-purification process per se*: VCG-02 uses cha.ta in its ritual-cleansing sense for sacred objects — this is not a person sinning unintentionally but a ritual process. The cultic register is included in the sub-group because it shares the same vocabulary and some of the same theological logic (objective defilement requiring remedy), but its inner-being content for persons is minimal.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Two distinct modes of operation within the personal-moral register (VCG-01). (1) *The ignorance mode*: the person acts without any awareness of the moral status of the act — Lev 4:2 ("sins unintentionally"), Num 15:27 ("sins unintentionally"). The conscience is not engaged at the moment of action because the morally relevant fact is not yet known. This is the purest form of the characteristic: the will acts, but not against a registered moral reality. (2) *The disclosure mode*: the sin becomes known — "when he realizes his guilt" (Lev 5:5), "the sin which they have committed becomes known" (Lev 4:14, adjacent to this corpus), "the sin which he has committed is made known to him" (Lev 4:23). At this point conscience engages and the person moves into the confession-and-remedy sequence. The characteristic thus operates in a before-awareness and after-awareness mode, with the transition point being the moment of disclosure or realisation.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes. The corpus evidences contextual differentiation. (1) *Individual context* (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27; Num 22:34): a single person sins unintentionally and the remedy is personal (a female goat, a sin offering, confession). (2) *Leadership/priestly context* (Lev 4:3; Lev 8:15/9:15): the anointed priest's sinning "brings guilt on the people" — the same unintentional act carries communal consequence when the actor holds representative authority, requiring a bull (costlier offering) and broader atonement. (3) *Communal context* (Lev 9:15 — "the people's offering"): the community's collective sin addressed through Aaron's prescribed communal sin offering. (4) *Cultic-spatial context* (VCG-02 — altar, sanctuary): the cha.ta verb applied to sacred spaces rather than persons. The direction also differs: individual sin operates vertically (person → God's standard violated); priestly sin radiates outward to the community.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Limited speech-based content. Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses the sin he has committed") shows the confession as the verbal component — the spoken acknowledgment of the sin once it surfaces to awareness. Lev 5:1 (the borderline witness-silence verse) involves a speech failure: the person who should testify but does not speak — sin of omission through silence. Num 22:34 (Balaam's confession — "I have sinned, for I did not know") is a speech-act: verbal confession of the inadvertent sin with an offer to reverse course. The communicative mode is primarily *confessional* (speech following realisation) rather than the *constitutive* role speech plays in CHAR-1 (where speech can itself be the sinning act). For CHAR-2, speech is the remedy-initiating mechanism rather than the mode of the sinning itself.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The immediate inner-being response, once the sin surfaces to awareness, is *guilt-recognition and its confessional expression*. Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he has committed" — names realisation-plus-confession as the immediate sequence. Balaam (Num 22:34) responds immediately to the angel's disclosure with: "I have sinned, for I did not know... if it is evil in your sight, I will turn back" — the immediate inner response is acknowledgment plus readiness to reverse. This immediate response distinguishes CHAR-2 from CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression): the person who suppresses awareness is not exhibiting CHAR-2 but its failure mode. The normative immediate response evidenced in the corpus is conscience engaging promptly once awareness arrives.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The immediate-response evidence is consistent within the VCG-01 moral register: realisation → confession/remedy in Lev 5:5, Num 22:34, and the cultic remedy framework of Lev 4:2/Num 15:27/28. The 1Sa 14:33/34 verses show a slightly different pattern: Saul responds to the *report* of the people sinning by urgently stopping the behaviour ("You have dealt treacherously; roll a great stone to me here") — the immediate response is corrective action rather than personal confession, because Saul is the leader addressing corporate sinning rather than the sinner confessing personally. The VCG-02 cultic-purification verses evidence no inner personal response (the ritual is applied to objects, not persons). The response is consistent in the personal-moral register and absent in the cultic-object register.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The VCG-02 cultic-purification verses (Exo 29:36; Lev 8:15; Lev 9:15; Eze 43:20/22; Eze 45:18) are silent on inner personal response — because in this register no personal moral act is in view; the cha.ta verb applies to the cleansing of objects. This silence is expected given the register, not a gap in the evidence.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The corpus for CHAR-2 is primarily an act-and-remedy corpus — it does not evidence sustained-effect trajectories in the way CHAR-1 (escalating sinning) does. The Levitical framework (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) addresses individual occurrences rather than a pattern that produces inner-being states over time. The sustained-effect evidence that is present: Lev 4:3 ("the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people") shows that repeated priestly sinning would accumulate communal guilt — a sustained inner-being effect on the community (collective guilt-load). The Eze 43/45 purification corpus suggests that sacred spaces accumulate defilement over time requiring periodic remediation — a sustained ritual-contamination dynamic. Within the personal-moral register, the corpus is predominantly episodic rather than developmental.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The 18-verse corpus provides insufficient evidence to characterise the sustained inner-being effects of repeated unintentional sinning on the inner person. The Levitical system's standing provision for unintentional sin (daily altar purification: Exo 29:36; annual sanctuary purification: Eze 45:18) implies that the accumulated effect of unintentional violations on the person and community requires ongoing remedial attention — but the corpus does not describe the inner-being state produced by this accumulation. This is a genuine evidential silence within the CHAR-2 corpus.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Within the evidenced material: the immediate response (realisation → confession → remedy) operates at the level of specific episodes. The standing cultic-remedy institutions (Exo 29:36 daily offering; Eze 45:18 annual sanctuary purification) operate at the sustained level — they address the ongoing accumulation of inadvertent defilement that episodic confession alone cannot clear. This suggests that even the act-by-act immediate response is insufficient as a complete remedy for the sustained effect of unintentional sinning in a community; the institutional purification system addresses what the episodic response leaves unremediated.

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable this characteristic to occur?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The enabling conditions for unintentional sinning are primarily *epistemic* — lack of knowledge or awareness of the moral status of the act. Lev 4:2 ("sins unintentionally in any of the Lord's commandments about things not to be done") — the enabling condition is not knowing that the specific act was prohibited. Num 22:34 (Balaam — "I did not know that you stood in the road against me") — lack of perception of the morally relevant fact. The 1Sa 14:33/34 context (people eating meat with blood in the urgency of battle) suggests situational pressure or distraction as an enabling condition — the full remedy was rushed and the prohibition bypassed in the urgency. The enabling conditions are thus: limited knowledge, limited perception, situational distraction, and the finitude of creaturely awareness.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The conditions that prevent unintentional sinning are primarily *instructional and ritual*: Lev 4:2's entire Levitical-law context presupposes that teaching the commandments reduces unintentional violations — knowing "things not to be done" means the violation is less likely to occur unintentionally. The Lev 5:1 borderline verse (witness who "knows the matter" but doesn't testify) shows that *knowledge* is the primary preventive factor: once knowledge is present, the person can no longer sin "unintentionally" in the pure sense. The prescribed cultic remediation (Exo 29:36 daily; Eze 45:18 annual) is not preventive but restorative — it does not prevent unintentional sinning but addresses it after the fact.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The question requires reframing for CHAR-2: unintentional sinning is not "received" from outside but committed. The more relevant question for this characteristic is: what is the inner-being state of the person who *fails to respond* when unintentional sinning surfaces to awareness — who does not confess or seek remedy? The corpus does not directly evidence this failure mode within CHAR-2's 18 verses. By implication, the person who suppresses awareness after the sin surfaces moves from CHAR-2 into CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression) territory. The CHAR-2 corpus presupposes a response; the absence of response is not evidenced here but is implicitly the boundary marker beyond which CHAR-2 ends.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary inner-being dimension of unintentional sinning is **moral accountability / conscience** — specifically conscience in its post-act engagement mode. The definitional distinction from CHAR-1 is that conscience does not engage at the moment of the act (because knowledge is absent) but engages upon realisation. Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses") and Num 22:34 (Balaam's immediate confession upon disclosure) show conscience as the faculty that fires when the morally relevant information arrives. The conscience is thus the primary inner faculty that processes unintentional sinning — not the will (which did not knowingly choose the wrong) but the conscience (which responds to the moral reality once it becomes available).

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The conscience-as-primary-dimension is supported by: (1) Lev 5:5 — the transition from "realizes his guilt" to "confesses" is a conscience-activation event; (2) Num 22:34 — Balaam's immediate moral response ("I have sinned") upon seeing the angel demonstrates conscience engaging on new information; (3) The entire Lev 4/Num 15 framework presupposes that once the sin surfaces to awareness, the person is morally obligated to respond — this obligation is the conscience's claim. The will is involved in the remedy (bringing the offering, confessing) but not in the original sinning act. Conscience is the faculty that bridges the gap between the uninformed act and the informed response.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, do they compete with the primary classification?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Secondary dimensions are present. (1) *Moral liability / guilt*: unintentional sinning immediately creates an objective state of guilt before God (Lev 4:2 — real sin; Num 15:28 — requires atonement). This guilt-state is the condition that conscience processes upon realisation; it is the objective counterpart to the conscience's subjective response. (2) *Communal accountability*: Lev 4:3 (priest's sin implicating the community) and Lev 9:15 (community sin offering) show that unintentional sinning carries a communal dimension — individual inadvertent violation can produce corporate moral liability. These secondary dimensions do not compete with the conscience-primary classification; they describe the objective moral landscape within which conscience operates.
