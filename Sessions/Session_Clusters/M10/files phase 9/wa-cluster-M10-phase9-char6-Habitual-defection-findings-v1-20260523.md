## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection reveals God's character through two lenses. (1) *God as the one who is sinned against habitually*: 2Ki 17:7 — "the people of Israel had sinned against the Lord their God, who had brought them up out of the land of Egypt" — God is the covenant Lord whose redemptive action is the context against which habitual sinning occurs. The relationship God established is the relational framework within which the recurring defection takes place. (2) *God as the one whose patience is provoked for an extended period*: Heb 3:17 — "with whom was he provoked for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned?" — God's provocation over forty years reveals his holiness responding to sustained rebellion — not immediate termination but prolonged moral witness sustained across a whole generation's habitual sinning. The character revealed is: morally serious toward sustained defection, patient in the sense of bearing it over time, and ultimately resolute in consequence (the bodies fell in the wilderness).

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute habitual defection to God?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — No verse in the 14-verse corpus attributes habitual defection to God. God appears as the party against whom Israel habitually sins (2Ki 17:7), the one provoked by sustained rebellion (Heb 3:17), and the one who will redirect the sinner's wealth (Pro 13:22). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes a creature whose will can establish a settled pattern of departure from God, which is structurally inapplicable to God himself.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The silence on God possessing habitual defection (T0.1.2) combined with 1Jo 3:6's positive statement — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — reveals that habitual defection is definitionally opposed to the condition of abiding in Christ (who images God perfectly). Abiding in him and keeping on sinning are mutually exclusive (1Jo 3:6). Since God's character is perfectly expressed in the one who abides in him — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — the silence confirms that habitual defection has no place in the divine nature. The characteristic is the negative inverse of the abiding relationship: where abiding produces freedom from habitual sinning, habitual defection is the condition of those who have "never seen him or known him" (1Jo 3:6).

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose habitual defection serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for habitual defection. The verses present it uniformly as a moral deficit with consequences: inescapable recompense (Pro 11:31), lost inheritance (Pro 13:22), destructive capacity (Ecc 9:18), extended divine provocation (Heb 3:17), and exclusion from knowledge of Christ (1Jo 3:6). The characteristic serves no positive created purpose; it is the settled will's direction toward sin, which the creation did not design.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether habitual defection is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus evidence points to habitual defection as entirely belonging to the fallen condition. 1Ki 8:46 — "there is no one who does not sin" — is the universal baseline: sinning is a feature of the present human condition, not the original design. Hos 10:9 — "from the days of Gibeah, you have sinned, O Israel; there they have continued" — the Gibeah origin-point situates habitual sinning within Israel's fallen historical narrative, not in creation. 1Jo 3:6's structural statement — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — implies that the abiding relationship (approximating the original created design) is precisely what prevents habitual sinning, confirming that the habitual pattern belongs to the fallen departure from that design.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that habitual defection is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus evidence orients habitual defection toward judgment and consequence, not toward fullness. Pro 11:31 — "how much more the wicked and the sinner!" — the sinner faces amplified recompense. Heb 3:17 — "whose bodies fell in the wilderness" — the 40-year pattern of habitual sinning ended in physical death-in-judgment. The trajectory evidenced is toward consequence and loss rather than fullness. 1Jo 3:6 points to a positive alternative — abiding in Christ — as the trajectory that breaks the habitual pattern, but the habitual sinning itself points in the opposite direction: toward the condition of one who "has never seen him or known him."

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does habitual defection express the divine image?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes the image's core features. The universality statements — 1Ki 8:46 ("no one who does not sin") and Ecc 7:20 ("not a righteous man on earth who does good and never sins") — apply to all image-bearers: the universal scope means the characteristic is found across all humans precisely because all are moral creatures with genuine wills that can be settled in the sinning direction. The capacity for the will to establish a settled pattern (whether toward God or away from him) is an image-bearing feature. Habitual defection uses the image's volitional-habituation capacity in the direction away from God.

---

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest habitual defection is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The corpus contains no divine analogue, and 1Jo 3:6 makes the structural incompatibility explicit: abiding in God is the condition that prevents habitual sinning. The characteristic is inapplicable to God.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence of habitual defection in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The presence of habitual defection reveals the divine image in a condition of settled misdirection: the will's habit-forming capacity has been trained in the direction away from God rather than toward him. Hos 8:11 — "altars multiplied for sinning, they have become altars for sinning" — shows how habitual sinning becomes self-perpetuating: the image's capacity to institutionalise and form patterns has been given to sinful worship, creating structures that reinforce the misdirection. 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" — reveals that habitual defection indicates a fundamental absence of the saving knowledge of God that would redirect the image's volitional capacities. The image is present (habits are being formed, the will is active) but its directional orientation has been captured by the sinning pattern.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use habitual defection typologically?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. Heb 3:17 deploys the wilderness generation's 40 years of habitual sinning typologically: "Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies fell in the wilderness?" — the wilderness generation's sustained habitual rebellion is presented as a *warning type* for the Hebrews 3 audience. The OT instance of 40-year habitual sinning functions as the negative typological precedent: this is what happens when the inner pattern of rebellion is sustained — the bodies fall. The typology warns the NT community against repeating the pattern. The direction is human instance (wilderness generation) → warning application to human readers.

---

**T0.4.2 — If typological use is present, what is the direction?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The direction is *past human instance functioning as warning type for present human instance*. Heb 3:17 uses the wilderness generation not to point toward a divine provision but to warn against the repetition of their pattern. The typological use is protective: the habitual sinning of the wilderness generation is deployed as a cautionary type, with the implied antitype being the community that avoids that pattern through faith and perseverance. The direction: OT negative precedent → NT warning application.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Typological use is evidenced (Heb 3:17 — T0.4.1 and T0.4.2). This prompt is superseded.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Habitual defection." The name encodes two claims. (1) *Habitual*: the characteristic is not a single act but a pattern settled over time — the will's default direction has been established toward sin. This contrasts with CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning — single act) and CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent — the decision-point of non-departure). The habit dimension signals that the sinning has moved beyond the episodic into the dispositional: it is the will's natural default rather than a departure from it. (2) *Defection*: the sinning is a going-away-from, an abandonment of loyalty — the image of someone who was in or should be in a covenant relationship but has repeatedly moved away from it. 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord their God, who had brought them up out of the land of Egypt" — the redemptive background makes the sinning a defection: they were brought out, they have defected back. The name signals: a formed inner orientation of departure from God, not merely occasional sin but the will's settled direction.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **cha.ta (H2398)** in its person-type participial/nominal forms: *hachotet* ("the sinner" — Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18; Pro 11:31; 13:22) and as a verb in pattern-contexts (Exo 9:34 — "sinned yet again"; Hos 8:11/10:9 — recurring sinning; Heb 3:17). The participial/nominal form *hachotet* is the distinctive marker of this characteristic: it names the settled person-type ("the sinner") rather than a specific act of sinning. The NT primary term is **hamartanō (G0264)** in the *present continuous* form: 1Jo 3:6 — *hamartanōn* (present participle: "the one keeping on sinning") — the continuous present aspect names the ongoing pattern rather than the completed act. The definitional revelation: habitual defection is expressed primarily through *person-type* terms (the sinner) and *continuous-action* forms (keeps on sinning), both of which distinguish settled pattern from single act.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — All three implications are present. *Directional*: "defection" names a movement away-from — the habitual sinner is oriented away from God and toward sin as the default direction. 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord... and had feared other gods" — the directional movement is from God-fearing to other-gods-fearing, a reorientation of the will's basic directional commitment. *Relational*: habitual defection occurs within and against a relational context — the Lord who "brought them up out of the land of Egypt" (2Ki 17:7) is the relational foundation against which the defection is measured. *Constitutional*: "habitual" signals a constitutional formation — the will has been shaped into its sinning direction by repeated acts. 1Jo 3:6's "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" names a constitutional-relational condition: the habitual sinner lacks the fundamental inner-being relationship with Christ.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is habitual defection?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection is a *settled dispositional condition* — a formed pattern rather than an act or episode. The dominant evidence shape in the corpus is person-type language: *hachotet* (the sinner — Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18; Pro 11:31/13:22) names a constituted inner orientation rather than an event. 1Jo 3:6's "keeps on sinning" (*hamartanōn* — present continuous) is the verbal equivalent of the person-type: it names a continuous state of the will rather than a discrete choice. The single act-evidence (Exo 9:34 — "sinned yet again") becomes habitual only through the "yet again" — it is the repetition that makes it habitual. The characteristic is dispositional: it names the will's settled direction, the inner orientation that produces sinning as the default output rather than the exception.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus reveals three constituent elements. (1) *Hardened will driving repetition*: Exo 9:34 — "he sinned yet again and hardened his heart" — the repeated sinning is linked to heart-hardening, showing that the mechanism of habituation is the will's increasing rigidity in the sinning direction. (2) *Self-reinforcing institutionalisation*: Hos 8:11 — "altars multiplied for sinning, they have become altars for sinning" — the sinful pattern builds structures that produce more sinning; the habitual defection institutionalises itself. (3) *Universal underlying condition*: 1Ki 8:46; Ecc 7:20 — "no one who does not sin" — the universal baseline from which habitual defection grows; all persons have the sinning susceptibility; habitual defection is when that susceptibility has become the settled orientation rather than the occasional exception.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Working description: *Habitual defection is the settled condition of the will in which sinning has become the default directional pattern rather than the episodic exception. It is characterised by: the person-type terms of the sinner (*hachotet*) as a constituted moral identity (Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18; Pro 11:31/13:22); continuous-present sinning as the operational state (1Jo 3:6 — "keeps on sinning"); the self-reinforcing dynamic of institutionalised sinning (Hos 8:11 — altars for sinning producing more sinning); and the historical persistence of the pattern across generations (Hos 10:9 — from Gibeah; Heb 3:17 — forty years). The characteristic is distinct from the single act (CHAR-1) and from the specific volitional refusal to turn (CHAR-5): it is what those characteristics produce when sustained — a formed inner disposition that requires no new decision to sin, because sinning has become the will's operational default.*

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of habitual defection?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The structural opposite is named in 1Jo 3:6: "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — *abiding in Christ* is the structural opposite. Where habitual defection is the settled pattern of ongoing sinning, abiding in Christ is the settled pattern of ongoing relationship that prevents habitual sinning. The Ecclesiastes person-type pair — sinner vs God-pleaser — also names the opposite: Ecc 7:26 — "he who pleases God escapes her, but the sinner is taken by her" — pleasing God (the inner disposition of God-orientation) is the structural opposite of the sinner's disposition. Pro 13:22's implicit opposite is the "good man" who leaves an inheritance: moral integrity and legacy as the opposite of the sinner's redirected wealth.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does habitual defection explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection explicitly excludes: (1) *Abiding in Christ*: 1Jo 3:6 — the mutually exclusive relationship: "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning; no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" — abiding and habitual sinning are structural contraries. (2) *Pleasing God*: Ecc 7:26 — "he who pleases God escapes her, but the sinner is taken" — the sinner's orientation is defined by not pleasing God. (3) *Moral legacy*: Pro 13:22 — the sinner's wealth cannot be transmitted; habitual defection excludes the capacity to build a lasting good inheritance. (4) *Escape from moral entrapment*: Ecc 7:26 — the sinner "is taken" by the snare — habitual defection is the condition of being caught rather than escaping.

---

**T1.3.3 — What is habitual defection not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three clear boundaries. (1) *Not CHAR-1 (Wilful sinning)*: CHAR-1 is the discrete act of committing a known wrong; CHAR-6 is the settled pattern that those acts have established over time. The single act of CHAR-1 may not constitute CHAR-6; CHAR-6 requires the pattern. (2) *Not CHAR-5 (Refusal to repent)*: CHAR-5 is the specific volitional moment of withholding the turn; CHAR-6 is the dispositional state that makes that refusal natural rather than contested — the habitual sinner may not need to actively refuse to repent because turning has become so alien to the formed will's direction. (3) *Not CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression)*: conscience suppression is the failure of the conscience to register sin; habitual defection is the settled pattern of sinning regardless of whether the conscience registers it. The two can co-exist but are analytically distinct.

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does habitual defection operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three modes. (1) *Will-hardening through repetition*: Exo 9:34 — "sinned yet again and hardened his heart" — the act of sinning again hardened the will's resistance capacity; each repetition makes the will more disposed toward the next sinning act. (2) *Self-reinforcing institutional embedding*: Hos 8:11 — "altars multiplied for sinning, they have become to him altars for sinning" — the sinning pattern builds structures (altars, institutions, practices) that produce more sinning; the inner disposition externalises into structures that feed back and deepen the inner disposition. (3) *Historical-generational entrenchment*: Hos 10:9 — "from the days of Gibeah, you have sinned... there they have continued" — the pattern persists across time and generations as an unbroken inner orientation that each generation receives and sustains.

---

**T1.4.2 — Does habitual defection operate differently depending on context?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. (1) *Individual psychological context*: Exo 9:34 — Pharaoh's individual will-hardening cycle (plague ceases → sin again → harden heart → next plague) — the pattern operates through individual psychological cycles of relief-from-pressure enabling sinning. (2) *National/communal context*: 2Ki 17:7; Hos 10:9 — habitual defection as a communal pattern of a whole people — national defection from covenant allegiance operating through shared practices (idolatry, false worship). (3) *Institutional context*: Hos 8:11 — the characteristic operating through religious institutions (altars) that have been misdirected and now reproduce the sinning orientation. (4) *Ontological/theological context*: 1Jo 3:6 — habitual sinning as the defining marker of not knowing Christ — the characteristic operates as an indicator of fundamental spiritual condition.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does habitual defection have a communicative or speech-based mode?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The corpus contains no explicit speech-based mode for habitual defection. The characteristic operates primarily through inner dispositional formation (Exo 9:34 — hardened heart), institutional structure (Hos 8:11 — altars), and sustained relational orientation (2Ki 17:7 — fearing other gods). The Ecclesiastes person-type statements (Pro 11:31; 13:22; Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18) are observer-descriptions rather than speech acts of the habitually sinning person. 1Sa 2:25 — "they would not listen to the voice of their father" — names a speech-reception failure (habitual non-listening) that could be considered communicative, but this is a resistance to others' speech rather than the characteristic's own communicative mode.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response when habitual defection is confronted?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two response patterns. (1) *Continued hardening*: Exo 9:34 — Pharaoh "sinned yet again and hardened his heart" immediately upon the plague ceasing — the response to the relief from external pressure is to sin again and harden further. The confrontation (plague) produces temporary cessation, then resumed sinning with increased hardening. (2) *Non-response to correction*: 1Sa 2:25 — Eli's sons "would not listen to the voice of their father" — the habitual defector's characteristic response to correction is non-listening. The habitual pattern has become sufficiently entrenched that the father's voice produces no corrective effect.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent or variable?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The corpus shows a consistent pattern: habitual defection does not yield to ordinary correction. Exo 9:34 (resumed sinning after plague-cessation), 1Sa 2:25 (non-listening to father), Heb 3:17 (40 years of sustained sinning despite divine provocation) — all show the pattern persisting under pressure, warning, and correction. The only structural break in the corpus is 1Jo 3:6's positive alternative: abiding in Christ breaks the pattern — but this requires a fundamental inner reorientation, not merely correction or warning. The immediate response to correction is consistently continued defection; only transforming union with Christ produces structural change.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The inner phenomenological experience of the person within habitual defection — what the pattern feels like from within — is not evidenced in the corpus. The corpus describes the condition (settled direction toward sin) and its external markers (altars multiplied, non-listening, sinning yet again) but does not describe the inner subjective experience of being habitually oriented toward sin.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does habitual defection produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two sustained inner-being effects. (1) *Progressive will-hardening*: Exo 9:34 — "sinned yet again and hardened his heart" — each iteration of sinning produces a harder inner disposition; the pattern is self-reinforcing and self-deepening. (2) *Structural blindness to God*: 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" — sustained habitual sinning produces or reveals a fundamental inability to perceive and know God. The sustained effect is not merely moral but epistemic-relational: the habitual sinner cannot see God.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained habitual defection establish?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Sustained habitual defection establishes: (1) *Moral identity as "the sinner"*: the Ecclesiastes person-type (Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18; Pro 11:31/13:22) describes someone whose moral identity has been constituted by the sinning pattern — the sinner is a type, a settled orientation, not merely an occasional actor. (2) *Vulnerability to entrapment*: Ecc 7:26 — "the sinner is taken by her" — the habitual sinner lacks the inner resource to escape the snare; the established sinning pattern has removed the dispositional capacity to resist. (3) *Destructive capacity*: Ecc 9:18 — "one sinner destroys much good" — the sustained sinning orientation gives the habitually sinning person a negative formative power disproportionate to their number: one established sinner can undo much accumulated good.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. The immediate response to confrontation is continued non-departure (the sinning continues — Exo 9:34; 1Sa 2:25). The sustained effect is more structural: moral identity formation as "the sinner" (Ecc 7:26; 9:2; Pro 11:31); progressive loss of the capacity to resist moral entrapment (Ecc 7:26 — taken); and the total relational-epistemic consequence (1Jo 3:6 — never seen or known Christ). The sustained effect thus moves from the behavioural level (continuing to sin) to the constitutional and relational-epistemic level (formed as the sinner-type; unable to perceive God).

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable habitual defection?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Pressure-relief cycles without inner reorientation*: Exo 9:34 — the plague ceasing is the enabling condition for Pharaoh's resumed sinning; the removal of external pressure allows the inner sinning disposition to re-express. The enabling condition is: no inner change during the relief period. (2) *Absence of the God-pleasing disposition*: Ecc 7:26 — the sinner "is taken" because he lacks what the God-pleaser has; the enabling condition is the absence of the God-oriented inner disposition that would enable escape. (3) *Fear of other gods / transferred allegiance*: 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord their God... and had feared other gods" — the inner transfer of fear/allegiance from God to other objects enables the sustained pattern of defection.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or prevent habitual defection?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The blocking conditions from the corpus: (1) *Abiding in Christ*: 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — the abiding relationship with Christ is the primary structural preventive against habitual defection. (2) *Pleasing God*: Ecc 7:26 — "he who pleases God escapes her" — the inner disposition of God-pleasing (God-orientation) enables escape from the entrapment that habitual defection produces. (3) *Responsive listening*: 1Sa 2:25 — the blocking condition opposite to "they would not listen" is responsive hearing of the corrective voice; hearing and responding to God and his representatives.

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who does not receive habitual defection — who breaks or avoids the pattern?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — 1Jo 3:6 provides the clearest evidence: the person who "abides in him" is the one who does not keep on sinning — the inner-being state is one of *sustained relational union with Christ* that structurally prevents the habitual pattern. Ecc 7:26 — "he who pleases God escapes her" — the inner-being state is one of active God-pleasing orientation, which provides the dispositional resource for escaping the snare that traps the sinner. The positive inner-being state is thus: abiding in Christ (1Jo 3:6), pleasing God (Ecc 7:26), and responsive hearing (the opposite of 1Sa 2:25) — all of which represent an active God-oriented inner constitution that prevents the sinning pattern from becoming habitual.

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of habitual defection?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary inner-being dimension is **will/volition** — specifically the will in its *dispositional/formational* mode rather than its moment-of-decision mode. The will that has been habituated to sinning is the primary faculty: Exo 9:34 — "sinned yet again and hardened his heart" — the will expressed through the hardened heart drives the repetition. 1Jo 3:6's "keeps on sinning" names the will's continuous operational state. The will here is not making a fresh choice at each moment (that would be CHAR-1) but has been formed into a direction that produces sinning as its default output.

---

**T1.8.2 — What evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — (1) Exo 9:34 — "hardened his heart" — the heart (as the volitional centre) is the faculty that is hardened; (2) 1Sa 2:25 — "they would not listen" — the "would not" is volitional; (3) Hos 10:9 — "you have sinned... there they have continued" — the continuation is volitional persistence; (4) 1Jo 3:6's "abides in him" as the structural opposite — abiding is a volitional-dispositional state; (5) 2Ki 17:7 — "feared other gods" — the fear/allegiance is a dispositional volitional commitment. All evidence confirms the will/disposition as the operative faculty.

---

**T1.8.3 — Does habitual defection carry secondary dimensions?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Relational/covenantal*: 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord their God, who had brought them up" — the habitual sinning is measured against a covenantal relationship; the relational capacity is damaged by the sustained defection. (2) *Moral identity*: the Ecclesiastes sinner-type — habitual defection produces a moral identity (the sinner as type) that is a character/person-type dimension. (3) *Epistemic*: 1Jo 3:6 — "never seen him or known him" — habitual defection produces an epistemic deficit (inability to know and see God). These secondary dimensions do not compete with the will-primary classification; they describe what the habituated will produces in other dimensions.

## T2 — Constitutional Location (31 prompts)

**T2.1.1 — Is habitual defection explicitly located at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — The 14-verse corpus contains no explicit spirit-level (ruach/pneuma) location for habitual defection. The characteristic operates through the heart (Exo 9:34), the will (throughout), and relational orientation (2Ki 17:7), but not through explicit spirit-level terminology.

---

**T2.1.2 — Does habitual defection originate at the spirit level?**

**[CHAR-6]** S — No evidence locates the origin at the spirit level. The origin evidence points to the heart's hardening (Exo 9:34), transferred allegiance to other gods (2Ki 17:7), and inherited communal patterns (Hos 10:9). These are heart-will-relational-level phenomena.
