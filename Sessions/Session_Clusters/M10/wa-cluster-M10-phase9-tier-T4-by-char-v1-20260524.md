## T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

### T4.1.1

**Question:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The God-to-human direction of wilful sinning operates in two modes in the corpus. (1) *God as restrainer*: Gen 20:6 — "it was I who kept you from sinning against me. Therefore I did not let you touch her" — God acts externally toward the human to prevent the sinning act. This is the most direct God-to-human operation: God intervening in the space between temptation and commission. (2) *God as the moral standard that constitutes the sin*: the God-directed sinning in VCG-01 is structured around God as the offended party whose character defines the boundary — Eze 14:13 ("when a land sins against me by acting faithlessly"), Jer 40:3 ("because you sinned against the Lord and did not obey his voice"), Jer 44:23. God does not commit wilful sinning but establishes the relational framework within which human sinning is defined.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — God operates toward the human in two modes within the CHAR-2 corpus. (1) *God as standard-setter whose commandments define the inadvertent violation*: Lev 4:2 ("in any of the Lord's commandments about things not to be done") — God's commands are the reference frame within which unintentional sinning is defined. The commandment is not here experienced as relational address but as objective moral standard. (2) *God as the one who forgives upon remedy*: Num 15:28 ("the priest shall make atonement before the Lord for the person who makes a mistake, when he sins unintentionally... and he shall be forgiven") — forgiveness is God's act, the divine response to the priestly mediation on behalf of the person who sinned inadvertently. God's movement toward the human is the granting of forgiveness — a relational restoration initiated from the divine side.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — God operates toward the human in the CHAR-3 corpus in two modes connected to confession. (1) *God as the one who receives and responds to confession*: Eze 33:16 — "None of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him. He has done what is just and right; he shall surely live" — God's response to the person who has turned and implicitly confessed is the active cancellation of the sin-record. This is God's movement toward the confessor. (2) *God as the one whose character is affirmed by confession*: Exo 9:27 — "the Lord is in the right, and I and my people are in the wrong" — Pharaoh's confession names God's righteousness as part of its content; the confession itself is a statement about God's moral character. Confession thus reveals God to be both *the standard who is vindicated* and *the one who receives and cancels* what is confessed.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — God operates toward the conscience-suppressed person in two modes. (1) *As the one who pronounces judgment on the denial*: Jer 2:35 — "Behold, I will bring you to judgment for saying, 'I have not sinned'" — God actively responds to the active-denial mode of conscience suppression with a declaration of accountability. God does not accept the false innocence claim but counters it with the announcement of judgment. (2) *As the standard against which the suppression is measured*: Ecc 8:12 — "those who fear God, because they fear before him" is the contrasting standard God represents. The person who suppresses conscience operates in the space of not fearing God — God's moral authority is what the suppression implicitly refuses. God's movement toward the conscience-suppressed person is primarily one of judgment-announcement and persistent prophetic witness (Jer 16:10 — the prophetic word declared as the context of the people's question).

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — God operates toward the person who refuses to repent in two modes. (1) *As the one who warns and calls to turn*: Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back to your law" — God's movement toward the refusing person is persistent invitational: warnings given, calling to the law, seeking their return. This reveals God's patient initiative even toward the refusing will. (2) *As the one who announces judgment when refusal persists*: Eze 18:24 — "for the treachery of which he is guilty and the sin he has committed, for them he shall die"; Heb 10:26 — "there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins" — when refusal becomes definitive and sustained, God's response shifts from warning to announced consequence. God's movement toward the refusing person is thus first redemptive (warnings) and then judicial (announced consequence).

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — God operates toward the habitually sinning person in two evidenced modes. (1) *Prolonged provocation*: Heb 3:17 — "with whom was he provoked for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies fell in the wilderness?" — God's sustained provocation across forty years of habitual sinning names both his holiness (he responds to sin) and his patience (the provocation is sustained, not instantly terminal). (2) *Redirecting consequence*: Pro 13:22 — "the sinner's wealth is laid up for the righteous" — God's providential ordering redirects the accumulated resources of the habitual sinner toward the righteous; the sinner's moral condition disqualifies the inheritance. Both responses show God actively engaged with habitual defection — not indifferent to it but consistently responding through both personal provocation and providential consequence.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — God operates toward the leader and community in two modes in the CHAR-7 corpus. (1) *Preventive warning*: Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18 — God issues commands specifically designed to prevent contagious sin before it occurs. The preventive word reveals God actively working to interrupt the transmission pathway before it opens. (2) *Judicial charge and consequence*: 1Ki 16:2 — "you have walked in the way of Jeroboam and have made my people Israel to sin, provoking me to anger with their sins" — God directly charges the leader with the act of contagious sin and announces consequence; 1Ki 21:22 — God's house-destroying judgment falls specifically because Ahab "made Israel to sin." God's movement toward the contagious-sin leader is one of explicit accountability — naming the act, its relational dimension ("my people"), and its consequence.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — God's operation toward the human in connection with political revolt is evidenced through 2Ch 21:10's causal structure: "because he had forsaken the Lord, the God of his fathers." God is the one who is forsaken — and the revolts that follow are presented as the consequence of God's withdrawal of covenantal support from the governing authority who forsook him. God does not initiate the political revolt, but his withdrawal from the forsaking ruler creates the conditions under which the revolts occur. God's operation is thus *consequential withdrawal* — his active covenantal engagement had sustained the political order; his withdrawal (in response to being forsaken) enables the political fragmentation.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — God operates toward the person who commits sinful speech in two modes. (1) *Declaration of consequence*: Mat 12:31 — "the blasphemy against the Spirit will not be forgiven" — God's word through Jesus declares the irreversible consequence of the most severe form; God's judicial word names what sinful speech against the Spirit produces. (2) *Mercy for lesser forms*: 1Ti 1:13 — "I received mercy because I had acted ignorantly in unbelief" — God's mercy extends to the blasphemer whose sinful speech arose from ignorance rather than hardened defiance. God thus operates toward sinful speech with both irreversible judgment (Spirit-blasphemy) and restorative mercy (ignorant blasphemy), depending on the form and the inner condition that generated it.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — God's operation toward those employing these mechanisms is evidenced through divine judgment. 2Pe 2:14 — "Accursed children!" — God's curse-verdict is pronounced against the enticers whose hearts are trained in greed and who entice unsteady souls. This divine judgment operates from God toward the practitioners of enticement as a relational consequence of the mechanism's operation. For hypocrisy and apostasy in this 5-verse corpus, God's direct operation toward the person is not explicitly named.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — God operates toward the person under the universal sin-condition in five distinct modes. (1) *Judicial non-forgiveness*: Joh 8:21/24 — "you will die in your sin... unless you believe that I am he you will die in your sins" — Jesus declares the eschatological consequence of the condition without remedy: dying in the condition produces final separation. (2) *Judicial condemnation of sin in the flesh*: Rom 8:3 — "he condemned sin in the flesh" — God acts judicially against the condition within the incarnation. (3) *Provision of purification*: Heb 1:3 — Christ making "purification for sins" — God provides the cleansing the condition requires. (4) *Declaration of forgiveness*: VCG-02 throughout — "your sins are forgiven" (Mat 9:2; Mar 2:5; Luk 5:20) — God through Jesus declares the condition's debt-dimension addressed. (5) *Spirit conviction*: Joh 16:8/9 — the Spirit convicts the world concerning the condition — God operates through the Spirit to make the condition known to those who cannot self-recognise it.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — God operates toward the person under sin's enslaving power in three modes evidenced by the corpus. (1) *God as liberator through new birth*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him" — God's operative act is the new birth that installs his seed as the constitutional counter to sin's enslaving hold; this is God's action toward the enslaved person. (2) *God as protector through Christ*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him" — God's protective action through the born-of-God (Christ) guards the liberated person against sin's re-enslavement. (3) *God as the one whose promise is the purpose of the imprisonment*: Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin, so that the promise by faith in Jesus Christ might be given to those who believe" — God's operation through the imprisoning declaration of Scripture is purposive: the confinement under sin is the necessary backdrop against which God's liberating promise operates.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — God operates toward the person bearing the divine record in four modes. (1) *Divine scrutiny*: Job 10:6 — "that you seek out my iniquity and search for my sin" (VCG-03); Job 10:14 — "if I sin, you watch me and do not acquit me" — God's active surveillance of the record directed toward the individual. (2) *Divine visitation of the record*: Exo 32:34 — "in the day when I visit, I will visit their sin upon them" — God's deferred enforcement of the record at its appointed time. (3) *Divine erasure of the record*: Isa 43:25 — "I will not remember your sins"; Jer 31:34 — "I will remember their sin no more" — God's sovereign act of record-cancellation directed toward the person. (4) *Covenant discipline*: Lev 26:18/21/24/28 — God's sevenfold escalating discipline in response to the accumulated record, directed toward the people to provoke turning.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the granting dimension is entirely God-to-human. (1) *Declaration*: Luk 7:48 — "your sins are forgiven" — Jesus's direct declaration toward the woman; (2) *Non-imputation*: 2Cor 5:19 — "in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses against them" — God's sovereign decision not to count; (3) *Making alive*: Col 2:13 — "God made alive together with him, having forgiven us all our trespasses" — God is the subject of both the making-alive and the forgiving; (4) *Provision of the mechanism*: Exo 29:36 — God institutes the daily sin offering; Lev 23:27/28 — God appoints the Day of Atonement; Mat 26:28 — Jesus pours out covenant-blood "for many for the forgiveness of sins."

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — God operates toward the community bearing the generational sin-condition in three modes. (1) *Anger and judgment*: Isa 64:5 — "you were angry, and we sinned" — God's anger is a response to the accumulated sinning; the divine anger is experienced as a force operating against the community; Jer 8:14 — "the Lord our God has doomed us to perish and has given us poisoned water to drink, because we have sinned against the Lord" — divine doom and poisoned water are God's active judgment directed toward the community bearing the generational condition. (2) *Naming the ancestral origin*: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — God's prophetic word operates toward the present community by naming the generational chain; this is itself a relational act. (3) *Redemptive promise*: Eze 37:23 — "I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — God's sovereign promise to save and cleanse operates toward the community from outside the condition.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — God operates toward the person bearing the sinner-identity in four modes. (1) *Divine naming*: 1Sa 15:18 — "Go, devote to destruction the sinners, the Amalekites" — God designates the people as sinners, which is itself a relational act of moral categorisation; Amo 9:8 — "the eyes of the Lord God are upon the sinful kingdom" — divine surveillance of the sinner-identity. (2) *Divine instruction*: Psa 25:8 — "he instructs sinners in the way" — God's grace toward sinners takes the form of moral guidance; the sinner is not abandoned but taught. (3) *Divine judgment*: Isa 13:9 — sinners destroyed on the day of the Lord; Psa 104:35 — "let sinners be consumed from the earth"; Jude 15 — "to execute judgment on all... ungodly sinners." (4) *Divine saving mission*: 1Ti 1:15 — "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners" — God's direction toward the sinner-identity is ultimately salvific; Mat 9:13 — "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners."

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — God operates toward the guilt-bearing person in five modes. (1) *Strategic withdrawal to provoke recognition*: Hos 5:15 — "I will return again to my place, until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God's withdrawal is purposeful; the relational absence is designed to provoke guilt-acknowledgment; (2) *Exposure of hidden guilt*: Jer 2:22 — "the stain of your guilt is still before me" — God's gaze penetrates concealment; Gen 44:16 — "God has found out the guilt of your servants"; (3) *Bringing guilt to remembrance in judgment*: Eze 21:23/24 — "he brings their guilt to remembrance... that they may be taken" — God reactivates the guilt-record at the moment of judgment; (4) *Removing guilt by sovereign act*: Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" — God's agent removes the guilt; Jer 33:8 — "I will cleanse them from all the guilt"; (5) *Exacting less than guilt deserves*: Job 11:6 — "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" — divine mercy moderates the punitive dimension.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — God operates toward the iniquity-bearing person in five modes. (1) *Measuring toward threshold*: Gen 15:16 — "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete" — God tracks and measures the accumulation; (2) *Seeing and not forgetting*: Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you, our secret sins in the light of your presence"; Jer 16:17 — "nor is their iniquity concealed from my eyes"; Hos 13:12 — "bound up; his sin is kept in store"; (3) *Visiting iniquity upon generations*: Exo 20:5 — "visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation"; (4) *Punishing at the appointed time*: Isa 26:21 — God comes out to punish the earth's inhabitants for their iniquity; Jer 25:12 — after seventy years, Babylon's iniquity is reckoned; (5) *Pardoning and removing*: Isa 40:2 — "her iniquity is pardoned"; Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more"; Zec 3:4 — "I have taken your iniquity away from you."

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — God operates toward the transgression-bearing person in five modes. (1) *Establishing the boundary that makes transgression possible*: Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — God's prior law-giving is the precondition; Exo 23:21 — "obey his voice; do not rebel against him" — the command precedes and enables transgression; (2) *Counting and tracking toward threshold*: Amos 1:3 — "for three transgressions... and for four, I will not revoke the punishment" — God's patient counting; Dan 9:24 — the decreed seventy weeks "to finish the transgression"; (3) *Withholding pardon in certain cases*: Exo 23:21 — "he will not pardon your transgression, for my name is in him"; Jos 24:19 — "he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins"; (4) *Blotting out by sovereign act*: Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake"; Psa 103:12 — removing transgressions as far as east from west; (5) *Declaring it through prophets*: Isa 58:1 — "declare to my people their transgression"; Mic 3:8 — "to declare to Jacob his transgression."

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — God operates toward the faithless person in five modes. (1) *Witnessing covenant bonds*: Mal 2:14 — "the Lord was witness between you and the wife of your youth" — God is the named witness of the covenant whose breaking is faithlessness; (2) *Declaring faithlessness through prophets*: Jer 5:11 — "the house of Israel and the house of Judah have been utterly treacherous to me, declares the Lord"; Eze 14:13 — "when a land sins against me by acting faithlessly" — prophetic declaration of the faithlessness; (3) *Judging faithlessness*: Eze 17:20 — "enter into judgment with him there for the treachery he has committed against me"; 1Ch 10:13 — Saul died for his breach of faith; (4) *Hiding his face*: Eze 39:23 — "they dealt so treacherously with me that I hid my face from them" — the divine-face withdrawal is the most intimate relational response; (5) *Responding to confession*: Lev 26:40-42 — "if they confess their iniquity and... treachery... I will remember my covenant" — God's covenant-memory restoration follows honest confession.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — God operates toward the perversion-bearing person in four modes. (1) *Hiding his face*: Deu 32:20 — "I will hide my face from them; I will see what their end will be, for they are a perverse generation" — God's response to perversity is relational withdrawal; (2) *Discipline rather than abandonment*: 2Sa 7:14 — "when he commits iniquity, I will discipline him with the rod of men... not take my steadfast love from him" — God disciplines the perverse act within sustained love; (3) *Observing and overturning*: Pro 21:12 — "the Righteous One observes the house of the wicked; he throws the wicked down to ruin" — God sees perversity and acts against it; (4) *Judging the perverting agent*: Rev 19:2 — "he has judged the great prostitute who corrupted the earth" — eschatological judgment of the corrupting agent; 1Cor 3:17 — "if anyone destroys God's temple, God will destroy him."

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — God operates toward the injustice-bearing person in five modes. (1) *Defining his own character as without injustice*: Deu 32:4; 2Ch 19:7; Psa 92:15; Job 34:10; Zep 3:5 — God's character is the standard against which injustice is measured; (2) *Hating injustice*: Isa 61:8 — "I hate robbery and wrong" — God's moral opposition is expressed as hatred; (3) *Commanding against injustice*: Lev 19:15/35 — "you shall do no injustice" — direct divine command; 2Ch 19:7 — "let the fear of the Lord be upon you"; (4) *Judging injustice*: 2Pe 2:9 — "keep the unrighteous under punishment until the day of judgment"; Rev 18:5 — "God has remembered her iniquities"; (5) *Rewarding faithfulness against injustice*: Heb 6:10 — "God is not unjust so as to overlook your work and the love that you have shown."

### T4.1.2

**Question:** What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-1]** S — God does not extend wilful sinning toward humans; wilful sinning is a human act directed against God, not a divine operation toward the human. The question as framed (God extending this characteristic) does not apply to CHAR-1. What the evidence does show is the basis on which God *responds* to human sinning: (1) *Restraint* (Gen 20:6 — preventive grace), (2) *Judgment* (Eze 18:4 — "the soul who sins shall die"), (3) *Forgiveness on confession* (2Sa 12:13 — "the Lord also has put away your sin"). The basis for God's responses is his own moral character and covenant commitment, not an extension of the characteristic itself.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — God does not extend unintentional sinning toward humans; it is a human act. The basis on which God responds to it is evidenced: forgiveness is provided through the prescribed remedy (Num 15:28; Lev 19:22 — "and he shall be forgiven for the sin that he has committed"). The basis appears to be the person's use of the prescribed atonement mechanism — the remedy is conditional on the offering and priestly intercession, not unconditional. God's provision of the remedy in the first place (the Levitical system) is the expression of his grace toward those who sin without knowing.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The basis evidenced for God's response to confession is *the turning of the will toward him*. Eze 33:16 — "He has done what is just and right; he shall surely live" — the context (Eze 33:14–16) shows God's response is conditioned on the person turning from wickedness to justice. Mic 7:9 — "until he pleads my cause and executes judgment for me" — the confessor trusts God to act on the basis of his own covenant faithfulness, not on the basis of the confessor's merit. The basis is not merit but covenantal relationship: God responds to the confession-and-turning because he is the covenant God who has bound himself to hear and respond when his people turn back.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — God's judgment is specifically on the *speech-act of denial*: Jer 2:35 — "I will bring you to judgment for *saying*, 'I have not sinned'" — not merely for sinning (already judged) but for the verbal denial of the sin. The basis is the active suppression of moral reality combined with the false innocence claim. God's response to the perceptual-blindness mode (Jer 16:10) is the prior declaration of judgment — the prophetic word has already been sent; the people's "what sin?" is asked in the face of that prior word. The basis is covenantal: God holds Israel accountable because they have his word, his commandments, and his prophetic witness — the conditions for moral self-knowledge were provided and are being refused or have been lost.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — God's warnings (Neh 9:29) are based on his covenantal commitment to call Israel back to his law — the basis is relational obligation, not mechanical operation. God warns because he is Israel's covenant Lord who desires their return. God's judicial response (Eze 18:24 — death for the sinning righteous person who does not repent; Heb 10:26 — sacrifice foreclosed) is based on the moral reality of sustained deliberate refusal: the basis is the person's own persistent choice. The shift from invitational to judicial is not arbitrary but is based on the person's own sustained exercise of the will against the available turn.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — God's response to habitual defection is covenantal and moral. 2Ki 17:7 — "the people of Israel had sinned against the Lord their God, *who had brought them up out of the land of Egypt*" — the basis of the relational expectation is the prior redemptive act; God's response to habitual defection is measured against what he has done in establishing the covenant. Heb 3:17's forty years of provocation — ending in "bodies fell in the wilderness" — is the consequence of sustained habitual rebellion against the one who had redeemed Israel from Egypt. The basis is not arbitrary but grounded in the relational-covenantal prior action of God.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — God's response is based on two things simultaneously: (1) *The leader's personal accountability*: 1Ki 16:2 — "you have walked... and you have made my people to sin" — the charge is personal and direct; God holds the leader accountable for both their personal sinning and its communal transmission. (2) *The relational stakes of the community's deviation*: 1Ki 14:16 — "he will give Israel up because of the sins of Jeroboam" — God's response is proportional to the national scope of the transmission. The basis is covenantal: God established the leader-people relationship as part of the covenant structure; the leader's abuse of that structure to transmit sin to God's covenant people is the specific ground of the judicial response.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — God's response in 2Ch 21:10 is covenantal and moral: the basis is Jehoram's personal act of forsaking the Lord ("forsaken the Lord, the God of his fathers"). The specific framing — "the God of his fathers" — names the covenantal continuity that Jehoram has broken; his forsaking is a breach of the inherited covenant faithfulness. God's response (the withdrawal that enables the revolts) is conditional on the covenant breach: fidelity to the covenant sustains the political order; faithlessness to the covenant allows the political order to fragment.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — God's response is differentiated by the inner ground of the sinful speech. 1Ti 1:13 — "I had acted ignorantly in unbelief" — the ignorance-ground qualifies for mercy. Mat 12:31 — blasphemy against the Spirit — the form that exceeds all others in its irreversibility; God's response is permanent non-forgiveness. Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man, and whatever blasphemies they utter" — the general category of blasphemy falls within the scope of divine forgiveness. The basis for the differentiated response is the specific form of the sinful speech and the inner condition (ignorance vs hardened defiance) from which it flows.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — God's response to the enticers (2Pe 2:14 — "Accursed children!") is based on: (1) the hearts trained in greed — the formed inner character of sinful appetite; (2) the deliberately targeting of "unsteady souls" — the predatory relational dimension of the enticement. The basis is both constitutional (what their hearts have become) and relational (what they do to others through the mechanism). The divine response is to those whose sinful mechanism has become fully formed (trained hearts) and is being actively deployed against the vulnerable.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — God's response rests on two foundations simultaneously. (1) *Covenantal faithfulness*: Rom 11:27 — "this will be my covenant with them when I take away their sins" — the removal of the condition is the content of God's covenantal promise; the basis is covenantal identity and promise. (2) *Holiness requiring propitiation*: Heb 2:17 — "to make propitiation for the sins of the people" — the condition creates a standing offence against God's holiness that requires propitiation; God's response (through Christ's provision) is calibrated to his own holiness. The basis is thus both covenantal (God's promise to take away sin) and moral (God's holiness that cannot absorb sin without propitiation).

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — God's response to sin's enslaving power rests on two bases. (1) *The purpose of the universal imprisonment*: Gal 3:22 — the Scripture's confining of everything under sin is "so that the promise by faith in Jesus Christ might be given" — God's response (the promise) is grounded in his redemptive purpose that the imprisonment was designed to serve. (2) *The birth-relationship*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God" — God's liberating response is grounded in the birth-relationship he establishes with the person; the new birth is the basis of the counter-power he installs (God's seed) against the enslaving force.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — God's response to the divine record rests on three bases. (1) *Holiness that cannot ignore the record*: Jos 24:19 — "he is a holy God. He is a jealous God; he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins" — Joshua's warning names God's holiness and jealousy as the basis for the record's moral force; (2) *Covenant faithfulness that promises record-erasure*: Jer 31:34 — the new covenant's record-erasure is grounded in God's own covenantal initiative; (3) *His own name's honour*: Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake" — the basis of record-cancellation is not the person's merit but God's own sovereign purpose for his name's glory.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Two bases are evidenced. (1) *Grace — God's own initiative*: Eph 1:7 — "the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace" — the basis is the riches of divine grace, not human merit; 2Cor 5:19 — "God was reconciling" — God initiates; 2Ch 29:24 — "for all Israel" — the scope is determined by God's command through the king. (2) *Covenant blood*: Mat 26:28 — "this is my blood of the covenant... for the forgiveness of sins" — the covenantal blood-provision is the specific ground on which God grants forgiveness; the atonement-mechanism is the covenant-established basis.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Two bases are evidenced. (1) *Moral causation*: Jer 8:14 — "because we have sinned against the Lord" — the divine doom is explicitly caused by the accumulated sinning; God's judgment-response is morally grounded in what the generational pattern has produced; Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the transgression against God is the basis of the relational deterioration. (2) *Sovereign redemptive purpose*: Eze 37:23 — "I will save them... and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — the covenant-restoration promise is grounded in God's own sovereign purpose, not in the community's remediation of the condition; God acts because of who he is (the covenant God) not because the community has earned rescue.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Two bases. (1) *Holiness that cannot coexist with the sinner-identity*: Heb 7:26 — "separated from sinners" — Christ's holiness is the basis of his total separation; Isa 33:14 — "who among us can dwell with the consuming fire?" — the consuming holiness is what the sinner-identity confronts; Joh 9:31 — "God does not listen to sinners" — the divine hearing is based on the person's moral standing before God. (2) *Grace that moves toward the sinner-identity*: 1Ti 1:15 — "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am the foremost" — the saving mission is directed precisely toward the worst sinner-identity; Rom 5:8 — "while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" — the basis of God's response is not the sinner's merit but his own love demonstrated at the worst moment of the human moral condition.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Two bases. (1) *Holiness that registers guilt infallibly*: Jer 2:22 — the guilt remains "before me" regardless of self-cleansing; Psa 69:5 — "the wrongs I have done are not hidden from you" — God's perfect moral knowledge is the basis for registering guilt that others cannot see; (2) *Covenant purpose that moves toward cleansing*: Jer 33:8 — "I will cleanse them from all the guilt of their sin against me, and I will forgive all the guilt" — the double-cleansing promise is grounded in God's covenantal purpose; Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" — the removal is by God's own initiative and agent.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Two bases. (1) *Holiness that cannot ignore accumulated crime*: Psa 130:3 — "if you, O Lord, should mark iniquities, O Lord, who could stand?" — God's holiness is the basis for the marking; Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God" — the separation is the consequence of holiness encountering crime; (2) *Steadfast love and covenant faithfulness that moves toward forgiveness*: Exo 34:7 — "keeping steadfast love for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin" — the forgiveness flows from steadfast love; Num 14:18/19 — "the Lord is slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love, forgiving iniquity... please pardon the iniquity of this people, according to the greatness of your steadfast love"; Mic 7:18 — "pardoning iniquity... he does not retain his anger forever, because he delights in steadfast love."

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Two bases operating in tension. (1) *Holiness and covenant faithfulness that must reckon with transgression*: Exo 23:21 — "for my name is in him" — the divine name's honour is the basis for non-pardon in this case; Isa 66:24 — "the men who have rebelled against me... their worm shall not die" — divine holiness produces irrevocable judgment on persistent rebellion; (2) *Steadfast love that moves toward pardon*: Num 14:18 — "the Lord is slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love, forgiving iniquity and transgression"; Mic 7:18 — "pardoning iniquity and passing over transgression for the remnant of his inheritance... because he delights in steadfast love" — steadfast love is explicitly named as the basis of pardon.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Two bases. (1) *Covenantal faithfulness that cannot ignore betrayal*: Mal 2:14 — God as witness means the covenant's breaking is registered in divine witness-memory; Eze 39:23 — God hiding his face is grounded in the treachery's personal directedness "against me"; (2) *Steadfast love that moves toward restoration*: Lev 26:42 — "I will remember my covenant with Jacob, and I will remember my covenant with Isaac and my covenant with Abraham, and I will remember the land" — God's covenant-memory is the basis of restoration; the very covenant that was betrayed is the ground of the restoration. The faithfulness God displays in remembering the covenant despite its betrayal is the basis for the restoration possibility.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Two bases. (1) *Holiness and wisdom that hates perversity*: Pro 8:13 — "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil. Pride and arrogance and the way of evil and perverted speech I hate" — God's character (wisdom, holiness) is constitutively opposed to perversity; (2) *Steadfast love that disciplines within relationship*: 2Sa 7:14 — "I will discipline him with the rod of men... but my steadfast love will not depart from him" — the steadfast love remains the relational basis even while discipline responds to perversity.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Two bases. (1) *God's intrinsic righteousness*: Deu 32:4 — "without iniquity, just and upright is he" — God's own character as the basis; Zep 3:5 — "the Lord... is righteous; he does no injustice; every morning he shows forth his justice" — God's consistent daily justice is the basis; (2) *God's love of justice*: Isa 61:8 — "I the Lord love justice" — the affective commitment to justice as the basis for hating its opposite; Heb 6:10 — "God is not unjust" — God's own justice-character prevents overlooking faithful service.

### T4.1.3

**Question:** What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-1]** S — Since God does not extend wilful sinning (T4.1.2), this prompt cannot be answered from the corpus as framed. What the corpus reveals about God's disposition toward sinning humans is: (1) *Moral seriousness* — "the soul who sins shall die" (Eze 18:4/20); (2) *Preventive care* — Gen 20:6 (active restraint to protect both Abimelech and Abraham's wife); (3) *Forgiveness available upon confession* — 2Sa 12:13; 1Ki 8:33/35/50 (Solomon's prayer structure). God's disposition toward sinning humans is one of moral seriousness combined with covenantal availability of forgiveness — not extension of the sinning characteristic itself.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — God's provision of a comprehensive remedial system for unintentional sinning (Lev 4:2 — the entire sin-offering structure; Num 15:27/28 — the individual inadvertent offering; Exo 29:36 — daily altar purification) reveals a disposition of *gracious moral realism* toward the creature. God holds moral standards that apply even when the creature acts without knowing (Lev 4:2 — the sin is real even when unintentional); simultaneously he provides the precise remedy that addresses the resulting liability (Num 15:28 — "he shall be forgiven"). The disposition is neither moral laxity (the sin is treated as real and requires remedy) nor moral severity without provision (the remedy is prescribed and forgiveness follows its use). God's disposition is structured around the creature's epistemic limitation — he provides for what he knows the creature will inadvertently do.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — God's disposition toward the confessing person is one of *merciful reception*. Eze 33:16 — sins not remembered when the person has turned — reveals a God who does not hold the confessed sin against the person indefinitely. Mic 7:9 — "He will bring me out to the light; I shall look upon his vindication" — the confessor trusts God's disposition to be ultimately restorative, moving from indignation to vindication. The corporate confessions (Judg 10:10/15; 1Sa 7:6/12:10) show God as the one before whom Israel prostrates in confession and to whom they appeal for deliverance — presupposing a God whose disposition is to receive corporate confession and respond with covenantal intervention. God's disposition is holy-and-merciful: he does not ignore the sin, but he does not permanently hold it against the one who confesses and turns.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — God's disposition toward the conscience-suppressed person is one of *unflinching moral honesty*: he does not accept the false innocence claim (Jer 2:35), does not allow the perceptual blindness to go unchallenged (Jer 16:10 — the prophetic word has been sent), and does not exempt the person from accountability (Jer 2:35 — "I will bring you to judgment"). God's response also reveals patience through the very existence of the prophetic word (Jer 2:35; 16:10): he has spoken before the judgment falls, giving opportunity for the suppressed conscience to be re-engaged. The disposition is: morally serious, unwilling to collude with the denial, and persistent in providing the moral truth that the suppressed conscience needs to hear.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — God's disposition toward the refusing person is: (1) *Patient and persistent in invitation*: Neh 9:29 — "you warned them" — the warnings are given before judgment; God does not immediately move to judgment but warns repeatedly. (2) *Morally serious in consequence*: Eze 18:24; Heb 10:26 — when refusal persists, the consequences are real and severe. God's disposition is not one of indifference to the refusal (he continues to warn) nor one of unlimited tolerance (the consequence is real and announced). The disposition is holy patience that does not capitulate to the refusal: God maintains his moral standard ("the law") while continuing to call the refusing person toward it.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — God's disposition toward the habitually sinning person is one of *patient holiness that reaches a consequence*. Heb 3:17 — provoked for forty years before the bodies fall — reveals patience: God sustained the relationship through prolonged habitual rebellion. 1Ki 8:46 — Solomon's prayer presupposing that God will hear the prayer of those who sin and then are taken captive — implies God's continuing receptivity to prayer and repentance even after habitual sinning. The disposition is not one of immediate retributive termination but sustained moral seriousness that both endures the habitual sinning and eventually brings its consequence. Pro 11:31 — "how much more the wicked and the sinner!" — the amplified consequence reveals a disposition of moral proportionality: the sinner's habitual defection receives proportionally amplified consequence.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — God's disposition toward the leader who practises contagious sin is one of *direct personal accountability without diminishment of the community's responsibility*. 1Ki 16:2 — "you have made my people Israel to sin" — God claims the people ("my people") even while the leader has corrupted them; the relational claim over the community is not dissolved by the leader's contagious sin. 1Ki 21:22 — house-destroying judgment — God's disposition is morally decisive when the leader's contagious sin has reached the scope of drawing the whole nation away. The disposition is not arbitrary severity but proportional moral seriousness: the scale of the leader's contagious sin determines the scale of the divine response.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — God's disposition toward the person who forsakes him is revealed by the consequence: the political order sustained by God's covenantal presence begins to fragment when that presence is forsaken. God does not impose the revolt directly — the revolt is the natural consequence of the forsaking. The disposition is: *covenantal faithfulness sustains order; faithlessness allows fragmentation*. The political revolt is God's providential consequence for the ruler's forsaking — not a punitive imposition from outside but the structural result of the ruler's own abandonment of the covenantal ground of legitimate governance.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — God's disposition is: (1) *Merciful toward those whose sinful speech arose from ignorance*: 1Ti 1:13 — Paul the former blasphemer received mercy — God's mercy is not withheld from those who spoke sinfully in ignorant unbelief; (2) *Forgiving toward most forms of sinful speech*: Mar 3:28 — "whatever blasphemies they utter" fall within the scope of forgiveness — the breadth of God's forgiveness extends to the category of sinful speech generally; (3) *Irrevocably judicial toward Spirit-blasphemy*: Mat 12:31 — a single specific form of sinful speech exceeds the scope of forgiveness entirely. The disposition is thus characterised by wide mercy and a single threshold of irreversible judgment.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — 2Pe 2:14's "Accursed children" reveals a disposition of judicial seriousness toward those who deliberately practise enticement against the vulnerable. The specific targeting of "unsteady souls" (2Pe 2:18) — those barely escaping from error — intensifies the moral severity. God's disposition is not indifference to the mechanism's operation but active judicial opposition to those who exploit it against the vulnerable.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — God's disposition is one of *engaged mercy within maintained holiness*. (1) *Mercy*: Mat 1:21 — "he will save his people from their sins" — God's purpose is salvific, not merely judicial; the mission of Jesus names mercy as God's disposition toward those in the condition; (2) *Maintained holiness*: Heb 5:1/3 — the persistent need for sacrifice for sins reveals that God's holiness does not simply absorb the condition — the condition requires addressed mediation; (3) *Patience*: 1Th 2:16 — the accumulated measure of sins that brings wrath "at last" implies that God's response has been withheld while the measure fills — patience is part of the disposition toward those in the condition.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — God's disposition is one of purposive liberating provision. (1) *Purposive imprisonment leading to promise*: Gal 3:22 — the confinement under sin is not God's final word but the penultimate condition that makes the faith-promise possible; God's disposition is toward liberation through faith, not permanent confinement. (2) *Protective guardianship*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him" — God's disposition toward the liberated person is one of active ongoing protection against re-enslavement; the disposition is not withdrawal after liberation but continued guardianship.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — God's disposition is one of *patient scrutiny moving toward either judgment or erasure*. The sevenfold discipline of Lev 26:18-28 reveals a patient escalating response designed to provoke turning rather than immediately destroying. Exo 32:34 — "in the day when I visit" — the deferred reckoning reveals patience. Isa 43:25 — "for my own sake" — the erasure reveals mercy grounded in God's own character. Amos 5:12 — "I know how many are your transgressions" — God's complete knowledge of the record does not require the person to confess what God already knows; the knowledge is already complete. The disposition is thus patient-knowing-and-ready-to-erase rather than eager to condemn.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — God's disposition is one of *welcoming and ready forgiveness grounded in provided mechanism*. Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man, and whatever blasphemies they utter" — the scope of divine willingness to forgive is total (all sins, all blasphemies) with a single exception (Mar 3:29). Eph 1:7 — "according to the riches of his grace" — the richness of the forgiveness reveals the richness of the disposition: God does not forgive grudgingly but abundantly. Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness" — the universality of "everyone" reveals no favouritism in the disposition; the forgiveness is available to all who believe.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — God's disposition is one of *morally engaged anger moving toward sovereign redemption*. Isa 64:5 — "you were angry, and we sinned" — the anger is real and provoked by the sinning-pattern; God is not indifferent to the accumulated condition. Jer 8:14 — doom declared — the judgment is the consequence of the sustained sinning. But Eze 37:23 — "I will save them... I will cleanse them... they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — the disposition moves through judgment toward restoration: God's ultimate purpose is not the community's destruction under the generational burden but their cleansing and covenant-restoration.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — God's disposition is one of *holy opposition moving through judgment toward redemption*. Isa 13:9 and Psa 104:35 name the judgment-disposition; 1Ti 1:15 and Rom 5:8 name the redemptive-disposition. Both are simultaneously operative. The defining statement is Rom 5:8 — "while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" — God's love is directed toward the person in their worst moral condition. Psa 25:8 — "he instructs sinners in the way" — God's disposition toward sinners includes ongoing moral guidance rather than mere abandonment to judgment. The twin disposition (judgment of unrepentant extreme sinners; redemptive engagement with repentable sinners) characterises the full corpus.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — God's disposition is one of *precise moral justice moving toward purposeful mercy*. Job 11:6 — "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" — mercy moderates justice. Hos 5:15 — the withdrawal until guilt is acknowledged reveals a patient relational strategy rather than immediate destruction; God waits for the acknowledgment. Jer 33:8 — "I will cleanse them from all the guilt" — the totality of the cleansing-promise ("all the guilt") reveals an intention to fully resolve rather than partially address. The disposition is neither indifferent (God sees and responds — Jer 2:22) nor punitive-only (mercy moderates — Job 11:6; cleansing promised — Jer 33:8).

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — God's disposition is one of *precise moral justice held in tension with enduring steadfast love*. Exo 34:7 — God both forgives iniquity and visits it on children — both responses flow from the same character; Psa 78:38 — "yet he, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often" — compassion repeatedly moderates full justice; Job 11:6 — "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" — mercy consistently moderates the full punitive application. The disposition is neither indifferent (Hos 13:12 — the record is maintained) nor implacably punitive (Mic 7:18 — he delights in steadfast love). The defining eschatological posture: Jer 50:20 — iniquity shall be sought and none found — total removal is God's ultimate goal.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — God's disposition is *patient justice sustained by steadfast love*. Amos 1:3 — the three-and-four threshold formula shows patience exhausted only at a specific limit; God has counted and waited. Isa 43:25 — "for my own sake" — the pardon flows from God's own character rather than the transgressor's merit. Isa 48:8 — "from before birth you were called a rebel" — God identifies the rebel-identity before birth yet still speaks to the person through Isaiah; identification of the transgressor does not equal abandonment. The defining eschatological statement: Dan 9:24 — "to finish the transgression" — God has appointed an end to transgression; his ultimate disposition is toward transgression's completion and termination.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — God's disposition is one of *personally wounded faithfulness sustaining covenant-memory toward restoration*. Ize 39:23 — "I hid my face" — the face-hiding is a personal response to personal betrayal; it is not mere judicial distance but relational withdrawal. Lev 26:42 — God remembers the covenant that was betrayed — the willingness to remember the very covenant whose breaking caused the wound reveals a disposition that sustains loyalty even through the experience of disloyalty. The eschatological resolution (Eze 39:26 — "they shall forget their shame") confirms: God's ultimate goal is not retribution but the restoration in which even the shame of the treachery is eclipsed.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — God's disposition is one of *holy opposition to perversity combined with disciplinary love toward the perverted person*. Pro 8:13 — wisdom hates perverted speech (holy opposition to the characteristic); 2Sa 7:14 — "my steadfast love will not depart from him" (disciplinary love toward the person). The distinction is important: God's hatred is of the perversity itself; his sustained commitment is to the person even as he disciplines the perverse conduct. Deu 32:20's face-hiding is for a time ("I will see what their end will be") rather than permanent abandonment.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — God's disposition is one of *righteous opposition to injustice combined with sustained moral patience toward the unjust person*. 2Pe 2:9 — "keep the unrighteous under punishment until the day of judgment" — not immediate destruction but sustained custody; Rev 18:5 — God "remembers" (retains in mind rather than acting immediately) — patience before final reckoning. Eze 33:15 — the wicked who stops doing injustice "shall surely live" — God's disposition sustains the possibility of reversal. The eschatological judgment (Rev 18:5; 2Pe 2:9) is deferred precisely because God's disposition holds open the path to repentance while maintaining the certainty of final accountability.

## T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

### T4.2.1

**Question:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The human-to-God direction is the primary directional axis of VCG-01 (32 verses). Wilful sinning *directed against God* is the defining characteristic of VCG-01: Gen 39:9 ("sin against God"), Psa 51:4 ("against you, you only, have I sinned"), Eze 14:13 ("sins against me"), Jer 40:3/44:23 ("sinned against the Lord"). In each case the sinning act is the human will moving *against* God — a negative movement toward God in which the relational bond is violated rather than honoured. Conversely, VCG-03's deterrence register shows the human-to-God movement in its positive mode: Psa 119:11 ("stored up your word in my heart, that I might not sin against you") — the word-storage is a positive movement toward God that guards against the negative sinning movement.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes. The remedy sequence in the VCG-01 corpus is fundamentally a human-toward-God movement. Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses the sin he has committed") — confession is directed toward God, an acknowledgment before the divine of what the person did. Num 15:27 ("he shall offer a female goat a year old for a sin offering") — the offering is brought "to the Lord" (Num 15:27 context: "before the Lord") — the act of bringing the offering is a human movement toward God in the wake of inadvertent sinning. Num 22:34 (Balaam — "if it is evil in your sight, I will turn back") — "in your sight" addresses the angel of the Lord, orienting Balaam's will toward God's evaluation.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes — this is the dominant directional axis of CHAR-3. The overwhelming majority of confessions in the corpus are explicitly directed toward God: "I have sinned against the Lord" (1Sa 7:6), "we have sinned against you" (Judg 10:10; Dan 9:11), "I have sinned against him" (Mic 7:9), "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you" (Luk 15:21). Confession is the human person's movement toward God after the movement away from God that sin constituted. Neh 1:6 — praying "day and night" with confession as the primary posture — shows sustained human movement toward God through the confessional posture. The direction is unambiguous: confession is the person moving back toward the God they sinned against.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression produces a *false or disrupted* human-to-God movement. Jer 2:35 — the person claims "surely his anger has turned from me" — they believe they are in good standing with God without the act of confession and turning that genuine standing requires. The movement toward God is simulated: the person acts as though the relationship is untroubled, but the simulation is based on a false self-assessment rather than genuine reconciliation. Jer 16:10 — the people ask "what sin have we committed?" in the context of a prophetic confrontation — their question may be an implicit appeal to God, but it is an appeal that does not acknowledge the relationship's actual state.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The human-to-God movement is disrupted by refusal to repent. The corpus describes the absence of the movement toward God that repentance would constitute: 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord, the God of Israel" — the movement toward God through law-walking is precisely what is withheld. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back to your law. Yet they acted presumptuously" — the movement toward God (turning back to his law) is available but the will moves in the opposite direction (presumptuousness). Refusal to repent is the blocking of the human-to-God movement: the will that could move toward God in turning chooses not to.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection *blocks and reverses* the human-to-God movement. 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord their God... and had feared other gods" — the direction of movement is explicitly away from the Lord and toward other gods; the human-to-God movement has been replaced by human-to-false-gods movement. 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" — the habitual sinner is not moving toward God; they are in a condition of fundamental non-encounter with God. The human-to-God movement that covenant life requires is absent: instead of approaching, the habitual sinner is persistently withdrawing.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin *reverses* the human-to-God movement in the community. 2Ki 17:21 — "Jeroboam drove Israel from following the Lord" — the community's movement toward God (following the Lord) is explicitly reversed by the leader's contagious sin; the movement now runs away from God rather than toward him. 1Ki 14:22 — Judah "provoked him to jealousy with their sins" — the community's movement is not toward God in worship but toward provoking him in sinning. The human-to-God movement that covenant life requires has been inverted by the contagious sin transmission: the community moves toward idols and away from God.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt's relationship to the human-to-God movement is evidenced negatively in 2Ch 21:10: the movement toward political revolt is linked to the *prior withdrawal from God* — "he had forsaken the Lord." The forsaking of the Lord (a movement away from God) precedes and enables the political revolts. Political revolt thus operates in a context where the ruler's human-to-God movement has already been reversed (the Lord was forsaken). The political revolt is a downstream consequence of the broken human-to-God movement.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Sinful speech directed at God is the *anti-movement* of the human-to-God direction: Rev 13:6 — "It opened its mouth to utter blasphemies against God, blaspheming his name and his dwelling" — the creature's speech is directed against God rather than toward him. VCG-02's blasphemy-charge against Jesus (Mar 2:7; Joh 10:33) represents the religious authorities' speech moving against the one through whom the human-to-God movement is restored. Sinful speech against God is constitutively the reversal of human-to-God movement — it is the creature speaking in the opposite direction.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — All three mechanisms disrupt the human-to-God movement. (1) *Enticement*: Jam 1:14 — "lured and enticed by his own desire" — the enticement draws the person away from God toward the desire-object, disrupting the human-to-God movement. (2) *Hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — "led astray by their hypocrisy" — the authentic covenantal identity through which the person moves toward God is falsified; the performed identity cannot sustain genuine God-directed movement. (3) *Apostasy*: Act 21:21 — "forsake Moses" — the departure from the covenantal framework is a movement away from the God-given covenantal structure rather than toward God within it.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The condition *blocks and reverses* the human-to-God movement. Joh 8:21 — "where I am going, you cannot come" — the condition creates an absolute barrier to the human person's movement toward where Jesus goes; Act 26:18 — "from the power of Satan to God" — the condition places the person in the domain opposite to God; Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — spiritual death prevents movement toward God. The condition is constitutively a relational barrier: the movement toward God requires addressing the condition first (repentance — Mar 1:4; forgiveness — Luk 1:77; the Spirit's conviction — Joh 16:8).

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Sin's enslaving power *blocks* the human person's movement toward God. Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin, so that the promise... might be given to those who believe" — the universal imprisonment under sin is what prevents human movement toward God through human effort; the imprisonment makes the movement possible only through faith (not through the imprisoned person's own power). Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin" — the slave's will cannot freely move toward God; the enslaving power constrains the will's God-directed freedom. Rom 6:2 — "how can we who died to sin still live in it?" — living in sin is incompatible with the God-oriented life of the one who has died to sin.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The record *blocks and burdens* the human person's movement toward God. Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you so that he does not hear" — the record severs both divine attention and divine response to prayer, blocking the human-to-God movement at the communication level. Exo 34:9 — Moses's prayer — "pardon our iniquity and our sin, and take us for your inheritance" — the movement toward God requires first naming and petitioning for the record's removal; the record is the obstacle that must be addressed before God-directed movement can succeed.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the seeking dimension is entirely human-toward-God. Exo 32:30 — "I will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin" — Moses's movement upward toward God is the seeking's directional metaphor; Act 2:38 — "repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness of your sins" — the commanded human movement toward God in repentance and baptism; Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives" — the believing is a movement of trust toward Christ; Lev 23:27 — "you shall afflict yourselves and present a food offering to the Lord" — the self-affliction and offering are Israel's collective movement toward God.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — through the corporate confession and repentance movement. 1Ki 8:47 — "if they turn their heart... and repent and plead with you... saying, 'We have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly'" — the movement toward God is explicit: turning, repenting, pleading, and confessing are all movements directed toward God from within the generational-sin condition. Jer 8:14 — "why do we sit still? Gather together; let us go into the fortified cities and perish there" — paradoxically, this text shows a movement away from seeking God (going to fortified cities) in hopeless acceptance of doom; the movement toward God is by contrast the preferred response named in 1Ki 8:47.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — through two contrasting modes. (1) *Positive movement toward God*: Luk 5:8 — "Depart from me, for I am a sinful man, O Lord" — Peter's movement is paradoxically a confession that draws him toward the holy even as it registers his unworthiness; Luk 18:13 — "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" — the movement toward God is explicit: the tax collector addresses God directly with the sinner-identity as the basis of the plea; Psa 51:13 — "sinners will return to you" — the return movement is the sinner's God-directed restoration; (2) *Negative movement against God*: Gen 13:13 — "sinners against the Lord" — the sinner-identity's directional orientation is against God; Heb 12:3 — hostility from sinners directed against Christ.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — through the acknowledgment-and-seeking movement. Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face, and in their distress earnestly seek me" — the guilt-acknowledgment is paired with the face-seeking of God; acknowledging guilt is simultaneously a movement toward God; Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God" — even the shame-posture of guilt is directed toward God; the face may not be lifted, but the address is toward God. Gen 44:16 — "God has found out the guilt of your servants; behold, we are my lord's servants" — the confession before Joseph is simultaneously a posture of submission that mirrors the guilt-before-God movement.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — through the acknowledgment-confession-plea movement. Exo 34:9 — Moses pleads "pardon our iniquity and our sin, and take us for your inheritance" — the movement toward God is through honest naming and plea; Psa 38:18 — "I confess my iniquity; I am sorry for my sin" — the voluntary movement toward God through confession; Hos 14:2 — "take with you words and return to the Lord; say to him, 'Take away all iniquity'" — the commanded movement toward God with the iniquity explicitly named; Psa 25:11 — "for your name's sake, O Lord, pardon my guilt, for it is great" — the movement toward God specifically through the acknowledgment of iniquity's greatness as the basis for the plea.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — through the acknowledgment-repentance-return movement. Psa 32:5 — "I will confess my transgressions to the Lord, and you forgave the iniquity of my sin" — the movement toward God is through verbal confession; Eze 18:30 — "repent and turn from all your transgressions, lest iniquity be your ruin" — the commanded movement is volitional repentance-turning; Isa 59:20 — "a Redeemer will come to Zion, to those in Jacob who turn from transgression" — the human turning is the condition for the divine coming; Hos 14:1 — "return, O Israel, to the Lord your God, for you have stumbled because of your iniquity."

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Yes — through the confession-and-return movement. Lev 26:40 — "if they confess their iniquity and the iniquity of their fathers in their treachery that they committed against me, and also in walking contrary to me" — the confession is the movement toward God; the treachery itself is named in the movement-toward; Ezr 10:2 — "we have broken faith with our God and have married foreign women from the peoples of the land, but even now there is hope for Israel" — the confession of faithlessness is simultaneously a movement toward God with hope; Ezr 10:10 — Ezra's declaration "you have broken faith and married foreign women" — the communal acknowledgment initiating the corporate return.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — through the confession-movement. 1Ki 8:47 — "we have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly" — the exile's confession of perversity is the movement toward God (followed in 1Ki 8:48 by "if they return to you with all their mind and with all their heart"); Dan 9:5 — "we have sinned and done wrong and acted wickedly" — Daniel's prayer naming perversity as part of the comprehensive movement toward God; Job 33:27 — "I sinned and perverted what was right" — the individual's confession before God.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — through the confession-and-cessation movement. Job 34:32 — "teach me what I do not see; if I have done iniquity, I will do it no more" — the movement toward God is through acknowledgment and volitional commitment to cease; Eze 33:15 — "the wicked restores the pledge... not doing injustice, he shall surely live" — the movement toward God through restitution and conduct-change; 2Sa 3:34 — "as one falls before the wicked you have fallen" — the lament implies the expectation of justice from God for unjust death.

### T4.2.2

**Question:** What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The sinning movement toward God requires an inner posture of deflection from God's moral claim — the will turning away from the covenantal obligation. 1Sa 15:24 shows this posture: Saul's fear of the people took priority over his fear of God — the inner posture was one of displaced allegiance. Jer 44:23 names the inner posture explicitly: "you did not obey the voice of the Lord or walk in his law and in his statutes" — non-listening and non-walking as the inner posture that constitutes sinning against God. The posture that prevents sinning (VCG-03) is named as its opposite: fear of God before you (Exo 20:20), word stored in heart (Psa 119:11).

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The inner posture required for the human-toward-God movement is *honest acknowledgment of the inadvertent sin*. Lev 5:5 — "confesses the sin he has committed" — the confession must name the sin as sin, even though it was unintentional. Num 22:34 — Balaam does not minimise ("it wasn't my fault, I didn't know") but takes full ownership ("I have sinned") — the inner posture is one of accountability without defensiveness. The posture does not require pre-existing awareness at the moment of sinning (since the characteristic is defined by its absence) but requires honest responsiveness once awareness arrives. The movement toward God is enabled by the conscience's retrospective engagement (T3.9) and blocked by its suppression.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The inner posture required for the human-toward-God confessional movement is *honesty without self-protection*. 1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned and done great evil. But these sheep, what have they done?" — the posture is complete ownership of the sin without deflection, combined with concern for the innocent. Luk 15:21 — "I am no longer worthy to be called your son" — the posture includes honest self-assessment of unworthiness without using that assessment as a bargaining position (the prodigal does not demand a position; he simply names the truth). Contrast: 1Sa 15:30 (Saul) — "I have sinned; yet honor me now before the elders" — the inner posture is compromised by self-protective concern. The required posture is honest self-naming without attached conditions.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression produces an *incorrect inner posture* for the human-to-God movement: Jer 2:35 — the posture of assumed innocence rather than the posture of acknowledged sin. The correct posture (confession — CHAR-3; fear of God — Ecc 8:12 contrast) is exactly what conscience suppression prevents. The inner posture of the conscience-suppressed person toward God is one of false confidence or genuine blindness — neither of which positions the person to receive what the relationship with God actually requires.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The corpus names the required inner posture for the human-to-God movement by its absence: 2Ki 10:31 — "with all his heart" — wholehearted commitment is the required posture; its absence ("not with all his heart") is what enables the refusal. Neh 9:29's "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" describes the actual posture: bodily-metaphor resistance, the opposite of submissive receptivity. The inner posture required for movement toward God is willing surrender of the neck and shoulder to the yoke of God's law — the posture of humility and submission that refusal to repent explicitly withholds.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The inner posture required for the human-to-God movement is named by contrast in two verses. 1Ki 8:46 — Solomon's prayer presupposes that hearing in captivity and turning (repenting) is the required posture for restoration: the movement toward God requires the acknowledgment of sin and the turning from it. 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — the abiding posture is the one that enables the sustained human-to-God orientation. The required inner posture is thus: the shuv-turn (repentance that 1Ki 8:46 envisions) and the abiding relationship (1Jo 3:6) — both of which habitual defection explicitly withholds.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The inner posture required for genuine human-to-God movement is named through contrast: 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's demolition of the altar — the posture of a leader whose inner commitment to God drives the community back toward God through structural dismantling. Josiah's act is the implicit positive model: a leader whose inner God-orientation enables the community's movement back toward God rather than away from him. Exo 23:33's preventive command — not allowing the sinning influence to dwell in the land — names the required posture of maintaining the community's God-orientation by protecting it from contagious sin transmission before it begins.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — 2Ch 21:10's causal structure implies the positive: the ruler whose inner posture is *faithful to the Lord* (not forsaking) maintains the covenantal ground that prevents political revolt. The required inner posture for the human-to-God movement — covenant fidelity, not forsaking the Lord — is what sustains the political order against revolt. When that posture is maintained, the revolts do not occur (or are resisted); when it is abandoned, the revolts follow.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — The inner posture that enables genuine human-to-God movement is implied through contrast with sinful speech. Jude 9 — Michael's posture: "The Lord rebuke you" — deferring to God's authority rather than presuming to speak God's verdict. This creaturely deference is the inner posture for movement toward God: acknowledging God's authority and not presuming to speak what only God can speak. 1Ti 1:13's reversal — from blasphemer to apostle — shows that the inner posture of receiving mercy and knowing God restores the human-to-God movement disrupted by sinful speech.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The positive inner posture is revealed through the mechanisms' negation. (1) *Against enticement*: stable desire oriented toward God (the opposite of "his own desire" drawing toward sin — Jam 1:14); (2) *Against hypocrisy*: authentic covenantal identity — living truly rather than performing (the opposite of Gal 2:13's collective performance); (3) *Against apostasy*: covenantal faithfulness — remaining in the framework rather than departing (the opposite of Act 21:21's forsaking). The required posture is: desire oriented toward God, authentic identity, and covenantal perseverance.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — VCG-02 establishes the required inner posture for movement toward God under the condition: (1) *Faith*: Mat 9:2; Mar 2:5 — "when Jesus saw their faith, he said... your sins are forgiven" — faith is the inner posture that enables the forgiveness-declaration to address the condition; Joh 8:24 — "unless you believe that I am he you will die in your sins" — belief in Jesus is the posture that breaks the condition's fatal hold; (2) *Repentance*: Mar 1:4; Luk 3:3; Act 3:19 — baptism of repentance for forgiveness — repentance is the inner turning required; (3) *Confession*: Mat 3:6; Mar 1:5 — "confessing their sins" — the inner act of acknowledging the condition is part of the required posture.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The inner posture required for human-to-God movement against sin's enslaving power is *faith receiving the promise*: Gal 3:22 — "the promise by faith in Jesus Christ might be given to those who believe" — faith-reception is the only posture that can receive the promise the universal imprisonment points toward; no human effort (imprisoned under sin) can achieve the promise. Additionally, *the posture of the born-of-God non-sinner*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning" — the born-of-God posture is the result of God's initiative (new birth) rather than human effort; the posture of non-sinning that enables God-movement is itself God-given.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Acknowledgment*: Psa 32:5 — "I acknowledged my sin to you, and I did not cover my iniquity; I said, 'I will confess my transgressions to the Lord'" — the posture of non-concealment and active acknowledgment is the first requirement; (2) *Turning*: Eze 18:21 — "turns away from all his sins that he has committed and keeps all my statutes and does what is just and right" — the volitional turning from sin is the required posture for the record to be addressed; (3) *Dependence on divine erasure*: Psa 51:2 — "wash me thoroughly from my iniquity, and cleanse me from my sin" — the posture of dependence on God's action rather than self-cleansing is the foundational human-to-God posture under the record.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Acknowledgment of the sin*: Exo 32:31 — Moses names it "a great sin" before God; Lev 4:14 — recognition when sin "becomes known"; (2) *Self-affliction*: Lev 23:27 — "you shall afflict yourselves" — the inner posture of humbling and penitential self-examination; (3) *Faith toward the forgiver*: Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives" — trust directed toward Christ; Act 2:38 — "repent" (metanoeō) — cognitive and volitional reorientation toward God. All three are required: mere recognition without self-affliction is incomplete; self-affliction without faith in the specific forgiver (Christ's name — Act 10:43) does not produce reception.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Heart-turning*: 1Ki 8:47 — "if they turn their heart" — the heart's volitional reorientation from the sinning-direction toward God is the primary required posture; (2) *Repentance*: 1Ki 8:47 — "repent" — the inner volitional act of changing direction within the inherited condition; (3) *Honest confession*: 1Ki 8:47 — "saying, 'We have sinned and have acted perversely and wickedly'" — the corporate speech-act of naming the condition accurately ("perversely and wickedly" — not minimising) is the verbal dimension of the required posture.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Honest self-naming*: Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man" — the movement toward God requires naming the identity accurately without minimisation; Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me, a sinner" — the self-naming precedes and enables the appeal; (2) *Self-abasement*: Luk 18:13 — "standing far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his breast" — the physical and inner posture of self-lowering is the required orientation; (3) *Appeal for mercy rather than justice*: Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me" — the movement toward God with the sinner-identity requires appealing to grace rather than claiming entitlement.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Acknowledgment*: Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt" — the first movement is honest inner acknowledgment; Jer 3:13 — "only acknowledge your guilt" — acknowledgment is the sine qua non; (2) *Confession*: Lev 5:5 — "confesses the sin he has committed" — verbal naming of what conscience has registered; Num 5:7 — "he shall confess his sin" — the speech-act before God; (3) *Restitution*: Lev 6:4 — "will restore what he took" — the movement toward God under guilt is not merely verbal but restitutive; the wronged person must be made whole.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Humbled heart*: Lev 26:41 — "if then their uncircumcised heart is humbled and they make amends for their iniquity" — the uncircumcised (unreformed) heart must be humbled before movement toward God becomes possible; (2) *Honest verbal acknowledgment*: Lev 26:40 — "if they confess their iniquity and the iniquity of their fathers"; Hos 14:2 — "take with you words" — the movement requires spoken words; (3) *Non-concealment*: Psa 32:5 — "I did not cover my iniquity; I said, 'I will confess my transgressions to the Lord,' and you forgave the iniquity of my sin" — the movement toward God requires uncovering what was concealed.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Volitional turning*: Eze 18:28 — "he considered and turned away from all the transgressions that he had committed" — the turn is both cognitive (considered) and volitional (turned away); (2) *Honest verbal confession*: Psa 32:5 — "I said, 'I will confess my transgressions to the Lord'" — the non-concealment; Pro 28:13 — "he who confesses and forsakes them will obtain mercy"; (3) *Non-self-justification*: Lam 3:42 — "we have transgressed and rebelled, and you have not forgiven" — the honest acknowledgment without self-excuse, even while still bearing unforgiven guilt.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Verbal acknowledgment of the specific treachery*: Lev 26:40 — "confess their iniquity and the iniquity of their fathers in their treachery" — the confession must name the faithlessness specifically, including generational dimensions; (2) *Humility (not pride)*: 2Ch 33:19 — Manasseh's prayer "and how God was moved by his entreaty" — the humbling (2Ch 33:12 — "he humbled himself greatly before the God of his fathers") is the counterpoint to 2Ch 26:16's pride that generated the faithlessness; humility is the required inner posture; (3) *Hope-maintained movement*: Ezr 10:2 — "but even now there is hope for Israel in spite of this" — movement toward God under faithlessness requires hope that the relationship remains accessible.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Honest naming of the perversity*: 1Ki 8:47 — "acted perversely" — the confession must name the specific distortion, not generic sin; Dan 9:5 — the comprehensive self-indictment includes "done wrong" (a.vah) specifically; (2) *Putting off the old self*: Eph 4:22-24 — "put off your old self which... is corrupt" → "put on the new self, created after the likeness of God" — the movement requires deliberate interior action at the level of identity; (3) *Sowing to the Spirit rather than the flesh*: Gal 6:8 — the orientation-change from flesh-sowing to Spirit-sowing is the practical inner posture required.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Three required inner postures. (1) *Volitional commitment to cease*: Job 34:32 — "if I have done iniquity, I will do it no more" — the will's commitment to stop; (2) *Active restitution*: Eze 33:15 — "restores the pledge, gives back what he has taken by robbery" — the movement toward God under injustice requires concrete material restitution, not merely inner intention; (3) *Hand-withholding*: Eze 18:8 — "withholds his hand from injustice, executes true justice between man and man" — the active volitional restraint paired with positive just conduct.

### T4.2.3

**Question:** What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The human-to-God direction of wilful sinning reveals that the relationship with God is the primary relational context in which sinning is defined. Psa 51:4 ("against you, you only, have I sinned") makes explicit what is implicit throughout VCG-01: even when sinning involves human victims (David's sin against Bathsheba and Uriah), the fundamental violation is against God. Eze 14:13 ("when a land sins against me by acting faithlessly") shows that faithlessness — covenant betrayal — is the relational content of sinning against God. The relationship with God is not a neutral backdrop but the constitutive framework within which sinning is possible: only within a covenant relationship can the breach be called sinning against God.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The human-to-God movement of confession and offering (Lev 5:5; Num 15:27; Num 22:34) reveals that the person's relationship with God is one in which *objective moral standing matters regardless of subjective intent*. The person who confesses an inadvertent sin is not confessing a felt rebellion — they are acknowledging that a real violation of God's standard occurred, even without deliberate defiance. This reveals a relationship defined by God's objective holiness (the violation was real) and the person's responsive accountability (I must address what I did, even though I did not know I was doing it). The relationship is not merely experiential or intentional — it is constituted by objective moral reality.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The God-ward direction of confession reveals that the relationship with God is the *primary relational frame* within which sin is understood and addressed. Psa 51:4 (contextually adjacent to this corpus — the paradigmatic confession verse) and Dan 9:11 ("we have sinned against him") both name God as the primary party against whom sin was committed. The human-to-God movement of confession reveals that the relationship is not destroyed by sin — the sinner still has access to God through confession (Neh 1:6 — "let your ear be attentive... to hear the prayer of your servant"). The relationship is damaged but not closed: confession is the act that re-opens the closed space between the sinner and God by naming what closed it.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The disrupted human-to-God movement reveals that conscience suppression has *severed the person's accurate relational self-knowledge before God*: Jer 2:35 — the person believes they are in a relational state (reconciled, anger turned away) that is the opposite of their actual state (under judgment). The relationship with God is real (God sees clearly — T4.1.1), but the person's understanding of where they stand in it is completely false. This false relational self-understanding is what makes conscience suppression so dangerous: the person is not seeking restoration because they do not know they need it.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The blocked human-to-God movement reveals that refusal to repent constitutes a *sustained relational posture of non-return*: the person is in relationship with God (within his covenant, subject to his law and warnings) but maintaining a direction away from him rather than toward him. The relationship is not terminated — God continues to warn (Neh 9:29), continues to call — but the person's directional movement is persistently away. Rom 6:15's emphatic rejection of "sinning under grace" reveals that even the grace-relationship does not dissolve the moral requirement to turn; the person who refuses to repent under grace is misusing the relational framework itself.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The disrupted human-to-God movement reveals that habitual defection constitutes a *comprehensive relational withdrawal* from God: 2Ki 17:7 — both the sinning (against the Lord) and the fearing of other gods name the same comprehensive re-orientation. The relationship with God is not severed from God's side (he continues to warn, to be provoked, to redirect consequence) but the human side has persistently withdrawn its directed movement. 1Jo 3:6 — "never seen him or known him" — the relational condition of the habitual sinner is one of never having had the foundational God-encounter that would orient the inner person toward him.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The blocked human-to-God movement (2Ki 17:21 — "drove... from following the Lord") reveals that contagious sin constitutes a *communal relational break with God*: the entire community's movement toward God is interrupted and reversed. The relationship is not severed from God's side — he continues to call (through prophets, through warnings — 1Ki 16:2), to claim the people ("my people" — 1Ki 16:2), and to announce judgment. But the community's side of the relationship has been redirected by the leader's transmitted sinning away from the Lord and toward false gods. The relational break is therefore asymmetric: God continues to maintain the relational claim while the community's directed movement has been reversed.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — 2Ch 21:10 — "because he had forsaken the Lord, the God of his fathers" — reveals that the ruler's relationship with God has been decisively broken by the act of forsaking. The forsaking is a prior act of political revolt at the theological level: the same pa.sha dynamic (breaking submission to governing authority) that produces political revolt against human authority mirrors the ruler's inner break with the Lord's covenantal authority. The political revolt of subject peoples reflects the ruler's own theological revolt against God.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Sinful speech against God reveals a *constitutive directional inversion*: the creature whose speech is directed against God has inverted the fundamental creature-Creator directional relationship. Rev 13:6 — blaspheming God's name, dwelling, and people — shows the full scope of the inversion: every dimension of the creature's proper God-orientation (acknowledging God's name, his presence, his people) is reversed into speech against them. The disrupted human-to-God direction in sinful speech is not merely broken but actively reversed — the creature speaks against God rather than merely failing to speak toward him.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The disrupted human-to-God direction in each mechanism reveals a specific form of misdirection: enticement misdirects the person's desire-motivation (away from God toward sinful objects — Jam 1:14), hypocrisy misdirects the person's social identity (toward performance rather than authentic God-oriented living — Gal 2:13), and apostasy misdirects the person's covenantal commitment (away from the God-given framework — Act 21:21). Each mechanism attacks the human-to-God movement through a different faculty and pathway.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The condition's blocking of the human-to-God movement reveals that the relationship between God and the sinful person is one of *created distance*: the condition has not eliminated God's claim on the person (he continues to pursue — Mat 1:21 — "save his people"; Luk 24:47 — gospel proclaimed to all nations) but has erected a barrier that only divine action can remove. Rom 5:12 — "death through sin... spread to all" — the death-barrier is universal; every person's movement toward God is under the condition's obstruction. The relationship is thus asymmetric: God continues to move toward the person (VCG-02 — forgiveness declared); the person cannot move toward God under the condition without the condition first being addressed.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The disruption reveals that sin's enslaving power creates a *structural incapacity* for God-directed movement: Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything" — the everything-imprisonment means no human power can escape toward God; the incapacity is total. Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin" — the slave cannot freely choose the master's opposite; the will's captivity is the precise mechanism of the incapacity. The disruption reveals that sin's enslaving power does not merely hinder human-to-God movement (making it difficult) but structurally prevents it from within; the captivity is constitutional, requiring a new birth (1Jo 3:9) to overcome.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The disruption reveals that the divine record is precisely a *relational barrier*: Isa 59:2 — the record makes separation and hides God's face. The barrier has two faces: it blocks God's hearing (divine-to-human communication blocked) and it blocks the person's effective prayer (human-to-God movement blocked). The dual blockage reveals the record's function as a comprehensive relational wall: accumulated sin does not merely inconvenience the relationship but structurally prevents its full operation until addressed.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The seeking direction (human toward God) reveals that the God-human relationship, though ruptured by sin (Isa 59:2 in CHAR-13 — separation), remains accessible: God has not withdrawn so completely that seeking is futile. Exo 32:30 — Moses says "perhaps I can make atonement" — the "perhaps" acknowledges the uncertainty but the going-up confirms the accessibility. Mat 26:28 — God's provision of the blood "for the forgiveness of sins" means the movement toward God is not toward an empty divine absence but toward a God who has already provided the mechanism of reception. The seeking thus reveals a relationship that is damaged but not destroyed.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — The movement-toward-God (1Ki 8:47 — turning, repenting, pleading) reveals that the God-community relationship, though severely damaged by the accumulated generational sinning (Jer 8:14 — doomed), remains accessible for the seeking community. God has not withdrawn so completely that turning is futile: the conditional "if they turn their heart... then hear from heaven" (the broader 1Ki 8 prayer context) implies God will respond to the turning. The relationship is damaged but not destroyed by the generational weight; the movement toward God can still reach him.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals two things: (1) the sinner-identity does not permanently foreclose movement toward God — Luk 18:13's tax collector can still address God; Luk 15:7/10 — joy in heaven over the returning sinner confirms God welcomes the movement; (2) the sinner-identity shapes the character of the movement — it cannot be approached with self-justification (implied by Mat 9:13 — sinners are those who know they need calling; the self-styled righteous do not recognise this need) but only with the posture of Luk 18:13's naked plea. The God-relationship is accessible to the sinner specifically through the sinner-identity's honest acknowledgment.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the God-relationship remains accessible to the guilt-bearing person precisely through the guilt-acknowledgment pathway. Hos 5:15 — God waits for acknowledgment as the condition of his return; the relationship is therefore not terminated by guilt but suspended pending acknowledgment. Ezr 9:6/15 — Ezra addresses God directly while bearing guilt ("I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God") — the guilt-bearing person can still address God from within the guilt-state; the relationship remains open even while the guilt-barrier persists.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the God-relationship remains accessible through iniquity-acknowledgment. Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God waits for the acknowledgment; the relationship is suspended rather than terminated. Psa 130:3/4 — "if you, O Lord, should mark iniquities, O Lord, who could stand? But with you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared" — the juxtaposition reveals the relationship's structure: the iniquity-reality would make standing impossible (v.3) but God's forgiveness character makes the approach possible (v.4). Movement toward God under iniquity is possible precisely because God's character is forgiveness.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the relationship remains accessible through the specific act of turning. Isa 59:20 — "a Redeemer will come to Zion, to those who turn from transgression" — God moves toward the one who turns; the turn is the signal of relational re-engagement. Isa 44:22 — "return to me, for I have redeemed you" — God's prior redemptive act is the ground for the human's return. The relationship is not terminated by transgression but suspended awaiting the turn: Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God waits, and the relationship is restored when the person moves.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the covenant relationship remains accessible through the specific act of naming the faithlessness before God. Lev 26:42 — "I will remember my covenant" following the confession — God's covenant-memory is reactivated by honest naming; Ezr 10:2 — "even now there is hope" — the movement is possible even after extensive faithlessness. The relationship is not terminated by faithlessness but suspended: Eze 39:23 — "I hid my face... and gave them into the hand of their adversaries" — the face-hiding is a relational suspension, not termination. The movement toward God with honest confession of treachery reopens the suspended relationship.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the perversion-corrupted person retains the capacity for movement toward God through confession and repentance. 1Ki 8:47-48 — the exile-caused perversion does not permanently foreclose return: "if they return to you with all their mind and with all their heart... and pray toward... this temple... then hear from heaven" — the sequence confession → return → prayer → divine hearing remains accessible. The perversion, even when it has produced generational consequences (exile), does not terminate the movement-toward-God pathway.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The human-to-God direction reveals that the relationship remains accessible through cessation and restitution. Eze 33:15 — "he shall surely live; he shall not die" — the full relational restoration following cessation of injustice; Job 34:32 — the movement is through acknowledging unseen injustice and committing to stop. The relationship is not terminated by injustice but held under the condition of God's patient custody (2Pe 2:9) awaiting either repentance or final judgment.

## T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

### T4.3.1

**Question:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. VCG-02 (18 verses) is entirely composed of interpersonal wilful sinning — one person extending sinning toward another. Gen 42:22 (brothers sinning against Joseph), Gen 20:9 (Abraham's deception bringing sin upon Abimelech), 1Sa 19:4/5 (Jonathan warning Saul against sinning against David), Mat 18:15 (brother sinning against brother), Luk 17:3/4 (repeated sinning against another), 1Cor 8:12 (sinning against brothers by wounding their conscience). The extension is always in the sinning direction: the one who sinning acts *against* the other, violating the relational claim.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — Limited evidence. Lev 6:2 ("sins and commits a breach of faith against the Lord by deceiving his neighbor in a matter of deposit or security, or through robbery, or if he has oppressed his neighbor") and Lev 6:3 (lying about a found item) present interpersonal sinning that is simultaneously a breach against the Lord — one person extending sinning toward another through deception and misappropriation. The 1Sa 14:33 context (people sinning against the Lord by eating with blood) is communal but not specifically one person extending sinning toward another person. Lev 4:3 (priestly sinning "bringing guilt on the people") shows inadvertent leadership sinning radiating outward to affect the community — an unintended interpersonal extension.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. Person-to-person confessions appear in several instances. (1) Exo 10:16 — Pharaoh confesses "against the Lord your God, and against you" — Moses as a human recipient alongside God. (2) 2Sa 19:20 — Shimei confesses "I have sinned" to the returning King David — a direct interpersonal confession. (3) Mat 27:4 — Judas confesses "I have sinned by betraying innocent blood" to the chief priests — a person-directed confession. (4) 1Sa 26:21 — Saul confesses "I have sinned" in the context of David's mercy — a relational confession between king and subject. The person-to-person direction is present but secondary to the God-directed primary axis.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Limited evidence. Tit 3:11 — the divisive person who is "warped and sinful" operates within a community, and the divisiveness itself is an extension of the conscience-suppressed character into the community — the person is causing division without apparently registering the sinfulness of doing so. 1Cor 15:34 — "some have no knowledge of God" — the knowledge-absence that enables ongoing sinning within a community of faith suggests that conscience suppression operates at a communal level where it may influence others through modelled behaviour. However, the corpus does not show one person explicitly extending conscience suppression to another.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Yes — the transmission dimension is extensively evidenced. The Jeroboam-sin formula includes the phrase "which he made Israel to sin" (2Ki 3:3; 10:29; 13:2/6/11; 14:24; 15:9/18/24/28) — each king's refusal to repent is extended to the people through leadership. The king's non-departure from Jeroboam's sins draws the whole nation into the same non-departure. 1Ki 16:31 — Ahab's treatment of Jeroboam's sins as "a light thing" while going further to serve Baal: the leader's refusal extends and escalates the communal sinful course. The characteristic is transmitted from leader to people through the leadership's continued walking in the sinful pattern.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. Ecc 9:18 — "but one sinner destroys much good" — the habitually sinning person's settled sinning orientation has a destructive extension toward others: the one sinner dismantles much accumulated good in the surrounding community. 1Sa 2:25 — Eli's sons' habitual defection ("sinned against the Lord") operates in a priestly-leadership context where their sinning directly affects the people they serve and the sacred functions they perform. Hos 8:11 — altars multiplied for sinning — the institutionalised sinning extends through community structures to shape the sinning orientation of all who participate in the misdirected worship.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes — this is the characteristic's defining relational operation. The Hiphil causative formula "made Israel/Judah to sin" appears across 12+ verses, each naming the leader extending the sinning direction to the community. 1Ki 14:16 — "the sins of Jeroboam, which he sinned and made Israel to sin" — the personal sinning and the extending to Israel are named side by side. 1Ki 16:2 — "made my people Israel to sin" — God explicitly identifies the giving-direction as the indictment. Jer 32:35 — "they built the high places of Baal... to cause Judah to sin" — a deliberate constructive act whose stated purpose is causing the community to sin.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Yes — this is the defining relational operation of political revolt: a subordinate community extending the revolt against a governing community. 2Ki 1:1 / 3:5 — Moab extending revolt against Israel; 2Ki 8:20 / 2Ch 21:8 — Edom extending revolt against Judah; 2Ki 8:22 / 2Ch 21:10 — Libnah extending revolt against Jehoram. The direction is always upward (subordinate breaking from governing authority) rather than downward (governing authority imposing revolt on subordinate). The giving-direction is the subordinate extending the revolt to the governing authority by breaking submission.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Yes — VCG-01 slander is constitutively directed from one person toward another. Eph 4:31 — "slander be put away from you" — the community context shows slander as the speech that one community member directs toward others. Act 6:11 — "We have heard him speak blasphemous words against Moses and God" — false witnesses direct blasphemous claims against Stephen, extending sinful speech as a weapon against him. Rev 2:9 — "the slander of those who say that they are Jews and are not" — a false identity claim directed against the true community. The person-to-person giving direction is present throughout VCG-01.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Yes — primarily for enticement. 2Pe 2:14 — "they entice unsteady souls" — the enticers deliberately extend the enticement mechanism toward others as targets. 2Pe 2:18 — "they entice by sensual passions of the flesh those who are barely escaping from those who live in error" — specific targeting of the vulnerable. Gal 2:13 — collective hypocrisy is extended from the initial actor (Peter) to the broader group — "the rest of the Jews acted hypocritically along with him, so that even Barnabas was led astray" — the hypocrisy mechanism spreads from person to person. Act 21:21 — the accusation claims Paul extends the apostasy-inducing teaching toward the Jews in the Gentile mission.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes — in several forms. (1) *The Adamic transmission*: Rom 5:12 — "sin came into the world through one man... death spread to all" — Adam's sin was extended to all humanity through the representative structure; (2) *Complicity*: 1Ti 5:22 — "nor take part in the sins of others" — sharing in another's sin is a form of extension; (3) *Targeted vulnerability*: 2Ti 3:6 — "capture weak women, burdened with sins" — the condition-burdened person's vulnerability is exploited by others extending the condition's reach; (4) *Intercession*: Act 7:60 — Stephen's dying prayer "do not hold this sin against them" — the sin of the persecutors extends to a level requiring intercessory forgiveness-appeal.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The extension dimension is present but indirect. 1Jo 3:8 — "the devil has been sinning from the beginning" — the devil's originary sinning is extended through those who practice sin; those who make a practice of sinning are "of the devil," meaning the enslaving power flows from its originary source (the devil's sinning) through individual human practitioners. The devil's enslaving power is extended through the human practices that align the practitioner with the devil's domain. Additionally, 1Jo 5:16 — "if anyone sees his brother committing a sin not leading to death, he shall ask" — the communal observation of a brother's sinning implies that sin's enslaving grip can be observed and addressed by others; the enslaving power operates within the community's relational sight.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — primarily through the Jeroboam-sin legacy (VCG-04). 1Ki 14:16 — "he will give Israel up because of the sins of Jeroboam, which he sinned and made Israel to sin" — Jeroboam's recorded sin is extended to Israel; the leader's divine-record entry becomes the nation's inheritance. Exo 32:21 — "what did this people do to you that you have brought such a great sin upon them?" (VCG-02) — Aaron's leadership failure extended a great sin onto the people. Gen 20:9 — "you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin" (VCG-02) — Abraham's deception transferred a sin-record entry onto Abimelech's household.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — through intercessory and priestly mediation. (1) *Intercessory seeking on behalf of others*: Exo 32:30 — Moses seeks atonement for the people's sin, not his own — "I will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin"; (2) *Priestly bearing of others' iniquity*: Lev 10:17 — "it is most holy and has been given to you that you may bear the iniquity of the congregation, to make atonement for them" — the priest extends the seeking on behalf of the whole congregation; (3) *National priestly act*: 2Ch 29:24 — "the king commanded that the burnt offering and the sin offering should be made for all Israel" — the seeking is extended by king and priests to encompass all Israel.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — through the foundational ancestral act and through mediatorial leadership failure. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the first father extended the sinning-condition to all subsequent generations through his originating act; the mediators extended and deepened it through their institutional transgressions. The extension mechanism is representational: the first father's position as the founding ancestor makes his sinning the starting-point of the lineage's condition; the mediators' position as covenant-leaders makes their transgressions part of what the community inherits.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — through social categorisation and active enticement. (1) *Social assignment*: Joh 9:24 — "we know that this man is a sinner" — the Pharisees extend the sinner-label to Jesus; Luk 7:39 — "for she is a sinner" — the Pharisee assigns the label to the woman; Luk 19:7 — "he has gone in to be the guest of a man who is a sinner" — the crowd extends the label to Zacchaeus; (2) *Active recruitment*: Pro 1:10 — "if sinners entice you, do not consent" — sinners actively extend their identity-pattern to others by enticement; (3) *Constituted through representation*: Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners" — Adam extended the sinner-identity to all humanity through his representative act.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — through the leadership-propagation and priestly-transference mechanisms. (1) *Leadership propagation*: Lev 4:3 — "if it is the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people" — the priest's sin extends guilt to the whole community; 1Ch 21:3 — "why should it be a cause of guilt for Israel?" — a leader's decision would propagate guilt to the nation; (2) *Voluntary self-assumption*: 1Sa 25:24 — "on me alone, my lord, be the guilt" — Abigail assumes Nabal's guilt for David; 2Sa 14:9 — "on me be the guilt, my lord the king" — the woman of Tekoa volunteers to bear the guilt from the king; (3) *Priestly representative bearing*: Exo 28:38 — Aaron bears Israel's cultic guilt on his forehead; Exo 28:43 — priests bear guilt if they enter improperly.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — through three extension mechanisms. (1) *Leadership propagation*: 1Sa 3:13/14 — Eli's iniquity extends to his house through his failure to restrain his sons; Lev 4:3 — "if it is the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people"; (2) *Generational transmission*: Exo 20:5 — "visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation" — God visits the fathers' iniquity on descendants; Lam 5:7 — "our fathers sinned, and are no more; and we bear their iniquities"; (3) *Representative loading*: Lev 16:21 — Aaron "confess over it all the iniquities of the people of Israel, and all their transgressions... and he shall put them on the head of the goat" — the community's iniquity is verbally transferred onto the representative goat.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — through three extension mechanisms. (1) *Leadership propagation*: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the mediators' transgression extended to the community they were meant to represent; Jer 2:8 — "the shepherds transgressed against me" — leaders' transgression affects those they lead; (2) *Anger-driven contagion*: Pro 29:22 — "a man of wrath stirs up strife, and one given to anger causes much transgression" — anger in the leader generates transgression in others; (3) *Communal multiplication*: Pro 29:16 — "when the wicked increase, transgression increases" — the social-contagion mechanism; Amos 4:4 — "come to Bethel and transgress... multiply transgression" — the social encouragement of communal transgression.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Yes — through three extension mechanisms. **[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — through social corruption and the great-prostitute archetype. (1) *Social corruption contagion*: 1Cor 15:33 — "bad company ruins good morals" — the perverse person's company corrupts the morals of those in proximity; the perversion extends outward through social influence; (2) *Cosmic-scale corruption*: Rev 19:2 — "the great prostitute who corrupted the earth with her immorality" — the archetype of the perverted agent whose corruption extends to the whole earth; (3) *False-teacher corruption*: 2Cor 7:2 — "we have corrupted no one" — Paul's denial implies the false teachers did corrupt — they extend perversion to their hearers; 2Cor 11:3 — "as the serpent deceived Eve... your thoughts will be led astray" — the external corrupting agent working on the hearers.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

(1) *Judicial extension*: Psa 82:2 — "how long will you judge unjustly and show partiality to the wicked?" — judges extending injustice to those who appear before them; (2) *Violent extension*: 2Sa 7:10 — "violent men shall afflict them" — the violent extending harm; Psa 58:2 — "your hands deal out violence on earth" — distributing injustice outward; (3) *Commercial extension*: Lev 19:35 — "do no wrong in judgment, in measures of length or weight or quantity" — commercial relationships as the context for extending injustice to counterparties; (4) *Civic-structural extension*: Mic 3:10 — "who build Zion with blood" — leaders extending injustice to the whole city through structural wrongdoing.

### T4.3.2

**Question:** What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The inner conditions that enable interpersonal sinning are evidenced as: (1) *Deception / self-protective fear*: Gen 20:9 — Abraham's fear-driven deception produced sinning against Abimelech's household. (2) *Hostile intent*: 1Sa 19:5 — sinning against innocent blood by killing David without cause — the hostile will as enabling condition. (3) *Contempt for the neighbour*: Pro 14:21 ("whoever despises his neighbor is a sinner") — the inner attitude of contempt enables and constitutes the sinning. (4) *Impulsive action*: Pro 19:2 — haste without knowledge misses the way — the impulsive will bypassing discernment enables sinning. (5) *Wounding disregard for another's conscience*: 1Cor 8:12 — acting without regard for the weak brother's conscience enables sinning against him.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — For the interpersonal sinning in VCG-01 (Lev 6:2/3 — deception, robbery, oppression), the enabling inner conditions are the same as the personal-moral register: acting without recognising the full moral weight of what is being done. However, Lev 6:2/3's Pass A meanings note elements of deliberateness ("deliberate lying," "willful act of inner deception"), which places these verses at the border with CHAR-1 rather than squarely in CHAR-2. The clearer CHAR-2 interpersonal extension is Lev 4:3 (priestly inadvertent sinning bringing communal guilt) — the enabling condition is simply that the priest acted in his representative capacity without full moral awareness, and the community is affected because of his representative role.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The enabling conditions for interpersonal confession parallel those for God-directed confession: (1) *Active conscience*: 2Sa 19:20 — "your servant knows that I have sinned" — the interpersonal confession is grounded in inner knowledge. (2) *Absence of self-protective calculation*: the person-directed confessions that are most genuine (2Sa 19:20 — Shimei, who comes first) show the confessor approaching without knowing the outcome — a posture of moral honesty. The compromised forms (Mat 27:4 — Judas, who immediately acts on the priests' indifference by returning the silver and hanging himself) show conscience driving the confession but the relational outcome being rejected.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — The corpus does not evidence the mechanism by which one person extends conscience suppression to another. Tit 3:11's divisive person may influence others through modelled behaviour, but the inner conditions for this transmission are not specified. The corpus is insufficient to characterise this mechanism.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The enabling conditions for transmitting refusal to repent: (1) *Authority and representative position*: every king in the corpus holds the authority through which the sinful pattern is transmitted — "he made Israel to sin" — the enabling condition is positional authority that gives the leader's inner disposition communal scope. (2) *Modelled non-departure*: 2Ki 15:9 — "as his fathers had done" — the next generation's refusal is enabled by the prior generation's modelled refusal; the enabling condition is the observable pattern of the predecessor. (3) *The sinful course presented as normative*: when the king walks in Jeroboam's sins without departing, the community receives an implicit signal that this is the normative path — the community's refusal is enabled by the leader's presentation of the sinful course as acceptable.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The enabling conditions for transmitting habitual defection to others: (1) *Positional authority*: 1Sa 2:25 — Eli's sons are priests, giving their habitual defection communal scope through their representative role; (2) *Institutionalised sinning*: Hos 8:11 — the multiplication of altars creates a communal infrastructure that transmits the sinning orientation to all participants; (3) *Destructive influence at scale*: Ecc 9:18 — "one sinner destroys much good" — the sinner's settled orientation has disproportionate destructive reach. The enabling condition in each case is a pre-existing relationship of influence (priestly authority, institutional membership, communal proximity) through which the inner sinning orientation flows outward.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Three enabling conditions in the leader. (1) *Personal inner commitment to the sinning course*: 1Ki 16:26 — "walked in all the way of Jeroboam" — the leader's own sinning direction is the source; without it there is nothing to transmit. (2) *Occupation of and active exercise of authority*: 1Ki 16:2 — "I made you leader over my people" — the authority position is both the enabler and the medium of transmission; the authority must be actively exercised toward the sinning direction. (3) *The will to extend the sinning direction to others*: Jer 32:35 — the deliberate building of sinning structures with the purpose of causing others to sin — the intentional causative aspect is part of the enabling condition.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Two evidenced enabling conditions. (1) *Perceived political opportunity*: 2Ki 1:1 / 3:5 — "after the death of Ahab" — the governing authority's perceived weakness at a moment of leadership transition enables the revolt. (2) *Spiritual-authority vacuum in the governing authority*: 2Ch 21:10 — "because he had forsaken the Lord" — the governing authority's inner spiritual condition (forsaking God) creates a vacuum of legitimate covenantal authority that enables the revolts of Edom and Libnah. In both cases the enabling condition is a perceived weakening or delegitimisation of the governing authority.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Corrupted heart*: Mat 15:19 — "out of the heart come... slander" — the corrupted inner moral centre is the primary enabler; (2) *Bitterness, wrath, and anger toward the target*: Eph 4:31 — the affective cluster of bitterness/wrath/anger is the emotional enabling condition for slander; (3) *Conceit and diseased craving*: 1Ti 6:4 — pride inflated by self-regard and the appetite for controversy enables the targeting of others with slanderous speech.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — (1) *Enticement*: 2Pe 2:14 — "hearts trained in greed" — the enticing person's own formed inner character is the enabling condition; they can only extend what their hearts have been trained toward. "Eyes full of adultery, insatiable for sin" — the full formation of the desire-apparatus enables and drives the extending of the mechanism toward others. (2) *Hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — Peter's initial hypocrisy (the prominent person's action) creates the social-pressure enabling condition for others to extend the same performance; (3) *Apostasy*: Act 21:21 — the accusation implies that teaching-authority is the enabling condition for extending apostasy to others.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Representative position*: Rom 5:12 — Adam's extension of the condition to all humanity was possible through his representative position as head of humanity; (2) *Shared vulnerability*: 1Ti 5:22 — hasty endorsement makes one share in the other's sin-condition — the shared human vulnerability under the condition enables extension through complicity; (3) *Predatory exploitation*: 2Ti 3:6 — those who target the condition-burdened exploit the weakness the condition creates; the enticers' own formed character (burdened with sin, led by passions) enables them to extend the condition's reach.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil" — the inner condition enabling the extension is the person's own habitual sinning practice that aligns them with the devil's domain; the extending mechanism is not willed transmission to others but the person's own enrollment in the devil's sinning-identity. The practice (habitual sinning) is what enables the extension of the enslaving power's reach through the individual's own constitution.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Representative authority*: VCG-04 — the Jeroboam-pattern extension operates through royal representational authority; the king's record-generating acts extend to the people through his role as their covenantal leader. Exo 32:21 — Aaron's priestly-leadership role is what enabled his sin's extension to the people; (2) *Relational proximity and dependence*: Gen 20:9 — Abraham's deception affected Abimelech because Abimelech had received Abraham in relational trust; the extending person's record impacts those who depend on or are relationally bound to them.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Priestly appointment and authority*: Lev 10:17 — the sin offering is "given to you" — the priest's authority to bear the congregation's iniquity is divinely granted, not self-assumed; the mediatorial role is received, not created; (2) *Proper inner disposition*: Lev 10:19 — Aaron's question about whether his grief would make the sin-offering unacceptable implies that the mediator's inner state matters; the priest must be in an appropriate inner condition to extend the seeking effectively on behalf of others.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Representative position*: Isa 43:27 — the first father's position as the ancestral head and the mediators' positions as covenant-leaders are what makes their sinning generationally transmissible; ordinary community members do not extend the condition in the same way; (2) *The sinning acts themselves*: the extension requires actual sinning ("sinned... transgressed") — the representative position plus the sinning acts together create the transmissible condition.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Authority or representative position*: Rom 5:19 — Adam's representative headship makes his sinning identity-constituting for all; Joh 9:24 — the Pharisees' authoritative social position makes their label-declaration socially operative; (2) *Moral certainty*: Joh 9:24 — "we know" — the Pharisees extend the label with claimed certainty; Luk 7:39 — the Pharisee's internal certainty about the woman's sinner-status enables the label's extension. The honest alternative (Joh 9:25 — "I do not know") refuses the extension without sufficient grounds.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Representative position*: Lev 4:3 — the anointed priest's position as representative of the community is what makes his sin guilt-propagating to all; 1Ch 21:3 — the king's position makes his census decision guilt-generating for Israel; (2) *Voluntary assumption*: 1Sa 25:24 — Abigail's "on me alone, my lord, be the guilt" — the voluntary self-insertion into the guilt-bearing position; 2Sa 14:9 — the woman's "on me be the guilt" — the willing assumption of another's liability. The conditions are distinct: representative-position guilt-propagation is structural; voluntary-assumption is agentive.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Representative position*: Lev 10:17 — "that you may bear the iniquity of the congregation" — the priests' position as representatives enables them to bear the community's iniquity; Lev 16:21 — Aaron's high-priestly office enables the transferral; 1Sa 3:13 — Eli's position as father and priest makes his iniquity community-affecting; (2) *Ancestral headship*: Exo 20:5 — "the fathers" whose idolatrous hatred of God generates the iniquity that is transmitted; the generational transmission operates through the ancestral position, not merely biological descent.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Authority or representative position*: Isa 43:27 — "mediators" and "first father" — the representative position makes one's transgression community-affecting; Jer 2:8 — "shepherds" whose transgression affects the flock; (2) *Volitional invitation*: Amos 4:4 — "come to Bethel and transgress; to Gilgal, and multiply transgression" — the intentional invitation of others into transgressive practice; the extending person's will is engaged in actively recruiting others.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Prior position of trust*: Mal 2:14 — "your companion and your wife by covenant" — the treachery is enabled by the prior intimate covenant that created the trust-position; Jer 12:6 — brothers and family-members — the betrayal is possible because of the prior bond; Jos 7:1 — Achan's position within Israel as a covenant-member; (2) *Internal desire-orientation toward false objects*: 1Ch 5:25 — "whored after the gods of the peoples" — the will's desire transferred to false objects is the enabling condition that makes the covenant-faithlessness an act of inner betrayal rather than mere preference-change.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Two enabling conditions. (1) *Relational or positional access*: 1Cor 15:33 — "bad company" — the company-relationship is the access-point; 2Cor 11:3 — the false teacher's preaching relationship with the congregation; Rev 19:2 — Babylon's position in the world-system; (2) *Active deception*: 2Cor 11:3 — "as the serpent deceived Eve by his cunning" — cunning deception is the mechanism; the extension of perversion requires active misrepresentation rather than transparent wrongdoing.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Three enabling conditions. (1) *Authority or positional access*: Psa 82:2 — judges who have positional authority to determine others' outcomes; Lev 19:35 — the seller who controls the weights and measures; Mic 3:10 — leaders who direct civic construction; (2) *Appetitive internalization*: Job 15:16 — "drinks injustice like water" — the thoroughly internalized appetite enables effortless extension to others; (3) *God-ignorance*: Job 18:21 — "him who knows not God" — the absence of divine accountability in the inner life removes the restraint that would prevent extending injustice to others.

### T4.3.3

**Question:** What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning is not received before being given — it is generated by the sinner's own will in the moment of decision. However, the corpus identifies enabling conditions that precede interpersonal sinning: (1) Gen 20:9 shows that Abraham first held the inner posture of fear-driven deception before extending sinning to Abimelech; (2) Pro 14:21 shows that contempt of the neighbour precedes and constitutes the sinning; (3) 1Sa 15:24 shows that fear of people precedes the sinning act against God. The "received" condition is not sinning-from-another but the inner dispositions (fear, contempt, deception) that make the sinning act possible.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — Unintentional sinning is not a characteristic that the person intentionally "gives" to another — it is an inadvertent act that may affect others (Lev 4:3). The question of what must be received or become before extending this characteristic does not apply in the same way as for volitionally given characteristics. The corpus is silent on this framing.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The person must first have had the conscience activated — the inner recognition must precede the interpersonal naming. 2Sa 19:20 — Shimei comes "the first" because his conscience is already active about his past wrong; the interpersonal confession flows from the prior inner recognition. The person must also have accepted that they have sinned — not merely that they might have. The equivocation present in Saul's confession (1Sa 15:30 — "I have sinned; yet honor me") shows that incomplete inner acceptance of the sin-verdict produces compromised interpersonal confession.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — The corpus does not address the preconditions for extending conscience suppression to another. This is not a characteristic that is intentionally given — it is a condition that may be transmitted through influence or example, but the corpus does not evidence the transmission mechanism directly.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The transmitting person must have: (1) *received the inherited sinful orientation and accepted it* — 2Ki 15:9 "as his fathers had done" — they themselves did not depart, making the transmission a continuation of their own inner condition; (2) *held leadership authority* that gives their inner condition communal scope — the kings who transmit the refusal hold the authority through which their personal non-departure becomes the community's non-departure; (3) *established the sinful pattern as the operative norm* — by walking in it, by treating it as light (1Ki 16:31), by not publicly marking the path as wrong.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The transmitting person must themselves have become the sinner-type — Ecc 9:18 names "one sinner" as the agent of destruction, presupposing a settled sinner-identity. 1Sa 2:25 — Eli's sons are characterized by persistent sinning against the Lord; their extension to others follows from their own habituated condition. The person extends what they are, not merely what they do: habitual defection is transmitted from a settled inner character, not from isolated acts. The precondition for transmission is the formation of the habitual-defection character in the transmitter.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The transmitting leader must have: (1) *Received the sinning orientation themselves*: 1Ki 22:52 — Ahaziah followed the way of his father, mother, and Jeroboam — the leader first receives the sinning direction from prior authorities before they can transmit it; (2) *Internalised the sinning course as their own committed direction*: 1Ki 16:19 — "walking in the way of Jeroboam, and for his sin which he committed" — the personal commitment is a prerequisite; (3) *Occupied the leadership position that gives their inner commitment communal reach*: without the authority, the same inner sinful commitment produces only personal sinning (CHAR-1/5/6) rather than communal transmission.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — The revolting community must have formed a collective will for independence that outweighs the political cost of revolt. 2Ki 8:20 — "set up a king of their own" — implies that the community had developed sufficient internal cohesion to establish alternative governance; revolt without this internal capacity would be premature. The community must have assessed both the governing authority's vulnerability (2Ki 1:1 — Ahab's death as opportunity) and their own capacity for self-governance (2Ki 8:20 — they can set up their own king).

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — The person extending sinful speech must have: (1) *A corrupted heart condition*: Mat 15:19 — the heart must have been shaped toward corruption before it generates slander; (2) *An affective state of hostility*: Eph 4:31/Col 3:8 — the bitterness/wrath/anger affective cluster must be present; (3) *A conscience insufficiently engaged*: the conscience's natural resistance to sinful speech must have been bypassed or numbed (T3.9.2). 1Ti 1:13's Paul — a blasphemer before receiving mercy — was in a condition of hardened unbelief from which the sinful speech flowed naturally.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — (1) *Enticement*: 2Pe 2:14 — the enticers must have become people "with hearts trained in greed" — the mechanism can only be extended by those who have themselves been formed by it; the enticement-extension capacity is a product of the enticers' own formation. (2) *Hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — the extending persons must themselves have adopted the hypocritical identity performance (they "acted hypocritically along with him" — they are performing the identity before extending it to others through contagion). (3) *Apostasy*: Act 21:21 — the extending person must themselves have departed from or rejected the covenantal framework in order to teach its abandonment.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — For the Adamic transmission (Rom 5:12), Adam's position as head of humanity was the prerequisite — the extension was made possible through representative identity, not merely personal sinning. For the complicity dimension (1Ti 5:22), the person must have made a hasty endorsement that aligns them with another's sin-condition. For the predatory exploitation (2Ti 3:6), the person must themselves be operating from within the condition's own corruption. In all cases: participation in the condition is the ground from which extension to others operates.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — 1Jo 3:8 — "makes a practice of sinning... is of the devil" — the extending person must have become a habitual sinner, enrolled by their practice into the devil's domain of originary sinning. The practice-to-slavery mechanism (Joh 8:34) must have operated: the person must have practiced sinning to the point of constitutional enslavement before their condition reflects the devil's enslaving domain. The devil's originary sinning (1Jo 3:8 — "from the beginning") is what they are now aligned with through their formed habit.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The extending person must hold a position of influence, authority, or relational access that makes their sin-record transferable. VCG-04 — the kings who extended the Jeroboam-sin to Israel had first received the royal role (exalted "out of the dust" — 1Ki 16:2 — "since I exalted you out of the dust and made you leader over my people Israel"). The extension-capacity is grounded in the authority-position received: having been made leader, their record-generating acts become the nation's burden. Aaron likewise (Exo 32:21) had received the priestly-leadership role whose authority made his failure extend to all.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The extending person must have received the mediatorial appointment: Lev 10:17 — "given to you that you may bear the iniquity of the congregation" — the priestly appointment is the prerequisite for bearing others' iniquity. Exo 32:30 — Moses's authority to seek atonement for the people is grounded in his unique standing before God as Israel's covenant-mediator. In the NT: Mat 26:28 — Christ's blood is poured out "for many" — Christ's unique position as the covenant-establisher is what enables his extending of forgiveness to the many.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Isa 43:27 — the first father must have occupied the foundational ancestral position before his sinning could become generationally transmissible; the mediators must have been appointed to their leadership roles before their transgressions could be transmitted to those under their care. The extension requires both the representative position (received through appointment or birth-order) and the sinning act. Without the representative position, individual sinning does not generate the same generational transmission.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — For label-assignment: the authority or knowledge-claim to render moral verdicts on others (Joh 9:24 — "we know"). For Adam's constitutive extension (Rom 5:19): his position as the covenant-representational head of humanity. For enticement (Pro 1:10): the established sinner-identity that makes the extending person an active recruiter. The extension capacity is grounded either in positional authority (Adam, the Pharisees) or in the fully formed sinner-identity (Pro 1:10's enticing sinners who have become the identity they extend).

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — For representative-propagation: the person must hold the representative position (Lev 4:3 — the anointed priest; 1Ch 21:3 — the king) before their sin propagates to others. For priestly-bearing: Exo 28:38 — Aaron must be vested in the high-priestly garments with the plate "on Aaron's forehead" — the office and its vestments are prerequisites for the guilt-bearing function. For voluntary-assumption: 1Sa 25:24 — Abigail must have relational access and standing before David to make the guilt-transfer offer effective.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — For priestly-ritual extension (Lev 16:21): Aaron must be fully vested in the high-priestly office and garments before the iniquity-transfer through confession is valid — the office is the prerequisite; Exo 28:38 — "it shall regularly be on his forehead" — the gold plate inscription is prerequisite for bearing iniquity. For generational extension (Exo 20:5): the fathers must have committed the originating idolatrous iniquity — "those who hate me" — the hatred of God is the prerequisite moral condition that generates the transmissible iniquity.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — For leadership-propagation (Isa 43:27; Jer 2:8): the person must hold the representative office (mediator, shepherd) whose transgression carries community-weight. For anger-contagion (Pro 29:22): the person must have allowed anger to become their constitutive inner disposition — "one given to anger" — before that disposition generates multiple transgressions in the social environment. For communal invitation (Amos 4:4): the person must be already established in transgressive practice before they can credibly invite others to "come and transgress."

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — The person must have been party to the covenant whose breaking constitutes the faithlessness. Mal 2:14 — "though she is your companion and your wife by covenant" — the prior covenant-membership is the prerequisite that makes the departure faithlessness rather than mere leaving. Jos 22:22 — "if it was in rebellion or in breach of faith against the Lord" — the eastern tribes were already covenant-members whose actions would constitute faithlessness; the covenant-membership precedes any faithlessness.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The person must have become constitutionally perverted before they can extend it. 2Pe 2:19 — "they themselves are slaves of corruption" — the false teachers who promise freedom are already enslaved to corruption before they extend it to others; the extending of perversion to others follows the consolidation of the perversion within the person. The vine-degeneracy pattern (Jer 2:21) applies: the vine must first turn wild from within before it produces wild fruit that corrupts the vineyard.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The extending person must have developed the unjust character-trait (Luk 16:10 — dishonest across all scales) and suppressed the shame-response (Zep 3:5 — knows no shame) before they can consistently extend injustice to others. For the judicial context (Psa 82:2), they must also have received a bribe or partiality-inducement (2Ch 19:7 — "partiality or taking bribes") that has corrupted the inner judgment-faculty before extending unjust judgments to those before them.

## T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

### T4.4.1

**Question:** Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The recipient of sinning is extensively evidenced in VCG-02. Gen 20:9 — Abimelech receives sinning from Abraham: "you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin." Gen 42:22 — Joseph received sinning from his brothers: "Do not sin against the boy." 1Sa 19:4/5 — David as the would-be recipient of Saul's sinning: "Let not the king sin against his servant David." Mat 18:15 — the brother who has been sinned against and must decide whether to confront. 1Cor 8:12 — the weak brother whose conscience is wounded by another's sinning. The reception operates as a *harm received* — moral, relational, and sometimes physical damage inflicted on the recipient by the sinner's act.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The receiving direction is present in Lev 4:3: the community receives the guilt consequence of the anointed priest's inadvertent sinning — "the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the people." The people receive a moral liability they did not personally incur through any act of their own. Lev 9:15 (Aaron's communal sin offering "for the people") addresses this received communal guilt. The receiving is involuntary and inadvertent on both sides — the priest did not deliberately sin against the community, and the community did not knowingly receive the guilt.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The reception of confession is evidenced in the human relational instances. Mat 27:4 — the chief priests receive Judas's confession: "I have sinned by betraying innocent blood" — their response: "What is that to us? See to it yourself." This is the paradigm of rejected reception: the confession is heard but the recipient disengages. 1Sa 26:21 — Saul's confession received by David in the field — David's response confirms he received it: "Return, my son David" (Saul's words) implies a relational response to the confession. 2Sa 19:20 — Shimei's confession received by King David — the outcome is that David's response is affected by the confession (the king grants mercy).

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The communal dimension of Jer 16:10 provides the clearest evidence of received/transmitted conscience suppression: "And when you tell this people all these words, and they *say* to you, 'Why has the Lord pronounced all this great evil against us? What is our iniquity?'" — the people's collective conscience suppression is a communally held condition, not merely individual. This suggests that conscience suppression can be received through communal formation: the entire community fails to recognise its sin together, implying a shared moral blindness that was formed across generations (Jer 16:11–13 context — "your fathers forsook me").

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Yes — the communal reception of the refusal pattern is evidenced. 2Ki 13:6 — "Israel did not depart from the sins of the house of Jeroboam, which he made Israel to sin, but walked in them" — the whole people receive and enact the refusal as a communal pattern. 2Ki 15:9 — "as his fathers had done" — each king receives the refusal-pattern from his fathers and adopts it as his own. The reception is not forced but constituted by the community's own walk: "walked in them" — they received the pattern and actively sustained it.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. The communal reception of the habitual defection pattern is evidenced. 2Ki 17:7 — Israel's communal habitual sinning — the whole people receive and sustain the defection pattern as a community. Hos 10:9 — "from the days of Gibeah, you have sinned, O Israel; there they have *continued*" — the Gibeah-origin sinning has been received and continued across generations. Hos 8:11 — altars for sinning — the institutional sinning structures that the community has received and participated in. The reception is not necessarily conscious adoption but occurs through inhabiting the sinning institutions and patterns that prior generations have established.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. The receiving dimension is present throughout the Kings corpus but described from the giving side. The community's reception is evidenced by the communal outcomes: 1Ki 14:22 — "Judah did what was evil in the sight of the Lord, and they provoked him to jealousy with their sins" — Judah has received and enacted the transmitted sinning direction. 1Ki 22:52 — Ahaziah received the sinning direction from his father, mother, and Jeroboam — the most explicit multi-source reception in the corpus. 2Ki 17:21 — "made them commit great sin" — the community has received and committed the transmitted sin. Exo 23:33 — the anticipated reception through the snare mechanism — the community will receive the foreign nations' sinning direction if the boundary is not maintained.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Yes — the governing authority receives the revolt as a relational rupture. Israel receives Moab's revolt (2Ki 1:1; 3:5/7 — Jehoram receives the revolt as the named occasion for military response). Judah receives Edom's revolt (2Ki 8:20/22; 2Ch 21:8/10). The house of David receives Israel's revolt (1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19). The receiving party is the governing authority that suffers the breaking of submission.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Yes. The receiving dimension is evidenced through the targets of sinful speech. Act 6:11 — Stephen receives the false blasphemous witness — the sinful speech is directed at him as its target. Rev 2:9 — the community of Smyrna receives the slander of those who falsely claim to be Jews — they are named as the recipients of the sinful speech against them ("the slander of those who say..."). Mat 26:65 / Mar 14:64 — Jesus receives the blasphemy-charge as the accused — he is the named recipient of the religious authorities' sinful speech verdict.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Yes. (1) *Enticement received*: 2Pe 2:18 — "those who are barely escaping from those who live in error" receive the enticement directed at them by the false teachers; the mechanism is received by those at the vulnerable threshold between error and escape. (2) *Hypocrisy received*: Gal 2:13 — "even Barnabas was led astray by their hypocrisy" — Barnabas receives the collective hypocrisy and is drawn into it; the reception is described as "being led astray" — passive in form, showing the mechanism's power over the receiver.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes — the reception dimension is the condition's defining feature. (1) *Universal reception through Adam*: Rom 5:12 — "death spread to all men because all sinned" — the universal condition is received by all humanity through the Adamic representative structure; Joh 9:34 — "born in utter sin" — the condition is received at birth; (2) *Reception of forgiveness as the reversal*: VCG-02 throughout — "your sins are forgiven" (Mat 9:2) — the forgiveness is received by the person from Jesus; (3) *Reception of the Spirit's conviction*: Joh 16:8 — the Spirit convicts the world — the condition's recognition is received from the Spirit's external work.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes — the reception is the enslaving mechanism's primary operation. Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — the slavery is received through the practice: the person receives the enslaved state as the constitutional consequence of sustained sinning practice. Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin" — the universal imprisonment is the structural condition received by all persons under the declaration of Scripture; the reception is universal and not individually chosen but corporately received.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes — through multiple reception modes. (1) *Inherited birth-condition*: Psa 51:5 — "in sin did my mother conceive me" (VCG-01) — the record-bearing condition is received at conception, prior to any individual act; (2) *Corporate inheritance through leaders*: VCG-04 — Israel received Jeroboam's sin-record as national inheritance through 46 verses of royal succession; (3) *Priestly loading*: Lev 16:21 — "he shall put them on the head of the goat" — the people's sins are loaded onto the scapegoat in the Day of Atonement ritual; (4) *Unexpected reception*: Gen 20:9 — Abimelech received Abraham's sin-record through deception; he was "about to have sinned" without knowledge.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the receiving dimension is the characteristic's completing act. Lev 16:30 — "you shall be clean before the Lord from all your sins" — the community receives the cleanliness that atonement confers; Luk 7:47/48 — the woman receives the forgiveness Jesus declares; Col 2:13 — "having forgiven us all our trespasses" — the "us" receives the comprehensive forgiveness; Act 2:38 — "you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" — forgiveness enables receipt of the Spirit.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — the present generation receives the accumulated condition from the ancestors. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the "your" addresses the present community as the receivers of the ancestral condition; the present generation is characterised by the first father's sin without having personally committed it. Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the prolonged condition encompasses more than the present generation's own acts; the "long time" implies receipt of a condition that predates the present generation's own sinning.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes — through three reception modes. (1) *Constituted imputation*: Rom 5:19 — "the many were made sinners" — the sinner-identity is received through Adam's representative act, prior to any individual's personal sinning; (2) *Social reception*: Luk 7:37 — "a woman of the city, who was a sinner" — the city's label has been received as her public identity; Luk 18:13 — "be merciful to me, a sinner" — the tax collector has internalised the label as self-description; (3) *Authentic self-recognition*: Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man" — Peter receives the sinner-identity as an accurate inner self-description prompted by conscience.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — through three reception modes. (1) *Individual incurrence*: Lev 5:19 — "he has indeed incurred guilt before the Lord" — the individual receives guilt through their own act; Lev 22:16 — "cause them to bear iniquity and guilt, by eating their holy things" — guilt is incurred through improper action; (2) *Communal inheritance*: Ezr 9:7 — "from the days of our fathers to this day we have been in great guilt" — the community receives inherited guilt across generations; Jer 32:18 — "you repay the guilt of fathers to their children after them" — intergenerational guilt-reception; (3) *Voluntary assumption*: Exo 28:38 — Aaron receives Israel's guilt for bearing; 1Sa 25:24 — Abigail proposes to receive David/Nabal's guilt.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — through four reception modes. (1) *Personal incurrence*: Num 15:31 — "his iniquity shall be on him" — iniquity is received through one's own act and attaches to the person; Lev 5:17 — "he shall bear his iniquity" — the individual receives and carries the iniquity arising from their own violation; (2) *Generational inheritance*: Lam 5:7 — "our fathers sinned, and are no more; and we bear their iniquities" — the present generation receives the iniquity-burden of deceased ancestors; Jer 32:18 — "you repay the guilt of fathers to their children after them"; (3) *Priestly representative receiving*: Lev 10:17 — the priests receive the congregation's iniquity through the sin-offering; Num 18:1 — "you and your sons and your father's house with you shall bear iniquity connected with the sanctuary"; (4) *Servant's vicarious receiving*: Isa 53:6 — "the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all" — the servant receives the iniquity of all through divine appointment.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — through three reception modes. (1) *Personal commission*: Psa 51:3 — "I know my transgressions" — personally owned; Rom 2:23 — "you who boast in the law dishonor God by breaking the law" — personally incurred; (2) *Generational inheritance*: Isa 57:4 — "children of transgression, offspring of deceit" — inheriting a transgression-identity from the parental generation; Isa 48:8 — "from before birth you were called a rebel" — the rebel-label received before personal choice; (3) *Adamic universal*: Rom 5:15/16/17/18 — "one man's trespass" producing "condemnation for all men" — the transgression received through the Adamic representative.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Yes — through three reception modes. (1) *Personal commission*: Lev 26:40 — "treachery that they committed against me" — personally owned; 1Ch 10:13 — "Saul died for his breach of faith. He broke faith with the Lord" — named personal receipt of faithlessness as self-committed; (2) *Generational inheritance*: 2Ch 30:7 — "do not be like your fathers and your brothers, who were faithless" — the generational pattern received; Lev 26:40 — "the iniquity of their fathers in their treachery" — the fathers' faithlessness inherited as a pattern; (3) *Communal attribution*: Jos 7:1 — "the people of Israel broke faith" — individual faithlessness attributed to the whole community; Ezr 9:2 — "in this faithlessness the hand of the officials and chief men has been foremost" — communal reception of the faithlessness-charge.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — through three reception modes. (1) *Personal inner generation*: Pro 6:14 — "with perverted heart devises evil" — the perversity is generated from within; Eph 4:22 — "corrupt through deceitful desires" — the old self receiving corruption through its own deceitful desires; (2) *External social reception*: 1Cor 15:33 — "bad company ruins good morals" — good morals received from without initially are ruined by external corrupting company; 2Cor 11:3 — "your thoughts will be led astray" — the congregants receive corrupted thinking from the false teacher; (3) *Generational inheritance*: Deu 32:20 — "perverse generation" — the perversity characterises the generation inherited as a communal condition.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — extensively as victim-experience. (1) *Personal receipt*: Job 6:29 — "let no injustice be done" — Job as the intended recipient; Psa 43:1 — "from the deceitful and unjust man deliver me" — the psalmist as the one receiving unjust oppression; (2) *Communal receipt*: 2Sa 7:10 — "violent men shall afflict them" — Israel as collective recipient; Psa 125:3 — "the scepter of wickedness shall not rest on the land allotted to the righteous, lest the righteous stretch out their hands to do wrong" — the righteous as recipients of the pressure of injustice's rule; (3) *Cosmic receipt*: Rev 18:5 — the whole earth as the recipient of Babylon's accumulated injustice.

### T4.4.2

**Question:** What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Reception of sinning (being sinned against) is not primarily a matter of the recipient's inner conditions — the sinning act is committed by another and lands on the recipient whether or not they are internally prepared. However, the corpus does evidence the recipient's inner conditions that shape the response to sinning received: (1) *Moral clarity about what was done*: Gen 20:9 (Abimelech's clear protest), 1Sa 19:4/5 (Jonathan's clear naming of Saul's potential wrong). (2) *The capacity to forgive*: Mat 18:21 (Peter asking how many times to forgive a sinning brother), Luk 17:3/4 (the obligation to forgive repeated sinning upon repentance). The inner condition that shapes the reception's aftermath is the recipient's moral discernment and willingness to forgive.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — Reception of communal guilt from a representative's inadvertent sinning (Lev 4:3) is not conditioned on the community's inner state — it arises from the structural representative relationship between the priest and the people. No inner condition in the recipients enables or blocks the communal guilt consequence. The corpus is silent on inner conditions affecting reception in this register.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Enabling reception: the recipient who receives confession with mercy (David receiving Shimei — 2Sa 19:20; the father receiving the prodigal — Luk 15:20/21) is characterised by a disposition of mercy and an orientation toward relational restoration. Blocking reception: Mat 27:4 — the chief priests are blocked from receiving Judas's confession by their own moral complicity in the betrayal ("What is that to us?") — they cannot receive what would implicate them. The blocking condition is the recipient's investment in the sinning act that is being confessed — they cannot acknowledge the sin without acknowledging their own role in it.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The communal formation pattern (Jer 16:10) suggests that conscience suppression is *received* through: (1) inherited moral framework that excludes accurate self-assessment (generational patterns of unfaithfulness); (2) communal reinforcement of the false innocence claim; (3) absence of the God-knowledge that would provide the corrective reference point (1Cor 15:34). Blocking the reception of conscience suppression: the fear of God (Ecc 8:12 contrast) and knowledge of God (1Cor 15:34) are the inner conditions that, if operative, prevent conscience suppression from taking hold.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Existing alignment with the sinful course*: a community already walking in the sinful direction (Jeroboam's golden calves as established worship) does not need much additional influence to continue not departing; (2) *Leadership modelling*: 2Ki 15:9 — the father's example is the primary transmission mechanism; receptivity is enabled by the leader's modelled non-departure. Reception-blocking conditions: the fear of God (1Ti 5:20 — the fear produced by public rebuke in others prevents their adopting the pattern), wholehearted law-walking (2Ki 10:31 — the positive alternative).

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Absence of the God-pleasing disposition*: Ecc 7:26 — the sinner who lacks the inner God-orientation is vulnerable to reception of the sinning pattern; (2) *Inhabiting the sinning institutions*: Hos 8:11 — participating in the altars for sinning transmits the sinning orientation; (3) *Following established precedent*: Hos 10:9 — "there they have continued" — the pre-existing communal sinning pattern makes reception automatic for those who do not actively resist it. Blocking conditions: 1Jo 3:6 — abiding in Christ as the structural preventive; Ecc 7:26 — pleasing God as the inner disposition that enables escape rather than reception.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Structural position under the authority*: the community members are under the leader's authority and therefore within the transmission pathway; (2) *Absence of a countervailing God-orientation strong enough to resist*: Exo 23:33 — the preventive command implies that without the protective boundary, the community will receive the contagious sin; (3) *Ambient community sinning already present*: 1Ki 14:22 — "more than all that their fathers had done" — suggests the community was already receiving and escalating the transmitted pattern, implying prior reception had reduced resistance. Blocking conditions: Josiah's model (2Ki 23:15) — a leader whose God-orientation does not receive the inherited sinning pattern but reverses it.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — 2Ki 3:7 — Jehoram's immediate response (seeking military alliance) shows that the governing authority responds with force when it perceives the capacity to counter-revolt. The governing authority's response to received revolt is shaped by its assessment of its own military-political capacity: strength enables military response (2Ki 3:7); inability or other factors produce the acceptance of the "to this day" permanent fracture (1Ki 12:19). The inner condition that shapes the governing authority's response is its own perception of power and capacity.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Vulnerability in communal context*: Act 6:11 — Stephen is vulnerable to the false witness because the charge is made before an authority capable of acting on it; (2) *The authority's capacity to enforce the verdict*: Mar 14:64 — the council's verdict is received as actionable. Blocking conditions: the inner-being of the person receiving sinful speech is not the corpus's focus — the question is about the speaker's inner condition rather than the receiver's capacity for reception. Rev 2:9 — Christ's word to the Smyrna community frames their reception of slander within his knowledge and care: "I know your tribulation and your poverty (but you are rich)" — the receiver's true condition is known by God and not defined by the sinful speech directed at them.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Reception-enabling conditions: (1) *Enticement*: 2Pe 2:18 — "barely escaping" — residual weakness and unstable footing is the primary enabling condition for receiving enticement; (2) *Hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — the presence of authoritative figures (Peter, then the rest of the Jews) performing the hypocrisy makes individual resistance difficult; the social weight of the collective enables reception. Blocking conditions: stability (the opposite of "unsteady souls" — 2Pe 2:18 — is firm establishment); Paul's confrontation of Peter (Gal 2:11 context) models the blocking condition for hypocrisy-reception: direct honest confrontation of the performing group.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Reception-enabling conditions for the remedy: (1) *Faith*: Mat 9:2; Joh 8:24 — the faith-posture enables reception of forgiveness; the absence of faith means dying in the condition; (2) *Repentance and confession*: Mar 1:4; Mat 3:6 — the turning and acknowledging are enabling conditions for receiving forgiveness. Reception-blocking conditions: (1) *Unbelief*: Joh 16:9 — the Spirit convicts of the specific sin of not believing — unbelief blocks reception of the remedy; (2) *False perceptive certainty*: Joh 9:41 — "now that you say, 'We see,' your guilt remains" — claiming to have no condition is what blocks the remedy's reception.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Reception-enabling: (1) *The practice of sinning*: Joh 8:34 — the practice is the enabling condition for receiving the enslaved state; (2) *Absence of the new birth*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning" — the absence of the new-birth constitution enables reception of the enslavement. Reception-blocking: (1) *New birth*: 1Jo 3:9 — God's seed abiding constitutionally prevents sustained sinning practice from taking hold; (2) *Christ's protection*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him" — the protective guardianship blocks the evil one from regaining the foothold through which sin's enslavement operates.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Reception-enabling conditions for the remedy: (1) *Realisation and acknowledgment*: Num 5:6 — "that person realizes his guilt" — conscience engagement enables reception of the remedy process; Psa 32:5 — "I acknowledged my sin" — active acknowledgment opens the remedy path; (2) *Turning*: Eze 18:21 — "turns away from all his sins" — the volitional turn enables divine erasure. Reception-blocking: (1) *Concealment*: Psa 32:5's positive implies that "covering" (the opposite) blocks reception; (2) *Self-deception*: Hos 12:8 — "they cannot find in me iniquity or sin" — prosperity-based self-exculpation blocks remedy reception; (3) *Hardening*: VCG-04 — "he did not depart from" — volitional persistence in the record blocks its erasure.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Reception-enabling: (1) *Faith*: Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives" — belief is the specific reception-enabling condition in the NT; (2) *Repentance*: Act 2:38 — "repent... for the forgiveness" — repentance precedes and enables forgiveness-reception; (3) *Recognition*: Lev 4:14 — sin "becomes known" — recognition enables seeking which enables receiving. Reception-blocking: (1) *The eternal-sin condition*: Mar 3:29 — "never has forgiveness" — the one condition that permanently blocks reception; (2) *Unrecognised sin*: without recognition (Lev 4:14), the seeking cannot begin and reception cannot follow.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Receipt is constituted by lineage and community membership rather than by inner conditions. Isa 43:27 — the "your" addresses those who stand in the lineage of the first father; membership in the lineage is the enabling condition for receiving the transmitted condition. What blocks the condition's full hold is: 1Ki 8:47 — "if they turn their heart" — the volitional turning within the inherited condition is what prevents the condition from remaining unopposed; and Eze 37:23 — God's cleansing — the divine act is the ultimate blocking condition that removes the inherited accumulation.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Reception-enabling (authentic): (1) *Proximity to holiness*: Luk 5:8 — the sinner-identity's reception is enabled by standing near divine power; conscience activates in holiness's presence; (2) *Honest conscience*: Luk 18:13 — the tax collector's self-abasement indicates an undefended conscience that accepts the verdict. Reception-blocking: (1) *Self-righteousness*: Mat 9:13 — those who perceive themselves as "righteous" cannot receive the sinner-label even when it applies; (2) *Suppressed conscience*: Amo 9:10 — the presumptuous claim of safety blocks authentic reception; (3) *Wrongful external assignment*: Joh 9:25 — the healed man's "I do not know" refuses to receive a wrongly assigned label.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Reception-enabling: (1) *Acknowledgment*: Hos 5:15 — "until they acknowledge their guilt" — acknowledgment is the condition of God's return and remedy-reception; (2) *Seeking*: Hos 5:15 — "seek my face... earnestly seek me" — the active movement toward God in distress enables remedy-reception; (3) *Confession + restitution*: Lev 5:5; Num 5:7 — the full guilt-to-remedy pathway. Reception-blocking: (1) *Self-concealment*: Jer 2:22 — attempting to wash away guilt blocks remedy because it substitutes self-effort for divine action; (2) *Pride*: Eze 16:49 — "pride... excess of food" — Sodom's pride blocked guilt-recognition and therefore remedy-reception; (3) *False-heart*: Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the false heart cannot rightly receive the remedy.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

Reception-blocking: (1) *Concealment*: Pro 28:13 — "whoever conceals his transgressions will not prosper"; (2) *Self-justification*: Jer 2:29 — "why do you contend with me?" — self-defensive posture against God; (3) *Love of transgression*: Pro 17:19 — "whoever loves transgression loves strife" — affective attachment to the transgression forecloses remedy.

Reception-blocking: (1) *Pride*: 2Ch 26:16 — "when he was strong, he grew proud" — pride generating faithlessness blocks the humility required for remedy; (2) *Hardening under distress*: 2Ch 28:22 — "became yet more faithless" — distress deepening the faithlessness forecloses remedy; (3) *Concealment*: 1Ch 10:13 — Saul's consulting a medium secretly — the concealment of divided loyalty blocks remedy.

### T4.4.3

**Question:** What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The person who is sinned against but refuses to be overcome by it — who does not allow the sinning received to corrupt their own inner state — is evidenced in David (1Sa 24:11: "I have not sinned against you, though you hunt my life to take it") and Jeremiah (Jer 37:18: "What wrong have I done to you or your servants or this people?"). Their inner-being state is one of moral clarity and conscientious self-examination: they are confident their own hands are clean even as another sins against them. The sinning received does not produce reciprocal sinning in them — the inner state is one of conscious integrity maintained under pressure.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — The corpus does not evidence this scenario for CHAR-2. The communal guilt from priestly sinning (Lev 4:3) is received structurally, not optionally — the community cannot decline to receive it. The individual unintentional sin (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27) is not something one receives from another in a way that could be accepted or refused. The corpus is silent on this framing.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Mat 27:4 provides the clearest evidence: the chief priests who dismiss Judas's confession respond with moral deflection ("What is that to us? See to it yourself") — their inner-being state is one of refusal to engage with the moral reality the confession names. They neither receive it (which would require acknowledging the injustice they commissioned) nor respond with mercy. The dismissal reveals an inner state of self-protective moral closure — the conscience that would be implicated by receiving the confession is closed against it. The non-receiving party's inner state is the inverse of the confessor's: where the confessor has opened to moral honesty, the non-receiver has closed against it.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — The corpus does not directly evidence the inner-being state of someone who resists receiving conscience suppression from another. The contrasting figure in Ecc 8:12 — "those who fear God, because they fear before him" — represents the person who is not in the conscience-suppressed state, but the verse does not describe the dynamic of resisting transmission from another.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — 1Ti 5:20 provides the clearest evidence of the non-receiving person's inner state: "so that the rest may stand in fear" — the person who witnesses the public rebuke of the persistent sinner but does not adopt the pattern of persistent sinning is in a state of *fear* — moral alertness produced by seeing the consequence of refusal to repent. This fear-state is the opposite of the dull moral affect (1Ki 16:31 — "a light thing") that enables reception of the refusal. The non-receiving person retains the moral alertness that the receiving person has lost.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — 1Jo 3:6 and Ecc 7:26 evidence the non-receiving person's inner state. 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — the person who does not receive the habitual defection pattern is one who abides in Christ; their inner-being state is the abiding relationship that structurally prevents the pattern from taking hold. Ecc 7:26 — "he who pleases God escapes her" — the God-pleaser's inner state is one of active God-orientation that provides the inner resource for escape from the sinning trap. The non-receiving person is characterised by the positive orientation (abiding, God-pleasing) that the habitual sinner lacks.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-7]** S — The corpus does not directly evidence the inner-being state of a community member who resists the leader's transmitted sinning direction. Josiah (2Ki 23:15) is the closest analogue as a leader who does not receive the inherited pattern — but the corpus focuses on his actions (demolishing the altar) rather than his inner state during the reception context. Individual community-member resistance to the leader's contagious sin is not addressed in the 19-verse corpus.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(S)*

**[CHAR-8]** S — The corpus does not describe the inner-being state of the governing authority that accepts the revolt without military contest. The "to this day" markers name the permanent fracture as a fact without describing the inner experience of acceptance. The corpus is silent on this dimension.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Rev 2:9 provides the clearest evidence: Christ addresses the Smyrna community who receive the slander of false Jews — "I know your tribulation and your poverty (but you are rich)" — the true inner-being state of the recipient is not determined by the sinful speech directed at them. The parenthetical "(but you are rich)" is the counter-declaration: the slanderer's speech does not constitute the recipient's identity. The inner-being state of the person who receives sinful speech without being defined by it is one of Christ-given true identity that stands against the false verdict of the slander.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(S)*

**[CHAR-10]** S — The corpus does not directly evidence the inner-being state of the person who resists enticement, refuses collective hypocrisy, or maintains covenantal faithfulness against apostasy-pressure. Paul (Gal 2:11 context) is the implicit positive exemplar against hypocrisy but his inner state is not described in the 5-verse corpus.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The received-remedy inner-being state is described across VCG-02 and VCG-03. (1) *Freed and reallocated*: Rom 6:18 — "set free from sin... become slaves of righteousness" — the inner state is liberation from the old master and re-allocation to a new one; (2) *Dead to sin and alive to God*: Rom 6:11 — "consider yourselves dead to sin and alive to God in Christ Jesus" — the inner state requires cognitive self-reckoning; (3) *Sins forgiven and covered*: Rom 4:7/8 — "whose sins are covered... against whom the Lord will not count his sin" — the relational standing is transformed; (4) *Spirit-life*: Rom 8:10 — "the Spirit is life because of righteousness" — the Spirit's indwelling is the new inner condition replacing the condition's hold.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — 1Jo 3:9 and 1Jo 5:18 together name the non-enslaved inner-being state: (1) *Born of God*: a constituted divine-birth identity that redirects the deepest allegiance toward God rather than the devil; (2) *God's seed abiding*: an indwelling constitutional principle that prevents sustained sinning; (3) *Kept by Christ*: the born-of-God person's non-enslavement is actively maintained by Christ's protection; (4) *Evil one unable to touch*: the enslaving power's entry-agent (the evil one — 1Jo 5:18) is barred from gaining access. This is a positively constituted freedom, not merely the absence of the enslaving power.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The addressed-record inner-being state is described through multiple metaphors. (1) *Blessedness*: Psa 32:1 — "blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered" — the inner state is beatitude; (2) *Relief from physical weight*: Psa 38:3 — "no health in my bones because of my sin" implies its opposite: health restored when the record is addressed; (3) *Restored relationship with God*: Jer 31:34 — when God remembers sins no more, the new-covenant relational fullness is restored; (4) *No condemnation from the record*: Eze 33:16 — "none of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him" — the record generates no further accusation; the person's inner state is free from the record's testimony.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The received-forgiveness inner state is the richest positive inner-being state described in M10. (1) *Clean before God*: Lev 16:30 — relational purity restored; (2) *Alive*: Col 2:13 — "God made alive" — constitutional life replacing death-in-trespasses; (3) *Loved and loving*: Luk 7:47 — "she loved much" — the received forgiveness produces the love-orientation; (4) *Redeemed*: Col 1:14; Eph 1:7 — "we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins" — permanent identity as redeemed; (5) *Spirit-indwelt*: Act 2:38 — "you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" — the deepest positive inner-being consequence.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Eze 37:23 names the freed state: "they shall not defile themselves anymore with their idols and their detestable things, or with any of their transgressions. But I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God." The freed inner state is: (1) *Non-defilement* — no longer participating in idolatrous defilement; (2) *Divine cleanliness* — cleansed by God's act; (3) *Covenant-people identity restored* — "they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — the relational formula restored.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Luk 15:7/10 names the cosmic response to the repentant sinner: "more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents" — the repentant sinner becomes the occasion of heavenly joy, implying their own inner state is transformed. Psa 51:13 — "sinners will return to you" — the return names a restored directional orientation toward God. Luk 5:32 — "sinners to repentance" — the repentance is the sinner-identity's reversal. 1Ti 1:15/16 — Paul as the foremost-sinner-transformed-to-apostle is the paradigm: the inner state after the sinner-identity's reversal is not merely neutralised sinnership but active redemptive purpose.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for" — the immediate state is fitness for divine commission: Isaiah is sent immediately after the guilt-removal (Isa 6:8/9 — "here I am! Send me"); the guilt-removed state enables full engagement with divine purpose. Jer 33:8 — "I will cleanse them from all the guilt... and I will forgive" — the cleansed state is simultaneously a forgiven state; the inner-being condition is both guilt-free and forgiven. Psa 34:22 — "none of those who take refuge in him will be condemned" — the not-guilty state is characterised as taking refuge in God; the guilt-removed person is in the position of one sheltered rather than exposed.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Three states are evidenced. (1) *Fitness for divine commission*: Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away" immediately precedes Isaiah's commissioning ("here I am! Send me" — Isa 6:8); the iniquity-removed state enables active divine engagement; (2) *Blessed state of not-counted iniquity*: Psa 32:2 — "blessed is the man against whom the Lord counts no iniquity, and in whose spirit there is no deceit" — the blessedness of the cleared account; (3) *Full relational restoration*: Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more" — the removed-iniquity state is one of complete relational restoration, including God's memory being cleared; Isa 40:2 — "her warfare is ended" — the relational conflict is resolved.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Three states evidenced. (1) *Blessedness*: Psa 32:1 — "blessed is the one whose transgression is forgiven, whose sin is covered" — the primary affective state is blessedness; (2) *Teaching capacity*: Psa 51:13 — "then I will teach transgressors your ways, and sinners will return to you" — the forgiven transgressor's inner state enables them to become a guide for other transgressors; the experience of forgiveness is converted into instructional wisdom; (3) *Relational restoration*: Eze 18:22 — "none of the transgressions he has committed shall be remembered against him; for the righteousness that he has done he shall live" — the forgiven state is one of full life-standing before God, unclouded by prior transgression-record.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Two states evidenced. (1) *Restored covenant relationship*: Lev 26:42-45 — "I will remember my covenant... I will not spurn them... I will be their God" — the forgiven-faithlessness state is one of restored covenant relationship with God; (2) *Forgotten shame in secure dwelling*: Eze 39:26 — "they shall forget their shame and all the treachery they have practiced against me, when they dwell securely in their land with none to make them afraid" — the fully-restored state is one in which the treachery-shame is eclipsed by the security of covenant-dwelling. The forgiven-faithlessness state is characterised by secure dwelling and forgotten shame — the opposite of the exile-and-open-shame state (Dan 9:7) that faithlessness produced.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Three states evidenced. (1) *New self in God's likeness*: Eph 4:24 — "put on the new self, created after the likeness of God in true righteousness and holiness" — the addressed-perversion state is the new-self condition, reconstituted in the image-likeness; (2) *Participation in divine nature*: 2Pe 1:4 — "become partakers of the divine nature, having escaped from the corruption" — the escape from corruption is simultaneously entry into divine-nature participation; (3) *Renewed mind*: Eph 4:23 — "renewed in the spirit of your minds" — the depraved mind's remedy is the renewal at the spirit-level of the mind.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Three states evidenced. (1) *Living rather than dying*: Eze 33:15 — "he shall surely live; he shall not die" — the cessation of injustice restores the life-condition; Eze 18:26/33:18 — the death that doing injustice would have brought is averted; (2) *Restored integrity*: Zep 3:13 — "those who are left in Israel... shall do no injustice and speak no lies" — the purified remnant's restored integrity; (3) *Trustworthy stewardship*: Luk 16:11 — the inverse implies that faithfulness with unrighteous wealth qualifies for true riches — cessation of injustice restores the capacity to be trusted.

## T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

### T4.5.1

**Question:** Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The corpus shows clear differentiation. Within existing bonds: Gen 42:22 (brothers sinning against a brother — deep family bond), 1Sa 19:4/5 (king sinning against a servant who has done only good — overlord/servant bond), Mat 18:15 (brother sinning against a brother within the community of disciples). Across bonds: Gen 40:1 (cupbearer and baker sinning against their lord — authority relationship). The evidence shows that sinning within existing bonds is treated as particularly grievous — Jonathan frames sinning against innocent David as a moral horror ("Why then will you sin against innocent blood?", 1Sa 19:5) precisely because the king-servant bond makes the sinning a betrayal. Sinning against relational partners magnifies the moral weight.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes. Lev 4:3 (priestly sinning affecting the community) operates specifically within the representative covenant relationship between the priest and Israel — the guilt-transfer is a function of the pre-existing bond, not of relational distance. Lev 6:2 (sinning against a neighbor in matters of deposit or security) likewise operates within existing economic and social relationships — a trusted custodial bond. The inadvertent violations in Lev 4:2 and Num 15:27 (individual sinning against God's commandments) operate within the covenant relationship between Israel and the Lord. The pattern suggests that CHAR-2 in the interpersonal and representative registers operates *within existing bonds*, where the relational structure shapes how the inadvertent violation propagates.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. Within existing bonds: the individual confessions to God (VCG-01) operate within the covenant bond — the confessor is already in a relationship with God that confession addresses and repairs. Shimei's confession (2Sa 19:20) operates within the subject-king bond. The prodigal's confession (Luk 15:21) operates within the father-son bond. These within-bond confessions presuppose a relationship that sin has damaged but not destroyed. Across relational distance: Exo 9:27/10:16 — Pharaoh, who has no covenant bond with the Lord, confesses using the covenant language ("the Lord is in the right") — a confession that operates across relational distance. Judas (Mat 27:4) confesses to the priests with whom he was in a transactional (not covenant) relationship.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes. The Jeremiah passages (Jer 2:35; 16:10) show conscience suppression operating specifically within the covenantal relationship: Israel denies sin against the Lord who has been in sustained covenant with them. The depth of the suppression is related to the depth of the relationship — the people have received the prophetic word, the law, and the covenant promises, and yet their conscience is suppressed. The Ecclesiastes passages (Ecc 2:26; 8:12) present conscience suppression in the general human register — applying to anyone who lacks the fear of God, not specifically to covenant Israel. The characteristic is more explicitly addressed within the covenantal bond, where the conditions for moral self-knowledge were most fully provided.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Yes. The Kings corpus shows refusal to repent operating *within existing covenantal bonds*: each king stands in the Davidic kingship tradition, within Israel's covenant with the Lord, and their refusal is a refusal within that relationship. The urgency of Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them *back* to your law" — the word "back" (*shuv*) confirms a pre-existing relational orientation that the refusal is turning away from. The depth of the relational bond (the covenant) makes the refusal more morally serious, not less — refusing to turn within an established covenant is a greater moral act than refusing within no relationship.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. Within the covenant bond: 2Ki 17:7 — "sinned against the Lord their God, *who had brought them up out of Egypt*" — habitual defection within the established covenant relationship is qualitatively more severe because it operates against a relational foundation of redemptive grace. 1Sa 2:25 — "if someone sins against the Lord, who can intercede for him?" — sinning against the Lord directly (as opposed to against another person) is categorically different because the relationship involves the primary covenantal party. The depth of the established relational bond makes habitual defection within it the more morally weighty instance.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. Contagious sin operates most powerfully within existing covenantal authority bonds: the Kings corpus shows contagious sin transmitted through the established king-people covenant relationship — a pre-existing bond of authority and accountability. The transmission is effective precisely because the bond is pre-existing: the community already recognises the king's authority. The pre-entry context (Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18) addresses contagious sin across relational distance — from foreign nations who are not yet in a covenant relationship with Israel. The mechanism is different: proximity and teaching rather than authority. The corpus shows contagious sin operating through both existing bonds (covenantal-authority) and across relational distance (proximity-influence), but most severely through existing authority bonds.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Yes. Two relational contexts appear. (1) *Vassal-state bond* (Moab-Israel; Edom-Judah): the revolt breaks a suzerain-vassal political bond — an external governing relationship between distinct peoples. (2) *Intra-covenantal bond* (Israel from house of David — 1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19): the revolt breaks a covenantally grounded internal governing relationship — the Davidic authority structure within the covenant community itself. The intra-covenantal revolt carries greater theological weight because the bond broken is not merely political but covenantally-theologically grounded.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Yes. VCG-01's slander within community context (Eph 4:31; Col 3:8) operates within existing bonds — slander between people who are in relationship damages from the inside: the relational bond is exploited by the sinful speech. Act 6:11's false witness against Stephen operates by introducing sinful speech into a judicial-social context — the speech targets a person across a judicial relationship. VCG-02's blasphemy against Jesus operates in a formal judicial context (the trial) — the charge is brought by religious authorities against one who stands before them. The characteristic operates most destructively within existing bonds of trust and authority.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Yes. (1) *Enticement*: 2Pe 2:18 — the enticers use the teaching relationship (they are teachers with authority — "speaking loud boasts") as the delivery medium; the existing relational bond between teacher and student is the channel. Within existing relational bonds, enticement is more effective (the trust is already established). (2) *Hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — the collective hypocrisy operates entirely within existing relational bonds — the Jewish community at Antioch; sunupokrinomai requires pre-existing social bonds to operate. (3) *Apostasy*: Act 21:21 — the covenantal community bond (the Jewish covenantal identity) is what is being abandoned; apostasy operates by dissolving an existing relational-covenantal bond.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes. (1) *Within the covenant community*: Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — the condition operates without distinction within the covenant community (Jews) and outside it (Greeks); even the covenant relation does not exempt persons from the condition. Heb 5:3 — the high priest must sacrifice for his own sins alongside the people's — the condition operates within the most intimate covenant-service relationship. (2) *Across relational distance*: Luk 24:47 — "repentance for the forgiveness of sins should be proclaimed to all nations" — the remedy is extended across all relational and cultural distances; the condition's universality makes the remedy's scope equally universal.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. Within the community context: 1Jo 5:16 — "if anyone sees his brother committing a sin not leading to death, he shall ask, and God will give him life" — the enslaving power's grip on a sinning brother is addressed within the existing relational bond of community membership; the intercessory prayer operates through the relational bond between brother and brother. The enslaving power that holds the sinning brother can be addressed from within the relational bond. For sin leading to death (1Jo 5:16) — even the relational bond cannot provide sufficient intercessory access; the enslavement has moved beyond the relational-bond remedy.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes. Within existing covenantal bonds: Lev 26:18-28 — the sevenfold discipline is specifically covenant-internal; the record's consequences escalate within the established God-Israel relationship. The record's weight is heavier within the covenant precisely because the relational expectations are higher (1Ki 14:22 — Judah "provoked him to jealousy... more than all that their fathers had done"). Across relational distance: Gen 18:20 — Sodom's sin is great enough to reach God as an outcry even from outside the covenant community; the divine record operates without respect to covenantal membership.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes. Within the covenant community (VCG-01/03): the priestly atonement system operates within the established God-Israel covenant bond; the atonement is conducted within the Tabernacle/Temple as the designated relational space between God and Israel. Exo 30:16 — the atonement money brings Israel "to remembrance before the Lord" — the covenant relationship is the operative relational field. Across relational distance: Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins" — the scope extends beyond Israel to all who believe (Act 10 context — Cornelius, a Gentile); the NT register extends forgiveness across the prior relational boundary of covenant membership.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes. Within the covenant community: the condition operates through the covenant-relational bonds of lineage, national identity, and shared worship history — Isa 43:27 addresses Israel specifically ("your first father," "your mediators"); Psa 78:17/32 addresses the wilderness generation as the covenant community. The condition is transmitted through the specific relational bonds of the covenant people's shared history. Beyond the covenant: the corpus does not address how generational sin operates outside Israel's specific covenant lineage.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes. Within relational bonds (social community): Psa 1:1 — the "way of sinners" is a social fellowship that the blessed man must actively avoid; Pro 1:10 — sinners entice those in relational proximity; the sinner-identity operates through relational proximity as a field of moral influence. Across the God-human relational bond: Joh 9:31 — "God does not listen to sinners" — the relational bond with God is impaired from the sinner's side; Heb 7:26 — "separated from sinners" — the relational distance from holiness is structural.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes. Within covenant relational bonds: Ezr 9:7 — the guilt operates as a generational burden within the sustained covenant community; the guilt has accumulated through the ongoing relational context of Israel-God covenant; Lev 22:16 — guilt incurred within the priestly service relationship. Across relational distance: Gen 42:21 — the brothers' guilt toward Joseph, now separated by years and captivity, remains operative across the relational distance; guilt persists through relational disruption without diminishing.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes. Within the covenant relational bond: Amo 3:2 — "you only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities" — the covenant relationship intensifies rather than excuses accountability; intimate knowledge makes the iniquity more weighty; Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — God's weariness is a covenant-relational experience. Across relational distance: Gen 15:16 — the Amorites' iniquity is tracked by God without explicit covenant relationship; Jer 25:12 — Babylon's iniquity is reckoned after seventy years without covenant standing.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — relational depth intensifies the transgression's moral weight. Isa 1:2 — "children have I reared and brought up, but they have rebelled against me" — the parental-investment relationship makes the rebellion more morally severe; Pro 18:19 — "a brother offended is more unyielding than a strong city" — the closer the relational bond, the harder the barrier created by transgression. Across relational distance: Amos 1:3/6/9/11/13/2:1 — foreign nations (Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab) without covenant relationship still accumulate transgression before God and face judgment. Both distances operate; the covenant relationship intensifies accountability (Amos 3:2 — "you only have I known... therefore I will punish you").

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Yes — the prior relational bond is constitutive of faithlessness: there can be no faithlessness without it. Within the divine-covenant bond: Eze 20:27 — "your fathers blasphemed me, by dealing treacherously with me" — the blasphemy-equivalence of faithlessness is only possible within the intimate covenant relationship; Amos 3:2 (CHAR-19 adjacent) — "you only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities" — the uniquely intimate covenant intensifies the accountability. Within marriage bonds: Mal 2:14 — "your companion and your wife by covenant" — the companionship-depth makes the faithlessness devastating.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — relational proximity intensifies perversion's corrupting reach. 1Cor 15:33 — "bad company ruins good morals" — the company-relationship (close proximity) is the mechanism of corruption; Pro 16:28 — "a whisperer separates close friends" — the close friendship is precisely what perversity targets and destroys; 2Cor 11:3 — the pastoral relationship (Paul's anxiety for his congregation) is the context in which thoughts are led astray. Across distance: Rev 19:2 — Babylon's corruption reaches the whole earth — the corrupt agent of sufficient power corrupts even across relational distance.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes. Within intimate bonds: Jer 2:5 — "what wrong did your fathers find in me that they went far from me?" — God's rhetorical question to Israel as his covenant-people; the closest relational bond (covenant-parent/child) makes the "no injustice" claim most morally weighty. Within judicial bonds: Psa 82:2 — judges who should protect those before them; the positional bond intensifies the injustice. Across relational distance: the nations in Amos 1–2 (CHAR-19 adjacent) commit cross-boundary injustice; Rev 18:5 — Babylon's corruption reaching the whole earth across all relational distances.

### T4.5.2

**Question:** Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries. VCG-01 is primarily covenantal (Israel sinning against the Lord — the covenant framework defines the sin). But VCG-02 includes sinning across non-covenantal boundaries: Gen 20:9 (Abraham sinning against Abimelech — a pagan king outside the covenant); Gen 42:22 (Joseph — a Hebrew sinned against by his brothers before the Sinai covenant). Mat 18:15/21 (community of disciples context — a new covenant setting). The characteristic is not confined to the Mosaic covenant but operates wherever relational bonds and moral claims exist. Eze 18:4/20 (individual soul-level accountability) operates without covenantal specification.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary CHAR-2 evidence base is covenantal throughout: Lev 4:2 and Num 15:27/28 address sinning in the context of Israel's covenant with the Lord. Num 22:34 (Balaam) represents a non-Israelite encountering the characteristic — suggesting that the principle extends beyond the Sinai covenant to any context where God's moral reality intersects with creaturely epistemic limitation. Balaam is not an Israelite under the Mosaic covenant, yet his inadvertent sinning (not knowing the angel stood against him) follows the same pattern of inadvertence → disclosure → confession → offer of reversal. The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries in its logic, even if the prescribed Levitical remedies are specific to the covenant context.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession crosses covenantal boundaries. Pharaoh's confessions (Exo 9:27/10:16) are confessions by a non-covenant party to the covenant Lord — showing that the confession-form operates across the boundary, even without the covenant relationship that would make it fully operative. Judas (Mat 27:4) confesses to the chief priests — a transactional rather than covenantal relationship. However, the corpus's most substantial and productive confessions are covenantal: the VCG-02 corporate confessions (Judg 10:10; 1Sa 7:6; Dan 9:5) all operate within Israel's covenant framework and produce covenantal renewal. The form crosses the boundary; the full productive effect is covenantal.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries. Ecc 8:12 — "the sinner does evil a hundred times... the absence of the fear of God within" — wisdom literature addresses the human condition generally, not specifically Israel. 1Cor 15:34 — the NT community context. Tit 3:11 — divisive person within a community of faith. The characteristic applies wherever conscience can be suppressed — within and outside the Mosaic covenant structure. The covenantal instances (Jer 2:35; 16:10) are the most fully developed because the moral accountability there is most fully specified, but conscience suppression is a characteristic of human moral failure generically.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The primary corpus is covenantal: Israel's covenant with the Lord is the frame for the Kings corpus and Neh 9:29. However, Rom 6:15 — "are we to sin because we are not under law but under grace?" — extends the characteristic into the new-covenant context: refusing to repent under grace is possible and explicitly rejected. Heb 10:26 — "sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth" — operates in a context where the new covenant's truth has been received. The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries and intensifies within them: the more clearly the covenant's moral requirements are known, the more culpable the refusal to repent.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection crosses covenantal boundaries. The Ecclesiastes person-type statements (Ecc 7:26; 9:2/18; Pro 11:31/13:22) address the sinner as a general human type, not specifically as a covenant member. 1Ki 8:46 — "there is no one who does not sin" — universal scope including all humans. The characteristic is covenantally intensified when it operates against the covenant Lord (2Ki 17:7; 1Sa 2:25) but is not confined to the covenantal context: the sinner of wisdom literature is a human type applicable across all moral contexts.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin crosses covenantal boundaries. Exo 23:33 and Deu 20:18 address the danger from non-covenantal foreign nations: the Canaanites and their neighbours are not in covenant with Israel or the Lord, yet their sinning influence can transmit through proximity and teaching. The teaching mode (Deu 20:18) requires no prior covenantal bond — proximity and instructional authority are sufficient. The Kings corpus shows contagious sin within the covenant (Israelite kings transmitting to the covenant people). Both registers are present: covenantal-authority transmission (dominant — Kings) and proximity-teaching transmission (pre-entry warning — Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18).

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt crosses covenantal boundaries. Moab's revolt against Israel (2Ki 1:1; 3:5/7) and Edom's revolt against Judah (2Ki 8:20/22; 2Ch 21:8/10) are extra-covenantal: Moab and Edom are not members of the Israelite covenant. The revolt operates in the suzerain-vassal political register regardless of covenantal membership. Israel's revolt from the house of David (1Ki 12:19; 2Ch 10:19) is intra-covenantal. Both registers are present; the covenantal context intensifies the theological weight without being the only context.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Sinful speech crosses covenantal boundaries. VCG-01's slander operates in the general community context (Eph 4:31 — the Ephesian community; Col 3:8). VCG-02's blasphemy-charge occurs within the Jewish covenant context but is directed at Jesus who transcends it. VCG-03's beast-blasphemy (Rev 13:1/5/6) is a cosmic-eschatological context that exceeds any specific covenant — it is universal in scope, directed against God's name, dwelling, and all who dwell in heaven. The characteristic operates across all relational and covenantal contexts without restriction.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The mechanisms cross covenantal boundaries. Enticement (Jam 1:14; 2Pe 2:14/18) applies universally — "each person is tempted" (Jam 1:14) — regardless of covenant membership. Hypocrisy (Gal 2:13) operates within the covenantal community (Jewish believers) but its principle applies beyond covenant boundaries. Apostasy (Act 21:21) is specifically covenantal in this instance (forsaking Moses), but the apostasia root applies to any covenantal-standing departure.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The condition crosses all covenantal boundaries. Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — the foundational statement that the condition is not restricted to those outside the covenant; Rom 2:12 — "all who have sinned without the law will perish... all who have sinned under the law will be judged" — whether under the law (covenant) or not, the condition applies and produces judgment. The universal scope of the condition (Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned") is not qualified by covenantal membership.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The enslaving power crosses all boundaries. Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — "everyone" is universal, not restricted to covenant membership. Gal 3:22 — "imprisoned everything under sin" — the "everything" encompasses all persons regardless of covenantal standing. 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil" — "whoever" is universal. The enslaving power is not covenantally bounded; it holds all who practice sin regardless of identity or membership.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The record crosses all covenantal boundaries. Gen 18:20 — Sodom (outside the covenant) has a sin-record before God; Amo 5:12 — "I know how many are your transgressions" is addressed to Israel within the covenant; Rom 3:25 — "former sins" encompassed all humanity in divine forbearance. The divine record is not covenantally bounded: every person, in every relationship with or without the covenant, stands with a sin-record before God. What differs across the covenantal boundary is the specific content of the record (covenant-specific obligations generating covenant-specific entries) rather than the record's existence.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries in the NT register. Act 10:43 — "everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through his name" — "everyone" in context (Cornelius the Gentile) explicitly crosses the Jewish-covenant boundary; Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man" — "children of man" (sons of Adam) is a universal human category, not a covenantal one; 2Cor 5:19 — "God was reconciling the world to himself" — "the world" is the broadest possible scope. The OT register (VCG-01/03) operates primarily within the covenant community; the NT register explicitly extends to all who believe.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-15]** S — The 8-verse corpus addresses generational sin exclusively within the covenant community of Israel (Isa 43:27 — "your first father"; Psa 78:17/32 — Israel in the wilderness; 1Ki 8:47 — the exiles; Jer 8:14 — Judah; Eze 37:23 — Israel). No verse in the corpus explicitly extends the generational mechanism beyond the covenant community. The question of whether generational sin operates across covenantal boundaries (e.g. in non-Israelite lineages) cannot be answered from this corpus.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-identity crosses covenantal boundaries. Gal 2:15 — "Gentile sinners" — the label applies to those outside the Jewish covenant. Gen 13:13 — Sodom (outside Israel's covenant) bears the sinner-identity. 1Sa 15:18 — the Amalekites (outside Israel's covenant) are designated sinners by God. Mat 9:10/11 — "tax collectors and sinners" spans Jewish and social categories. The sinner-as-moral-character is not a covenantally bounded category: it applies wherever sinfulness defines a person's moral identity, regardless of covenant membership.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The characteristic operates primarily within covenant contexts (VCG-01's Levitical sequences; VCG-02's accumulation in Israel; VCG-03's priestly transference; VCG-04's punitive consequence in Israel/Judah). However, it also crosses covenantal boundaries: Gen 4:13 — Cain's guilt before God precedes the formal covenant; Gen 42:21 — the brothers' guilt toward Joseph involves inter-personal guilt without explicit covenantal framing; Hab 1:11 — "guilty men, whose own might is their god" — the Babylonian armies bear guilt outside Israel's covenant. The guilt-state is not exclusively covenantal.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries. Gen 15:16 — the Amorites (outside Israel's covenant) accumulate iniquity before God; Jer 25:12 — Babylon's iniquity is measured and punished; Eze 28:18 — "the multitude of your iniquities" is addressed to the king of Tyre (outside Israel's covenant). The characteristic is universal in scope: Psa 130:3 — "if you, O Lord, should mark iniquities, O Lord, who could stand?" — the universality implies all persons, not merely covenant members, accumulate iniquity before God.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — The characteristic crosses covenantal boundaries extensively. VCG-03's Amos oracle-series (Amos 1:3–2:6) covers eight nations including six outside Israel's covenant: Damascus, Gaza, Tyre, Edom, Ammon, Moab all accumulate transgression that reaches God's threshold. Dan 8:23 — "when transgressors have reached their limit" — a non-Israelite (Seleucid) context. The parabasis-family NT verses (VCG-04) apply to all law-knowers regardless of ethnic covenant membership (Rom 2:23/25/27 — the law-boaster and the law-keeper, Jew and Gentile).

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Primarily within covenantal contexts — unlike CHAR-19 (which operated universally across all nations in the Amos oracle-series), CHAR-20 is more bounded by the covenant-relationship as its definitional context. Judg 9:23 — the leaders of Shechem dealing treacherously with Abimelech — a non-explicitly covenantal political context; Hab 1:13 — traitors who swallow up the righteous — a general moral context. These indicate the characteristic can cross beyond explicit covenant contexts into any context where loyalty was owed, but the primary domain is covenantal.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The characteristic crosses all boundaries. Lev 18:23/20:12 (VCG-04) — the sexual perversions apply universally regardless of covenant; Deu 16:19 — bribery-perversion of judgment is addressed to Israel's covenant courts but the principle applies universally; 1Cor 15:33 — "bad company ruins good morals" — a universal principle; Rev 19:2 — Babylon's corruption of the whole earth — crosses all covenantal and non-covenantal contexts. CHAR-21 is universal in scope: any human moral faculty can be twisted regardless of covenant membership.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The characteristic crosses all boundaries. VCG-01's universal Levitical commands (Lev 19:15/35) apply to all Israel regardless of specific relational context; Deu 25:16 — "all who act dishonestly are an abomination" — universal condemnation; Mat 5:45 — "he makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good, and sends rain on the just and on the unjust" — God sustains even the unjust regardless of covenant membership; Rev 18:5 — Babylon's accumulated injustice crosses all covenantal and non-covenantal contexts.

### T4.5.3

**Question:** What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The relational scope is comprehensive: VCG-01 encompasses the human-God relation; VCG-02 encompasses all human-human bonds (family, king-servant, community, neighbour); VCG-05 adds the self-relation (1Cor 6:18 — sinning against one's own body) and the Christ-relation (1Cor 8:12 — sinning against a brother reaches Christ). The scope thus encompasses the full relational map of the human person: God, other humans, self, and mediately Christ. No relational category is excluded from the scope of wilful sinning.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Individual-to-God*: Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28 (individual's inadvertent violation of divine command). (2) *Representative-to-community*: Lev 4:3 (priest's sinning implicating the people). (3) *Individual-to-neighbor*: Lev 6:2/3 (interpersonal sinning through deception, misappropriation). (4) *Community-to-God*: Lev 9:15 (communal sin offering). (5) *Sacred-space*: VCG-02 (altar, sanctuary defilement). The relational scope is broad — the characteristic does not operate in one relational domain but across the full range of human-God and human-human bonds. The common thread is that inadvertent violation creates real liability in every relational domain it touches.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Individual-to-God* (VCG-01 primary — 2Sa 24:10; Mic 7:9; Luk 15:21); (2) *Corporate-to-God* (VCG-02 — Judg 10:10; 1Sa 7:6; Dan 9:5; Psa 106:6); (3) *Individual-to-person* (Exo 10:16; 2Sa 19:20; Mat 27:4); (4) *Representative individual confessing for a community* (Neh 1:6; Dan 9:5 — Daniel including himself in the community's confession). The relational scope is comprehensive and matches the scope of sin: wherever sin creates a relational breach, confession is the appropriate naming of that breach. The dominant scope is vertical (God-directed); the secondary scope is horizontal (person-directed).

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Individual-to-God* (primary — Jer 2:35; Ecc 8:12): conscience suppression most fully manifests in the person's false moral standing before God. (2) *Corporate-to-God* (Jer 16:10): communal conscience suppression manifesting as collective moral blindness before the Lord. (3) *Within-community* (1Cor 15:34; Tit 3:11): conscience suppression operating within the community of faith, producing ongoing sinning and divisive character. The relational scope is primarily vertical (God-directed) with secondary horizontal (community) dimensions.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The relational scope is: (1) *Individual-to-God* (Eze 18:24 — the formerly righteous person's personal refusal; Heb 10:26 — individual deliberate sinning); (2) *Leadership-to-community* (the Kings pattern — the leader's refusal transmitted to the people); (3) *Community-to-God* (Neh 9:29; 2Ki 13:6 — communal non-departure from the sinful course). The relational scope is wide: the characteristic operates in every relational configuration, from the most individual (Eze 18:24) to the most communal (the whole nation refusing to depart — 2Ki 13:6).

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Individual-to-God* (1Jo 3:6 — never seen or known him; 2Ki 17:7 — sinning against the Lord); (2) *Communal-to-God* (Heb 3:17 — wilderness generation over forty years; 2Ki 17:7 — "the people of Israel"); (3) *Individual-to-community* (Ecc 9:18 — one sinner destroying much good; 1Sa 2:25 — priestly sinning against the Lord affecting the community); (4) *General human* (1Ki 8:46; Ecc 7:20 — universal scope). The relational scope is the widest of any characteristic studied: habitual defection applies across every relational configuration from the individual's God-relationship to the universal human moral condition.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *National scale*: "Israel" (1Ki 14:16; 2Ki 17:21) and "Judah" (2Ki 21:11) as entire national communities receiving the transmitted sinning; (2) *Multi-generational*: 1Ki 22:52 — three generations receiving successively; (3) *Cross-cultural*: Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18 — foreign nations transmitting to Israel across cultural-ethnic boundaries; (4) *Structural-institutional*: 2Ki 23:15 — the altar at Bethel as the physical-structural scope of the transmission outlasting any individual. The relational scope of contagious sin is the widest of any M10 characteristic: it operates at national, multi-generational, cross-cultural, and structural-institutional levels simultaneously.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Vassal-nation level*: Moab against Israel; Edom and Libnah against Judah — nation-to-nation governing relationships; (2) *Intra-covenant political level*: northern Israel against the house of David — internal covenant-community governing relationships; (3) *Spiritual-political level*: 2Ch 21:10 — the ruler's relationship with God as the ultimate ground of political authority. The scope ranges from the immediately political (vassal-nation) to the covenantally grounded (Davidic) to the theologically foundational (ruler-to-God).

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Horizontal person-to-person*: VCG-01 — slander directed at community members; (2) *Judicial-social*: Act 6:11 — false witness in a formal juridical context; (3) *Christological*: VCG-02 — blasphemy charge directed at the Son of God; (4) *Spirit-level*: Mat 12:31 — blasphemy against the Spirit; (5) *Cosmic-eschatological*: VCG-03 — beast's blasphemy against God's name, dwelling, and heavenly community. The relational scope spans from the interpersonal-communal to the eschatological-cosmic — the widest relational range of any single characteristic in the M10 cluster.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The relational scope covers: (1) *Universal individual scope*: Jam 1:14 — "each person" — enticement through desire applies to all moral subjects; (2) *Community scope*: Gal 2:13 — the entire community at Antioch is drawn into collective hypocrisy; (3) *Covenantal-communal scope*: Act 21:21 — the Jewish diaspora community as the scope of the apostasy accusation; (4) *Teacher-student relational scope*: 2Pe 2:18 — specific teaching relationships as the enticement channel.
