## T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

### T0.1.1

**Question:** What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning reveals the character of God negatively: it is the one thing the verse evidence consistently shows God holding the person morally accountable for, which illuminates God as the moral reference point against whom all wilful sin is ultimately directed. Psa 51:4 states the paradigmatic formula — "Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight" — locating God as the primary party offended by every act of wilful sinning, even sins with human victims (David's sin against Bathsheba and Uriah). Gen 39:9 (Joseph) frames the refusal to sin as inseparable from reverence for God: "How then can I do this great wickedness and sin against God?" — implying that God's moral character defines the boundary the sinning will crosses. The characteristic thus reflects God's holiness by negation: wilful sinning is the inner act that violates what God is.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning reveals the character of God primarily through the remedial system God established to address it — a system that reflects God's moral seriousness about sin at every intentionality level, and his provision for the person who has sinned without knowing. Lev 4:2 — "if anyone sins unintentionally in any of the Lord's commandments about things not to be done" — presupposes a God who holds commandments whose violation is real even without deliberate defiance. The prescribed remedy (Num 15:28 — "the priest shall make atonement before the Lord for the person who makes a mistake, when he sins unintentionally... and he shall be forgiven") reveals a God who is both morally exacting (even inadvertent violation requires remedy) and graciously provident (a remedy is prescribed and forgiveness follows). The character of God visible here is *holy and merciful simultaneously*: the holiness requires that inadvertent sin be addressed; the mercy ensures that address is available.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession reveals God's character in two directions. (1) *God as the one to whom confession is rightly directed*: throughout the corpus, genuine confession is oriented toward God as the primary offended party — "I have sinned against the Lord" (1Sa 7:6; Jer 14:20; Dan 9:11; Luk 15:18/21). God's moral authority as the standard against which sin is measured makes him the proper recipient of confession — which reveals him as *holy and morally sovereign*, the reference point of all moral reality. (2) *God as the one who cancels sin upon genuine confession*: Eze 33:16 — "None of the sins that he has committed shall be remembered against him" — reveals God as a God who holds a moral record but is prepared to erase it when the person turns. The characteristic exists in a landscape where confession is heard and responded to by a God who both takes sin seriously and provides for its cancellation.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression reveals the character of God primarily through contrast and consequence. Jer 2:35 — God declares: "Behold, I will bring you to judgment for saying, 'I have not sinned'" — God's response to the false innocence claim reveals him as a God who sees through self-deception and judges not only the sin but the denial of it. God's moral omniscience is the implicit premise: the person may suppress awareness of their sin, but God's awareness is undiminished. Jer 16:10 — the people ask "what sin have we committed?" in the face of declared judgment — God's announcement of judgment presupposes his accurate moral knowledge even when the persons themselves lack it. The characteristic thus reveals God as the one whose perception cannot be suppressed: what the conscience suppresses in the human, God sees clearly and will bring to judgment.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent reveals God's character primarily through the expectation it violates and the response it draws. Neh 9:29 — "you warned them in order to turn them back to your law. Yet they acted presumptuously and did not obey" — reveals God as a God who warns before judging, who desires turning, and who regards the refusal to turn as a presumptuous overriding of his patient initiative. The God who warns is a God of moral patience and persistent invitation; the human refusal to turn is the creature's rejection of that invitation. Heb 10:26 — "if we go on sinning deliberately after receiving the knowledge of the truth, there no longer remains a sacrifice for sins" — reveals that God's provision of sacrifice presupposes the creature's willingness to engage it; refusal to turn after knowing the truth closes off the divine provision. The characteristic reveals God as one whose moral provisions (sacrifice, warning, the turn) are refused by the creature that should receive them.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection reveals God's character through two lenses. (1) *God as the one who is sinned against habitually*: 2Ki 17:7 — "the people of Israel had sinned against the Lord their God, who had brought them up out of the land of Egypt" — God is the covenant Lord whose redemptive action is the context against which habitual sinning occurs. The relationship God established is the relational framework within which the recurring defection takes place. (2) *God as the one whose patience is provoked for an extended period*: Heb 3:17 — "with whom was he provoked for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned?" — God's provocation over forty years reveals his holiness responding to sustained rebellion — not immediate termination but prolonged moral witness sustained across a whole generation's habitual sinning. The character revealed is: morally serious toward sustained defection, patient in the sense of bearing it over time, and ultimately resolute in consequence (the bodies fell in the wilderness).

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin reveals the character of God through three lenses. (1) *God as the one who warns in advance*: Exo 23:33 and Deu 20:18 are preventive commands — God specifies before entry into Canaan that foreign nations will "make Israel sin" and that being taught their abominable practices will result in sinning against the Lord. God's foreknowledge of the contagion mechanism and his preventive instruction reveal him as a God who sees the transmission pathways of sin before they operate. (2) *God as the one who holds leaders accountable for what they transmitted*: 1Ki 16:2 — God directly charges the king: "you have made my people Israel to sin, provoking me to anger" — naming the contagious transmission as the specific indictment. 1Ki 21:22 — God declares Ahab's house will be destroyed "because you have made Israel to sin." (3) *God as the one who is provoked through transmitted sin*: 1Ki 14:22; 1Ki 16:13/26 — Israel's sins (transmitted from the leader) provoke God to anger through their idolatrous objects. The communal sin that leaders generate reaches God as a relational offence from the community.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt reveals God's character primarily through one distinctive verse: 2Ch 21:10 — "So Edom revolted from the rule of Judah to this day... At that time Libnah also revolted from his rule, *because he had forsaken the Lord, the God of his fathers*." The causal connection — Jehoram forsaking the Lord producing Libnah's revolt — reveals God as the constitutive source of covenantal-political order. When the king abandons God, the political coherence that God's covenantal relationship sustained begins to fragment. God's character is revealed as the foundation of ordered authority: his being forsaken produces political disorder; his presence maintains the relational-political structure. The remaining verses address political revolt at the human-authority level without explicit God-reference, but 2Ch 21:10 establishes that God is the ultimate ground of the authority structure from which revolt breaks.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Sinful speech reveals God's character through three lenses. (1) *God as the exclusive holder of divine prerogatives*: Mar 2:7 — "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" and Joh 10:33 — "you, being a man, make yourself God" — blasphemy is defined as the improper claiming of what belongs exclusively to God. The characteristic reveals God as the one whose authority, identity, and prerogatives are absolute and non-transferable. (2) *God as the one whose Spirit cannot be blasphemed without eternal consequence*: Mat 12:31 — "the blasphemy against the Spirit will not be forgiven" — God's Spirit is the one reality against which blasphemous speech carries irreversible moral weight. (3) *God as the one against whom the beast's constitutive speech is directed*: Rev 13:6 — "It opened its mouth to utter blasphemies against God, blaspheming his name and his dwelling, that is, those who dwell in heaven" — God's name, presence, and people are the targets of the most extreme form of sinful speech.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The three mechanisms reveal God's character through contrast and implication. (1) *God as the one who does not entice through desire*: Jam 1:14 — "each person is tempted when he is lured and enticed by his own desire" — the verse is set within a context (Jam 1:13 — not quoted but implied) that explicitly distinguishes God from the enticement mechanism: God does not entice toward evil. The mechanism of desire-enticement is constitutively not-God's mode of operation. (2) *God as the ground of covenantal identity*: Act 21:21 — the apostasy charge describes forsaking Moses — the covenantal framework given through Moses is the gift of God's self-revelation; forsaking that covenantal identity is a defection from what God established. (3) *God as the one whose image requires authenticity*: Gal 2:13 — sunupokrinomai (collective hypocrisy) falsifies identity and corrupts community integrity; authenticity before God and in community is what the characteristic destroys.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The 112-verse corpus reveals God's character through five distinct dimensions. (1) *God as the one whose glory constitutes the standard from which sin's universality is measured*: Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — God's glory is the normative standard; sin is defined as the universal human falling-short of that glory, revealing God as the one in whose image humanity was made to live but from whose likeness all have departed. (2) *God as the one who alone can forgive sins*: the VCG-02 forgiveness register — Mar 2:7; Luk 5:21 — "who can forgive sins but God alone?" — God's exclusive authority to forgive is what makes Jesus's forgiveness-declarations a claim to divine identity. The character of God as the exclusive forgiver is the foundation on which the entire VCG-02 corpus rests. (3) *God as the one who condemns sin in the flesh through the Son*: Rom 8:3 — "he condemned sin in the flesh" — God's active judicial movement against sin through the incarnation reveals his moral seriousness and salvific intent simultaneously. (4) *God as the one whose holiness requires propitiation and purification*: Heb 2:17; Heb 1:3 — sin creates an offense requiring propitiation and a defilement requiring purification; both reveal God as holy in a way that cannot simply absorb sin's presence. (5) *God as the one whose new-covenant promise is specifically the removal of sin*: Rom 11:27 — "when I take away their sins" — God's covenantal commitment is defined by what he will do about sin.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Sin as enslaving power reveals God's character primarily through the liberation it requires. (1) *God as the liberator from sin's mastery*: Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — Jesus names the slavery as the condition requiring the freedom that the Son can give (the broader Joh 8:36 context: "if the Son sets you free, you will be free indeed" — implied by the slavery statement). (2) *God as the source of the indwelling seed that prevents sin's dominion*: 1Jo 3:9 — "no one born of God makes a practice of sinning, for God's seed abides in him" — the divine birth and the indwelling seed are the counter-powers to sin's enslaving function; these are God-given. (3) *God as protector against sin's dominion*: 1Jo 5:18 — "he who was born of God protects him, and the evil one does not touch him" — God actively protects the born-of-God person from sin's re-enslavement; the protecting agent (born of God — Christ) is the divine counter to the enslaving power.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The 205-verse corpus reveals God's character across five dimensions. (1) *God as the one who sees and records*: Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God maintains a divine register; Gen 18:20 — "the outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is great and their sin is very grave" — the sin has reached God as a measurable, audible reality. (2) *God as the one who remembers sin precisely*: Jer 14:10 — "he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins"; Hos 13:12 — "his sin is kept in store" — the divine memory is reliable and permanent. (3) *God as the one who searches for sin*: Job 10:6 — "that you seek out my iniquity and search for my sin" (VCG-03) — God actively investigates and scrutinises the person. (4) *God as the one who blots out the record*: Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake, and I will not remember your sins" — God can erase the divine record as a sovereign act. (5) *God as the one who bears the burden of his people's sin*: Isa 43:24 — "you have burdened me with your sins; you have wearied me with your iniquities" — the accumulated record is experienced by God as a burden on the divine-human relationship.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The 34-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who provides the means of atonement*: Exo 29:36 — the daily sin offering is God's prescribed institution; Lev 23:27/28 — the Day of Atonement is God's appointed holy convocation; he has established the framework within which forgiveness is available. (2) *God as the one who does not count trespasses*: 2Cor 5:19 — "in Christ God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses against them" — the divine disposition is toward non-imputation, not toward accumulating the record against the person. (3) *God as the one who provides blood-atonement*: Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — God himself provides the atoning blood rather than requiring the creature to supply it. (4) *God as the one whose forgiveness has eschatological scope*: Mar 3:28 — "all sins will be forgiven the children of man" — God's forgiveness has universal potential scope, with only one exception (Mar 3:29 — blasphemy against the Holy Spirit).

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — The 8-verse corpus reveals God's character across three dimensions. (1) *God as the one who holds generations accountable to the ancestral record*: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — God addresses present Israel through the lens of ancestral failure; the present generation is addressed as the inheritors of what the first father began. (2) *God as the one who responds to prolonged corporate sin with judgment*: Jer 8:14 — "the Lord our God has doomed us to perish... because we have sinned against the Lord" — the corporate sinning of the present generation in continuity with the past produces divine doom; (3) *God as the redeemer who saves from ancestral backsliding*: Eze 37:23 — "I will save them from all the backslidings in which they have sinned, and will cleanse them; and they shall be my people, and I will be their God" — God's redemptive purpose addresses the inherited sinning pattern from outside it.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The 64-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who names and categorises the sinner*: 1Sa 15:18 — "Go, devote to destruction the sinners, the Amalekites" — God designates the Amalekites as "sinners" as the basis for their destruction; the divine naming is the authoritative moral verdict; Amo 9:8 — "the eyes of the Lord God are upon the sinful kingdom" — God's omniscient gaze identifies the sinful character of a people. (2) *God as the one whose holiness defines the sinner's incompatibility*: Isa 33:14 — "who among us can dwell with the consuming fire?" — the sinner's dread reveals God as a consuming holiness that the sinner-identity cannot survive; Psa 1:5 — sinners excluded from the congregation of the righteous under God's ordering. (3) *God as the one who pursues sinners for redemption*: Psa 25:8 — "he instructs sinners in the way"; Mat 9:13 — "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners"; 1Ti 1:15 — "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners." (4) *God as the one who constitutes sinner-status through Adam*: Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners" — God's judgment of Adam's act imputes the sinner-identity to all.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The 97-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who sees guilt when the person cannot hide it*: Jer 2:22 — "though you wash yourself with lye and use much soap, the stain of your guilt is still before me, declares the Lord God" — divine omniscience makes guilt permanently visible regardless of the person's concealment effort; Psa 69:5 — "the wrongs I have done are not hidden from you" — the divine gaze penetrates guilt's concealment. (2) *God as the one who withholds to provoke guilt-recognition*: Hos 5:15 — "I will return again to my place, until they acknowledge their guilt and seek my face" — God strategically withdraws until guilt is acknowledged; the withdrawal is purposeful. (3) *God as the one who removes guilt by sovereign act*: Isa 6:7 — "your guilt is taken away, and your sin atoned for" — God removes the guilt-burden through the seraph's action; (4) *God as the one who exacts less than guilt deserves*: Job 11:6 — "God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves" — divine mercy moderates the full punitive weight.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — The 162-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as moral auditor who measures and tracks iniquity to completion*: Gen 15:16 — "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet complete" — God tracks iniquity as a measurable quantity with a threshold; Hos 13:12 — "the iniquity of Ephraim is bound up; his sin is kept in store" — God maintains a sealed record; Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you, our secret sins in the light of your presence" — nothing is hidden from his omniscient gaze. (2) *God as just judge who acts when iniquity reaches its measure*: Isa 26:21 — "the Lord is coming out from his place to punish the inhabitants of the earth for their iniquity"; Jer 25:12 — after seventy years God punishes Babylon for their iniquity. (3) *God as sovereign pardoner who removes iniquity by his own act*: Isa 40:2 — "her iniquity is pardoned"; Mic 7:18/19 — "pardoning iniquity... he will tread our iniquities underfoot"; Jer 31:34 — "I will forgive their iniquity." (4) *God as the one wearied by accumulated iniquity*: Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — the accumulation has a relational weight that strains even divine forbearance.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — The 147-verse corpus reveals God's character across five dimensions. (1) *God as the one who establishes boundaries and commands*: Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — the transgression is possible only because God has established a knowable order; transgression presupposes divine command; (2) *God as the one who counts and tracks transgressions*: Amos 1:3 — "for three transgressions of Damascus, and for four, I will not revoke the punishment" — God keeps a precise count toward a threshold; Dan 9:24 — "seventy weeks are decreed... to finish the transgression" — a divine timeline governs transgression's end; (3) *God as the one who does not pardon certain transgressions*: Exo 23:21 — "he will not pardon your transgression, for my name is in him" — some transgressions exceed the reach of pardon; Jos 24:19 — "he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins"; (4) *God as the one who pardons through steadfast love*: Mic 7:18 — "pardoning iniquity and passing over transgression for the remnant of his inheritance"; Isa 43:25 — "I, I am he who blots out your transgressions for my own sake"; (5) *God as the one who is pierced by transgression through the servant*: Isa 53:5 — "he was pierced for our transgressions" — the transgression's cost falls on the servant whom God appoints.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — The 101-verse corpus reveals God's character across five dimensions. (1) *God as the one who is personally betrayed*: Lev 26:40 — "treachery that they committed against me"; Eze 39:23 — "they dealt treacherously with me"; Dan 9:7 — "treachery that they have committed against you" — the faithlessness is directed against God personally; it wounds the relational bond from within; (2) *God as the faithful covenant-keeper whose faithfulness is the measure of the betrayal*: Exo 21:8 — breaking faith implies a prior faith that was owed; 1Ch 10:13 — "Saul died for his breach of faith... he did not keep the command of the Lord" — faithfulness to divine command is the standard violated; (3) *God as the divine witness of covenant bonds*: Mal 2:14 — "the Lord was witness between you and the wife of your youth" — God himself is the named witness of the covenant whose breaking constitutes treachery; (4) *God as the one who judges treachery*: Eze 17:20 — "enter into judgment with him there for the treachery he has committed against me"; 2Ch 30:7 — "he made them a desolation"; (5) *God as the one who responds to confession of treachery*: Lev 26:40 — "if they confess their iniquity and... treachery" — God's response to honest confession of faithlessness is embedded in the text; restoration follows confession.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The 64-verse corpus reveals God's character across four dimensions. (1) *God as the one who hates perversity*: Pro 8:13 — "pride and arrogance and the way of evil and perverted speech I hate" — wisdom (personified as God's attribute) explicitly names hatred of perversity; (2) *God as the one who watches over the perversion of judgment*: Exo 23:8; Deu 16:19 — bribery blinds the wise and subverts the righteous; God's justice is the standard against which the perversion of justice is measured; (3) *God as the one who judges perversity*: Pro 21:12 — "The Righteous One observes the house of the wicked; he throws the wicked down to ruin"; Rev 19:2 — "he has judged the great prostitute who corrupted the earth with her immorality"; (4) *God as the one who disciplines rather than abandons the inwardly twisted*: 2Sa 7:14 — "when he commits iniquity, I will discipline him with the rod of men... not take my steadfast love from him" — God's response to perversity-as-iniquity is corrective discipline within sustained love rather than abandonment.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The 72-verse corpus reveals God's character primarily through explicit negation of injustice. (1) *God structurally defined as without injustice*: Deu 32:4 — "a God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he"; 2Ch 19:7 — "there is no injustice with the Lord our God, or partiality or taking bribes"; Psa 92:15 — "there is no unrighteousness in him"; Job 34:10 — "far be it from God that he should do wickedness, and from the Almighty that he should do wrong"; Zep 3:5 — "the Lord within her is righteous; he does no injustice"; (2) *God as the one who hates injustice*: Isa 61:8 — "I the Lord love justice; I hate robbery and wrong"; (3) *God as the righteous judge of injustice*: Rev 18:5 — God has remembered Babylon's crimes; 2Pe 2:9 — God keeps the unrighteous under punishment; (4) *God as the faithful rewarder against injustice*: Heb 6:10 — "God is not unjust so as to overlook your work."

### T0.1.2

**Question:** Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-1]** S — The verse corpus for CHAR-1 contains no attribution of wilful sinning to God. This absence is analytically significant: the entire 98-verse corpus is structured around human sinning against God (VCG-01, 32V), human sinning against persons (VCG-02, 18V), and human sinning requiring restraint or remedy. God appears throughout as the offended party, the restraining agent (Gen 20:6 — "it was I who kept you from sinning against me"), and the judge (Eze 18:4 — "the soul who sins shall die"), but never as a sinning subject. The silence establishes wilful sinning as an exclusively creaturely phenomenon — something that arises from the constitution of the creature, not from the nature of God.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — The corpus contains no attribution of unintentional sinning to God. God appears in the CHAR-2 corpus as the standard-setter whose commandments are violated (Lev 4:2), the one whose altar requires purification (Exo 29:36; Eze 43:20), and the one whose forgiveness resolves the condition (Num 15:28; Lev 19:22). The absence is constitutive: God cannot sin unintentionally because he cannot be without knowledge of the moral reality of any act. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes the limitation of creaturely knowledge that God does not share.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(S)*

**[CHAR-3]** S — The corpus contains no attribution of confession to God. God does not confess sin because God does not sin. The entire 34-verse corpus is structured around human persons (individual and corporate) confessing before God — with God as the consistent recipient, never the confessor. The absence is constitutively significant: confession is exclusively a creaturely act, presupposing both the capacity to sin (which the creature has and God does not) and the relational standing before a holy God that requires acknowledgment of sin.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — No verse in the 8-verse corpus attributes conscience suppression to God. God appears throughout as the one who sees, knows, and judges the moral reality that the human conscience fails to register. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes the creaturely capacity to fail to perceive moral reality that God perceives fully. God cannot suppress awareness of moral reality because he is its author and standard. The absence is constitutive.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(S)*

**[CHAR-5]** S — No verse in the 18-verse corpus attributes refusal to repent to God. God appears throughout as the one whose law is not followed (2Ki 10:31), whose warnings are not heeded (Neh 9:29), and whose sacrifice is closed off by deliberate refusal (Heb 10:26). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely: it presupposes a will that can choose to turn but chooses not to — a freedom God possesses but in the opposite direction (God always acts in accordance with his own moral nature). God cannot refuse to repent because God does not sin and therefore has no turning to withhold.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(S)*

**[CHAR-6]** S — No verse in the 14-verse corpus attributes habitual defection to God. God appears as the party against whom Israel habitually sins (2Ki 17:7), the one provoked by sustained rebellion (Heb 3:17), and the one who will redirect the sinner's wealth (Pro 13:22). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it presupposes a creature whose will can establish a settled pattern of departure from God, which is structurally inapplicable to God himself.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-7]** S — No verse in the 19-verse corpus attributes contagious sin to God. God appears throughout as the one who warns against it (Exo 23:33; Deu 20:18), is provoked by it (1Ki 14:22; 16:13), and holds leaders accountable for it (1Ki 16:2; 21:22). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it requires a human leader with a sinful inner commitment and a community that receives the transmitted direction. God does not sin, therefore cannot make others sin in the morally culpable sense the corpus describes.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(S)*

**[CHAR-8]** S — No verse in the 9-verse corpus attributes political revolt (pa.sha — breaking submission) to God. God does not revolt against authority; God is the source and ground of authority. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely — it is what subordinate parties do when they break submission to governing authority.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(S)*

**[CHAR-9]** S — No verse in the 22-verse corpus attributes blasphemy or sinful speech to God. God appears as the target of blasphemy (Rev 13:6), the one whose authority blasphemy presumes to claim (Mar 2:7; Joh 10:33), and the one whose Spirit suffers the unforgivable form (Mat 12:31) — never as a blasphemer. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely in both its slander and its blasphemy registers.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(S)*

**[CHAR-10]** S — No verse in the 5-verse corpus attributes enticement (deleazō), collective hypocrisy (sunupokrinomai), or apostasy (apostasia) to God. God does not entice through desire (Jam 1:13–14 context), does not act hypocritically (whose nature is consistent and true), and does not defect from his own covenantal commitments. All three mechanisms are exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Partially — in an inverse form. Joh 8:46 — "Which of you convicts me of sin?" — and Heb 4:15 — "tempted as we are, yet without sin" — and 2Cor 5:21 — "him who knew no sin" — all confirm that the sinless state is attributable to God (in Christ), which is the structural opposite of the universal sinful condition. God does not possess the condition; God's sinlessness is the standard against which the universality of human sin is measured and from which all humanity has fallen (Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God"). The explicit divine sinlessness functions as the positive ground of the characteristic's definition.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(S)*

**[CHAR-12]** S — No verse in the 9-verse corpus attributes sin's enslaving power to God. God appears as the liberator (1Jo 3:9 — God's seed; 1Jo 5:18 — the born-of-God protects) and as the one against whom the enslaved person sins (the broader biblical framework), but not as the enslaving power itself. The characteristic is exclusively a fallen-condition creaturely reality.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Partially — in the positive keeping-record mode. God is the keeper of the book from which sinners are blotted (Exo 32:32/33) and the one who searches for sin (Job 10:6; 14:16 — VCG-03). God maintains the record; he does not bear the sinful-record status. Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God is the author of the record, not a participant in the condition recorded in it. The characteristic thus reveals God as the moral-record keeper rather than as the one who carries a sin-record before another.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — on the giving side. God is the one who forgives: 2Cor 5:19 — "God was reconciling the world to himself, not counting their trespasses"; Col 2:13 — "God made alive together with him, having forgiven us all our trespasses"; Eph 1:7 — "the forgiveness of our trespasses, according to the riches of his grace." The seeking side is human (the person seeks forgiveness); the granting side is divine. The characteristic thus involves both divine action (granting) and human action (seeking); Scripture attributes the granting explicitly to God.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-15]** S — No verse in the 8-verse corpus attributes the inherited sin-condition of generational sin to God. God appears as the one who addresses the condition (Isa 43:27 — naming the ancestral origin; Eze 37:23 — promising redemption from the backslidings) but not as the source or bearer of the condition. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(S)*

**[CHAR-16]** S — No verse in the 64-verse corpus attributes the sinner-identity to God. God appears as the one who names sinners (1Sa 15:18; Amo 9:8), instructs them (Psa 25:8), came to save them (1Ti 1:15), and is separated from them (Heb 7:26 — "separated from sinners"). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(S)*

**[CHAR-17]** S — No verse in the 97-verse corpus attributes the inner experience of guilt to God. God appears as the one who sees guilt (Jer 2:22), responds to it with judgment or mercy, removes it (Isa 6:7), and withholds until it is acknowledged (Hos 5:15) — but does not bear guilt as a subjective inner state. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(S)*

**[CHAR-18]** S — No verse in the 162-verse corpus attributes the iniquity-as-crime condition to God. God appears as the one who measures iniquity (Gen 15:16), remembers it (Hos 13:12; Eze 21:24), punishes it (Isa 26:21), bears its weight vicariously through the servant (Isa 53:6 — "the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all"), and removes it (Zec 3:4). God does not bear iniquity as a personal moral crime; the characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(S)*

**[CHAR-19]** S — No verse in the 147-verse corpus attributes transgression to God. God appears as the one who establishes the boundaries crossed (Rom 4:15), counts transgressions (Amo 1:3), withholds or grants pardon (Exo 23:21; Mic 7:18), and absorbs their cost through the servant (Isa 53:5/8). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-20]** S — No verse in the 101-verse corpus attributes faithlessness to God. God appears as the one against whom faithlessness is directed (Lev 26:40; Dan 9:7), as the faithful witness (Mal 2:14), as the judge of treachery (Eze 17:20), and as the faithful standard against which Israel's unfaithfulness is measured. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — One verse approaches this — Lam 3:9 — "he has blocked my ways with blocks of stones; he has made my paths crooked" — God is the subject of the crookedness-making action. However, the verse describes God's judicial action upon the sufferer (making their path crooked as discipline/judgment) rather than attributing moral perversity to God's character. God here acts as the one who twists the path, not as a morally perverted being. All other verses place perversity exclusively on the human side.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The corpus explicitly tests and denies this: Rom 3:5 — "That God is unrighteous to inflict wrath on us? (I speak in a human way.)" — the suggestion of divine injustice is raised as a rhetorical foil and immediately repudiated; Job 36:23 — "who can say, 'You have done wrong'?" — the question is rhetorical, implying the answer is no one. God's absolute freedom from injustice is one of the most densely evidenced theological claims in this corpus: five verses directly assert it (Deu 32:4; 2Ch 19:7; Psa 92:15; Job 34:10; Zep 3:5). The characteristic is exclusively creaturely.

### T0.1.3

**Question:** Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The structural silence on God possessing wilful sinning (see T0.1.2) combined with the corpus's consistent framing of God as moral standard and judge (Eze 18:4; Eze 18:20; Gen 20:6; Eze 3:21) suggests that wilful sinning belongs to the fallen condition of the image-bearer rather than to the original divine image. The capacity for moral choice — the will — is part of the image; sinning is what happens when that will is turned against its Maker. 1Jo 2:1 ("I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin") presupposes that sinning is avoidable — that believers retain moral agency — but it is something Scripture explicitly aims to prevent rather than express. The imago Dei includes the will; wilful sinning represents the will's defection from the direction the image was designed to express.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The silence on God's possession of unintentional sinning (T0.1.2) reveals that this characteristic is grounded in *creaturely epistemic limitation* — the capacity to act without full knowledge of the moral implications of the act. God's omniscience excludes this capacity entirely: no act of God can be unintentional in the sense of proceeding without knowledge of its moral reality. The silence confirms that unintentional sinning belongs to the human condition as a *creature* (limited knowledge, limited perception) rather than to the divine image as such. The human capacity for inadvertent sin is the negative imprint of the creature's finitude, not a reflection of the divine image directly.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The structural silence on God confessing (T0.1.2) reveals that confession belongs to the *fallen creature's response to God* rather than to the original divine image. It is not a capacity the creature exercises because it shares it with God, but a capacity the creature requires because it has sinned against God. The corpus does evidence that confession is *divinely designed for* — God hears it (Neh 1:6 — Nehemiah prays "day and night" and God's ear is invoked as "attentive"), provides for it (Eze 33:16 — sins cancelled upon turning), and responds to it (1Sa 7:6 — corporate confession at Mizpah precedes Samuel's judgeship and renewal). The silence on divine confession and the presence of divine response together suggest that confession expresses the image not by sharing a divine quality but by enabling the image-bearer's restoration toward the God in whose image they were made.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The silence on God possessing conscience suppression (T0.1.2) indicates that the characteristic belongs entirely to the fallen creature rather than to any dimension of the divine image. Conscience itself is an image-bearing faculty — the moral witness within the person that reflects God's own moral awareness. Conscience suppression is the malfunction of that image-bearing faculty — the image's moral-witness capacity turned off or refused. The silence on God's possession confirms: the image includes conscience; conscience suppression is what happens to the image when it is damaged by sin and sustained rebellion. The characteristic is not a reflection of the divine image but its negation.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The absence of any divine refusal-to-repent in the corpus (T0.1.2) combined with the evidence of God's persistent moral invitation (Neh 9:29 — warnings given to turn them back) reveals that refusal to repent belongs entirely to the fallen creaturely condition. The divine image includes the *capacity* to turn (genuine moral agency, genuine will) but the fallen creature's use of that capacity in the direction of refusal is not a divine image expression but its negation. The silence confirms: the image provides the capacity for moral turning, and refusal to repent is what the will does with that capacity when it is captured by sin.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The silence on God possessing habitual defection (T0.1.2) combined with 1Jo 3:6's positive statement — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — reveals that habitual defection is definitionally opposed to the condition of abiding in Christ (who images God perfectly). Abiding in him and keeping on sinning are mutually exclusive (1Jo 3:6). Since God's character is perfectly expressed in the one who abides in him — "no one who abides in him keeps on sinning" — the silence confirms that habitual defection has no place in the divine nature. The characteristic is the negative inverse of the abiding relationship: where abiding produces freedom from habitual sinning, habitual defection is the condition of those who have "never seen him or known him" (1Jo 3:6).

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The silence on God possessing contagious sin combined with his direct indictment of leaders who practise it (1Ki 16:2; 21:22) reveals that the characteristic represents an abuse of the God-given authority structure. Leaders are given authority so that their inner moral direction shapes the community toward God; contagious sin is what happens when that authority structure operates in the opposite direction — transmitting sin rather than righteousness. The silence confirms that the characteristic is the *perversion* of the image's authority-bearing capacity, not an expression of it. God-given authority is designed for moral formation toward God; contagious sin corrupts it into moral formation away from God.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — The silence on God revolting combined with 2Ch 21:10's causal link (God forsaken → revolts occur) reveals that political revolt is the characteristic that emerges when the God-grounded authority structure is abandoned. God himself does not revolt because he is the source and legitimising ground of all authority — revolt against God is the primordial rebellion that the pa.sha register of the H6586 root names in other M10 contexts. The political-revolt register of pa.sha in this corpus applies the same root to the human-political level: just as theological revolt breaks the God-human covenantal relationship, political revolt breaks the authority-subject governing relationship. The silence on divine political revolt confirms: God is on the receiving end of revolt (covenantal-theological), not its practitioner.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — The silence on God blaspheming reveals two things. (1) God possesses speech — he speaks, names, declares, and pronounces — but his speech is always aligned with his own nature and truth; the creature's sinful speech is the perversion of the speech-capacity God gave. (2) Even the highest creaturely agents — angels — restrain blasphemous speech: Jude 9 — "even the archangel Michael... did not presume to pronounce a blasphemous judgment" and 2Pe 2:11 — "angels, though greater in might and power, do not pronounce a blasphemous judgment." If angelic speech is constrained from blasphemy, God's speech is infinitely more so. The silence confirms: sinful speech is the perversion of the image's speech-capacity, a capacity God gave and cannot himself misuse.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The silence confirms the nature of each mechanism as a perversion of a God-given inner capacity. (1) *Desire*: God gave the creature desire as a designed faculty; enticement exploits what God gave. (2) *Social identity*: God gave the creature communal identity; hypocrisy falsifies what God designed for authentic community. (3) *Covenantal identity*: God gave Israel its covenantal identity through Moses; apostasy abandons what God established. The silence on God possessing these mechanisms confirms: each mechanism is the fallen-condition corruption of a created capacity that God gave and does not himself corrupt.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The silence on God possessing sin as universal condition is absolute and explicit — reinforced positively by the sinlessness of Christ (Heb 4:15; 2Cor 5:21; Joh 8:46) and the requirement for propitiation and purification that implies God's own holiness is not under sin's dominion (Heb 1:3; 2:17). The silence suggests that sin as universal condition is precisely the condition that separates the creature from the Creator: it is the defining gap between the human being as made and the God in whose image the human was made. Gen 4:7 — sin "crouching at the door" — presupposes a moral agency that can resist (unlike God who does not face such a force); the addressability of the command ("you must rule over it") presupposes a creaturely confrontation with sin that is categorically absent from the divine nature.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The silence on God possessing sin's enslaving power is absolute and structurally significant: the characteristic presupposes a *master-slave relationship* in which sin is the master and the creature is the slave (Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin"). God cannot be enslaved — God is sovereign; and God does not enslave through sin — his purpose is liberation (1Jo 3:9; 1Jo 5:18). The silence reveals that the enslaving-power characteristic is constitutively the anti-God power structure: sin enslaves by claiming the governing role that belongs to God. 1Jo 3:8 — "the devil has been sinning from the beginning" — names the adversarial spiritual ground of the enslaving power; it is not God's power but the devil's originary sinning that grounds the enslaving structure.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — God does not carry a sin-record before anyone else. He is the moral record-keeper, the one before whom the human sin-record exists. The silence on God possessing the record-as-burden reveals: (1) the characteristic is constitutively relational — it is the condition of a creature standing before a perfectly righteous Creator whose memory is complete; (2) Isa 43:24 — "you have burdened me with your sins" — God is burdened by human sin but as the one receiving the offense, not as the one who has an offense-record of his own. The asymmetry is structural: God keeps the record; the creature is the subject of it.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — God never seeks forgiveness — he has no offense-record requiring another's pardon. The silence confirms the asymmetry at the characteristic's core: the seeking is constitutively creaturely (the fallen creature's movement toward God), while the granting is constitutively divine. This asymmetry reveals that the characteristic cannot be abstracted from the God-creature relationship: it names a specific direction of moral movement (creature → God in seeking; God → creature in granting) that requires both parties to be what they are.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — The silence on God possessing the generational sin condition confirms its entirely fallen-creaturely nature: generational sin is the moral weight that accumulates through creaturely failure across generations. God cannot inherit sin because he has no predecessors whose sinning he would receive. Isa 43:27's structure — "your first father sinned" — presupposes a beginning-point of sinning in the first human ancestor, which God by definition does not share. The silence reveals that generational sin is the specific form of moral inheritance that belongs to finite creatures embedded in lineages, families, and nations — not to the eternal God who stands outside such transmission chains.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The silence on God possessing the sinner-identity is absolute and defining: Heb 7:26 — "holy, innocent, unstained, separated from sinners, and exalted above the heavens" — Christ's high-priestly character is defined precisely by his total separation from and non-participation in the sinner-identity. The silence reveals that the sinner-as-moral-character is constitutively the anti-image: it is the condition that arises when the image-bearer's moral character becomes defined by what God is not (sinless, holy, righteous). The characteristic exists only in the contrast with God's character.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The silence confirms that guilt-as-inner-state is constitutively creaturely: it is the conscience's recognition of moral liability *before* God — a relational inner state that presupposes both an offender (the person) and the one before whom the offense exists (God). God cannot be guilty before another, having no superior moral authority. Ezr 9:15 — "we are before you in our guilt, for none can stand before you because of this" — guilt is the condition of standing before God with an unresolved moral debt; God stands in no such relational position before another. The silence is structurally necessary.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — The silence confirms iniquity-as-accumulated-crime is constitutively creaturely: it is the human moral account of wrongdoing accumulated before the one who measures and judges it. Isa 43:24 — "you have wearied me with your iniquities" — positions God as the one upon whom iniquity's weight falls relationally; he is not the one who carries it as personal crime but the one who absorbs its relational burden. The silence is structurally necessary: God cannot commit moral crimes against himself or a higher authority, nor can he accumulate iniquity within himself. The very act of God pardoning iniquity (Mic 7:18) presupposes his total absence of it.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — The silence is structurally necessary: transgression requires a higher boundary to cross, and God stands as the boundary-setter. Rom 4:15 — "where there is no law there is no transgression" — God is the law-giver whose command makes transgression possible; God cannot transgress his own commands. The silence reveals that transgression is constitutively creaturely — it can only exist where a creature stands under divine authority and chooses to cross the known line. God's absolute holiness (Exo 23:21 — "my name is in him" as ground for no pardon) is what makes the crossing possible as transgression.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — The silence is definitionally necessary: faithlessness requires a covenant bond whose violation constitutes the betrayal. God is the faithful party (the covenant-initiator) whose faithfulness is the very standard that makes human faithlessness intelligible as betrayal. Mal 2:10 — "Have we not all one Father? Has not one God created us? Why then are we faithless to one another?" — the appeal to God as common Father is the reason faithlessness toward one another is wrong; God's faithfulness is the constitutive ground that makes human faithlessness a moral failure. God cannot betray himself.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The silence (apart from Lam 3:9's judicial use) confirms that moral perversity is constitutively creaturely. God's nature is the straight standard against which perversion is defined: Pro 8:13 — wisdom hates perverted speech; Deu 16:19 — God's justice is what bribery subverts. The characteristic requires a prior moral order (which God embodies) that can be twisted; God is the source of the moral order, not subject to its distortion. Isa 29:16's "you turn things upside down" implies a right-side-up that God's own nature constitutes.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The corpus is not silent — it is emphatically declarative. God's freedom from injustice is the defining standard against which all human injustice is measured. Deu 32:4's "without iniquity, just and upright is he" establishes the divine character as the ontological baseline for justice; Eze 18's detailed Ezekiel-chapters ground individual moral accountability in God's own justice. The repeated explicit denials confirm: injustice requires a moral order to violate, and God is that moral order's author and embodiment — he cannot violate what he constitutes.

## T0.2 — Created Purpose

### T0.2.1

**Question:** What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-1]** S — The verse corpus offers no evidence that wilful sinning serves a positive created purpose. No verse in the 98-verse corpus describes sinning as enabling, equipping, or fulfilling the person. On the contrary, the evidence uniformly presents sinning as producing rupture (national defeat, drought, exile — 1Ki 8:33/35), death (Eze 18:4/20), and damage to the one sinned against (Gen 20:9 — "you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin"). The evidence is silent on any positive created purpose for wilful sinning — which analytically confirms that it belongs to the fallen condition, not to a positive dimension of the created design.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — The verse evidence does not suggest that unintentional sinning serves a positive created purpose. Like CHAR-1, it is not something the created design equipped the person to express; rather, the prescriptions for addressing it (Lev 4:2; Num 15:27/28) are remedial responses to a condition that requires correction. The cultic-purification corpus (VCG-02: Exo 29:36; Eze 43:20/22/45:18) similarly addresses contamination of sacred spaces, not a positive created function. The evidence is uniformly remedial — unintentional sinning creates a condition requiring resolution, not a capacity serving flourishing.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences confession serving three distinct purposes. (1) *Relational restoration*: Luk 15:18/21 — the prodigal's planned and enacted confession is explicitly oriented toward restoring the father-son relationship: "I will arise and go to my father." 1Sa 7:6 — corporate confession at Mizpah marks the people's turning back toward God and precedes Samuel's renewal of the community. (2) *Removal of guilt's weight*: Dan 9:5 — Daniel's multi-layered confession ("we have sinned and done wrong and acted wickedly and rebelled") is a comprehensive naming of what has been done, providing the inner person with clarity about what they carry. 2Sa 24:10 — David's confession "please take away the iniquity of your servant" shows confession as the instrument of guilt-removal. (3) *Alignment of inner reality with moral truth*: Num 21:7 — "we have sinned, for we have spoken against the Lord and against you" — the confession names the inner rebellious attitude as the root of the wrongdoing. Confession equips the person to be a morally honest creature before God and to seek restored relationship.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that conscience suppression serves any positive created purpose. No verse presents the absence of moral self-awareness as an equipping or enabling capacity. The corpus presents conscience suppression uniformly as a moral deficit: Jer 2:35 (denial of sin brings judgment), Jer 16:10 (failure to recognise sin despite facing judgment), Ecc 8:12 (sinning without the fear of God — the absence of the right inner orientation). The characteristic serves no created purpose; it is a failure of the moral-witness faculty that belongs to the image.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(S)*

**[CHAR-5]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that refusal to repent serves any positive created purpose. The evidence uniformly presents it as a moral failure: 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord with all his heart" (failure of alignment); Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" (hardened resistance to God's word); Heb 10:26 — sinning deliberately after knowing the truth (closing off divine remedy). None of these evidence a created purpose; all evidence a failure of the created design.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(S)*

**[CHAR-6]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for habitual defection. The verses present it uniformly as a moral deficit with consequences: inescapable recompense (Pro 11:31), lost inheritance (Pro 13:22), destructive capacity (Ecc 9:18), extended divine provocation (Heb 3:17), and exclusion from knowledge of Christ (1Jo 3:6). The characteristic serves no positive created purpose; it is the settled will's direction toward sin, which the creation did not design.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-7]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that contagious sin serves any positive created purpose. Every verse presents it as a moral failure with severe consequences: God abandoning Israel (1Ki 14:16), destruction of royal houses (1Ki 21:22), driving Israel from following the Lord (2Ki 17:21), causing child sacrifice (Jer 32:35). The characteristic serves no positive created purpose; it is the corruption of the created authority structure.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(S)*

**[CHAR-8]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for political revolt. The 9 verses uniformly present it as a negative event (rebellion, revolt, defection from governing relationships). The "to this day" markers (1Ki 12:19; 2Ki 8:22; 2Ch 10:19; 2Ch 21:10) name persistent political fracture as the ongoing consequence. No verse presents revolt as serving the created person's designed function.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(S)*

**[CHAR-9]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for sinful speech. The 22 verses uniformly present it as originating in inner moral corruption (Mat 15:19), requiring renunciation (Eph 4:31; Col 3:8), flowing from diseased pride (1Ti 6:4), and constituting the beast's defiant identity (Rev 13:1/5/6). No verse presents sinful speech as serving any designed function.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(S)*

**[CHAR-10]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for enticement, collective hypocrisy, or apostasy. All three mechanisms are presented as destructive: enticement draws toward sin (Jam 1:14), collective hypocrisy corrupts community integrity (Gal 2:13), apostasy abandons covenantal identity (Act 21:21). No verse presents them as serving any designed function.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(S)*

**[CHAR-11]** S — The corpus provides no evidence that sin as universal condition serves any positive created purpose. The entire corpus presents it as the condition requiring salvation (VCG-02), the enslaving power requiring liberation (VCG-03), and the defining quality of the wicked requiring judgment (VCG-01). Rom 3:23 — "fall short of the glory of God" — frames sin as a deficiency from the creature's intended end, not a feature of the created design.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(S)*

**[CHAR-12]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for sin's enslaving power. The 9 verses present it as: enslavement requiring freedom (Joh 8:34), universal imprisonment requiring faith-promise (Gal 3:22), a condition that born-of-God persons are liberated from (1Jo 3:9; 5:18), and a danger that anger can lead to if unchecked (Eph 4:26). No verse presents the enslaving power as serving any designed function in the created person.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(S)*

**[CHAR-13]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for sin as divine record. The characteristic is entirely fallen-condition: it is the record of offenses against God that accumulates in the post-fall human condition. No verse presents the divine-sin-record as a designed feature of the created person; it presupposes the fall.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic serves the purpose of *restoring the God-human relationship ruptured by sin*. Lev 16:30 — "you shall be clean before the Lord from all your sins" — the purpose is restored purity before God; Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... God made alive" — the purpose is life where death held sway; Act 2:38 — repentance and baptism → forgiveness → receipt of the Holy Spirit — the purpose is access to the Spirit who was blocked by the sin-record. The characteristic thus serves the restoration of the person's full relational capacity before God.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-15]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for generational sin. The characteristic is presented entirely as a fallen-condition burden: Isa 64:5 — "in our sins we have been a long time" — the prolonged state of sin is experienced as a problem requiring divine resolution, not as a serving-a-purpose feature. Eze 37:23's divine redemption-from-backsliding confirms the condition has no created-design function.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(S)*

**[CHAR-16]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of any positive created purpose for the sinner-as-moral-character identity. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition category: Psa 1:5 — excluded from the congregation of the righteous; Isa 33:14 — seized with dread before God's holiness. No verse presents the sinner-identity as a designed feature.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The characteristic serves the purpose of *conscience-activation toward remedial action*. Lev 5:5 — "when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he has committed" — guilt-recognition triggers confession; Lev 6:4 — "if he has sinned and has realized his guilt and will restore what he took" — guilt-realization drives restitution; Num 5:7 — "he shall confess his sin that he has committed. And he shall make full restitution" — guilt produces the double movement of confession and restitution. The characteristic thus equips the person to recognise moral failure and initiate repair of what the failure broke.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(S)*

**[CHAR-18]** S — The corpus provides no evidence of a positive created purpose for iniquity-as-accumulated-crime. The characteristic is entirely a fallen-condition category: the accumulation of moral wrongdoing before God. No verse presents iniquity-as-crime as a designed feature of the human person. Its nearest functional dimension is its measurability (Gen 15:16 — "not yet complete"), which implies a divinely calibrated accounting system, but this accounting system responds to wrongdoing rather than being its purpose.

(1) *Judgment endpoint*: 2Ch 30:7 — "he made them a desolation" — sustained faithlessness reaches its judgment-endpoint; Eze 39:23 — God hid his face and delivered to adversaries; 1Ch 10:13 — "Saul died for his breach of faith" — the individual death-verdict; (2) *Resolution and forgetting*: Eze 39:26 — "they shall forget their shame and all the treachery they have practiced against me, when they dwell securely" — the eschatological fullness is the dwelling-secure state in which the treachery has been fully resolved and its shame eclipsed; Lev 26:40-42 — "if they confess their iniquity and the iniquity of their fathers in their treachery... I will remember my covenant" — the covenant-memory restoration is the positive eschatological orientation.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — in two directions. (1) *Judgment endpoint*: Rev 19:2 — "he has judged the great prostitute who corrupted the earth" — eschatological judgment falls on the corrupting agent; Pro 10:31 — "the perverse tongue will be cut off" — judgment silences perversity; (2) *Liberation from corruption*: Rom 8:21 — "the creation itself will be set free from its bondage to corruption and obtain the freedom of the glory of the children of God" — the eschatological fullness is freedom from corruption as a cosmic reality; 2Pe 1:4 — "having escaped from the corruption that is in the world because of sinful desire" — the believer's orientation is toward escape from corruption into divine nature.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — two eschatological orientations. (1) *Judgment of the unjust*: 2Pe 2:9 — "the Lord knows how to... keep the unrighteous under punishment until the day of judgment" — the unrighteous's current condition anticipates the definitive judgment; Rev 18:5 — "God has remembered her iniquities" — the accumulated record awaits final reckoning; (2) *Eschatological elimination of injustice*: Zep 3:13 — "those who are left in Israel... shall do no injustice and speak no lies" — the purified remnant's future is characterised by injustice's total absence; the eschatological fullness is the restored right-conduct community.

## T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

### T0.3.1

**Question:** In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — Wilful sinning does not express the divine image positively, but it does presuppose the image's most distinctive feature: genuine moral agency. The corpus consistently treats sinning as a personal, accountable, wilful act. Eze 18:4 declares "the soul who sins shall die" — with no mitigation for ignorance or compulsion in the wilful register. Eze 18:20 underscores personal ownership: "the soul who sins alone shall die; the son shall not suffer for the iniquity of the father." Gen 43:9 (Judah) and Gen 44:32 both show a human person voluntarily assuming moral accountability for another — an act only possible because genuine moral agency exists. The capacity for wilful sinning thus presupposes the image-dimension of moral agency: the person who can wilfully sin is the same person who can wilfully choose righteousness. The image provides the capacity; sinning is its misuse.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — Unintentional sinning does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes two image-related features. (1) *Moral creaturely accountability*: Lev 4:2 ("if anyone sins unintentionally... and does any one of them") establishes that even inadvertent violation creates real moral liability — the person bears responsibility regardless of intent. This moral accountability presupposes the image-dimension of being a morally serious creature before God. (2) *Conscience-engagement post-awareness*: Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he has committed") shows conscience activating when the person comes to know what they did. The conscience that fires upon realisation is an image-bearing feature: it is the moral witness that marks the person as accountable to God. Unintentional sinning thus reveals the image's moral-seriousness feature by showing that even its inadvertent violation carries weight.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Confession expresses the divine image through the capacity for *moral self-knowledge and honest self-naming*. The image-bearer who confesses demonstrates: (1) *Moral cognition*: the ability to assess their own act against a moral standard and name it correctly as sin (2Sa 24:10 — "I have sinned greatly"; Dan 9:5 — the full cascade of named failures). (2) *Relational accountability*: the capacity to stand before God and speak truthfully about what was done — "Against you, you only, have I sinned" (Psa 51:4, the paradigmatic confession-verse contextually). (3) *Moral integrity of the will*: the willingness to own what was done rather than deny or displace it (1Ch 21:17 — "It is I who have sinned... do not let the plague be on your people"). The image is expressed not because God confesses but because God's image-bearer is made for moral transparency — honesty about the self before God.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — Conscience suppression does not express the divine image positively; it reveals the image's degraded condition. However, even within the suppressed state, the image is not wholly absent. Tit 3:11 — "he is self-condemned" — the self-condemnation is the conscience's witness operating implicitly against the person, even though not acknowledged. The image-bearing conscience is still present, still functioning as a moral witness — but its witness is suppressed and refused rather than voiced and acted on. The characteristic thus reveals the image's *persistence under suppression*: the moral faculty is still active (hence self-condemnation is possible) but its output is refused (hence the suppression). The image is present but its proper operation is blocked.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Refusal to repent does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes the image's central feature: genuine moral agency — a will that can choose. The corpus's language is uniformly volitional: "he did not depart," "he clung to," "they would not obey" (Neh 9:29), "we go on sinning deliberately" (Heb 10:26). These formulations presuppose that departure was available and choosing not to depart is a genuine exercise of will. The image's volitional capacity — the freedom to turn or not turn — is what makes refusal to repent possible. The characteristic perverts the image's most distinctive feature (moral freedom to turn toward God) by using it to remain away from him.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Habitual defection does not express the divine image positively, but it presupposes the image's core features. The universality statements — 1Ki 8:46 ("no one who does not sin") and Ecc 7:20 ("not a righteous man on earth who does good and never sins") — apply to all image-bearers: the universal scope means the characteristic is found across all humans precisely because all are moral creatures with genuine wills that can be settled in the sinning direction. The capacity for the will to establish a settled pattern (whether toward God or away from him) is an image-bearing feature. Habitual defection uses the image's volitional-habituation capacity in the direction away from God.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Contagious sin does not express the divine image positively but perverts one of its core capacities: the image-bearer's designed capacity to shape and form others through moral authority. 1Ki 16:2 — "I exalted you out of the dust and made you leader over my people Israel" — God explicitly names the elevation to leadership as his own action, confirming that leadership authority is image-related (the image-bearer is created to bear authority and responsibility within God's creation). Contagious sin is what happens when this image-capacity is captured by sin: instead of the leader's inner commitment to God shaping the community toward God, the leader's inner commitment to sin shapes the community toward sin.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt does not express the divine image positively but perverts one of the image's core capacities: the created person's capacity for covenantal relationship (including political authority structures) and for loyalty within those relationships. The image-bearer is designed for relationships of ordered loyalty and covenant fidelity; political revolt is the decisive breaking of that loyalty. 2Ch 21:10's causal chain — Jehoram forsaking the Lord → Libnah revolting — shows the image's capacity for loyalty operating in inversion: when the image-bearer breaks loyalty to God, the political-relational structures dependent on that loyalty fragment.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Sinful speech perverts one of the image's most central capacities: the gift of speech. The image-bearer is a speaking creature — created to name (Gen 2:19–20, contextually), to declare, to praise, and to communicate moral truth. Mat 15:19 — "out of the heart come... slander" — the same heart-to-mouth channel designed for God-honouring speech is the channel through which slander flows. The image's speech-capacity is constitutively good; sinful speech is its inversion. Jude 9 and 2Pe 2:11 confirm that even the speech of angelic creatures is constrained from blasphemy — establishing that sinful speech is not merely a human failure but a creaturely violation of what speech is for.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Each mechanism perverts a distinct image-bearing capacity. (1) *Enticement perverts the image's desire-faculty*: Jam 1:14 — desire (*epithumia*) is a God-given motivational capacity of the image-bearer; enticement uses this capacity as the hook for temptation. The image's motivational capacity is the mechanism through which sin captures the person. (2) *Collective hypocrisy perverts the image's social-identity capacity*: Gal 2:13 — the image-bearer is designed for authentic communal identity; sunupokrinomai uses the capacity for social belonging to falsify identity through conformity. (3) *Apostasy perverts the image's covenantal-identity capacity*: Act 21:21 — the image-bearer is designed for covenant relationship with God; apostasy uses the capacity for identity-formation to abandon the covenantal framework.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Sin as universal condition does not express the divine image positively but reveals its fallen state. Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — the "glory of God" that human beings fall short of is the standard of what the image should reflect; the universal falling-short reveals the image in its failed state. The characteristic is the negative diagnostic of the image's condition: the image was designed to reflect God's glory (the positive); sin as universal condition is the universal failure to do so. Gen 4:7 — sin crouching at the door, desiring to master — shows the image-bearer (Cain) facing a force that seeks to capture the image's ruling capacity; the image-bearer was created to rule (Gen 1:28) but sin seeks to reverse that rule by mastering the image-bearer.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Sin as enslaving power does not express the divine image positively but perverts the image's designed governance capacity. The image-bearer is created to govern (the broader Genesis mandate), but under sin's enslaving power, what should be the image's governing self becomes a slave to what it should govern. Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin" — the practice-to-slavery mechanism inverts the image's design: the image-bearer was designed to exercise governing authority (ruling the creation), but habitual sinning reverses this — the image-bearer is ruled by sin rather than exercising rule over it (cf. Gen 4:7 — "you must rule over it" — the contrast to the enslavement state).

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The divine image's moral accountability before God is the constitutive dimension of CHAR-13: only an image-bearer who stands in moral relationship with God can have a sin-record before God. Animals do not carry a divine sin-record. Psa 51:5 — "in sin did my mother conceive me" — the conceived state is that of an image-bearer accountable to God. The image-bearer's creation for moral relationship with God is what makes the divine-record characteristic possible: the record exists because the person stands before God as a morally accountable subject.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The characteristic expresses the divine image through the image-bearer's capacity for *repentant return to God*: only a being made for God-relationship can seek forgiveness from God. Exo 32:30 — Moses "will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin" — the intercessory seeking is the image-bearer exercising the mediatorial capacity that belongs to the image's vocation. Act 2:38 — the capacity to repent, be baptised, and receive the Holy Spirit names the image-bearer's full moral-and-relational restoration capacity, which only makes sense for a being whose image-bearing orientation is toward God.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Generational sin reveals the divine image through its *transmission capacity*: only image-bearers who stand in lineages and covenant communities can transmit a morally weighted sinning-history to their descendants. The image was designed for community and continuity (the creation mandate involves generations — "be fruitful and multiply"); generational sin is the fallen-condition distortion of this design: what should transmit blessing, calling, and covenant transmits instead the sin-pattern and its moral weight. Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — presupposes the image-bearer's capacity for a sinning act that can become the starting-point of a generational chain.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The sinner-as-moral-character expresses the divine image through its *inverted moral characterisation*: the image-bearer is designed for a constituted moral identity oriented toward God; the sinner-identity is that constituted moral identity oriented away from God and toward sin. Luk 5:8 — "I am a sinful man, O Lord" — Peter's recognition of his sinner-identity in the presence of the Holy One is the clearest image-expression: only a being made for God-relationship can stand in the relational field of that holiness and recognise itself as a "sinful man." The capacity for moral self-identification (whether as sinner or as righteous) belongs to the image.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Guilt-as-inner-state expresses the image through the image-bearer's *moral accountability before God*: only a being made in God's image stands before God in a morally accountable relationship. Ezr 9:15 — "we are before you in our guilt, for none can stand before you because of this" — the guilt is experienced as a barrier to standing before God; only a being designed to stand before God (the image-bearer) experiences this specific form of conscience-burden. Gen 42:21 — the brothers' conscience connects past cruelty to present suffering, demonstrating the moral-causal awareness that belongs to the image-bearing conscience.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Iniquity-as-accumulated-crime expresses the image through its *inverted moral accounting*: the image-bearer is constituted as a moral being whose acts are weighed and measured before God (Gen 15:16 — iniquity measurable; Hos 13:12 — bound up in divine record). Only a being made in God's image can accumulate a moral account before him — only the image-bearer stands in the morally accountable relationship that makes iniquity possible. Psa 90:8 — "you have set our iniquities before you" — the "before you" is the relational positioning of the image-bearer before God; the capacity to accumulate iniquity in God's sight belongs to the image's constituted God-relationship.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Transgression expresses the image through its *inverted exercise of the will*: the image-bearer is constituted with a will capable of obedience to God's known commands — only a being made in God's image can knowingly receive a command and choose to cross it. Rom 5:14 — Adam's transgression is the paradigmatic knowing-violation; Rom 4:15 — transgression requires prior knowledge of the law. The capacity to know the boundary and choose to cross it belongs to the image's rational-moral constitution. Isa 1:2 — "children have I reared and brought up, but they have rebelled against me" — the parental image reveals that the capacity for known defiance is what makes the rebellion a moral reality rather than mere animal deviation.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Faithlessness expresses the image through its *inverted relational capacity*: the image-bearer is constituted as a covenant-capable being — one who can make, sustain, and break binding relational commitments. Only a being made in God's image can enter a covenant with God (as Israel did) and then betray it as a moral act. Mal 2:14 — "the Lord was witness between you and the wife of your youth, to whom you have been faithless, though she is your companion and your wife by covenant" — the covenant-witnessing capacity and the covenant-betrayal capacity belong to the same image-bearing creature. The capacity for faithfulness (the image's positive dimension) is the prerequisite for faithlessness (its fallen expression).

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Perversion expresses the image through its *inverted exercise of the moral faculty*: the image-bearer is constituted with a morally ordered inner life — a mind, will, heart, and speech designed for alignment with God's order. Perversion is the twisting of these faculties against their designed grain. Isa 29:16 — the creature denying the potter's authority — is the most direct image-inversion: the image-bearer's cognitive faculty, designed to know God as creator, is so perverted it denies the creator relationship. The capacity for moral ordering (the image's positive dimension) is precisely what perversion distorts.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Injustice expresses the image through its *inverted exercise of the moral-conduct faculty*: the image-bearer is constituted with the capacity for just conduct — to judge rightly, act fairly, and treat persons according to their worth (the M26 righteousness the cross-register flag tracks). Injustice is this same capacity deployed against its designed purpose. Psa 58:2 — "in your hearts you devise wrongs" — the heart's deliberating capacity (a mark of the image's rational design) is deployed toward unjust schemes. The very faculty of moral reasoning that enables justice also enables injustice.

### T0.3.2

**Question:** Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence establishes wilful sinning as exclusively creaturely with no divine analogue. Every instance of sinning in the corpus is human or creaturely: human persons sin against God (VCG-01), against other persons (VCG-02), or even angels sin (2Pe 2:4 — "God did not spare angels when they sinned"). God appears uniformly as the offended party, the restrainer, or the judge — never as a sinning subject. The closest the corpus comes to a divine-side parallel is Gen 20:6, where God actively restrains Abimelech from sinning — positioning God as the external check on creaturely sinning. There is no divine analogue to wilful sinning evidenced in this corpus.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(S)*

**[CHAR-2]** S — Unintentional sinning is exclusively creaturely (see T0.1.2 and T0.1.3). The corpus contains no divine analogue and the concept is constitutively inapplicable to God. This characteristic has no shared element with the divine nature — it is grounded entirely in creaturely epistemic limitation and fallen moral liability.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(S)*

**[CHAR-3]** S — Confession is exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). No divine analogue is evidenced. The characteristic has no shared dimension with the divine nature — it is grounded entirely in the creature's sinfulness and the relational structure of the creature's accountability before God.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — Exclusively creaturely (see T0.1.2). God cannot suppress awareness of moral reality; the characteristic is structurally impossible for God. The absence of any divine analogue is complete.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(S)*

**[CHAR-5]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 and T0.1.3). God does not sin and therefore has no turning to withhold. The characteristic is structurally inapplicable to God.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(S)*

**[CHAR-6]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The corpus contains no divine analogue, and 1Jo 3:6 makes the structural incompatibility explicit: abiding in God is the condition that prevents habitual sinning. The characteristic is inapplicable to God.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-7]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God is the one against whom contagious sin is directed and who holds the sinning leaders accountable. No divine analogue exists; the characteristic is the corruption of God-given authority, not an expression of God's own character.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(S)*

**[CHAR-8]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The characteristic requires a subordinate party breaking submission to a governing authority — a relationship God does not occupy as a subordinate.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(S)*

**[CHAR-9]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). God cannot blaspheme because blasphemy requires either false claims about God (which God cannot make about himself) or speech originating in inner moral corruption (which God does not possess).

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(S)*

**[CHAR-10]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). None of the three mechanisms applies to God.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Exclusively creaturely — and universally so among creatures. Rom 3:23 — "all have sinned" — the "all" encompasses every human being without exception. The contrast with Christ (Heb 4:15 — "without sin"; 2Cor 5:21 — "knew no sin") confirms that the one fully divine-human person did not share the universal condition; his sinlessness is what qualified him to bear what others could not bear (2Cor 5:21 — "made him to be sin who knew no sin").

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(S)*

**[CHAR-12]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God cannot be enslaved by sin; his nature is sovereign and holy. The enslaving power operates on created persons (Joh 8:34 — "everyone who practices sin"; Gal 3:22 — "everything" imprisoned under sin). The characteristic is inapplicable to the divine nature.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Exclusively creaturely. God is the keeper of the record, not its subject. Exo 32:33 — "whoever has sinned against me, I will blot out of my book" — God is the record-keeper; the humans are the entries. Amo 5:12 — "for I know how many are your transgressions" — God knows the record; he does not have one. The asymmetry is absolute: God's perfect moral record is the standard against which the human's sin-record is measured (Rom 3:23 in CHAR-11 — falling short of the glory of God).

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The seeking dimension is exclusively creaturely; the granting dimension is divine. The characteristic is thus relationally shared: God grants (2Cor 5:19; Col 2:13; Eph 1:7) and the creature seeks (Exo 32:30 — Moses; Act 2:38 — repentance; Act 10:43 — belief). The characteristic requires both poles: without the creature's seeking it is not enacted; without God's granting it is not completed. It is the most explicitly bi-directional characteristic in M10.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-15]** S — Exclusively creaturely. God has no ancestors whose sinning he could inherit; the transmission mechanism (lineage, family, national belonging) presupposes finite creatures embedded in historical succession. The characteristic is inapplicable to the divine nature.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Exclusively creaturely. Heb 7:26 — Christ is "separated from sinners" — the sinner-identity is constitutively absent from Christ and therefore from God. Rom 5:19 — "the many were made sinners" through Adam's disobedience — the making-sinners applies to humanity in Adam, not to God. The sinner-identity is the fallen creature's constituted moral character, not shared with the divine nature.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(S)*

**[CHAR-17]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 — God does not bear guilt as a subjective inner state). The characteristic requires the position of moral debtor before a higher moral authority, which God does not occupy.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(S)*

**[CHAR-18]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2 — God does not bear iniquity as personal moral crime). The characteristic requires the position of moral offender accumulating crime before a higher moral authority; God occupies no such position.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(S)*

**[CHAR-19]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). The characteristic requires a known boundary to cross and a higher authority to transgress against; God occupies no such position. The characteristic is constitutively relational in a downward direction (the creature under the Creator).

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(S)*

**[CHAR-20]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). God is the faithful standard; faithlessness is the creature's betrayal of the bond God constituted. The characteristic has no divine analogue.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(S)*

**[CHAR-21]** S — Exclusively creaturely in the moral sense. Lam 3:9's judicial use (God making paths crooked) is not God being morally perverted but God acting as judge. The characteristic in its inner-moral sense is exclusively creaturely.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(S)*

**[CHAR-22]** S — Exclusively creaturely (T0.1.2). God is not only free from injustice but constitutes its standard-opposite. The characteristic has no divine analogue.

### T0.3.3

**Question:** What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The presence of wilful sinning reveals a will that has turned against its Maker — a damaged but not destroyed divine image. The evidence treats sinning as a real inner act carrying personal moral consequence (Eze 18:4/20), not as something that annihilates the person's moral agency or their status as an image-bearer. Eze 3:21 presupposes that a sinning person can still be warned and can still choose not to sin — moral agency persists. 1Jo 1:10 ("if we say we have not sinned, we make him a liar") treats sinning as a universal creaturely condition whose honest acknowledgment itself requires a functioning conscience. The pattern suggests: the divine image remains (the will is present, moral agency persists), but wilful sinning represents that image operating in defection from its designed orientation toward God.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The presence of unintentional sinning in a person reveals that they are a morally accountable creature operating within the bounds of limited knowledge — the image is present (they are accountable before God) but the creature is finite (they can act without full moral knowledge). The *response* to unintentional sinning is more revealing of image-condition than the sinning itself: Lev 5:5 ("when he realizes his guilt... and confesses") shows the image working correctly — conscience engaged, acknowledgment made, remedy sought. Num 22:34 (Balaam — "I have sinned... if it is evil in your sight, I will turn back") shows the will responding rightly to the realisation of inadvertent sin. The person who responds to unintentional sinning with confession and remedy demonstrates an image functioning in its conscience-and-accountability dimension. The person who does not — who suppresses the realisation — would be approaching CHAR-4 (Conscience suppression), not CHAR-2.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus provides evidence at both ends. (1) *Presence of genuine confession*: signals an image functioning with active moral conscience, honest self-knowledge, and relational accountability before God. David's conscience-struck confession (2Sa 24:10 — "David's heart struck him") before any external word of judgment represents the image at near-full functioning in this mode: conscience registers sin, will names it, mouth speaks it to God, plea for mercy follows. Luk 15:21 (the prodigal's enacted confession: "I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son") shows total self-knowledge and relational honesty. (2) *Hollow or externally-coerced confession* (VCG-03: Deu 1:41; VCG-01: Exo 9:27/10:16 — Pharaoh; 1Sa 15:30 — Saul): the verbal form is present but the will does not follow. These cases reveal an image that can produce the *form* of confession without the *substance* — the moral self-knowledge is present (they know what they did) but the will has not turned. The image is partially functioning: cognition and speech operate but the volitional transformation is absent.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(E)*

**[CHAR-4]** E — The presence of conscience suppression reveals the divine image in a state of active malfunction at its moral-witness faculty. Jer 2:35 — the person who says "I have not sinned" is not merely ignorant; they are actively claiming a false moral verdict — the image's moral-evaluation capacity is producing incorrect output and refusing correction. Ecc 8:12 — the sinner who "does evil a hundred times" without the fear of God reveals an image from which the God-orientation has been removed at the most fundamental level: "the absence of the fear of God within" (Pass A meaning) names a constitutional God-orientation gap. Jer 16:10 — the people's "what sin have we committed?" reveals an image whose capacity for self-moral-assessment has been so degraded that severe external judgment cannot trigger self-recognition. The image is present (the person is still a moral creature, still accountable) but its operational integrity is profoundly compromised.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — The presence of refusal to repent reveals the divine image in a condition of *persistent volitional capture*: the will that was designed to turn toward God is entrenched in a sinful direction and choosing not to reverse. 2Ki 10:31 — "not careful to walk in the law of the Lord... with all his heart" — the divided or entirely un-aligned heart is the image-condition. Neh 9:29 — "stubborn shoulder and stiffened their neck" — the image's bodily and volitional faculties are set in resistance rather than responsive turning. The image is present (the will exists, agency exists) but has been persistently directed away from God rather than toward him. The image's capacity for turning — and the sustained refusal to use it — is what makes refusal to repent a particularly revealing indicator of the image's compromised condition.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — The presence of habitual defection reveals the divine image in a condition of settled misdirection: the will's habit-forming capacity has been trained in the direction away from God rather than toward him. Hos 8:11 — "altars multiplied for sinning, they have become altars for sinning" — shows how habitual sinning becomes self-perpetuating: the image's capacity to institutionalise and form patterns has been given to sinful worship, creating structures that reinforce the misdirection. 1Jo 3:6 — "no one who keeps on sinning has either seen him or known him" — reveals that habitual defection indicates a fundamental absence of the saving knowledge of God that would redirect the image's volitional capacities. The image is present (habits are being formed, the will is active) but its directional orientation has been captured by the sinning pattern.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — The presence of contagious sin in a leader reveals the divine image in a condition of *inverted authority*: the image's authority-bearing capacity is operating in the opposite direction to its design. 1Ki 16:2 — "I exalted you... and you have walked in the way of Jeroboam and have made my people Israel to sin" — the contrast is stark: God elevated the person (image-bearing design) for their people's benefit; the person used the elevation to transmit sin (image-bearing capacity inverted). 2Ki 21:11 — Manasseh "committed abominations... more evil than all that the Amorites did... and has made Judah also to sin" — the image's capacity for moral formation of others has been deployed not merely neutrally but actively against the image's designed function. The more clearly the leader's authority is God-given (1Ki 16:2 — explicit elevation by God), the more severe the inverted use of that authority appears.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Political revolt reveals the divine image in a condition of broken covenantal loyalty. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day" — the ongoing rebellion names a condition in which the image-bearing community refuses the authority structure that God established. 2Ch 21:10's causal chain reveals the deepest dimension: the image-condition that produces political revolt is first a condition of having forsaken the Lord — the vertical loyalty broken before the horizontal political loyalty fractures. Political revolt is thus a surface manifestation of a deeper image-condition of broken loyalty, both toward God and toward his established order.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — The presence of sinful speech reveals the image's condition across three levels. (1) *VCG-01 (slander from inner corruption)*: Mat 15:19 — "out of the heart come evil thoughts... slander" — sinful speech from inner corruption reveals the heart's own moral condition; the speech is a diagnostic of the heart's state. 1Ti 6:4 — slander arising from "conceit and unhealthy craving" — the image's inner moral centre is diseased. (2) *VCG-02 (divine-authority usurpation)*: when sinful speech claims divine authority (Mar 2:7; Joh 10:33), the image has inverted its relationship to God — instead of the image reflecting God, the creature is claiming to be God. (3) *VCG-03 (constitutive defiance — beast)*: Rev 13:1 — "blasphemous names on its heads" — the most extreme image-condition: the entire character constituted by defiance of God rather than by reflection of him.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — The presence of each mechanism reveals a distinct image-condition. (1) *Enticement through desire*: 2Pe 2:14 — "hearts trained in greed" — the image's desire-faculty has been formed away from God and toward sinful appetite through deliberate training; the image-bearing capacity for motivation is captured by wrong objects. (2) *Collective hypocrisy*: Gal 2:13 — "even Barnabas was led astray" — the image's social-identity capacity has been distorted to prioritise social conformity over covenantal authenticity; the image's communal dimension is operating through performance rather than integrity. (3) *Apostasy*: Act 21:21 — the image's covenantal-identity capacity is turned against the covenantal framework itself — the person who abandons the covenant has turned the identity-forming capacity against the relationship it was designed to sustain.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — The universal presence of sin as condition reveals that the divine image is comprehensively fallen: Rom 3:9 — "all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin" — the "all" without exception names a total condition affecting every image-bearer without remainder. Rom 7:14 — "sold under sin" — the slave-market metaphor reveals the image's condition as ownership-by-another: the image is held under a master it was not designed to serve. Eph 2:1 — "dead in trespasses and sins" — the image in sin-conditioned humanity is not merely damaged but spiritually dead; the image remains (humanity still exists) but its life-capacity before God has been extinguished. The universality of the condition — including the "righteous" (Rom 3:10 context — no one righteous) — means the image cannot correct itself from within; only the divine provision (VCG-02 — forgiveness; VCG-03 redemption) can address it.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — The presence of sin's enslaving power reveals the divine image in a condition of *reversed governance*: instead of the image-bearer governing rightly (the created design), the image-bearer is governed by sin as its master. Joh 8:34 — "a slave to sin" — the slave-to-sin condition names the image in its most degraded constitutional form: the governance capacity has been captured. 1Jo 3:8 — "whoever makes a practice of sinning is of the devil" — the deepest revealing consequence: the image-bearer under sin's enslaving power has an allegiance-marker that declares its belonging not to God but to the devil. The image is present (the person still exists and wills) but its allegiance-orientation and governing-structure have been wholly captured.

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — The presence of a sin-record reveals the image in its primary condition of fallen moral accountability. Jer 17:1 — "the sin of Judah is written with a pen of iron; with a point of diamond it is engraved on the tablet of their heart" (VCG-02) — the record is not external only but engraved internally on the heart; the image-bearer carries the record within as well as before God. Isa 59:2 — "your iniquities have made a separation between you and your God, and your sins have hidden his face from you" — the divine record is the relational barrier; the image designed for God-relationship now stands behind a wall of recorded sin. The depth of the record's internal engraving (Jer 17:1 — iron pen, diamond point) reveals how thoroughly the fallen condition has shaped the image from within.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — The presence of the seeking dimension reveals the image in its *fallen-but-recoverable* condition: the image bears the sin-record (CHAR-13) that requires forgiveness, is capable of recognising this (conscience), and has the volitional capacity to turn toward God in seeking (Act 2:38 — "repent"). Col 2:13 — "dead in your trespasses... God made alive" — the image's condition before forgiveness is spiritual death; the forgiveness-received transforms the condition to life. The characteristic's presence thus reveals both the depth of the image's fallen condition (requiring forgiveness) and the fullness of its recovery capacity (able to seek, receive, and be made alive).

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — The presence of generational sin reveals that the image-bearer's moral condition is not individually self-contained but socially and historically embedded: Isa 43:27 — "your first father sinned" — the present image-bearer carries a moral liability whose origin precedes their individual existence. The image is thus not merely individually fallen (through personal sinning acts) but corporately and historically fallen through the accumulated sinning of predecessors. Psa 78:17/32 — the wilderness generation's persistent sinning despite wonders reveals an image whose sinning patterns are formed and reinforced across communal experience; the generational dimension deepens the image's fallen condition beyond what individual sinning alone would produce.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — The presence of the sinner-identity reveals the image in its *characterologically defined fallen state*: the image-bearer is not merely doing sinful acts (CHAR-1) or under a sin-condition (CHAR-11) but has become a recognisable moral type — "the sinner." Gen 13:13 — the men of Sodom "were wicked, great sinners against the Lord" — the image-bearer has been constituted as a recognisable moral category of wrongdoing. This is the image's deepest fallen characterological expression: the person has been formed into what they do — sinning has become who they are.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — The presence of guilt as inner-being state reveals the image in its *morally accountable fallen condition*: the conscience is functional enough to recognise guilt (Lev 4:13/22/27 — "realizes his guilt") but the recognition occurs after the violation rather than preventing it. The guilt-awareness reveals a conscience operating in its reactive mode: the image-bearer's moral self-assessment is post-hoc. Hos 10:2 — "their heart is false; now they must bear their guilt" — the false heart is the image's condition that generates guilt; the guilt-bearing confirms the image's fallen state rather than the absence of conscience.

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — The presence of iniquity-as-accumulated-crime reveals the image in its *morally corrupted and accumulating fallen condition*: the image-bearer not only commits individual sinning acts but has accumulated a store of moral wrongdoing that constitutes a standing crime before God. Isa 1:4 — "a people laden with iniquity" — the weight of the accumulation is the image's fallen condition expressed as moral burden; Ezr 9:6 — "our iniquities have risen higher than our heads" — the accumulation has exceeded the person's capacity to address it through self-effort. The image is not destroyed but its moral account before God is deeply defiled and growing.

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — The presence of transgression-as-deliberate-boundary-crossing reveals the image in its *wilfully defiant fallen condition*: the image-bearer's rational capacity to know God's command has been deployed against God rather than for him. Isa 48:8 — "from of old your ear has not been opened... from before birth you were called a rebel" — the image is so thoroughly characterised by transgression that it has become a defining label. Eze 2:3 — "nations of rebels who have transgressed against me... from their fathers to this very day" — the sustained multi-generational character of the transgression reveals an image whose volitional capacity for knowing defiance has become its dominant orientation.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Faithlessness reveals the image in its *relationally broken condition*: the covenant-capable image-bearer has deployed their relational capacity against the covenant-partner rather than for them. 2Ch 36:14 — "all the officers of the priests and the people likewise were exceedingly unfaithful, following all the abominations of the nations. And they polluted the house of the Lord" — the image's relational capacity has been redirected from God-toward to God-against, and the consequence is the pollution of the very sanctuary that the image was designed to maintain. 1Ch 5:25 — "they broke faith with the God of their fathers, and whored after the gods of the peoples of the land" — the image's capacity for covenantal devotion has been transferred to false objects.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — The presence of perversion reveals the image in its *internally distorted condition*: the faculties that were designed for moral alignment have been twisted against their grain. 1Ti 6:5 — "depraved in mind and deprived of the truth" — the mind (the image's cognitive faculty) has been corrupted at its very seat of understanding. Eph 4:22 — "old self... corrupt through deceitful desires" — the desire-faculty of the image has been turned into the agent of the corruption. The image is not destroyed but its inner faculties (mind, will, heart, desire, speech) are bent away from the moral order they were designed to instantiate.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — The presence of injustice reveals the image's *moral-conduct faculty in its perverted expression*: the image-bearer designed for just conduct (Lev 19:15 — "in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor") has turned their judicial, commercial, and interpersonal conduct away from God's standard. Psa 53:1 — "the fool says in his heart, 'There is no God.' They are corrupt, doing abominable iniquity" — the godless heart produces unjust conduct; the image's knowledge of God is what sustains just conduct, and its denial produces injustice. Job 18:21 — the unjust person "knows not God" — the absence of God-knowledge is the diagnostic marker of the unjust character.

## T0.4 — Typological Significance

### T0.4.1

**Question:** Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate (covenantal, eschatological, christological)?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus contains clear typological deployment of wilful sinning in two directions. First, Eze 28:16 ("you sinned; so I cast you as a profane thing from the mountain of God, O guardian cherub") uses the cherub's sinning as a paradigmatic instance that illuminates the inner dynamic of how sinning produces expulsion from the divine presence — a pattern that maps onto human sinning and its consequences. Second, the Levitical sin-offering system (VCG-04: Lev 4:14, 4:22, 4:23, 4:27, 4:28, 4:35, 5:6–13) is typological throughout — the sacrificial remedy for wilful sinning points forward to the substitutionary atonement that resolves sin's debt. The Lev 5:13 formula ("and he shall be forgiven") and Lev 4:35 ("the priest shall make atonement for him for the sin which he has committed, and he shall be forgiven") foreshadow a forgiving transaction that the NT corpus (VCG-05) casts as fulfilled in Christ's advocacy (1Jo 2:1: "we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous").

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The cultic-purification corpus (VCG-02) carries clear typological freight. The daily sin offering for the altar (Exo 29:36 — "every day you shall offer a bull as a sin offering for atonement... you shall purify the altar... and anoint it to consecrate it") is part of a larger typological system: the repeated daily and annual cleansing of the altar and sanctuary (Eze 45:18) points toward a final, comprehensive atonement that the sacrificial system cannot itself achieve but only anticipates. The individual unintentional-sin offering (Num 15:27/28 — goat offered, priest makes atonement, forgiven) similarly functions typologically: the external remedy for an inadvertent moral failure anticipates a remedy that addresses the inner moral condition more completely than a prescribed animal sacrifice can. Lev 4:3 (priestly sin implicating the community) prefigures the priest-as-representative who mediates between the community and God.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, in two directions. (1) *The corporate confessional sequence as typological pattern*: the Judges-Samuel pattern (Judg 10:10/15; 1Sa 7:6; 12:10) — confession leading to divine intervention and leadership renewal — is a recurring pattern that typologically prefigures the deeper restoration in which the new covenant provides a once-for-all resolution of what corporate confession could only address episodically. (2) *Daniel's prayer (Dan 9:5/8/11/15)* functions typologically: a righteous man praying on behalf of a sinful community, including himself in the confession, points forward to the intercessory representative who identifies with the sinners he confesses for. The sustained, comprehensive character of Daniel's confession — multiple specific dimensions named — anticipates the total coverage of sin that the new covenant promises.

**[CHAR-4] Conscience suppression** *(S)*

**[CHAR-4]** S — The 8-verse corpus contains no typological deployment of conscience suppression. The verses address the characteristic directly as a present moral condition (Jer 2:35; Jer 16:10) or as a described inner state (Ecc 8:12; Tit 3:11; 1Cor 15:34). No verse deploys it to point toward a christological, eschatological, or covenantal reality beyond the immediate. Judg 20:16 is physically literal with no inner-being content.

**[CHAR-5] Refusal to repent** *(E)*

**[CHAR-5]** E — Limited typological deployment. The Kings Jeroboam-pattern corpus (1Ki 16:31; 2Ki 3:3; 10:29/31; 13:2/6/11; 14:24; 15:9/18/24/28) functions as a *cumulative typological warning*: each king who "did not depart from the sins of Jeroboam" both perpetuates the sin and adds to the typological portrait of a leadership class whose refusal to turn models — and transmits — a pattern of sustained refusal. This cumulative pattern typologically anticipates the NT's sharpest formulation in Heb 10:26: the persistent, deliberate sinning after knowing the truth as the ultimate form of refusal to repent. The OT pattern points toward the NT's eschatological intensification.

**[CHAR-6] Habitual defection** *(E)*

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes. Heb 3:17 deploys the wilderness generation's 40 years of habitual sinning typologically: "Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies fell in the wilderness?" — the wilderness generation's sustained habitual rebellion is presented as a *warning type* for the Hebrews 3 audience. The OT instance of 40-year habitual sinning functions as the negative typological precedent: this is what happens when the inner pattern of rebellion is sustained — the bodies fall. The typology warns the NT community against repeating the pattern. The direction is human instance (wilderness generation) → warning application to human readers.

**[CHAR-7] Contagious sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. The Jeroboam-formula ("who made Israel to sin") operates as a *negative typological benchmark* — Jeroboam is the established type of the sin-transmitting leader against whom every subsequent king is measured. 2Ki 23:15 — Josiah's destruction of Jeroboam's altar — is typologically significant: the dismantling of the physical monument to Jeroboam's contagious sin points toward the eschatological dismantling of all sin-transmitting structures. The altar at Bethel was the enduring structural legacy of Jeroboam's contagious sin; its demolition under Josiah prefigures the complete removal of the structures that sin-transmitting leadership builds. The OT pattern thus points forward to a leadership that transmits righteousness rather than sin — the antitype of Jeroboam is the leader through whom God's people are formed toward God rather than away from him.

**[CHAR-8] Political revolt** *(E)*

**[CHAR-8]** E — Limited typological evidence. 1Ki 12:19 / 2Ch 10:19 — "Israel has been in rebellion against the house of David to this day" — the narrator's summary functions as a theological-historical marker that points forward: Israel's persistent political revolt from the Davidic house is an ongoing wound in the covenant history that ultimately points toward the eschatological restoration of the Davidic covenant (the hope of the restored Davidic king). The political revolt marks a fracture in the covenant history that requires future divine resolution. The typological direction is: the present fracture anticipating future restoration.

**[CHAR-9] Sinful speech** *(E)*

**[CHAR-9]** E — Yes. VCG-02's blasphemy-charge against Jesus (Mat 26:65; Mar 14:64; Luk 5:21; Joh 10:33) operates typologically: the religious authorities' charge of blasphemy against Jesus — based on his claim to divine authority and his forgiveness of sins — is the type that ironically inverts; the one accused of blasphemy is actually the one who is God. The typological direction is: the human accusation of blasphemy (false, directed against the true Son of God) points toward the revelation of who Jesus is — the charge that is meant to condemn becomes the evidence that he is what he claims.

**[CHAR-10] Specialised sinful mechanisms** *(E)*

**[CHAR-10]** E — Limited typological evidence. Act 21:21's apostasy charge against Paul operates in the context of the Acts narrative's broader argument about the relationship between the Mosaic covenant and the new covenant: the accusation that Paul teaches apostasy from Moses is itself addressed within the narrative's argument that Paul is not abandoning the covenant but fulfilling it through Christ. This is not typology in the strict sense but covenantal-hermeneutical deployment: the apostasia charge is the occasion for clarifying what authentic covenantal identity means in the new-covenant era.

**[CHAR-11] Sin as universal condition** *(E)*

**[CHAR-11]** E — Yes — in two directions. (1) *Adam as type*: Rom 5:12/14 — "just as sin came into the world through one man... Adam, who was a type of the one who was to come" — Adam's original sin-entry is the negative type that establishes the universal condition; Christ as the antitype reverses what Adam established. The typological use of sin's universal entry (through one man) points toward sin's universal removal (through one man — the last Adam). (2) *Israel's trespass as type*: Rom 11:11/12 — "through their trespass salvation has come to the Gentiles" — Israel's corporate participation in the universal sinful condition becomes the occasion for the redemptive purpose to extend to all nations; the trespass is deployed typologically as the instrument of unexpected global blessing.

**[CHAR-12] Sin as enslaving power** *(E)*

**[CHAR-12]** E — Yes. Gal 3:22 — "the Scripture imprisoned everything under sin, so that the promise by faith in Jesus Christ might be given to those who believe" — the universal imprisonment under sin functions typologically: the condition of universal imprisonment is the necessary precondition and backdrop against which the promise by faith operates. The imprisonment-type is not an end in itself but points toward the faith-promise as its resolution. The negative type (imprisonment under sin) points toward the positive antitype (liberation through faith in Jesus Christ). The typological use is explicitly purposive: "so that the promise... might be given."

**[CHAR-13] Sin as divine record** *(E)*

**[CHAR-13]** E — Yes. (1) *Day of Atonement typology*: Lev 16:21 — "Aaron shall lay both his hands on the head of the live goat, and confess over it all the iniquities of the people of Israel, and all their transgressions, all their sins. And he shall put them on the head of the goat and send it away into the wilderness" — the annual ritual of sin-record being placed on the scapegoat and sent to the wilderness typologically points toward the Lamb of God (Joh 1:29 — "who takes away the sin of the world"). (2) *Moses's divine-book intercession*: Exo 32:32 — Moses's offer to be blotted from God's book if the people's sin is not forgiven typologically prefigures Christ's bearing the sin-record of the people.

**[CHAR-14] Forgiveness sought and received** *(E)*

**[CHAR-14]** E — Yes — the most concentrated typological evidence in M10. The entire VCG-01 kip.pu.rim system (12 verses) is typological: the Day of Atonement (Lev 23:27/28; 25:9), the daily sin offerings (Exo 29:36; 30:10), and the priestly bearing of iniquity (Lev 10:17) all function as institutional types pointing toward the antitypical atoning work of Christ. Mat 26:28 — "my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" — is the antitype declaration: the covenant blood replaces the animal blood; the permanent forgiveness replaces the annual provisional covering.

**[CHAR-15] Generational sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-15]** E — Yes — through Isa 43:27's structure. "Your first father sinned, and your mediators transgressed against me" — the naming of the "first father" as the origin-point of Israel's sinning functions as the typological ground: the ancestral sinning is the type that the present generation's condition fulfils. The first father's sin becomes the pattern that the mediators continued and the present nation inherits — a typological escalation-chain where the ancestral type is expanded and confirmed in each subsequent generation.

**[CHAR-16] The sinner as moral character** *(E)*

**[CHAR-16]** E — Yes. Rom 5:19 — "by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous" — the sinner-identity constituted through Adam is the type that Christ's righteousness reverses; Adam as the type of the sinner-constituting one, Christ as the antitype of the righteous-constituting one. 1Ti 1:15/16 — Paul as the "foremost sinner" functions paradigmatically: the mercy shown to him (the worst case) becomes a demonstration for all future believers that Christ's saving mission extends to the extreme sinner-type.

**[CHAR-17] Guilt as inner-being state** *(E)*

**[CHAR-17]** E — Yes — through the transference dimension (VCG-03). Exo 28:38 — "Aaron shall bear any guilt from the holy things that the people of Israel consecrate" — the high priest's guilt-bearing on his forehead is typological: Aaron as the one who bears the community's guilt before God prefigures the NT sin-bearer. Eze 4:4/5/6 — the prophet Ezekiel is commanded to bear Israel's and Judah's punishment symbolically by lying on his side — the enacted guilt-bearing is typological of the servant-bearing of iniquity (Isa 53:12 — "he bore the sin of many" — in CHAR-13's corpus).

**[CHAR-18] Iniquity as accumulated moral crime** *(E)*

**[CHAR-18]** E — Yes — through the servant-bearing passages (VCG-06 Isa 53 sub-set). Isa 53:5 — "he was crushed for our iniquities" — the servant's bearing of the people's accumulated iniquity is typological: the priestly scapegoat bearing (Lev 16:21/22 — VCG-01) is the OT type; the servant's bearing is the eschatological fulfilment-type pointing toward Christ. Isa 53:6 — "the Lord has laid on him the iniquity of us all" — the universal scope of the iniquity-bearing is the typological pinnacle: all accumulated human iniquity placed upon the one servant. Zec 3:4 — "I have taken your iniquity away from you" — Joshua's single-person iniquity-removal prefigures the land's total removal (Zec 3:9) and the new covenant's complete forgiveness (Jer 31:34).

**[CHAR-19] Transgression as deliberate boundary-crossing** *(E)*

**[CHAR-19]** E — Yes — through two typological axes. (1) *Adam-paradigm transgression* (VCG-05): Rom 5:14 — "Adam... a type of the one who was to come" — Adam's transgression is explicitly named as a type; the trespass's death-consequence (VCG-05 — Rom 5:15/16/17) is the negative type whose positive antitype is Christ's one act of righteousness; (2) *Servant-bearing* (VCG-07 cross-reference; VCG-06): Isa 53:5 — "he was pierced for our transgressions"; Isa 53:8 — "stricken for the transgression of my people" — the servant's bearing of transgression's cost is the OT type pointing toward Christ. Isa 53:12 — "numbered with the transgressors; yet he bore the sin of many, and makes intercession for the transgressors" — the intercession-for-transgressors is the typological bridge.

**[CHAR-20] Faithlessness as covenant-breaking sin** *(E)*

**[CHAR-20]** E — Yes — through two typological axes. (1) *The marital-faithlessness type of Israel's unfaithfulness to God*: Jer 3:20 — "as a treacherous wife leaves her husband, so have you been treacherous to me, O house of Israel" — the marital faithlessness is the type of the Israel-God covenant betrayal; Mal 2:14-16 — marital faithlessness is treated with the same theological seriousness as covenant-faithlessness toward God; the marriage-covenant is the human type pointing toward the divine-Israel bond; (2) *Adam-type covenant-breaking*: Hos 6:7 — "like Adam they transgressed the covenant; there they dealt faithlessly with me" — Adam's covenant-breach is the founding type of which Israel's faithlessness is the national repetition.

**[CHAR-21] Perversion as inner inversion** *(E)*

**[CHAR-21]** E — Yes — through the vine-degeneracy type and the creature-creator inversion type. (1) *Vine type*: Jer 2:21 — "I planted you a choice vine... how then have you turned degenerate and become a wild vine?" — Israel's degeneracy is the anti-type of the faithfully-producing vine; this vine-type points toward the true vine (Joh 15 — in CHAR-20's adjacent cluster); (2) *Creature-creator inversion*: Isa 29:16 — "shall the potter be regarded as the clay... 'He did not make me'?" — the cognitive inversion of creature denying creator is the type of the ultimate eschatological reversal in which God will act as the definitive potter who reshapes the perverted creation.

**[CHAR-22] Injustice as moral failure of right conduct** *(E)*

**[CHAR-22]** E — Yes — through the Christological substitution type. 1Pe 3:18 — "Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, that he might bring us to God" — the unjust (*adikoi*) are the precise moral category for whom the righteous Christ substitutes; the unrighteous/unjust person-type is the direct object of Christ's redemptive action. The unjust person-type (VCG-03 — *adikos*) is deployed typologically: the human category of the unjust points toward the divine provision of the righteous One who takes their place.

### T0.4.2

**Question:** If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?

**[CHAR-1] Wilful sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-1]** E — The typological direction is bi-directional. In the Levitical sin-offering corpus (VCG-04), the human instance of sinning points forward to a divine resolution: the sacrificial system is a human-facing institution that anticipates the divine act of atonement. In the Eze 28:16 cherub passage, the creaturely (angelic) instance of sinning establishes the pattern — falling from the presence of God — that the human instance participates in and illuminates. The NT corpus (VCG-05) shows the reverse movement: 1Jo 2:1's advocacy language presupposes that Christ (the divine) addresses the human instance of sinning, making the divine-to-human direction primary in the NT resolution of the type.

**[CHAR-2] Unintentional sinning** *(E)*

**[CHAR-2]** E — The direction is *human instance pointing forward to divine resolution*. The unintentional-sin offerings (Lev 4/5; Num 15:27/28) are human-institution responses to human moral liability that point forward to a divine act of atonement more complete than any animal offering can achieve. The ritual establishes the *shape* of atonement (substitutionary offering + priestly intercession + forgiveness declared) without being the substance. The direction runs from the repeated human cultic act toward the once-for-all divine provision. The cultic-purification VCG-02 (altar purification in Eze 43/45) points toward the eschatological sanctuary where God himself purifies the sacred space comprehensively.

**[CHAR-3] Confession** *(E)*

**[CHAR-3]** E — The direction is *human instance pointing forward to divine resolution*. The human confessional acts (Judges, Samuel, Daniel) establish the pattern and the need — repeated corporate confession that must be renewed repeatedly reveals the inadequacy of episodic confession to resolve the deep root of sin. The forward pointing is toward a covenant arrangement in which the sin that requires confession is comprehensively addressed rather than repeatedly named. Eze 33:16 ("None of the sins shall be remembered against him") points toward a state of total non-remembrance that the episodic confessional system could not achieve permanently — the type is the human confession; the antitype is the divine forgetting of sin in its totality.
