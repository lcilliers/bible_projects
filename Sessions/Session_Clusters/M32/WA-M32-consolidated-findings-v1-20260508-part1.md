## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A — Conscience]** E — 1Cor 4:4: the Lord is named as the one who judges — the one whose knowledge of the person exceeds the person's own self-knowledge. Conscience as inner moral self-knowing reflects God's own complete self-knowledge. The characteristic images divine self-knowing in creaturely, bounded form.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Jer 31:21: God commands Israel to set attention on their own history ("consider well the highway, the road by which you went"). God calls the faculty into exercise, which reflects his own sustained attentiveness to his people's condition and history.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Both sub-groups reveal a God who knows and attends completely and without remainder (kardiognōstēs, Act 1:24; Act 15:8 — BOUNDARY). The cluster characteristics image this in creaturely, limited form: the human person knows themselves partially through conscience; attends deliberately but can refuse. The gap between divine knowing and human knowing is constitutive of both characteristics.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Act 1:24, Act 15:8 (BOUNDARY, kardiognōstēs): God is explicitly named as heart-knower — his inner-being knowledge of every person is complete. This is the divine form of what conscience attempts in the human: knowing the inner moral reality. Attribution is explicit.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** S — None of M32-B's seven verses explicitly attribute the inner-attending faculty (shit) to God. God commands its exercise (Jer 31:21) but is not himself described as exercising it.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**[A — Conscience]** E — The silence on God possessing conscience in the bounded, epistemically-limited form of 1Cor 4:4 is significant. A conscience that operates under a higher court it cannot itself access is a specifically creaturely structure. God has no higher court. The silence confirms the characteristic as creaturely in this form.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — The silence on God exercising shit is structurally informative: the term names directed attention that presupposes incomplete knowledge. God's omniscience makes this specific attentive structure inapplicable to him. The silence locates the characteristic as exclusively creaturely in this form.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Act 5:2: conscience registers moral participation — the person knows what they have done and holds that knowledge. 1Cor 4:4: conscience provides the person with a genuine inner verdict on their own moral standing. Created purpose: to equip the person to know their own moral condition as preparation for standing before God's definitive judgment.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Psa 48:13: attend carefully to transmit covenantal knowledge to the next generation. Pro 24:32: attend to receive instruction. Jer 31:21: attend to one's own history as the basis for return. Created purpose: to equip the person to engage properly with moral, relational, and covenantal reality by deliberately directing inner attention toward it.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Both, though the verses do not explicitly address creation vs fall. Act 5:2 shows conscience operating in a fallen context. 1Cor 4:4 shows it operating in a redeemed person. The faculty is present in fallen persons; its presence in both contexts suggests it is a created capacity expressed in fallen conditions.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Both. The faculty is a created capacity (Jer 31:21 — God commands its exercise as a natural human capacity). Its mis-exercise and refusal (Exo 7:23) and its collapse (1Sa 4:20) are fallen realities. The faculty is created; its failure modes are fallen.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**[A — Conscience]** E — 1Cor 4:4: "It is the Lord who judges me" — the conscience-verdict is provisional, pointing forward to the Lord's definitive judgment as its eschatological completion. The present conscience operates under the awareness of a coming definitive verdict.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Jer 31:21 sits in Jer 31 (new covenant chapter). The commanded attending toward one's own history anticipates the new covenant renewal (Jer 31:33 — law written in hearts). The characteristic is oriented toward the future fullness in which inner attending is replaced by direct inner knowledge.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?

**[A — Conscience]** E — 1Cor 4:4: the person holds a genuine inner moral verdict on themselves — moral self-transparency, however partial. This self-assessing capacity images God's own complete self-knowledge in creaturely, bounded form.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Psa 48:13: the person attends carefully to transmit covenantal knowledge to the next generation — deliberate attending in the service of covenant continuity, imaging God's own sustained care for his people's knowledge of him.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Shared in kind, asymmetric in form. God knows hearts completely (kardiognōstēs, Act 1:24). Human conscience knows the self partially (1Cor 4:4 — clear conscience does not acquit). The knowing faculty is shared; its completeness is not.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** S — M32-B's verses do not explicitly address whether God exercises directed inner attention in the form shit names. The structural argument (T0.1.3) suggests the specific form is creaturely. S on explicit verse evidence.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Act 5:2: conscience operates in a person engaged in active deception — the image-bearing faculty is present even while wrong is being committed. Its presence does not guarantee righteousness. Its suppression or searing (implied by 1Tim 4:2, referenced in registry finding 112-F006) reveals image-damage.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Exo 7:23: Pharaoh's deliberate refusal to take the plagues to heart names moral hardness — the image-bearing capacity for attending is present but wilfully resisted. The refusal of the faculty is itself a moral indicator of image-condition.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**[A — Conscience]** S — M32-A's four verses do not deploy conscience typologically. The uses are forensic, situational, and epistemic. Explicitly noted: no typological use in M32-A.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Jer 31:21 sits in the Jer 31 new covenant oracle. The commanded self-attending ("consider the road by which you went, return") anticipates the new covenant reality of Jer 31:33 where God writes his law in hearts. The human act of self-reflexive attending that enables return is the precursor to the divine act of renewal.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[A — Conscience]** S — Not applicable; T0.4.1 silent for M32-A.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Human → divine. The human act of self-reflexive attending (Jer 31:21) points toward the divine new covenant act (Jer 31:33). The human precedes and anticipates the divine.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[A — Conscience]** S — Explicitly noted: no typological use of conscience in M32-A's verse evidence.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Typological use evidenced in Jer 31:21. T0.4.3 not applicable for M32-B.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[A — Conscience]** E — suneidō (G4894), sunoida (G6083). Both carry the sun- prefix (= with, together). The name signals co-knowing — knowing alongside oneself. Conscience is not mere self-observation but inner co-witnessing: the person is both knower and known in the same act.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — H7896K shit: "to set: consider." The root image is intentional placement — setting the mind on something. The name signals directedness and volition: inner attention is an active placing of the cognitive faculty, not passive reception.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**[A — Conscience]** E — suneidō: "to share knowledge with; to be conscious aware" (Act 5:2 — moral co-knowledge of deception; Act 12:12 — cognitive recognition). sunoida: "to be conscious of, know" (1Cor 4:4 — self-consciousness of moral standing). The sun- prefix in both: knowing-with. Definitional signal: conscience is structured as inner co-witnessing — the self alongside itself.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — shit belongs to the SHIT root family: to set: make, put, appoint, accuse — all sharing intentional placement. shit (consider) = placing the mind with intention. The root family shows the characteristic as one of a family of deliberate-positioning acts — the one directed at cognition and inner attending.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Relational: the sun- prefix names a co-structure — knowing with. In Act 5:2 the "with" is shared complicity (Sapphira knowing alongside her husband). In 1Cor 4:4 the "with" is self-witnessing (Paul knowing alongside himself). The relational implication is intrinsic: conscience is the self as both witness and witnessed.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Directional: shit always takes an object — the attending is directed toward something. External (Psa 48:13), inward (Psa 13:2), historical-self (Jer 31:21), morally significant event (Exo 7:23). Constitutional implication: the characteristic is an act requiring a direction, not a state.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**[A — Conscience]** E — A faculty-in-act: simultaneously the faculty of inner moral knowing and the act of that knowing exercised. 1Cor 4:4 shows the act (Paul actively self-assessing); Act 5:2 shows the faculty operative alongside an ongoing moral situation. Act and faculty are not separable in the evidence.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — An act of the will-and-cognition. shit is verbal throughout — what the person does, not what they have. The act is volitional: it is exercised (Psa 48:13), refused (Exo 7:23), collapsed (1Sa 4:20), redirected (2Sa 13:20).

**BOUNDARY — kardiognōstēs (G2589):** Divine-attribute term. Names God as the one whose epistemic access to the human inner being is complete and unmediated. Role in cluster economy: the outer epistemological frame within which both M32-A and M32-B operate — God's heart-knowledge is the definitive court above conscience (1Cor 4:4) and above all human inner-knowing. Evidence: Act 1:24 (invoked as ground for decisive divine selection); Act 15:8 (invoked as ground for divine reception of Gentiles). Both instances: God's heart-knowledge settles what human knowledge cannot.

**BOUNDARY — enthumeomai (G1760):** Reflective-capacity term. Names the act of sustained inner deliberation — the person turning something over in the mind. Role in cluster economy: the cognitive deliberation that bridges initial attending (M32-B) and moral self-assessment (M32-A). Evidence: Mat 1:20 (Joseph holds situation in inner reflection before divine direction); Mat 9:4 (scribes' inner reflection perceived by Christ — establishes inner deliberation as transparent to divine perception); Act 10:19 (Peter pondering the vision before divine communication). The Mat 9:4 instance connects structurally to kardiognōstēs: inner reflection is not hidden from God.

**BOUNDARY — autokatakritos (G0843):** Outcome-terminus term. Names the terminal state of conscience — the condition in which the person's own beliefs or conduct become the instrument of their condemnation. Role in cluster economy: shows where conscience leads when a person persists in known wrong — the conscience-faculty turns prosecutor. Negative complement to 1Cor 4:4: where sunoida gives a clear verdict and acknowledges God as final judge, autokatakritos shows conscience having issued a condemning verdict the person cannot escape because it comes from within. Evidence: Tit 3:11 ("such a person is warped and sinful; he is self-condemned").

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Not fully simple. Two modes evidenced: moral-complicity knowing (Act 5:2) and moral-self-assessment (1Cor 4:4). A third mode (general cognitive awareness: Act 12:12, Act 14:6) shows semantic breadth. Unified by the single faculty of inner co-witnessing.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Two structural modes established in Phase 6: active attending (group 3578-A: Psa 13:2, Psa 48:13, Pro 24:32, Jer 31:21) and absent/refused attending (group 3578-B: Exo 7:23, 1Sa 4:20, 2Sa 13:20). These are modes of one faculty, not separate characteristics.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Conscience is the inner faculty of moral co-knowing by which the person holds awareness of their own moral participation, standing, or condition. It operates as self-witness and co-witness; issues real verdicts on the self; its verdicts are epistemically limited — genuine but not final, always operating under God's higher judgment (1Cor 4:4).

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Inner attention is the deliberate act of setting the cognitive-attentive faculty on a matter, person, or one's own history. It can be actively exercised (producing learning, testimony, return: Psa 48:13, Pro 24:32, Jer 31:21) or refused, collapsed, and suppressed (Exo 7:23, 1Sa 4:20, 2Sa 13:20). It is the attentive capacity that underlies conscience without being conscience itself.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Moral self-ignorance or insensibility — not knowing one's own moral condition. Gestured at by the M32-B parallel (Exo 7:23 — Pharaoh not taking the plagues to heart: the absence of attending that would engage conscience).

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Named directly by group 3578-B: non-attending, refusal to take to heart (Exo 7:23), collapse of the faculty (1Sa 4:20), social suppression (2Sa 13:20). The opposite is evidenced by the sub-group's own negative instances.

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A — Conscience]** E — 1Cor 4:4: conscience excludes false certainty (Paul does not over-claim his clear verdict as final) and moral blindness (he does not claim ignorance). Conscience resists both self-deception and unjustified self-condemnation.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Exo 7:23: the faculty excludes casual dismissal — to shit is to take seriously. The characteristic excludes passive, unreflective engagement with what matters morally and covenantally. 2Sa 13:20: the faculty can be suppressed by counsel, but this is presented as a suppression, not a neutral act.

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Not final judgment (1Cor 4:4 — "I am not thereby acquitted"). Not omniscient self-knowledge. Not merely emotion. Not external accusation — it is the person's own inner co-witness.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Not conscience itself (which assesses; shit only attends). Not memory per se (though Jer 31:21 directs it toward memory of one's past). Not enthumeomai (BOUNDARY — sustained deliberative reflection): shit initiates attending, enthumeomai sustains deliberate turning-over of what has been attended to.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Three modes: (1) moral complicity — inner co-knowing of participation in a morally charged act (Act 5:2); (2) moral self-assessment — active examination of one's own standing with a verdict (1Cor 4:4); (3) general cognitive awareness — the faculty registering external situations (Act 12:12, Act 14:6).

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Five modes: (1) sustained inward attending under distress (Psa 13:2); (2) purposive outward attending for testimony (Psa 48:13); (3) productive attending generating instruction (Pro 24:32); (4) self-reflexive attending to own moral history for return (Jer 31:21); (5) refused/failed/suppressed attending (Exo 7:23, 1Sa 4:20, 2Sa 13:20).

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A — Conscience]** E — Yes. Context shapes what is known: active deception (Act 5:2 — moral complicity); retrospective self-examination (1Cor 4:4); sudden situational recognition (Act 12:12). The faculty is consistent; context determines moral weight.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Yes. The object determines the mode: external object → informational attending (Psa 48:13); internal state → sustaining attending (Psa 13:2); own history → self-reflexive attending (Jer 31:21); morally significant event → moral attending that can be refused (Exo 7:23).

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?

**[A — Conscience]** S — M32-A's verses do not show conscience operating through speech. The act is internal. S.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Psa 48:13: "consider well... that you may tell the next generation." The attending is explicitly oriented toward a communicative act — attend carefully so you can accurately report. The faculty has a testimonial output mode.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A — Conscience]** S — M32-A's verses are situational — moments of inner knowing rather than sustained effects. S.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Jer 31:21: sustained self-reflexive attending produces orientation for return. Psa 13:2: sustained attending under distress keeps the person engaged with their own condition rather than numbing out. Pro 24:32: attending produces instruction received.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure establish?

**[A — Conscience]** S — Not evidenced in M32-A. S.

**[B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention]** E — Wisdom (Pro 24:32), testimonial capacity (Psa 48:13), orientation for return (Jer 31:21), emotional-cognitive endurance under distress (Psa 13:2).

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?

**[A — Conscience]** S — Not determinable; T1.5 and T1.6 both silent for M32-A.

Attending produces the orientation from which return becomes possible — immediate and sustained effects are structurally continuous.
