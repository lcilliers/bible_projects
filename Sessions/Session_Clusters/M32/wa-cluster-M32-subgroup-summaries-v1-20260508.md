## Sub-group M32-A — Conscience (4 verses)

*Terms: G4894 suneidō, G6083 sunoida*

The four verses of M32-A present conscience as a genuinely inner faculty — an act of knowing exercised by the person about their own inner moral state and situation. The sub-group holds two modes in tension. In Act 5:2, suneidō operates as moral co-knowing: Sapphira holds in her inner awareness the knowledge of what she and her husband have done together. This is not theoretical knowledge but participatory moral knowledge — the conscience is engaged because she is herself implicated. In 1Cor 4:4, sunoida operates as moral self-assessment: Paul holds an inner verdict on his own standing — he is not aware of anything against himself — and then immediately qualifies that verdict by placing it under God's higher judgment. The conscience speaks, but its word is not the last word.

Two of the three suneidō verses (Act 12:12, Act 14:6) operate in a general cognitive-awareness mode — becoming aware of a situation, learning of a threat — without explicit moral self-assessment. This is the term's semantic breadth: the same inner-knowing faculty that assesses moral standing also registers situational awareness. The moral and cognitive modes share a single inner faculty.

The governing structural insight of M32-A is 1Cor 4:4: conscience gives a real verdict, but that verdict does not exhaust the judgment. The conscience is a real court, but not the final one. God's judgment stands above and beyond whatever the conscience pronounces — including a clear conscience. This epistemological limitation is built into the sub-group's characteristic: conscience is genuine inner knowing, but knowing under constraint.

## Sub-group M32-B — Self-Awareness / Inner Attention (7 verses)

*Terms: H7896K shit*

The seven verses of M32-B trace the inner-attending faculty across the full range of its engagement and failure. The core characteristic is consistent across all seven: the deliberate act of setting the mind — placing the cognitive-attentive faculty upon something with intention. What varies is the object and outcome.

The faculty can be refused: Pharaoh encounters the plagues and does not take even this to heart (Exo 7:23). It can collapse under extremity: the dying woman in 1Sa 4:20 no longer pays attention. It can be redirected by another's counsel: Absalom urges Tamar to withdraw her inner attention from what has happened to her (2Sa 13:20). These three verses reveal the faculty through its absence or suppression — the characteristic is visible by what is missing.

Where the faculty is actively engaged, the verses show its range of application. It can be directed inward, where the psalmist sustains inner attending on his own distress (Psa 13:2). It can be directed outward for testimony purposes — attend carefully so you can accurately report (Psa 48:13). It can be productive of instruction — deliberate observation and consideration that issues in wisdom received (Pro 24:32). And in the most self-reflexively directed instance (Jer 31:21), the faculty is explicitly turned onto the self's own past: consider the road by which you yourself went, as the basis for return and repentance.

Jer 31:21 is analytically significant: it is the only verse in M32-B where the object of inner attending is the self's own history, directed toward moral return. This places self-awareness-as-inner-attention at the threshold of conscience: when the attending faculty is directed at the self's own past and moral path, it functions as the precondition for repentance. The sub-group is entirely OT Hebrew — there is no NT counterpart for this characteristic in M32.
