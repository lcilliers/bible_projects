- *T1.3.3* — _What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?_
  → Doubt (distazō) is not unbelief — Mat 28:17 shows doubt and worship coexisting; doubt is the incomplete move from seeing to trusting, not the rejection of what is seen. Indecision (dipsuchos) is not neutral ambivalence — it is a morally loaded state of divided allegiance that excludes receiving from God.

---

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation
- *T1.4.1* — _In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?_
  → Two modes: (1) Situational wavering — distazō as faith faltering in a specific encounter moment; (2) Dispositional indecision — dipsuchos as the settled state of chronic non-commitment.
- *T1.4.2* — _Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?_
  → Yes: temporal scale differs — distazō situational and acute; dipsuchos dispositional and chronic.
- *T1.5.1* — _What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?_
  → For distazō: doubt arises immediately in the presence of the threatening stimulus (the wind and waves in Mat 14:30-31) — Peter's faith-wavering is simultaneous with his perception of danger.
- *T1.5.2* — _Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?_
  → Consistent for distazō (situational, immediate).
- *T1.5.3* — _Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly._
  → For dipsuchos, no immediate response is described — the characteristic is dispositional, not event-triggered.

---

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect
- *T1.6.1* — _What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?_
  → Total instability — Jam 1:8 ("unstable in all his ways"); the sustained effect of double-mindedness is the destabilisation of all of life's orientation, not merely one decision.
- *T1.6.2* — _What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?_
  → Sustained double-mindedness produces chronic instability — a person who cannot hold a settled orientation in any domain.
- *T1.6.3* — _Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?_
  → For dipsuchos: the characteristic is already dispositional (chronic) at first encounter — the sustained effect (instability in all ways) is its inherent consequence.

---

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception
- *T1.7.1* — _What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?_
  → Doubt is received when circumstances challenge the reliability of what is trusted (Peter on the water — Mat 14:30), or when a person has never settled into single-minded commitment to begin with (dipsuchos).
- *T1.7.2* — _What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?_
  → Whole-heartedness — the purified heart (Jam 4:8) that blocks double-mindedness; the experience of Jesus's reliable action in crisis (Mat 14:31 — the hand grasped immediately) that forms trust against future doubt.
- *T1.7.3* — _What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?_
  → The person who does not fall into situational doubt (distazō) completes the move from seeing to trusting — the disciples who worshipped without doubting at Mat 28:17 are the implied contrast.

---

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification
- *T1.8.1* — _What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?_
  → Cognitive-volitional — distazō is primarily cognitive (epistemic wavering); dipsuchos is primarily volitional (dispositional non-commitment). Both involve a failure to settle, which is a volitional act.
- *T1.8.2* — _What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?_
  → Jam 1:8 ("unstable in all his ways") locates the consequence in behavioural-volitional instability; Jam 4:8 ("purify your hearts") locates the remedy in the heart's volitional-affective centre.
- *T1.8.3* — _Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?_
  → Relational — dipsuchos's divided allegiance has a relational dimension (divided between God and world). Affective — distazō involves emotional fear (Mat 14:30 — Peter saw the wind and was afraid before he doubted).
