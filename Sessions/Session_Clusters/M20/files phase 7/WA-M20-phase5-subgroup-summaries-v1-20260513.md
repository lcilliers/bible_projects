## M20-A — Anxiety and Worry (30 verses, 3 terms)

The sub-group's verses evidence anxiety as a condition of the inner person — specifically of the heart and the attentional faculty — in which the person's orientation is displaced from God toward temporal concerns. The displacement is not neutral: merimnaō and merimna consistently appear in the same semantic field as desire, riches, and pleasures (Mat 13:22; Mar 4:19; Luk 8:14), and the combined effect is described as choking — the productive capacity of the inner person is strangled, not merely diminished. The anxiety is not always freely chosen: it descends like thorns around a plant. Yet Jesus addresses it with commands (Mat 6:25, 31, 34; Phili 4:6), which implies the person participates in maintaining it and can be turned from it.

The sub-group carries a significant register split. merimnaō's 2709-003 group (1Cor 7:32–34; 1Cor 12:25; Phili 2:20) names directed attentional care — for the things of the Lord, for others' welfare — that is not anxiety in any disordering sense. The same verb that describes the person trapped in material worry also describes Timothy's genuine concern for the Philippians' welfare. The positive register constitutes roughly a third of merimnaō's verses and requires separate VCG treatment in Phase 6.

Hebrew da.ag (259-001) shows anxiety oriented toward specific, named, real threats — a missing son, an invading army, exile — rather than the diffuse material-provision worry of the Gospel texts. The tree of Jer 17:8 that is not anxious in drought evidences the structural opposite: rootedness in God as the condition that prevents anxiety when circumstances are genuinely threatening.

One verse in this sub-group (Psa 38:18, da.ag 259-002) carries sorrow-over-sin rather than anxiety. It does not belong to the anxiety characteristic and will be addressed in Phase 6.

## M20-B — Despair and Hopelessness (8 verses, 2 terms)

The sub-group evidences despair as the collapse of the inner person's forward-orientation — but two distinct profiles emerge from the eight verses, one circumstantial and one volitional.

Greek exaporeō (2Cor 1:8; 4:8) names despair as the outer limit of bearable inner experience — the point at which the person is burdened beyond their strength and can no longer see forward. Paul's language is precise: "beyond our strength" is not merely heavy but capacity-exceeding. The second exaporeō verse (2Cor 4:8) is equally significant: Paul is "perplexed but not driven to despair" — held short of the edge. Despair here is the terminus that divine sustaining prevents, not merely an emotional state. This is despair as overwhelming external pressure meeting the limit of inner endurance.

Hebrew ya.ash presents a markedly different profile. Ecc 2:20 — "gave my heart up to despair" — involves a volitional act: the heart is handed over. Jer 2:25 and Jer 18:12 show ya.ash as a verbal posture, a declaration of hopelessness that is used to justify continued rebellion ("it is hopeless, I have loved foreigners"). Job 6:26 names the quality of the despairing person's speech as "wind." This is not despair as collapse under pressure but despair as an active inner posture — a giving up that involves the will and, in some cases, moral self-justification.

The sub-group's internal diversity is analytically significant: circumstantial despair (exaporeō) and volitional despair (ya.ash) describe related but distinct inner movements that share the characteristic of lost forward-orientation.

## M20-C — Discouragement and Loss of Heart (15 verses, 5 terms)

The sub-group evidences the inner person's courage and resolve diminishing — but the five terms distribute this across markedly different causes, and the distinctions carry analytical weight.

ekkakeō (6 verses, all NT) operates in sustained-effort contexts: persistent prayer (Luk 18:1), apostolic ministry (2Cor 4:1, 4:16), doing good over time (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13), bearing another's suffering (Eph 3:13). This is the discouragement of long-haul endurance failing, not the shock of sudden crisis. The antidotes offered are theological — mercy received, inner renewal, a promised future harvest — rather than circumstantial relief. Notably, 2Cor 4:16 draws the explicit contrast: the outer person wastes away but the inner person is renewed day by day. Loss of heart is specifically an inner-person risk in the face of outward deterioration.

ka.ah and ka.eh (shared verses: Psa 109:16; Eze 13:22; Dan 11:30) show discouragement within a victim-and-aggressor structure: the disheartened are pursued to death (Psa 109:16), falsely disheartened by prophets (Eze 13:22), or retreating under military pressure (Dan 11:30). Here discouragement is something done to a person, not merely experienced.

athumeō (Col 3:21) is the relational face of discouragement — produced by parental provocation, the only term in this sub-group where the cause is an explicit interpersonal act.

oligopsuchos (1Th 5:14) — the fainthearted who need encouragement specifically — points to a diminished-soul state that is addressed by being drawn alongside, not corrected or assisted.

## M20-D — Doubt and Indecision (4 verses, 2 terms)

The sub-group evidences the inner person failing to maintain single-minded commitment — but across different registers of time and cause.

distazō (Mat 14:31; Mat 28:17) names doubt in specific encounter moments. Peter walking on water doubts and begins to sink; the disciples at the resurrection appearance worship but some doubt. In both cases the person is in the presence of the miraculous and fails to complete the move from seeing to trusting. The doubt is not absence of evidence — the evidence is present and perceived — but failure to sustain commitment in the face of it. Mat 28:17 is particularly striking: doubt and worship coexist in the same group, suggesting that doubt is not incompatible with some response to Jesus but is incompatible with full commitment. Jesus names and questions the doubt directly (Mat 14:31), then rescues — the remedy is not argument but the grasp of his hand.

dipsuchos (Jam 1:8; Jam 4:8) names a dispositional state rather than a moment. The double-minded person is "unstable in all his ways" — the instability is total and ongoing, not situational. Jam 4:8 calls for purification of the heart, locating the remedy in the affective-volitional centre. The double-minded person is not merely intellectually uncertain but relationally uncommitted — not having settled toward God.

The tension between the two terms noted in Phase 4 is confirmed by the verses: distazō is epistemological-in-the-moment; dipsuchos is dispositional-in-all-ways. They share the profile of non-commitment but describe it at different temporal scales and depths.

*Word count: 252.*
