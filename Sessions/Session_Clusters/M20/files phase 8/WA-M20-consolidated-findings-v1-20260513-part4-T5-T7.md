## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**T5.1.1** — Does this characteristic produce transformation?

**[A]** The sustained operation of anxiety produces transformation toward unfruitfulness — the word choked (Luk 8:14), fruitlessness established. This is a negative transformation of the person's productive capacity. The positive merimnaō register (2709-003) produces transformation of orientation: directed care forms the person as one who genuinely cares for others (2Cor 11:28; Phili 2:20). **[B]** Ya.ash's sustained operation produces transformation of the person's moral orientation — Jer 2:25 shows a person whose sustained despair-posture has formed them as one who has closed off return. The transformation is of the person's openness to repentance. **[C]** The absence of ekkakeō (not losing heart) is associated with transformation — 2Cor 4:16's inner renewal day by day is a positive developmental movement. Losing heart stops this movement. oligopsuchos (1Th 5:14) describes a state of diminished soul-capacity that, if sustained, would continue to reduce the person's inner resource. **[D]** S — the doubt and indecision vocabulary does not describe transformation produced by the characteristic itself.

**T5.1.2** — Reversible or irreversible?

**[A]** Reversible — the command structure (do not be anxious, Phili 4:6) presupposes the person can be turned from the state. 1Pe 5:7's casting implies the burden can be transferred. **[B]** Partially reversible — Isa 57:10 shows despair-declaration resisted and the person finding renewed strength. However Jer 2:25's "I have loved foreigners and after them I will go" suggests a point where the transformation toward closed-off-return becomes hardened. Ya.ash's weaponised form (Jer 18:12) is not shown as reversible in the verse. **[C]** Reversible — commands to not lose heart (ekkakeō) and exhortations to encourage the fainthearted (oligopsuchos, 1Th 5:14) presuppose the state can be remedied. **[D]** Reversible — the remedy (purify your hearts, draw near to God — Jam 4:8) is presented as achievable. Peter's doubt (Mat 14:31) is immediately addressed by Jesus's hand and presumably resolved.

**T5.1.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

No silence — transformation evidence is present in all four sub-groups.

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states?

**[A]** Yes — Mat 6:25-34 describes an implicit sequence: the person encounters uncertainty about provision → anxiety arises → Jesus names it and addresses it → the alternative is prayer (Phili 4:6) → peace (Phili 4:7). The sequence: threat → anxiety → command → prayer → peace. **[B]** 2Cor 1:8-9 describes a sequence: affliction → burdened beyond strength → despaired of life → received sentence of death → reliance on God who raises the dead. The sequence moves from circumstance to inner collapse to theological reorientation. Isa 57:10 — wearied but did not say "it is hopeless" → found new strength → not faint. A sequence where despair-declaration is resisted and renewal follows. **[C]** 2Cor 4:16 — outer person wasting → risk of losing heart → inner renewal day by day → sustained engagement. The sequence of not-losing-heart under bodily deterioration. **[D]** Mat 14:29-31 — Peter walks on water → sees wind → is afraid → doubts → begins to sink → cries out → Jesus grasps him. The most detailed inner-state sequence in the cluster: perception of threat → fear → doubt → consequence → cry → divine response.

**T5.2.2** — What are those states and what does the sequence reveal?

**[D]** The Mat 14:29-31 sequence is analytically the richest in the cluster. It shows: (1) faith enables action (walking on water); (2) perception of threat produces fear; (3) fear produces doubt (the faith enabling action is compromised); (4) doubt produces physical consequence (sinking); (5) consequence produces cry; (6) divine action precedes resolution of the doubt. The sequence reveals that doubt is both preceded by fear and productive of consequence — it is not a stable intermediate state but a deteriorating one.

**T5.2.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

All four sub-groups have sequencing evidence.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change?

**[A]** Anxiety changes the person through gradual choking — not sudden transformation but progressive displacement of fruitfulness (Luk 8:14: "as they go on their way they are choked"). The image is agricultural — slow strangulation over time. **[B]** Ya.ash's mechanism is declarative — the speech-act of declaring hopelessness changes the person's inner state by hardening it. Despair produces change through its own declaration; saying "it is hopeless" makes the state more entrenched. **[C]** Discouragement changes the person through depletion — the gradual loss of the inner energy that sustains engagement. The mechanism is attrition: long-haul effort without visible fruit slowly drains the inner resource. **[D]** S — the doubt vocabulary does not explicitly describe a mechanism of change produced by the doubt itself.

**T5.3.2** — Distinct mechanisms in different contexts?

**[B]** Two mechanisms: overwhelming (exaporeō — sudden, circumstantial overwhelm) vs gradual-declarative (ya.ash — accumulated conclusion leading to a hardening declaration). **[C]** Two mechanisms: inflicted suddenly (provocation-discouragement, athumeō; false-speech disheartening, ka.eh) vs accumulated gradually (ekkakeō's long-haul fatigue).

**T5.3.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

**[D]** Mechanism of change is silent for M20-D — distazō and dipsuchos do not describe how the characteristic itself produces further change in the verse evidence.

**T5.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering?

The anxiety is a response to real, threatening suffering. **[B]** Yes — exaporeō (2Cor 1:8: "affliction we experienced in Asia") is explicitly in the context of severe physical-ministerial suffering. Ya.ash in 1Sa 27:1 ("I shall perish one day by the hand of Saul") is in the context of prolonged mortal threat. **[C]** Yes — strongly. ekkakeō in 2Cor 4:1, 4:16, and Eph 3:13 is explicitly in the context of apostolic suffering (imprisonment, outer body wasting). ### T6.5 — Distinctions

**T6.5.1** — Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what distinguishes them?

**[A]** Nearest neighbour: M01 Fear. Distinction: anxiety (merimnaō) is cognitive-attentional and future-oriented; fear (phobos) is acute, reactive, and threat-present. Anxiety is sustained anticipatory engagement with possible threat; fear is immediate response to present threat. The science extract confirms this: anxiety is cortically mediated, anticipatory, and ruminative; fear is subcortical, immediate, and reactive. **[B]** Nearest neighbour: M20-C (Discouragement). Distinction: despair is the collapse of hope/forward-orientation (the person gives up expecting good outcomes); discouragement is the depletion of courage/resolve in sustained effort (the person loses the energy to continue). Despair ends the orientation toward the future; discouragement ends the energy for the present effort. They can coexist but are distinct. **[C]** Nearest neighbour: M20-B (Despair). Distinction as above — discouragement is the energy-failure, despair is the hope-failure. **[D]** Nearest neighbour: Unbelief (apistia). Distinction: distazō is not the rejection of what is seen but the failure to complete the move from seeing to trusting — doubt is post-perceptual failure, not pre-perceptual rejection. Mat 28:17 shows doubt and worship coexisting — unbelief would not produce worship.

**T6.5.2** — Where apparent overlap, precise boundary?

**[A/M01]** The boundary between anxiety and fear: anxiety is about the future (anticipated threat); fear is about the present (experienced threat). Mat 14:30 illustrates: Peter sees the wind (present threat → fear → phobos) and this produces distazō (doubt). Fear and doubt are distinguishable at the boundary; anxiety (merimnaō) is the sustained version of the forward-looking element.

**T6.5.3** — Distinction one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A/M01]** Kind — anxiety and fear are different kinds of inner response (cognitive-anticipatory vs acute-reactive). **[B/C]** Degree and direction — despair and discouragement differ in degree (despair is more total) and direction (despair collapses forward-orientation; discouragement depletes present-effort energy). **[D/unbelief]** Kind — doubt and unbelief are different kinds of inner state (doubt is mid-process, incomplete trust; unbelief is closed, rejection).

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — Primary Hebrew and Greek terms and root meanings?

**[A]** merimna / merimnaō (Greek): root merizo (to divide) — anxiety as inner division of attention. da.ag (Hebrew): root family DA.AG — focused anxious concern; noun form de.a.gah (anxiety, H1674) within the same root. **[B]** ya.ash (Hebrew): root YAASH — to despair, give up; minimal related-word family (ye.a.te.ray is an unrelated proper name). exaporeō (Greek): ek (out from) + aporeō (to be without resource) — to be completely resourceless. **[C]** ekkakeō (Greek): root family COURAGE — ek (out from) + kakos (evil/harm) — to be exhausted of inner energy/courage. ka.ah (Hebrew): root KA.AH — to be faint, disheartened. ka.eh: adjective of the same root. athumeō: a (without) + thumos (inner energy/passion) — without inner vitality. oligopsuchos: oligos (little) + psuchē (soul) — small-souled. **[D]** distazō (Greek): root DISTAZŌ, from dis (double) — to be of two minds. dipsuchos: dis (double) + psuchē (soul) — two-souled.

**T7.1.2** — Grammatical range?

**[A]** merimna: noun (6 verses). merimnaō: verb (17 verses — all indicative, infinitive, or imperative). da.ag: verb (7 verses). The Greek terms cover both noun and verb; the noun (merimna) tends to name the state (heart weighed down, casting); the verb (merimnaō) tends to name the activity (being anxious, caring for). **[B]** ya.ash: verb exclusively. exaporeō: verb exclusively. Despair in both languages is a verbal characteristic — something the person does or undergoes, not a stable noun-condition. **[C]** ekkakeō: verb exclusively. ka.ah: verb. ka.eh: adjective. oligopsuchos: adjective (noun-used). athumeō: verb. The discouragement cluster has the richest grammatical range: verb, adjective, noun-adjective. **[D]** distazō: verb. dipsuchos: adjective. Both terms maintain the Greek pattern of the verbal-activity (distazō) and the adjectival-condition (dipsuchos).

**T7.1.3** — Semantic range?

**[A]** merimnaō spans: anxious preoccupation with material provision, anticipatory performance anxiety, attentional scattering across many things, and rightly-directed care for God and others — the widest semantic range in the cluster. da.ag spans: relational worry for an absent person, anxiety under concrete threat, anxiety producing deception, and the anxious energy of contrition. **[B]** ya.ash spans: volitional surrender of hope, anticipated exhaustion of pursuit, declarative hopelessness as speech-act, and despair weaponised as justification for rebellion. A relatively narrow but deep semantic range — all within the domain of hope's collapse. exaporeō is semantically narrow: exclusively the overwhelm of inner endurance. **[C]** ekkakeō spans: prayer-persistence failure, ministry-fatigue, doing-good fatigue, bearing another's suffering. ka.ah/ka.eh span: overwhelming of the vulnerable victim, infliction by the aggressor, retreat under pressure. athumeō is semantically narrow (1 verse). oligopsuchos is semantically narrow (1 verse). **[D]** distazō: situational faith-wavering in encounter moments. dipsuchos: dispositional divided allegiance. Both are semantically focused.

**T7.1.4** — Terms distinguishing distinct aspects?

**[A]** merimna (noun: the state) vs merimnaō (verb: the activity). **[B]** ya.ash (volitional-declarative) vs exaporeō (circumstantial-overwhelm) — two distinct aspects of despair named by different vocabulary. **[C]** ka.ah (verb: to be disheartened) vs ka.eh (adjective: the disheartened state) — action and condition distinguished within a single root. **[D]** distazō (situational-momentary) vs dipsuchos (dispositional-chronic) — the momentary act and the settled condition distinguished.

**T7.1.5** — Term for structural opposite or absence?

**[A]** Yes — amerimnos (G0275, untroubled) — explicitly the opposite of merimna within the same word family. The structural-opposite verse (Jer 17:8) shows the opposite of da.ag as rootedness-in-God producing continued fruitfulness. **[B]** Isa 57:10 — the construction "you did not say 'it is hopeless'" implies the positive (ya.ash's absence) — but no dedicated opposite-term is within the ya.ash word family. **[C]** euthumeō (be cheerful, G2114) — within athumeō's thumos word family — is the positive pole of the same inner energy that athumeō names in depletion. Not a strict opposite but a directional counterpart. isopsuchos (G2473 — like-minded) is within the psuchē family and represents the positive-unity counterpart to oligopsuchos (depleted) and dipsuchos (divided). **[D]** isopsuchos (G2473 — like-minded, harmonious) in the dipsuchos word family — the united-soul as the positive counterpart to the divided-soul. sumpsuchos (G4861 — harmonious) similarly.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type term?

**T7.1.9** — NT neologism?

**[D]** dipsuchos — this term is widely considered a neologism coined by James (or possibly from a pre-Jamesian Jewish source). It does not appear in classical Greek literature with the double-soul meaning prior to James. The coinage of dis + psuchē to name the double-minded state reveals that the NT writer found existing vocabulary insufficient to name the specific inner condition of divided allegiance — a new word was needed for a theologically significant inner reality. **[C]** oligopsuchos — similarly a compound not found in classical Greek with this meaning; appears to be distinctively NT/LXX vocabulary. **[A]** amerimnos — appears to be NT vocabulary.

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc?

**[CLUSTER]** The vocabulary arc of M20 moves from Hebrew anxiety-for-specific-objects (da.ag) and Hebrew despair-as-volitional-surrender (ya.ash), through Greek generalisation of the anxiety-faculty to all objects (merimnaō) and Greek naming of the soul's constitutional states (oligopsuchos — depleted; dipsuchos — divided), to the Greek intensification of despair as total-overwhelm (exaporeō). The arc shows increasing constitutional precision in the Greek vocabulary — the Greek terms locate the characteristics in the soul's specific constitutional structure (two-souled, small-souled) rather than describing the behavioural-phenomenological experience (anxious, despairing). The NT adds conceptual precision about what is happening at the soul-level; the OT terms describe what is experienced.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — Function of primary term within its primary verse?

**[A]** Mat 6:25 (merimnaō): the term functions as the negative command's object — "do not be anxious about your life." The verse's argument rests on the implied absurdity of anxiety: life is more than food; the body more than clothing. The term functions as the thing being forbidden and the thing being shown to be unnecessary. **[B]** Ecc 2:20 (ya.ash): the term functions as the act named in the verse — "I turned about and gave my heart up to despair." It is the culminating act of the Preacher's reflection on the futility of labour. The term carries the weight of the Preacher's personal existential conclusion. **[C]** Luk 18:1 (ekkakeō): the term functions as the alternative the parable is designed to prevent — "they ought always to pray and not lose heart." The term defines the negative pole of the parable's argument; its prevention is the parable's purpose. **[D]** Jam 1:8 (dipsuchos): the term functions as the explanation of the instability described in the verse — "he is a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways." The dipsuchos is the subject; the term explains the total instability as its predicate.

**T7.2.2** — Literary form?

**[A]** Epistolary (Phili 4:6; 1Pe 5:7; 2Cor 11:28), Wisdom discourse (Mat 6:25-34; Luk 12:22-26), Parable (Luk 10:41 — Martha/Mary), Prophetic (Isa 57:11; Jer 17:8; 38:19; 42:16). M20-A's verse evidence spans the widest literary range of any M20 sub-group. **[B]** Wisdom (Ecc 2:20; Job 6:26), Narrative (1Sa 27:1), Prophetic (Isa 57:10; Jer 2:25; 18:12), Epistolary (2Cor 1:8; 4:8). M20-B has the widest Testament range (OT wisdom and prophecy; NT epistle). **[C]** Parabolic (Luk 18:1), Epistolary (2Cor 4:1, 4:16; Gal 6:9; Eph 3:13; 2Th 3:13; Col 3:21; 1Th 5:14), Prophetic (Eze 13:22; Dan 11:30), Psalm (Psa 10:10; 109:16). **[D]** Epistolary (Jam 1:8; 4:8), Narrative Gospel (Mat 14:31; 28:17).

**T7.2.3** — Logical structure of key arguments?

**[A]** Mat 6:25-34 has a two-part logical structure: (1) futility argument — anxiety cannot add an hour (6:27) — premise: anxiety is useless; conclusion: do not be anxious; (2) provision argument — the birds/lilies are provided for (6:26, 28-30) — premise: God provides for lesser creatures; conclusion by analogy: God will provide for you. The command not to be anxious rests on two independent logical supports. **[B]** 2Cor 1:8-9 — premise: we were burdened beyond capacity, despaired of life; conclusion: this was to make us rely not on ourselves but on God who raises the dead. The logical structure is purposive: the despair was the designed means of producing reliance on God. Despair is not presented as failure but as the pedagogical mechanism of faith-reorientation. **[C]** Gal 6:9 — "do not grow weary of doing good, for in due season we will reap, if we do not give up" — premise: a harvest is coming; condition: if we do not give up; conclusion: do not grow weary. The logical structure grounds the command in a conditional promise. **[D]** Jam 1:5-8 — premise: God gives wisdom generously; condition: ask in faith without doubting; result: the double-minded person must not suppose they will receive anything. The logical structure makes receiving from God conditional on single-minded trust.

**T7.2.4** — Contextual setting?

**[A]** Liturgical-instructional (Sermon on the Mount), pastoral epistolary (Phili, 1Pe), prophetic-covenant (Isa, Jer). **[B]** Wisdom-experiential (Qohelet, Job), prophetic-judgment (Jer), apostolic-suffering (2Cor). **[C]** Parabolic-instructional (Luk 18:1), apostolic-suffering (2Cor), pastoral epistolary (Gal, Eph, 1Th, 2Th, Col), prophetic-judgment (Eze, Dan), Psalm-lament (Psa). **[D]** Wisdom-epistolary (Jas), Gospel-narrative (Mat).

**T7.2.5** — Primary anchor verse?

**[A]** Mat 6:25 (merimnaō) — the foundational command that opens the longest and most systematic treatment of anxiety in the cluster. 1Pe 5:7 (merimna) — the most direct statement of the cluster's theological resolution: cast all anxieties on God because he cares. **[B]** Ecc 2:20 (ya.ash) — the most explicit statement of despair as a volitional act, the heart given up. 2Cor 1:8 (exaporeō) — the most extreme statement of despair as circumstantial overwhelm. **[C]** Luk 18:1 (ekkakeō) — the parable's stated purpose is the prevention of this characteristic; the most direct framing of the characteristic's risk and remedy. **[D]** Jam 1:8 (dipsuchos) — the most direct definition of the double-minded person and its consequence. Mat 14:31 (distazō) — the most immediate divine encounter with the doubter.

**T7.2.6** — What does the primary anchor reveal that no other verse reveals?

**[A]** 1Pe 5:7 reveals what no other M20-A verse reveals: that the transfer of anxiety is grounded in God's own care-character (melo — he cares). The anxiety is not merely resolved through the person's effort of release; the transfer is possible because God is characterised by attentive care for the person. This is the theological foundation of M20-A's resolution. **[B]** Ecc 2:20 reveals what no other verse reveals: that ya.ash is the heart's own act — the person is not overwhelmed into despair but gives the heart up. The volitional dimension of despair is most precisely named here. **[C]** Luk 18:1 reveals what no other verse reveals: that the specific link between losing heart and stopping prayer means the characteristic's prevention is identical to the maintenance of the prayer relationship. The most precise statement of what ekkakeō destroys (prayer) and what prevents it (persistence in prayer). **[D]** Jam 1:8 reveals what no other verse reveals: that double-mindedness produces instability "in all his ways" — the totality of the consequence is uniquely named here. No other verse shows the comprehensive, all-domains instability that dipsuchos produces.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Which human science framework is most useful?

**[A]** Clinical psychology — anxiety research (Beck, Emery, Borkovec, Dugas). The cognitive model of anxiety (threat appraisal + insufficient coping resources) maps directly onto M20-A's profile: the person perceives threat and does not appraise their own resources (or God's provision) as sufficient. Borkovec's verbal-cognitive characterisation of worry maps onto merimnaō's ruminative-attentional dimension. **[B]** Clinical psychology — depression and hopelessness research (Seligman, Abramson, Beck). The hopelessness triad (negative view of self, world, and future) maps onto ya.ash's cognitive structure. Seligman's learned helplessness maps onto the accumulated-futility dimension of ya.ash (1Sa 27:1: Saul will despair of seeking David — the pursuit has been unproductive long enough). **[C]** Motivational psychology — learned helplessness for the depletion dimension (oligopsuchos, ekkakeō's long-haul fatigue); relational psychology for the inflicted-discouragement dimension (athumeō, ka.eh). Beck's behavioural activation (restoration of small effective action) maps onto the ekkakeō-remedy of persistent doing-good (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13). **[D]** Cognitive psychology — ambivalence and decision research (Festinger, Priester and Petty, Kahneman). Festinger's cognitive dissonance maps onto dipsuchos: holding two incompatible commitments (God and world) produces the psychological discomfort named as double-mindedness. Priester and Petty's ambivalence research (simultaneous positive and negative evaluation of the same object producing instability) maps precisely onto the double-minded person whose evaluation of God and the world is simultaneously positive in both directions.

**T7.3.2** — Where science illuminates the verse evidence?

**[A]** Borkovec's identification of worry as verbal-linguistic and future-oriented illuminates Mat 6:34 ("anxious about tomorrow") — the futility argument and the verbal-rumination character of merimnaō are directly supported. Dugas's intolerance-of-uncertainty model illuminates why merimnaō is addressed with the certainty of God's provision (the birds are fed; tomorrow has its own trouble) — the anxiety is produced by the refusal to trust in an uncertain situation, and the remedy addresses the uncertainty directly. **[B]** Beck's hopelessness triad illuminates ya.ash's cognitive structure: the despairing person's declaration "it is hopeless" reflects negative views of self (nothing better for me — 1Sa 27:1), world (toil is futile — Ecc 2:20), and future (I have loved foreigners and after them I will go — Jer 2:25). **[C]** The science's identification of behavioural activation as the primary depression-intervention maps onto ekkakeō's counter-commands: do not stop doing good (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13), do not stop praying (Luk 18:1). Restoration of small effective action is the mechanism the verse evidence also uses. **[D]** Priester and Petty's ambivalence research illuminates dipsuchos with precision: the double-minded person's instability (Jam 1:8) is the psychological signature of simultaneous strong evaluations in two conflicting directions — exactly what the science predicts for ambivalence.

**T7.3.3** — Where verse evidence and science diverge?

**[A]** Science treats anxiety as value-neutral and addresses it through cognitive reappraisal or mindfulness — techniques that restructure the person's own cognitive processing. The verse evidence grounds anxiety's resolution in God's actual care and faithfulness (1Pe 5:7; Phili 4:6-7) — the resolution is not cognitive restructuring but theological orientation toward a relational reality external to the person. The ground of peace in Phili 4:7 ("peace of God which surpasses understanding") explicitly exceeds what cognitive science can provide. **[B]** Science treats despair (hopelessness) primarily as a cognitive distortion (global, stable, internal attributions that are inaccurate) and addresses it through attribution reappraisal. The verse evidence presents despair as sometimes an accurate assessment that is nonetheless resolved through theological reality beyond the circumstances (God who raises the dead — 2Cor 1:9). The resurrection as the ground of hope is not a cognitive reappraisal of circumstances; it is an ontological claim that the circumstances do not have the final word. **[C]** Science addresses learned helplessness through behavioural activation and restoration of perceived agency. The verse evidence adds theological grounding as the primary resource: mercy received (2Cor 4:1), inner renewal (2Cor 4:16), promised harvest (Gal 6:9). The verse evidence does not rely on the person's restored perception of their own agency but on God's sustaining provision from outside the person. **[D]** Science's ambivalence research addresses double-mindedness through post-decisional commitment — choosing one option and acting on it. The verse evidence addresses dipsuchos through heart-purification (Jam 4:8) — a deeper change than behavioural decision-making. The difference: science works at the cognitive-behavioural level; Scripture calls for a transformation of the heart's fundamental orientation, which is a deeper change of the whole inner person.

**T7.3.4** — Does science surface aspects not yet addressed by the verse evidence?

**[A]** The intolerance-of-uncertainty construct surfaces a question not fully addressed in the M20-A verse evidence: does the cluster's anxiety reflect specifically the inability to tolerate not knowing? The verse evidence addresses the content of anxiety (provision, tomorrow, many things) but does not name the underlying disposition toward uncertainty. G — requires further verse investigation to determine whether intolerance of uncertainty (as a disposition) is addressed in the cluster's evidence or must be referred to M19 (Trust). **[B]** The role of communal hope resources in buffering individual despair (noted in the science extract as a consistent finding) surfaces a question: does the verse evidence show communal context functioning as a despair-buffer? The evidence shows Paul's individual experience of despair-and-resolution (2Cor 1:8) and his prophetic confrontation of communal despair (Jer passages), but does not explicitly show community buffering individual despair. G — this may require cross-cluster investigation with the ecclesiology-community vocabulary. **[D]** The science's distinction between trait ambivalence (dispositional, like dipsuchos) and state ambivalence (situational, like distazō) maps exactly onto the cluster's own distinction between its two M20-D terms. The science confirms the analytical value of the split proposed in Phase 4 — retaining distazō and dipsuchos together but acknowledging their different temporal registers.
