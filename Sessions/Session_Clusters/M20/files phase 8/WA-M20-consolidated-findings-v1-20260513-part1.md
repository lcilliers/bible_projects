## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A — Anxiety and Worry]** The anxiety-faculty is shown as the capacity for directed attentive concern — the inner ability to fix attention on something of value and be troubled by its welfare. 1Pe 5:7 reveals God's character most directly in relation to this: "he cares for you" (merimna cast onto God) — the command to transfer anxiety is grounded in God's active, attentive care. The same term (merimna) that names human anxiety about valued things names God's disposition toward the human person. This suggests the anxiety-faculty at its root images God's caring attentiveness — anxiety is the misdirected form of a faculty whose proper direction is the same as God's own orientation toward what he values.

**[B — Despair and Hopelessness]** 2Cor 4:8 — "perplexed but not driven to despair" — is grounded in the whole Pauline argument that the treasure is in jars of clay, showing that God deliberately places his power in weak vessels. The implication: God is never in despair about the outcomes his word will achieve. The cluster's despair vocabulary has no divine counterpart — God does not despair. The evidence reveals this characteristic as the antithesis of divine faithfulness, not its image.

**[C — Discouragement and Loss of Heart]** 2Cor 4:1 — "having this ministry by the mercy of God, we do not lose heart" — grounds the absence of discouragement in God's mercy. The implied divine character: God's mercy is the inexhaustible resource that makes sustained inner resolve possible. Luk 18:1-8 (the widow and the judge) presents God as the one who will "give justice speedily" — the parable grounds not-losing-heart in God's character as a just, attentive, and responsive Father who is not indifferent. God does not grow weary in his purposes.

**[D — Doubt and Indecision]** Mat 14:31 — Jesus directly addresses Peter's doubt, then grasps his hand — revealing that God in Christ does not condemn the doubter but reaches toward them. Jam 1:5 (context for Jam 1:8 dipsuchos) presents God as giving wisdom "generously to all without reproach" — his character is characterised by single-minded generosity that does not withhold from those who are double-minded. **[CLUSTER]** Across all sub-groups, the cluster's vocabulary is uniformly negative in the divine portrait — none of the M20 characteristics are attributed to God. Instead, each sub-group reveals God's character as the structural opposite: attentive care (against anxiety), faithful forward-orientation (against despair), inexhaustible mercy (against discouragement), single-minded generosity (against indecision).

---

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?

**[A]** No. merimnaō and merimna are not attributed to God. The closest approach is 1Pe 5:7 (he cares for you — melo, not merimnaō) — a different verb. **[B]** No. ya.ash and exaporeō are never attributed to God. **[C]** No. ekkakeō, ka.ah, ka.eh, oligopsuchos, athumeō are not attributed to God. **[D]** No. distazō and dipsuchos are not attributed to God. **[CLUSTER]** Scripture does not attribute any M20 characteristic to God. Silence is uniform and analytically significant — these characteristics are consistently human-condition vocabulary, not divine-nature vocabulary.

---

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**[CLUSTER — all sub-groups]** The silence is consistent and instructive. M20 names characteristics of the human person in their fragility, limitation, and fallenness — states that arise from finitude (not knowing the outcome, not having sufficient strength, not knowing which way to go) and from sin (using despair to justify rebellion, failing to trust what is reliable). God has none of these limitations. The silence suggests these characteristics are not part of the divine image in the sense of being reflected qualities — they are creaturely conditions that arise when the image-bearer fails to trust the one whose image they bear. The positive faculty underlying each (attentive care, hope, resolve, single-mindedness) is divine; the M20 forms are its failure or misdirection.

---

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**[A — Anxiety and Worry]** The evidence from 2709-003 (merimnaō in the positive-care register) is decisive: the anxiety-faculty serves the created purpose of directed attentive concern — equipping the person to invest their inner attention in what is genuinely valued (the Lord's things, others' welfare). 1Cor 7:32-34 shows the unmarried person merimnaō-ing about the Lord's things — the faculty directed as it was designed to be directed. The faculty that becomes anxiety when misdirected is the same faculty that constitutes love and devotion when rightly directed. Created purpose: attentive care.

**[B]** S — The despair vocabulary does not surface a positive created-purpose register. The closest is the ya.ash-resistance (Isa 57:10: not saying "it is hopeless") — but this names the absence of despair, not a positive purpose. Despair appears to be a response to fallen conditions, not a created faculty with a positive purpose.

**[C]** The discouragement vocabulary similarly does not surface a positive created-purpose register. The ekkakeō evidence (Luk 18:1 — pray and not lose heart) implies that the person's inner capacity for sustained engagement (prayer, doing good) is the created faculty; ekkakeō is its failure. The created purpose implied is sustained, persistent orientation in prayer and goodness.

**[D]** S — distazō and dipsuchos do not surface a created-purpose positive register. Double-mindedness and situational doubt are not presented as created faculties with positive purposes.

---

**T0.2.2** — Is this characteristic part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A]** Both. The anxiety-faculty (directed attentive concern) is part of created design — the capacity to care about what matters. Anxiety as misdirected worry (material provision, tomorrow, many things) is a response to the fallen condition, specifically to living in a world of genuine scarcity and uncertainty without perfect trust in God. **[B]** Response to fallen condition — despair arises from overwhelming circumstances, from the collapse of hope in a world where loss is real. **[C]** Response to fallen condition — discouragement arises from sustained opposition, suffering, and the failure to see the fruit of long-haul effort in a world where evil is real and resistance is real. **[D]** Response to fallen condition — both distazō and dipsuchos are failures of trust and commitment that would not arise if the person related to God with complete, undivided trust from the beginning.

---

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**[A]** Phili 4:6-7 — the remedy for anxiety is prayer "and the peace of God which surpasses understanding will guard your hearts." The anxiety-faculty, when resolved through prayer, anticipates a peace that is eschatologically grounded. **[B]** S — the despair vocabulary does not explicitly orient toward eschatological fullness. The Pauline hope (2Cor 4:8 — not driven to despair) is grounded in present resurrection power but does not name a future fullness of despair's absence. **[C]** Gal 6:9 — "in due season we will reap" — the promise of a future harvest is the grounds for not losing heart now. ekkakeō is specifically counteracted by eschatological promise. **[D]** S — the doubt vocabulary does not orient toward eschatological fullness explicitly.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image?

**[A]** The anxiety-faculty (directed attentive concern) expresses the divine image's relational attentiveness — the image-bearer's capacity to orient the inner person toward what is genuinely valued and be troubled by its welfare, as God himself is attentive to those he loves. When misdirected it becomes anxiety; when rightly directed it is the faculty of love and care (1Cor 12:25; Phili 2:20). **[B, C, D]** S — the despair, discouragement, and doubt characteristics do not express the divine image; they represent its impairment or failure under fallen conditions.

---

**T0.3.2** — Shared between God and human person, or exclusively creaturely analogue?

**[A]** The underlying faculty (caring attentiveness) is shared — God cares (1Pe 5:7). The anxiety form is exclusively creaturely. **[B, C, D]** The M20 forms (despair, discouragement, doubt) are exclusively creaturely — arising from finitude and fallenness with no divine counterpart. **[CLUSTER]** The cluster contains one faculty with a divine counterpart (attentive concern — M20-A's positive register) and three characteristics that are purely creaturely.

---

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**[A]** Anxiety's presence reveals the image-bearer operating with the caring-attentiveness faculty misdirected — loving the right things (provision, safety, others) but without the trust in God that would resolve the anxiety. The anxiety-faculty itself is the image working; its misdirection reveals the image's fallenness. **[B]** Despair's presence reveals the collapse of hope — the image-bearer unable to sustain forward-orientation toward God's redemptive purposes. **[C]** Discouragement's presence reveals the inner person's resolve failing under sustained pressure — the image-bearer's created persistence not holding without renewal from outside. **[D]** Doubt and double-mindedness reveal the image-bearer failing to commit to the one in whose image they are made — the divided loyalty of dipsuchos is precisely the failure to orient the whole self toward God as the image-original.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme and what does the name signal?

**[A]** Anxiety and Worry. The name signals both the affective dimension (anxiety — the felt state of distress) and the cognitive-behavioural dimension (worry — the repetitive mental engagement with anticipated threat). The conjunction "and" acknowledges the duality within a single sub-group. **[B]** Despair and Hopelessness. Names the state (despair) and its cognitive structure (hopelessness — the absence of hope). **[C]** Discouragement and Loss of Heart. Names both the general condition (discouragement) and its most evocative scriptural expression (loss of heart — ekkakeō's literal register). **[D]** Doubt and Indecision. Names the epistemological dimension (doubt — uncertainty about what is true or reliable) and the volitional dimension (indecision — failure to commit).

---

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at definitional level?

**[A]** merimna / merimnaō (Greek): from merizo (to divide) — anxiety as the divided inner person, attention split across concerns. da.ag (Hebrew): root meaning unclear but consistently associated with anxious concern for valued persons or objects — worry as preoccupied attention. The Greek root (division) and the Hebrew usage (anxious concern for specific valued things) together define the sub-group as the inner person's attentional capacity divided or misdirected under threat. **[B]** ya.ash (Hebrew): consistently means to despair, to give up, to declare hopeless — strong volitional and verbal dimensions. exaporeō (Greek): ek (out from) + aporeō (to be without resource) — to be completely resourceless, beyond one's capacity to cope. Together: OT despair is active and volitional (giving up); NT despair is passive and circumstantial (overwhelmed). **[C]** ekkakeō (Greek): ek (out) + kakos (evil/bad) — to be exhausted out of courage, to give out. ka.ah (Hebrew verb) / ka.eh (Hebrew adjective): to be faint, disheartened, crushed — a state of inner depletion. oligopsuchos: oligos (little/few) + psuchē (soul) — small-souled. athumeō: a (without) + thumos (spirit/passion) — without inner energy. **[D]** distazō (Greek): from dis (twice, double) — to be of two minds, to waver. dipsuchos: dis (double) + psuchē (soul) — two-souled. The Greek terms for both doubt and double-mindedness carry the same "double/division" root structure — unifying the sub-group at the etymological level around inner division.

---

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[A]** Directional: anxiety implies misoriented direction — the attentional faculty turned toward threat rather than toward God. Relational: the remedy in every case is relational — cast on God (1Pe 5:7), pray to God (Phili 4:6), trust God's provision (Mat 6:26). **[B]** Directional: despair implies forward-orientation collapsed — the person can no longer face what is ahead. **[C]** Constitutional: loss of heart is an explicitly inner-person constitutional language — the heart as the seat of courage and resolve losing its capacity. **[D]** Constitutional: both terms (distazō, dipsuchos) carry division within the self as the constitutional implication — the self is not unified.

---

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**[A]** A condition with volitional dimension — anxiety descends as a condition (thorns choking, heart weighed down — 350-001, Luk 21:34) but also can be commanded away from (Mat 6:25, Phili 4:6), implying the person participates in maintaining it. Mixed: primarily a condition, with a volitional secondary register. **[B]** M20-B contains both a condition (exaporeō — overwhelmed beyond capacity; 2Cor 1:8) and an act (ya.ash — the heart given up, the declaration made; Ecc 2:20, Jer 2:25). The sub-group spans a condition and an act. **[C]** Primarily a condition — discouragement and loss of heart are states the person finds themselves in, produced by external causes (opposition, provocation, suffering). The commands not to lose heart (ekkakeō) imply some volitional resistance is possible, but the primary register is a condition received. **[D]** Mixed: distazō is a momentary condition (doubt arising in a specific encounter); dipsuchos is a dispositional quality (the settled state of the double-minded person).

---

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple or does it have constituent elements?

**[A]** Complex — M20-A has at minimum three constituent dimensions: (1) anxiety as choking weight (350-001), (2) anxiety as misdirected attentional preoccupation (2709-001, M20-A-NEW-04), (3) the anxiety-faculty rightly directed as care (2709-003, M20-A-NEW-02). These are related but distinct inner-being phenomena. **[B]** Complex — two distinct profiles: circumstantial despair-as-overwhelm (exaporeō) and volitional despair-as-surrender/declaration (ya.ash). **[C]** Complex — five terms across five distinct causal contexts. **[D]** Simple in structure but with two temporal registers: situational (distazō) and dispositional (dipsuchos).

---

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description?

**[A — Anxiety and Worry]** A condition of the inner person in which the faculty of directed attentive concern is misdirected toward anticipated threat, material need, or uncertain outcomes — producing a state of preoccupied, scattered, or weighted inner orientation that competes with trust in God. The same faculty, rightly directed toward God and others, constitutes love and devotion.

**[B — Despair and Hopelessness]** The collapse of the inner person's forward-orientation — whether as overwhelming circumstance exceeding the person's endurance (exaporeō) or as the active surrendering of hope, the giving up of pursuit, or the verbal declaration that return is impossible (ya.ash). The inner person can no longer face what is ahead.

**[C — Discouragement and Loss of Heart]** The inner person's courage and resolve diminishing under sustained external pressure — opposition, suffering, provocation, or temporal frustration — producing a state of depleted inner energy that requires external encouragement, theological grounding, or inner renewal to recover.

**[D — Doubt and Indecision]** The inner person failing to maintain a unified, committed orientation — either in a specific encounter moment when faith wavers between seeing and trusting (distazō) or as a settled dispositional state in which the person does not commit and is therefore unstable in all ways (dipsuchos).

---

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite?

**[A]** amerimnos (untroubled, G0275 — related word in M20-A lexical data) — the state of being free from merimna. More substantially: peace (eirēnē, Phili 4:7) and trust/faith (pistis). The structural opposite named within the VCGs: Jer 17:8 — the tree planted by water that is not anxious in drought; rootedness in God as the condition that produces the opposite of da.ag anxiety. **[B]** Hope (elpis) — the forward-orientation that despair extinguishes. Isa 57:10 names it: "you found new life for your strength, and so you were not faint" — the energised, hopeful continuation as the opposite of the despair-declaration. **[C]** Courage (tharseō), perseverance, the parakaleō (encouragement) that restores the fainthearted. Within the VCG evidence: 2Cor 4:16 names it — the inner person being renewed day by day as the opposite of losing heart. **[D]** Single-mindedness — the undivided heart and whole-hearted orientation toward God. Jam 4:8 implies it: "purify your hearts" — the purified, unified heart as the opposite of double-mindedness.

---

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A]** Trust in God's provision (Mat 6:26-30 — the birds and lilies argument), peace (Phili 4:7), fruitfulness (350-001 — anxiety chokes the word). **[B]** Hope and the declaration of hope — Isa 57:10 evidences this directly: the person who does not say "it is hopeless" finds renewed strength; ya.ash and hopeful perseverance are mutually exclusive. **[C]** Persistent prayer (Luk 18:1 — the person who loses heart stops praying), sustained doing of good (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13). **[D]** Stable receiving from God — Jam 1:7 (context) states the double-minded person "must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord." Double-mindedness excludes effective reception from God.

---

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end?

**[A]** Anxiety is not fear (M01) — it is future-oriented and cognitive rather than acute and subcortical. Anxiety is not sorrow (M19 territory) — it is forward-oriented worry, not backward-looking grief. The da.ag-as-contrition verse (Psa 38:18) shows the border: anxiety can fuel sorrow over sin, but the contrition itself is a different characteristic. **[B]** Despair is not grief or sorrow — it is the collapse of hope and forward-orientation, not mourning of what has been lost (though they can coexist). Despair ends where hope begins to revive. **[C]** Discouragement is not depression — it is the failure of sustained effort-based engagement, not the comprehensive inner collapse of despair. ekkakeō appears in contexts where the person is still engaging (praying, doing good) but losing the energy to continue — not the state of having given up. **[D]** Doubt (distazō) is not unbelief — Mat 28:17 shows doubt and worship coexisting; doubt is the incomplete move from seeing to trusting, not the rejection of what is seen. Indecision (dipsuchos) is not neutral ambivalence — it is a morally loaded state of divided allegiance that excludes receiving from God.

---

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A]** Three modes evidenced: (1) Choking/weighting — anxiety operates as a passive force that descends on the inner person and strangles spiritual receptivity (350-001); (2) Attentional scattering — anxiety proliferates across many things, fracturing the inner person's concentration (M20-A-NEW-04, Luk 10:41); (3) Directed preoccupation — anxiety fixes attention on specific objects: material provision (2709-001), anticipated performance (M20-A-NEW-03), absent loved ones (M20-A-NEW-05), concrete threats (M20-A-NEW-06). **[B]** Two modes: (1) Overwhelm-from-without — exaporeō as the condition produced when circumstances exceed the inner person's endurance; (2) Surrender-from-within — ya.ash as the act of giving up, declaring hopeless, or weaponising hopelessness; the inner person participates actively in its own despair. **[C]** Three modes: (1) Inflicted — discouragement produced by another's action (provocation, cruelty, deceptive speech: athumeō, ka.ah/ka.eh in C-C1 and C-C2); (2) Long-haul fatigue — ekkakeō as the accumulated failure of sustained effort; (3) Soul-diminishment — oligopsuchos as the small-souled state of depleted inner capacity. **[D]** Two modes: (1) Situational wavering — distazō as faith faltering in a specific encounter moment; (2) Dispositional indecision — dipsuchos as the settled state of chronic non-commitment.

---

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A]** Yes. M20-A operates differently depending on direction of the faculty: misdirected toward temporal objects → anxiety; rightly directed toward God and others → love and devotion (2709-003). The same constitutional faculty at the same level produces opposite inner-being outcomes depending on the direction of its object. **[B]** Yes. exaporeō is circumstance-driven (direction: inward, from overwhelming circumstances pressing on the inner person); ya.ash is volitionally-driven (direction: outward, the inner person actively declaring and closing off). **[C]** Yes: context-dependent (opposition, provocation, fatigue each produce different manifestations of the same discouragement characteristic). **[D]** Yes: temporal scale differs — distazō situational and acute; dipsuchos dispositional and chronic.

---

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?

**[A]** Yes — Martha's complaint (Luk 10:41: "Martha, Martha, you are anxious...") is named by Jesus in speech addressed to her. The anxiety is named by the one who observes it, not declared by the one who experiences it. da.ag's anxious question (1Sa 10:2: "What shall I do about my son?") is a speech-act of relational anxiety. **[B]** Strong yes — ya.ash has a prominent speech-based mode: the declaration "it is hopeless" (Isa 57:10; Jer 2:25) and "that is in vain" (Jer 18:12). Despair declares itself and in declaring, hardens itself. Job 6:26 names the reverse: the speech of the despairing man is "wind" — a quality of output that is characteristically produced by the state. **[C]** S — the discouragement vocabulary does not have a primary speech-based mode. The commands not to lose heart (ekkakeō) are speech addressed to the discouraged, not declarations by them. **[D]** S — the doubt vocabulary does not have a primary speech-based mode. Doubt appears in action (Peter sinking) and observation (Mat 28:17: "some doubted") but not in self-declaration.

---

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response?

**[A]** The choking/weighting mode produces a condition that descends on the inner person — there is no described immediate response because the person is already in the condition when we encounter them. In the anticipatory-anxiety verses (Mat 6:25ff), the immediate response to threat is anxiety itself (the preoccupied turning of attention toward the threatening object). **[B]** For exaporeō: despair of life itself (2Cor 1:8) — the immediate response to circumstances exceeding capacity is the collapse of the will to continue. For ya.ash: the giving up of the heart (Ecc 2:20) — the immediate response to accumulated futility is the surrender of inner engagement. **[C]** The immediate response to provoking-action is discouragement (Col 3:21 — provoke → become discouraged; the causal chain is immediate). For ekkakeō in sustained-effort contexts, the response is not immediate but accumulated. **[D]** For distazō: doubt arises immediately in the presence of the threatening stimulus (the wind and waves in Mat 14:30-31) — Peter's faith-wavering is simultaneous with his perception of danger.

---

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent?

**[A]** Varies by mode — choking descends without a described immediate response; preoccupied anxiety is the immediate response to perceived threat. **[B]** Consistent for exaporeō (overwhelm); varies for ya.ash (volitional — can be sudden surrender or accumulated declaration). **[C]** Consistent for inflicted discouragement (immediate); inconsistent for long-haul fatigue (accumulated). **[D]** Consistent for distazō (situational, immediate).

---

**T1.5.3** — Where silent on immediate response, note explicitly.

**[C]** The discouragement vocabulary for ka.ah/ka.eh does not specify an immediate temporal response — the state is described but its onset is not sequenced. **[D]** For dipsuchos, no immediate response is described — the characteristic is dispositional, not event-triggered.

---

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A]** Unfruitfulness — Luk 8:14 (anxiety chokes until fruitlessness); the word is heard but does not mature. Dulled alertness — Luk 21:34 (heart weighed down, that day comes suddenly like a trap). The sustained effect of anxiety is the progressive displacement of the word's productive work in the person. **[B]** Spiritual abandonment — Jer 2:25 (the sustained posture of despair closes off repentance; "I have loved foreigners, and after them I will go" — the declaration sustained as a life-orientation). For exaporeō, the sustained effect is the depletion of life-force: 2Cor 1:8 (despaired of life itself). **[C]** The failure to pray (Luk 18:1 — the one who loses heart stops praying); the cessation of sustained doing of good (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13). Sustained discouragement produces withdrawal from active engagement — the person stops doing the thing they were doing when the discouragement came. **[D]** Total instability — Jam 1:8 ("unstable in all his ways"); the sustained effect of double-mindedness is the destabilisation of all of life's orientation, not merely one decision.

---

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained possession establish?

**[A]** A person habitually anxious about many things (Luk 10:41 — Martha pattern extended) loses the capacity for the one-thing orientation that spiritual receptivity requires. The sustained anxious state forms a person whose inner attention is habitually scattered. **[B]** Sustained despair closes off the capacity for repentance (Jer 2:25; 18:12) — the person loses access to the inner posture of turning. **[C]** Sustained discouragement produces a soul that is chronically small (oligopsuchos) — habitually diminished in inner courage, habitually requiring external encouragement to engage. **[D]** Sustained double-mindedness produces chronic instability — a person who cannot hold a settled orientation in any domain.

---

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**[A]** Yes — the immediate response is anxious preoccupation; the sustained effect is unfruitfulness and dulled alertness. The second is more destructive than the first. **[B]** Yes for ya.ash — the immediate surrender (Ecc 2:20) develops into a closed posture (Jer 2:25; 18:12) that forecloses transformation. The sustained effect of despair is the hardening of the closed inner orientation. **[C]** Yes — the immediate response is loss of heart in a specific context (prayer, doing good); the sustained effect is withdrawal from active engagement and the formation of a chronically small-souled person. **[D]** For dipsuchos: the characteristic is already dispositional (chronic) at first encounter — the sustained effect (instability in all ways) is its inherent consequence.

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions enable genuine reception?

*Note: these prompts ask about reception of the characteristic — what enables the characteristic to take hold in the person. This is asking for conditions of vulnerability, not conditions of cure.*

**[A]** Anxiety is received when the inner person's attentional faculty is directed toward objects it cannot secure or control (material provision, future outcomes, others' welfare in their absence) and when trust in God's provision is absent or thin. The conditions of reception: real or perceived threat + insufficient trust. The Jer 17:8 structural opposite reveals this by negation: rootedness in God prevents anxiety even under genuine threat — meaning lack of rootedness is the condition that enables it. **[B]** Despair is received when circumstances genuinely exceed the inner person's endurance (2Cor 1:8), or when the person reaches the accumulated conclusion that their efforts have been futile (1Sa 27:1; Ecc 2:20), or when the self-serving utility of despair-language is available (Jer 18:12 — the people's posture). **[C]** Discouragement is received when sustained effort meets no visible fruit (ekkakeō contexts), when a trusted authority inflicts it through deceptive speech (ka.eh, Eze 13:22), or when relational provocation is experienced (athumeō, Col 3:21). **[D]** Doubt is received when circumstances challenge the reliability of what is trusted (Peter on the water — Mat 14:30), or when a person has never settled into single-minded commitment to begin with (dipsuchos).

---

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[A]** Rootedness in God (Jer 17:8 — the tree planted by water), peace received through prayer (Phili 4:6-7), single-minded focus (Mary's one-thing orientation contra Martha's many-things). **[B]** Active hope — "you found new life for your strength and were not faint" (Isa 57:10). The person who maintains forward-orientation blocks the declaration of hopelessness. Pauline theological grounding: "having this ministry by the mercy of God" (2Cor 4:1) — the theological certainty of received mercy blocks ekkakeō. **[C]** God's mercy (2Cor 4:1), inner renewal (2Cor 4:16), the promise of future harvest (Gal 6:9). **[D]** Whole-heartedness — the purified heart (Jam 4:8) that blocks double-mindedness; the experience of Jesus's reliable action in crisis (Mat 14:31 — the hand grasped immediately) that forms trust against future doubt.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A]** The non-anxious person in threatening circumstances is described in Jer 17:8 — leaves remain green, fruit continues. The state is one of sustained fruitfulness and alertness under pressure. **[B]** The person who encounters despair-circumstances and does not receive it is described in Isa 57:10 — wearied but found new strength, "and so were not faint." The non-despair state in the face of genuine cause for despair is wearied-but-renewed perseverance. In 2Cor 4:8 — "perplexed but not driven to despair" — the state is pressed but held. **[C]** The person who does not lose heart in sustained effort (ekkakeō context) is described as continuing to pray (Luk 18:1), continuing to do good (Gal 6:9), experiencing inner renewal even as the outer wastes away (2Cor 4:16). **[D]** The person who does not fall into situational doubt (distazō) completes the move from seeing to trusting — the disciples who worshipped without doubting at Mat 28:17 are the implied contrast.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension?

**[A]** Affective-attentional — the characteristic is primarily located in the inner person's capacity for directed feeling and attention. The cognitive dimension (thought about threat) is present but secondary. **[B]** Volitional-affective — ya.ash involves the will (giving up, declaring); exaporeō is affective (collapse of inner endurance). The two terms carry different primary dimensions, but the volitional register of ya.ash is the most analytically distinctive finding. **[C]** Affective-agency — the inner energy that sustains action (prayer, doing good, perseverance) fails; the primary dimension is the loss of the affective-energy foundation for sustained agency. **[D]** Cognitive-volitional — distazō is primarily cognitive (epistemic wavering); dipsuchos is primarily volitional (dispositional non-commitment). Both involve a failure to settle, which is a volitional act.

---

**T1.8.2** — What evidence supports this classification?

**[A]** The choking and weighting images (350-001, Luk 21:34) locate anxiety in the affective-constitutional domain; the attentional-scattering image (Luk 10:41 — "anxious about many things") locates it in the attentional faculty. **[B]** Ecc 2:20 ("gave my heart up to despair") locates ya.ash's primary dimension in the will; exaporeō in 2Cor 1:8 ("despaired of life itself") locates it in the affective-endurance faculty. **[C]** 2Cor 4:16 ("our inner self is being renewed") locates the inner-person renewal that counteracts ekkakeō — implying the primary dimension is inner-personal energy/resolve. **[D]** Jam 1:8 ("unstable in all his ways") locates the consequence in behavioural-volitional instability; Jam 4:8 ("purify your hearts") locates the remedy in the heart's volitional-affective centre.

---

**T1.8.3** — Secondary dimensions?

**[A]** Cognitive — merimnaō involves sustained thought about anticipated outcomes (the ruminative quality identified in the science extract as Borkovec's verbal-cognitive worry). Relational — da.ag shows anxiety as relational (care for an absent loved one). **[B]** Cognitive — ya.ash's weaponised form (Jer 18:12) involves deliberate cognitive framing (deploying futility-language). Verbal/speech — the declaration mode of ya.ash is a secondary speech-dimension. **[C]** Relational — athumeō (Col 3:21) is produced relationally; ka.ah/ka.eh show discouragement within social vulnerability. **[D]** Relational — dipsuchos's divided allegiance has a relational dimension (divided between God and world). Affective — distazō involves emotional fear (Mat 14:30 — Peter saw the wind and was afraid before he doubted).
