### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**T2.2.1** — Is this characteristic identified as a soul-level phenomenon?

**[C]** Yes, explicitly — oligopsuchos (1Th 5:14) is etymologically soul-level: oligos (little) + psuchē (soul). The fainthearted state is literally the soul diminished in its capacity. This is the clearest soul-level constitutional location in the cluster. **[D]** Yes — dipsuchos (Jam 1:8; 4:8) is etymologically dis (double) + psuchē (soul) — the double-minded state is literally a divided-soul state. The soul is the constitutional location of the divided allegiance. **[A, B]** S — merimna/merimnaō, da.ag, ya.ash, and exaporeō are not explicitly located at the soul level in the verse evidence.

**T2.2.2** — What does soul-level location reveal?

**[C]** oligopsuchos reveals that discouragement at its root is a depletion of soul-capacity — the soul's inner energy is diminished. The constitutional location is the soul itself, not merely its faculties. The remedy (paraklēsis — drawing alongside, 1Th 5:14) addresses the soul by relational proximity, not correction. **[D]** dipsuchos reveals that double-mindedness is a divided soul — the inner person constitutionally split between two orientations. The soul is not merely expressing indecision; it is structurally divided. Jam 4:8's remedy (purify your hearts) locates the solution in the heart, suggesting the heart is the volitional centre through which the divided soul is resolved.

**T2.2.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

**[A, B]** Soul-level location is silent for M20-A and M20-B sub-groups.

### T2.3 — Heart

**T2.3.1** — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?

**[A]** Yes — Luk 21:34: "your hearts be weighed down with cares of this life" — merimna explicitly located in the heart as the weighing-down agent. **[B]** Yes — Ecc 2:20: "gave my heart up to despair" — ya.ash explicitly located in the heart as the organ that is surrendered. **[C]** Yes — ekkakeō is addressed as a heart phenomenon: Luk 18:1 (the whole parable is about not losing heart in prayer), 2Cor 4:16 (inner self renewed — the heart as the inner organ of renewal that prevents ekkakeō). Eze 13:22 — false prophets "disheartened the righteous" — the disheartening is an inner act affecting the heart. **[D]** Yes — Jam 4:8: "purify your hearts, you double-minded" — the heart is the constitutional location where double-mindedness resides and where its resolution must occur. The double-minded person is called to purify the heart, not the mind.

**T2.3.2** — What does heart-location reveal?

**[A]** Anxiety is located in the heart as the organ of orientation — the weighing-down of the heart (Luk 21:34) means the inner person's capacity to be alert and oriented toward what matters is impaired. The heart is the faculty of orientation, and anxiety corrupts it. **[B]** ya.ash's location in the heart reveals that despair is not merely a cognitive conclusion (hopelessness) but a volitional-affective act — the heart is the organ that surrenders. The heart does not merely register despair; it hands itself over. **[C]** The heart is the seat of inner resolve and courage; losing heart (ekkakeō) is losing the heart's capacity to sustain its orientation. The inner renewal (2Cor 4:16) is the heart being sustained against its natural tendency to fail under sustained pressure. **[D]** The heart's role in double-mindedness is volitional-affective — dipsuchos is resolved not by correct thinking (the mind) but by purifying the heart's orientation. The heart is where commitment is formed and where divided allegiance resides.

**T2.3.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

All four sub-groups have heart-location evidence. No silence.

### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**T2.7.1** — Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run?

**[C]** The direction in 2Cor 4:16 runs from the body upward to the soul/heart — or more precisely, the text denies that this direction reaches its apparent terminus. The body wastes away (physical deterioration) but the inner self is renewed (the inner does not follow the outer's trajectory). The characteristic (ekkakeō) is the risk of the soul following the body's declining trajectory; the text says the inner renewal prevents this. Direction: body decline does not necessarily produce inner collapse — but the assumed natural direction (body → soul vulnerability) is the risk the verse names and counters.

**T2.7.2** — What is the consequence of that directionality?

**[C]** The directionality reveals that the body's suffering is a genuine pressure on the inner person's resolve — physical deterioration creates the condition in which ekkakeō is most likely. Inner renewal is required specifically because the body's trajectory exerts downward pressure on the soul's capacity for sustained engagement.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally?

**[A]** Multiple origins evidenced: (a) from within the person — the attentional faculty misdirecting itself toward threatened objects; (b) from external circumstances — threatening situations that trigger the inner response; (c) from relational absence — the parent's anxiety arises from the child's absence (M20-A-NEW-05: 1Sa 9:5, 10:2). The anxiety-faculty is internal; what activates it is external. **[B]** Mixed origin: exaporeō's origin is external (circumstances exceeding capacity — 2Cor 1:8); ya.ash's origin is internal-volitional (the heart giving itself up — Ecc 2:20) and sometimes social (the declaration made in response to prophetic pressure — Jer 18:12). **[C]** Primarily external origin: provocation by another (athumeō — Col 3:21), infliction by cruelty or deceptive speech (ka.eh — Eze 13:22, Psa 109:16), military pressure (Dan 11:30), long-haul temporal frustration (ekkakeō). The origin is consistently external to the inner person. **[D]** Mixed: distazō arises from external threat perceived (Mat 14:30 — the wind and waves, external); dipsuchos appears to be dispositional — the person is already double-minded, with no specific external trigger named in the verse evidence.

**T2.9.2** — Singular or multiple origin?

**[A]** Multiple. **[B]** Multiple — circumstantial (exaporeō) and volitional-dispositional (ya.ash). **[C]** Primarily external but from multiple distinct external sources (provocation, infliction, pressure, fatigue). **[D]** Multiple — situational-external (distazō) and dispositional-internal (dipsuchos).

**T2.9.3** — Does origin change across contexts?

**[A]** Yes — anxiety has different origins in different VCGs: material-provision context (Mat 6:25ff), relational-absence context (1Sa 9:5), concrete-threat context (Jer 38:19). **[B]** Yes — overwhelm (2Cor 1:8) vs accumulated futility (Ecc 2:20) vs weaponised posture (Jer 18:12). **[C]** Yes — five distinct causal-origin contexts across five terms. **[D]** Yes — situational crisis of faith (Mat 14:31) vs settled dispositional state (Jam 1:8).

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels?

**[A]** Evidence suggests downward movement: Luk 21:34 (hearts weighed down → dulled alertness to coming day) — anxiety in the heart impairs the whole person's orientation and action. The word-choking (Mat 13:22; Mar 4:19; Luk 8:14) shows anxiety moving from the inner person outward to produce unfruitfulness — a movement from constitutional location to behavioural consequence. **[B]** Ecc 2:20 (gave my heart up to despair → ceased to find meaning in labour) — despair moves from the heart outward to extinguish engagement with external activity. **[C]** 2Cor 4:16 — the outer person wasting away exerts downward pressure on the inner person's resolve (body → soul pressure). The inner renewal counters this by moving upward (theological reality → inner renewal → sustained outer engagement). **[D]** Jam 1:8 — double-mindedness at the soul level produces instability "in all his ways" — movement from constitutional division outward to behavioural instability across all domains.

**T2.10.2** — Sequence or pattern?

**[A]** Pattern: external threat → anxious attention → inner preoccupation → unfruitfulness / dulled alertness. The movement is inward then outward: threat → inner → consequence. **[C]** Pattern: bodily deterioration → pressure on inner resolve → risk of ekkakeō / inner renewal → sustained engagement. **[D]** Pattern: divided inner orientation (soul level) → instability in all ways (behavioural level). The constitutional division radiates outward to pervade all action.

**T2.10.3** — If no constitutional movement evidenced, note explicitly.

**[B]** Constitutional movement for ya.ash is implied (Ecc 2:20 — heart → ceased engagement with labour) but not sequenced explicitly in the verse evidence beyond the single act of surrender.
