## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty?

**[A]** Yes — indirectly. Luk 21:34: hearts weighed down with cares produce failure to perceive the coming day clearly ("that day come upon you suddenly like a trap"). Anxiety impairs perceptive alertness. Mat 10:19 / Luk 12:11: the disciples are told not to be anxious about what to say — implying that anxiety about the situation could close off the Spirit's provision of perception in that moment. **[B]** S — the despair vocabulary does not directly engage the perceptive faculty. **[C]** S — the discouragement vocabulary does not engage the perceptive faculty explicitly. **[D]** Yes — distazō is engaged by perception: Peter sees the wind and waves (Mat 14:30) — perceptual awareness of threat triggers the doubt. Mat 28:17 — they see Jesus and some doubt; the doubt arises from the act of seeing and failing to complete the perceptual-to-trust movement. Doubt in distazō is specifically post-perceptual — it arises after seeing, not before.

**T3.1.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?

**[A]** Impairs — anxiety dulls alertness to what is coming (Luk 21:34: hearts weighed down → day comes suddenly). **[D]** Complicates — distazō is not a failure to perceive but a failure to move from perception to trust. The perceptive faculty is functioning; the doubt is the failure to complete the movement from seeing to believing.

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[A]** Anxiety impairs perception by filling the inner person with preoccupying concerns that crowd out alertness to God's action and to eschatological realities. **[D]** Doubt engages the perceptive faculty but cannot complete the process — the person is stuck between seeing and trusting.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?

**[A]** Yes — strongly. The merimnaō vocabulary is deeply cognitive: the inner person thinks about provision (Mat 6:25ff: "what shall we eat, drink, wear?"), calculates outcomes (Mat 6:27: "which of you by being anxious can add an hour?"), projects into the future (Mat 6:34: "anxious about tomorrow"). Worry is a cognitive faculty in sustained engagement with anticipated threat. **[B]** Yes — ya.ash's verbal-declaration mode (Jer 2:25; 18:12) involves cognitive conclusions: the person reaches the conclusion "it is hopeless" and speaks it. The cognitive assessment precedes and produces the declaration. **[C]** The long-haul fatigue (ekkakeō) implies cognitive frustration — the person who does not see the harvest (Gal 6:9) is making a cognitive assessment of outcomes. **[D]** Yes — distazō is fundamentally cognitive-evaluative: the person is between two cognitive states (seeing/accepting and doubting). dipsuchos implies two competing cognitive orientations held simultaneously.

**T3.2.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**[A]** Impairs — anxiety traps the cognitive faculty in repetitive, futile engagement with threat outcomes it cannot change ("cannot add a single hour" — Mat 6:27). The cognitive faculty is captured by anxiety and rendered useless for the purpose of changing outcomes. **[B]** Distorts — ya.ash's weaponised form (Jer 18:12) shows the cognitive faculty deploying hopelessness-language strategically, not truthfully. The cognitive faculty is distorted by sin to justify what the person already wants to do. **[D]** Destabilises — dipsuchos prevents stable cognitive orientation by holding two competing frameworks simultaneously; the cognitive output is unstable across all domains (Jam 1:8).

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[A]** Anxiety colonises the cognitive faculty — turning thought from its proper function (discernment, trust-grounded evaluation) to futile threat-projection. The futility argument (Mat 6:27) is the text's counter to this colonisation. **[D]** Both sub-group D terms show the cognitive faculty unable to land on a stable orientation — in distazō because of situational threat, in dipsuchos because of dispositional division.

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[A]** Isa 57:11 (da.ag group) — "whom did you dread and fear, so that you lied, and did not remember me" — anxiety is directly linked to the failure of memory of God. The anxious-person's fear has displaced the memory of God as the ground of security. **[B]** S — despair vocabulary does not directly engage memory. **[C, D]** S — discouragement and doubt vocabulary do not directly engage memory.

**T3.3.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**[A]** Impairs — da.ag anxiety in Isa 57:11 is connected to forgetting God ("did not remember me"). The anxiety is both the symptom and the cause of this memory-failure — the person who does not hold God in active memory is more vulnerable to anxiety; the anxiety, in turn, deepens the forgetting.

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[A]** Anxiety and the failure of God-memory are linked: Isa 57:11 shows that dread and anxiety accompany the failure to remember God, which in turn produces moral failure (lying). This is a finding about M20-A: the cluster's anxiety is in part a memory-disorder — the failure to hold in active recollection the reliable character of God.

**[B, C, D]** S — memory engagement is silent for M20-B, M20-C, and M20-D.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[CLUSTER]** Yes across all sub-groups. Anxiety, despair, discouragement, and doubt are all affective states — they involve the person's feeling-dimension. **[A]** merimna as "weighing down the heart" (Luk 21:34) is affective — a felt heaviness. **[B]** exaporeō ("despaired of life itself" — 2Cor 1:8) is an extreme affective state — the total collapse of the will-to-continue. **[C]** ka.ah/ka.eh ("crushed, brokenhearted, disheartened") are primarily affective — the felt state of inner depletion and sorrow under pressure. **[D]** distazō includes an affective dimension — Peter's doubt arises alongside fear (Mat 14:30: "he saw the wind, was afraid, and began to sink").

**T3.4.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**[A]** Intensifies affect in the distress direction — anxiety generates and sustains affective distress, producing a persistent felt state of worry. The positive register (2709-003: merimnaō as care for others) shows affect directed as love — the same affective faculty expressing love when rightly directed. **[B]** Depletes affect — exaporeō (2Cor 1:8) is the total depletion of affective vitality. ya.ash's heart-surrender (Ecc 2:20) involves the affective collapse of engagement. **[C]** Depletes and dampens affect — discouragement is the loss of the inner energy/affect that sustains active engagement. The fainthearted soul (oligopsuchos — 1Th 5:14) is the affectively depleted state. **[D]** Destabilises affect — fear and doubt arise together in distazō (Mat 14:30-31), showing that the affective faculty is caught between fear and the pull toward trust.

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** M20 is a predominantly affective cluster — the characteristics are felt states that engage and distort the affective faculty across its entire range: from the persistent distress of anxiety (M20-A) through the affective collapse of despair (M20-B) to the depleted affect of discouragement (M20-C) and the affectively destabilised state of doubt (M20-D). The cluster's inner-being territory is the affective domain under pressure.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[A]** Yes — command-structure evidence: Jesus commands the disciples not to be anxious (Mat 6:25, 31, 34; Phili 4:6). The command implies the volitional faculty is engaged — the person can be directed to turn from anxiety. The anxiety-faculty's positive register (2709-003: merimnaō as care for God's things) shows the volitional faculty choosing the object of directed concern. **[B]** Yes — strongly. ya.ash is explicitly volitional: Ecc 2:20 ("gave my heart up") — the will surrenders. Jer 2:25 / 18:12 — the people use the volitional faculty to deploy despair-language as a choice. **[C]** The commands not to lose heart (ekkakeō: Luk 18:1; Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13) address the volitional faculty — the person is called to continue praying and doing good despite loss of heart. The volitional faculty is the site where the battle against ekkakeō is engaged. **[D]** Yes — dipsuchos is fundamentally volitional: the person does not decide, does not commit, hedges. The remedy (purify your hearts — Jam 4:8) is a volitional act of commitment. distazō's command form (Mat 14:31: "why did you doubt?") implies the doubt was something Peter could have not done — volitional capacity is present.

**T3.6.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[A]** Impairs volitional capacity for trust — anxiety traps the will in threat-response rather than freeing it for trust. But the command structure shows volition is not fully overcome — the person retains the capacity to be commanded away from anxiety. **[B]** Severely impairs — ya.ash in its declaration form (Jer 2:25; 18:12) shows the will used against its own best interest, choosing to remain in the closed posture. In this register, the volitional faculty is operating, but perverted — it chooses to stay in despair. **[C]** Impairs sustained volitional engagement — ekkakeō is specifically the failure of the will to continue in long-haul effort. The person still has the volitional capacity to restart (hence the commands), but the energy to sustain is depleted. **[D]** Impairs commitment-capacity — dipsuchos is the failure of the will to settle on an orientation. Distazō shows the will caught between two positions without completing the act of trust.

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** M20 characteristics are consistently associated with volitional impairment — each sub-group names a state in which the volitional faculty is compromised: captured by anxiety, surrendered in despair, depleted by discouragement, or frozen in doubt. The commands not-to (do not be anxious, do not lose heart, do not be double-minded) presuppose residual volitional capacity — the person is not fully overcome but is in a state of impaired will.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?

**[A]** Yes — the choking-of-the-word (Mat 13:22; Luk 8:14) shows anxiety impeding fruitful agency. The person's capacity to produce fruit is strangled by anxiety. **[B]** Yes — ya.ash produces cessation of pursuit (1Sa 27:1: Saul will despair of seeking David), and cessation of engaged work (Ecc 2:20: gave heart up to despair over toil of labours). **[C]** Yes — strongly. The ekkakeō group (Luk 18:1; Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13) shows agency failing: the person stops praying, stops doing good. Discouragement is specifically a failure of sustained agency. Dan 11:30 shows agency-in-retreat — the king withdraws. **[D]** Yes — Jam 1:7: the double-minded person "must not suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord" — dipsuchos renders ineffective the person's agency in seeking from God. The divided person cannot act effectively.

**T3.7.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[A]** Impairs — anxiety prevents fruitful agency (choking, Luk 8:14). The positive register of merimnaō (2709-003) shows what rightly-directed care-agency looks like (Paul caring effectively for the churches — 2Cor 11:28). **[B]** Severely impairs and stops — ya.ash is associated with the cessation of agency. The person gives up pursuit (1Sa 27:1), gives up engaged work (Ecc 2:20), or strategically misdirects agency toward continued rebellion (Jer 18:12 — "we will follow our own plans"). **[C]** Impairs sustained agency — ekkakeō stops prayer and the doing of good; ka.eh produces retreat (Dan 11:30). **[D]** Blocks effective agency toward God — dipsuchos prevents the person from receiving from God (Jam 1:7); the divided person's agency is non-productive in the God-ward direction.

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** M20 characteristics consistently impair agency across all sub-groups. The cluster is constitutively anti-agency — each of its characteristics either stops agency (despair), impairs its fruitfulness (anxiety), depletes its sustaining energy (discouragement), or blocks its effectiveness toward God (doubt/indecision). This is a CLUSTER finding of significant consequence for Session C.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[A]** Yes — Isa 57:11: anxiety produces deception ("whom did you dread and fear, so that you lied?"). The anxious person's moral evaluation is distorted — they choose deception over truthful witness because anxiety drives them. **[B]** Yes — ya.ash's weaponised form (Jer 18:12) shows moral evaluation deployed to construct a justification for sin ("that is in vain, we will follow our own plans"). Despair is used to foreclose the moral challenge of repentance. **[C]** Eze 13:22 — the false prophets' disheartening of the righteous prevents the wicked from turning from their evil way — discouragement functions to impair the moral evaluation of others, keeping them from facing their moral condition. **[D]** Jam 4:8 — the double-minded are addressed alongside sinners ("cleanse your hands, sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded") — double-mindedness is placed in explicit moral-evaluation territory.

**T3.8.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[A]** Impairs — anxiety drives the person to deceive rather than witness truthfully (Isa 57:11). The anxious person's moral evaluation is compromised by self-protective fear. **[B]** Bypasses and perverts — ya.ash weaponised (Jer 18:12) shows the moral evaluation faculty deliberately constructing a justification for sin using despair-language. The faculty is active but in the service of moral evasion. **[C]** The infliction of discouragement on the righteous (Eze 13:22) shows that discouragement can be used to disable the moral evaluation of a third party — the wicked are kept from facing their evil way because the righteous who might have challenged them are disheartened. **[D]** Bypasses — dipsuchos places the person's moral evaluation in a holding pattern — not yet committed, not yet deciding. The purification of the heart (Jam 4:8) is what restores moral evaluation capacity.

**T3.8.3** — Pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** M20 characteristics show consistent adverse effects on moral evaluation: anxiety drives to deception, despair justifies sin, discouragement disables moral challenge to others, and double-mindedness suspends moral commitment. The cluster has a broader moral consequence than its immediate inner-being profile suggests.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[A]** Psa 38:18 (da.ag group VCG 259-002) — "I am sorry for my sin" — da.ag operating as the affective energy of conscience's conviction. The anxious, troubled quality of da.ag fuels the conscience's response to sin. **[B]** ya.ash in Jer 2:25 and 18:12 shows despair being used to silence conscience — the declaration "it is hopeless" forecloses the repentance that conscience would call for. **[C]** Eze 13:22 — false prophets using disheartening speech to prevent the wicked from turning from evil — the discouragement disables conscience-driven repentance in the wicked. **[D]** Jam 4:8 ("purify your hearts, you double-minded") implies the double-minded person's conscience is involved in the remedy — the call to purification is a conscience-engaging command.

**T3.9.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[A]** Enables (in the positive register): da.ag in Psa 38:18 deepens conscience's affective force — the anxious, troubled quality of the feeling drives genuine contrition. **[B]** Impairs — ya.ash silences conscience by deploying hopelessness to foreclose repentance. **[C]** Impairs in others — inflicted discouragement prevents others from responding to conscience's call (Eze 13:22). **[D]** Impairs commitment — dipsuchos prevents the full moral commitment that conscience's conviction calls for.

**T3.9.3** — Pattern reveal?

**[A]** The finding that da.ag can fuel contrition (Psa 38:18) is unique in the cluster — the only instance where an M20 characteristic enables and deepens a positive faculty (conscience's convicting work). All other sub-groups show M20 characteristics impairing conscience-driven responses.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness?

**[A]** Yes — anxiety blocks the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action that conscientiousness requires. The person cannot sustain conscientious engagement because anxiety has colonised the attentional and volitional faculties. The choking-of-the-word (350-001) specifically impairs the fruit-producing integrated response that is the hallmark of conscientiousness. **[B]** ya.ash — the volitional surrender (Ecc 2:20) and the weaponised form (Jer 18:12) both represent failures of conscientiousness: the person does not sustain moral engagement. **[C]** ekkakeō specifically engages conscientiousness — the sustained doing of good (Gal 6:9; 2Th 3:13) is the conscientious-engagement activity that ekkakeō threatens. Losing heart stops the conscientious action. **[D]** dipsuchos — the double-minded person's failure to commit prevents the integrated moral engagement of conscientiousness.

**T3.10.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[C]** Most directly engages and impairs — ekkakeō is specifically the failure of perseverance in conscientious action (doing good, praying, ministry). The commands not to lose heart are commands to maintain conscientiousness.

**T3.10.3** — Pattern?

**[C]** ekkakeō is the M20 term most directly in the domain of conscientiousness — it names the failure of sustained, integrated, morally-engaged action that conscientiousness constitutes. The cluster's most concrete impact on active moral living is through M20-C's domain.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?

**[A]** Yes — relational anxiety (M20-A-NEW-05: da.ag for an absent son) shows the relational capacity in its anxious mode. 2Cor 11:28 (Paul's merimna for the churches) shows relational capacity expressing as pastoral burden. Athumeō (Col 3:21) in M20-C is relational-origin discouragement — produced by a relational act (provocation). **[B]** Isa 57:10-11 (da.ag/ya.ash context) — the relational dimension: dread of the wrong persons (idols, foreigners) rather than God; the relational displacement of trust. **[C]** Multiple relational-origin findings: athumeō (Col 3:21 — provocation), ka.eh (Eze 13:22 — false prophets disheartening), Psa 109:16 (the brokenhearted pursued). The discouragement characteristic has a predominantly relational origin. **[D]** dipsuchos — the divided person is relationally fractured: "draw near to God, and he will draw near to you" (Jam 4:8 context) — the remedy for double-mindedness is relational re-orientation toward God.

**T3.11.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**[A]** The positive merimnaō register (2709-003) shows relational capacity enabled and expressed — merimnaō as directed concern for others constitutes relational capacity in its love-form (1Cor 12:25; Phili 2:20). The negative register impairs relational availability — the anxious person is preoccupied with self-securing. **[C]** Discouragement is both produced by relational damage (provocation, false speech) and resolved through relational action (parakaleō — encouragement). The relational capacity is the site of origin and the site of remedy.

**T3.11.3** — Pattern?

**[C]** M20-C has the strongest relational profile in the cluster — five of its terms are relationally-caused or relationally-resolved. This makes discouragement uniquely relational in both its origin and its remedy. **[CLUSTER]** Across the cluster, M20's relational dimension is significant: the cluster's characteristics are frequently triggered relationally (provocation, absence of loved one, community of despairing persons) and consistently resolved relationally (encouragement, divine care, drawing near to God).

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?

**[A]** Yes — 1Pe 5:7 ("cast your anxieties on him, because he cares for you") shows the divine movement toward the anxious person: God's active care (melo) is the basis for the transfer of anxiety. God's orientation toward the human person is one of attentive care. **[B]** 2Cor 1:8-9 — Paul's despair-of-life is followed by "we felt that we had received the sentence of death. But that was to make us rely not on ourselves but on God who raises the dead" — despair is the context in which God reveals his character as the one who raises the dead; God moves toward the despairing person by providing the theological reality that grounds hope beyond circumstances. **[C]** 2Cor 4:1 — "having this ministry by the mercy of God" — God's mercy is the divine movement toward the potentially-discouraged person; mercy grounds the basis for not losing heart. 2Cor 4:16 — "our inner self is being renewed day by day" — the renewal is divine action, not human effort; God moves toward the ekkakeō-threatened person with daily inner renewal. **[D]** Mat 14:31 — Jesus reaches out his hand to Peter — the most direct divine movement toward the doubter in the entire cluster. God in Christ acts toward the doubting person before they have resolved their doubt.

**T4.1.2** — Basis of God's extension — conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

**[A]** Responsive to the person's anxiety — 1Pe 5:7's command to cast presupposes the person is already anxious; God's care is the relational ground that makes the transfer possible, not a conditional reward for not being anxious. **[B]** Contextual — God's self-revelation as the one who raises the dead (2Cor 1:9) is provided in the context of Paul's extreme despair; it is responsive to the extremity of the situation. **[C]** Covenantal and responsive — "having this ministry by the mercy of God" (2Cor 4:1) grounds the divine provision in God's mercy (covenantal); the daily inner renewal (2Cor 4:16) is ongoing and responsive to sustained outer deterioration. **[D]** Unconditional in action — Jesus grasps Peter's hand while Peter is still doubting, not after doubt is resolved (Mat 14:31).

**T4.1.3** — What does God's extension reveal about his disposition?

**[CLUSTER]** God's disposition toward the M20-experiencing person is consistently one of movement toward, not withdrawal. The anxious person is not condemned but invited to cast (1Pe 5:7); the despairing person receives the ground of resurrection hope (2Cor 1:9); the discouraged person receives daily inner renewal (2Cor 4:16); the doubting person receives the hand of Christ (Mat 14:31). God's character as revealed in M20 contexts: responsive, proximate, sustaining, and ahead of the person's resolution of their characteristic.

**T4.1.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

No silence — God's movement toward the M20-experiencing person is evidenced across all four sub-groups.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic in the human person's movement toward God?

**[A]** Yes — Phili 4:6: "by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God" — the command against anxiety is paired with prayer-movement toward God. The person's response to anxiety is to turn toward God in prayer. 1Pe 5:7 — the act of casting anxieties onto God is itself a movement toward God. **[B]** 2Cor 1:8-9 — Paul's response to despair is the theological recognition that this makes the person rely on God who raises the dead — the movement from despair toward God is mediated by theological reframing, not immediate experiential resolution. **[C]** Luk 18:1 — the parable of the widow and the judge grounds not-losing-heart in persistent prayer toward God. The movement toward God in prayer is specifically the alternative to ekkakeō — prayer and not-losing-heart are the same inner movement. **[D]** Jam 4:8 — "draw near to God and he will draw near to you; cleanse your hands, sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded" — the remedy for dipsuchos is explicitly a movement toward God.

**T4.2.2** — Inner posture required?

**[A]** The posture of release — casting presupposes the person is not holding on. The prayer of Phili 4:6 requires thanksgiving alongside supplication, showing that the movement toward God is not driven by anxiety but by trust that God receives. **[C]** Perseverance — the widow's persistence (Luk 18:1ff) shows that the movement toward God in prayer must be sustained; it is not a single act but a maintained orientation. **[D]** Single-heartedness — "draw near to God" (Jam 4:8) requires a movement that begins with purifying the heart, suggesting that the movement toward God is impossible while the heart remains divided.

**T4.2.3** — What does the human-to-God direction reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** Every M20 sub-group has a God-ward resolution: cast onto God (M20-A), rely on God who raises the dead (M20-B), pray and not lose heart (M20-C), draw near to God (M20-D). The cluster consistently points toward God as the resolution of its characteristics — M20 is not resolved by circumstances changing but by the person's orientation toward God. This is a CLUSTER-level finding of significant consequence.

**T4.2.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

No silence — God-ward movement is present in all four sub-groups.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another?

**[A]** The positive register of merimnaō (2709-003) shows directed care extended by one person toward another: Paul's merimna for the churches (2Cor 11:28), Timothy's concern for the Philippians (Phili 2:20), mutual care in the body (1Cor 12:25). The anxiety-faculty, rightly directed, is the vehicle of love given to others. **[C]** Two distinct giving modes: positive (parakaleō — encouragement given to the discouraged: 1Th 5:14) and negative (discouragement inflicted — athumeō from provocation Col 3:21; ka.eh disheartened by false prophets Eze 13:22). The characteristic can be given in its negative form — one person can impose discouragement on another.

**T4.3.2** — Inner conditions enabling genuine extension?

**[A]** The rightly-directed care (2709-003) requires that the anxiety-faculty be oriented toward the other rather than toward self-securing. The person who gives genuine care-merimnaō is not captured by self-anxiety. **[C]** Encouragement (parakaleō) can be extended by those who are themselves sustained — Paul's encouragement not to lose heart (2Cor 4:16; Eph 3:13) is given from within his own experience of not losing heart through inner renewal. The giver must have received the theological grounding before they can pass it to the discouraged.

**T4.3.3** — What must the giver have received before genuinely giving?

**[C]** The encourager must have received the mercy of God and the experience of inner renewal (2Cor 4:1, 4:16) before they can call others not to lose heart. The command to encourage the fainthearted (1Th 5:14) is given to those who are themselves sustained.

**T4.3.4** — If silent on giving direction, note explicitly.

**[B, D]** The giving direction is not directly evidenced for M20-B and M20-D in the verse data. Despair is not shown being extended by one person to another in the cluster's verse evidence. Doubt is not shown being given or extended in the verse evidence.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another?

**[C]** Yes — strongly. athumeō (Col 3:21): children receive discouragement from parental provocation. ka.eh (Eze 13:22): the righteous receive false disheartening from false prophets. Psa 109:16: the brokenhearted receive cruelty from the wicked. M20-C is the sub-group most clearly showing the characteristic received from another person.

**T4.4.2** — What inner conditions enable or block reception?

**[C]** The righteous who are falsely disheartened (Eze 13:22) do not appear to have specific inner conditions that could block reception — the false prophets' deceptive speech is effective even though the disheartening is false. The vulnerability is not an inner failure of the recipient but a consequence of relational trust or authority.

**T4.4.3** — Inner state of the person who does not receive?

**[C]** S — the verse evidence does not describe the state of the person who encounters provocation or deceptive disheartening but does not receive it. The evidence shows the receiving of discouragement, not its resistance.

**T4.4.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

**[A, B, D]** The receiving direction from another person is silent for M20-A, M20-B, and M20-D — anxiety, despair, and doubt are not shown being received from another person in the verse evidence (though they may have relational triggers, the characteristic is not portrayed as being passed from one person to another in these sub-groups).

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — Does the characteristic operate differently within existing relational bonds vs across distance?

**[A]** The relational-anxiety group (M20-A-NEW-05: 1Sa 9:5; 10:2) shows da.ag operating specifically within a family bond (parent-child). The anxiety is proportional to the relational bond — the father is anxious about the son, not about strangers. **[C]** The provocation-discouragement dynamic (athumeō, Col 3:21) operates within the parent-child family bond — the most intimate human relational context.
