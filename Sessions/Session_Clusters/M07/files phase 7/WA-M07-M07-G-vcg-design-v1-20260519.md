1. **Dismissive contempt as inner attitude toward persons** — the attitude of treating others as of no account; self-righteous dismissal of fellow believers; the devaluing inner stance expressed toward Paul, toward Timothy, toward prophetic speech; God choosing the despised as the divine reversal of human contempt-judgments; the poor shamed at the Lord's Supper through callous disregard

## VCG M07-G-VCG-01 — Dismissive contempt as inner attitude: treating persons as of no account

**Description:** Verses where the contempt-producing-shame mechanism operates primarily through an inner attitude of dismissal — treating others as of no worth or account, despising fellow believers, devaluing ministry, rejecting Spirit-given speech. Exoutheneō is the dominant term across these verses, evidencing the inner disposition of contemptuous dismissal in its full range: self-righteous contempt toward those who abstain (Rom 14:3, 14:10), low esteem for certain members being acted on destructively (1Cor 6:4), contempt toward Paul's bodily presence and words (2Cor 10:10), and the restraint Paul calls for toward Timothy (1Cor 16:11) and toward prophetic utterance (1Th 5:20). God choosing the despised and of no account (1Cor 1:28) is the divine reversal: the world's contempt-judgments of worth are overturned by divine election. The shameful contempt directed at the poor at the Lord's Supper (1Cor 11:22 kataischunō) belongs here — callous disregard as contempt enacted through social exclusion. Cross-register note: contempt (M06) is the source-side of each of these; what M07 holds is the shame produced in the recipient of contempt.

**Anchor:** vc=54643 — 1Cor 1:28 — exoutheneō — "God chooses what is despised and of no account in the world to nullify things that are, showing that divine election deliberately overturns the world's inner judgments of worth." Anchors because the divine reversal most sharply exposes the mechanism: human contempt-judgments assign shame to the despised; God's election overturns those judgments, vindicating the shamed.

## VCG M07-G-VCG-02 — Active contempt expressed through violence, mockery, and rejection

**Description:** Verses where contempt is enacted actively through physical violence against authority-bearers, public mocking, or wilful rejection. The servants struck and treated shamefully in the vineyard (atimazō — Mar 12:4, Luk 20:11); Herod and soldiers expressing dismissive contempt through mocking of Jesus (Luk 23:11); the builders' willful inner dismissal of the cornerstone (Act 4:11 — a decision to reject that God overturned by making him the cornerstone). Bartimaeus throwing off his cloak (Mar 10:50 — apoballō) belongs here: the cloak was the mark of his beggar-identity, a social identity shaped by contempt; throwing it off is the decisive abandonment of that contempt-formed identity at Jesus's call. Cross-register note: the contempt enacted here (M06 mechanism) produces shame in the recipient — borne by the servants, by Jesus, by the rejected stone; what M07 holds is that received face of contempt's shame.

**Anchor:** vc=54638 — Act 4:11 — exoutheneō — "Jesus was rejected by the builders—a willful inner dismissal of his worth—yet God overturned that judgment by making him the cornerstone, vindicating him against human contempt." Most directly evidences the rejection-producing-shame dynamic and its divine reversal: the contempt produced a shame-verdict (rejection, worthlessness) that God's act overturned.

## VCG M07-G-VCG-03 — Verbal contempt and reputation attack as shaming mechanism

**Description:** Verses where shame is produced through speech — verbal attacks on reputation, hostile reviling, slanderous assault, the scoffer's verbal abuse generating dishonour. The inner will is directed toward verbal destruction of the target's standing (loidoria — 1Ti 5:14, 1Pe 3:9); hostile verbal contempt combines unbelief with public assault on Paul's message (kakologeō — Act 19:9); the scoffer's presence generates quarreling and verbal dishonour, ceasing only when he is expelled (qa.lon — Pro 22:10). The appropriate inner response to verbal contempt — not returning reviling with reviling but blessing — is also evidenced here (1Pe 3:9), showing the inner faculty that absorbs rather than retaliates the shame-through-speech. Balaam's madness rebuked by a donkey (2Pe 2:16 afōnos) closes this VCG: the prophet's irrationality is the occasion of reproach from an unexpected source, positioning verbal/vocative shame in its most extreme reversal.

**Anchor:** vc=37633 — 1Ti 5:14 — loidoria — "Younger widows are urged to marry and manage households so as to give opponents no occasion for slander — reviling is an external verbal attack on reputation that right conduct is meant to preempt." Most directly evidences the mechanism: reviling as reputation-attack producing shame; right conduct as the preemptive shield.
