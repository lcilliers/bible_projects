## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame as an experienced inner state reflects God's moral seriousness and his investment in the dignity of persons. The evidence from M07-C is most direct: the God who receives Ezra's shame-before-holiness (Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you") is a God before whom exposure is real and moral standing matters. The repeated Psalm promise that those who trust in God "will not be put to shame" (Psa 22:5; 25:2; 31:1; 119:31; Rom 5:5; 9:33; 10:11) reveals that God is the guardian of the inner dignity of those who rely on him — he takes personal responsibility for protecting them from shame's inner collapse. The M07-B evidence shows God actively deploying shame as a moral verdict (putting enemies to shame; exposing idol-makers' inner bankruptcy), revealing that shame participates in divine judgment and justice. Shame also reflects God's holiness — the fact that coming into his presence without atoned sin produces the inability to lift one's face (Ezr 9:6; Eze 36:32) reveals a God whose holiness generates the experience of shame in the morally uncleansed.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The evidence does not directly attribute shame as an experienced inner state to God himself. Psa 113:6 (sha.phel, set aside at Phase 8.5) uses sha.phel for God's own "stooping down" to behold creation, but this is God's condescension rather than shame. No verse in the 224-verse CHAR-1 corpus attributes shame as a felt inner experience to the divine person. The analytical significance of this silence is considerable: shame as experienced here is the product of exposure, inadequacy, or moral failure — none of which can characterize God. The characteristic is distinctively creaturely. The one asymmetry is that the evidence shows God *producing* shame in others as a moral and judicial act, which is a function of his holiness and justice rather than an experience he shares with the human person.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The silence confirms that shame as experienced inner state is a creaturely characteristic arising from moral vulnerability, exposure, and the gap between what the person is and what is required. God possesses no such gap. The characteristic's absence from the divine nature establishes it as a response-function rather than an originating-function — it is what happens to a morally situated creature when exposed before what is holy, when trust collapses, or when moral failure is uncovered. The creation-contrast is sharpest in Gen 2:25 (vc=34835): before the fall, the human pair "were not ashamed," evidencing shame as belonging to the fallen creaturely condition rather than the created design. This suggests shame is a post-fall reality that nonetheless reflects real moral structure (the capacity for guilt, the awareness of one's standing before God and community) that the divine image-bearer retains even in the fallen state.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Gen 2:25 (vc=34835) is the critical datum: the absence of shame in the pre-fall pair marks the state of "inner transparency and relational wholeness." Shame as experienced inner state is therefore not part of the original created design's positive content — it does not serve a purpose in the unfallen person. Its design-function appears only in the fallen context: the M07-B evidence shows shame functioning as a moral corrective (entrepō — 2Th 3:14; 1Cor 6:5 entropē; Eze 43:10-11), exposing moral failure and driving the person toward right orientation. The M07-C evidence shows shame before God functioning as the gateway to restoration — "only if Israel is ashamed of all they have done will the full design of the temple be disclosed to them" (Eze 43:11, vc=38514). In the fallen condition, shame serves the purpose of moral orientation — it registers reality accurately about the person's standing, prompting return and correction.

---

**T0.2.2 — Is this characteristic part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence positions shame as primarily a response to the fallen condition, not original created design. Gen 2:25 (vc=34835) explicitly names the pre-fall state as one without shame — the absence of shame is the positive mark of created wholeness. The fall produces the shame-condition. However, the capacity for shame — the inner moral apparatus that registers exposure, guilt, and relational brokenness — appears to belong to the image-bearing structure itself. The evidence from M07-C (Ezr 9:6; Jer 31:19; Eze 43:10-11) shows shame operating as the morally appropriate response to one's own sin, suggesting that the faculty which produces shame (moral consciousness, conscience) is part of the created design, while the experience of shame is a response to the fallen condition. Both dimensions are present: shame-as-experience is post-fall; shame-as-moral-signal depends on image-bearing moral awareness.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Strong evidence. The M07-A evidence (VCG-04) documents God's explicit promises to remove shame permanently: "you will forget the shame of your youth" (Isa 54:4, vc=34838/37319/37352/38534), shame transformed into a double portion (Isa 61:7, vc=37320/37482), "my people shall never again be put to shame" (Joe 2:26, vc=65582; Joe 2:27, vc=65583). The M07-C evidence (VCG-04) confirms the eschatological direction: everlasting salvation means never put to shame or confounded "to all eternity" (Isa 45:17, vc=65543). Rev 3:18 (vc=34975) — the naked shame of spiritual destitution covered by Christ's garments — frames the present shame-condition within an eschatological transaction. The future fullness is a state where shame's inner collapse is permanently resolved — not merely managed but removed by divine act, replaced by honour, double portion, and praise. Shame's orientation toward its own removal is built into the evidence.

---

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame as experienced inner state expresses the divine image primarily through the moral consciousness it presupposes. The person who can feel shame is a person who has a built-in reference point for moral standing — a sense of what they should be, how they should stand before others and before God. The M07-C evidence (Ezr 9:6 — "I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God") shows that shame before God is possible only because the image-bearer retains the capacity for moral self-assessment in the divine presence. The M07-B evidence (Jer 6:15 — "they did not know how to blush," vc=65551; Jer 8:12, vc=65553) reveals the inverse: the loss of the capacity for shame marks the degradation of the image, specifically the destruction of the moral-evaluative faculty. Shame as experienced inner state therefore expresses the divine image's moral dimension — the capacity to stand before the holy and register the gap between what one is and what one ought to be.

---

**T0.3.2 — Is this characteristic shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — As noted at T0.1.2, the evidence does not attribute shame as experienced inner state to God. It is a creaturely analogue: the moral seriousness that shame reflects (the reality of moral standing, exposure before holiness, the weight of guilt) has a divine correlate in God's holiness and just judgment, but God does not experience the felt collapse of dignity that constitutes shame. The characteristic is exclusively creaturely in its experiential form.

---

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The M07-B seared-conscience evidence (VCG-04) is definitive: the loss of the capacity for shame — "they did not know how to blush" (Jer 6:15; 8:12) — is presented as moral collapse, the sign that the inner faculty of conscience is "dead" (M07-B-VCG-04 Phase 7 description). This is the image degraded. Conversely, the capacity for shame — the ability to feel the inner weight of one's own moral failure, to be "ashamed and blush to lift one's face" before God (Ezr 9:6) — is evidence that the moral-evaluative faculty remains operative. Zophar's challenge to Job (Job 11:3, vc=38520 in M07-B-VCG-03) implies that the capacity for shame in response to moral failure is part of what healthy moral persons should have. The presence of appropriate shame signals the image functioning; its absence signals its corruption.

---

**T0.4.1 — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in at least three directions. First, the servant's bearing of disgrace and spitting (Isa 50:6, vc=37481 in M07-A-VCG-02) — "I hid not my face from disgrace and spitting" — functions typologically toward Christ's passion, with Act 8:32's silence of the lamb (vc=28925) confirming the christological reading: the willed inner stillness under unjust suffering that shame normally overwhelms. Second, Heb 12:2 (vc=34970) explicitly deploys Jesus's endurance of the cross's shame as the anchor for CHAR-1's voluntary-suffering register — "despising the shame" in full awareness, motivated by the joy set before him. Third, the eschatological shame-removal promises (Isa 54:4; 61:7; Joe 2:26; Rev 3:18) function typologically toward the final state — shame's removal is the image-bearing person's eschatological fullness, toward which the present experience of shame (and its partial lifting through faith and trust) is oriented.

---

**T0.4.2 — What is the direction of the typology?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Primarily human-to-divine with christological fulfilment, and secondarily eschatological anticipation. The human experience of bearing unjust disgrace without self-defense (servant, the silent lamb) points toward Christ's passion. Christ's despising the cross's shame (Heb 12:2) establishes the pattern that human believers are invited to inhabit (1Pe 4:16 — "let him not be ashamed, but let him glorify God in that name"). The direction is bidirectional in the VCG-03 material: human unjust shame points toward Christ; Christ's disposition toward shame in the cross establishes the pattern for human response.

---

**T0.4.3 — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; typological use is evidenced above at T0.4.1.

## T1 — Defining the Characteristic

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called, and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — "Shame as experienced inner state." The name's three components are all analytically load-bearing: "shame" names the emotion/condition; "as experienced" marks it as a phenomenological reality — the felt inward event, not a moral verdict assigned from outside; "inner state" locates it constitutionally within the person, not merely in their reputation or social standing. The name explicitly distinguishes this characteristic from M07's other characteristics (enforced humiliation = M07-D; relational worth-denial = M07-E; shamefulness as moral judgment = M07-F). The CHAR-1 name signals that what is under investigation is the inner life of the person who feels shame — not what others think of them, not the moral quality of what they did, but the collapse of dignity, confidence, and standing that they experience.

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary Hebrew root *bosh* (H0954, 108 verses in cluster, majority in CHAR-1) means to be ashamed, to feel shame — a stative verb whose basic sense is the felt inner state of shame. The root produces the nominal forms *bo.shet* (H1322 — shame as noun, the condition itself) and *bu.shah* (H0955 — the covering/enveloping shame). *Ke.lim.mah* (H3639 — dishonour, ignominy) names the experience of being publicly exposed and reduced — the social-facing dimension of shame. *Cha.pher* (H2659) adds the element of embarrassment and being disgraced, often with the connotation of being found out or let down. The Greek *aischunē* (G0152) and *aischunō* (G0153) are closely parallel to *bosh* — the noun and verb of the felt shame experience. *Kataischunō* (G2617) adds the prefix kata- (down, thoroughly) giving "to thoroughly shame" or "to put to shame completely." Together the terms confirm at the definitional level: CHAR-1 is about what happens *inside the person* — the felt state of inner collapse in the face of exposure, inadequacy, or moral failure.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Directional: shame moves the person *downward* — toward collapse, withdrawal, covering, face-hiding. The evidence consistently shows the shamed person unable to lift their head (Job 10:15, vc=37502), covering their face (Jer 14:3, vc=38535; Eze 7:18, vc=34978), unable to lift their face toward God (Ezr 9:6, vc=65495). Relational: shame is inherently relational — it arises in the face of someone (God, enemies, community). The three sub-group registers (unjust wound, moral consequence, divine encounter) all locate shame in a relational field. Constitutional: the name's "inner state" tag signals that the primary register is psychological/volitional — the collapse happens to the faculty of dignity, confidence, and self-perception. These implications together orient the enquiry: CHAR-1 is a downward, relational, inner event.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — CHAR-1 appears primarily as a *condition* — a state that falls upon the person or arises within them — rather than a discrete act or a stable disposition. The bosh corpus evidences this: "they are ashamed" (stative), "shame has covered my face" (condition received), "my days are spent in shame" (ongoing condition). However, CHAR-1 also has a volitional dimension: the corrective-shame register (M07-C-VCG-03, M07-B-VCG-05) shows shame being invoked or directed — used as a moral tool. And the borne-for-God's-sake register (M07-A-VCG-03) shows shame voluntarily accepted, which requires an act of the will. CHAR-1 is therefore primarily a *condition* with volitional dimensions — the condition can be received passively, resisted by trust, accepted willingly, or deployed correctively.

---

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — CHAR-1 is structurally complex. The volume-split into three sub-groups (M07-A: unjust wound; M07-B: moral consequence; M07-C: divine encounter) reflects three distinguishable registers, all of which are manifestations of the same underlying phenomenon — the felt collapse of dignity and standing. The constituent elements identifiable from the evidence include: (1) a trigger — the exposure, failure, or enemy-action that initiates shame; (2) the felt inner collapse — the specific phenomenology of shame's impact; (3) the behavioural expression — face-covering, head-bowing, silence, turning back; (4) the moral assessment — whether the shame is deserved or unjust; (5) the relational direction — toward God, toward enemies, toward community; and (6) the resolution direction — removal, persistence, or acceptance. These are not separate characteristics but the internal anatomy of CHAR-1.

---

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame as experienced inner state is the felt collapse of a person's inner dignity, confidence, and standing — produced by exposure to what exceeds one's moral capacity, by the failure or destruction of what one trusted, or by the discovery of one's own moral failure before God and community. The experience is inner (the collapse happens within), relational (it always has a referent — God, enemies, community, self-assessment), and directional (it drives the person toward concealment, withdrawal, or stillness). It manifests across three registers in the evidence: as an unjust wound received from enemies or circumstances (M07-A), as a deserved moral consequence of sin and folly (M07-B), and as the appropriate inner response of the person before God's holiness (M07-C). The characteristic is primarily a condition rather than an act, though it has volitional dimensions. Its vocabulary centres on *bosh*, *ke.lim.mah*, *aischunē*, and their cognates.

---

**T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The structural opposite is *honour* or *dignity* — the inner standing, confidence, and worth-recognition that shame displaces. The evidence names this opposition explicitly: "instead of your shame there shall be a double portion" (Isa 61:7); "I will change their shame into praise and renown" (Zep 3:19); shame and honour are the opposing poles throughout the royal clothing metaphors (enemies clothed with shame vs. the king's crown shining, Psa 132:18). The M07-C evidence (VCG-02) identifies trust in God as the present experiential opposite of shame — the inner act that protects against shame's collapse. The evidence also identifies innocence (M07-H, niq.qa.von) as the structural inner-being counterpart — the blameless conscience that shields against guilt-shame. At the deepest level, the structural opposite of shame is the inner confidence that one's standing is secure — before God, before community, within oneself.

---

**T1.3.2 — What does CHAR-1 explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame in its acute form excludes the upright face, bold speech, confident self-presentation, and the capacity to approach. The covering of the face (Jer 14:3; Eze 7:18), the inability to lift the face toward God (Ezr 9:6), the shutting of the mouth (Eze 16:63 — "never open your mouth again because of your shame"), the backward turning (Psa 35:4; 129:5) — all evidence what shame excludes. In the M07-B seared-conscience register, the absence of shame is what the wicked person has achieved — they have excluded shame from their inner repertoire, which is not a positive attainment but a moral collapse. Shame also resists the fraudulent: blameless conduct produces opponents' shame precisely because it refuses to give them grounds for accusation (Tit 2:8; 1Pe 3:16).

---

**T1.3.3 — What is CHAR-1 not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — CHAR-1 is not M07-D (humiliation as enforced abasement), which concerns the external, social, downward movement of standing rather than the inner felt state. CHAR-1 is not M07-F (shamefulness as moral-evaluative judgment), which concerns the qualitative assessment of what is shameful rather than the experience of being ashamed. CHAR-1 is not guilt per se (M12 territory) — guilt is the fact of moral failure, shame is the felt inner response to it. Shame can exist without guilt (M07-A — unjust wound) and guilt can exist without felt shame (M07-B seared conscience). CHAR-1 is not contempt-produced shame (M07-G), though contempt can trigger shame. The boundary with M07-C (shame before God) is within CHAR-1 itself by the volume-split; the boundary with M07-D is that humiliation can happen without shame being experienced (you can be publicly demoted without feeling the inner collapse).

---

**T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does CHAR-1 operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Four distinct operational modes emerge: (1) **Passive reception** — shame falls upon the person from outside (enemies, circumstances, misplaced trust collapsing; M07-A-VCG-01, -02). The person does not choose it; it arrives as the inner impact of what is done to them. (2) **Guilt-collapse** — shame arises from within, produced by the person's own moral failure as it stands before their conscience and before God; the "face that cannot be lifted" mode (M07-C-VCG-01; Ezr 9:6). (3) **Corrective induction** — shame is deployed instrumentally by another (Paul: 1Cor 6:5; community discipline: 2Th 3:14) or activated by encounter (Eze 43:10-11 — the temple's perfection producing shame). (4) **Voluntary acceptance** — shame is consciously borne for a greater purpose; the servant not hiding the face from spitting (Isa 50:6); the apostles rejoicing in dishonour for Christ (Act 5:41); Jesus despising the cross's shame (Heb 12:2). These four modes operate differently on the person's faculties, especially volition (see T3.6).

---

**T1.4.2 — Does CHAR-1 operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the three sub-group registers evidence three contextual modes. M07-A (unjust wound): shame operates as a received wound — the inner impact is felt as injustice, and the resolution is divine vindication. M07-B (moral consequence): shame operates as deserved outcome — the inner experience registers moral reality accurately. M07-C (divine encounter): shame operates at the vertical level — it is the inner posture of the creature before the holy, with the specific quality that it can be the gateway to renewed relationship (Eze 43:11). The M07-C mode is constitutionally deeper: it engages the person's standing before God rather than their standing before community or circumstance. The moral-consequence mode (M07-B) is the most cognitively engaged — it involves moral self-assessment. The unjust-wound mode (M07-A) is the most affectively prominent — the pain of undeserved exposure.

---

**T1.4.3 — Does CHAR-1 have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, with a striking characteristic: shame's primary communicative expression is *silence*. Eze 16:63 (vc=65575) — "never open your mouth again because of your shame" — names this directly. The sinner before atonement is silenced by shame. Job 10:15 (vc=37502) — "I cannot lift up my head" — evidences the non-verbal, postural form of shame's expression. The head-covering gestures (Jer 14:3; Eze 7:18) are communicative but nonverbal. The M07-A-VCG-03 evidence (Act 8:32, vc=28925) — the lamb silent before the shearer — presents silence as the appropriate inner-outer expression of bearing unjust shame with willed stillness. Conversely, bold speech is what shame suppresses: the psalmist who speaks of God's testimonies before kings "shall not be put to shame" (Psa 119:46) — trust enables speech; shame removes it.

---

**T1.5.1 — What is the most immediate inner-being response to shame?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence points to three closely related immediate responses: (1) **Collapse** — the inner state of confidence and standing falls. The psalmist's honour "turned into shame" (Psa 4:2, vc=37469); shame fills Job's inner being so completely that he cannot lift his head (Job 10:15, vc=37502). (2) **Concealment** — the face is covered, the head lowered: servants covering their heads (Jer 14:3, vc=38535/65555), horror covering bodies alongside shame (Eze 7:18, vc=34978), Jerusalem groaning and turning her face away (Lam 1:8, vc=21682). (3) **Turmoil** — enemies' confident opposition collapsing into "sudden, disorienting disgrace" (Psa 6:10, vc=34839 — "ashamed and greatly troubled"). These three are facets of the same immediate response: the inner collapse of standing expressed in bodily posture and inner agitation.

---

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The collapse-concealment-turmoil cluster is consistent as the core immediate response. However, it varies by mode. In the voluntary-acceptance mode (M07-A-VCG-03), the immediate response to shame's threat is not collapse but willed steadiness: Jesus "despising the shame" (Heb 12:2); the servant setting his face like flint (Isa 50:7, vc=65546); Paul not being ashamed because his authority is genuinely from God (2Cor 10:8, vc=34830). In the corrective mode, the immediate response is moral activation — "they consecrated themselves" (2Ch 30:15, vc=38506) — shame prompting action rather than paralysis. The variation confirms that CHAR-1 is a condition with multiple operational modes (T1.4.1), not a single-track response.

---

**T1.5.3 — Where the evidence is silent on immediate response, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The evidence is relatively complete on immediate response; the verse corpus is unusually rich in phenomenological detail about the experience of shame. No significant silence on this prompt.

---

**T1.6.1 — What does CHAR-1 produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Three long-term patterns emerge from the evidence. (1) **Sustained inner wound** — Psa 44:15 (vc=37310/37473): "all day long my disgrace is before me, and shame has covered my face" — shame as perpetual condition. Jer 20:18 (vc=37316): "spend my days in shame" — shame as the chronic condition of Jeremiah's existence. The ke.lim.mah passages in Eze 32:24-30 (shame "carried into death") show shame persisting as an inner-social verdict even beyond life. (2) **Moral desensitisation** — sustained absence of appropriate shame produces the seared conscience (Jer 6:15; 8:12). The long-term effect of refusing corrective shame is hardening: "brazen forehead, refuses to be ashamed" (Jer 3:3, vc=38501). (3) **Identity formation** — Jeremiah's chronic shame becomes part of his identity (Jer 20:18); Israel's shame is carried generationally (Ezr 9:7 — "from the days of our fathers to this day"). Sustained shame either wounds identity (chronic inner burden) or is consciously borne (VCG-03 register) or is resolved by divine act.

---

**T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, or orientations does sustained CHAR-1 establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — In the unjust-wound register (M07-A), sustained shame establishes vulnerability and isolation — Psa 127:5, the man without sons "put to shame when he speaks with his enemies" — chronic social exposure. In the moral-consequence register (M07-B), sustained appropriate shame ought to produce contrition and return; but where it is resisted, it establishes moral hardening (Jer 3:3; 6:15; 8:12). In the divine-encounter register (M07-C), sustained appropriate shame before God establishes the foundation for renewed relationship — Eze 43:11: shame is the precondition for receiving the temple's full design, meaning shame functions as an ongoing gateway posture before God, not merely a one-time event. The corrective-shame passages (entropē — 1Cor 6:5; 15:34) show shame intended to establish heightened moral sensitivity and community accountability.

---

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, markedly. The immediate response is acute — the collapse, the covering, the turmoil. The sustained effect is more varied. Where shame is unjust and unresolved, the sustained effect is chronic inner burden — Jer 20:18 ("spend my days in shame"), Psa 44:15 ("all day long"). Where shame is the appropriate response to sin and is received as such, the sustained effect is the opening of the moral gateway — contrition, restored relationship, renewed obedience. Where shame is refused, the sustained effect is moral hardening. Where shame is voluntarily borne, the sustained effect is vindication — the servant's face like flint (Isa 50:7), the apostles rejoicing (Act 5:41). The asymmetry between immediate collapse and sustained transformation is characteristic of CHAR-1's complexity.

---

**T1.7.1 — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of shame's corrective signal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The M07-C evidence is most direct: (1) **Conscience remaining operative** — those who "know how to blush" (the positive state behind Jer 6:15's negation) have the inner faculty intact. The priests whose conscience-shame moved them to consecration (2Ch 30:15, vc=38506) had a functional conscience that could receive shame's signal. (2) **Moral self-awareness** — Jer 31:19 (vc=38509/65562): "after I was instructed, I struck my thigh; I was ashamed" — instruction activates shame, meaning the cognitive faculty must be engaged to produce the shame response. (3) **Willingness to face what is true** — Eze 43:10: the house of Israel must be willing to "measure the plan" after shame — shame produces constructive response only when the will cooperates.

---

**T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block or distort reception?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The M07-B seared-conscience evidence (VCG-04) identifies the primary blocking condition: the hardened inner conscience that "refuses to be ashamed" (Jer 3:3, vc=38501). The brazen forehead, the lost capacity to blush — these represent the destruction of the shame-receptive faculty through sustained moral resistance. The M07-B idolatry register (VCG-02) identifies a secondary blocking condition: investment in the false object (the idol) so total that the shame of its exposure cannot be received without cognitive collapse. The M07-A-VCG-02 evidence shows that misplaced trust also blocks appropriate shame: those who trusted Egypt could not receive the shame signal until Egypt visibly failed (Isa 20:5; 30:3-5).

---

**T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters shame but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two distinct non-reception states emerge. (1) **Moral hardening** — the person who refuses shame's corrective signal (Jer 3:3; 6:15) is in a state of inner desensitisation: the conscience cannot register what should register. The immediate consequence is ongoing sin without corrective friction, and the long-term consequence is divine judgment (Jer 6:15 — "therefore they shall fall among those who fall"). (2) **Willed resilience** — the person in the voluntary-acceptance register (M07-A-VCG-03; Heb 12:2; Isa 50:7) does not receive shame's collapse-signal not through hardening but through the stronger orientation of will toward a greater object. Jesus "despising the shame" is not moral hardening — it is the engagement of a greater joy. Paul "will not be ashamed" because his authority is genuinely from God — not denial of the shame-pressure but its resolution through grounded confidence. These two non-reception states are analytically distinct: the first is pathological (inner death), the second is volitionally mature (inner strength).

---

**T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary dimension is **emotion/affect** — shame as experienced inner state is fundamentally a felt reality, the collapse of inner dignity and standing as an experienced event. The bosh corpus, the ke.lim.mah corpus, the aischunē corpus all centre on the phenomenology of the experience. The eschatological resolution (Isa 54:4; Zep 3:19) restores the agency that shame had suppressed: shame removed means the person can once again speak and act without the inner paralysis.

---

**T3.7.3 — What does this reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That CHAR-1 is fundamentally an agency-impairment phenomenon in its ordinary operation, and that the characteristic's resolution is tied to the restoration of agency. The shame-lifting promises are not merely about dignity restored in the abstract — they are about the concrete capacity to stand, speak, and act in the world and before God being restored to the person from whom shame had taken it.

---

**T3.8.1 — Does CHAR-1 engage the moral evaluation faculty?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Pervasively. The moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, good, and true — is the primary internal generator of shame in the M07-B and M07-C registers. Shame is what the moral evaluation faculty produces when it accurately registers failure. The Proverbs pattern (Pro 3:35; 6:33; 13:5; 13:18; 18:3; 18:13) consistently presents shame as the moral-evaluation output of specific failures: folly inherits disgrace, deception produces shame, wickedness generates dishonour. The M07-C guilt-shame register is entirely about the moral evaluation faculty registering the person's own sin before God's holiness. The M07-B seared-conscience register is about the destruction of the moral evaluation faculty: those who cannot blush have lost the capacity for moral evaluation that should produce shame.

---

**T3.8.2 — Does CHAR-1 enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — In its corrective form, shame **enables and deepens** moral evaluation: entropē (1Cor 6:5; 15:34) uses shame to awaken the community's moral evaluation of their own failure. The gateway-of-shame passages (Eze 43:10-11) show shame as the precondition for the house of Israel receiving the moral design of the temple — shame clears the inner space for accurate moral perception. In its hardened form, shame's absence **impairs** moral evaluation catastrophically — the seared conscience neither evaluates nor corrects. In its acute wound-form (M07-A unjust shame), shame can **bypass** moral evaluation by arriving before it — the unjust wound produces shame regardless of what the moral evaluation faculty says about the person's actual standing.

---

**T3.8.3 — What does this reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That CHAR-1's relationship with moral evaluation is among its most analytically significant features. Shame is simultaneously produced by moral evaluation (when functioning), and can be the tool that restores moral evaluation (when deploying the corrective signal). The destruction of shame-receptivity (seared conscience) is simultaneously the destruction of moral evaluation — they are the same faculty in its shamed and shameless modes.

---

**T3.9.1 — Does CHAR-1 engage the conscience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, and this is CHAR-1's most distinctive inner-faculty relationship. Conscience is the faculty that registers guilt, moral failure, and deviation from the right — and shame is what the conscience *feels* when it makes those registrations. The M07-B-VCG-04 material (Jer 6:15; 8:12; 3:3) is entirely about the conscience's relation to shame: the loss of the capacity for shame = the death of the conscience. The M07-C-VCG-01 material (Ezr 9:6; 2Ch 30:15; Jer 31:19; Eze 43:10-11) shows the conscience receiving the shame signal and activating corrective response. The M07-B-VCG-05 corrective-shame passages (1Cor 6:5; 15:34) explicitly invoke shame to activate the conscience. Conscience and shame are functionally fused in the CHAR-1 evidence.

---

**T3.9.2 — Does CHAR-1 enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame is conscience's primary experiential output, so the relationship is constitutive rather than enabling or impairing in the normal sense. Where the conscience functions, shame results from its registrations. Where shame is absent (seared conscience), conscience is absent. However, shame can also be the tool that *restores* conscience: the corrective-shame passages show shame being directed at someone to wake up their conscience (1Cor 6:5 — "I say this to your shame... can it be that there is no one among you wise enough?"). In this register, shame is the awakener of conscience rather than merely its product.

---

**T3.9.3 — What does this reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That the shame-conscience relationship is the structural core of CHAR-1. Shame is what a functioning conscience feels; the capacity for shame is the sign that conscience is alive; the loss of the capacity for shame (Jer 6:15; 8:12) is the death of conscience. The corrective deployment of shame (1Cor 6:5; 15:34) is the attempt to reactivate a conscience that has fallen asleep. This means CHAR-1 is essentially a conscience-phenomenon in its moral dimensions, and its engagement with conscience is the basis for its role in moral formation (see T5.5).

---

**T3.10.1 — Does CHAR-1 engage conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — is engaged at the output end of shame's corrective function. The priests of 2Ch 30:15 (vc=38506) demonstrate full conscientiousness in response to shame: inner shame (moral awareness activated) → decision to consecrate (volition) → immediate consecration (action). Eze 43:10-11 shows shame as the gateway to conscientious reception of the temple's design — shame activates the full integrated response. The M07-B-VCG-03 wisdom pattern (Pro 13:18 — "poverty and disgrace come to him who ignores instruction") is about the failure of conscientiousness (ignoring instruction = failure of the shame-conscience-volition-action integration) producing shame as the consequence.

---

**T3.10.2 — Does CHAR-1 enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Corrective shame **enables** conscientiousness by producing the moral-awareness that feeds the will and issues in action. Acute unjust shame **impairs** conscientiousness by paralyzing the agency component. The seared-conscience register **bypasses** conscientiousness entirely — the person acts without the moral awareness that should govern their acts.

---

**T3.10.3 — What does this reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That CHAR-1 is positioned as both a product and a trigger of conscientiousness. The person who lacks shame lacks the first component of conscientious functioning (moral awareness activated by guilt). The person who receives corrective shame has the first component restored. Shame's role in the conscientiousness system is that of moral-awareness trigger — without it, the system cannot function.

---

**T3.11.1 — Does CHAR-1 engage the relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Extensively and across all three registers. Shame is inherently relational — it always arises in relation to someone: enemies (M07-A-VCG-01), community (M07-A-VCG-01, M07-B), God (M07-C), allies (M07-A-VCG-02). The M07-A evidence shows shame damaging the relational capacity: the shamed person withdraws (turns back, covers the face), loses public voice (Psa 127:5 — cannot speak at the gate), and has their relational standing stripped. The M07-B corrective-shame passages show shame deployed within existing relationships (Paul to the Corinthians; community isolation of the disobedient in 2Th 3:14) — shame as a relational instrument for restoring broken relational faithfulness. The M07-C divine-encounter register shows shame as the inner state of a broken relational posture before God.

---

**T3.11.2 — Does CHAR-1 enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — In its ordinary wounded form, shame **impairs** relational capacity — withdrawal, face-covering, inability to speak at the gate. In its corrective form, shame is a *relational tool* — it is extended by one person toward another to restore right relationship (2Th 3:14; 1Pe 3:16). In the M07-C mode, shame before God is the precondition for restored relationship: Eze 43:10-11 — only when Israel is ashamed can the temple design (the design of right relationship with God) be disclosed. Shame before God, in the M07-C mode, is not the end of the relational capacity but the gateway to its restoration.

---

**T3.11.3 — What does this reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That CHAR-1's relational dimension is among its most theologically significant features. Shame is both the product of relational rupture (unjust wound from enemies, moral failure before God) and the gateway to relational restoration (corrective shame, penitential shame before God). The shame that closes the relationship (face turned away, mouth shut, presence withdrawn) is, when received as corrective, the opening through which relationship can be rebuilt. This makes shame a distinctively relational characteristic — it never operates in isolation from the person's relational field.

## T4 — Interfaces

**T4.1.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate from God toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in a specific and distinctive way. God deploys shame as a judicial verdict and as a moral instrument directed at human persons. The M07-B evidence documents God "putting enemies to shame" (Psa 44:7; 53:5; 86:17; Zec 10:5), exposing idol-makers to shame (Isa 44:9-11; 45:16), and placing shame on those who opposed his servant (Isa 41:11; 42:17). This is God using shame as a tool of judgment — it moves from God toward the person as a consequence of their action against God or his people. More remarkably, God also deploys shame as a restorative instrument toward his own people: the commands in Eze 16:52; Eze 36:32; Eze 43:10 — "be ashamed and confounded for your ways, O house of Israel" — are divine invocations of shame directed at his covenant people as the gateway to renewal. God extends shame toward the human person both as judgment (on enemies) and as corrective mercy (on his own people).

---

**T4.1.2 — What is the basis on which God extends shame?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals two distinct bases. To enemies and opponents, God extends shame as *responsive and conditional*: their hostility toward God's servant and people is what triggers the divine shaming. The shame is proportionate and retributive — their malice meets its reversal. To his own people, God extends shame as *covenantal and corrective*: the commands to "be ashamed" in Eze 36:32 ("it is not for your sake that I will act") are explicitly grounded in God's unilateral covenantal action *apart from* their deserving — the basis is grace rather than merit. The shame commanded before grace is not a precondition of merit but the appropriate inner posture of a person receiving what they have not earned.

---

**T4.1.3 — What does God's extension of shame reveal about his disposition?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — God's extension of shame as judgment reveals his holiness and justice — he takes moral reality seriously enough to expose and reverse what opposes it. God's extension of shame as corrective mercy toward his own people reveals his pastoral investment in their inner moral integrity — he does not want them to receive his grace without moral self-awareness, because the grace that bypasses shame-recognition is grace that cannot transform. The command "be ashamed" before "I will act for you" (Eze 36:32) reveals a God who is committed to the whole person's inner life, not merely their external circumstances. This disposition is consistent throughout M07-C: God arranges encounters (the temple design, Eze 43:10) specifically to produce shame in the inner person because shame before holiness is what opens the person to receive what God offers.

---

**T4.1.4 — If silent on God-to-human operation, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; God-to-human operation is extensively evidenced.

---

**T4.2.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate in the human person's movement toward God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, and the M07-C corpus (M07-C-VCG-01 and -03) is the primary evidence. The person in the M07-C mode moves toward God *through* shame: the penitential posture of lying down in shame (Jer 3:25, vc=37308/37470), the inability to lift the face to God that nonetheless constitutes a kind of prayer (Ezr 9:6), the conscience-driven shame that moves toward renewed service (2Ch 30:15). The M07-C-VCG-02 material shows the complementary movement: the person's movement toward God through trust simultaneously protects from shame — these are the two faces of the human-toward-God movement in shame's register (moving toward God through shame's guilt; moving toward God through trust and word). Both are movements toward God; they differ in what drives them (guilt-shame vs. confident trust).

---

**T4.2.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The inner posture required for the shame-mediated movement toward God is *moral honesty* — willingness to let shame be what it is, to "lie down in our shame" (Jer 3:25) without defensiveness or hardening. The M07-B seared-conscience material shows what happens when this posture is refused: no movement toward God is possible because the inner faculty that registers failure (conscience) is dead. The M07-C gateway passages show that where this posture is maintained, shame becomes the specific inner preparation for receiving God's self-disclosure (Eze 43:10-11).

---

**T4.2.3 — What does the human-to-God direction reveal about the relationship?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — That shame before God is not the destruction of the relationship but its specific form of honesty. The relationship between the person and God can bear shame — it can receive the one who comes with the face not lifted, who lies down in shame, who groans and turns away. The Psalm evidence (Psa 25:2 — "in you I trust; let me not be put to shame; let not my enemies exult") shows the psalmist bringing shame-threat to God as a relational appeal. God is the one before whom shame is felt and to whom shame is brought for resolution. The human-to-God direction reveals a relationship in which honest inner moral experience is the appropriate content of the movement toward God.

---

**T4.2.4 — If silent on human-to-God operation, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; human-to-God operation is extensively evidenced in M07-C.

---

**T4.3.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate in one person extending shame toward another?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in two distinct ways. (1) **Unjust infliction** — enemies, adversaries, those who love falsehood actively working to cover another person's face with shame (Psa 4:2; 69:7; 44:15 — M07-A-VCG-01). This is shame as a weapon directed against the innocent. (2) **Corrective deployment** — Paul directing shame at the Corinthians (1Cor 6:5; 15:34), community isolation producing corrective shame in the disobedient (2Th 3:14), blameless conduct putting accusers to shame (Tit 2:8; 1Pe 3:16). The M07-B-VCG-05 material is the extensive evidence base for legitimate corrective deployment of shame person-to-person.

---

**T4.3.2 — What inner conditions enable genuine extension of corrective shame?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corrective deployment evidence (Paul's letters; the community-discipline passages) shows that the giver of corrective shame must be acting from a position of genuine moral concern rather than hostility. Paul's distinction in 1Cor 4:14 (vc=34992) — "I do not write these things to *make you ashamed*, but to *admonish you as my beloved children*" — shows the fine line between legitimate corrective shame (motivated by affection and moral concern) and destructive shame-induction (motivated by hostility or self-elevation). The corrective giver must have their own standing grounded in what they appeal to — blameless conduct as the basis for causing opponents' shame (Tit 2:8; 1Pe 3:16).

---

**T4.3.3 — What must the giver have received before they can genuinely give?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence from the corrective-shame passages implies that the person who can legitimately invoke shame in another must themselves have received the corrective-shame signal and responded to it. Paul's own apostolic authority (2Cor 10:8) is the basis for his freedom from shame — his authority is genuinely from God. The person who invokes corrective shame without having themselves experienced and received shame's corrective function is working from a position of moral inconsistency that undermines the legitimacy of the invitation.

---

**T4.3.4 — If silent on the giving direction, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; the giving direction is evidenced in the corrective-shame passages.

**T4.4.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate as received by a person from another?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, extensively in M07-A. The unjust-wound register (M07-A-VCG-01, -02) is almost entirely about shame received — the psalmist whose honour is exchanged for shame by enemies' deceit (Psa 4:2), Israel whose shame comes from surrounding nations (Eze 34:29; 36:6; 36:15), the mother whose shame comes from bereavement (Jer 15:9), the servants who receive shame through drought and helplessness (Jer 14:3-4). Received shame is the primary operational mode in the unjust-wound register. The corrective-shame register (M07-B-VCG-05; M07-C-VCG-03) also operates as received — the community receiving Paul's shame-direction (1Cor 6:5), Israel receiving the shame of the temple's design (Eze 43:10).

---

**T4.4.2 — What inner conditions enable or block reception?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — For corrective received shame: an operative conscience enables reception (2Ch 30:15 — the priests receive the shame signal and act); moral hardening blocks it (Jer 3:3; 6:15 — the brazen forehead refuses shame). For unjust received shame: the question is not whether it is received (it is received regardless of the person's inner state) but how it is received — whether it overwhelms (Psa 44:15 — "all day long my disgrace is before me") or is absorbed with inner resilience (Pro 12:16 — the prudent ignores an insult) or is voluntarily borne (Heb 12:2; Act 5:41).

---

**T4.4.3 — What is the inner state of the person who encounters shame from another but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — In the corrective mode: the person who refuses corrective shame from another is in a state of moral hardening — conscience closed, inner faculty sealed against the signal. The M07-B-VCG-04 material (Jer 3:3; 6:15; 8:12; Mic 2:6) documents this state: brazen, desensitised, dismissive of the possibility that disgrace could overtake them. In the unjust-wound mode: the person who successfully does not receive unjust shame is not hardened but strong — the prudent who "ignores an insult" (Pro 12:16), the servant who sets his face like flint (Isa 50:7). The inner state is grounded confidence rather than armoured hardening.

---

**T4.4.4 — If silent on the receiving direction, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; received shame is extensively evidenced.

**T4.5.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate differently within existing bonds versus across relational distance?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Within existing covenantal bonds (Israel-God relationship in M07-C; Paul-Corinthians in M07-B-VCG-05; family relationships in M07-B-VCG-03), shame functions as a relational signal — it can be received, corrected, and lead to restored relationship because the bond exists and can bear the weight. Across relational distance (enemies in M07-A, opponents in M07-B-VCG-01), shame is a judicial/moral verdict rather than a relational instrument — it is the exposure and collapse of standing before one who stands against the person, not a call to relational restoration. The community discipline passage (2Th 3:14) is notable: isolation from the disobedient person is prescribed precisely to signal the relational rupture as a precondition for shame-producing awareness. Relationship is the context within which corrective shame can work.

---

**T4.5.2 — Does CHAR-1 operate within covenantal contexts only, or cross covenantal boundaries?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — CHAR-1 crosses covenantal boundaries. The psalmist's enemies experience shame; Edom is covered with shame (Obd 10); Babylon's gods are put to shame (Jer 50:2) — these are not covenantal contexts. God's judicial deployment of shame against opponents crosses covenantal boundaries freely. What is covenantally specific is the *corrective and restorative* mode of shame: the commands to "be ashamed" in Eze 16:52; 36:32; 43:10 are directed at God's covenant people specifically, not universally. Corrective shame as gateway to renewed relationship appears to operate within the covenantal context, while judicial shame as consequence operates universally.

---

**T4.5.3 — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — CHAR-1's scope is universal in its judicial form (any moral agent can be put to shame by God's judgment); covenantally specific in its corrective-restorative form (shame as the gateway to knowing God's design is offered to those in covenantal relationship with him); and interpersonally broad in its unjust-wound form (anyone can inflict unjust shame on anyone else, as the psalmist's enemy-evidence shows).

## T5 — Transformation

**T5.1.1 — Does CHAR-1 produce transformation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, in both directions. In the corrective mode (M07-B-VCG-05; M07-C-VCG-01 and -03), shame produces transformation of the person's orientation — from wrong-facing to right-facing, from self-defensive to penitent, from morally asleep to morally awake. 2Ch 30:15: shame → immediate consecration (condition transformed). Eze 43:10-11: shame → openness to receive God's design (orientation transformed). In the hardening mode (M07-B-VCG-04), shame's absence marks a transformation in reverse — the progressive desensitisation of the conscience. In the unjust-wound mode, shame damages the person's sense of standing and dignity — a transformation of condition.

---

**T5.1.2 — Is the transformation reversible or irreversible?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Both conditions are evidenced. The shame of the unjust wound is explicitly declared *reversible* by divine act: Isa 54:4 — "you will forget the shame of your youth"; Isa 61:7 — "instead of your shame there shall be a double portion." The shame of the seared conscience is not declared reversible within the evidence — the passages (Jer 6:15; 8:12) record it as a state of judgment with no restoration promised in context. The transformation shame produces in the corrective mode (2Ch 30:15; Eze 43:10-11) appears stable rather than temporary. The evidence thus shows: unjust shame is reversible by divine promise; appropriate guilt-shame is resolvable through atonement (Eze 16:63); seared-conscience shamelessness is presented as catastrophic and its reversal is not evidenced.

---

**T5.1.3 — If silent on transformation, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; transformation is extensively evidenced.

---

**T5.2.1 — Does the evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which CHAR-1 moves the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The M07-C evidence is richest for sequence description. The guilt-shame sequence (M07-C-VCG-01): *awareness of sin → inner collapse before God's holiness → shame-posture (face not lifted, blushing) → corrective action or penitential stillness → (gateway opens to) renewed relationship with God*. Jer 31:19 evidences this: "after I had turned away, I relented; after I was instructed, I struck my thigh; I was ashamed, and I was confounded, because I bore the disgrace of my youth" — the sequence of turning → instruction → bodily expression of shame → confounded recognition. The M07-A unjust-wound sequence: *enemy aggression → received inner wound → face-covering → petition to God for vindication → (eventually) shame-removal and restoration*.

---

**T5.2.2 — What are those states and what does the sequence reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corrective sequence (M07-C) reveals that shame is not the end-state but a *transition state* — it exists to produce the gateway opening, not to be the final condition. The unjust-wound sequence (M07-A) reveals that unresolved shame is a sustained inner burden that requires divine intervention for resolution — it does not self-resolve. The moral-consequence sequence (M07-B-VCG-03) is the simplest: moral failure → shame as the natural consequence, without necessarily implying a further sequence (the person may receive the shame and be changed, or may harden). The sequence across the sub-groups suggests that CHAR-1's transformative potential depends on which sequence is operative: the corrective sequence (M07-C) has a built-in forward movement; the unjust-wound sequence (M07-A) depends on external divine resolution; the hardening sequence (M07-B-VCG-04) has no built-in forward movement.

---

**T5.2.3 — If silent on sequence, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable.

---

**T5.3.1 — What mechanism does CHAR-1 use to produce change?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Three mechanisms emerge from the evidence. (1) **Conscience-activation** — the primary corrective mechanism: shame activates the conscience, which then activates the will, which then activates action (2Ch 30:15; Jer 31:19; Eze 43:10). (2) **Encounter-triggered** — shame produced by confrontation with holiness (the temple's design, Eze 43:10) or divine grace (Eze 36:32) or atonement (Eze 16:63). The mechanism is the encounter itself rather than self-generated shame. (3) **Social/relational** — corrective shame deployed through community (2Th 3:14; 1Cor 6:5) — isolation, admonition, or blameless conduct producing the shame-signal in the target. These three mechanisms are distinct but all work through the conscience as the receiving faculty.

---

**T5.3.2 — Does the evidence distinguish mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The conscience-activation mechanism operates primarily in prophetic and epistolary contexts (Jeremiah, Paul). The encounter-triggered mechanism operates primarily in the priestly and temple contexts (Eze 43; 2Ch 30). The social mechanism operates in communal contexts (2Th 3; 1Cor 6; Tit 2). Each mechanism is contextually appropriate — the mechanism that works in each context matches the social, institutional, and relational structure of that context.

---

**T5.3.3 — If silent on mechanism, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; mechanisms are evidenced.

---

**T5.4.1 — Does CHAR-1 operate in relation to suffering or affliction?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Extensively in M07-A. The unjust-wound sub-group is essentially suffering-and-shame: the suffering of enemy aggression, bereavement (Jer 15:9), drought (Jer 14:3-4), national catastrophe (Eze 7:18) produces shame as a component of the suffering experience. Shame is one of the inner dimensions of suffering when suffering involves exposure, loss of standing, or the collapse of what was trusted. The M07-A-VCG-03 (voluntary acceptance register) shows suffering and shame being consciously integrated: the servant accepts physical humiliation without flinching (Isa 50:6), the apostles rejoice in shame-suffering (Act 5:41), Christ endures the cross's shame (Heb 12:2).

---

**T5.4.2 — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — All four. Suffering *produces* unjust shame (M07-A-VCG-01 — the victim of enemy aggression). Suffering *reveals* the inner quality of the person through their response to shame: Heb 12:2 reveals Christ's inner orientation by how he related to the cross's shame (joy-motivated will overcoming shame-pressure). Suffering *tests* the shame-bearing capacity: 1Pe 4:16 — "if anyone suffers as a Christian, let him not be ashamed" — the test of whether suffering produces shame-collapse or God-honouring response. Unjust shame *deepens* suffering: the mother whose bereavement is compounded by the "shaming and disgracing" (Jer 15:9) shows shame intensifying the suffering experience.

---

**T5.4.3 — If silent on the relationship to suffering, note explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Not applicable; the suffering-shame relationship is extensively evidenced.

---

**T5.5.1 — Does CHAR-1 participate in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The M07-C evidence is the primary basis: the series Eze 16:52 → 16:54 → 16:61 → 16:63 → 36:32 → 43:10-11 presents a progressive formation arc in which shame before God's holiness is not a one-time event but an ongoing posture — Israel is repeatedly called to bear its disgrace, remember its ways, be ashamed before grace — and this shame-before-grace is the inner environment of ongoing covenant relationship with God. The M07-C-VCG-03 material (entropē — 1Cor 6:5; 15:34) shows shame as a sanctification tool — Paul uses it to form the Corinthians toward greater moral seriousness. The Proverbs wisdom pattern (M07-B-VCG-03) presents shame as a formative consequence that shapes the person who receives it toward wisdom (the one who receives instruction avoids disgrace; the one who ignores it suffers it).

---

**T5.5.2 — What does the evidence reveal about CHAR-1's role in this arc?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Shame's role in the sanctification arc is specifically that of *moral-honesty maintenance*. ## T7 — Lexical, Literary, and Scientific Analysis

**T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms, and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Primary Hebrew: *bosh* (H0954) — root meaning: to be ashamed, to become pale/confounded, to dry up (the withered-grass image of 2Ki 19:26 echoes this); the root suggests inner collapse/paling. *Ke.lim.mah* (H3639) — from *ka.lam* (to be wounded, humiliated); root suggests a cutting, wounding inner state of disgrace. *Bo.shet* (H1322) — the nominal form of *bosh*, naming shame as a condition or state. *Cha.pher* (H2659) — to be disappointed, disgraced, confounded; root related to searching/digging (seeking what is not found, then becoming ashamed of the failed seeking). Primary Greek: *aischunē* (G0152) — from *aischros* (ugly, base, shameful); root suggests the perception of ugliness or baseness in oneself. *Kataischunō* (G2617) — thoroughly put to shame, downward-shaming; the kata- prefix intensifies and specifies direction (downward). The roots collectively reveal: shame is an inner paling/collapsing, a wound that cuts, a seeking that fails, a perception of one's own baseness before what is right or holy.

---

**T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — *Bosh* functions as both verb (to be ashamed — the most common usage) and noun (via *bo.shet* and *bu.shah*). The verbal usage dominates in the CHAR-1 corpus: "they are ashamed," "they were put to shame," "you shall not be ashamed" — the stative/passive verb is the primary grammatical form. This reveals that shame as experienced inner state is primarily *received* or *arrived at* rather than *active* — the person does not do shameing to themselves; shame happens to them or in them. The passive construction confirms the condition-character of CHAR-1 (T1.2.1): it is undergone rather than performed.

---

**T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — *Bosh* ranges from acute momentary shame (the servants covering heads before drought) to chronic existential shame ("spend my days in shame") to moral shame-as-consequence (the wicked clothed with shame) to shame-before-God (Ezr 9:6) to cosmic shame (even the moon will be ashamed, Isa 24:23). The semantic range is almost the entire register of human inner moral exposure — making *bosh* one of the most semantically broad shame-vocabulary items in the evidence. *Ke.lim.mah* is narrower: primarily the social-facing, communally-visible disgrace, the ongoing wound of being seen as dishonoured.

---

**T7.1.4 — Does the vocabulary distinguish distinct aspects of CHAR-1?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The distinction between *bosh* (the felt experience of shame — more subjective, inner) and *ke.lim.mah* (the disgrace as witnessed and socially visible — more objective, outward) maps onto a genuine inner-outer distinction within CHAR-1. *Cha.pher* (disappointed, confounded) carries the cognitive-collapse dimension (the failing of a confident expectation). *Bu.shah* (shame as covering, enveloping condition) captures the sustained-burden dimension. *Aischunē* names the condition; *aischunō* the verbal action of being ashamed; *kataischunō* the thoroughly-shamed state. The vocabulary thus distinguishes inner subjective experience, social visibility, cognitive collapse, chronic burden, and the act vs. condition of shame.

---

**T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The structural opposite of shame is named through honour-vocabulary: *kabod* (glory/honour), *timē* (honour), *doxa* (glory) — these are not in the CHAR-1 corpus itself but appear in the contrast structures ("instead of your shame there shall be a double portion," Isa 61:7; "enemies clothed with shame but his crown shines," Psa 132:18). The term for the absence of shame is evidenced in the negative constructions: "you will not be ashamed" (future promise), "were not ashamed" (Gen 2:25), "will not be put to shame" (Psa 22:5 et al.) — the negated verb serves as the structural opposite vocabulary.

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary divergence is the theological content of CHAR-1. The human science framework treats shame as a psychological phenomenon arising from self-evaluation and social comparison — it is horizontally grounded (in relation to others' evaluations and one's own self-concept). The verse evidence adds a vertical dimension that the science framework does not access: shame before God's holiness (M07-C) is not primarily self-evaluation or social comparison but the response of the creaturely self to the divine holiness that exposes the gap between creature and creator. The M07-C mode (Ezr 9:6; Eze 36:32; 43:10) cannot be reduced to social psychology. The divergence reveals that CHAR-1 has a dimension (the shame-before-holiness) that exceeds what any horizontal framework can account for — it requires a theology of divine holiness and creaturely moral accountability.
