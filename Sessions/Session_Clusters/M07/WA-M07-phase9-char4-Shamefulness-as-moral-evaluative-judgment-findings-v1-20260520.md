## T1 — Defining the Characteristic

**T1.1.1 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — "Shamefulness as moral-evaluative judgment." The name's structure: "shamefulness" names the quality being evaluated (the condition of being shameful, as distinct from the experience of shame); "as moral-evaluative judgment" specifies the operation — this is the conscience's assessment rather than a felt experience. The name distinguishes CHAR-4 from CHAR-1 (the felt inner experience of shame) by locating it in the evaluative rather than the experiential register: CHAR-4 is what conscience says about conduct or objects, not what the person feels in response to their own exposure.

---

**T1.1.2 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — Aischros (G0150) — shameful, base, disgraceful — an adjective naming the moral quality of conduct or objects. Aischunē (G0152) in its evaluative register (2Cor 4:2; Phili 3:19) — the shame-quality of conduct recognised by conscience. Aschēmosunē (G0808) — indecency, improper form — the quality of what violates proper form or order. Atimia (G0819) in VCG-02 — dishonorable passions — names the moral-evaluative quality of certain appetites. Together: CHAR-4 is the adjectival-quality register of shame — shameful is a quality of things and conduct, not only an experience of persons.

---

**T1.1.3 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 is constitutive rather than directional: it names a quality (what is shameful) rather than a direction. The relational implication is toward God's sight and the community's moral standard — the shameful is what cannot bear scrutiny before God (2Cor 4:2) or community (Eph 5:12). The constitutional implication is that CHAR-4 is located in the conscience — the faculty that registers moral quality.

---

**T1.2.1 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 is primarily a quality — a property of conduct, objects, or inner orientations — rather than an act, condition, or disposition in the usual sense. "It is shameful for a woman to speak in church" (1Cor 14:35) — shamefulness is a quality of the act. "Disgraceful, underhanded ways" (2Cor 4:2) — shameful is the quality of the conduct-type. In VCG-03, the inverted-conscience cases (Phili 3:19; Hos 4:18) are a disposition: the person has a perverted evaluative faculty.

---

**T1.2.2 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — Three constituent elements across the three VCGs: (1) Shameful conduct and objects (VCG-01): the moral quality of specific conduct-types and objects — violations of inner propriety, deceptive ways, idolatrous shameful objects. (2) Indecent desires and moral disorder (VCG-02): the moral quality of specific inner-appetite types — dishonorable passions as the inner driving force of moral disorder. (3) Inverted conscience (VCG-03): the corruption of the evaluative faculty itself — the conscience that treats disgrace as honour.

---

**T1.2.3 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — Shamefulness as moral-evaluative judgment is the conscience's recognition that certain conduct, objects, or inner orientations violate the moral propriety established by God's standard and mediated through created order, community norms, and scripture. It is the evaluative-qualitative dimension of shame (what is shameful) rather than the experiential dimension (what shame feels like). Primary vocabulary: aischros, aschēmosunē. Secondary: aischunē and atimia in their evaluative registers. Three registers: the shameful quality of conduct/objects, the shameful quality of disordered desires, and the inverted evaluative faculty that treats shameful as honourable.

---

**T1.3.1 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — The structural opposite of CHAR-4 is the honourable, the proper, the decent — conduct and objects that can bear scrutiny before God and community. The evidence pairs these: "we refuse to practice cunning or to tamper with God's word, but by the open statement of the truth we would commend ourselves to everyone's conscience in the sight of God" (2Cor 4:2) — the honourable opposite of shameful underhanded ways is open, truthful conduct that commends itself to the conscience.

---

**T1.3.2 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 excludes conduct that can bear open scrutiny (2Cor 4:2 — "shameful, underhanded ways" are the opposite of openness). CHAR-4 excludes proper appetitive ordering (VCG-02 — "dishonorable passions" are the corruption of natural relations). CHAR-4 excludes genuine moral seriousness: "it is shameful even to speak of the things that they do in secret" (Eph 5:12) — shameful conduct excludes the possibility of open acknowledgment.

---

**T1.3.3 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 is not CHAR-1 (the felt experience of shame): CHAR-4 is the conscience's assessment of what is shameful, not the felt collapse of dignity in the person who experiences shame. CHAR-4 is not CHAR-2 (enforced humiliation of standing): shamefulness as a quality of conduct is a moral-evaluative determination, not a social-enforced downward movement. CHAR-4 is not CHAR-3 (dishonour as relational worth-denial): dishonour is what one does to another; shamefulness is the quality of conduct assessed by conscience against a standard.

---

**T1.4.1 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — Three operational modes corresponding to the three VCGs: (1) Conscience identifying shameful conduct and objects (VCG-01): the moral-recognitive operation of the conscience that names specific conduct-types or objects as shameful — deeds in secret are so degraded that speaking of them is shameful (Eph 5:12). (2) Conscience identifying shameful inner desires (VCG-02): the moral-recognitive operation identifying certain appetites as the inner driving force of moral disorder — dishonorable passions (Rom 1:26). (3) Inverted conscience (VCG-03): the corruption of the evaluative faculty itself — glorying in shame (Phili 3:19), loving dishonour (Hos 4:18).

---

**T1.4.2 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 operates differently depending on the object of the evaluative judgment. When the conscience evaluates external conduct (VCG-01), it is assessing what actions or objects are shameful in relation to community norms and God's standard. When it evaluates inner desires (VCG-02), it is assessing the quality of the appetitive faculty itself. When it inverts (VCG-03), the evaluative faculty itself is the subject of concern rather than any specific conduct or desire.

---

**T1.4.3 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — CHAR-4 has a communicative mode: the conscience that has recognised shamefulness can articulate it. 1Cor 14:35 ("it is shameful for a woman to speak in church") is the articulated moral evaluation. 2Cor 4:2's renunciation of shameful ways is the public declaration of the conscience's finding. The inverted conscience of Phili 3:19 "glories in their shame" — it communicates its corrupted evaluation as honour.

---

**T1.5.1 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — The immediate response to genuine recognition of shamefulness (functioning CHAR-4) is recoil and renunciation: Paul renounces disgraceful, underhanded ways (2Cor 4:2). The conscience that recognises Eph 5:12's deeds as shameful will not name them — the immediate response is avoidance of speech and act. In the inverted mode (VCG-03), the immediate response is glorying — the conscience, having inverted its evaluative standard, embraces what it should recoil from.

---

**T1.5.2 — (no inline label)**
**[CHAR-4]** E — Variation exists between VCGs. VCG-01 immediate response: recoil and renunciation. VCG-02: moral-recognitive identification of disordered desire as dishonorable. VCG-03: glorying (the inverted response). The variation confirms the three distinct registers of CHAR-4.

**T7.1.1 — (no inline label)**
