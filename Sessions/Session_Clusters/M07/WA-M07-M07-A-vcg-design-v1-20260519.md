## Reading observations

Reading across all 63 verses, five registers emerge within the unjust-wound characteristic:

1. **Shame from enemy aggression and opposition** — shame called upon enemies who attack the innocent; shame threatened by hostile opposition but averted by God; the psalmist's dishonour from those who hate without cause.
2. **Shame from disappointed trust in human powers** — shame arising when Egypt, Cush, or other allies fail; the inner wound of misplaced reliance exposed when the trusted object collapses.
3. **Shame as perpetual burden carried by the innocent/suffering** — ongoing, inescapable dishonour covering the face; shame borne for God's sake; the servant willingly enduring humiliation; the chronic condition of one unjustly disgraced.
4. **Shame promised removal / transformation** — God's promise to remove shame permanently; shame of Israel's past to be forgotten; restoration replacing disgrace; shame transformed into double portion.
5. **Shame averted by divine vindication / righteousness** — the righteous not put to shame because trust is well-placed; God's help protecting from inner collapse; Paul's confidence vindicated; hope shielding from shame.

Five VCGs. The "shame averted" and "shame promised removal" registers are closely related but distinguishable: VCG-04 covers active promises and divine acts of removal/transformation; VCG-05 covers the present shield — the inner confidence that shame will not overtake because of trust or divine help. They may read as one larger group; I keep them separate because the temporal orientation differs (future reversal vs present protection).

## VCG M07-A-VCG-01 — Shame from enemy aggression and opposition

**Description:** Verses where shame is threatened, invoked, or experienced as the result of hostile action by enemies, accusers, and opponents. The psalmist is under attack from those who seek his ruin, plot his downfall, or gloat over his suffering; shame is both the wound he suffers from their pressure and the counter-invocation he calls upon them as the just reversal of their malice. The inner dynamic centres on the tension between the psalmist's vulnerability to disgrace and his confidence that God will expose the enemies' hostility as the true occasion for shame. Jonathan's pain at David's public disgrace and Job's accusation of his friends' shameless wrongdoing belong here — shame as the wound inflicted by others' aggression on the innocent.

**Anchor:** vc=37310 — Psa 44:15 (bo.shet) — "Shame is a constant visible covering over the psalmist's face — it is an all-day, inescapable inner-and-outer state of disgrace that cannot be hidden or shaken off." Anchors the VCG because it most directly names the unjust wound: constant, perpetual, all-day, inescapable — not a moment's exposure but a sustained injury caused by enemies.

## VCG M07-A-VCG-02 — Shame from disappointed trust in human powers

**Description:** Verses where shame arises specifically from misplaced reliance on human allies, military powers, or earthly resources that prove incapable of delivering. The inner dynamic is the collapse of confidence: those who leaned on Egypt, Cush, or other powers expected shelter and strength; what they receive instead is exposure, disgrace, and the felt wound of having trusted the wrong object. The shame is not moral condemnation of idolatry (that belongs to M07-B) but the experienced inner wound of the one who trusted and was let down.

**Anchor:** vc=65531 — Isa 20:5 — bosh — "Those who placed their hope and boast in Cush and Egypt will be dismayed and ashamed when those powers fail — the shame flows from misplaced relational trust, felt inwardly when the object of hope collapses." Most directly evidences the mechanism: hope placed, then shame received.

## VCG M07-A-VCG-03 — Shame borne for God's sake / shame of the righteous sufferer

**Description:** Verses where shame is carried by the innocent or righteous specifically in the context of suffering for God's sake, bearing disgrace as part of faithful witness, or enduring unjust exposure with inner steadfastness. The servant of Isaiah who endures physical humiliation without hiding his face, the apostles counted worthy to suffer dishonour for Christ's name, Christ himself despising the cross's shame — these evidence a register within M07-A where shame is not merely received passively but consciously borne as the cost of faithfulness. The inner faculty engaged is will directed toward a greater purpose, allowing the shame-wound to be absorbed rather than crushing the person.

**Anchor:** vc=34970 — Heb 12:2 — aischunē — "Jesus endured the cross by despising its shame — the inner will consciously set aside the disgrace attached to crucifixion, motivated by the joy set before him, showing shame can be overcome by a greater inner object of hope." Most definitionally evidences the borne-for-God's-sake register.

## VCG M07-A-VCG-04 — Shame promised removal and transformation

**Description:** Verses where God explicitly promises to remove, forget, or transform the shame that has covered his people. The unjust wound is not permanent; God's restorative act reverses it. Israel's youthful shame will be completely forgotten; dishonour will give way to double portion and rejoicing; the outcast's shame will be turned into praise. The inner-being content is shame as an identifiable condition that God acts to undo — the named wound and its promised healing belong together. The temporal orientation is future: "you will not remember the shame of your youth."

**Anchor:** vc=37320 — Isa 61:7 — bo.shet — "Shame will be replaced by a double portion; the inner weight of disgrace that once defined Israel's identity will be overturned into everlasting joy and abundant possession." Anchors because it most directly names shame as a defined inner burden that divine reversal transforms into its opposite.

## VCG M07-A-VCG-05 — Shame averted: trust, hope, and divine protection

**Description:** Verses where shame is not yet removed but is actively averted or does not overtake because trust in God functions as the present inner shield. The righteous are not put to shame in evil times because their trust is well-placed. Paul will not be ashamed before Christ because hope directs his will. The abiding believer does not recoil in shame at Christ's appearing. Suffering as a Christian does not produce shame but God-honouring response. This register is distinguished from VCG-04 (future removal) by its present orientation: shame is the immediate inner threat; the trusting faculty is the present protection.

**Anchor:** vc=34837 — Psa 37:19 — bosh — "The righteous are not put to shame even in evil times—their inner security is unaffected by external crisis because their trust is well-placed, and shame (as inner collapse) does not overtake them."
