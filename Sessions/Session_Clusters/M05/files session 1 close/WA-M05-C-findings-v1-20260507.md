## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The verse evidence reveals mercy as God's prerogative toward the guilty — the exercise of clemency that overrides the strict application of condemnation where guilt is real. Group 1628 (eleeō — divine mercy as sovereign ground, 12 verses): Rom 9:15-16 — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy... so then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy" — mercy is presented as God's sovereign prerogative, not a response to human merit. Rom 11:32 — "God has consigned all to disobedience, that he may have mercy on all" — mercy's scope is universal and its source is solely in God's will. Group 1632 (eleos — God's mercy as attribute, 14 verses): Eph 2:4 — "God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us" — mercy is named as a divine richness, an abundance that exceeds what is deserved. The character of God revealed is one who is disposed toward clemency when guilt stands before him — whose default inclination is to show mercy rather than to exact the full weight of condemnation.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, extensively. Group 1628 (12 verses, Rom 9:15-32 and the parallel passages) is entirely composed of divine mercy as sovereign ground. Group 1632 (14 verses) covers the full range from Luke's infancy narrative to the epistles. Luk 1:50 — "his mercy is for those who fear him from generation to generation"; Luk 1:72 — "to show the mercy promised to our fathers"; Luk 1:78 — "the tender mercy of our God." The significance for the human person: the human who shows mercy is acting in the image of a God who is constitutively disposed to show it. Mat 5:7 (Groups 1630-1631) — "blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy" — the human's mercy participates in the divine mercy-economy: showing mercy reflects the image and receives the benefit. The human's mercy is not self-generated but is the echo of the divine mercy by which they themselves have been received.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**[C — Mercy]** S — Scripture is not silent. Mercy is among the most explicitly attributed divine characteristics across M05-C. No silence finding applies.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose mercy serves in the human person as created?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The verse evidence reveals mercy's purpose as the maintenance of relationship across the gap created by guilt, failure, or helplessness. Group 1630 (eleeō — human mercy shown, 4 verses): Mat 18:33 — "should not you have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I had mercy on you?" — mercy in the human person sustains the community by preventing the escalation of obligation into condemnation. Rom 12:8 (Group 1630) — "the one who does acts of mercy, with cheerfulness" — mercy as a charismatic function within the body, equipping the community to absorb and relieve the conditions of need and failure. Group 1631 (eleēmōn — mercifulness as character, 2 verses): Heb 2:17 — "that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in the service of God" — mercy in the representative-mediatorial role is the purpose for which Christ took on human experience. Mercy equips the person to be a bridge across the inequality-and-guilt gap — to hold relationship open where strict justice would close it.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether mercy is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The evidence most clearly positions mercy as a response to the fallen condition. The judicial frame that mercy presupposes — guilt, condemnation, inequality — is a feature of the fallen world. Group 1628 (12 verses): every instance of divine mercy in this group is exercised toward persons who are disobedient (Rom 11:30-32), guilty (1Ti 1:13 — Paul as "blasphemer, persecutor, insolent opponent"), or in need of mediation (2Cor 4:1). Group 1629 (12 verses): the mercy cry (eleēmē) is always the cry of the sick, blind, leprous, or condemned — conditions that reflect the fallen order. Heb 2:17 (Group 1631): Christ became human specifically to be a merciful high priest — the Incarnation is the response to fallen humanity's need for mercy. The evidence does not evidence mercy as a pre-fall category; it presupposes guilt and need as its field of operation.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that mercy is oriented toward a future fullness?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. Jude 21 (Group 1633): "keep yourselves in the love of God, waiting for the mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ that leads to eternal life" — mercy is eschatologically directed: the mercy of Christ is what the believer is waiting for at the final day. 2Ti 1:18 (Group 1633): "may the Lord grant him to find mercy from the Lord on that day!" — eschatological mercy as the final reception of the one who has been faithful. Group 1632: Tit 3:5 — "he saved us... according to his own mercy... so that being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life" — mercy as the ground of salvation that leads to the inheritance. Mercy's future fullness is its eschatological vindication of those who have received and shown it (Mat 5:7 — "they shall receive mercy").

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does mercy express the divine image in the human person?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy expresses the image by placing the human in the position of the one who determines whether to condemn or to show clemency — imaging the God who holds the same position over guilty humanity. Group 1630 (Mat 18:33): the forgiven servant who withheld mercy from his fellow servant failed to image God precisely at this point — he occupied the judge's position but did not exercise it as God exercises his. Luk 10:37 (Group 1632): "the one who showed him mercy" as the definition of being a neighbour — mercy as the image-bearing act in the neighbour-relation. Group 1631 (Heb 2:17): Christ as "merciful and faithful high priest" — the image is expressed most fully in the mediatorial role, where the one with authority over the guilty chooses to act on their behalf rather than against them.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest mercy is shared between God and the human, or exclusively analogical?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Genuinely shared through the mercy-economy. Mat 5:7 (Groups 1630-1631): "the merciful shall receive mercy" — the human's mercy participates in the divine mercy-economy: showing mercy connects the person into the same relational dynamic by which they themselves stand. Mat 18:33 (Group 1630): the servant received God's mercy and was required to extend it — the shared mercy flows from God through the recipient-turned-giver. The sharing is real but asymmetric: God's mercy is sovereign (Rom 9:15-16 — Group 1628), inexhaustible (Luk 1:50 — Group 1632), and unearned; human mercy is derived, limited, and commanded.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of mercy reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1625 (aneleēmōn — mercilessness, 1 verse): Rom 1:31 — "ruthless (aneleēmōn)" placed in the list of characteristics that mark the person whose inner moral architecture has collapsed in Paul's descending depravity sequence. Mercilessness is a diagnostic of image-collapse. Group 1626 (anileōs — mercilessness forfeiting mercy, 1 verse): Jam 2:13 — "judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy" — the person who shows no mercy has forfeited participation in the mercy-economy; their image-bearing capacity at this point is inoperative. Conversely, Group 1631 (eleēmōn — mercifulness as character): Mat 5:7 — the merciful person is blessed, positioned within the mercy-receiving community — their image-bearing is active and reciprocally confirmed.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use mercy typologically?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. Heb 2:17 (Group 1631) — "he had to be made like his brothers in every respect, so that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in the service of God, to make propitiation for the sins of the people" — the Levitical high priest is the type; Christ is the antitype. The OT high priest's mercy (ritual mediation for sin) is the typological vehicle pointing toward Christ's definitive mercy (propitiation). Mat 9:13 and 12:7 (Group 1633): "I desire mercy and not sacrifice" — Hos 6:6 is quoted typologically: the OT ritual sacrifice is the type whose inner reality is mercy; mercy is what sacrifice was always meant to express and what Christ's sacrifice now achieves in fullest form. Group 1628: Rom 11:30-32 — the mercy shown to Jews and Gentiles through disobedience is the eschatological fulfilment of the mercy-pattern running through Israel's history.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Dual direction, with the dominant movement from OT pattern to NT fulfilment. The Levitical high priest's mercy (OT type) → Christ as merciful high priest (NT antitype — Heb 2:17, Group 1631). The Hosea "I desire mercy" (OT prophetic critique of ritual) → Christ's citation of it (NT fulfilment — Mat 9:13, Group 1633) as the definition of his own mission. The dominant direction: OT mercy-forms establish the pattern; Christ fulfils and exceeds them. The human direction: human mercy (Mat 5:7, Mat 18:33) participates in and reflects the divine mercy that grounds it.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note explicitly.

**[C — Mercy]** — Not applicable; typological use is evidenced.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The programme names this sub-group "Mercy." The name signals clemency exercised by one with authority toward one who is guilty, helpless, or in an inferior position — the override of strict judgment in favour of the lesser party. "Mercy" carries the judicial-relational frame directly: it presupposes a position of power (the one who could condemn) and a position of need (the one who cannot save themselves). This distinguishes it from compassion (M05-B — triggered by suffering, not specifically by guilt) and from love (M05-A — the prior constitutive orientation, not a specific judicial act). The name also signals the characteristic's boundary: mercy is not the routine expectation of the guilty but the gift of the one who holds power.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[C — Mercy]** E — M05-C is entirely Greek — no Hebrew terms in this sub-group's groups, confirming it is primarily the NT mercy vocabulary.

*G1656 eleos* (Groups 1632, 1633): the noun — "mercy" as an attribute and as the quality exercised in specific acts. Definitionally: mercy as the inner disposition of clemency operating within relational and covenant contexts. Eph 2:4 (Group 1632) — "rich in mercy" — mercy as an abundant inner quality. Mat 9:13 (Group 1633) — "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — mercy as what God values above ritual, naming it as the substance of right religion.

*G1653 eleeō* (Groups 1628, 1629, 1630): the verb — "to show mercy / to have mercy." Definitionally: mercy as an enacted disposition — the movement from inner clemency to specific act of help, release, or pardon. The mercy-cry instances (Group 1629) use the imperative eleēsōn — "have mercy on me!" — showing that eleeō names the act the petitioner wants performed: a turning of the one with power toward the one in need.

*G1655 eleēmōn* (Group 1631): the adjective — "merciful" as character quality. Definitionally: the person who is constitutively disposed to show mercy. Mat 5:7 — "blessed are the merciful" — the merciful person as a character type, not merely one who occasionally performs merciful acts.

*G1652 eleeinos* (Group 328): the adjective (passive form) — "pitiable / to be pitied." Definitionally: mercy from the receiving side — the condition that calls forth mercy. 1Cor 15:19, Rev 3:17 — the pitiable state as the occasion for mercy.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[C — Mercy]** E — All three. *Directional*: mercy flows from the one with power toward the one in need — always downward in the relational hierarchy (God → humanity; king → debtor; neighbour → wounded man). Group 1629 (12 mercy-cry verses) confirms this: the petitioners cry up toward Jesus as the one with power to grant mercy. *Relational*: mercy presupposes a pre-existing relational framework — covenant (Luk 1:72 — "the mercy promised to our fathers"; Group 1632), or common humanity (Mat 5:7 — the merciful receiving mercy within the human community). *Constitutional*: the eleēmōn adjective (Group 1631) names mercy as a character quality of the person, not merely an occasional act — the merciful person is one in whom clemency is a standing inner disposition.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does mercy appear to be?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is primarily a *disposition of clemency* — the inner orientation of the one with authority toward the one who stands guilty or helpless before them, that inclines toward acquitting rather than condemning, toward helping rather than withholding. It is closer to a disposition than an act, closer to a quality than an event — though it generates specific acts of clemency. Group 1628 (12 verses): Rom 9:15-16 — God's mercy as his sovereign inner inclination, not conditioned by human action. Group 1632 (14 verses): "God, being rich in mercy" (Eph 2:4) — the richness is an inner abundance, a standing quality. The eleēmōn character (Group 1631, Mat 5:7) — the merciful person is characterised by this disposition constitutively. Distinguished from compassion (M05-B) which is an inner movement triggered by encounter; mercy is the standing disposition of the one in authority that determines how they will act when guilt or need stands before them.

**T1.2.2** — Is mercy simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The 9 groups reveal at least four constituent dimensions: (1) *Sovereign divine mercy* — beyond human merit, determined by God's will alone (Group 1628). (2) *Covenant-historical mercy* — fulfilling what was promised to the fathers (Group 1632 — Luk 1:54, 72, 78). (3) *The mercy cry and its reception* — the dynamic of petition directed toward the merciful one (Group 1629). (4) *Human reciprocal mercy* — the mercy-economy in which received mercy generates shown mercy (Groups 1630-1631). Additionally, M05-C evidences the structural opposites: mercilessness-as-character-failure (Group 1625), mercilessness-forfeiting-mercy (Group 1626), and the pitiable-condition (Group 328). Mercy's structure is therefore defined both by its positive expressions and by these three negative poles.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is the disposition of clemency in the one with authority — the inner inclination toward acquitting, helping, and releasing rather than condemning, withholding, and restraining — exercised toward the guilty, helpless, or inferior, within a relational and often covenantal framework. Constitutional location: not explicitly named in M05-C's verse evidence as a specific inner location, but associated with the heart in the compassionate-heart framework (Col 3:12 — Group 1638 in M05-B which is adjacent). Faculties engaged: volition (mercy is chosen and commanded — Mat 5:7, Jam 2:13), moral evaluation (mercy is assessed against justice — Jam 2:13: "mercy triumphs over judgment"), and relational capacity (mercy operates within a specific relational asymmetry). Impact: mercy holds relationship open across the guilt-and-inequality gap, enabling the community to sustain bonds that strict justice would sever.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Two structural opposites evidenced in the verse base. (1) *Mercilessness as character failure* (Group 1625, aneleēmōn — Rom 1:31): "ruthless" in the context of total moral collapse. Mercilessness as the absence of mercy in the person's character — not merely the failure to show mercy in a specific instance but the absence of the clemency-disposition altogether. (2) *Mercilessness that forfeits mercy* (Group 1626, anileōs — Jam 2:13): "judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy" — the structural opposite named in the context of the mercy-economy: the one who withholds mercy from others has forfeited their participation in mercy's reception. The structural opposite is therefore both (a) the absence of the disposition (Group 1625 — character deficiency) and (b) the active withholding that forfeits mercy's reception (Group 1626 — relational consequence).

**T1.3.2** — What does mercy explicitly exclude or resist?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1633 (eleos — mercy as virtue): Mat 23:23 — "you tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness" — mercy resists the substitution of religious ritual for genuine covenantal obligation. Mat 9:13 and 12:7 (Group 1633): "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" — mercy resists the reduction of right religion to ritual performance without the inner disposition of clemency. Jam 2:13 (Groups 1626/1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — mercy resists the untempered application of strict judgment where clemency is possible.

**T1.3.3** — What is mercy not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is not grace (though adjacent — grace is the free gift given to one who has no claim; mercy is specifically clemency toward the guilty). Mercy is not tolerance or indifference (it requires acknowledging the guilt or need of the other). Mercy is not justice (it is the override of justice in favour of clemency — Jam 2:13: "mercy triumphs over judgment"). Mercy is not compassion (M05-B): compassion is triggered by suffering; mercy is exercised toward guilt specifically. The Lukan infancy narrative (Group 1632: Luk 1:50, 54, 58, 72, 78) clusters eleos with covenantal faithfulness and memory — showing that mercy in that register operates within the covenant framework in a way that is adjacent to but distinct from the more general compassion of M05-B.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does mercy operate within the inner person?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Four modes evidenced. (1) *As sovereign inner disposition* (Group 1628): Rom 9:15 — God's mercy as the free inner inclination that is not conditioned by human effort or will. Mercy operates internally before any external petition reaches it. (2) *As covenant-historical reliability* (Group 1632): Luk 1:72 — "to show the mercy promised to our fathers" — mercy operating as the fulfilment of prior covenantal commitments; the inner disposition of faithfulness to what was pledged. (3) *As response to petition* (Group 1629): the mercy-cry produces a response — Mar 10:48 (anchor): "he cried out all the more, 'Son of David, have mercy on me!'" — the petition engages the mercy-disposition of Jesus and draws a response from it. (4) *As reciprocal human obligation* (Group 1630): Mat 18:33 — "should not you have had mercy on your fellow servant?" — mercy as a moral obligation generated by having received mercy. The received mercy creates an inner debt of mercy owed to others.

**T1.4.2** — Does mercy operate differently depending on context or direction?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, markedly. *Divine mercy* (Groups 1628, 1632) is sovereign, covenant-grounded, and inexhaustible — "rich in mercy" (Eph 2:4, Group 1632). *Human mercy* (Groups 1629-1631) is commanded, limited, and structured by the prior receipt of divine mercy. *Eschatological mercy* (Jude 21 — Group 1633; 2Ti 1:18 — Group 1633): mercy as what is awaited at the last day — the final exercise of clemency by the Lord over his servant. The mercy that operates eschatologically is the same characteristic but its final and most comprehensive expression. Within the human register: mercy as petition (Group 1629 — crying to Jesus) differs operationally from mercy as shown (Group 1630 — the merciful person acts).

**T1.4.3** — Does mercy have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in two registers. (1) *The mercy cry* (Group 1629): eleēsōn me — "have mercy on me!" — the speech-act of petition that directs the cry toward the mercy-holder and solicits its exercise. The mercy cry is performative — it initiates the mercy-dynamic. Mar 10:48 (anchor, Group 1629): the persistence of the cry against social pressure reveals the mercy-cry as a communicative act that must be heard for mercy to be received. (2) *The apostolic mercy-blessing* (Group 1633): 1Ti 1:2, 2Ti 1:2, 2Jo 3, Jude 2 — "grace, mercy, and peace" as a triple benediction. Mercy named in a blessing-formula is a speech-act that invokes the divine mercy upon the recipient. The benediction-formula is a communicative mode of mercy.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — From the verse evidence: (1) *Relief and renewed access* (Group 1629): the healed-and-cleansed lepers (Luk 17:13/19), the restored blind men (Mar 10:52) — the immediate response to received mercy is physical restoration, which in these cases is inseparable from the relational restoration of inclusion. (2) *Praise and glorification* (Group 1632): Luk 1:46-55 (Mary's Magnificat context — the mercy just spoken of generates the praise of Luk 1:46ff); Rom 15:9 — "the Gentiles might glorify God for his mercy" — received mercy generates praise. (3) *The obligated extension of mercy* (Group 1630): Mat 18:27-29 — the servant released from debt should immediately have shown mercy to his fellow servant. The correct immediate response is the extension of the same mercy — the mercy-economy requires the one who received mercy to become a mercy-giver.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Consistent in two patterns: (a) received divine mercy generates praise (Group 1632 — Lukan narrative); (b) received divine mercy should generate human mercy shown (Group 1630 — Mat 18 pattern). The inconsistency — evidenced by the unforgiving servant (Mat 18:28-30) — is the moral violation that the parable targets: received mercy failing to generate shown mercy is the failure of the mercy-economy.

**T1.5.3** — Note silence on immediate response.

**[C — Mercy]** S — The verse evidence is relatively sparse on the phenomenology of the mercy-cry's inner experience before the cry reaches Jesus (Group 1629). What does it feel like to be the blind man before Bartimaeus receives mercy? The verse evidence describes the cry and its persistence (Mar 10:48) but not the inner experience of waiting.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does mercy produce in the inner being over time?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1632 (eleos — God's mercy as attribute, 14 verses): Tit 3:5 — "he saved us... according to his own mercy... so that being justified by his grace we might become heirs according to the hope of eternal life" — divine mercy received produces salvation, which then generates hope of eternal life. The sustained effect is the entire new existence of the saved person. 1Pe 1:3 (Group 1632): "according to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection" — the sustained effect of received mercy is a living hope. Group 1630-1631: the sustained practice of mercy shown produces the eleēmōn character (Mat 5:7) — the person who regularly shows mercy becomes constitutively merciful.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, or capacities does sustained mercy establish?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *Hope* — 1Pe 1:3 (Group 1632): new birth to living hope through mercy. (2) *Access to God* — Heb 4:16 (Group 1633): "let us draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy" — the mercy available at the throne establishes a standing access to God. (3) *Character formation toward mercifulness* — Mat 5:7 (Groups 1630-1631): the merciful shall receive mercy — sustained showing of mercy shapes the character into the eleēmōn type. (4) *Communal sustainability* — Jam 2:13 (Group 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — the merciful community is one that can sustain bonds across failure because the mercy-disposition absorbs rather than condemns.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. The immediate response to mercy is relief and praise (T1.5.1). The sustained effect is the transformation of the person's foundational orientation: new birth to living hope (1Pe 1:3), access to God (Heb 4:16), and the formation of the merciful character (eleēmōn — Mat 5:7). The immediate is relational-situational (rescue, relief); the sustained is constitutional-formational (a changed person with a new orientation toward God and others).

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Acknowledgement of need/guilt*: Group 1629 (mercy cry) — all 12 instances involve explicit crying out from a position of need. The condition for receiving mercy is the recognition that one needs it. 1Ti 1:13-16 (Group 1628): Paul received mercy as one who was ignorant and acted in unbelief — the condition for mercy was the position of genuine guilt, not manufactured guilt. (2) *Persistent petition* — Mar 10:48 (Group 1629, anchor): Bartimaeus "cried out all the more" despite social pressure — persistence in the mercy-cry is evidenced as a condition for reception. (3) *Fear of God* — Luk 1:50 (Group 1632): "his mercy is for those who fear him from generation to generation" — the fear of God as the dispositional ground for receiving mercy across generations.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 328 (eleeinos — pitiable, 2 verses): Rev 3:17 — "you say, 'I am rich, I have prospered, and I need nothing,' not realizing that you are wretched, pitiable, poor, blind, and naked" — self-sufficiency and self-deception block the recognition of the pitiable condition that would enable mercy-reception. The Laodicean church cannot receive mercy because it does not recognise its need. Group 1626 (anileōs — Jam 2:13): the one who showed no mercy cannot receive mercy — the failure to participate in the mercy-economy blocks reception.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner state of the person who encounters mercy but does not receive it?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Rev 3:17 (Group 328): self-satisfied self-deception — "I am rich... and need nothing." The person who encounters mercy but does not receive it is the one who does not recognise the pitiable condition that mercy addresses. The inner state is a combination of self-sufficiency and blindness to one's actual condition. The mercy is available but the person's inner self-assessment prevents access to it.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy belongs primarily to the *relational-judicial* dimension — it is the inner disposition of clemency that operates within the framework of guilt, authority, and judgment. The judicial frame is constitutive: mercy without a judicial context (where guilt is the given condition) becomes something else — compassion or kindness. Jam 2:13 (Group 1633) — "mercy triumphs over judgment" — names mercy's relationship to the judicial dimension explicitly.

**T1.8.2** — What evidence supports this classification?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) The sovereign-mercy language (Group 1628 — Rom 9:15-32): mercy as God's judicial prerogative over those he has "consigned to disobedience" — the judicial frame is explicit. (2) The mercy-cry pattern (Group 1629): every cry for mercy is directed toward Jesus as the one with authority to grant or withhold it — the judicial asymmetry is the relational structure. (3) The reciprocal-mercy warning (Group 1630 — Mat 18:33; Group 1626 — Jam 2:13): the one who withholds mercy will not receive it at judgment — the judicial frame governs the mercy-economy from both ends.

**T1.8.3** — Does mercy carry secondary dimensions?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. (1) *Covenantal-historical*: Group 1632's Lukan narrative (Luk 1:50, 54, 58, 72, 78) — mercy as the fulfilment of covenantal promises across generations. The covenant is the relational frame; mercy is the content of its maintenance. (2) *Affective-compassionate*: Luk 1:78 (Group 1632) — "the tender mercy (splanchna eleous — literally: bowels of mercy) of our God" — the tender/bowel-language brings the somatic-affective dimension of M05-B's compassion vocabulary into M05-C's mercy context. This is the clearest point of contact between mercy and compassion: the splanchna-eleos compound confirms they are structurally related.

### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**T2.6.1** — What is the significance of the body in relation to mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The mercy cry (Group 1629) is consistently embodied: Bartimaeus "cried out" — the physical cry is the body's vehicle for the mercy-petition. The healing responses to mercy are bodily: the blind receive sight (Mat 20:34 — adjacent in M05-B, consistent pattern), the lepers are cleansed (Luk 17:14 — cleansing is bodily). The mercy received in Group 1629 is always expressed and received in bodily form — the restored body is the evidence of mercy shown.

**T2.6.2** — Is there direct bodily involvement?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, through the mercy-cry and the bodily restoration. The cry (Group 1629) is a vocal-bodily act; the healing/cleansing (the standard response to the mercy-cry in the gospels) is a bodily transformation. Mercy in M05-C operates in the relational-judicial domain but its expression and effects are bodily.

**T2.6.3** — What does the body's role reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The body's role in the mercy-cry sequence reveals that mercy is not a purely inner transaction — it requires the embodied expression of need (the cry) and produces bodily change (the healing). The mercy that operates in the judicial-relational domain also touches and transforms the body. This confirms mercy's connection to the M05-B compassion register: mercy that is truly exercised does not remain purely judicial but engages the whole person, including the body.

### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**T2.7.1** — Does mercy flow inner to outer or outer to inner?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Primarily inner to outer: God's mercy is the prior inner disposition (Rom 9:15 — Group 1628: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" — the disposition precedes the encounter) that flows outward in specific acts of clemency. The mercy-cry (Group 1629) does not generate mercy in God; it draws forth what was already present as an inner disposition. The direction is: inner disposition → outer act, with the petition as the occasion rather than the cause.

**T2.7.2** — What does this reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The inner-to-outer direction confirms mercy as a genuine inner disposition rather than a reactive mechanism — God is not merciful because he is petitioned but is petitioned because he is merciful. The sovereignty language of Group 1628 (Rom 9:15-16) directly supports this: the petition does not determine whether mercy is shown; the inner divine disposition does.

**T2.7.3** — Note bidirectional evidence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — No significant bidirectional or anomalous evidence in M05-C. The direction is consistently inner (divine disposition) → outer (enacted mercy) → the petitioner's body and inner being.

### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**T2.8.1-3** — Does mercy leave a bodily deposit?

**[C — Mercy]** S — No somatic deposit evidenced in M05-C's verse base. The effects of mercy are relational (restored standing before God), constitutional (new birth — 1Pe 1:3, Group 1632), and social (community sustainability through the mercy-economy), not somatic. The healings in Group 1629 restore bodily function but this is the occasion and evidence of mercy, not a lasting somatic deposit of the characteristic itself.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — What does the evidence reveal about the origin or source of mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1628 (12 verses): mercy originates in God's sovereign will — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" (Rom 9:15). The source is internal to God's character, not generated by external petition or human worthiness. Group 1632 (14 verses): Eph 2:4 — "God, being rich in mercy" — the richness is intrinsic, not earned. The human mercy (Groups 1630-1631) is sourced from the received divine mercy — "as I had mercy on you" (Mat 18:33, Group 1630). Human mercy has a derivative source: it flows from having received divine mercy.

**T2.9.2** — Is the source external or internal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Divine mercy: internal to God, flowing outward. Human mercy: derived externally (from received divine mercy), expressed outward. The distinction is important: the human person does not possess mercy from within themselves as a native capacity in the same way they might possess natural affection; they show mercy because they have received it from the one whose mercy is internally sourced.

**T2.9.3** — What does the source reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The sovereign-internal source of divine mercy (Group 1628) reveals that mercy cannot be coerced, manipulated, or generated by human action — it can only be received. The petition (Group 1629) is the appropriate posture: not demanding but crying out. The derivation of human mercy from divine mercy (Group 1630) reveals that the mercy-economy is theologically structured: human mercy is a participation in what has been received, not an independent virtue.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does mercy involve inner movement?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in two directions. (1) *The movement of divine mercy toward the guilty*: the consistent pattern of Group 1628 (Rom 11:30-32 — mercy shown to the disobedient) and Group 1632 (Eph 2:4-5 — "even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ") shows divine mercy as a movement toward persons who cannot move toward God. (2) *The movement of the petitioner toward the mercy-holder*: Group 1629 (12 mercy-cry verses) — the persistent crying-out is a movement of the entire person toward Jesus. The mercy-cry is a constitutional movement of the whole self toward the one who holds mercy.

**T2.10.2** — Direction and character.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Divine mercy moves downward (from the one with authority toward the one in need); the mercy-petition moves upward (from the needy toward the one with authority). The meeting-point is the moment of mercy-reception — when the downward movement of divine mercy meets the upward movement of petition. The mercy-cry passages (Group 1629) all evidence this meeting-point as the structural moment of the characteristic.

**T2.10.3** — Constitutional transformation produced.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. 1Pe 1:3 (Group 1632): "born again to a living hope" — the received mercy produces constitutional transformation: the person is born again, which is the most fundamental inner-being transformation in the NT vocabulary. The constitutional movement of mercy therefore produces the deepest constitutional change available in M05-C's evidence: new birth.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does mercy engage the perceptual faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in the mercy-cry sequence. Group 1629: the consistent pattern is that the petitioner perceives Jesus passing (Mar 10:47 — "when he heard that it was Jesus of Nazareth") and the perception triggers the cry. Mar 10:48 (anchor, Group 1629): the crowd perceives the blind man's cry and rebukes him; Jesus stops — the perception of the cry determines the response. The perception of need by the one with mercy-capacity is also evidenced: Luk 7:13 (Group 333 — M05-B adjacent): "when the Lord saw her" — the seeing precedes the compassion/mercy response. Heb 4:16 (Group 1633): "let us... draw near to the throne of grace that we may receive mercy... in time of need" — the need must be perceived and the appropriate source approached.

**T3.1.2** — Is perception a trigger, component, or product?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Perception is the *trigger for petition*: the petitioner perceives Jesus and cries out. Perception also functions as the *mercy-holder's moment of engagement*: Jesus stops, Jesus turns, Jesus addresses the petitioner — perception of the need precedes the mercy-act. Unlike compassion (M05-B) where the perception of suffering directly triggers the inner bowel-convulsion, mercy's perceptual trigger is more mediated: the perception of Jesus (the mercy-holder) enables the petition, which then engages his mercy.

**T3.1.3** — What does this reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy requires a two-way perceptual engagement: the needy person perceives the mercy-holder and cries out; the mercy-holder perceives the cry and responds. The mercy-cry sequence (Group 1629) is constitutively dialogical — both parties must perceive and respond. This distinguishes mercy from the more spontaneous one-way perceptual trigger of compassion (M05-B).

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does mercy engage the cognitive faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in the mercy-evaluation dimension. Jam 2:13 (Groups 1626, 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — the cognition required is the assessment of when mercy is appropriate relative to judgment. Mat 23:23 (Group 1633): "the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness" — mercy as a cognitive moral category alongside justice, requiring assessment in specific cases. Rom 9:15-16 (Group 1628): God's sovereign mercy is not cognitively conditioned by human action, but its application involves the election of specific objects — a sovereign cognitive act.

**T3.2.2** — What is the relationship between mercy and cognition?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy operates in the judicial-relational frame (T1.8), which is inherently a cognitive frame: judging, evaluating, weighing guilt against clemency. The cognition is not the source of mercy (which is the inner disposition of clemency) but the faculty that applies mercy to specific cases. The merciful person must cognitively assess when mercy is appropriate and how to express it. Group 1633 (Jam 2:13 — "mercy triumphs over judgment") names a cognitive moral conclusion: mercy, properly understood, is the superior claim.

**T3.2.3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — The evidence is silent on mercy as a cognitively generated phenomenon — mercy does not arise through reasoning but is applied through it. The inner disposition precedes and is not produced by cognitive assessment.

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does mercy engage the memory faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in the covenant-historical register. Group 1632 (Luk 1:54): "he has helped his servant Israel, in remembrance of his mercy" — God's mercy is exercised in remembrance of prior covenantal commitment. Luk 1:72 (Group 1632): "to show the mercy promised to our fathers and to remember his holy covenant" — memory of promise is the ground of mercy's exercise. The mercy that fulfils the covenant is exercised through divine memory — God remembers the mercy he pledged and shows it.

**T3.3.2** — What role does memory play?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Memory of prior mercy received creates the expectation and ground for mercy-petition: "do not remember against us our former iniquities; let your compassion come speedily" (Psa 79:8 — M05-B adjacent, but the pattern applies). The memory of the mercy promised to the fathers (Luk 1:72, Group 1632) is what makes the NT mercy's exercise a covenant-fulfilment rather than a novel act. Memory links the mercy shown in Christ to the mercy pledged to Abraham — the covenant-memory is the thread of continuity.

**T3.3.3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — The evidence is relatively sparse on the human's use of memory in the mercy-economy within M05-C specifically. The primary memory evidence is in the divine memory of covenant-promise (Group 1632 — Lukan narrative).

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does mercy engage the affective faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in the tender-mercy register. Luk 1:78 (Group 1632): "the tender mercy (splanchna eleous) of our God" — the splanchna compound imports the somatic-affective register of M05-B into M05-C. The "tender" quality of mercy names an affective warmth within the judicial disposition. Group 1629 (mercy-cry, 12 verses): the anguish of the blind men, the grief of the Canaanite mother (Mat 15:22), the desperation of the leprous (Luk 17:13) — the petition is affectively saturated, and the mercy-response addresses this affective state.

**T3.4.2** — Is affect central or secondary to mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Secondary to the judicial-dispositional character but genuinely present. Mercy is more judicial than compassion (M05-B) and less affective in its primary operation. The Luk 1:78 tender-mercy compound is the most direct affective evidence, and it is the one place where the splanchna register enters M05-C. The primary character of mercy is judicial clemency; the affective warmth of the splanchna-eleos compound enriches this without constituting it.

**T3.4.3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — The evidence is more sparing on affective content in M05-C than in M05-B. The judicial frame dominates; the affective is present at the margins (Luk 1:78 tender mercy; the anguish of the mercy-petition).

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1-3** — Does mercy engage the creative faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** S — The verse evidence does not evidence creativity as a dimension of mercy's operation. Mercy is responsive (to guilt and need) and judicial (involving evaluation and exercise of authority). Silent on creativity.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does mercy engage the volitional faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, centrally. Group 1628 (12 verses): Rom 9:15-16 — divine mercy as sovereign will — "I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy... it depends... on God, who has mercy." The divine volition is the determinative ground of mercy. For the human person: Mat 5:7 (Group 1631) — "blessed are the merciful" — mercifulness is a character quality achieved through volitional commitment to showing mercy. Mat 18:33 (Group 1630) — "should not you have had mercy?" — the question names the volitional failure: the servant chose not to show mercy when he could and should have.

**T3.6.2** — What is the relationship between volition and mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is eminently volitional: it is not forced by external compulsion but freely chosen by the one with authority. Rom 9:15-16 (Group 1628) establishes that divine mercy is purely volitional — not determined by human action. The human mercy (Group 1630) is commanded (Mat 18:33 — the rhetorical question is a moral command) and therefore volitionally engaged. The unforgiving servant's moral failure was precisely the failure of the will to choose mercy when it was both possible and obligated.

**T3.6.3** — Is mercy fundamentally volitional?

**[C — Mercy]** E — More fundamentally volitional than affective, and more judicial than somatic. This distinguishes it from compassion (M05-B) which is more affective-somatic, and aligns it with the judicial-authority frame. The merciful person exercises a choice: to condemn or to acquit, to withhold or to extend. The choice is determinative — mercy is not compelled but given.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does mercy engage the agency faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. Group 1628 (12 verses): divine mercy as God's agential initiative toward the disobedient — "God has consigned all to disobedience, that he may have mercy on all" (Rom 11:32). The mercy-exercise is an agential act: God determines and acts. Group 1630 (4 verses): the human exercise of mercy is an agential act — Rom 12:8 (Group 1630): "the one who does acts of mercy (eleeō)" — the doer of mercy is an active agent. Luk 10:37 (Group 1632): "the one who showed him mercy" — the Samaritan's mercy is named as the decisive agential act that defines neighbourliness.

**T3.7.2** — What is the character of mercy's agency?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy's agency is the exercise of authority in the direction of clemency rather than condemnation. It is the most decisive single agential act within a judicial relationship: to acquit or to condemn, to help or to withhold. The Samaritan's mercy (Luk 10:37, Group 1632) is the paradigm: an initiative crossing social and religious barriers to help the wounded man. Mercy's agency is always initiative — it moves toward the one in need rather than waiting.

**T3.7.3** — Does mercy enable broader human agency?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. 2Cor 4:1 (Group 1628): "having this ministry by the mercy of God, we do not lose heart" — the received mercy enables sustained apostolic agency under pressure. The mercy that undergirds Paul's ministry is the ground of his persistence. Received mercy enables the agency of the one who would otherwise be disqualified from service (1Ti 1:13-16 — Group 1628: Paul received mercy precisely so that he might be an example and serve).

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does mercy engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, structurally. Mercy is constitutively a moral-evaluation category: it determines how the person in authority treats the guilty. Mat 23:23 (Group 1633): "you have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness" — mercy named alongside justice as a moral evaluation standard. Jam 2:13 (Groups 1626, 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — the moral claim that mercy is the superior moral category to strict judgment in specific contexts. This is a moral-evaluation conclusion with structural implications: mercy is not merely a nice addition to justice but its corrective and fulfilment.

**T3.8.2** — Is mercy a moral standard or subject to evaluation?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Both. As *standard*: "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" (Mat 9:13, 12:7 — Group 1633) — mercy as the evaluative criterion by which religious practice is assessed. As *subject to evaluation*: Group 1626 (Jam 2:13) — whether mercy has been shown or withheld is subject to moral evaluation with consequences. The person who shows mercy receives mercy; the one who withholds it faces judgment without mercy.

**T3.8.3** — What does mercy's role reveal about its structural place?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy occupies a high position in the moral hierarchy — not the apex (love does — M05-A, 1Cor 13:13) but structurally above ritual observance (Mat 9:13 — Group 1633) and in a determinative relationship with judgment (Jam 2:13 — Group 1633: "mercy triumphs over judgment"). Mercy evaluates religious practice and determines judicial outcome — it is an evaluative characteristic, not merely a personal virtue.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does mercy engage the conscience?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in the reciprocal-mercy obligation. Mat 18:33 (Group 1630): "should not you have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I had mercy on you?" — the rhetorical question names a conscience-level obligation. The unforgiving servant's conscience should have registered the obligation to show mercy after receiving it. The failure was a conscience-failure. Group 1626 (Jam 2:13): the person who shows no mercy faces judgment — the conscience registers this obligation and its consequences.

**T3.9.2** — What is the conscience's relationship to mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The conscience registers the obligation of received mercy: having been shown mercy by God, the person is obligated to show it to others. The conscience is mercy's internal accountability mechanism within the mercy-economy — it records the debt of mercy owed. The unforgiving servant violated this conscientious obligation; the merciful person fulfils it.

**T3.9.3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — The evidence is relatively sparse on mercy forming or transforming the conscience directly. The conscience appears as the registrant of mercy-obligation, not as the primary site of mercy's formation.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does mercy engage conscientiousness?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, through the eleēmōn character (Group 1631). Mat 5:7 — "blessed are the merciful (eleēmōn)" — the merciful person is one who consistently shows mercy, implying a sustained, conscientious pattern. Rom 12:8 (Group 1630): "the one who does acts of mercy, with cheerfulness" — cheerful consistency is the mark of the mercy-practitioner. The conscientiousness of mercy-showing is the formation of the eleēmōn character over time.

**T3.10.2** — Is conscientiousness a significant dimension?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Secondary but real. The eleēmōn character requires sustained commitment — mercy shown once does not make the eleēmōn; it is the consistent pattern of clemency-expression that constitutes the character. This is conscientiousness in the mercy-register.

**T3.10.3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — Less central than in the chesed-love register of M05-A. Mercy is more judicial (specific acts in specific relational moments) than covenantally sustained in the way that chesed is.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does mercy engage the relational capacity faculty?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, constitutively. Mercy is inherently relational — it requires a relational pair (the one with authority and the one in need). The mercy-economy (Mat 5:7, Mat 18:33) is a relational structure: God shows mercy → human receives → human shows → human receives. Every group in M05-C involves at least two relational parties. Luk 10:37 (Group 1632): the definition of neighbourliness is mercy-shown — mercy is the relational capacity in action toward the one who needs it.

**T3.11.2** — Is mercy the same as relational capacity, or its specific deployment?

**[C — Mercy]** E — A specific deployment: mercy is the relational capacity operating in the frame of guilt-and-authority, in the mode of clemency-rather-than-condemnation. Other deployments of relational capacity are love (the fundamental orientation), compassion (the response to suffering), and fellowship (the shared-life bond). Mercy is the specifically judicial-clemency deployment.

**T3.11.3** — What does mercy's relational character reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — It reveals that the human person's relational life cannot be sustained by strict justice alone — the community requires mercy to absorb failure and maintain bonds across guilt. Jam 2:13 (Group 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — not that judgment is wrong but that mercy is the higher relational principle that enables the community to survive its own failures. Without mercy, relational capacity collapses into the strict accounting of debts.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — How does mercy function in the God-to-human interface?

**[C — Mercy]** E — This is the most extensively evidenced interface in M05-C. Group 1628 (12 verses, primarily Romans 9-11): God's mercy as the ground of election and salvation — "God has consigned all to disobedience, that he may have mercy on all" (Rom 11:32). Group 1632 (14 verses): Eph 2:4 — "God, being rich in mercy... made us alive together with Christ even when we were dead in our trespasses" — mercy as the motivation of salvation against the condition of total spiritual inability. Tit 3:5 (Group 1632) — "he saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" — mercy as the operative ground of salvation, specifically excluding human merit.

**T4.1.2** — What characterises divine mercy in its human-directed form?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *Sovereign and unearned*: Rom 9:16 (Group 1628) — "it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy." (2) *Covenant-faithful*: Luk 1:54, 72, 78 (Group 1632) — mercy fulfilling what was promised to Abraham, showing faithfulness to the Abrahamic covenant. (3) *Tender in mode*: Luk 1:78 (Group 1632) — "tender mercy (splanchna eleous) of our God" — not cold judicial clemency but warm, inward-parts-level mercy. (4) *Productive of new life*: 1Pe 1:3 (Group 1632) — "born again to a living hope through the resurrection... according to his great mercy."

**T4.1.3** — What does this interface reveal about mercy's inner-being significance?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The God-to-human interface reveals that the human person's existence in right relationship with God is entirely grounded in divine mercy — not in human achievement. Eph 2:4-5 (Group 1632): "even when we were dead in our trespasses" is the most extreme expression of this: the recipients of mercy were in the worst possible state (spiritually dead, guilt-laden) and still received mercy. Mercy therefore is the characteristic that holds open the God-human relationship at its most broken point.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — How does mercy function in the human-to-God direction?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The primary form is the mercy-cry (Group 1629, 12 verses) — the human crying out to God/Jesus for mercy. This is the constitutive human-to-God mercy movement in M05-C: the petition that acknowledges need and appeals to the mercy-holder's disposition. Heb 4:16 (Group 1633): "let us draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" — the confidence to approach God is grounded in the knowledge of his mercy. The human-to-God direction is entirely petitionary.

**T4.2.2** — Is there evidence of humans showing mercy toward God?

**[C — Mercy]** S — No. Mercy flows from the one with authority toward the one in need; the human cannot occupy the mercy-giving position toward God. The direction is structurally one-way (God-to-human). This confirms the positional asymmetry noted in T4.2 (M05-B was similarly asymmetric for compassion). Mercy's relational structure requires authority + guilt/need — the human does not hold this position toward God.

**T4.2.3** — What does the directional asymmetry reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The asymmetry reveals that mercy is constitutively hierarchical — it requires that one party hold a position of authority over the other. This distinguishes it from love (which operates between God and humans in both directions) and confirms mercy as specifically the characteristic of the greater toward the lesser. The human's posture before God is always mercy-recipient, never mercy-giver; the human is always the needy party before the one with authority to condemn or acquit.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — How does mercy function when the human gives it?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1630 (4 verses): Mat 5:7 — the merciful person gives mercy; Mat 18:33 — the servant should have shown mercy to his fellow servant; Rom 12:8 — the one who does acts of mercy with cheerfulness; Jude 22 — "have mercy on those who doubt." Group 1632: Luk 10:37 — the Samaritan's mercy toward the wounded man as the definition of neighbourliness. The human giving of mercy is consistently described as the extension of clemency, help, or care toward one in a worse position.

**T4.3.2** — What is the inner-being experience of the mercy-giver?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Rom 12:8 (Group 1630): "with cheerfulness (hilarotēs)" — the mercy-act is characterised by joyful willingness, not reluctant obligation. The cheerfulness implies an inner gladness in the act of mercy-giving. Heb 2:17 (Group 1631) — Christ's mercy as "faithful" — the mercy-giving of the merciful high priest is faithful/reliable, implying a settled inner commitment rather than a variable response.

**T4.3.3** — What does mercy-giving reveal about the inner being?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy-given reveals the inner being as one that has been transformed by received mercy into a mercy-giver. Mat 18:33 (Group 1630): the servant who received mercy and did not show it reveals that received mercy does not automatically produce shown mercy — it requires the inner reception of mercy to penetrate and reshape the person's own dispositional orientation. Mercy-given cheerfully (Rom 12:8) is the evidence that the transformation has occurred.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — How does mercy function when the human receives it?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1629 (12 mercy-cry verses) provides the fullest evidencing of mercy-reception. The pattern: cry → response → restoration/relief. Mar 10:48 (anchor, Group 1629): persistent crying despite opposition → Jesus stops → healing. Group 1628 (12 verses): 1Ti 1:13-16 — Paul received mercy as the worst-case-scenario recipient (blasphemer, persecutor) — the mercy-reception establishes him as the paradigm example of mercy's scope. Group 1632: 1Pe 1:3 — "born again to a living hope... according to his great mercy" — mercy-reception produces new birth.

**T4.4.2** — What is the inner-being experience of mercy-reception?

**[C — Mercy]** E — From 1Ti 1:13-16 (Group 1628): Paul describes receiving mercy as being put on display — "for this reason I received mercy, that in me, as the foremost, Jesus Christ might display his perfect patience as an example." The inner experience of received mercy is not described primarily in emotional terms but in terms of its transformative consequence: the worst case becomes the best example. The inner experience of mercy-reception is an experience of being placed on the other side of condemnation — not merely emotionally relieved but structurally repositioned.

**T4.4.3** — What does mercy-reception reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy received positions the person as the paradigm case of what mercy can do. 1Ti 1:16 (Group 1628): Paul received mercy to be an example for those who would believe. Mercy-reception is not merely a private transaction but a public demonstration — it reveals the scope and character of the mercy-giver. The person who has received mercy becomes evidence that mercy was real and effective.

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — What are the limits of mercy in the human-to-human interface?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1626 (Jam 2:13): "judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy" — the mercy-economy has a boundary: those who persistently withhold mercy forfeit its reception. This is not a limit on the mercy available but on the conditions under which it can be received. Group 328 (Rev 3:17 — eleeinos): the self-deceived person who does not recognise their pitiable condition cannot receive mercy — not because mercy is withheld but because the condition for its reception (recognition of need) is absent.

**T4.5.2** — Is mercy bounded by justice?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, in tension with it. Jam 2:13 (Group 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — the relationship is not that mercy negates justice but that it overrides it where both are relevant. The command to show mercy (Mat 23:23, Group 1633 — "the weightier matters: justice and mercy and faithfulness") places mercy alongside justice as co-requirements. They are not alternatives but operate in productive tension, with mercy being the disposition that prevents justice from becoming mercilessness.

**T4.5.3** — Are there objects toward which mercy cannot properly be directed?

**[C — Mercy]** S — M05-C's verse evidence does not evidence objects toward which mercy is prohibited (unlike M05-B's Group 3599 — disobedient compassion). The mercy-boundary in M05-C is defined by the mercy-economy's reciprocal structure (show mercy to receive it) rather than by prohibited objects. The evidence is silent on disobedient-mercy in the M05-C register.

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**T4.6.1** — Does mercy operate in relation to spiritual beings?

**[C — Mercy]** S — The verse evidence in M05-C does not evidence mercy operating specifically toward or from spiritual beings (angels, demons). The characteristic operates in the human and divine-to-human registers within this sub-group.

**T4.6.2-3** — Note silence.

**[C — Mercy]** S — Silent on spiritual beings interface for mercy in M05-C. The evidence is human-and-divine focused.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**T5.1.1** — What transformation does mercy produce in the inner being?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Group 1632 (14 verses): the most explicit transformation: 1Pe 1:3 — "born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead... according to his great mercy." New birth is the transformation — the most comprehensive constitutional change in the NT vocabulary. Tit 3:5 (Group 1632): "the renewing of the Holy Spirit... he saved us according to his own mercy" — mercy is the ground of the Spirit's renewing work. Group 1628: 1Ti 1:13-16 — Paul's transformation from "the foremost of sinners" to apostle and example — mercy produces the person's functional transformation from disqualified to commissioned.

**T5.1.2** — Is the transformation instantaneous or gradual?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Both, depending on the register. New birth (1Pe 1:3 — Group 1632) is a decisive event — received mercy producing birth-to-hope is not gradual. But the formation of the eleēmōn character (Group 1631 — Mat 5:7) is gradual: the person becomes constitutively merciful through sustained practice of mercy-showing. The initial reception of divine mercy may be decisive; the formation of the merciful character is cumulative.

**T5.1.3** — Evidence for mercy as transformative.

**[C — Mercy]** E — 1Ti 1:13-16 (Group 1628) is the primary transformative evidence: Paul's self-identification as the foremost sinner transformed by mercy into the foremost example. The scope of the transformation (from worst-case to paradigm-case) is the evidence of mercy's transformative power.

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — What sequence does mercy produce or require?

**[C — Mercy]** E — From the verse evidence: (1) *Guilty/needy → mercy-petition → mercy-received → transformation* (Groups 1628-1629, 1632): the foundational sequence. (2) *Mercy-received → mercy-owed → mercy-shown or mercy-withheld* (Group 1630 — Mat 18 pattern): the reciprocal-obligation sequence. (3) *Mercy-withheld → judgment-without-mercy received* (Group 1626 — Jam 2:13): the consequence sequence.

**T5.2.2** — Is the sequence consistent?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Consistent in its structural shape: the mercy-economy has a fixed logic (received → owed → shown/withheld → consequence). The variation is in the response at the owed-stage: the unforgiving servant broke the sequence; the merciful person maintains it.

**T5.2.3** — Where does the sequence break?

**[C — Mercy]** E — At the mercy-owed stage: Mat 18:28-30 (Group 1630 context) — received mercy not translated into shown mercy is the canonical breaking point. The sequence can also break before it begins: Rev 3:17 (Group 328) — self-sufficient self-deception means the mercy-petition never arises, and the sequence does not start.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — What mechanisms produce change in mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *Reception of divine mercy*: the primary mechanism for producing the merciful character — having been shown mercy creates the ground for showing it. Mat 18:33 (Group 1630): "as I had mercy on you" — the divine mercy received is the mechanism and model. (2) *The command*: Mat 5:7 (Group 1631) — "blessed are the merciful" — the beatitude as a constitutive statement that shapes the community's valuation; Rom 12:8 (Group 1630) — the gift of mercy as a charismatic function requiring conscientious exercise. (3) *Covenantal memory*: the Lukan infancy narrative (Group 1632) — the recitation of God's mercy through history (Luk 1:54, 72, 78) shapes the community toward mercy by keeping the historical record of received mercy alive.

**T5.3.2** — Role of practice or habit.

**[C — Mercy]** E — The eleēmōn character (Group 1631) implies formation through practice. Rom 12:8 — "the one who does acts of mercy" — the habitual doer of mercy is distinguished as a specific charismatic function, suggesting sustained practice as the formation mechanism.

**T5.3.3** — Divine or human mechanism primarily?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Primarily divine, in two respects: (1) divine mercy is the source (Group 1628-1632); (2) the Spirit's renewing work is the mechanism of mercy's inner transformation (Tit 3:5, Group 1632). Human co-operation is required — the mercy must be received, the practice sustained — but the enabling source is divine.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — How does mercy relate to suffering and affliction?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Two relationships. (1) *Mercy as the response to suffering*: Group 1629 — the mercy-cry arises from the suffering of the blind, the leprous, the demon-possessed. Mercy is petitioned in and for suffering. (2) *Mercy as the ground of endurance through suffering*: 2Cor 4:1 (Group 1628) — "having this ministry by the mercy of God, we do not lose heart" — the received mercy is the inner-being resource that sustains the sufferer through apostolic affliction. Mercy received enables the person to endure suffering without losing heart.

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering test, refine, or reveal mercy?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Suffering reveals the mercy-holder's character. Heb 2:17 (Group 1631): Christ "had to be made like his brothers in every respect" — the shared suffering is what qualifies him as a merciful high priest. Christ's mercy is revealed and validated through his willingness to share human affliction. The suffering is the context in which mercy's quality is most clearly evidenced: whether the one with power chooses clemency or condemnation when the other suffers is the revealing moment.

**T5.4.3** — Does mercy produce or alleviate suffering?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy alleviates suffering in the mercy-cry sequence (Group 1629 — healing, cleansing, restoration). Mercy also prevents suffering: the debtor released from obligation (Mat 18:27 — M05-B adjacent pattern) does not suffer the consequences of the debt. Mercy does not typically produce suffering in the mercy-giver (unlike compassion's co-suffering dynamic in M05-B's Isa 63:9). The mercy-giver exercises authority from a position of relative ease, not through shared affliction — except in the Christ-instance (Heb 2:17, Group 1631), where the merciful high priest became so by entering into human affliction.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — How does mercy relate to formation and sanctification?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is both the ground of sanctification and its mark. As *ground*: Tit 3:5 (Group 1632) — "he saved us according to his own mercy by the renewing of the Holy Spirit" — mercy is what enables the Spirit's sanctifying work to begin. As *mark*: Mat 5:7 (Groups 1630-1631) — the merciful person is declared blessed — mercy-shown is evidence of the sanctified character. Mat 9:13 (Group 1633) — "I desire mercy and not sacrifice" — genuine sanctification expresses itself in mercy rather than ritual performance.

**T5.5.2** — Means or outcome?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Both simultaneously in M05-C. Mercy received (Group 1628-1632) is the means of salvation and therefore sanctification; mercy shown (Groups 1630-1631) is the outcome of the sanctified character. The same characteristic operates as both enabler and evidence.

**T5.5.3** — Mercy's role in characterological formation.

**[C — Mercy]** E — The eleēmōn character (Group 1631, Mat 5:7) is the characterological target: the person whose inner disposition is constitutively oriented toward clemency and help rather than strict judgment and withholding. Formation toward this character proceeds through the practice of mercy (Rom 12:8 — with cheerfulness), the receipt of mercy (1Ti 1:13-16 — the paradigm example), and the command of mercy (Mat 18:33 — the moral obligation). The fully formed person is one in whom the disposition of mercy has become constitutional rather than merely occasional.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Does mercy have an eschatological trajectory?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes, clearly. Group 1633 (12 verses): Jude 21 — "waiting for the mercy of our Lord Jesus Christ that leads to eternal life" — mercy as the content of the believer's eschatological hope. 2Ti 1:18 (Group 1633) — "may the Lord grant him to find mercy from the Lord on that day!" — the eschatological day as the context for mercy's final and definitive expression. Heb 4:16 (Group 1633) — "draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" — the mercy available at the present throne of grace is the anticipation of the eschatological mercy that completes the journey.

**T5.6.2** — What is mercy's eschatological form?

**[C — Mercy]** E — From Jude 21 (Group 1633): mercy that leads to eternal life — the final reception into eternal life as the eschatological mercy-act. 2Ti 1:18: "mercy from the Lord on that day" — the eschatological day as the context in which mercy is either found or not found. The eschatological mercy is the final judicial act of the sovereign mercy-holder (Group 1628: "I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy") executed in favour of those who are found in him.

**T5.6.3** — What does eschatological mercy reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — It reveals that mercy is not only a present-world corrective to strict judgment but an eschatological reality — the final state of the saved is a state constituted by received mercy, not earned righteousness. Tit 3:5 (Group 1632): "not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" — the final state is mercy-grounded, not achievement-grounded. Mercy's eschatological form is its fullest expression.

### T5.7 — Deposit Consequence

**T5.7.1** — What does mercy deposit in the inner being over time?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *Salvation and new birth*: 1Pe 1:3 (Group 1632) — mercy deposits the most fundamental constitutional change: new birth to living hope. (2) *Apostolic capacity and courage*: 2Cor 4:1 (Group 1628) — mercy deposits the persistence ("we do not lose heart") required for sustained ministry. (3) *The eleēmōn character*: Mat 5:7 (Group 1631) — the sustained practice of mercy deposits the merciful character. (4) *Access to God*: Heb 4:16 (Group 1633) — the mercy available at the throne establishes the standing access of the believer.

**T5.7.2** — Is there a negative deposit from mercy's absence?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Yes. Group 1626 (Jam 2:13): "judgment is without mercy to one who has shown no mercy" — the failure to show mercy deposits a future exposure to merciless judgment. Group 1625 (Rom 1:31 — aneleēmōn): mercilessness as a character quality in the descending-depravity sequence deposits the full consequence of moral collapse. The negative deposit of mercilessness is participation in the condition Paul describes as the ultimate human moral failure.

**T5.7.3** — What does the deposit analysis reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy's deposit analysis confirms the double structure of the mercy-economy: received mercy deposits new life, access, and character; withheld mercy deposits exposure to judgment. The same characteristic that is the ground of salvation is also the ground of condemnation when absent — mercy is therefore not a supplementary virtue but a foundational and determinative one.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**T6.1.1** — What characteristics most co-occur with mercy in the verse evidence?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *Grace (charis)*: Group 1633 — 1Ti 1:2, 2Ti 1:2, 2Jo 3, Jude 2: "grace, mercy, and peace" — the triple benediction pairs grace and mercy consistently. Heb 4:16 (Group 1633): "throne of grace... receive mercy" — the two are adjacent in the access-to-God context. (2) *Peace (eirēnē)*: same triple benediction — grace, mercy, peace. (3) *Love (agapē)*: Eph 2:4 (Group 1632) — "God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us" — mercy and love are named together as the co-motivating inner qualities of God's saving act. (4) *Faithfulness (pistos)*: Heb 2:17 (Group 1631) — "merciful and faithful high priest" — mercy and faithfulness as the paired qualities of the high priest. (5) *Compassion (splanchna)*: Luk 1:78 (Group 1632) — "tender mercy (splanchna eleous)" — compassion-vocabulary embedded within the mercy-declaration.

**T6.1.2** — What does co-occurrence reveal?

**[C — Mercy]** E — The grace-mercy pairing (consistently in the benediction formula) reveals these as structurally allied but distinct: grace names the free gift regardless of desert; mercy names the clemency shown to the guilty. They are listed separately because they name distinct aspects of God's relating to humanity. The love-mercy pairing in Eph 2:4 confirms that mercy is love's expression under the condition of guilt — consistent with the M05-A finding that love is the prior orientation and mercy is its form under specific conditions. The faithfulness-mercy pairing in Heb 2:17 confirms that mercy in the high-priestly function requires both the inner disposition of clemency and the reliable, sustained commitment of faithfulness.

**T6.1.3** — Surprising co-occurrences.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Jude 22 (Group 1630): "have mercy on those who doubt" — the object of mercy here is doubt rather than sin or physical need. This is an unexpected co-occurrence: mercy directed toward the epistemic condition of doubt. Doubt as the occasion for mercy (rather than correction or dismissal) is a distinctive finding in Group 1630 — it reveals that mercy's scope includes the intellectually uncertain, not only the morally guilty or physically suffering.

### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**T6.2.1** — Does mercy follow or precede other characteristics?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy *follows* guilt and need (it presupposes the fallen condition — T0.2.2). Mercy *precedes* forgiveness and restoration (it is the inner disposition from which the acts of forgiveness and restoration flow — Isa 55:7 in M05-B, and similarly Eph 2:4-5 in M05-C: mercy → making alive). Mercy *follows* love in the structural hierarchy: Eph 2:4 (Group 1632) — "because of the great love with which he loved us... even when we were dead in our trespasses [God] made us alive... by grace" — love is the prior disposition; mercy is its form under the condition of sin and death. The sequence: love → mercy (as love's judicial-clemency form) → forgiveness and new life.

**T6.2.2** — What sequences are most consistently evidenced?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) Guilt/need → mercy-petition → mercy-received → transformation (Groups 1628-1629, 1632). (2) Mercy-received → mercy-owed → mercy-shown/withheld → reciprocal consequence (Groups 1630, 1626). (3) Mercy → grace → peace (the benediction sequence — Group 1633). (4) Love → mercy → making-alive (Eph 2:4-5 sequence — Group 1632).

**T6.2.3** — Mercy's position in the hierarchy of characteristics.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy occupies a high position but not the apex. Jam 2:13 (Group 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — mercy is positioned above strict judgment within the relational-judicial framework. But love (M05-A) encompasses mercy as its form under specific conditions, placing love structurally above mercy. Mercy is the form love takes when guilt stands before it; love is broader and prior.

### T6.3 — Causal and Constitutive Relationships

**T6.3.1** — What does mercy cause or produce?

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) New birth and living hope (1Pe 1:3 — Group 1632). (2) The saving and renewing action of the Spirit (Tit 3:5 — Group 1632). (3) Making-alive from spiritual death (Eph 2:4-5 — Group 1632). (4) The eleēmōn character (Mat 5:7 — Group 1631 — through received and shown mercy). (5) Access to God (Heb 4:16 — Group 1633). (6) Apostolic persistence (2Cor 4:1 — Group 1628).

**T6.3.2** — Is mercy caused by other characteristics?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is caused by: (1) Love — Eph 2:4 (Group 1632): "because of the great love with which he loved us... made us alive." Love is the motivating ground; mercy is its expression toward the guilty. (2) Faithfulness — Luk 1:54, 72 (Group 1632): the mercy fulfils the promise to the fathers — covenantal faithfulness generates the mercy-expression. (3) The pitiable condition of the recipient (Group 328): the eleeinos state calls forth mercy — the condition of need is the occasion of mercy's exercise.

**T6.3.3** — Is mercy constitutive of other characteristics?

**[C — Mercy]** E — Mercy is partially constitutive of salvation — "he saved us according to his own mercy" (Tit 3:5 — Group 1632). Salvation is not merely mercy but is grounded in it. Mercy is also constitutive of the high-priestly function: Heb 2:17 (Group 1631) — the priest who is not merciful cannot fulfil the function of mediation. Mercy is partially constitutive of the mediation function.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — What does the lexical range reveal about mercy's breadth and depth?

**[C — Mercy]** E — M05-C is entirely Greek vocabulary — a significant finding in itself. The eleos/eleeō family covers: the noun as attribute (Group 1632 — God's richness in mercy), the verb as enacted show-of-mercy (Groups 1628-1630), the adjective as character-quality (Group 1631 — eleēmōn), and the passive adjective as the pitiable condition that calls forth mercy (Group 328 — eleeinos). The negative forms (aneleēmōn — Group 1625; anileōs — Group 1626) define mercy by its absence. The lexical range is coherent: it spans the full arc from the divine attribute (eleos — Group 1632) to the human character (eleēmōn — Group 1631) to the pitiable condition (eleeinos — Group 328) to the merciful act (eleeō — Group 1629-1630) to the absence (aneleēmōn, anileōs — Groups 1625-1626). The range maps the complete mercy-economy: attribute → act → reception → character formation → pitiable condition → absence.

**T7.1.2** — Lexical findings that challenge or complicate.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Two notable lexical findings. ### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — Interpretive challenges in the verse evidence.

**[C — Mercy]** E — Three primary challenges. (1) *Rom 9:15-18 (Group 1628)*: "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion... so then he has mercy on whomever he wills, and he hardens whomever he wills." The sovereign-mercy language requires interpretation within the context of Paul's argument in Romans 9-11 — the hardening is not God's final word (Rom 11:32: "he may have mercy on all") but operates within the narrative of elective purpose. The interpreter must hold the sovereign particularity of election (v.15-18) and the universal scope of mercy (v.32) in tension without dissolving either. (2) *Jam 2:13* (Groups 1626, 1633): "mercy triumphs over judgment" — the literary context is a warning against partiality, not a general statement that mercy always overrides judgment. The triumphant-mercy claim must be read in its specific context (the community's treatment of the poor vs rich) rather than as an absolute principle that mercy cancels judgment. (3) *Mat 5:7* (Groups 1630-1631): "blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy" — this has been read both as a statement about the mercy-economy's reciprocity (show mercy → receive mercy) and as a beatitude about character (the merciful person inhabits the kingdom). **T7.2.2** — Literary features affecting interpretation.

**[C — Mercy]** E — (1) *The Lukan infancy narrative (Group 1632, Luk 1:50-78)*: the hymnic form (Magnificat, Benedictus) shapes the mercy-vocabulary in a liturgical register — these declarations of mercy are not propositional theology but praise-speech. The literary form (hymn) means the mercy-claims are expressed at full-extension: "his mercy is for those who fear him from generation to generation" — not a careful systematic statement but a doxological affirmation. The interpreter must read them as such. (2) *The parabolic frame (Mat 18:23-35)*: the unforgiving servant parable uses the mercy-vocabulary within a narrative that is itself a theological illustration, not a direct description of specific events. The parabolic frame allows the mercy-economy to be presented in its full logic without requiring every detail to be historically specific. (3) *The apocalyptic frame (Rev 3:17)*: the eleeinos-applied-to-Laodicea is within the seven-letters section — the literary register is prophetic-judicial. The mercy-vocabulary in this frame carries the weight of divine assessment, not merely description.

**T7.2.3** — Where interpretation requires caution.

**[C — Mercy]** S — Caution is needed at two points: (1) Rom 9:15-18 must not be read as a statement of eternal fatalism — the hardening serves the mercy's universal scope (Rom 11:32); the text is not a closed door but an open question serving a larger mercy-narrative. (2) Jam 2:13's "mercy triumphs over judgment" must not be read as universalism or as a principle that mercy always cancels moral consequence — the verse is within a section on communal partiality and must be read in that context.
