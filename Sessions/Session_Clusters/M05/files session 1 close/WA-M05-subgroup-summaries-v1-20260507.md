## M05-A — Love (23 terms · 500 active verses)

The love characteristic, as evidenced by M05-A's verses, is fundamentally the inner orientation of directed attachment — the person turned toward another with loyal, committed goodwill. The Hebrew centre is che.sed: a covenantal faithfulness that never ceases (Lam 3:22), that is inscribed on the heart (Pro 3:3), that God names as his defining attribute (Exo 34:6). The Greek centre is agapē: the love that is patient and kind (1 Cor 13:4), that God first showed in Christ (1 Joh 4:19), that is poured into hearts by the Spirit (Rom 5:5), and that binds the community (Joh 13:35). The philo- compound family names the specific relational directions of the same orientation: brotherly love (filadelfia), family affection (filostorgos), love of children (filoteknos), love of the stranger (filoxenia), love of what is good (filagathos). These are not peripheral varieties — they are the same inner orientation expressing itself through the specificity of the relationship. The person-types (cha.sid, a.hav) confirm that love is constitutive of character: the chasid is not someone who occasionally shows che.sed but someone whose inner life is structured by it. The disordered register — love of the world (filia in Jas 4:4), Amnon's love that became hatred (2 Sam 13:15) — reveals that love operates as the inner mechanism of directedness and that its direction, not its mere presence, determines its moral quality. The structural opposite is not mere absence but active directional reversal: hatred is love running backwards. Constitutional location: the heart is named repeatedly as love's primary site (Pro 3:3 inscription; the great commandment — whole heart, soul, mind, strength).

*Phase 5 observation:* The philo- compounds and the che.sed-agapē families will likely form distinct groups within Phase 6. The disordered love register is a distinct and significant group. The person-types (cha.sid) are a third distinct group.

## M05-B — Compassion (15 terms · 188 active verses)

The compassion characteristic, as evidenced by M05-B's verses, is a visceral inward movement triggered by the encounter with suffering — a stirring that precedes and motivates the responding act. The somatic grounding is explicit: ra.cham is womb-derived (Isa 49:15), splanchnizō is bowel-located (Mat 9:36), and the sumpathēs family names the capacity to enter into and share another's experience (Heb 4:15; 1 Pet 3:8). What distinguishes compassion from love is its trigger — compassion is activated by the encounter with need or suffering, not simply by the relationship. Jesus's compassion in the gospels is consistently triggered by seeing crowds, disease, grief, or hunger. The OT divine compassion is similarly triggered by Israel's affliction and cry. The judicial withholding of compassion (chus in Ezekiel's repeated "my eye shall not pity") reveals that compassion's presence is a deliberate restraint from automatic judgment — its removal is the activation of strict justice. The Lamentations corpus reveals compassion through its absence: God has withdrawn it, and this withdrawal is itself described as an act, showing that compassion is the default mode of relational engagement that must be actively suspended in judgment. The racham family names the womb-root of this disposition in God: constitutively one who is moved by the sight of his people's suffering. Constitutional location: compassion appears to operate at the gut-visceral level of the inner being — the splanchn- root is somatic — but its activation produces soul-level responses and issues in acts.

*Phase 5 observation:* The judicial withholding/absence register (chus, cha.mal in Ezekiel) may form a distinct group from the positive compassion register. The splanchnizō gospel instances deserve their own group given their consistent contextual pattern.

## M05-C — Mercy (6 terms · 60 active verses)

The mercy characteristic, as evidenced by M05-C's verses, is the compassionate disposition of the greater toward the lesser — operating within a covenantal-judicial frame where guilt or helplessness is the operative context. It is most visible as what is called upon in the mercy cry: Bartimaeus, the ten lepers, the Canaanite woman, the publican in the temple — all address Jesus with eleēmē as a petition to the one with authority to grant clemency. This judicial-covenantal register distinguishes mercy from compassion: compassion is triggered by suffering; mercy operates in the frame of guilt, helplessness, or inequality. The beatitude "blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy" (Mat 5:7) identifies mercy as a reciprocal characteristic — the one who shows it participates in the mercy-logic that structures the divine-human relationship. Mat 18:33 makes this structural: having received mercy, the servant was obligated to show it. Mercy triumphs over judgment (Jas 2:13) — mercy is not the absence of judgment but its override in favour of the lesser party. The Lukan birth narrative uses eleos extensively as the content of God's covenantal faithfulness in action — mercy as the form that divine chesed takes when encountering human need. The negative forms (aneleēmōn in Rom 1:31; anileōs in Jas 2:13) confirm that mercilessness is a moral failure of inner character. Eleeinos names the condition that calls forth mercy, confirming the asymmetry of condition between giver and receiver.

*Phase 5 observation:* The mercy cry pattern (eleēmē as petition to Jesus) is a distinct group from the divine mercy as attribute group. The negative forms may be grouped together as the structural opposite group.

## M05-D — Kindness and Goodness (10 terms · 127 active verses)

The kindness and goodness characteristic, as evidenced by M05-D's verses, is the inner quality of gracious generous goodwill — oriented outward toward others and rooted constitutively in God's own character. God's kindness (chrēstotēs) is the agent that leads to repentance (Rom 2:4) — active and purposive, not passive; it is displayed in Christ (Eph 2:7) as the supreme expression of what God is constitutively like toward humanity. In the human person, kindness is Spirit-produced (Gal 5:22) and put on as a deliberate character garment (Col 3:12). Goodness (agathos) is most broadly evidenced: the good person produces good from the good treasure of the heart (Mat 12:35; Luk 6:45); no one is good except God alone (Mar 10:18) — making divine goodness the ontological ground of creaturely goodness. The agatho- verb compounds confirm that goodness is not passive: it is practised in suffering, directed toward enemies, and maintained under pressure. Haplotēs reveals that goodness has an undivided inner orientation — the openhanded person gives without reservation. The kindness/goodness characteristic's structural opposite is cruelty/severity (apotomia alongside chrēstotēs in Rom 11:22). Constitutional location: the heart is named as the treasury from which goodness flows (Mat 12:35) and the site of good conscience (1 Tim 1:5).

*Phase 5 observation:* The chrēstos/chrēstotēs kindness group and the agathos goodness group may require separate treatment in Phase 6 given the semantic distinction. The "easy/light" sense of chrēstos (Mat 11:30) is a BOUNDARY candidate that may have been set aside already.

## M05-E — Gentleness (9 terms · 30 active verses)

The gentleness characteristic, as evidenced by M05-E's verses, is the inner disposition of yielding, non-coercive, non-retaliatory strength — the power that chooses not to impose itself. The praus/praotēs family provides the clearest evidencing: the meek inherit the earth (Mat 5:5), the king comes meek on a donkey (Mat 21:5), Paul appeals by the meekness and gentleness of Christ (2 Cor 10:1), the Spirit-bearer restores the fallen in a spirit of gentleness (Gal 6:1). Gentleness operates precisely in contexts of potential force: correction, response to opposition, reception of God's word, wisdom displayed in conduct. Gentleness is not weakness — the epieikeia family reveals its character as gracious reasonableness, the willingness to yield where legal strictness would prevail. God's gentleness is itself evidenced: "your gentleness made me great" (2 Sam 22:36; Psa 18:35). The Hebrew an.vah/a.na.vah family confirms the OT grounding: humility before God and gentleness before others as a single disposition. Anexikakos adds the relational-endurance dimension: patient endurance of evil without resentment. Structural opposite: harshness, coercion, insistence on legal rights. Constitutional location: 1 Pet 3:4 is definitive — "the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit."

*Phase 5 observation:* The verse body is small (30 verses) — the Phase 6 group mapping will be efficient. The divine gentleness (2 Sam 22:36/Psa 18:35) is a distinct and analytically significant instance worth its own group designation.

## M05-F — Comfort and Encouragement (5 terms · 206 active verses)

The comfort and encouragement characteristic, as evidenced by M05-F's verses, is the inner orientation directed specifically toward the distressed, faltering, or grieving person — bringing relief, strengthening, and calling alongside. The Hebrew na.cham corpus is richest in grief and desolation contexts: Jacob refusing comfort (Gen 37:35); Rachel weeping with refusal of comfort (Jer 31:15); Lamentations' repeated lament "there is no one to comfort her." The comfort that is absent from these scenes reveals what it is when present: a bringing of inner relief that changes the person's inner state from grief to something liveable. God's comfort is eschatological and unconditional: "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12); the mother-comfort image (Isa 66:13) parallels the father-compassion image (Psa 103:13) but with specific orientation toward grief relief. The NT parakaleō family is semantically wider: comfort in affliction (2 Cor 1:3-7), exhortation toward faithful living (Acts 14:22), mutual building-up in community (1 Thes 5:11). The 2 Cor 1:3-7 passage is the richest single evidencing: comfort flows from God through the suffering of the comforter into those who suffer, with a cascading participatory structure. Structural opposite: the failed comforter (Job 16:2) and the total absence of any comforter (Lamentations).

*Phase 5 observation:* The parakaleō semantic range (comfort / appeal / exhort / urge) requires careful Phase 6 group-by-group validation. Some existing groups under parakaleō may carry appeal-urgings rather than comfort phenomena — these require conglomerate-group split testing per §9.

## M05-G — Fellowship and Participation (6 terms · 40 active verses)

The fellowship and participation characteristic, as evidenced by M05-G's verses, is the inner-relational bond of shared life — holding something in common, participating together, belonging to one another in a shared reality. The koinōnia vocabulary establishes the semantic centre: Act 2:42's fellowship as the fundamental community pattern; 1 Cor 10:16's participation in the blood/body of Christ as the vertical-divine dimension; 2 Cor 13:14's fellowship of the Holy Spirit as the Trinitarian grounding; 1 Joh 1:3, 6-7 as the most explicit statement — fellowship with the Father and Son, and with one another, as inseparable co-realities. The horizontal and vertical dimensions are not alternatives but simultaneous: walking in the light produces fellowship with one another and with God at the same moment. The koinōnos family names the person constituted by shared participation: partner in suffering, partner in the gospel, partaker of divine nature. The most analytically significant verse is 2 Pet 1:4 — participation in divine nature as the inner transformation the characteristic produces. Mat 23:30's negative instance confirms that participation constitutes moral solidarity. Katallagē evidences the restoration of the broken fellowship bond. Epoikodomeō confirms the formative dimension: the community is built up on its foundation as an ongoing participatory formation process. Structural opposite: exclusion, darkness, partnership with unrighteousness (2 Cor 6:14).

*Phase 5 observation:* The verse body is small (40 verses) but analytically dense. 2 Pet 1:4 (partaking of divine nature) is the deepest inner-being claim in the sub-group and deserves a distinct group. The koinōnia/koinōnos family will have at least two groups: vertical fellowship (with God/Christ/Spirit) and horizontal fellowship (among believers). Epoikodomeō may be a distinct group or may belong with horizontal fellowship depending on the verse evidence.
