## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence reveals that love is not primarily an act of God but an attribute of his being: Exo 34:6 names him "abounding in steadfast love" within the covenant self-disclosure formula; Lam 3:22 declares "the steadfast love of the Lord never ceases" as an unconditional ontological claim under Jerusalem's destruction. Group 1542 (agapē as God's inner source) extends this: Joh 5:42 names the love of God as an inner possession that can be absent from a person ("you do not have the love of God within you"), and 1Jo 4:8 via the group identifies "God is love" as the ontological ground. The divine love revealed in this sub-group is constitutive of who God is — not a response to human deserving but the permanent inner orientation from which all acts flow. Rom 5:8 (Group 1542) confirms: God shows his love precisely "while we were still sinners" — the love precedes and does not depend on the recipient's condition.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[A — Love]** E — Scripture attributes love to God with exceptional explicitness and frequency. Group 3603 (chesed-attribute, 59 verses) is entirely composed of declarations of divine chesed as a defining attribute: "the faithful God who keeps covenant and steadfast love" (Deu 7:9), "abounding in steadfast love to all who call on you" (Psa 86:5), the Psalm 136 litany. Group 1578 (God's a.ha.vah love, 8 verses) includes Jer 31:3: "I have loved you with an everlasting love." Group 1538 (God/Christ loves, 31 verses) includes Joh 3:16 and Joh 13:1. The significance for the human person is directional: love is first what God is, then what the human is commanded and enabled to be. Mat 22:37 (Group 1537) places the command to love in response to the prior reality of God's love — the human person's love is a reflexive participation in the divine characteristic, not an independent achievement. 1Jo 4:19 (within Group 1542's orbit): "we love because he first loved us."

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[A — Love]** S — Scripture is not silent here; love is one of the most extensively attributed divine characteristics across the entire sub-group. No silence finding is warranted.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence reveals several distinct purposes. First, love equips the person for covenantal relationship with God: Mat 22:37 (Group 1537) names love of God as the "greatest commandment," establishing love as the constitutional basis for the divine-human relationship. Second, love equips the person for the human community: Mat 22:39, Joh 13:34-35 (Group 1540) show that mutual love among believers is the mark of discipleship and the binding force of community. Third, love equips the person for moral integrity: Rom 13:10 (Group 1543) — "love is the fulfilling of the law" — love replaces the need for itemised obligation with a single constitutive orientation that generates all right conduct from within. Fourth, love equips the person to serve as an image-bearer: Joh 15:13 (Group 1542) — "greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends" — the sacrificial form of love mirrors the divine self-giving in Christ's death.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence supports both, with distinct registers. The covenantal chesed vocabulary in Group 3604 (enacted acts toward Lot, Abraham's servant, Joseph) operates within a fallen world and names God's love as responding to human vulnerability, need, and sin — this is the redemptive-response register. However, the philo- compound family (Groups 1556-1558, 1573-1574: filadelfia, filandros, filoteknos, filostorgos) names the natural affective bonds of human relationship — brotherly love, spousal love, parental love — that appear to belong to created order rather than being responses to fallenness. The Jonathan-David bond (Group 1579, 1Sa 18:3 — "he loved him as his own soul") evidences intense human love that is pre-redemptive in character. The evidence is mixed: the covenantal-sacrificial form is intensified by the fallen condition; natural affection appears to be original. The evidence does not explicitly address pre-fall love, so no strong pre-fall claim is available.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. Group 1543 (agapē as defining virtue) includes 1Cor 13:8: "love never ends. As for prophecies, they will pass away; as for tongues, they will cease; as for knowledge, it will pass away." Love is explicitly named as the one characteristic that persists into the eschatological future while other gifts cease. 1Cor 13:13 — "the greatest of these is love" — places love as the supreme abiding reality. The chesed vocabulary in Group 3603 includes Lam 3:22-23 ("new every morning; great is your faithfulness") and Psa 89:2 ("steadfast love will be built up forever") — both pointing to an eternal, inexhaustible fullness. Eph 3:19 (Group 1542): "to know the love of Christ that surpasses knowledge, that you may be filled with all the fullness of God" — the filling toward divine fullness is oriented toward love as its content.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence shows love instantiating the image in two primary ways. First, love as directed covenantal faithfulness: the human chesed in Group 1584 (Rut 3:10 — Boaz naming Ruth's faithful loyalty; the Jonathan-David covenant chesed) mirrors the divine chesed of Group 3603-3604. The human person who shows chesed is acting in the pattern of the divine character explicitly — 2Sa 9:3: "that I may show the kindness of God to him." Second, love as sacrificial self-giving: Joh 13:34 (Group 1540) grounds the command "love one another" in "just as I have loved you" — the pattern is Christ's love, which is in turn the expression of the Father's love (Joh 15:9). The human person who loves in this way instantiates the divine image at the level of its most costly expression.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence shows genuine sharing, not merely analogy. Joh 15:9 (Group 1542): "as the Father has loved me, so have I loved you" — the same love flows from Father through Son to believers, with the same verb and the same structure. Rom 5:5 (Group 1542): "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — the divine love is communicated into the human inner being, not merely imitated. The philo- compounds (Groups 1556-1558, 1573-1574) name the natural human affections which God also has (filostorgos appears in the divine economy implicitly through the father-of-Israel imagery of Hos 11:1-4). The sharing is real, though asymmetric: God's love is originating and inexhaustible; the human person's love is derivative and conditioned.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence makes this explicit. Group 1541 (disordered love, 5 verses) reveals the inverse: Joh 3:19 — "people loved the darkness rather than the light" — names love-misdirected as the defining diagnostic of the fallen condition; 2Ti 4:10 — "Demas, in love with this present world" — shows love-of-world as the mechanism of apostasy. Joh 5:42 (Group 1542): "you do not have the love of God within you" — the absence of God's love within is the diagnostic of the religious person who is nonetheless not right with God. 1Jo 4:8 (referenced in the group): "whoever does not love does not know God" — lovelessness is the diagnostic of not knowing God. The presence of love, conversely, is the mark of discipleship (Joh 13:35, Group 1540) and of belonging to God (3Jo 11, Group 1543). The image condition is visible in the direction of love.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, in the chesed vocabulary specifically. Isa 54:8 (Group 3604): "with everlasting love I will have compassion on you" — the divine chesed toward Israel after exile is framed in the terms of a renewed marriage covenant, pointing typologically toward the eschatological restoration. Isa 55:3 (Group 3604): "I will make with you an everlasting covenant, my steadfast love for David" — the Davidic chesed is extended universally, pointing toward the messianic covenant. Hos 2:19 (Group 3604): "I will betroth you to me in righteousness and in justice, in steadfast love" — the marital chesed is deployed typologically of the divine-human covenant. The philo- family's use in Joh 15:15 (Group 1572) — "I have called you friends (philos)" — is typologically significant: the friend-designation for Abraham (2Ch 20:7, Group 1576: "Abraham your friend") is taken up by Christ for his disciples, completing the typological trajectory.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[A — Love]** E — The direction runs from divine to human: the divine chesed (Groups 3603-3604) establishes the pattern; human chesed (Group 1584) is its creaturely expression. The divine love in Christ (Group 1538: Joh 3:16, Joh 13:1) establishes the pattern for human love (Group 1540: Joh 13:34 "as I have loved you"). The friend-typology runs from Abraham's designation as God's friend (OT precedent) to Christ naming his disciples friends (NT fulfilment). The direction is consistently: divine establishes pattern → human participates in or reflects it.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[A — Love]** — Not applicable; typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1).

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[A — Love]** E — The programme names this sub-group "Love." The name signals directed attachment — the person oriented toward an object with committed goodwill. "Love" as an English translation covers the full semantic range of the sub-group's 23 terms: from the covenantal-faithfulness register (chesed, a.ha.vah) through the sacrificial-agapic register (agapē, agapaō) to the natural-affection register (fileō, filadelfia, filostorgos) and the friendship register (filos, re.eh). The name's simplicity conceals structural complexity: the Hebrew and Greek terms are not synonyms but a family of related orientations sharing the common property of directed inner commitment toward another.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**[A — Love]** E — At the definitional level:

*H2617A che.sed* (Groups 3603-3604, 1584, 1585): the root signals faithfulness within a relational bond — covenant loyalty that persists through circumstance. The evidence shows it operating as both divine attribute (Exo 34:6) and human obligation (Rut 3:10, 1Sa 20:14).

*H0160 a.ha.vah* (Groups 1578-1580): names the strong affective bond of love — "I have loved you with an everlasting love" (Jer 31:3) — encompassing the intense human bonds of Jonathan-David (Group 1579) and the covenantal divine love toward Israel (Group 1578).

*G0025 agapaō / G0026 agapē* (Groups 1537-1544): at the definitional level signals love as the governing inner orientation — commanded ("love your God... love your neighbour"), defined by character ("patient, kind" — 1Cor 13:4), and named as never ceasing (1Cor 13:8). Its primary definition is moral and relational, not merely affective.

*G5368 fileō* (Groups 1562-1564): natural affection and friendship — the warmth of genuine personal attachment (Joh 11:36 "see how he loved him").

*G5384 filos* (Group 1572): the friendship bond as a social and relational category — Joh 15:15 distinguishing servants from friends by the criterion of shared knowledge.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, all three. *Directional*: love is oriented toward an object — "you shall love the Lord your God" and "love your neighbour as yourself" (Groups 1537, 1539). The direction is the primary structural feature: toward God, toward neighbour, toward enemy, toward the world (disordered). Group 1541 (disordered love) confirms this: the characteristic is the same inner mechanism; what varies is the direction. *Relational*: love presupposes a relational space — it names the inner orientation that constitutes relationship. Group 1572 (filos) makes this explicit: friendship-love names the bond that structures relationship. *Constitutional*: love operates from within the person. Mat 22:37 — "with all your heart and soul and mind" — names the inner constitutional depths from which love proceeds. The enquiry must attend to all three dimensions.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence reveals love as primarily a *disposition* that generates acts, with a secondary dimension as a *quality* of character. The disposition evidence: Group 3603 (chesed-attribute, 59 verses) names chesed as what God *is* constitutively, not merely what he does on occasion — "abounding in steadfast love" (Exo 34:6) is a character description, not an event report. Lam 3:22 — "the steadfast love of the Lord never ceases" — is the most definitive statement: chesed is the permanent inner orientation that does not cease even when its expression is suspended. Group 1543's 1Cor 13:4 — "love is patient and kind" — describes love by listing dispositional qualities: what love *is*, not what it does. The act dimension: Group 3604 (chesed-enacted, 56 verses) shows love as specific acts — "you have shown me great kindness in saving my life" (Gen 19:19) — but these acts flow from the prior disposition. The distinction between love-as-disposition and love-as-act is the analytical key for Phase 8.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

**[A — Love]** E — Complex, with constituent elements visible from the evidence. The 37 groups themselves reveal the structure: love has at minimum (1) a chesed-covenantal register (eternal attribute, enacted acts, human reciprocal loyalty, failure of loyalty), (2) an agapic register (God's initiating love, human love of God, love of neighbour/enemy, mutual community love, disordered love), (3) a filial/natural-affection register (friendship, family bonds, natural loyalty), and (4) a person-type register (the chasid — Group 1586 — as the one whose character is constituted by love-faithfulness). These are not arbitrary groupings but structurally distinct phenomena within the single characteristic. The evidence further reveals that love has directional sub-structures (toward God, toward neighbour, toward self, toward the world) and modal sub-structures (covenantal, sacrificial, natural, disordered).

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

**[A — Love]** E — Love is the inner orientation of directed attachment — the person turned toward another with committed goodwill that persists through circumstance. Constitutional location: the heart is its primary site (Mat 22:37 — "with all your heart"; Pro 3:3 — chesed inscribed on the heart); it also engages the soul and mind in the great commandment's formulation. Faculties engaged: volition (love is commanded — Mat 22:37 — implying it is not merely spontaneous affect but a decision of the will), affect (the fileō and a.ha.vah registers name genuine warmth and yearning — Group 1579, Gen 29:20: Jacob's service "seemed to him but a few days because of the love he had for her"), and relational capacity (love is the constitutive relational orientation toward the other). Impact on the person: love that is rightly directed constitutes the person as a moral and relational agent — "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Rom 13:10, Group 1543); love misdirected constitutes the person's departure from God and community — "people loved the darkness" (Joh 3:19, Group 1541).

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

**[A — Love]** E — The primary structural opposite is *hatred* (sane). The evidence makes this explicit in multiple directions. Group 1580 (a.ha.vah as moral loyalty) includes Pro 10:12: "hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offences" — the direct antithesis. Group 1579 (intense human love) includes 2Sa 13:15: "then Amnon hated her with very great hatred, so that the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her" — love and hatred occupy the same inner mechanism; the switch from one to the other is the most dramatic inner-being movement the verse evidence provides. Group 1541 (disordered love) in context makes the second structural opposite visible: love of God versus love of the world are mutually exclusive (1Jo 2:15, Group 1543's orbit: "if anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him"). Joh 3:19 (Group 1541): love of darkness excludes love of light. The structural opposite is thus both hatred (the active reversal) and love-of-the-wrong-object (the misdirected form of the same faculty).

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A — Love]** E — From 1Cor 13:4-6 (Group 1543): love excludes envy, boasting, arrogance, rudeness, self-seeking, irritability, and resentment — a sustained list of what love is constitutively incompatible with. Joh 3:19 (Group 1541): love of light excludes love of darkness. 1Jo 2:15 (Group 1543's boundary): love of the Father excludes love of the world. Mat 10:37 (Group 1563): proper love of Christ excludes placing natural love above it — love has an ordering that excludes inversion. Group 1567 (filia — Jam 4:4): "friendship with the world is enmity with God" — the inner allegiance of love-toward-world actively constitutes enmity.

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[A — Love]** E — From the verse evidence: love is not mere sentiment (it is commanded — Mat 22:37 — implying it involves the will, not just feeling). Love is not blind loyalty (Group 1585 — failure of chesed — names the cases where chesed should have been given but was withheld as moral failure; love has standards). Love is not identical with mercy (though related — the programme's cluster structure places them in the same cluster, but mercy operates specifically in the frame of guilt/helplessness, while love operates as the fundamental relational orientation). Love is not fellowship (Group 1572's friendship is a form of love but fellowship — M05-G — names the shared-life bond, not the directedness of love itself). The boundary between love and compassion is particularly important: compassion (M05-B) is triggered by the sight of suffering; love (M05-A) is the prior orientation that may generate compassion but is not constituted by it.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence reveals at least four modes of operation within the person.

(1) *As constitutive orientation*: the permanent inner direction that defines the person's fundamental allegiance. Group 1537 (love of God as ruling allegiance): Mat 6:24 — "no one can serve two masters; you cannot love God and money" — love as the inner master-orientation that organises the self.

(2) *As affective movement*: the felt pull toward the beloved. Group 1579 (a.ha.vah — intense love): Gen 29:20 — Jacob's seven years seemed like days "because of the love he had for her." The affective dimension of love is not the whole of it but is real and evidenced.

(3) *As enacted faithfulness*: love expressed in sustained loyal action over time. Group 1584 (human chesed): Rut 3:10 — "you have made this last kindness greater than the first" — love accumulating through loyal acts. Group 3604 (divine chesed-enacted): Exo 15:13 — "you have led in your steadfast love the people whom you have redeemed" — the Exodus as love in enacted form.

(4) *As sacrificial self-giving*: love at its most extreme. Joh 15:13 (Group 1542): "greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends." This mode is evidenced in Christ's love (Joh 13:1 — "having loved his own... he loved them to the end") and commanded of his followers (Joh 13:34).

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, in all three respects.

*By direction*: Love toward God (Group 1537) operates as ruling allegiance — "with all your heart" — and is commanded as total. Love toward neighbour/enemy (Group 1539) operates as the moral extension of the self beyond natural limits — "love your enemies." Love within marriage or family (Groups 1558, 1574) operates as natural affection shaped by covenant. Love of the world (Group 1541) operates as a disordering that constitutes departure.

*By context*: In the covenantal register (chesed groups), love operates as faithfulness within an established bond — it is provoked by prior relationship and sustained through it. In the agapic register (agapē groups), love operates as initiative — God loved while we were yet sinners (Rom 5:8, Group 1542) — and is not conditioned by prior relationship quality.

*By constitutional level*: The evidence is not fully explicit on this point. The heart is named as the primary site (Mat 22:37). The spirit level is not directly addressed in M05-A's verse evidence with respect to love's constitutional location — this will receive S-coding at T2.1 where relevant.

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, evidenced in two distinct registers.

(1) *Declaration*: The chesed vocabulary in Group 3603 operates through liturgical declaration — "his steadfast love endures forever" (1Ch 16:34, Psa 136 series, Ezr 3:11). The act of declaring God's love is itself a mode of love's operation in the community — it forms and anchors the community's trust. Jon 4:2 (Group 3603): Jonah cites the attribute formula "gracious, merciful, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love" — knowing and declaring God's character is the ground of prophetic complaint.

(2) *Command and invitation*: Mat 22:37 (Group 1537) — "you shall love" — love as commanded in speech; Joh 13:34 (Group 1540) — "a new commandment I give to you" — love operating as the content of divine speech to the disciples. The speech-act of the command is itself the mechanism by which love is placed on the person as an orientation.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence reveals varied immediate responses depending on the register.

*Receiving divine chesed (Group 3604)*: The immediate response is recognition and praise — "blessed be the Lord... who has not forsaken his steadfast love toward my master" (Gen 24:27). The encounter with divine chesed enacted produces immediate thanksgiving and recognition.

*Receiving God's agapic love (Group 1542)*: The response named in Rom 5:5 is hope that does not shame — "hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit." The poured-in love produces hope as its immediate inner effect.

*Receiving Christ's love (Group 1538)*: Joh 13:35 (via Group 1540) — the immediate community response to mutual love is recognition by outsiders: "by this all people will know you are my disciples." The love produces a visible social-relational effect.

*Receiving human love (Group 1579)*: Gen 29:20 — Jacob's love for Rachel produced the experience of time transformed (seven years as a few days). The immediate inner response to love-given is the transformation of experience.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

**[A — Love]** E — The response varies by register and direction. Divine chesed enacted → recognition/praise. God's agapic love received → hope and inner stability. Christ's love → compulsion/motivation (2Cor 5:14: "the love of Christ controls us"). Human love given and received → experiential transformation (Gen 29:20) and grief at its loss (2Sa 1:26 — David's lament over Jonathan). There is no single uniform immediate response; the variety reflects the range of love's registers and directions.

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence is relatively full on immediate responses to received love but is largely silent on the immediate inner experience of giving love — what it feels like from within the one who loves. The Hebrew terms give us some access (Gen 29:20, the Song of Songs register in Group 1579), but the NT agapic vocabulary is more focused on love's effects and orientation than on its inner phenomenology from the giver's perspective.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A — Love]** E — Sustained evidence across several groups:

*Chesed over time (Group 3603)*: Lam 3:22-23 — "his mercies never come to an end; they are new every morning" — the sustained divine chesed produces the capacity to endure. The sustained effect is inner resilience and hope anchored in the constancy of divine love.

*Agapē over time (Group 1543)*: 1Cor 13:8 — "love never ends" — love itself is the permanent inner virtue that endures when all else passes. Its sustained presence constitutes the person in the fundamental orientation that will characterise them eschatologically.

*Mutual love over time (Group 1540)*: Col 3:14 — "put on love, which binds everything together in perfect harmony" — the sustained practice of love produces inner coherence and communal unity. Love is the bond that integrates the virtues.

*Disordered love over time (Group 1541)*: 2Ti 4:10 — "Demas, in love with this present world, has deserted" — sustained love of the wrong object produces apostasy. The sustained effect of misdirected love is disintegration of the covenant relationship.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) Trustworthiness — Group 1584 (human chesed): sustained chesed creates the reputation for covenantal reliability that generates reciprocal trust. (2) Community belonging — Group 1540: sustained mutual love creates the social bond of discipleship. (3) Moral comprehensiveness — Group 1543 (Rom 13:10: "love is the fulfilling of the law") — sustained love produces a person who fulfils every moral obligation from a single interior orientation. (4) Endurance under pressure — Group 3603 (Lam 3:22: chesed never ceasing) produces the capacity to endure by anchoring hope in the unchanging divine character rather than in circumstances.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. The immediate response to divine love is recognition and praise (T1.5.1); the sustained effect is inner stability, hope, and trustworthiness (T1.6.2). The immediate experience of giving love is the affective bond (Gen 29:20 — time transformed); the sustained effect is moral formation (Col 3:14 — love as the bond of perfection). The difference is between the encounter-event (immediate) and the constitutional formation (sustained).

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Fear of the Lord*: Group 3603 — Psa 25:10: "all the paths of the Lord are steadfast love and faithfulness, for those who keep his covenant and his testimonies" — faithfulness to the covenant is the condition for receiving the fullness of divine chesed. Psa 33:18: "the eye of the Lord is on those who hope in his steadfast love." Hope is named as an enabling condition. (2) *Receptivity and obedience*: Joh 14:21 (Group 1537): "whoever has my commandments and keeps them... he it is who loves me. And he who loves me will be loved by my Father" — the condition for receiving the fuller expression of divine love is loving obedience. (3) *Purified hearts*: 1Pe 1:22 (Group 1556 — filadelfia): "having purified your souls by your obedience to the truth for a sincere brotherly love" — the purified soul enables genuine love reception and expression.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Love of the world*: 1Jo 2:15 (Group 1543's boundary) — "if anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him" — love of the wrong object blocks reception of divine love. (2) *Pride and self-seeking*: 1Cor 13:4-5 (Group 1543) — love "is not arrogant... does not insist on its own way" — arrogance and self-orientation are the inner conditions that prevent love from operating. (3) *Hardness*: Mat 24:12 (Group 1543) — "because lawlessness will be increased, the love of many will grow cold" — moral disorder produces inner cooling of love. (4) *Absence of God's love within*: Joh 5:42 (Group 1542) — "you do not have the love of God within you" — the Pharisees who are most externally religious lack the inner possession that enables genuine reception.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A — Love]** E — Group 1541 (disordered love) and Group 1542's negative instances provide the evidence. The person who encounters God's love but does not receive it continues to love the wrong object — "people loved the darkness rather than the light" (Joh 3:19). The state is not emptiness but misdirected love: the faculty of love is present and active but oriented wrongly. Mat 24:12 (Group 1543): the state of non-reception is "love grown cold" — not absent but thermally reduced through moral entropy. Luk 11:42 (Group 1542): the Pharisees who tithe mint but neglect justice and love — they possess religious practice without the inner love that would animate it.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**[A — Love]** E — Love belongs primarily to the *relational-moral* dimension — it is the constitutive inner orientation toward another that generates moral conduct and defines relationship. The great commandment structure (Mat 22:37-39, Group 1537) places love at the apex of the moral order. Rom 13:10 (Group 1543) confirms: "love is the fulfilling of the law" — love is the inner dimension that integrates and generates all moral obligations.

A secondary dimension is *affective*: the fileō and a.ha.vah registers (Groups 1562-1564, 1578-1580) name genuine inner warmth and yearning that engage the affective faculty specifically. The Song of Songs register in Group 1579 is explicitly affective.

**T1.8.2** — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

**[A — Love]** E — The relational-moral classification is supported by: (1) The command structure — love is commanded (Mat 22:37, Joh 13:34), which situates it in the volitional-moral space rather than purely the affective. (2) The law-fulfilling claim (Rom 13:10) — situates love as the moral integrator. (3) The covenantal faithfulness register (Group 3603-3604) — chesed names faithful conduct within a relational bond, not merely feeling.

The affective classification is supported by: (1) Gen 29:20 (Group 1579) — time transformed by love. (2) 2Sa 1:26 — love described in intensity terms ("surpassing the love of women"). (3) Song of Songs register — love as yearning and captivation.

**T1.8.3** — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

**[A — Love]** E — Secondary dimensions: (1) *Affective* (evidenced above — T1.8.2). (2) *Covenantal-judicial*: chesed operates within the covenantal framework where love and faithfulness are structurally bound — Deu 7:9 (Group 3603): "the faithful God who keeps covenant and steadfast love" — love and covenant loyalty are inseparable. (3) *Constitutive of identity*: the chasid (Group 1586 — 33 verses) — the person whose character is constituted by chesed-love — names love as a dimension of personal identity, not merely disposition or act.

These secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary relational-moral classification but enrich it: the covenantal dimension shows love's structural home; the identity dimension shows its character-constituting depth; the affective dimension shows its phenomenological reality.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

### T2.1 — Spirit-Level Location

**T2.1.1** — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?

**[A — Love]** S — The M05-A verse evidence does not explicitly place love at the spirit level. The primary constitutional location named in the evidence is the heart (Mat 22:37; Pro 3:3). The spirit is not named as love's location in the M05-A verse base.

**T2.1.2** — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?

**[A — Love]** E — Partially and indirectly. Rom 5:5 (Group 1542): "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — the Spirit is the *agent* of love's communication into the human heart, implying a spirit-mediated origin even if the location named is the heart. Gal 5:22 (Group 1543's boundary context): the fruit of the Spirit includes love as its first-named element — locating love's productive source in the Spirit. The evidence indicates spirit-level *origin* (the Spirit produces love) without explicitly naming the spirit as love's *constitutional location* in the human.

**T2.1.3** — What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — From Rom 5:5 and Gal 5:22: if love originates at the spirit level (via the Spirit's action), this indicates it is the deepest-level orientation of the person — not a surface affect but the most foundational directedness. The Spirit producing love suggests that genuine love is not merely human capacity cultivated but divine reality communicated — a depth-level phenomenon.

**T2.1.4** — If the evidence is silent on spirit-level location, note this explicitly.

**[A — Love]** — The evidence is silent on explicit spirit-level location. The spirit-level origin is inferable from Rom 5:5 and Gal 5:22 but not stated as the love's constitutional *home*. Heart is the named location; spirit is the named source.

### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**T2.2.1** — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the soul level in the verse evidence?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, partially. 1Sa 18:3 (Group 1579): "Jonathan made a covenant with David, because he loved him as his own soul (nephesh)" — love quantified by the soul's self-love. 1Sa 20:17 (Group 1579): "he loved him as he loved his own soul." These are the clearest soul-level localisations: love measured against and equated with soul-love. Additionally, 1Pe 1:22 (Group 1556): "having purified your souls by your obedience to the truth for a sincere brotherly love" — the purified soul is the condition for genuine love, situating love at the soul level as its operative environment.

**T2.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate a specific soul-level faculty or sub-location?

**[A — Love]** E — The nephesh (soul) as the measure of love's intensity (Group 1579) does not specify a sub-faculty but names the whole person's inner life as the reference point for love's depth. 1Pe 1:22's "purified souls" suggests love operates at the level of the soul's moral orientation rather than a specific sub-faculty.

**T2.2.3** — Where the evidence is silent on soul-level location, note this explicitly.

**[A — Love]** — The evidence is relatively sparse on soul-level location compared to heart-level. The Jonathan-David instances are the primary soul-level evidence; most of the sub-group's location language uses heart.

### T2.3 — Heart

**T2.3.1** — Is this characteristic explicitly located in the heart in the verse evidence?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, extensively. Mat 22:37 (Group 1537): "love the Lord your God with all your *heart*" — the heart is the primary constitutional site of the love command. Pro 3:3 (referenced in prior findings): chesed and faithfulness are to be "bound around your neck; write them on the tablet of your *heart*" — heart as love's inscription site. Rom 5:5 (Group 1542): "God's love has been poured into our *hearts*" — the heart as the receptacle of poured-in divine love. Mat 12:34 (Group 1097 adjacent, but the principle applies): "out of the abundance of the *heart* the mouth speaks" — contextually relevant to love's output from the heart.

**T2.3.2** — What does heart-location reveal about the constitutional depth of this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — Heart-location reveals that love is not a peripheral or surface characteristic but belongs to the person's core constitutional self. In the biblical vocabulary, the heart is the seat of intention, decision, and fundamental orientation. Love located in the heart is love as the governing inner commitment from which conduct flows. Mat 22:37's "with all your heart" establishes love as requiring the person's whole heart — it is not a partial or compartmentalised commitment but the total orientation of the heart's capacity.

**T2.3.3** — Is heart-location the primary or one of several constitutional locations?

**[A — Love]** E — Heart is the primary named location. Soul is secondary (Group 1579 — Jonathan-David instances). Mind is named in the great commandment formula (Mat 22:37: "with all your mind") but the heart appears first and most consistently in the verse evidence. The cumulative evidence positions the heart as love's primary constitutional home.

### T2.4 — Mind

**T2.4.1** — Is this characteristic explicitly located in the mind in the verse evidence?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, in the great commandment formulation: Mat 22:37 (Group 1537) — "with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your *mind*." The mind (dianoia) is named as one of the three constitutional registers from which love proceeds. This is the primary mind-level evidence.

**T2.4.2** — What does mind-location reveal about the nature of love?

**[A — Love]** E — The inclusion of "with all your mind" reveals that love is not merely affective or volitional but cognitive — it involves the mind's orientation toward the object. Love cannot be a wholly non-rational impulse if it is commanded to engage the mind. The mind's participation in love means that genuine love knows its object, thinks about its object, and remains oriented toward it in thought. The mind-location also supports the non-reduction of love to emotion: emotion may be love's affective expression, but love itself engages the mind as well.

**T2.4.3** — Is there further mind-level evidence beyond the great commandment?

**[A — Love]** S — The verse evidence for mind-level location beyond Mat 22:37 is sparse within M05-A. The great commandment is the defining evidence; the sub-group does not offer additional explicit mind-level localisations. The soul-engulfing love in Group 1579 does not specify the mind; the heart language dominates.

### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**T2.5.1** — Does the evidence locate this characteristic in other inner-being faculties or registers beyond heart, soul, and mind?

**[A — Love]** E — The bowel/gut register (splanchn-) is not present in M05-A (it belongs to M05-B compassion). However, the strength faculty is named in the great commandment: Mar 12:30 (Group 1537): "with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your *mind* and with all your *strength* (ischus)." The inclusion of strength suggests love also engages the volitional-capacity dimension — the person's full energy and power directed toward God. This is not a constitutional location as much as a mode of full-person engagement.

**T2.5.2** — What does this reveal?

**[A — Love]** E — The four-fold "all your" structure (heart, soul, mind, strength) reveals that love is unique among characteristics in explicitly requiring the engagement of every inner-being faculty. Love is not located in one part of the person but is the whole-person orientation — a comprehensive inner act that draws all faculties into its direction.

**T2.5.3** — Where the evidence is silent, note explicitly.

**[A — Love]** — The evidence is silent on specific conscience, will, or imagination as sub-locations within the soul. The four-fold commandment covers the territory comprehensively but without specifying sub-faculties further.

### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**T2.6.1** — What is the significance of the body in relation to this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — The kiss-greeting vocabulary (Groups 157-158: filoxenos, and the broader filos register including Group 1572) evidences love's bodily expression: the holy kiss (Rom 16:16 — Group 1573 adjacent; 1Pe 5:14 — Group 1823 adjacent) as love's embodied communal expression. The embrace/bosom (cheq) belongs to BOUNDARY and is not directly in M05-A's active groups. However, Joh 11:35 (Group 1562 adjacent) — "Jesus wept" — in the context of loving Lazarus (Joh 11:36 is Group 1562's anchor: "see how he loved him") shows love producing bodily expression (tears). The body is the expression-surface of love rather than its constitutional location.

**T2.6.2** — Is there direct bodily involvement in the characteristic's operation?

**[A — Love]** E — The most direct instance is Mar 10:21 (Group 1538): "Jesus, looking at him, *loved* him" — the loving is expressed through the gaze. Physical seeing as the act that precedes or accompanies love. Group 1572 (filos-friendship) includes communal practices — hospitality, meals — that embody friendship-love. The body is love's instrument of expression, not its seat.

**T2.6.3** — What does the body's role reveal about the constitutional structure of love?

**[A — Love]** E — The body's role as expression-instrument reveals that love's constitutional home is inner (heart, soul) but its communication is necessarily bodily — love that never externalises is at risk of being merely notional. The verse evidence consistently shows love producing bodily acts: service, sacrifice, hospitality, weeping, touching. This confirms the dispositional nature of love: it flows outward from the inner constitutional home through the body into relationship.

### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**T2.7.1** — Does the characteristic flow from inner to outer (body as expression) or from outer engagement inward (body as receptor)?

**[A — Love]** E — Primarily inner to outer. The command structure (Mat 22:37 — "you shall love") presupposes an inner act that then generates outer expression. Group 3604 (chesed-enacted): every instance of enacted divine chesed flows from the prior inner disposition (Group 3603 — attribute) outward into historical acts. The body is the delivery mechanism, not the generative source. The one exception is the receiving direction: Rom 5:5 — "God's love has been poured into our hearts" — the movement is from without (the Spirit's action) into the inner being. This is an outer-to-inner movement, but it is the Spirit acting, not the body receiving.

**T2.7.2** — What does this directional structure reveal?

**[A — Love]** E — The inner-to-outer direction reveals that genuine love cannot be merely behavioural performance (outer acts without inner orientation). The counter-evidence is in Group 1541 (disordered love) and Group 1585 (chesed-failure): the absence of inner love produces either misdirected outer acts (Group 1541) or absence of proper acts (Group 1585). Outer acts of love without inner love are possible (and warned against in 1Cor 13:1-3 — giving all one's goods without love) but they are not the genuine characteristic. The direction is inner → outer constitutionally; the Spirit acts outer → inner in the communication of love.

**T2.7.3** — Note any bidirectional or anomalous directional evidence.

**[A — Love]** S — No clear bidirectional evidence within M05-A's verse base. The Spirit's communication of love (outer → inner) is the most significant counter-directional element, but this is the Spirit's action rather than the body's.

### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**T2.8.1** — Does the characteristic leave a constitutive deposit in the body — a physical marker or embodied change resulting from the characteristic's operation?

**[A — Love]** S — The verse evidence does not speak of love leaving a physical/bodily deposit. The deposits it leaves are constitutional and relational (character formed, bonds established) rather than somatic. No bodily deposit evidence in M05-A.

**T2.8.2** — What does this absence or presence reveal?

**[A — Love]** S — The absence of somatic deposit evidence confirms that love's primary operation is inner (heart, soul) and relational, not somatic. This distinguishes love from characteristics that have strong bodily markers (physical health outcomes, somatic expressions of peace or distress).

**T2.8.3** — Note explicitly where evidence is silent.

**[A — Love]** — Silent on somatic deposit. Love produces character formation and relational change, not physical markers in the body.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the origin or source of this characteristic in the human person?

**[A — Love]** E — Two sources are evidenced. (1) *Divine communication*: Rom 5:5 (Group 1542) — "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — love in the human person originates from divine communication, not human self-generation. 1Jo 4:19 — "we love because he first loved us" — the causal priority is God's prior love. Gal 5:22 — love as Spirit-fruit — same conclusion. (2) *Created nature*: The philo- compounds (Groups 1556-1558, 1573-1574) and the natural-affection register (Group 1579) suggest that natural human love (family bonds, friendship) is part of created nature. The evidence allows both: love as natural capacity and love as Spirit-produced virtue, with the latter being love at its fullest and most definitive.

**T2.9.2** — Is the source external (given by God) or internal (native capacity)?

**[A — Love]** E — Both, as T2.9.1 establishes. The most definitive love (agapē in its self-giving, enemy-encompassing form) is externally sourced through the Spirit. Natural affection (fileō, filostorgos, filadelfia) appears as native created capacity. The evidence does not resolve whether natural love requires Spirit renewal to function properly or whether it operates as a remnant of the image in fallen humanity.

**T2.9.3** — What does the source reveal about the nature of the characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — The dual source (divine communication + created nature) reveals that love occupies a unique structural position in the inner-being architecture: it is both natural (the human is created for love as a relational being) and supernatural (genuine love in its fullest form requires the Spirit's communication). This positions love as the meeting point of creation and grace — the human person's love is simultaneously an expression of the image and a participation in the divine life.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does this characteristic involve movement within the inner being — a directional flow or dynamic process?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. The chesed-enacted group (Group 3604) reveals movement from divine attribute (Group 3603 — the permanent disposition) to specific acts — love flowing from the inner attribute outward into historical engagement. The great commandment (Group 1537) presupposes movement from the heart's centre outward to encompass soul, mind, and strength. 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544): "the love of Christ *controls* us" — sunechō (holds together, constrains, drives) — naming love as an inner force that moves the person in a specific direction.

**T2.10.2** — What is the direction and character of this movement?

**[A — Love]** E — The movement is centripetal-to-centrifugal: love is received and constituted at the person's inner centre (heart/soul), then flows outward toward its object. 2Cor 5:14 names this as controlling/driving movement — love as the inner energy that propels outward action. The enemy-love command (Group 1539: Mat 5:44) represents the most extreme extension of this outward movement — beyond natural affinity to encompass the hostile other.

**T2.10.3** — Is there evidence of constitutional transformation produced by this movement?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. Group 1540 (Joh 13:34: the new commandment) — "just as I have loved you" establishes a new pattern: the love of Christ becomes the new ground of community love, producing a transformed relational community (Joh 13:35: "by this all people will know you are my disciples"). The movement of love produces social-constitutional transformation in the community. Col 3:14 (Group 1544): love as "the bond of perfection" — the integrating force that holds all virtues together, producing characterological wholeness.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptual faculty — the capacity to see, hear, attend, notice?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. Mar 10:21 (Group 1538): "Jesus, *looking at* him, loved him" — the act of seeing precedes and occasions love. The perception of the other triggers the love-response. Exo 2:6 (adjacent — belongs to M05-B but reveals the perceptual-trigger pattern): "she saw the child, and behold, the baby was crying. She took pity on him." The perceptual opening (seeing, hearing) is the occasion for love's engagement.

**T3.1.2** — Is perception a trigger, a component, or a product of this characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — Perception appears as a *trigger* — the seeing of the beloved precedes and occasions love (Mar 10:21). But perception is also shaped *by* love: the loving person attends differently to the world. The chasid's "guard" (Group 1586, 1Sa 2:9: "he will guard the feet of his faithful ones") presupposes attentive perception of those in his care. The friend who sends intermediaries (Group 1572, Luk 7:6: centurion's friends acting on his behalf) demonstrates love-shaped perception of needs and social dynamics.

**T3.1.3** — What does this reveal about love's relationship to the perceptual faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Love opens and sharpens perception toward its object. The enemy-love command (Group 1539) requires the perception of the enemy as a person to whom love is owed — the command to love the enemy is simultaneously a command to perceive them differently. Group 1572 (filos): Joh 15:15 — "all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" — friendship-love as the ground of shared perception/knowledge. Love and perception are structurally interrelated: love directs perception; perception nourishes love.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — reasoning, understanding, discernment?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. Mat 22:37 (Group 1537): love requires "all your mind (dianoia)" — direct cognitive engagement. Joh 15:15 (Group 1572): "I have called you friends, for all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" — friendship-love produces cognitive sharing: the friend knows what the servant does not. The chasid's loyalty (Group 1586) presupposes discernment of covenantal obligation. 1Cor 13:2 (Group 1543): "if I... understand all mysteries and all knowledge... but have not love, I am nothing" — love and cognition are not identical; love is the inner reality that gives cognitive gifts their value.

**T3.2.2** — What is the relationship between love and understanding in the evidence?

**[A — Love]** E — Love and understanding are distinguished (1Cor 13:2 — having understanding without love produces "nothing") but related. Joh 15:15 shows love as the relational ground that enables deeper knowing — the friend is told what the servant is not. Eph 3:17-19 (Group 1542): "being rooted and grounded in love... [may you] know the love of Christ that surpasses knowledge" — the knowledge that love produces exceeds ordinary cognitive knowing; it is participatory knowing through relationship. Cognition informs love; love produces a form of knowing that transcends ordinary cognition.

**T3.2.3** — Where is the evidence silent on cognitive engagement?

**[A — Love]** S — The evidence is largely silent on love engaging the cognitive faculty in the *analytical* or *reasoning* sense — problem-solving, inference, logical deduction. Love's cognitive engagement appears primarily in the modes of attention, recognition, and relational knowing rather than analytical reasoning.

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, in the chesed vocabulary specifically. Psa 25:6 (Group 3603): "remember your mercy, O Lord, and your steadfast love, for they have been from of old" — the petition to God to remember his love appeals to the divine memory as the ground of present action. Psa 89:49 (Group 3603): "Lord, where is your steadfast love of old, which by your faithfulness you swore to David?" — the memory of past chesed-covenants as the ground of present petition. 2Ch 24:22 (Group 1585): "Joash did not *remember* the kindness that Jehoiada had shown him" — the failure to remember chesed received is the diagnostic of chesed-failure. Memory is structurally linked to love: love that is forgotten fails its obligation.

**T3.3.2** — What role does memory play in love's operation?

**[A — Love]** E — Memory functions as love's *anchor and obligation-reminder*. The remembered chesed of the past (divine or human) creates obligation in the present — the person who received chesed is bound to show it in return or to appeal to it as the ground of further petition. The chasid (Group 1586) is constituted by faithfulness-memory: they remember their covenantal commitments and act accordingly. The failure-of-chesed register (Group 1585) is consistently linked to forgetting — "Joash did not remember" (2Ch 24:22); Jer 2:2 recounts Israel's early love as something God remembers but Israel has forgotten.

**T3.3.3** — Is there evidence of love *creating* memories or being anchored in them?

**[A — Love]** E — The chesed-enacted group (Group 3604) creates memories of God's faithful acts that then anchor future trust — Exo 15:13 (the Exodus) becomes the ground for Psa 77:8's question "has his steadfast love ceased?" The memory of enacted love creates the framework within which current experience of apparent absence is interpreted. Love creates its own evidential history that sustains the person through apparent discontinuities.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, substantially. Group 1579 (a.ha.vah — intense love): Gen 29:20 — seven years seeming like days; 2Sa 1:26 — love described as "extraordinary, surpassing the love of women" — these are explicit affective intensity markers. Group 1542 (agapē as ground): Eph 3:19 — "to know the love of Christ that surpasses knowledge" — the experiential-affective register is present in the idea of love as something known beyond ordinary cognition. Group 1588 (ye.di.dut — beloved): Jer 12:7 — "I have given the beloved of my soul into the hands of her enemies" — the divine grief at abandoning the beloved names love's affective depth on God's side.

**T3.4.2** — Is affect central to love or secondary?

**[A — Love]** E — Secondary but real. The command to love (Mat 22:37, Group 1537; Joh 13:34, Group 1540) presupposes that love is not reducible to spontaneous affect — it is commanded, which means it engages the will even when affect is absent. 1Cor 13:3 (Group 1543): giving all without love is possible — this shows love is not identical with affective motivation. But the natural-affection register (Groups 1579, 1562-1564, 1573-1574) affirms that love includes genuine feeling. The evidence positions affect as love's real but not exclusive or sufficient component: love can exist without strong affect (the enemy-love command — Group 1539), and affect can exist without genuine love (Amnon's desire that became hatred — Group 1579, 2Sa 13:15).

**T3.4.3** — What is the affective character of love as evidenced?

**[A — Love]** E — The affective register includes: (1) *Yearning/intensity* (Group 1579 — Jacob for Rachel, Jonathan for David). (2) *Grief at loss* (Group 1579 — 2Sa 1:26 David's lament; Group 1588 — Jer 12:7 divine grief). (3) *Delight* (Group 1579 — Song of Songs register: Pro 5:19 — "be intoxicated always in her love"). (4) *Controlled affect under pressure* (Group 1539 — enemy-love: not the natural warm feeling but committed goodwill despite antipathy). Love's affective character ranges from intense yearning to disciplined commitment without warm feeling.

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1** — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Marginally and indirectly. The most suggestive evidence is from Group 1579 (Song of Songs register) where love generates creative expression — the poetry of longing. But this is the expression *of* love, not love engaging creativity as its mode of operation. Group 1572 (filos-friendship): Joh 15:15 — "all that I have heard from my Father I have made known to you" — friendship-love enables creative sharing of knowledge. The love that generates new forms of community (Group 1540 — "a new commandment") might be considered creative in that it produces a new social formation.

**T3.5.2** — Is creativity a significant dimension of love's operation?

**[A — Love]** S — The verse evidence does not strongly evidence creativity as a primary dimension of love's operation in M05-A. The creative expressions of love are products rather than constitutive modes. This is a silence, not a gap — the verse base simply does not foreground creativity as love's mode.

**T3.5.3** — Note silence explicitly.

**[A — Love]** S — Silent on creativity as a primary mode of love's inner operation. Love produces faithful acts and relational bonds; the evidence does not centrally engage the creative dimension.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, centrally. The command structure throughout M05-A establishes love as volitional: Mat 22:37 (Group 1537) — "you shall love" — the command presupposes a volitional capacity to comply or not comply. Mat 5:44 (Group 1539) — "love your enemies" — the most extreme volitional demand, loving those for whom no natural affection exists. Joh 13:34 (Group 1540) — "a new commandment I give to you" — the command-structure makes love a volitional act of obedience. The chasid (Group 1586) — constituted by covenantal faithfulness — is the person whose will is consistently aligned with love's demands over time.

**T3.6.2** — What is the relationship between volition and affect in love?

**[A — Love]** E — The enemy-love command (Group 1539) most clearly separates volition and affect: love of enemies is commanded without any suggestion that warm feeling will accompany it. The command is to act with committed goodwill regardless of affect. Conversely, the natural-love register (Group 1579) shows affect preceding and motivating volitional commitment — Jacob's seven years of service were volitional acts sustained by genuine affection. The evidence positions love as requiring volition (it can be commanded) while also involving affect (it produces genuine feeling in the natural register). Volition and affect are both real dimensions of love but neither is reducible to the other.

**T3.6.3** — Is love more fundamentally volitional or affective?

**[A — Love]** E — More fundamentally volitional in the NT agapic register; more fundamentally affective in the natural-affection register; both integrated in the covenantal chesed register (faithfulness as volitional commitment sustained through varying degrees of felt attachment). The evidence does not resolve this into a single answer — the characteristic genuinely spans both registers.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to initiate, decide, act, take responsibility?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, and it is central. Love is constitutively an *act* of initiative. Group 1538 (God loves, 31 verses): Joh 3:16 — "God so loved the world that he *gave*" — divine love as supreme initiative. Rom 5:8 (Group 1542): "God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died" — the initiative structure is explicit: God acts first, toward undeserving recipients. The human agency dimension: Group 1584 (human chesed): Rut 3:10 — Ruth's agential choice to show chesed greater than the first — love as a chosen and executed act. 1Cor 13:3 (Group 1543): "if I give away all I have" — love producing the most radical agential act (total self-divestiture).

**T3.7.2** — What is the character of love's agency?

**[A — Love]** E — Love's agency is characterised by initiative (first movement toward the other), constancy (sustained over time — Group 3603: chesed never ceasing), and sacrifice (Joh 15:13: laying down one's life). It is also characterised by *non-compulsion*: Phile 14 (Group 1044 adjacent) — "your goodness might not be by compulsion but of your own accord" — love acts freely, not under external force. The agency of love is therefore free, sustained, costly, and initiating.

**T3.7.3** — Is there evidence of love producing or enabling broader human agency?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544): "the love of Christ *controls* us, because we have concluded this: that one has died for all... and he died for all, that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died." Christ's love received produces a transformation of the believer's self-directed agency into Christ-directed agency. Love received changes the subject of one's agency — from self-for-self to self-for-Christ/others.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, and it provides the moral evaluation standard. Rom 13:10 (Group 1543): "love does no wrong to a neighbour; therefore love is the fulfilling of the law" — love is the criterion by which moral conduct is evaluated. 1Cor 13:1-3 (Group 1543): the evaluation of tongues, prophecy, knowledge, and sacrifice is conducted against the standard of love — all are found wanting without it. Group 1585 (chesed-failure, 10 verses): the moral evaluation of human conduct in these verses consistently uses chesed-love as the standard — "he who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty" (Job 6:14). Love is the evaluative touchstone.

**T3.8.2** — Is love a product of moral evaluation or its ground?

**[A — Love]** E — Love is the *ground* of moral evaluation, not its product. Love determines what counts as morally adequate — "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Rom 13:10) means that love-as-criterion subsumes the law's individual requirements. The moral evaluation faculty's standard is set by love, not the other way around.

**T3.8.3** — What does this reveal about love's structural place in the inner-being economy?

**[A — Love]** E — It reveals love as the most architecturally central characteristic in the inner-being economy — the one that sets the standard for all moral evaluation. This is consistent with the great commandment's priority (Mat 22:37-40: "on these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets"). Love does not occupy one position in a hierarchy of virtues; it occupies the position that governs the hierarchy.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[A — Love]** E — Partially and indirectly. Group 1585 (chesed-failure, 10 verses) evidences the conscience's role in love's failure: "Joash did not remember the kindness" (2Ch 24:22) — the failure to remember implies a conscience that should have registered the obligation. Job 6:14 — "he who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty" — the withholding of chesed is a conscience-level moral failure. Group 1543 (agapē-virtue, 29 verses): Rom 12:9 — "let love be genuine" — the requirement of genuineness implies a conscience-level distinction between authentic and performed love.

**T3.9.2** — What is the conscience's relationship to love?

**[A — Love]** E — The conscience registers the obligation of love and signals when love has failed. The chesed-failure register (Group 1585) is essentially a conscience register: these are the cases where love's obligation was clear and its withholding is morally indictable. The conscience is love's internal accountability mechanism — it registers the gap between love-commanded and love-performed.

**T3.9.3** — Is there evidence of love transforming or forming the conscience?

**[A — Love]** S — The verse evidence in M05-A does not explicitly address love forming or transforming the conscience. This is a gap in the M05-A evidence base; it may be addressed in the M05-C mercy or M05-D goodness registers.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscientiousness faculty — the capacity for sustained careful attention to duty?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, and it is closely related to the covenantal-faithfulness register. The chasid (Group 1586, 33 verses) is the embodiment of conscientiousness in love: the one whose character is constituted by covenantal faithfulness acts with sustained reliable attention to the obligations of love. Psa 4:3 (Group 1586): "the Lord has set apart the godly (chasid) for himself" — the chasid's conscientiousness in love-faithfulness constitutes their separation for God's purposes. Group 1584 (human chesed, 41 verses): the chesed-tradition of persons like Ruth (Rut 3:10 — "this last kindness greater than the first") shows sustained conscientiousness in love-expression over time.

**T3.10.2** — What is the relationship between love and conscientiousness?

**[A — Love]** E — Love provides the motivational foundation for conscientiousness: the chasid is conscientious because love-faithfulness is their constitutional orientation, not because external rules compel them. Group 1543 (1Cor 13:4: "love is patient") — patience is a form of conscientious perseverance; love produces the inner condition that sustains careful attention over time. Conscientiousness is love's temporal form — love expressed as sustained reliable duty-keeping.

**T3.10.3** — Where is the evidence silent?

**[A — Love]** S — The evidence is relatively full on conscientiousness within the chesed register. Sparse in the agapic register — agapē's conscientiousness dimension is less explicitly evidenced in M05-A's specific verses, though it is implied by the command to love (sustained obligation).

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity faculty?

**[A — Love]** E — Centrally and extensively — love is constitutively relational. The entire sub-group operates within relational space: every group involves a subject (the one who loves) and an object (the one loved) with the love-orientation as the constitutive bond between them. Group 1572 (filos-friendship, 27 verses): friendship-love is purely relational — it names the inner bond that constitutes a friendship. Group 1540 (mutual agapē, 34 verses): the new commandment (Joh 13:34) creates a new relational community constituted by mutual love. Group 1576 (a.hev — loyal friend, 13 verses): "a friend who sticks closer than a brother" (Pro 18:24) — the relational bond at its most enduring.

**T3.11.2** — Is love the same as relational capacity, or is it a specific deployment of it?

**[A — Love]** E — Love is a specific deployment of the relational capacity faculty — it is the *committed* or *directed* form of relational engagement. The relational capacity is broader (includes enmity, rivalry, indifference); love is the specific orientation of committed goodwill within that broader capacity. This is confirmed by the structural-opposite analysis (T1.3): hatred (sane) is also a relational orientation but inverted. Love and hatred share the relational faculty as their common ground.

**T3.11.3** — What does love's relational character reveal about its place in the inner-being economy?

**[A — Love]** E — It reveals love as the *generative core* of the relational dimension of the inner being — the characteristic that most centrally defines what it means to be a relational being. The great commandment's summary of all moral obligation in love (Mat 22:37-40) is simultaneously a summary of all relational obligation: right relationship with God and with neighbour is the totality of what love requires.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — How does this characteristic function in the God-to-human relational interface?

**[A — Love]** E — This is the most extensively evidenced interface in M05-A. Group 3603 (chesed-attribute, 59 verses) names divine love as the permanent inner disposition from which all God's acts toward humanity flow. Group 3604 (chesed-enacted, 56 verses) evidences the specific historical acts: the Exodus (Exo 15:13), specific persons (Gen 39:21 — Joseph in prison, Lam 3:22 — Jerusalem in ruins). Group 1538 (agapaō — God loves, 31 verses): Joh 3:16, Joh 13:1, Rom 5:8 — the NT form of the same divine-to-human love in Christ's person. Group 1542 (agapē as source): Rom 5:5 — love poured into human hearts. The God-to-human interface is the originating direction of love in the sub-group's evidence.

**T4.1.2** — What characterises God's love in its human-directed form?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Prior and unconditional*: Rom 5:8 (Group 1542) — "while we were still sinners"; Jer 31:3 (Group 1578) — "everlasting love." (2) *Covenantally faithful*: Deu 7:9 (Group 3603) — "keeps covenant and chesed to a thousand generations." (3) *Historically enacted*: Group 3604 — each specific act of chesed is a historical event with named beneficiaries. (4) *Inexhaustible*: Lam 3:22 (Group 3603) — "never ceases; his mercies never come to an end." (5) *Personally directed*: Joh 11:5 (Group 1538) — "Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus" — named, individual.

**T4.1.3** — What does this interface reveal about the characteristic's inner-being significance?

**[A — Love]** E — The God-to-human interface reveals that love is not a merely creaturely capacity but a divine communication — the human person's capacity for love is a participation in what God is. The inexhaustibility of divine love (Group 3603) provides the inner-being with an anchor that does not depend on circumstance; Lam 3:22 evidences this at its most extreme: divine love is the stable ground under the rubble of Jerusalem's destruction. The God-to-human interface establishes love as the most stabilising force available to the human inner being.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — How does this characteristic function in the human-to-God relational interface?

**[A — Love]** E — Group 1537 (agapaō — love of God, 20 verses) is the primary evidence. Mat 22:37 — "you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and soul and mind" — the commanding of human love toward God establishes this as the primary inner-being obligation. Joh 14:15 (Group 1537): "if you love me, you will keep my commandments" — human love of God expressed through obedience. Joh 14:21: "whoever loves me will be loved by my Father" — human love of Christ triggers the fuller expression of divine love in return. The human-to-God direction is the commanded response to the God-to-human prior love.

**T4.2.2** — What characterises human love toward God?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Total* — "with all your heart and soul and mind and strength" (Mat 22:37; Mar 12:30): love toward God demands the whole person. (2) *Expressed in obedience* — Joh 14:15: love of God is verified by keeping commandments, not merely professed. (3) *Exclusive* — Mat 6:24 (Group 1537): "you cannot love God and money" — love of God excludes divided allegiance. (4) *Responsive* — 1Jo 4:19: "we love because he first loved us" — human love of God is the response to prior divine love.

**T4.2.3** — What does this interface reveal about the characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — The human-to-God interface reveals love as the constitutive act of worship in its inner dimension — not ritual performance but the orientation of the whole person toward God. Mat 22:37's "greatest commandment" designation establishes that the inner-being's most fundamental and important orientation is love directed toward God. The interface also reveals love as *responsive* rather than spontaneously generated: the human loves God because God first loved.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — How does this characteristic function when the human person is the giver of love?

**[A — Love]** E — Group 1584 (human chesed-giving, 41 verses): Ruth's chesed to Naomi (Rut 1:8, 2:20, 3:10), Jonathan's chesed to David (1Sa 20:14-15), David's chesed to Mephibosheth (2Sa 9:1-7). Love-giving is characterised by initiative (David seeking out anyone remaining from Saul's house — 2Sa 9:1), loyalty (Jonathan maintaining chesed even when politically costly), and excess (Rut 3:10 — "this last kindness greater than the first"). Group 1539 (agapaō — love of enemy, 17 verses): the most extreme form of love-giving — toward those who are hostile or undeserving (Mat 5:44: "love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you").

**T4.3.2** — What is the inner-being experience of the one giving love?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence provides glimpses: Group 1579 (intense love): Jacob's service for Rachel — love-giving transforms the experience of labour (Gen 29:20 — seven years as a few days). Group 1544 (communal agapē): 2Cor 2:4 — Paul writing "out of much affliction and anguish of heart... with many tears" — love-giving can be accompanied by inner anguish when its object is in distress. 2Cor 12:15 (Group 1544): "I will most gladly spend and be spent for your souls" — love-giving as willing self-expenditure. The inner experience of love-giving ranges from joyful self-transcendence (Gen 29:20) to anguished self-expenditure (2Cor 2:4; 2Cor 12:15).

**T4.3.3** — What does this reveal about the nature of love-giving?

**[A — Love]** E — Love-giving is constitutively other-directed and self-expenditure — it moves the person away from self-interest toward the welfare of the beloved. This is most starkly stated in Joh 15:13 (Group 1542): "greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends." The extremity of the love-giving standard establishes that genuine love is structurally opposed to self-preservation.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — How does this characteristic function when the human person is the receiver of love?

**[A — Love]** E — Group 3604 (chesed-enacted, 56 verses): Lot receiving divine chesed (Gen 19:19), Joseph in prison (Gen 39:21), Daniel receiving favour (Dan 1:9). The receiving person recognises the chesed as received — "your servant has found favour in your sight, and you have shown me great kindness" (Gen 19:19) — recognition and acknowledgement are the immediate responses. Group 1542 (agapē as source): Rom 5:5 — love poured into hearts; the receiving is passive ("poured in") while the effect is active (hope produced). Group 1556 (filadelfia-receiving): 1Pe 1:22 — "sincere brotherly love" evidences both giving and receiving within the community.

**T4.4.2** — What is the inner-being experience of receiving love?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Recognition* — Gen 19:19: Lot recognises what has been shown him and names it. (2) *Hope and stability* — Rom 5:5: received love produces unashamed hope. (3) *Motivation* — 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544): "the love of Christ controls us" — received love becomes the inner motivating force. (4) *Joy* — Phile 7 (Group 1075 adjacent but relevant pattern): joy derived from receiving love. The inner experience of receiving love is not passive; it becomes active motivation and inner reorientation.

**T4.4.3** — What does love-receiving reveal about the characteristic?

**[A — Love]** E — Love received does not merely satisfy the recipient; it transforms them into a love-giver. The structural logic of 2Cor 5:14 — received love of Christ → living no longer for oneself → living for Christ — shows that receiving love is not the end of the movement but its beginning. Love received generates love given; the God-to-human direction (T4.1) generates the human-to-God direction (T4.2) and the human-to-human direction (T4.3).

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — What are the limits and boundaries of love in the human-to-human interface?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence reveals both structural limits and boundary violations. *Structural limits*: Group 1563 (fileō — ranking): Mat 10:37 — "whoever loves father or mother more than me is not worthy of me" — natural love has its proper ordering; exceeding that ordering (placing it above love of Christ) violates the structural boundary. *Boundary violations — failure*: Group 1585 (chesed-failure): Judg 8:35 (not showing chesed to Gideon's family), 2Ch 24:22 (Joash not remembering Jehoiada's chesed) — love that falls short of its covenantal obligation. *Boundary violations — inversion*: Group 1579 (2Sa 13:15 — Amnon's love turning to hatred) — love inverted into its opposite when thwarted or satisfied.

**T4.5.2** — Is love bounded by justice or other characteristics?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes. Group 1585 (chesed-failure, Hos 6:6): "I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings" — love is prioritised above ritual but operates within the framework of knowing God, which implies righteousness. The enemy-love command (Group 1539) does not negate justice — it extends love beyond what justice requires without contradicting it. The most explicit boundary: Mat 22:39 — love of neighbour *as yourself* — the self is the measure, which implies a proper self-regard that love does not negate.

**T4.5.3** — Are there types of objects toward which love cannot properly be directed?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, from Group 1541 (disordered love): love of the world (1Jo 2:15), love of darkness (Joh 3:19), love of money (implied by afilarguros — Group 1567 adjacent), love of self-glory (Joh 12:43 — Group 1541: "they loved the glory that comes from man"). These are not legitimate objects of love — directing love toward them constitutes disordering of the inner being. The characteristic has a proper direction-structure; love directed at improper objects is still love-functioning (the mechanism is the same) but constitutes the characteristic in its most dangerous form.

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**T4.6.1** — Does this characteristic operate in relation to spiritual beings?

**[A — Love]** E — Partially. Group 3603 (chesed-attribute): Psa 57:3 — "He will send from heaven and save me; he will put to shame him who tramples on me; God will send out his steadfast love and his faithfulness" — the divine chesed operates through heavenly agency. Group 1576 (a.hev — loyal friend): 2Ch 20:7 — Abraham named God's "friend" — the friend-relationship between God and Abraham implies a spiritual-being dimension of the love-bond (though Abraham is human, the relationship is with the divine). The verse evidence does not extensively address love in relation to angels or demonic beings within M05-A.

**T4.6.2** — Are there implications for spiritual warfare or protection?

**[A — Love]** E — Indirectly. The chasid's protection (Group 1586: 1Sa 2:9 — "he will guard the feet of his faithful ones") implies that love-faithfulness creates a protective relationship with God. The "guard" may operate through spiritual agency, but the evidence does not make this explicit. Group 1541 (disordered love): Jam 4:4 — "friendship with the world is enmity with God" — misdirected love creates an adversarial spiritual relationship, which implies that love's direction has spiritual-warfare implications even where these are not directly named.

**T4.6.3** — Note where evidence is silent.

**[A — Love]** S — The verse evidence is largely silent on love operating specifically in relation to angels, demons, or other spiritual beings as distinct objects or subjects of love. The spiritual-beings interface is minimally evidenced in M05-A.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**T5.1.1** — What transformation does this characteristic produce in the inner being?

**[A — Love]** E — The verse evidence points to several transformative effects. (1) *Reorientation of agency*: 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544) — "the love of Christ controls us... that those who live might no longer live for themselves but for him who for their sake died" — love transforms the person's fundamental agency-direction from self-referential to other-directed. (2) *Integration of virtues*: Col 3:14 (Group 1544) — "above all these put on love, which binds everything together in perfect harmony" — love produces the integration of all other virtues. (3) *Community constitution*: Joh 13:34-35 (Group 1540) — the community of love is a new social formation produced by love's mutual operation. (4) *Expansion of the moral self*: Mat 5:44 (Group 1539) — enemy-love transforms the moral self's scope from natural-affinity to universal.

**T5.1.2** — Is the transformation instantaneous or gradual?

**[A — Love]** E — The evidence suggests primarily gradual. Group 3604 (chesed-enacted): the accumulation of acts of divine chesed over Israel's history builds a record of faithfulness. Group 1584 (human chesed): Rut 3:10 — "this last kindness greater than the first" — love developing and deepening over time. 1Th 3:12 (Group 1544): "may the Lord make you increase and abound in love for one another" — love as something that increases, implying gradual formation. But there is also an instantaneous dimension: Rom 5:5 (Group 1542) — love "poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — the Spirit's communication of love may be conceived as a decisive moment.

**T5.1.3** — What evidence supports the characterisation of love as transformative rather than merely expressive?

**[A — Love]** E — 2Cor 5:14-15 (Group 1544) is the most explicit transformative evidence: received love of Christ produces a total reorientation of the person's self-understanding and purpose. This is not merely expression but structural transformation — "no longer for themselves... but for him." Rom 8:28-29 (Group 1099 adjacent): those who love God are being conformed to the image of the Son — love is the condition under which the transformative work proceeds.

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — What sequence of inner states does this characteristic produce or require?

**[A — Love]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Prior love-of-God* → *obedience* → *deeper love-of-God*: Joh 14:21 (Group 1537) — "whoever has my commandments and keeps them, he it is who loves me. And he who loves me will be loved by my Father, and I will love him" — love generates obedience which generates fuller love. (2) *Divine love received* → *hope* → *perseverance*: Rom 5:3-5 (Group 1542) — suffering → endurance → character → hope, grounded in the love poured into the heart. (3) *Chesed remembered* → *trust* → *renewed petition*: Group 3603 (Psa 25:6-7, Psa 86:5/13) — the memory of past chesed grounds present trust and petition.

**T5.2.2** — Is the sequence consistent across the evidence?

**[A — Love]** E — Broadly consistent in two main patterns: (a) love-received → love-activated (the 2Cor 5:14 pattern — received love produces active love-giving); (b) love-declared → love-trusted (the chesed-attribute pattern — Group 3603 — declared divine love becomes the ground of trust in difficulty). The sequences differ by register but share the common property that love in one form generates love in another — love is self-propagating in the inner-being economy.

**T5.2.3** — Where does the sequence break or face interruption?

**[A — Love]** E — Group 1585 (chesed-failure, 10 verses): the sequence can break when love is not remembered (2Ch 24:22) or when it is replaced by routine rather than genuine covenant (Jer 2:2 — Israel's early love forgotten, now nominal). Mat 24:12 (Group 1543) — "the love of many will grow cold" — the sequence can be interrupted by the increase of lawlessness in the environment. The interruption of love's developmental sequence is always associated with forgetting, moral disorder, or competing allegiances.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — What mechanisms produce change in this characteristic in the inner being?

**[A — Love]** E — Three mechanisms evidenced. (1) *The Spirit's communication*: Rom 5:5 (Group 1542) — "God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — the Spirit is the primary mechanism of love's production in the human person. (2) *The command and its reception*: Joh 13:34 (Group 1540) — "a new commandment I give to you" — the command functions as a formative word that directs the inner being toward love. Jam 1:21 (Group 1262 adjacent in M05-E but relevant principle): the word received with meekness is the mechanism of transformation. (3) *The experience of received love*: 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544) — experiencing Christ's love produces the inner change that redirects agency.

**T5.3.2** — Is there a role for practice or habit in the development of love?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, from the covenantal register. Group 1584 (human chesed): Ruth's increasing chesed (Rut 3:10 — "this last kindness greater than the first") implies cumulative practice. The chasid (Group 1586) — the person constituted by covenantal faithfulness — is formed through sustained practice of chesed-love over time. 1Th 3:12 (Group 1544): "may the Lord make you increase and abound in love" — the increase implies developmental practice, though the mechanism is prayer/divine enabling rather than self-discipline alone.

**T5.3.3** — Is the mechanism primarily divine or human?

**[A — Love]** E — Primarily divine, with human co-operation. Rom 5:5 (Group 1542), Gal 5:22 (Spirit-fruit) — the fundamental mechanism is divine. But the human person must receive, practise, and not suppress the love that is communicated: Joh 14:15 (Group 1537) — keeping commandments as the expression of love implies human volitional co-operation with the love that has been given.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — How does this characteristic relate to suffering and affliction?

**[A — Love]** E — Two directions evidenced. (1) *Love persisting through suffering*: Group 3603 (Lam 3:22) — the chesed-never-ceasing declaration is made under Jerusalem's destruction — love is the characteristic that does not cease when everything else fails. 2Cor 5:14 (Group 1544) — love of Christ drives Paul through apostolic suffering. 2Cor 12:15 (Group 1544): "I will most gladly spend and be spent for your souls. If I love you more, am I to be loved less?" — love persisting through relational rejection. (2) *Love as the ground of endurance through suffering*: Rom 5:3-5 (Group 1542): the love poured into hearts is the anchor that enables hope to endure through suffering.

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering test, refine, or reveal love?

**[A — Love]** E — Suffering functions as love's *revealer*. The chesed that never ceases (Lam 3:22 — Group 3603) is most definitively evidenced precisely in the extreme — the destruction of Jerusalem is the maximum test, and chesed persists through it. Rut 3:10 (Group 1584): Ruth's love demonstrated across adversity (widowhood, poverty, foreign land) shows love's quality under pressure. Group 1585 (chesed-failure): Joash's failure under prosperity (receiving chesed without returning it — 2Ch 24:22) and Israel's failure (Jer 2:2 — early love not sustained) show that love fails in the absence of adversity-testing discipline. Suffering tends to reveal genuine love; comfort tends to allow its evasion.

**T5.4.3** — Is there evidence of love producing or alleviating suffering?

**[A — Love]** E — Both. Love *produces* suffering in the giver: 2Cor 2:4 (Group 1544) — Paul's anguish of heart in love-giving. Joh 15:13 (Group 1542) — laying down one's life is love's ultimate expression, at ultimate cost. Love *alleviates* suffering in the receiver: Group 3604 (chesed-enacted) — each instance is God's love intervening in human suffering (Joseph in prison — Gen 39:21; Israel in slavery — Exo 15:13).

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — How does this characteristic relate to the process of formation and sanctification?

**[A — Love]** E — Love is the character-goal and the formation-ground simultaneously. Col 3:14 (Group 1544): love is "the bond of perfection" — the integrating virtue that constitutes holiness in its relational dimension. Gal 5:22 (adjacent): love as first-named Spirit-fruit positions love as the primary outcome of Spirit-formation. 1Th 3:12 (Group 1544): "may the Lord make you increase and abound in love" — growth in love is the formation objective. The chasid (Group 1586): the sanctified person is constituted by chesed-faithfulness — love is not merely a means to holiness but its content.

**T5.5.2** — Is love a means of sanctification or its outcome?

**[A — Love]** E — Both simultaneously. As *means*: love of God drives obedience (Joh 14:15, Group 1537), which is the mechanism of sanctification. Love of neighbour (Group 1539-1540) structures the person toward others rather than self, which is the direction of sanctification. As *outcome*: the sanctified person is characterised by love (1Ti 1:5 — adjacent in M05-D but relevant: "the aim of our charge is love that issues from a pure heart and good conscience and sincere faith"). The formation process both requires and produces love.

**T5.5.3** — What does the evidence reveal about love's role in characterological formation?

**[A — Love]** E — The chasid (Group 1586, 33 verses) is the most concentrated evidence: the chasid is the person who has been *constituted by* chesed-love — not merely one who performs loving acts but one whose character is structured by love-faithfulness. Psa 4:3: "the Lord has set apart the godly (chasid) for himself" — the character formation produces a distinctive identity. Love is not merely added to existing character; it is the integrating centre that makes character coherent.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Does this characteristic have an eschatological trajectory?

**[A — Love]** E — Yes, explicitly. 1Cor 13:8 (Group 1543): "love never ends. ### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Where does the verse evidence engage, confirm, or challenge human science frameworks for understanding love?

**[A — Love]** E — Three engagements from the evidence: (1) *Against love-as-feeling only*: The command-structure (Mat 22:37, Group 1537; Joh 13:34, Group 1540) directly challenges psychological frameworks that define love primarily as an emotion or feeling. Love in the verse evidence is commanded — it is a volitional orientation, not merely an affect. Human science frameworks that reduce love to neurochemistry or evolved pair-bonding fail to account for the commanded, universal (including enemies — Mat 5:44, Group 1539), and eschatologically permanent (1Cor 13:8, Group 1543) character of love as evidenced. (3) *Challenging frameworks that oppose love and justice*: Psa 85:10 (Group 3603) — "steadfast love and faithfulness meet; righteousness and peace kiss each other" — the verse evidence presents love and justice as complementary, not competing. Human-science frameworks that pit compassion against justice, or love against law (Rom 13:10: "love is the fulfilling of the law"), are challenged by the evidence's consistent integration of love and righteousness.

**[A — Love]** E — (1) *Love's divine-communicative origin*: Rom 5:5 (Group 1542) — "poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit" — places love's deepest form outside the scope of human developmental psychology. (2) *Love's eschatological permanence*: 1Cor 13:8 (Group 1543) — love that "never ends" when all gifts pass — exceeds any human-science framework which is bounded by mortality and history. (3) *The structural priority of divine love over human love*: "we love because he first loved us" (1Jo 4:19) — human love is derivative rather than originative. Human science frameworks that treat love as a naturally occurring capacity miss the theological claim that genuine love is first received before it can be given.

**T7.3.3** — What human science frameworks are most useful for illuminating the evidence, without being imposed on it?

**[A — Love]** E — (1) *Attachment theory* — useful for illuminating the bond-structure of love (filos groups, chasid, chesed covenantal patterns) without being imposed as the primary explanation. (3) *Moral philosophy's distinction between love-as-preference and love-as-commitment* — maps well onto the evidence's distinction between natural-affection (fileō) and commanded-love (agapaō). Useful as an analytical tool while recognising that the evidence integrates both rather than merely choosing between them.
