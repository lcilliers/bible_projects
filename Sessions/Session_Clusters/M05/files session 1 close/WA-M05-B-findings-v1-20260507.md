## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The verse evidence reveals compassion as a constitutive feature of God's character, not an occasional response. Group 1594 (ra.chum — compassionate as divine attribute, 13 verses): Exo 34:6 — "The Lord, the Lord, a God merciful and gracious (ra.chum), slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love" — ra.chum heads the divine self-disclosure formula, placing compassion as the first-named attribute. Psa 103:8 and Psa 145:8 repeat the formula; Jon 4:2 shows Jonah citing it as the ground of his complaint. Group 1595 (ra.cham — divine act of compassion, 43 verses): Psa 103:13 — "as a father shows compassion to his children, so the Lord shows compassion to those who fear him" — compassion is the constitutive relational disposition of God-as-father toward human-as-child. The father-child analogy names the structural relationship within which compassion operates. Group 1638 (oiktirmos, 5 verses): 2Cor 1:3 — "the Father of mercies and God of all comfort" — compassion is God's paternal identity-name, not merely an attribute he possesses. The evidence reveals that the God reflected in human compassion is a God who is moved by the encounter with vulnerability and suffering in a way that constitutes his character.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Extensively and explicitly. Group 1594 (13 verses) is entirely composed of declarations of divine compassion as attribute: the ra.chum formula recurs from Exo 34:6 through Psa 86:15, Psa 103:8, Psa 111:4, Psa 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2. Group 1595 (43 verses) spans the full narrative corpus from the Exodus to the prophets — divine compassion as the mode of restoration throughout Israel's history. The significance for the human person: compassion in the human is a participation in what God is. Luk 6:36 (Group 1639, anchor): "be merciful, even as your Father is merciful" — the human's compassion is commanded as correspondence to the divine compassion. The standard is not a general moral principle but the divine character itself. The human person who shows compassion is being shaped into the image of the God who is constitutively compassionate.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**[B — Compassion]** S — Scripture is not silent here. Compassion is one of the most explicitly and repeatedly attributed divine characteristics in M05-B's verse base. No silence finding applies.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The verse evidence reveals two created purposes. First, compassion equips the person to respond to suffering in the image of God: the Samaritan's compassion (Group 333, Luk 10:33 — anchor Luk 15:20) produces the concrete response that constitutes neighbourly love — "which of these three do you think was a neighbour?" Compassion is the inner movement that generates the acts that fulfil the love-command toward those in need. Second, compassion functions as a social-bonding and community-sustaining mechanism: Group 2717 (eusplanchnos — tenderhearted, 2 verses): Eph 4:32 — "be kind to one another, tenderhearted (eusplanchnos), forgiving one another" — tenderhearted compassion is the inner condition that enables forgiveness, which is the community-sustaining act. The created purpose is to equip the person as a responsive, other-directed social being capable of absorbing and relieving suffering.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The evidence most clearly positions compassion as a response to the fallen condition, while suggesting it reflects something in the created order. The triggers for compassion across M05-B are consistently suffering, need, guilt, and vulnerability — all features of the fallen world. Group 333 (splanchnizō, 12 verses): every instance of Jesus's visceral compassion is triggered by disease, grief, hunger, or lostness — conditions of the fallen order. The ra.cham/ra.cha.mim vocabulary (Groups 1595-1596) is overwhelmingly concerned with Israel's suffering, exile, and sin — again, fallen-world conditions. However, the maternal analogy (Isa 49:15, Group 1595 — "can a woman forget her nursing child?") suggests that compassion has a created-order root in the mother-infant bond, which is a feature of created human nature. The evidence suggests compassion as a natural capacity (mother-child bond) that is activated and intensified in response to fallen-world suffering.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, in the eschatological-restoration passages of Group 1595. Isa 49:13 — "for the Lord has comforted his people and will have compassion on his afflicted" — compassion as the mode of the eschatological comfort of Israel. Isa 54:7-8 (Group 1596) — "with great compassion I will gather you... with everlasting love I will have compassion on you" — the great compassion of the eschatological gathering is the future fullness toward which present compassion points. Mic 7:19 (Group 1595) — "he will again have compassion on us; he will tread our iniquities under foot" — eschatological compassion as the final resolution of both suffering and sin. Zec 1:16 (Group 1596) — "I have returned to Jerusalem with mercy (ra.cha.mim)" — the return-with-compassion as the shape of eschatological restoration.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion expresses the image primarily in two ways. First, through the somatic register: Group 333 (splanchnizō, 12 verses) — Jesus's compassion is visceral, located in the bowels (splanchnon — the seat of emotion in Greek physiology), and immediately expressed in action (touching, healing, feeding). The human person whose inner being is moved at the sight of suffering and whose compassion issues in act is imaging the God who is moved by the sight of his people's affliction (Isa 63:9, Group 339 — "in all their affliction he was afflicted"). Second, through the parental register: Psa 103:13 (Group 1595, anchor) — "as a father shows compassion to his children" — the father-to-child compassion is the human image of the divine compassion. The parent who is moved by the vulnerability of their child is imaging what God is toward the human person.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Genuinely shared. The command of Luk 6:36 (Group 1639, anchor) — "be merciful, even as your Father is merciful" — presupposes that the human can genuinely express what God is, not merely imitate it from outside. Group 2717 (eusplanchnos): Eph 4:32 — "be tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you" — the human's tenderhearted compassion is grounded in and shaped by God's compassion in Christ. The sharing is real but asymmetric: God's compassion is originating and inexhaustible; the human's is derivative, limited, and often failing (Group 3598/3599 — the cha.mal failure register; Lam 4:10 — the catastrophic collapse of compassion).

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Group 349 (ra.cha.ma.ni — compassionate women, 1 verse): Lam 4:10 — "the hands of compassionate women have boiled their own children" — this is the most dramatic image-condition diagnostic in M05-B. The word "compassionate" applied to the women who commit the act names the depth of the catastrophe: the image has been so distorted by judgment that the naturally compassionate are driven to the worst. Compassion's collapse is an image-collapse diagnostic. Conversely, Group 333 (splanchnizō): Jesus's compassion for the crowds who are "harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd" (Mat 9:36) reveals the image at its most intact — the one in whom the image is undefaced is the one most completely moved by the suffering of others.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. The maternal-compassion analogy in Group 1595 is typologically deployed: Isa 49:15 — "can a woman forget her nursing child, that she should have no compassion on the son of her womb? Even these may forget, yet I will not forget you" — the mother's compassion for her infant is used as the type of which divine compassion is the antitype. The human compassion-instance is the analogical vehicle; the divine exceeds it ("even these may forget, yet I will not"). Group 333 (splanchnizō): Jesus's compassion for the crowds (Mat 9:36) — "harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd" — is typological in that it deploys the shepherd-compassion of Num 27:17 (Moses requesting a leader so Israel is not "as sheep that have no shepherd") to identify Jesus as the eschatological shepherd. The compassion-in-the-wilderness (Mat 15:32) echoes the Exodus wilderness provision — the same divine compassion operating through the Son.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Direction is primarily divine-establishes-pattern → human-participates. God's compassion in the ra.cham/ra.cha.mim vocabulary (Groups 1595-1596) establishes the pattern; the human compassion commanded in Luk 6:36 (Group 1639) and Eph 4:32 (Group 2717) is the participation. Within the OT, the maternal-infant compassion (Isa 49:15) is the human-type pointing to the divine-antitype — here the direction is human analogy → divine reality (the lesser compassion illuminating the greater). Dual direction is present: human compassion → divine compassion (illustrative) AND divine compassion → human compassion (constitutive).

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

**[B — Compassion]** — Not applicable; typological use is evidenced (T0.4.1-2).

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The programme names this sub-group "Compassion." The name signals a movement of the inner being in response to the sight of another's suffering — a stirring that precedes and motivates responding action. "Compassion" carries the Latin etymology com-pati (to suffer with), which is lexically precise to the Greek splanchnizō (Group 333) and sumpatheō (Group 334) registers, but less precise to the Hebrew ra.cham/ra.cha.mim register where the primary root is the word for womb. The naming by "compassion" foregrounds the relational-responsive dimension; the Hebrew womb-root foregrounds the constitutional-visceral dimension. Both are present in the evidence.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Five primary terms with distinct definitional registers:

*H7349 ra.chum* (Group 1594, 13v): the adjective — "compassionate" as a divine attribute. Definitionally: compassion as what God IS constitutively, not merely what he does.

*H7355 ra.cham* (Group 1595, 43v): the verb — "to have compassion / to show compassion." Definitionally: compassion as an act proceeding from the attribute. The verbal form foregrounds the movement from inner disposition to outer engagement.

*H7356B ra.cha.mim* (Group 1596, 39v): the plural noun — "compassions / mercies" (the womb-word). Definitionally: compassion as a deep inner reservoir rather than a single act — the plural names the inexhaustible supply of God's compassion. The womb-root locates it at the deepest biological-intuitive level of human experience.

*G4697 splanchnizō* (Group 333, 12v): the verb — "to be moved with compassion in the bowels." Definitionally: compassion as a somatic-visceral event — the inner being convulsed by the encounter with suffering.

*G3628 oiktirmos* (Group 1638, 5v): the noun — "compassions / mercies." Definitionally: compassion as a named quality belonging to God's paternal identity (2Cor 1:3 — "Father of mercies").

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[B — Compassion]** E — All three. *Directional*: compassion is always triggered by an object — the suffering other. Group 333: every splanchnizō instance names what triggered the compassion (crowds, leper, blind men, grieving widow, hungry multitude, lost son). Direction is always from the one who is moved toward the one who suffers. *Relational*: compassion presupposes a relationship asymmetry — the one who shows compassion has power or capacity the sufferer lacks (the father has the ability to run and embrace; the physician has the ability to heal). *Constitutional*: the womb-root of ra.cha.mim places compassion at the deepest constitutional level — not a surface response but a stirring at the level of one's most fundamental biological self.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does compassion appear to be?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is primarily an *inner movement* — a stirring of the inner being triggered by external encounter with suffering — that then generates acts. It is neither a purely static disposition (like chesed's covenantal faithfulness) nor merely an act; it is the dynamic movement between the encounter and the response. Group 333 (splanchnizō) evidences this most clearly: the pattern across all 12 verses is encounter with suffering → inner convulsion → action (touching, healing, feeding, speaking, releasing). The inner movement is the middle term. Group 1595 (ra.cham) confirms: Jer 31:20 — "for as often as I speak against him, I do remember him still. Therefore my heart yearns for him; I will surely have mercy on him" — the compassion (ra.cham) is preceded by the inner movement of yearning (hamah — stirred/groaning), naming the dynamic quality precisely. This is distinct from the static-attribute dimension of ra.chum (Group 1594), where compassion names what God is rather than a specific stirring.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Complex, with at least three constituent elements visible in the evidence. (1) *Recognition of suffering*: compassion requires the perception of another's need — Mar 10:47 (Group 333, adjacent context) shows that the cry of the sufferer initiates the compassion sequence. (2) *Inner movement*: the stirring itself — the splanchnizō convulsion, the ra.cham yearning. (3) *Response-generation*: compassion issues in an act toward the sufferer — touching (Mar 1:41), releasing (Mat 18:27), feeding (Mat 15:32), gathering (Isa 54:7, Group 1596), restoring (Deu 30:3, Group 1595). The compassion that does not issue in action is not fully evidenced within M05-B — the inner movement in all evidenced cases produces a corresponding outer response. Additionally, M05-B evidences a structural complement: the *withdrawal* of compassion (Groups 338, 630, 3599) is an equally significant constituent — the deliberate suppression of the inner movement for judicial purposes.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is the visceral inner movement of the person toward the one who suffers or is in need — a stirring at the constitutional level (womb-depth, bowel-depth) triggered by the encounter with vulnerability, suffering, or lostness, that generates and drives the responding act. Constitutional location: the bowels/inward parts (splanchnon — Group 333), the womb-deep inner reservoir (ra.cha.mim — Group 1596). The faculties primarily engaged: perception (the encounter triggers compassion), affect (the inner movement is affective in character), and agency (compassion issues in initiative). The impact on the person: compassion produces the person as a responder — one who does not pass by but is moved to act, and whose inner being absorbs something of the other's suffering in the movement toward them.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The structural opposite is *the withholding of compassion for judicial-righteous reasons* and *cruelty/callousness as a character defect*. Two forms are evidenced. (1) *Judicial withholding* (Group 338, chus — 16 verses): "your eye shall not pity (chus)" — the repeated Deuteronomic formula names the deliberate suppression of the compassion-impulse as the requirement of righteous judgment. This is not cruelty but the active subordination of compassion to justice. (2) *Cruelty/callousness as character failure*: Isa 13:18 (Group 1595 — ra.cham negative instance): "they will have no mercy on the fruit of the womb; their eyes will not pity children" — the Babylonian army described in terms of total compassion-absence as the mark of their destructive character. Amo 1:11 (Group 1596): "because he pursued his brother with the sword and cast off all pity (ra.cha.mim), and his anger tore perpetually" — the absence of compassion as the diagnostic of Edom's sin. The structural opposite is therefore both the judicial requirement (which suspends compassion for justice's sake) and the moral failure (which is simply the absence of compassion as a character defect).

**T1.3.2** — What does compassion explicitly exclude or resist?

**[B — Compassion]** E — From the evidence: (1) Indifference to suffering — the splanchnizō pattern (Group 333) consistently contrasts with passing by (the Samaritan who stopped vs the priest and Levite who did not — Luk 10:33). (2) Exploitation of vulnerability — Eze 16:5 (Group 337 and 630) — "no eye pitied you" in the context of the abandoned infant: compassion resists the neglect and abandonment of the vulnerable. (3) Disproportionate judgment — Group 1596: the ra.cha.mim vocabulary in the prophets consistently operates as the force that restrains the full expression of divine wrath ("for a brief moment I deserted you, but with great compassion I will gather you" — Isa 54:7).

**T1.3.3** — What is compassion not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is not mercy (M05-C — though related): mercy operates specifically in the judicial frame of guilt/innocence/forgiveness; compassion operates in the frame of suffering/need/vulnerability. Compassion is not love (M05-A): love is the prior constitutive orientation toward the other; compassion is the specific movement triggered by the encounter with suffering within that loving relationship. Compassion is not comfort (M05-F): comfort is directed toward the grieving inner being specifically; compassion is the broader inner movement toward any form of suffering or need. The boundary between compassion and mercy is the most analytically significant: Exo 34:6 names both side by side (ra.chum — compassionate; chanun — gracious; chesed — steadfast love) but treats them as distinct. The ra.cham/eleos overlap (Group 1595's LXX equivalents) creates the lexical blurring, but the phenomena are distinguishable.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does compassion operate within the inner person?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Four modes are evidenced.

(1) *As visceral convulsion*: Group 333 (splanchnizō) — the inner being is physically stirred: "moved with pity, he stretched out his hand and touched him" (Mar 1:41). The compassion-movement precedes and generates the action without a deliberative stage — it is reflex-speed in its inner operation.

(2) *As constitutive attribute*: Group 1594 (ra.chum) — God's compassion as the permanent inner disposition that does not require triggering but IS what God is. The attribute-mode is the ground from which the movement-mode (ra.cham) flows.

(3) *As deliberate restraint from judgment*: Group 338 (chus — withholding) — the deliberate suppression of the natural compassion-impulse in order to carry out righteous judgment. This is compassion operating in its negative mode — it is named precisely because it must be deliberately overridden, implying the compassion-impulse is the default.

(4) *As fellow-suffering*: Group 334 (sumpatheō, 2 verses): Heb 4:15 — "we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses" — compassion as the capacity to enter into and share the inner experience of the sufferer. This is the most interior mode: not merely moved toward the sufferer but moving into their experience from within.

**T1.4.2** — Does compassion operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, significantly by direction and context.

*Divine compassion* (Groups 1594-1596): operates from the inexhaustible womb-depth reservoir — ra.cha.mim (plural) naming the abundance that is available to be drawn upon. The divine compassion is never exhausted.

*Human compassion* (Groups 333, 334, 335, 2717, 3598): operates from a limited inner reservoir that can be overwhelmed, suppressed, or withdrawn — Lam 4:10 (Group 349) names its catastrophic collapse; Heb 10:34 (Group 334) names it as a generous sustained act under pressure.

*Judicial compassion-withholding* (Groups 338, 630, 3599): operates as a deliberate inner act against the natural compassion-impulse — requiring sustained volitional effort to maintain over repeated encounters (Eze 5:11, 7:4, 7:9, 8:18, 9:5, 9:10 all repeat the formula, suggesting sustained effort to maintain the judicial posture).

**T1.4.3** — Does compassion have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, evidenced in two registers. (1) *Declaration of the attribute*: the ra.chum formula (Group 1594 — Exo 34:6 and its 11 repetitions) operates communicatively — the declaration "the Lord is compassionate" is a speech-act that constitutes the community's knowledge of God and anchors petitions to it. Jon 4:2: Jonah cites the formula as a complaint — even compassion-declaration can be the vehicle of theological argument. (2) *The compassion-speech of God*: Jer 31:20 (Group 1595) — "therefore my heart yearns for him; I will surely have mercy on him" — God speaks his compassion, naming both the inner movement and the action it will generate. The speech of divine compassion is itself a form of comfort and assurance to the recipient.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The evidence reveals distinct immediate responses for the one receiving compassion and the one experiencing it internally.

*For the recipient*: the most direct evidence is Group 333 (splanchnizō): Mat 18:27 — the servant who received the master's compassion (and consequent debt release) went out and immediately demanded payment from his fellow servant — the immediate response to received compassion in this case is not reciprocal compassion but its absence, which the parable deploys to make the moral point. The correct immediate response is implied in its violation: received compassion should generate compassion-shown.

*For the compassion-experiencer*: Mar 1:41 — "moved with pity, he stretched out his hand" — the immediate response to the compassion-movement is action, without deliberative gap. Luk 15:20 (Group 333, anchor) — "while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran" — ran is the immediate bodily expression of the inner compassion-movement.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Consistent in one direction: compassion experienced internally → immediate action. The splanchnizō pattern (Group 333) is without exception: every instance of Jesus's visceral compassion produces an immediate act (healing, feeding, raising, teaching, releasing). The consistency is itself the primary analytical finding here — compassion does not stall or deliberate; it moves directly to response.

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

**[B — Compassion]** E — The evidence is silent on the inner phenomenology of the one receiving compassion in M05-B's verse base. What is it like from within to be the recipient of compassion? The verse evidence describes the act of compassion-showing but does not directly access the recipient's inner experience in most cases. Dan 1:9 (Group 1596) — "God gave Daniel favour and compassion in the sight of the chief of the eunuchs" — names the outcome (favour received) but not Daniel's inner experience of receiving it.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does compassion produce in the inner being over time?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Two sustained effects evidenced.

(1) *Trust and hope anchored in God's compassion as reliable*: Group 1596 (ra.cha.mim — Psa 25:6, Psa 40:11, Psa 51:1, Psa 79:8) — the sustained appeal to God's compassions as the ground of petition across multiple Psalmic contexts establishes compassion as the inner anchor: the person who has experienced God's compassion returns to it as reliable. Lam 3:22 (Group 1596) — "his mercies never come to an end" — names the sustained quality directly.

(2) *Community-sustaining tenderness*: Group 2717 (eusplanchnos — Eph 4:32) — "be kind to one another, tenderhearted (eusplanchnos), forgiving one another" — the tenderhearted compassion is the sustained inner disposition that enables ongoing forgiveness within the community. Over time, compassion in this register produces communities capable of absorbing relational failure rather than fragmenting.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained compassion establish?

**[B — Compassion]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Receptivity to the other's suffering* — the person whose inner being is regularly moved by others' distress develops an increasing capacity to perceive and be moved. Group 334 (sumpatheō): Heb 4:15 — Christ's sustained incarnate experience of human weakness is the ground of his capacity to "sympathize with our weaknesses" — sustained exposure to suffering produces the sympathetic capacity. (2) *Humility before God in petition* — the sustained prayer-appeal to God's ra.cha.mim (Group 1596: Psa 51:1, Psa 79:8, Psa 119:77, 156; Neh 9:19, 27, 28, 31; Dan 9:9, 18) establishes the posture of dependence on divine compassion as the ground of all access to God. The person who sustains this prayer-orientation is formed in humility.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. The immediate response to compassion-movement is action (T1.5.1). The sustained effect is characterological formation: the one who regularly responds in compassion becomes tenderhearted (eusplanchnos — Group 2717), sympathetic (sumpathēs — Group 335), and capable of fellow-suffering (sumpatheō — Group 334). The immediate response is a single act; the sustained effect is a formed inner disposition — the one becomes constitutively compassionate through the repeated practice of compassion-response.

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**[B — Compassion]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Vulnerability and need*: Group 333 (splanchnizō) — compassion is consistently triggered by real vulnerability (Mat 9:36 — "harassed and helpless"; Luk 7:13 — a widow who has just lost her only son). The conditions that enable receiving compassion are the conditions of genuine need, not merely performance of need. (2) *Repentance and return*: Group 1595 (ra.cham) — Deu 30:3: "when you return to the Lord your God... then the Lord your God will restore your fortunes and have mercy on you." Isa 55:7 (Group 1595): "let the wicked forsake his way... and let him return to the Lord, who will have compassion on him." The return-condition is consistently named as enabling the fuller expression of divine compassion. (3) *Persistence in appeal*: Group 1672 (ra.cha.min — Dan 2:18): Daniel's company "seeking mercy from the God of heaven" — the active seeking-of-compassion as the condition for receiving it.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[B — Compassion]** E — From the evidence: (1) *Hardness/stubbornness*: Group 1594 — Neh 9:17 — "they... were not mindful of the wonders you performed... and were not willing to obey" — the refusal to acknowledge God's prior acts is the condition in which his compassion is withheld. (2) *Judicial guilt and persistence in sin*: Group 1595 — Hos 1:6 — "I will no more have mercy on the house of Israel, because I will utterly take them away" — persistent unfaithfulness blocks the reception of compassion in the prophetic register. Group 630 (cha.mal withdrawal): 2Ch 36:17 — "he gave them all into his hand" — the precondition is the persistent rejection of God's messengers (v.15-16). (3) *Self-sufficiency*: the implicit condition across Groups 333 and 337 — compassion flows toward the genuinely needy; those who do not recognise their need do not receive it.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters compassion but does not receive it?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Group 3599 (cha.mal — disobedient compassion, 11 verses): 1Sa 15:9 — Saul and the people "spared Agag and the best of the sheep" — the person who shows compassion toward what should not be spared reveals an inner-being state of misaligned compassion: the compassion-impulse is functioning (they were moved to spare) but directed at the wrong object, in violation of divine command. This is not failure to receive compassion but failure to subordinate it rightly. The inner state of the person who encounters divine compassion but does not receive it is more directly evidenced in the ra.cham negative instances (Group 1595): Isa 9:17 — "the Lord does not rejoice over their young men, and has no compassion on their fatherless and widows" — the people who experience this absence are in a state of divine discipline; their inner condition is one of hardness under judgment.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion belongs primarily to the *affective-relational* dimension — it is the inner movement of the person that responds to suffering with felt engagement and relational initiative. The splanchnizō vocabulary (Group 333) most directly names this: the bowel-level stirring is an affective event that produces relational action. The womb-rooted ra.cha.mim (Group 1596) places the characteristic at the deepest biological-affective level. Unlike love (M05-A), whose primary dimension is relational-moral (commanded, volitional), compassion's primary dimension is affective — it is triggered by encounter, not commanded into existence.

**T1.8.2** — What evidence supports this classification?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) The somatic vocabulary throughout M05-B (splanchnizō — bowels; ra.cha.mim — womb) consistently names the affective and bodily registers as compassion's home. (2) The trigger-structure: compassion is not commanded in M05-B in the way love is (Mat 22:37 — "you shall love"). Luk 6:36 (Group 1639) — "be merciful as your Father is merciful" — is the closest to a command, but it is formed by analogy rather than by command to a specific inner movement. (3) The immediacy of the response (T1.5.2) — compassion moves without deliberation, which is characteristic of affective rather than purely volitional phenomena.

**T1.8.3** — Does compassion carry secondary dimensions?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. (1) *Volitional*: the judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630, 3599) names the deliberate suppression of compassion as a volitional act — this is the most clearly volitional dimension, where the will overrides the affective impulse. (2) *Constitutional* (attribute): Group 1594 (ra.chum) — compassion as what God is constitutively, not merely what he feels in response to stimuli. This constitutional dimension exceeds the purely affective and places compassion in the dimension of character. (3) *Somatic*: the splanchn-/womb vocabulary names a somatic-visceral secondary dimension — compassion is felt in the body, not merely conceived in the mind.

**[B — Compassion]** — Silent on explicit spirit-level location for compassion in the human person. The womb/bowel vocabulary dominates. Spirit-level origin may be inferred but is not textually present in M05-B's evidence base.

### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**[B — Compassion]** — The evidence positions compassion in the visceral-interior register (bowels, inward parts, womb) rather than using explicit soul-faculty language. ### T2.3 — Heart

**T2.3.1** — Is compassion explicitly located in the heart?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Partially, and indirectly. **T2.3.2** — What does heart-location reveal about compassion's constitutional depth?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The heart's involvement (Jer 31:20 — lev as the affective centre from which compassion flows) confirms that compassion is not a peripheral reflex but belongs to the person's inner-being centre. The difference from love (M05-A) is that love has the heart as its primary named location, while compassion uses the bowel/womb vocabulary as equally or more primary. Compassion is heart-level but described in more visceral terms.

**T2.3.3** — Is heart-location the primary or one of several locations?

**[B — Compassion]** E — One of several, and not the most prominent. The hierarchy for M05-B: bowels/inward-parts (most directly named in Group 333 and the me'eh vocabulary) → womb-depth (ra.cha.mim, Group 1596) → heart (Jer 31:20) → attribute-level (ra.chum, Group 1594 — which transcends any single location). The constitutional location vocabulary in M05-B is more somatic-visceral than in most other characteristics.

### T2.4 — Mind

**T2.4.1** — Is compassion explicitly located in the mind?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The mind is not named as compassion's location in M05-B's verse evidence. Compassion operates below the deliberative level — the splanchnizō evidence (Group 333) is notably pre-deliberative, and the womb-vocabulary is constitutional rather than cognitive.

**[B — Compassion]** E — The absence of mind-language for compassion's location reveals its primarily affective-somatic character as distinct from characteristics that are more cognitive in their operation. This distinguishes it from mercy (M05-C), which operates within the judicial-cognitive frame of guilt/forgiveness, and from goodness (M05-D), which involves moral evaluation.

**T2.4.3** — Note silence.

**[B — Compassion]** — Silent on mind-level location. This silence is analytically significant: it confirms compassion as primarily affective-somatic rather than cognitive in its constitutional home.

### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**T2.5.1** — Does the evidence locate compassion in other inner-being faculties beyond heart, soul, and mind?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes — the bowels/inward parts (splanchnon/me'eh) is the most distinctive location in all of M05-B. Group 333 (splanchnizō) — the verb is formed from the noun splanchnon (bowels/intestines) — compassion as a bowel-level phenomenon. Group 1638 (oiktirmos): Col 3:12 — "put on... compassionate hearts (splanchna oiktirmou — literally: bowels of compassion)" — the compound naming both registers. **T2.5.2** — What does the bowel/womb location reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — It reveals compassion as the inner-being characteristic most deeply embedded in the body-soul interface — below the deliberative level, at the level of visceral reflex and constitutional biological experience (the nursing mother, the bowels that contract). This confirms the T1.8 finding that compassion is primarily affective-somatic rather than primarily volitional or cognitive. It also reveals that Scripture takes seriously the embodied nature of the human person's inner life — compassion is not a purely mental event but a whole-person disturbance.

**T2.5.3** — Note any remaining gaps.

**[B — Compassion]** — The evidence is relatively full on constitutional location for M05-B — more so than for most characteristics, precisely because the vocabulary is unusually somatic.

### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**T2.6.1** — What is the significance of the body in relation to compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The body is unusually central to compassion compared to other characteristics. (1) The vocabulary itself is somatic: splanchnizō (Group 333) names a bowel-level event; ra.cha.mim (Group 1596) names the womb. The body is not merely the expression-medium but the constitutional site of the compassion-movement. (2) Compassion consistently produces bodily action: Mar 1:41 — "he stretched out his hand and touched him"; Luk 15:20 — "he ran and embraced him and kissed him"; Mat 20:34 — "Jesus... touched their eyes." The bodily touch is the natural continuation of the inner bowel-movement — compassion moves from the bowels outward to the hands. (3) Heb 10:34 (Group 334): "you had compassion on those in prison and joyfully accepted the plundering of your own property" — compassion produces bodily sacrifice (loss of property).

**T2.6.2** — Is there direct bodily involvement in compassion's operation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, more directly than almost any other characteristic in the programme. The splanchnizō vocabulary (Group 333) names a bodily event as the compassion itself — not the product of compassion but its mode of occurrence. The body is not the instrument of compassion's expression after the fact; the bodily stirring IS the compassion-event at its moment of arising.

**T2.6.3** — What does the body's role reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — It reveals compassion as the characteristic in M05 most thoroughly integrated with the body. Compassion cannot be understood purely as a mental or spiritual phenomenon — it is constitutively bodily in its Hebrew and Greek vocabulary. This has an important implication for understanding the divine compassion: when God is said to be "moved with compassion," Scripture attributes to him the functional equivalent of the most bodily inner-being response available in human experience. The womb-rooted ra.cha.mim in God names the deepest possible level of inner-being response using the most intimate bodily register.

### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**T2.7.1** — Does compassion flow from inner to outer (body as expression) or outer to inner (body as receptor)?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both, with the inner-to-outer direction primary. The standard pattern in Group 333: encounter (visual perception) → inner movement (splanchnizō) → bodily action (touch, healing, feeding). The flow is: outer encounter → inner stirring → outer act. But the inner stirring is itself somatic — the bowel-convulsion is inner-bodily, not external. So the direction is: outer perception → inner/bodily stirring → outer action. The body participates at both ends: it perceives the suffering (outer) and it acts in response (outer), with the inner-bodily compassion-movement as the middle term.

**T2.7.2** — What does this directional structure reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion's directional structure is distinctive: it is neither purely inner-to-outer (generated from within regardless of external stimuli) nor purely outer-to-inner (received from without). It is a response-structure — triggered externally, processed viscerally, expressed externally. This makes compassion uniquely relational in its constitutional structure: it cannot occur in isolation (it requires the encounter with another's suffering to be triggered). This distinguishes it from love (which can be a permanent inner disposition regardless of external triggering) and from gentleness (M05-E, which is a standing character quality).

**T2.7.3** — Note bidirectional or anomalous evidence.

**[B — Compassion]** S — The judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630) evidences the deliberate blocking of the outer-to-inner trigger — the commanded suppression of compassion requires overriding the normal compassion-direction. This is the most anomalous element: the natural direction of compassion (encounter → stirring → action) is deliberately interrupted by judicial command.

### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**T2.8.1** — Does compassion leave a constitutive deposit in the body?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The verse evidence does not speak of compassion leaving a physical/bodily deposit. The deposits it leaves are relational and characterological (the community of recipients, the formed character of the one who practises it) rather than somatic.

**T2.8.2** — What does this absence reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** S — Despite compassion's unusually somatic vocabulary, it does not deposit in the body in a lasting way. The bowel-convulsion is the moment of compassion, not a lasting physical alteration. This confirms that compassion's somatic character is in its operation rather than in its deposit.

**T2.8.3** — Note silence.

**[B — Compassion]** — Silent on somatic deposit. Compassion's body-engagement is at the moment of occurrence and in the action it generates, not in a lasting physical change.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — What does the evidence reveal about the origin or source of compassion in the human person?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Three sources evidenced. (1) *Created nature* (maternal-infant bond): the nursing-mother analogy (Group 1595, Isa 49:15) and the mother-for-child compassion of 1Ki 3:26 (Group 1596) suggest that the deepest human compassion has a created-natural source — the biological bond of the nursing mother is the supreme human instance of natural compassion. (2) *Character formation*: Group 334 (sumpatheō): Heb 4:15 — Christ's compassionate capacity is grounded in his shared incarnate experience — "in every respect tempted as we are." Sustained exposure to human suffering produces the sympathetic capacity. Formation through shared experience is a source. (3) *Divine command and modelling*: Luk 6:36 (Group 1639) — "be merciful as your Father is merciful" — the divine compassion is both the source and the standard; human compassion is produced by the encounter with and command to image the divine character.

**T2.9.2** — Is the source external or internal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both, with a distinction by register. Natural compassion (maternal, visceral — Groups 1596, 333) appears to arise from within the created person, triggered externally. Commanded compassion (Luk 6:36 — Group 1639; Col 3:12 — Group 1638) is sourced from the divine model externally given and to be put on. The judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630) acknowledges that compassion is present as a natural internal source and must be overridden by external judicial command — confirming the internal origin of the compassion-impulse.

**T2.9.3** — What does the source reveal about compassion's nature?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion's dual source (natural creation + divine command) reveals it as a characteristic that bridges the created and the commanded: it is natural to the created human person (nursing mother, visceral response to suffering) but requires formation and command to be expressed consistently toward all objects (including enemies, Luk 6:36's context). This bridges the natural and the graced — compassion is not purely supernatural but is cultivated and extended through divine encounter and command.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does compassion involve movement within the inner being?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion IS a movement — it is constitutively dynamic, not static. Group 333 (splanchnizō): the verb itself names a movement — the bowels move, are stirred, convulse. This is the most movement-constituted characteristic in M05-B. Group 1595 (ra.cham): Jer 31:20 — "my heart yearns (hamah — stirs, groans) for him" — the inner movement precedes the compassion-speech. Isa 63:15 (Group 1596): "the stirring (hamon — tumult/yearning) of your inner parts and your compassion are held back from me" — the stirring and the compassion are named together, the stirring as compassion's accompanying or constitutive movement.

**T2.10.2** — What is the direction and character of this movement?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The movement is from within the person toward the sufferer — centrifugal, moving outward in response to what has been perceived. The character of the movement: visceral (bowel-deep), urgent (the father runs — Luk 15:20), and complete (leading inevitably to action — there is no arrested compassion in Group 333 that stirs but does not act). The judicial-withholding register (Group 338) evidences what happens when the movement is arrested: it must be repeatedly commanded not to flow ("your eye shall not pity" — repeated six times in Ezekiel — implying the natural movement is toward compassion and must be actively restrained).

**T2.10.3** — Is there constitutional transformation produced by the movement?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, in the character-formation register. Group 334 (sumpatheō): Heb 4:15 — Christ's sustained engagement with human suffering through incarnation produces a permanently sympathetic High Priest — the movement of entering into human suffering transformed his relational capacity. Heb 10:34 (Group 334) — the community's compassion toward those in prison, sustained under the plundering of their own property, produces a community of tested and deepened solidarity. The sustained practice of compassion-movement produces the sympathetic and tenderhearted character that is the dispositional deposit of the characteristic over time.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does compassion engage the perceptual faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, and perception is the trigger for compassion more directly than for any other characteristic in M05. Group 333 (splanchnizō, 12 verses): every instance begins with seeing — Mat 9:36: "when he saw the crowds"; Mar 6:34: "when he went ashore he saw a great crowd"; Luk 7:13: "when the Lord saw her"; Luk 15:20: "while he was still a long way off, his father saw him." The seeing precedes and occasions the compassion-movement without exception in this group. Mar 9:22 (Group 333): the father of the demoniac appeals directly to compassion: "if you can do anything, have compassion on us" — the request to see the suffering as triggering compassion. Eze 16:5 (Groups 337 and 630): "no eye pitied you" — the absence of the seeing-that-generates-compassion.

**T3.1.2** — Is perception a trigger, component, or product of compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Perception is the *trigger* — the external catalyst that initiates the compassion-sequence. But compassion in turn shapes perception: the one who has been moved to compassion repeatedly develops an increasingly attentive eye for suffering. Heb 4:15 (Group 334) — Christ who has "been tempted in every respect as we are" has a formed perceptual attunement to human weakness. The perception-compassion relationship is bidirectional over time: perception triggers compassion; compassion forms perception.

**T3.1.3** — What does this reveal about compassion and perception?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is the most perceptually-dependent inner characteristic in M05-B. It cannot arise without the perception of another's suffering; it forms the perceiver into one who looks more attentively for suffering. The judicial-withholding register (Group 338 — "your eye shall not pity") is formulated in terms of the eye because it is the perceptual faculty that must be disciplined to prevent compassion from arising. This confirms perception as compassion's gateway: controlling what one sees/attends to is the mechanism for controlling whether compassion arises.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does compassion engage the cognitive faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Minimally in the triggering phase; more significantly in the judicial-discernment phase. The splanchnizō pattern (Group 333) is pre-cognitive in its triggering — the compassion arises before deliberative reasoning. However, the judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630, 3599) requires cognitive assessment: one must evaluate whether the object of potential compassion is appropriate. 1Sa 15:9 (Group 3599): Saul's cognitive failure was not that he felt compassion but that he failed to apply the divine command to the specific case — a cognitive-evaluation failure. The distinction between appropriate and inappropriate compassion (Groups 3598 vs 3599) is a cognitive assessment.

**T3.2.2** — What is the relationship between compassion and cognition?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is primarily affective-somatic (T1.8) but requires cognitive direction to be expressed appropriately. The raw compassion-impulse is pre-cognitive; the question of whether to act on it requires cognitive assessment against divine command and situational reality. Rom 9:15 (Group 331) — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" — divine compassion as sovereign prerogative establishes that even the highest form of compassion is not a mechanical reflex but an inner disposition that involves selection and judgment.

**T3.2.3** — Where is the evidence silent on cognitive engagement?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The evidence is silent on compassion as a cognitively-generated phenomenon — there is no instance in M05-B where compassion arises through reasoning or deliberation rather than through encounter and visceral response. Cognition directs and sometimes redirects compassion; it does not generate it.

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does compassion engage the memory faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, in the appeal-to-divine-compassion register. Group 1596 (ra.cha.mim): Psa 25:6 — "remember your mercy, O Lord, and your steadfast love, for they have been from of old" — the petition appeals to God to remember his compassion as a ground for present compassionate action. Psa 79:8 — "do not remember against us our former iniquities; let your compassion come speedily to meet us" — memory and compassion in direct structural relationship: asking God not to remember sin so that compassion can flow. Neh 9:19, 27, 28, 31 (Group 1596) — the historical recitation of God's sustained compassion throughout Israel's wilderness failures: "you in your great mercies did not forsake them."

**T3.3.2** — What role does memory play in compassion's operation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Memory of prior compassion received is the ground for petitioning its renewal. The Neh 9 pattern (Group 1596) is the clearest: the liturgical recounting of God's sustained compassion through Israel's repeated failure creates the expectation and ground for appealing to that same compassion in the present. The memory of divine compassion sustains the community's hope. This is structurally parallel to M05-A's chesed-memory finding but more directly tied to petition: remembered compassion generates the confidence to appeal.

**T3.3.3** — Is there evidence of compassion creating memories or being anchored in them?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes — the recitation pattern of Group 1596 (Neh 9's extended liturgical memory) creates a corporate memory of compassion that then anchors communal identity. "You in your great mercies did not make an end of them" (Neh 9:31) — the memory of compassion sustained through failure is itself the most significant datum in Israel's self-understanding. The community is defined by the compassion it has received, not only by the faithfulness it has practised.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does compassion engage the affective faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Centrally — affect is compassion's primary faculty, more directly than any other faculty. Group 333 (splanchnizō): the bowel-convulsion is an affective-somatic event. Group 1596 (ra.cha.mim): Isa 63:15 — "the stirring (hamon — tumult/yearning) of your inner parts" names the affective turbulence that accompanies compassion. Jer 31:20 (Group 1595): "my heart yearns for him" — the inner affective movement as the companion of compassion. Luk 15:20 (Group 333, anchor): the father's running — the urgency is an affective urgency, the bodily expression of the inner affective convulsion.

**T3.4.2** — Is affect central to compassion or secondary?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Affect is more central to compassion than to any other characteristic in M05-B — more central even than it is to love in M05-A (where the command-structure gives volition equal prominence). The evidence positions compassion as the most affective characteristic in the cluster. The womb-vocabulary (ra.cha.mim) and the bowel-vocabulary (splanchnizō) are both affect-language at its most somatic, placing compassion's affective character below the level of deliberate choice and closer to biological constitution.

**T3.4.3** — What is the affective character of compassion as evidenced?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The affective character is: (1) *Convulsive* — splanchnizō names a bowel-level disturbance, not a gentle feeling. (2) *Urgent* — the father runs (Luk 15:20); the hand reaches out immediately (Mar 1:41). (3) *Sorrowful-sympathetic* — Isa 63:9 (Group 339): "in all their affliction he was afflicted" — the compassion involves co-suffering, a sharing in the emotional state of the other. (4) *Yearning* — Jer 31:20 (Group 1595): the divine heart yearns/groans — an affective longing toward the beloved that is prior to the compassion-act.

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1** — Does compassion engage the creative faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The verse evidence does not evidence creativity as a dimension of compassion's operation in M05-B. Compassion is responsive (triggered by suffering) rather than generative in the creative sense.

**T3.5.2** — Is creativity a significant dimension?

**[B — Compassion]** S — Silent on creativity as a mode of compassion's operation. The characteristic produces action (T1.5.2) but not creative action in the specific sense of new-thing-generation.

**T3.5.3** — Note silence.

**[B — Compassion]** — Silent on creativity. Compassion responds; it does not primarily generate new forms or expressions. This distinguishes it from characteristics with stronger creative dimensions.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does compassion engage the volitional faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, significantly in the judicial-withholding register specifically. Group 338 (chus — 16 verses): "your eye shall not pity" — this is a command to the will to override the natural compassion-impulse. The will must actively prevent the pity-response from occurring. Group 3599 (cha.mal — disobedient compassion, 11 verses): Saul's failure to execute the divine command (1Sa 15:3, 9, 15) — his will yielded to the compassion-impulse when it should have resisted. The volitional dimension of compassion is most visible in these cases where volition must discipline or redirect it.

**T3.6.2** — Is compassion more volitional or affective?

**[B — Compassion]** E — More affective at the triggering level; more volitional in its direction and expression. The initial compassion-movement (splanchnizō) arises pre-volitionally; the response to it (whether to act, how to act, whether to suppress it) is volitional. Luk 6:36 (Group 1639): "be merciful" is a command to the will, not only to the affections — this shows that the commanded expression of compassion requires volitional engagement.

**T3.6.3** — Is there a tension between compassion and volition?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, explicitly in Group 3599 (disobedient compassion). The tension is the primary analytical content of 1Sa 15: Saul's will yielded to the compassion-impulse (he felt for Agag and the best livestock) when the will should have executed the divine command. The tension between natural compassion (felt toward a living creature or person) and covenantal obedience (which sometimes requires overriding that compassion) is the central moral tension of the cha.mal vocabulary across both Groups 3598 and 3599.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does compassion engage the agency faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes — compassion consistently produces agential initiative. Group 333 (splanchnizō): every instance produces an act: healing (Mat 9:36ff), feeding (Mat 15:32), raising (Luk 7:13), releasing (Mat 18:27). The compassion-movement drives the agent toward the sufferer. Isa 54:7 (Group 1596, anchor): "with great compassion I will gather you" — divine compassion as the motivation of the gathering-act. Deu 30:3 (Group 1595): "the Lord your God will restore your fortunes and have mercy on you, and he will gather you again" — compassion generating the agential gathering.

**T3.7.2** — What is the character of compassion's agency?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion's agency is reactive-initiative: it is initiated by the encounter with suffering (reactive) but then takes the first agential step toward the sufferer (initiative). The father runs (Luk 15:20) — he does not wait for the son to reach him. Jesus reaches out and touches (Mar 1:41) — he crosses the ritual-boundary to make contact. The compassion-generated agency is typically characterised by crossing a barrier (social distance, ritual purity, geographic distance) to reach the sufferer. This is agential initiative in response to perceived need.

**T3.7.3** — Is there evidence of compassion producing or enabling broader human agency?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. Heb 10:34 (Group 334): "you had compassion on those in prison and joyfully accepted the plundering of your property" — the community's compassion for the imprisoned produced the agential willingness to absorb substantial personal loss. Compassion generates the agency of self-sacrifice — the willingness to bear cost for the sake of the sufferer. This is the compassion-generated agency at its most extensive: not merely crossing a social barrier but accepting material diminishment.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does compassion engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, in two distinct registers. (1) *Compassion as a moral standard*: Psa 72:13 (Group 337) — the messianic king "has pity on the weak and the needy" — compassion toward the vulnerable is a moral standard for rulers. Luk 6:36 (Group 1639, anchor): "be merciful as your Father is merciful" — divine compassion as the standard for human moral evaluation of one's own character. (2) *Compassion evaluated against justice*: Groups 338/3599 — the moral evaluation of whether a specific act of compassion is appropriate involves evaluating it against divine command. Compassion shown toward Agag (Group 3599) is morally evaluated as disobedience; compassion shown by Pharaoh's daughter toward Moses (Group 3598) is morally evaluated as right.

**T3.8.2** — Is compassion a moral standard or subject to moral evaluation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both. As *standard*: the absence of compassion is consistently a moral failure indicator — Amo 1:11 (Group 1596): Edom's sin named as "he cast off all pity." As *subject to evaluation*: Groups 3598 vs 3599 show that compassion itself must be evaluated — shown toward the right object in the right situation it is virtuous; shown toward the wrong object in violation of divine command it is sinful. Compassion is not self-justifying.

**T3.8.3** — What does this reveal about compassion's structural place?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion does not occupy the apex of the moral hierarchy (love does — M05-A, 1Cor 13:13). It is a constitutive moral virtue but one subject to moral evaluation against higher norms (divine command, justice). The judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630) is the primary evidence for compassion's subordination: when justice requires withholding compassion, compassion must yield. This subordination to justice distinguishes compassion from love, which encompasses justice.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does compassion engage the conscience?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, particularly in the failure-of-compassion register. Amo 1:11 (Group 1596): Edom's "casting off all pity" is a conscience-indictable failure — the absence of compassion is named as sin. Eze 16:5 (Groups 337/630): "no eye pitied you" in the context of the abandoned infant — the failure of compassion is a moral indictment. The conscience registers the obligation to respond with compassion to encountered suffering; its absence is moral failure.

**T3.9.2** — What is the conscience's relationship to compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The conscience functions as compassion's internal obligation-signal: it registers that the sight of suffering calls for a response, and that failing to respond is a moral failure. But the conscience also operates in the judicial-withholding register: 1Sa 15:9 (Group 3599) shows that Saul's conscience did not adequately register that compassion toward Agag was wrong in this context — a conscience-failure in the discernment of when compassion should be suppressed.

**T3.9.3** — Is there evidence of compassion forming or transforming the conscience?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The verse evidence in M05-B does not directly address compassion transforming the conscience. The conscience appears as an evaluative faculty that judges compassion, not one primarily transformed by it.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does compassion engage conscientiousness?

**[B — Compassion]** E — In the divine register, yes — God's sustained compassion through Israel's repeated failure (Group 1596 — Neh 9:19, 27, 28, 31: "in your great mercies you did not forsake them") evidences a sustained, conscientious compassion that does not exhaust itself through repeated disappointment. This is the divine compassion as sustained conscientious faithfulness to the compassion-commitment.

**T3.10.2** — Is conscientiousness a significant dimension of human compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Less directly evidenced for human compassion. Heb 10:34 (Group 334) — "you had compassion on those in prison" — the sustained compassion of the community under persecution is the closest human instance: a sustained conscientious compassion-practice over time. The eusplanchnos commanded disposition (Eph 4:32, Group 2717) implies ongoing, not merely occasional, expression — a standing inner disposition requiring conscientious maintenance.

**T3.10.3** — Note silence.

**[B — Compassion]** — The conscientiousness dimension is present but less central to M05-B than to the chesed register of M05-A. Compassion's primary character is responsive (triggered by encounter) rather than anticipatory (conscientiously maintained as a duty). This is a constitutive distinction between compassion and covenantal love.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does compassion engage the relational capacity faculty?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, and it is constitutively relational in its structure — it requires a relational pair (one who suffers, one who is moved). Group 334 (sumpatheō): Heb 4:15 — "sympathize with our weaknesses" — the relational capacity of entering into the experience of another. Group 335 (sumpathēs): 1Pe 3:8 — "have unity of mind, sympathy (sumpathēs), brotherly love, a tender heart" — sympathy as a community-relational disposition. Group 339 (chem.lah): Isa 63:9 — "in all their affliction he was afflicted" — the co-suffering dimension of compassion as the deepest relational movement: not merely responding to suffering but sharing in it.

**T3.11.2** — What is the relationship between compassion and relational capacity?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion is a specific activation of the relational capacity — the relational faculty running in the mode of responsive co-suffering rather than the mode of stable commitment (love) or shared life (fellowship). The relational capacity is the broader faculty; compassion is its activation under the specific condition of encountered suffering.

**T3.11.3** — What does compassion's relational character reveal about its inner-being significance?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion reveals that the human person is constitutively open to the suffering of others — the bowel-level stirring at the sight of another's distress is a design feature, not a defect. The relational capacity at the compassion-level names the human person as one who cannot be sealed off from others' suffering without violence to their constitutional design. The judicial-withholding (Group 338) requires effort precisely because the compassion-response is natural — overriding it demands deliberate volitional work.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — How does compassion function in the God-to-human relational interface?

**[B — Compassion]** E — This is the most extensively evidenced interface in M05-B. Groups 1594, 1595, 1596 together provide 95 verses of divine compassion directed toward human persons and Israel. The interface is characterised by: (1) *Initiative*: divine compassion is not waiting to be earned but flows from God's constitutive disposition (ra.chum — Group 1594). (2) *Restoration as the compassion-act*: Group 1595 (43 verses): Deu 30:3 — "restore your fortunes and have mercy"; Isa 14:1 — "will again choose Israel and settle them"; Jer 12:15 — "I will again have compassion"; Mic 7:19 — "he will again have compassion." The compassion-act is consistently the gathering/restoration act after judgment. (3) *Compassion-in-withdrawal as its own statement*: Group 630 (21 verses) — God's withdrawal of compassion is presented as a deliberate judicial act, not a failure of character — it is the same divine compassion operating in the mode of withheld expression for redemptive purposes.

**T4.1.2** — What characterises God's compassion in its human-directed form?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) *Inexhaustible* — Lam 3:22 (Group 1596): "his mercies never come to an end" — divine compassion has no depletion point. (2) *Asymmetric to human merit* — Rom 9:15 (Group 331, anchor): "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" — sovereign, not conditional on worthiness. (3) *Maternally intense* — Isa 49:15 (Group 1595): "even these may forget, yet I will not forget you" — the divine compassion exceeds the strongest human natural compassion. (4) *Eschatologically directed* — Isa 54:7-8 (Groups 1595-1596): the great-compassion gathering as the mode of eschatological restoration.

**T4.1.3** — What does this interface reveal about compassion's inner-being significance?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The God-to-human interface reveals that the human person's existence is grounded in divine compassion: the sustained compassion-in-the-wilderness (Neh 9, Group 1596), the compassion through exile (Isa 49, 54, Groups 1595-1596), and the compassion in the NT incarnation (Group 333 — Jesus's compassion as the enacted presence of God's compassion in embodied form) collectively establish that the human person is the persistent object of divine compassion and that divine compassion is the deepest structural ground of human existence in the fallen world.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — How does compassion function in the human-to-God relational interface?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The primary form is the *petition for divine compassion* — appealing to God's compassion as the ground of access and deliverance. Group 1596 (39 verses): Psa 51:1 — "have mercy on me, O God, according to your steadfast love; according to your abundant mercy blot out my transgressions"; Psa 119:77 — "let your mercy come to me that I may live"; Dan 9:9 — "to the Lord our God belong mercy and forgiveness, for we have rebelled against him"; Dan 2:18 (Group 1672) — "seeking mercy from the God of heaven concerning this mystery." The human-to-God direction for compassion is consistently petitionary: the human appeals to God's compassion, which is positioned as the ground of all favour, forgiveness, and deliverance.

**T4.2.2** — Is there evidence of the human expressing compassion toward God?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The verse evidence in M05-B does not evidence the human person showing compassion toward God. The human-to-God direction is consistently petitionary (appealing to God's compassion) rather than the human being moved with compassion toward God. This asymmetry is structurally significant: compassion in M05-B flows from the greater toward the lesser, from the one with power toward the one in need. God does not occupy the position of the sufferer requiring human compassion in this sub-group's evidence.

**T4.2.3** — What does this directional asymmetry reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — It reveals that compassion in M05-B is constitutively asymmetric — it flows downward in the relational hierarchy (from the one with capacity toward the one in need). This distinguishes it structurally from love (M05-A), which operates in both directions in the God-human interface (God loves humans, humans love God). The asymmetry is theologically significant: compassion requires a position differential (the compassionate one has what the sufferer lacks), while love can operate between equals.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — How does compassion function when the human person is the giver?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Three registers evidenced. (1) *Spontaneous positive compassion* (Group 3598 — cha.mal, 4 verses): Exo 2:6 — Pharaoh's daughter moved by the crying infant Moses; 1Sa 23:21 — Saul acknowledging that Ziph has shown compassion toward him. The spontaneous movement toward the vulnerable as the positive giving-form. (2) *Commanded tenderness* (Groups 334, 335, 2717): Heb 10:34 — the community showing compassion on prisoners; Eph 4:32 — the community commanded to be tenderhearted; 1Pe 3:8 — sympathy as a community virtue. The sustained, commanded form of compassion-giving within community. (3) *The disobedient form* (Group 3599 — cha.mal, 11 verses): Saul's compassion toward Agag — the example where giving compassion violates divine command.

**T4.3.2** — What is the inner-being experience of the compassion-giver?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The splanchnizō vocabulary (Group 333) provides the fullest evidence: it is a bowel-level disturbance that drives the person toward the sufferer. The experience is urgent (the father runs — Luk 15:20), physically expressed (hands touching — Mar 1:41), and complete (leading without hesitation to the responding act). Heb 10:34 (Group 334): "joyfully accepted the plundering" — compassion-giving can be accompanied by joy despite material cost, suggesting a positive inner-being experience even in self-sacrifice.

**T4.3.3** — What does compassion-giving reveal about the inner-being?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion-giving reveals the inner being as one that is not self-sealed — the person who gives compassion is one whose inner constitution absorbs something of the other's distress and is moved to address it. Isa 63:9 (Group 339): "in all their affliction he was afflicted" — the compassion-giver shares in the suffering they respond to. The giving of compassion is not a transaction from a position of unaffected superiority; it involves inner co-suffering.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — How does compassion function when the human person is the receiver?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The receiver of compassion in M05-B is consistently the vulnerable, suffering, or guilty person. Exo 2:6 (Group 3598): the infant Moses — the paradigm recipient is the completely helpless. Gen 43:30 (Group 1596): Joseph's compassion grew warm for his brother — the recipient is the one in the position of vulnerability before a powerful person (Benjamin before Joseph-as-governor). Mat 18:27 (Group 333): the servant released from debt — the recipient of compassion is the one who cannot pay.

**T4.4.2** — What is the inner-being experience of receiving compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The evidence is relatively sparse on the recipient's inner experience directly, but Mat 18:27-29 (Group 333) provides the clearest implicit evidence: the servant who received the master's compassion (debt released) went immediately to seize his fellow servant — the received compassion did not penetrate and transform his inner being. This is the parable's central point: compassion received should transform the recipient into a compassion-giver, but did not. The correct inner response to received compassion is implied: gratitude, transformation, and reciprocal compassion.

**T4.4.3** — What does compassion-receiving reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion received is not self-evidently transformative — it must be received in a way that penetrates the inner being. The Mat 18 parable (Group 333) evidences that compassion can be received externally (the debt was cancelled) without producing the corresponding inner transformation (the servant's character was unchanged). This is a critical finding: compassion's transformative potential requires the recipient's inner receptivity.

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — What are the limits and boundaries of compassion in the human-to-human interface?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 3599) defines the primary boundary: compassion must not override divine command. Deu 13:8 (Groups 338 and 3599): "your eye shall not pity him" — the case of the idolater within one's family where compassion would subvert justice. The boundary is covenantal justice: compassion that would undermine the covenant community's faithfulness must be restrained. Additionally, compassion directed toward the wrong object constitutes moral failure (Group 3599 — Saul's compassion toward Agag, costing him the kingdom).

**T4.5.2** — Is compassion bounded by other characteristics?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. (1) By *justice*: the judicial-withholding register (Group 338) explicitly places justice above compassion in certain contexts. (2) By *obedience*: Group 3599 — compassion that violates covenantal obedience must be overridden. (3) By *discernment*: Rom 9:15 (Group 331) — divine compassion is sovereign and selective; human compassion requires discernment about appropriate objects and contexts.

**T4.5.3** — Are there types of objects toward which compassion cannot properly be directed?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, from Group 3599: commanded-destruction targets (Agag and the Amalek cattle) are the explicit case where compassion toward them constitutes disobedience. The boundary is set by divine command rather than by a general principle — the object's inappropriateness for compassion is defined by God's specific instruction, not by a universal rule. This makes the boundary of compassion contextually determined rather than structurally fixed.

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**T4.6.1** — Does compassion operate in relation to spiritual beings?

**[B — Compassion]** S — The verse evidence in M05-B does not evidence compassion operating specifically in relation to angels, demons, or other spiritual beings. The characteristic operates in the human and divine-to-human registers.

**T4.6.2** — Are there implications for the spiritual dimension?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Indirectly. The divine compassion in Group 1595 operates through "the angel of his presence" (Isa 63:9, Group 339): "the angel of his presence saved them; in his love and in his pity he redeemed them" — the compassion-act is associated with angelic mediation. But this names the mechanism of divine compassion's expression rather than compassion directed toward spiritual beings.

**T4.6.3** — Note silence.

**[B — Compassion]** S — Silent on compassion in relation to spiritual beings as objects or subjects. The evidence is human-and-divine focused.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Nature of Transformation

**T5.1.1** — What transformation does compassion produce in the inner being?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Two primary transformations from the evidence. (1) *Formation of the sympathetic character*: Group 334 (sumpatheō): Heb 4:15 — Christ's sustained engagement with human weakness through incarnation produced a permanently sympathetic High Priest. The sustained practice of entering into others' suffering transforms the inner being into one constitutionally attuned to suffering. (2) *Community-enabling transformation*: Group 2717 (eusplanchnos): Eph 4:32 — "be tenderhearted, forgiving one another" — the tenderhearted disposition enables forgiveness, which is the community-sustaining act. Compassion transforms the inner being toward the capacity to absorb and forgive, rather than to insist and retaliate.

**T5.1.2** — Is the transformation instantaneous or gradual?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The splanchnizō movement (Group 333) is instantaneous in its occurrence — the bowel-convulsion is a moment, not a process. But the transformation of character toward sustained sympathetic attunement (Group 334 — sumpatheō as the result of incarnate experience) is gradual and cumulative. The moment of compassion is instantaneous; the formation of compassionate character is gradual.

**T5.1.3** — What evidence supports compassion as transformative?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Heb 4:15 (Group 334) is the primary transformative evidence: Christ's sustained experience of human temptation and weakness produced a permanently sympathetic High Priest — not merely one who can feel compassion situationally but one whose character is constitutively sympathetic. The transformation is from external compassion-response to constitutional compassionate attunement.

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — What sequence of inner states does compassion produce or require?

**[B — Compassion]** E — From the Group 333 pattern: encounter with suffering → bowel-convulsion → action toward the sufferer. This is the basic three-stage sequence. The more extended sequence evidenced in the prophetic restoration passages (Groups 1595-1596): Israel's suffering and cry → divine compassion stirred → compassion-restraint during judgment → compassion released in restoration → Israel returned. The sequence includes a phase of apparent compassion-withdrawal that is itself part of the compassion-economy (not the absence of compassion but its temporary judicial restraint).

**T5.2.2** — Is the sequence consistent?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Consistent in its core structure: encounter → stirring → response. Variable in the timing of response: Mat 18:27 (Group 333) — response is immediate (within the same encounter); Isa 54:7 (Group 1596) — "for a brief moment I deserted you, but with great compassion I will gather you" — the response is delayed by the judicial period but certain in the eschatological frame.

**T5.2.3** — Where does the sequence break?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Group 3599 (disobedient compassion, 11 verses): the sequence breaks at the response stage when the compassion-response violates divine command. Saul felt compassion toward Agag (the stirring occurred) but his response was wrong (he acted on the compassion rather than overriding it). The breaking point is the response-stage, not the stirring-stage.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — What mechanisms produce change in compassion in the inner being?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Three mechanisms evidenced. (1) *Encounter with suffering*: the primary mechanism — the sight of another's distress produces the compassion-movement (Group 333 pattern). Sustained encounters with suffering produce sustained compassion-capacity. (2) *Commanded analogy to divine character*: Luk 6:36 (Group 1639): "be merciful as your Father is merciful" — the mechanism is the commanded alignment of the human disposition with the divine model. Knowing and meditating on God's compassion (Group 1594's formula repetitions) shapes the person toward compassion through cognitive-affective formation. (3) *Shared experience*: Heb 4:15 (Group 334) — the mechanism for Christ's sympathetic capacity was shared experience of human weakness. The mechanism of compassion-formation through shared suffering applies to the community as well: Heb 10:34 — the community that has suffered together develops corporate compassion.

**T5.3.2** — Is there a role for practice or habit?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, indirectly. The commanded disposition (Group 2717 — eusplanchnos as a standing character quality; Group 335 — sumpathēs as a community virtue) implies that tenderhearted compassion is not only a triggered response but a practised and maintained inner orientation. The habit of attentiveness to others' suffering — which the command to "be tenderhearted" cultivates — is the formative practice.

**T5.3.3** — Is the mechanism primarily divine or human?

**[B — Compassion]** E — More balanced than M05-A. The triggering mechanism (encounter with suffering) is natural and human. The modelling mechanism (Luk 6:36 — "as your Father is merciful") is divinely sourced. The formation mechanism (Heb 4:15 — shared experience) is both: Christ's shared experience is the divine mechanism of forming incarnate sympathy; the community's shared suffering is the human mechanism of forming corporate compassion.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — How does compassion relate to suffering and affliction?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Suffering is the trigger, the occasion, and the shared content of compassion. Group 333 (splanchnizō): suffering encountered occasions compassion; Group 334 (sumpatheō): shared suffering is the mechanism of compassionate formation; Group 339 (chem.lah): Isa 63:9 — "in all their affliction he was afflicted" — the divine compassion involves co-suffering, not merely external response. Suffering is not merely what compassion responds to but what compassion enters into.

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering test, refine, or reveal compassion?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Suffering reveals compassion most directly. Lam 4:10 (Group 349): the catastrophe of Jerusalem's destruction revealed the collapse of maternal compassion — the most extreme suffering exposed what was present and what was absent. Lam 2:2, 17, 21 (Group 630) — the absence of divine compassion in judgment exposed the full weight of accumulated sin. Heb 10:34 (Group 334) — the community's suffering under persecution revealed genuine compassion as they supported those in prison. Suffering is compassion's revealer, not only its occasion.

**T5.4.3** — Is there evidence of compassion producing or alleviating suffering?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both. *Alleviating*: Group 333 — every compassion-act in the splanchnizō series alleviates physical or social suffering (healing, feeding, raising, restoring). Group 1595 (ra.cham — restoration) — divine compassion alleviates Israel's exile-suffering by gathering and restoring. *Producing*: Isa 63:9 (Group 339) — "in all their affliction he was afflicted" — divine compassion involves entering into and sharing in the suffering of the object, producing a form of co-suffering in the compassion-giver. Heb 10:34 (Group 334) — compassion toward prisoners produced the community's willingness to accept the plundering of their property — suffering as a product of compassion-expression.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — How does compassion relate to formation and sanctification?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Col 3:12 (Group 1638, anchor): "put on then, as God's chosen ones, holy and beloved, compassionate hearts (splanchna oiktirmou)" — compassionate hearts is the first-named virtue of the "put on" list, placed before kindness, humility, meekness, and patience. The priority placement establishes compassion as the primary formative virtue of the new humanity in Colossians. Eph 4:32 (Group 2717): "be tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you" — the compassionate-tender disposition enables forgiveness, which is the central sanctification-dynamic of the community.

**T5.5.2** — Is compassion a means of sanctification or its outcome?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both. As *means*: the practice of compassion (tenderhearted forgiveness — Eph 4:32) forms the community in the image of God and enables the common life of the sanctified community. As *outcome*: Col 3:12 — the "put on" language implies that compassionate hearts are the sanctification-goal, the target condition of the formed person, not merely a tool toward another end. The sanctified person IS compassionately hearted.

**T5.5.3** — What does the evidence reveal about compassion's role in characterological formation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Col 3:12 and Eph 4:32 together establish that compassion is the primary formative virtue of the new humanity: it is first-named in the virtue list (Col 3:12), it enables the community's most important act (forgiveness — Eph 4:32), and it images the divine character (Luk 6:36 — Group 1639). The characterologically formed person is constitutively compassionate — moved by the sight of others' suffering, tending toward them in tenderhearted response.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Does compassion have an eschatological trajectory?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes, extensively evidenced in Groups 1595 and 1596. Isa 49:13 (Group 1595): "for the Lord has comforted his people and will have compassion on his afflicted" — the eschatological comfort grounded in divine compassion. Isa 54:7-8 (Groups 1595/1596): "with great compassion I will gather you" — the eschatological gathering as the fullest expression of divine compassion. Mic 7:19 (Group 1595): "he will again have compassion on us; he will tread our iniquities underfoot and cast all our sins into the depths of the sea" — the eschatological compassion as the final resolution of both suffering and sin. Zec 1:16 (Group 1596): "I have returned to Jerusalem with mercy (ra.cha.mim)" — compassion as the mode of the eschatological divine return.

**T5.6.2** — What is compassion's eschatological form?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The eschatological form of compassion is the great gathering — "with great compassion I will gather you" (Isa 54:7, Group 1596, anchor). The eschatological compassion exceeds all preceding instances: "the mountains may depart and the hills be removed, but my steadfast love shall not depart from you, and my covenant of peace shall not be removed" (Isa 54:10). Compassion's eschatological form is permanent and irrevocable — the "brief moment" of desertion is contrasted with the "everlasting love" of the gathering.

**T5.6.3** — What does compassion's eschatological permanence reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion's eschatological permanence (Lam 3:22 — "his mercies never come to an end"; Isa 54:10 — irrevocable) reveals it as an eternal dimension of the God-human relationship, not merely a crisis-response to the fallen condition. Even in the new creation where the conditions that trigger compassion (suffering, exile, lostness) are resolved, the divine compassion remains as the eternal mode of God's relational engagement — now in fullness rather than in the mode of restoration from a fallen state.

### T5.7 — Deposit Consequence

**T5.7.1** — What does compassion deposit in the inner being over time?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) *Sympathetic attunement*: the sustained practice of compassion deposits an increasingly attentive sensitivity to others' suffering — the eusplanchnos disposition (Eph 4:32, Group 2717) as the standing character quality formed through compassion-practice. (2) *Hope grounded in divine compassion's reliability*: the sustained appeal to God's compassion (Group 1596 — Psa 51:1, 119:77, 156; Neh 9 series; Dan 9:9, 18) deposits the inner orientation of trust in divine compassion as reliable — "his mercies never come to an end" (Lam 3:22, Group 1596). (3) *Community solidarity*: Heb 10:34 (Group 334) — the community that showed compassion under persecution deposited a bond of solidarity through shared suffering.

**T5.7.2** — Is there a negative deposit from compassion's absence or misdirection?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Yes. Group 3599 (disobedient compassion): Saul's compassion toward Agag deposited the loss of the kingdom — "because you have rejected the word of the Lord, he has also rejected you from being king" (1Sa 15:23). Misdirected compassion (toward forbidden objects) deposits spiritual consequences. Lam 4:10 (Group 349): the collapse of maternal compassion under Jerusalem's siege deposited the most extreme form of social breakdown — the community defined by mutual compassion becomes one consuming itself.

**T5.7.3** — What does the deposit analysis reveal?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The deposit analysis reveals that compassion's consequences accumulate in both directions: practised rightly, it deposits sympathetic attunement, hope, and community solidarity; misdirected or absent, it deposits spiritual judgment and social collapse. Compassion is not a safe virtue — it is capable of generating both the deepest human solidarity and, when misdirected, the most serious moral failure (Group 3599 — compassion as the mechanism of Saul's downfall).

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**T6.1.1** — What characteristics most frequently co-occur with compassion in the verse evidence?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The most consistent co-occurrences within M05-B's verse base: (1) *Steadfast love (chesed)*: Group 1594 — Exo 34:6: "ra.chum... abounding in steadfast love (chesed)" — the divine self-disclosure formula pairs compassion and chesed. Psa 103:8, Psa 145:8, Joe 2:13, Jon 4:2 all repeat this pairing. (2) *Grace (chanun/charis)*: the same Exo 34:6 formula pairs ra.chum with chanun (gracious) — compassion and grace as twin attributes. (3) *Forgiveness (salach/aphiēmi)*: Group 1595 — Pro 28:13: "he who confesses and forsakes them will obtain mercy (ra.cham)"; Isa 55:7: "he will have compassion... for he will abundantly pardon." (4) *Faithfulness (emet)*: Group 1596 — Isa 63:7: "his steadfast love and his compassion... his great goodness and faithfulness to the house of Israel." (5) *Comfort (nacham)*: Isa 49:13 (Group 1595): "the Lord has comforted his people and will have compassion" — compassion and comfort co-occur consistently in the restoration texts.

**T6.1.2** — What does this co-occurrence pattern reveal?

### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**T6.2.1** — Does compassion follow other characteristics in a sequence, or precede them?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Both, with context-dependence. Compassion *follows* suffering — it is always a response to an antecedent condition of need. Compassion *precedes* forgiveness and restoration in the prophetic sequence: Isa 55:7 (Group 1595) — "he will have compassion on him... for he will abundantly pardon" — compassion is the prior movement from which forgiveness proceeds. Compassion *precedes* comfort in the restoration texts: Isa 49:13 (Group 1595) — compassion leads to the comforting acts. Compassion *follows* love (M05-A) in the structural hierarchy: love (chesed) is the permanent prior disposition; compassion (ra.cham) is the specific movement triggered by suffering within that loving relationship.

**T6.2.2** — What sequences are most consistently evidenced?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) Suffering/need → compassion → action/restoration (Groups 333, 1595-1596 — the basic trigger-response sequence). (2) Repeated sin → suspended compassion → repentance → renewed compassion → restoration (the Neh 9 and prophetic sequence — Group 1596). (3) Encounter with suffering → compassion → forgiveness/pardon (Group 1595 — Isa 55:7, Pro 28:13). The most consistent sequence: compassion operates *between* the conditions of need and the acts of relief/restoration.

**T6.2.3** — Does compassion occupy a specific position in a hierarchy of characteristics?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Compassion does not occupy the apex (love does — M05-A). Within M05, compassion appears positioned immediately under love: love is the constitutive relational orientation; compassion is the specific activation of that love in the face of suffering. Col 3:12 (Group 1638) places compassionate hearts first in the virtue list, suggesting a functional priority in the relational economy without claiming ontological supremacy. In the divine self-disclosure formula (Exo 34:6), ra.chum (compassionate) precedes even chesed (steadfast love) in the ordering — but this is likely rhetorical primacy (what is felt first) rather than ontological priority.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — What does the lexical range reveal about compassion's breadth and depth?

**[B — Compassion]** E — The 15-term vocabulary of M05-B reveals a characteristic with unusually strong somatic grounding. The primary Hebrew root ra.cham (verb, Group 1595), ra.chum (adjective, Group 1594), and ra.cha.mim (plural noun, Group 1596) derive from the womb (rechem — H7358), creating a biological-constitutional anchor for the characteristic. The lexical form confirms that ra.cha.ma.ni names a constitutional character-quality (these women are "the compassionate ones" by nature) while the context names the catastrophic collapse of that quality under extreme duress.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — What interpretive challenges does the verse evidence present?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Three primary challenges. (1) *Group 338 (judicial withholding — 16 verses)*: the dominant Ezekiel instances ("my eye will not spare, nor will I have pity" — repeated at 5:11, 7:4, 7:9, 8:18, 9:5, 9:10, 24:14) require careful literary-contextual reading: this is divine judgment speech addressed to Israel at the point of maximum covenantal violation. The interpreter must read these not as negations of divine compassion but as expressions of its judicial mode — compassion present as the thing being withheld, not as the thing absent from God's character. (2) *Group 3599 (disobedient compassion — 11 verses)*: the compassion Saul showed toward Agag (1Sa 15:9) is described using the same cha.mal vocabulary as genuine compassion (Group 3598). The literary challenge is to distinguish disobedient compassion (morally culpable) from genuine compassion (morally praiseworthy) when the vocabulary is identical. The distinction is contextual (divine command was given; the object was a condemned enemy), not lexical. (3) *Mat 18:27 (Group 333)*: the splanchnizō of the master in the parable — the only compassion-instance in Group 333 that is not Jesus's own compassion but a character's in a parable. The interpretive question is whether the parabolic compassion evidences the same inner-being characteristic as the narrative instances, or whether it is primarily functional (illustrating divine forgiveness).

**T7.2.2** — What literary features most significantly affect interpretation?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) *The prophetic judgment-speech frame* (Groups 338, 630): the repeated "my eye will not spare" formula in Ezekiel is divine judgment-oracle, not neutral description. The literary frame (oracle-of-judgment) is essential to reading these verses correctly — they are not characterisations of God's general disposition but declarations of a specific judicial action at a specific historical moment. (2) *The parabolic frame* (Mat 18:27, Group 333): the compassion of the master in the unforgiving-servant parable is a narrative device, not a direct divine-compassion-event. But the parable uses splanchnizō for a reason — it names the quality that distinguishes the master's initial response and whose absence in the servant is the moral failure. (3) *The lament-question frame* (Psa 77:9, Group 1596 — "has he in anger shut up his compassion?"): the question is not a theological proposition but a cry from the pit — the interpreter must read it as expressing the inner experience of apparent compassion-withdrawal, not as asserting that God's compassion has actually ceased.

**T7.2.3** — Where does interpretation require caution?

**[B — Compassion]** S — Caution is required at the judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630): these must not be read as evidence that God lacks compassion but as evidence that compassion can be and is appropriately withheld for judicial purposes. The caution is against reading the suspension of compassion as its absence from God's character. The evidence for permanent divine compassion (Groups 1594-1596) must be held alongside the judicial-suspension evidence (Groups 338, 630) without resolving the tension by suppressing either.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Where does the verse evidence engage, confirm, or challenge human science frameworks?

**[B — Compassion]** E — Three primary engagements. (1) *Confirming the somatic basis of compassion*: psychological and neuroscientific research on empathy consistently identifies somatic markers (vagal activation, physiological resonance with others' distress) as central to compassionate response. The splanchnizō/ra.cha.mim vocabulary independently confirms this — Scripture identifies the same somatic-visceral register as compassion's constitutional home. The verse evidence and human science converge. (2) *Challenging purely empathy-based accounts*: human science frameworks that define compassion primarily as emotional contagion or shared feeling are challenged by the judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630, 3599). Compassion must sometimes be deliberately suppressed — this shows it is not a fully automatic or uncontrollable response but is subject to volitional direction. The judicial dimension exceeds most human-science accounts. (3) *Challenging frameworks that equate compassion with passivity*: Group 333's consistent pattern — compassion → immediate action — challenges psychological frameworks that treat compassion as primarily an internal state. The verse evidence makes compassion inherently active-generative: it always produces response.

**T7.3.2** — What does the verse evidence reveal that human science frameworks miss?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) *The sovereign dimension of compassion*: Rom 9:15 (Group 331, anchor) — "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy" — divine compassion as sovereign prerogative, not a conditioned reflex to all suffering. Human science frameworks that treat compassion as a universal automatic response to perceived suffering cannot account for the selective, sovereign character of divine compassion. (2) *Compassion in the judicial economy*: the Groups 338/630/3599 evidence — compassion appropriate, inappropriate, and deliberately withheld — exceeds any framework that treats compassion as uniformly virtuous. Scripture evidences a moral economy in which compassion must be subjected to higher norms. (3) *The womb-root constitutional depth*: the ra.cha.mim vocabulary places compassion at a constitutional depth (womb-level) that human science approximates but does not fully account for — the divine compassion that "cannot forget" (Isa 49:15) operates at a depth that exceeds biological explanation.

**T7.3.3** — What human science frameworks are most useful for illuminating the evidence?

**[B — Compassion]** E — (1) *Neuroscience of empathy* — useful for understanding the somatic-visceral basis of compassion (splanchnizō, ra.cha.mim) without being imposed as the explanation. The convergence between contemporary affective neuroscience and the bowel/womb vocabulary is notable and illuminating. (2) *Psychology of compassion fatigue* — useful for understanding why the judicial-withholding register (Groups 338, 630) is coherent: compassion can be depleted and requires appropriate limits. But the divine compassion (ra.cha.mim — "never come to an end") cannot be subject to fatigue, which marks the limit of this framework. (3) *Attachment theory's parental-bond model* — useful for understanding the womb-root and the nursing-mother analogy (Isa 49:15, Group 1595) as the constitutional ground of the strongest human compassion. The biological bond between parent and infant is the most direct human analogue to the constitutional depth of divine ra.cha.mim.
