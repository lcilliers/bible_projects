## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — Three explicit divine attributions of comfort emerge from the evidence. (1) God as the comforter: "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742); "as one whom his mother comforts, so I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742). The mother-comfort image is the most intimate divine characterisation in the comfort vocabulary — God identifies himself with the most fundamental form of comfort the human person knows. (2) God as "Father of mercies and God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3 — Group 3073) — naming comfort as a defining divine attribute. (3) God who is "moved to pity by their groaning" (Judg 2:18 — Group 1742) and who "has compassion on his servants" (Deu 32:36 — Group 1742). The character of God revealed: one who actively moves toward the distressed with the specific intent to relieve and restore — not passively available but initiatorily present.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?

**E** — Yes, explicitly and repeatedly. The divine self-identification "I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742) is the strongest attribution in M05 — God names his comforting as an aspect of his self-identity. "God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3 — Group 3073) makes comfort a divine title. The attribution reveals that comfort is not remedial or reluctant but constitutive of God's character — he does not merely comfort when required; he is the comforter. For the human person, this means: the human comfort-act images a divine way of being, not merely a divine response. The person who comforts embodies something of what God is.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, note this explicitly.

**S** — Not applicable — divine possession of this characteristic is extensively evidenced. No silence.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**E** — The most explicit purpose statement is 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602): "who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction." The purpose of receiving comfort is the capacity to give it — the comforted person becomes a comforter. The characteristic equips the person to be a relational presence that relieves distress in others. The cascading structure of 2Cor 1:3-7 (Groups 602, 3073) names this explicitly: comfort flows from God through the suffering comforter into those who suffer — the person serves as a channel and embodiment of comfort in the human community.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**P** — The evidence does not directly address the original creation question. The Hebrew na.cham corpus appears overwhelmingly in grief and distress contexts — which are post-fall realities. However, the divine name "God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3 — Group 3073) may imply that divine comfort is constitutive of God's nature prior to and independent of the fall, with its human expression arising wherever distress is present. The la.vav term (Song 4:9 — Group 2741) — the captivating of the heart in the Song of Songs — may suggest a pre-fall dimension: the captivation and stirring of the heart through intimate encounter is not remedial but constitutive of intimate relationship. The evidence leans toward: primarily a response to the fallen condition in its dominant na.cham register, but with an original-design dimension in the intimate-heart-stirring register of la.vav.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**E** — Yes. "Eternal comfort and good hope through grace" (2Th 2:16 — Group 3074) names comfort with an eternal qualifier — the characteristic has an eschatological extent beyond the present. "As one whom his mother comforts, so I will comfort you; you shall be comforted in Jerusalem" (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742) places the comforting within the eschatological restoration of Jerusalem. "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted" (Mat 5:4 — Group 602) is a beatitude pointing toward eschatological consolation: the present mourning is oriented toward a future comfort.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image?

**E** — The divine-image expression of this characteristic is the person who becomes a comforter — one who relieves the distress of another. The human comforter images the divine comforter. 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) makes the structure explicit: God comforts → the person receives comfort → the person comforts others. The cascading of comfort — from divine to human and from human to human — is the image-bearing structure: the person participates in and extends what God is doing, becoming an image of the comforting God in the human community.

**T0.3.2** — Shared between God and the human, or exclusively creaturely?

**E** — Genuinely shared. Na.cham and parakaleō are applied to both God and human beings. Isaac was comforted after his mother's death (Gen 24:67 — Group 1741); God comforts Zion (Isa 51:3 — Group 1742). Paul comforts the afflicted (2Cor 7:6 — Group 602); God comforts the downcast (2Cor 7:6 — Group 602). The same Hebrew and Greek roots are used for both divine and human instances — the sharing is lexically and semantically real.

**T0.3.3** — What does presence or absence reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**E** — The absence of any comforter is presented as a condition of profound desolation: Lamentations names it repeatedly — "she has none to comfort her" (Lam 1:2, 1:9, 1:16, 1:17, 1:21 — Group 1741). The Lamentations pattern reveals that the comforter-function is so constitutive of human community life that its absence marks the total breakdown of communal care. Ecc 4:1 (Group 1741): "and they had no one to comfort them!" — the tears of the oppressed without comfort is the nadir of human social failure. The presence of comforters is, conversely, evidence of a community that still carries the image of the comforting God.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**E** — Yes. The most significant typological use is the Paraclēte: the Holy Spirit as "the Paraclete" (Comforter/Advocate) in the Johannine tradition draws on the parakaleō/paraklēsis vocabulary of comfort/calling-alongside. The Spirit who is the divine Comforter continues and exceeds the comfort that Jesus's physical presence provided. The Lamentations "no comforter" condition is resolved eschatologically by the divine Comforter's coming. Additionally, Isa 66:13 ("as one whom his mother comforts") uses the human mother-comfort as a typological pointer to the divine comfort that exceeds it — the human instance points beyond itself to the divine.

**T0.4.2** — Direction of the typology?

**E** — Both directions are present, but the primary direction is divine-to-human: the divine comfort is the original (God is the comforter; the Spirit is the Paraclēte); the human instance is derivative and participatory. The mother-comfort typology in Isa 66:13 (Group 1742) runs human-to-divine (the human mother-comfort is the image used to point toward the divine) — this is a rare upward-pointing typology, from human experience to divine reality.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use, note explicitly.

Not applicable — typological use is evidenced.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called — and what does the name signal?

**E** — "Comfort and Encouragement." The compound name covers two registers: comfort (the relief of distress and grief — na.cham, paraklēsis/parakaleō in comfort sense) and encouragement (the strengthening and calling-forward of the person — paraklēsis/parakaleō in encouragement sense, tan.chum). The compound name is apt because the same vocabulary (parakaleō, paraklēsis) spans both registers. The name signals a characteristic that is directionally oriented toward the distressed or faltering person — it is always relational in structure, always addressed to someone in a condition of need.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary terms reveal at the definitional level?

**E** — H5162G na.cham: root carries the sense of breathing deeply, sighing with emotion — the inward movement of relief or regret. The verb is used for human comforting, divine relenting, and the state of being comforted. H8575 tan.chum: consolation as gift — the received form of comfort, consolations cheering the soul. H3823A la.vav: the verb for captivating/encouraging the heart — the inner stirring through intimate presence. G3870 parakaleō: to call alongside — the one who comes to stand beside and address the person. G3874 paraklēsis: the act or experience of being called alongside — encouragement, exhortation, comfort as an inner experience.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**E** — Both directional and relational. The characteristic is always directionally toward the distressed person — it is not abstract but addressed. Parakaleō means literally to call alongside — the person moves to be present with another. The relational implication is constitutive: comfort and encouragement cannot exist in isolation; they always have a giver and a receiver in a relational context.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is this characteristic?

**E** — A compound of inner orientation and inner experience. On the giver's side: the inner orientation of directed compassionate presence toward the distressed (na.cham group 1741; parakaleō in comfort register — Group 602). On the receiver's side: the inner experience of relief, consolation, and strengthening (Group 3073 — "God of all comfort who comforts us in all our affliction"). Both sides are evidenced: the characteristic is simultaneously what the comforter does from the inside out, and what the comforted person experiences from the outside in.

**T1.2.2** — Simple or compound structure?

**E** — Compound. The 11-group structure reveals several distinct registers: (1) human comfort in grief (Group 1741 — na.cham in grief contexts); (2) divine comfort and compassion-action (Group 1742 — na.cham in divine pity/comfort contexts); (3) heart-stirring through intimate presence (Group 2741 — la.vav); (4) consolation as God's sustaining gift (Group 3079 — tan.chum); (5) apostolic comfort in affliction (Group 602 — parakaleō comfort register); (6) urgent plea from inner need (Group 603 — parakaleō plea register); (7) pastoral/apostolic appeal toward faithful conduct (Group 604-a — parakaleō appeal register); (8) social requests and urgings (Group 604-b — parakaleō social register); (9) comfort as God's gift in affliction (Group 3073 — paraklēsis comfort); (10) encouragement toward hope (Group 3074 — paraklēsis hope register); (11) mutual community encouragement (Group 3075 — paraklēsis community register). The parakaleō vocabulary is the most semantically wide in M05.

**T1.2.3** — Best working description?

**E** — Comfort and encouragement is the inner orientation directed specifically toward the distressed, grieving, or faltering person — bringing relief to inner grief (na.cham — Group 1741), producing inner pity-movement toward the suffering (Group 1742), stirring the heart through intimate presence (la.vav — Group 2741), giving consolation as a sustained gift (tan.chum — Group 3079), and calling alongside with presence, word, and strength (parakaleō/paraklēsis — Groups 602–3075). It is constitutively relational and always responsive to distress or need. Its divine form is the active, naming self-identification of God as comforter (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742); its human form is the cascading of received comfort outward toward others (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602).

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite?

**E** — The most explicit structural opposite is the failed comforter and the absent comforter. Job 16:2 (Group 1741): "miserable comforters are you all" — the failed comforter is one present in form but absent in substance. Lam 1:2, 1:9, 1:16, 1:17, 1:21 (Group 1741): the repeated "no comforter" — the total absence of the comforting presence. Zec 10:2 (Group 1741): "false dreams and give empty consolation" — comfort that is deceptive and empty. The structural opposite is therefore twofold: (1) total absence of comfort; (2) false/empty comfort that promises relief but delivers none.

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**E** — Empty comfort (Job 21:34 — Group 1741: "how then will you comfort me with empty nothings?"), deceptive consolation (Zec 10:2 — Group 1741), and the comfort of sin ("Esau comforts himself by planning to kill you" — Gen 27:42 — Group 1741 — the morally inverted comfort that plans evil). The characteristic in its genuine form excludes manipulation, deception, and comfort that serves the giver's purposes rather than the receiver's relief.

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not?

**E** — The Group 604-b split (social requests — Act 16:15, 19:31 — parakaleō in social-request register) confirms that not all parakaleō is comfort — the ordinary social request is a use of the same vocabulary but not the characteristic's core content. The characteristic is also not the same as compassion (M05-B): compassion is the inner movement triggered by suffering, producing action; comfort is the inner orientation directed toward the distressed person with the specific intent to relieve. Compassion is the trigger; comfort is the specific response-form. The Group 603 plea-register (urgent appeal from inner need) is a different phenomenon from comfort proper: it is the cry of the one in need, not the comfort of the one responding.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate?

**E** — Five modes are evidenced: (1) **Grief-relief**: bringing relief to acute inner grief through presence and words (na.cham — Group 1741; 2Cor 7:6 — Group 602); (2) **Heart-captivation**: stirring and moving the inner person through intimate encounter (la.vav — Group 2741; Song 4:9); (3) **Consoling sustenance**: the repeated sustaining consolation that addresses accumulated anxiety (tan.chum — Psa 94:19 — Group 3079); (4) **Pastoral calling-alongside**: the apostolic and pastoral presence that calls another toward faithful living (Group 604-a; 2Cor 1:3-7 — Group 3073); (5) **Community building**: the mutual encouragement of believers that refreshes hearts (Group 3075; Phile 7 — Group 3075).

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context?

**E** — Yes, markedly. In acute grief contexts (na.cham — Group 1741), the characteristic is a crisis-response — presence brought to the bereaved, the oppressed, the devastated. In the apostolic context (2Cor 1:3-7 — Group 3073), comfort flows through shared suffering — the comforter's own affliction is the credential for comforting others. In the pastoral-exhortation context (Group 604-a), the characteristic operates as authoritative calling-forward — urging toward faithful living. In the community context (Group 3075), it operates as the mutual refreshment of shared love. The core orientation is the same (moving toward the distressed or faltering person) but the mode of delivery varies substantially by context.

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?

**E** — Yes, prominently. Na.cham in the human comfort contexts (Group 1741) is frequently expressed through words — comfort spoken to the bereaved (Gen 50:21 — "he comforted them and spoke kindly to them"), pastoral speech (Job 29:25 — "like one who comforts mourners"). The parakaleō family is constitutively speech-based: "called alongside" is verbal in its primary mode. 1Cor 14:3 (Group 3075): "the one who prophesies speaks to people for their upbuilding and encouragement and consolation" — prophecy as a comfort-speech mode. Rom 15:4 (Group 3074): "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures" — Scripture as comfort-speech. The communicative mode is central for this characteristic in a way that distinguishes it from compassion (M05-B) and gentleness (M05-E), whose communicative modes are secondary.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first immediate inner response to receiving this characteristic?

**E** — Relief of grief and restoration of inner capacity. Gen 38:12 (Group 1741): Judah was "comforted" after his wife's death and was then able to proceed with life. 2Sa 13:39 (Group 1741): "the spirit of the king longed to go out to Absalom, because he was comforted about Amnon, since he was dead" — the comfort over one grief releases the person to move toward another relationship. Psa 94:19 (Group 3079): "your consolations cheer my soul" — the immediate response to tan.chum is cheer/gladness in the soul. Phile 7 (Group 3075): "the hearts of the saints have been refreshed through you" — heart-refreshment is the immediate response.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent?

**E** — Consistent in direction but varying in intensity. The direction is always: distress/grief → encounter with comfort → inner relief, gladness, or refreshment. The variation is in degree: the comfort of Psa 23:4 (Group 1741) produces the displacement of fear; the comfort of 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) produces the capacity to comfort others; the consolations of Psa 94:19 (Group 3079) produce cheer in the soul. All consistently move the person from inner distress toward inner relief, even where the distress remains (as in Job's comforters — where the comfort failed).

**T1.5.3** — Where the evidence is silent on immediate response, note explicitly.

**S** — The inner response to the parakaleō plea register (Group 603 — the cry from inner need directed toward Jesus) is the healing and mercy received — but this is the response of Jesus to the plea, not the inner response of the petitioner to receiving the characteristic. This ambiguity in Group 603 is noted; Group 603 is better understood as the seeking of comfort (the cry) than the experience of comfort received.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**E** — Three sustained effects are evidenced. (1) **The capacity to comfort others**: 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) — "so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction." The sustained reception of comfort produces the comforter-capacity. (2) **Hope**: Rom 15:4 (Group 3074) — "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures we might have hope." The sustained reception of scriptural encouragement produces hope as a stable inner orientation. (3) **Heart-refreshment across time**: Psa 94:19 (Group 3079) — "when the cares of my heart are many, your consolations cheer my soul" — the repeated sustaining of consolation against accumulated anxiety is a time-extended effect.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, or capacities does sustained exposure establish?

**E** — Endurance: Rom 15:4 (Group 3074) pairs "endurance" directly with "encouragement of the Scriptures." The sustained encouragement produces the capacity to endure over time. Hope: same verse. Capacity for mutual building-up: 1Th 5:11 (Group 604-a) — "encourage one another and build one another up" — the mutual encouragement produces a community with greater capacity for self-sustaining formation.

**T1.6.3** — Does sustained effect differ from immediate response?

**E** — Yes clearly. The immediate response is relief of acute distress and momentary cheer. The sustained effect is the stable capacity of the person to endure, hope, comfort others, and build community. The immediate is experiential and affective; the sustained is constitutional and relational.

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions enable genuine reception?

**E** — Mourning is the enabling condition: Mat 5:4 (Group 602) — "blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted." The one who mourns genuinely is the one positioned to receive comfort. The honest acknowledgement of inner grief — refusing to suppress or manage it — is what opens the person to receive the comfort directed toward them. Jacob's refusing comfort (Gen 37:35 — Group 1741) names the failed-reception condition: the grief is real but the refusal is also real — the person can be in distress and refuse comfort simultaneously.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block or distort reception?

**E** — Refusal of comfort: Gen 37:35 (Group 1741) — Jacob "refused to be comforted"; Psa 77:2 (Group 1741) — "my soul refuses to be comforted." The refusal is an inner act — not merely the absence of comfort but the active turning away from it. The false comfort context (Job 21:34 — Group 1741; Zec 10:2 — Group 1741) names a different blocking condition: when comfort is false/empty, genuine reception is not possible regardless of the recipient's openness.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner state of the non-receiver?

**E** — Sustained grief and desolation: Lam 1:16 (Group 1741) — "a comforter is far from me, one to revive my spirit; my children are desolate." The person who does not receive comfort remains in the un-revived state — spirit unreached, inner desolation unrelieved. The psalmist's refusal (Psa 77:2 — Group 1741) produces a state of seeking the Lord in prolonged night — the soul stretched out without comfort is the inner state. The Lamentations image of the comfortless city is the community version of this individual desolation.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — Primary dimension?

**E** — Relational-affective: the characteristic is constitutively relational (always between persons in a relational context) and engages the affective faculty primarily (grief, relief, soul-cheer, heart-refreshment). The affective engagement is the most prominent faculty in the evidence — this is the most explicitly soul-and-affect-level characteristic in M05.

**T1.8.2** — Evidence for classification?

**E** — Psa 94:19 (Group 3079): "your consolations cheer my soul" — soul-level affective state. Phile 7 (Group 3075): "hearts refreshed." Lam 1:16 (Group 1741): "a comforter... to revive my spirit." 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602): "comforts us in all our affliction" — affliction is an inner state, comfort addresses it. All primary anchors name the affective-inner dimension.

**T1.8.3** — Secondary dimensions?

**E** — (1) Volitional: the giving of comfort is a deliberate act — "I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742). (2) Communal: the mutual-encouragement mode (Group 3075) makes the characteristic constitutive of community life. (3) Eschatological: "eternal comfort and good hope" (2Th 2:16 — Group 3074); Mat 5:4 (Group 602) — future-oriented. The affective dimension is primary; the volitional, communal, and eschatological are secondary.

The evidence suggests spirit-level address without explicitly naming the spirit as the constitutional location of the characteristic itself.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — Constitutional origin?

**E** — God is the primary named source: "God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3 — Group 3073); "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742); "eternal comfort... through grace" (2Th 2:16 — Group 3074). The Scripture is a secondary source: "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures" (Rom 15:4 — Group 3074). The suffering comforter is a tertiary source: one who has been comforted in affliction is equipped to comfort others (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602). The originating source is God; the mediated sources are Scripture, the Spirit, and the comforting presence of another person.

**T2.9.2** — Singular or multiple?

**E** — Singular at source (God), multiple in mediation. God comforts through: his own direct initiative (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742), through the Spirit (Act 9:31 context), through Scripture (Rom 15:4 — Group 3074), through human comforters (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602), through the apostolic word (Group 604-a).

**T2.9.3** — Origin change across contexts?

**E** — The mediation path varies: OT divine comfort comes directly through divine action and prophetic word (Groups 1742, 3079); NT comfort comes through the Spirit, Scripture, apostolic presence, and community (Groups 3073–3075, 604-a). The mechanism language differs across testaments but the ultimate source is consistently God.

---

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels?

**E** — Yes, in two directions. (1) Downward/inward: God's comfort reaches from divine initiative into the soul (Psa 94:19 — Group 3079) and heart (Phile 7 — Group 3075) of the person. (2) Outward: received comfort moves from the person who has received it outward to others who are distressed (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602). This is the cascading structure of 2Cor 1:3-7: God comforts → Paul receives → Paul comforts the Corinthians → the Corinthians comfort others.

**T2.10.2** — What does the sequence reveal?

**E** — The cascading structure of 2Cor 1:3-7 (Groups 602, 3073) is the most analytically significant finding for constitutional movement: comfort flows not merely from God to person but through the person to others. The comforter is a conduit as well as a recipient — the characteristic is constitutively participatory. This distinguishes comfort from other M05 characteristics: the person who has been loved does not automatically become a channel of love in the same transparent way; the person who has been comforted in affliction specifically becomes the qualified comforter of others in affliction.

**T2.10.3** — If no movement, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Perception engagement?

**E** — Yes. The human comfort in grief contexts (Group 1741) is consistently triggered by the perception of another's distress: "when Job's three friends heard of all this evil that had come upon him... they came... to comfort him" (Job 2:11 — Group 1741). Hearing (auditory perception) of the other's suffering triggers the movement toward them. Judg 2:18 (Group 1742): "the Lord was moved to pity by their groaning" — auditory perception of the cry triggers divine comfort-movement. Perception of the other's inner state is the trigger for the comfort characteristic.

**T3.1.2** — Does it enable, deepen, bypass, or impair perception?

**E** — Deepens perceptive sensitivity: the person who has been comforted in affliction (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602) is equipped to "be able to comfort those who are in any affliction." The shared experience of affliction sharpens the comforter's perception of others' distress — suffering received becomes the credential for perceiving and addressing others' suffering.

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — The perception of another's distress is the triggering condition for the characteristic, parallel to M05-B (compassion). Both are triggered by perception of suffering. The distinction: compassion is triggered by sight of suffering and produces a visceral inner movement; comfort is triggered by hearing/perception of distress and produces a directional movement toward the person with the specific intent to relieve. Comfort is more relational-verbal in its expression; compassion is more somatic-visceral.

---

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Cognition engagement?

**E** — Yes, in the encouragement registers. Rom 15:4 (Group 3074): "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures we might have hope" — the Scriptures are engaged cognitively, and their encouragement operates through cognitive engagement with their content. The apostolic exhortation register (Group 604-a): "I appeal to you therefore, brothers, by the mercies of God" (Rom 12:1) — a cognitive argument is the vehicle of the encouragement. The encouragement-toward-hope mode requires the person to understand and internalise the grounds for hope.

**T3.2.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Enables and focuses. Scripture-encouragement (Group 3074) focuses the cognitive faculty on the grounds for hope — endurance and scriptural encouragement produce hope precisely because the mind is directed toward the content of what Scripture promises. The pastoral appeal (Group 604-a) similarly requires cognitive engagement with the theological argument being made.

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — The encouragement register of this characteristic is more cognitively engaged than the comfort register. Comfort (in grief) operates primarily at the affective and relational level; encouragement (toward hope and faithful living) requires and deepens cognitive engagement. This is a significant internal distinction within the sub-group.

---

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Memory engagement?

**E** — Yes, partially. Psa 119:52 (Group 1741): "When I think of your rules from of old, I take comfort, O Lord" — the act of remembering God's past ways produces comfort in the present distress. Memory is the mechanism: comfort arises through the retrieval of past evidence of God's faithfulness. The God who "remembered his steadfast love" (in the broader cluster context) and the psalmist who "thinks of your rules from of old" both evidence memory as a comfort-enabling faculty.

**T3.3.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Enables comfort: memory of God's past faithfulness is what makes the comfort-act possible for the psalmist. The person without memory of God's past faithfulness has nothing to draw on for comfort in the present.

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Memory is a support faculty for this characteristic — it enables comfort through the retrieval of past faithfulness rather than being directly constitutive of the characteristic. The comfort is not the memory itself but what the memory makes accessible: the ground for hope and trust in the present distress.

---

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Affect engagement?

**E** — Yes, centrally. Psa 94:19 (Group 3079): "your consolations cheer my soul" — cheer/gladness is an affective state produced by tan.chum. Phile 7 (Group 3075): "I have derived much joy and comfort from your love" — joy as affective response to comfort received. Gen 37:35 (Group 1741): Jacob's refusal to be comforted demonstrates that grief is an affective state that comfort seeks to address. The entire na.cham corpus in Group 1741 is situated within grief — the primary affective context. 2Cor 7:6 (Group 602): "God who comforts the downcast" — the downcast inner state is affective in character.

**T3.4.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Transforms affect: comfort moves the person from grief/distress toward relief/cheer. It does not bypass affect but addresses and transforms it. The failed comforter (Job 16:2 — Group 1741) fails precisely in the affective dimension — the words exist but the affective relief does not occur. Genuine comfort produces affective transformation; false comfort produces affective frustration.

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Affect is the most central faculty for this characteristic. The characteristic is constituted by addressing and transforming the inner affective state from grief/distress toward relief, cheer, and hope. This makes M05-F more explicitly affect-engaged than M05-D (goodness), M05-E (gentleness), and even M05-A (love) in its primary operation — though not more than M05-B (compassion) whose visceral somatic-affective engagement is even more primary.

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1-3** — Creativity engagement.

**S** — No evidence of creativity engagement for M05-F. Silence noted.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Volition engagement?

**E** — Yes. The giving of comfort is a volitional act: "I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742) — God's determination to comfort is expressed volitionally. Human comfort is similarly chosen: Job 29:25 (Group 1741) — "I chose their way and sat as chief... like one who comforts mourners" — the comfort-act is chosen. The apostolic appeal (Group 604-a) — "I appeal to you... to present your bodies as a living sacrifice" (Rom 12:1) — is an appeal to the will of the recipient.

**T3.6.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Strengthens volition: encouragement toward faithful conduct (Group 604-a; 2Th 2:17 — "establish them in every good work and word") produces stable volitional commitment. The comfort of God establishes the person for continued good action — comfort enables continued volitional engagement rather than passive receipt.

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — The giving of comfort requires volitional choice — the comforter must choose to move toward the distressed person. The receiving of encouragement strengthens the recipient's will for continued faithful living. The characteristic operates at the volitional level in both the giving and the receiving — though the affective level is more primary.

---

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Agency engagement?

**E** — Yes. Comfort is given through specific acts: "he comforted them and spoke kindly to them" (Gen 50:21 — Group 1741); "all who came to comfort him" (Job 2:11 — Group 1741); "God comforts the downcast" (2Cor 7:6 — Group 602). The characteristic requires the physical and relational act of coming toward the distressed person — it is not a passive disposition but an active movement toward.

**T3.7.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Enables action: "so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction" (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602) — comfort received enables the subsequent comfort-giving act. The characteristic restores agentic capacity in the recipient (who was distressed and incapacitated by grief) and produces a new agentic capacity (the ability to comfort others).

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Comfort is agentic in both directions: the comforter acts toward the distressed person; the comforted person is restored to action and equipped for a new comforting agency toward others. The cascading structure of 2Cor 1:3-7 (Groups 602, 3073) is the fullest expression of this: received comfort produces expanded comforting agency.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Moral evaluation engagement?

**E** — Partial. The distinction between genuine and false comfort (Job 16:2; Job 21:34; Zec 10:2 — Group 1741) requires moral evaluation — the comforter's genuineness must be assessed. The exhortation register (Group 604-a) involves moral evaluation of conduct: "I appeal to you... to present your bodies as a living sacrifice" — the appeal is toward a morally evaluated outcome.

**T3.8.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**P** — The evidence does not directly address whether comfort as such deepens or impairs moral evaluation. Partial: the pastoral exhortation register (Group 604-a) clearly engages moral evaluation as part of its operation.

**T3.8.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**S** — Moral evaluation is a peripheral rather than central faculty engagement for this characteristic. The characteristic's primary engagement is affective and relational. Silence on the moral evaluation dimension beyond what is noted.

---

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1-3** — Conscience engagement.

**S** — No explicit conscience-engagement in M05-F verses. The characteristic addresses grief and distress rather than guilt — which distinguishes it from mercy (M05-C) whose judicial-conscience register is significant. Comfort addresses the experiential state of the person rather than their moral state. Silence noted.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Conscientiousness engagement?

**E** — Partial. The apostolic exhortation toward faithful living (Group 604-a; 1Th 5:11 — "encourage one another and build one another up") engages conscientiousness — the integration of moral awareness, volition, and action in faithful living. The encouragement that produces this is itself a conscientious act by the encourager.

**T3.10.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Enables: encouragement toward faithful conduct (Group 604-a) strengthens conscientiousness by providing the relational and theological grounding for sustained integrated action.

**T3.10.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**P** — Conscientiousness is engaged primarily in the encouragement register (Group 604-a) rather than the comfort register (Groups 1741, 1742, 3079). The two sub-registers of this characteristic differ in faculty engagement: comfort is primarily affective; encouragement more conscientious. This is an important internal distinction.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Relational capacity engagement?

**E** — Yes, fundamentally. This characteristic is the most explicitly relational in M05 in its operational structure — it exists only in the movement of one person toward another in their distress. Phile 7 (Group 3075): "the hearts of the saints have been refreshed through you" — the relational mediation of comfort is the operative content. The cascading structure of 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) is a relational chain: God → Paul → Corinthians → others. Every link is a relational act.

**T3.11.2** — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair?

**E** — Deepens: the 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) structure reveals that receiving comfort deepens the person's relational capacity specifically toward those in similar distress. The shared experience of suffering and comfort creates a relational bond — the comforter who has suffered the same knows the person from the inside, not merely the outside. This is the relational deepening specific to this characteristic: suffering-shared-and-comforted produces a uniquely deep relational capacity for the same suffering in others.

**T3.11.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Comfort is the characteristic that most explicitly develops relational capacity through suffering — the pathway from suffering to relational depth runs through the experience of being comforted. The person who has never been comforted in affliction lacks the relational credential and capacity to comfort others in the same way. This is a distinctive finding: other characteristics (love, goodness, gentleness) build relational capacity through other pathways; comfort builds it specifically through shared suffering.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — God-to-human operation?

**E** — Yes, extensively. The divine comfort is the most extensive directional evidence in M05-F: Isa 40:1 ("Comfort, comfort my people, says your God" — Group 1742); Isa 51:3 ("the Lord comforts Zion" — Group 1742); Isa 51:12 ("I, I am he who comforts you" — Group 1742); Isa 66:13 ("as one whom his mother comforts, so I will comfort you" — Group 1742); 2Cor 1:3-4 ("God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction" — Groups 602, 3073); Psa 23:4 ("your rod and your staff, they comfort me" — Group 1741). The divine-to-human direction constitutes the majority of the comfort vocabulary in the OT.

**T4.1.2** — On what basis does God extend this characteristic?

**E** — Covenantal faithfulness and compassion: Psa 119:76 (Group 1741) — "Let your steadfast love comfort me according to your promise to your servant" — divine comfort is grounded in chesed/covenant promise. Judg 2:18 (Group 1742) — God is "moved to pity by their groaning" — the suffering-cry of the people triggers the divine comfort-movement. The basis is simultaneously covenantal (promise) and responsive (to their cry and groaning). God's comfort is both promised and responsive.

**T4.1.3** — What does this reveal about God's disposition?

**E** — God's disposition is one of active movement toward the distressed — he does not wait to be asked but initiates the comfort: "Comfort, comfort my people" (Isa 40:1 — Group 1742) is a commissioning of comfort directed outward. His disposition is named as that of a mother toward her child (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742) — the most tender and instinctive form of comfort. The divine inner disposition toward the suffering person is one of unconditional, tender, initiatory relational presence.

**T4.1.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T4.2 — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Human-to-God direction?

**P** — Limited evidence. The cry for comfort directed toward God (Group 603 parakaleō in plea register — Bartimaeus, lepers) is the closest human-toward-God direction, though this is the seeking of comfort rather than the giving of it. Psa 119:82 (Group 1741) — "When will you comfort me?" — the human addressing God with the request for comfort names a human-toward-God orientation of seeking. The person cannot comfort God — comfort flows asymmetrically downward (from greater to lesser, from the comforter to the distressed).

**T4.2.2-4** — Human-to-God direction silences.

**S** — Comfort in the active giving sense does not flow from human to God. The asymmetry is structural: the characteristic flows from strength to weakness, from the one with resources of comfort to the one who needs them. God has no grief or distress that the human person could relieve. Silence on human-to-God giving direction is a structural finding, not an evidential gap.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — Human giving of comfort?

**E** — Yes, extensively. Group 1741 contains many human comfort-giving instances: Isaac comforted after his mother's death (Gen 24:67); Joseph comforting his brothers (Gen 50:21); David comforting Bathsheba (2Sa 12:24); Job's friends coming to comfort (Job 2:11); Job sitting like one who comforts mourners (Job 29:25). The community encouragement register (Group 3075; Phile 7; 1Th 5:11) is constitutively about human giving.

**T4.3.2** — Conditions enabling genuine extension?

**E** — Shared suffering experience: 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) — "so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted." The credential for genuine comfort-giving is having received comfort in one's own suffering. Without the shared experience, the comfort remains external — words without inner authority.

**T4.3.3** — What must the person have received?

**E** — The comfort of God: 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) — the comforter must themselves have been comforted by God in affliction in order to comfort others. The chain is necessarily: divine comfort received → human comfort given. The person who has not been comforted in affliction lacks the inner resource for genuine comfort-giving.

**T4.3.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — Receiving direction?

**E** — Yes, extensively evidenced. The entire na.cham grief-comfort corpus (Group 1741) names the receiving experience: Isaac "was comforted" (Gen 24:67); Judah "was comforted" (Gen 38:12); David "comforted" Bathsheba (2Sa 12:24); "the saints' hearts refreshed" (Phile 7 — Group 3075). The receiving of comfort is the characteristic's most primary experiential form.

**T4.4.2** — Conditions enabling reception?

**E** — Grief and honest mourning (see T1.7.1 above — Mat 5:4 — Group 602). The refusal of comfort (Gen 37:35 — Group 1741; Psa 77:2 — Group 1741) names the blocking condition: an inner determination not to be comforted can prevent reception even when comfort is genuinely offered.

**T4.4.3** — Non-receiving inner state?

**E** — Sustained desolation: Lam 1:16 (Group 1741) — the spirit un-revived, the children desolate. The refusal to be comforted (Gen 37:35 — Group 1741 — Jacob) leaves the person in sustained mourning, unable to move forward. The non-receiver remains in the grip of grief that has no relief.

**T4.4.4** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

**T4.5.1** — Differently within bonds vs. across distance?

The characteristic operates most evidently within existing relational bonds.

**T4.5.2** — Covenantal contexts only?

**E** — Primarily covenantal and relational but not exclusively. The prophetic comfort of Israel (Isaiah — Groups 1741, 1742) is covenantal; the comfort of Job (Group 1741) is within a relational friendship framework; the apostolic comfort of believers (Groups 602, 604-a, 3073-3075) is community-internal.

**T4.5.3** — Who is included?

**E** — The mourning person, the afflicted person, the distressed or faltering person — defined by their condition of need rather than by any social category. Mat 5:4 (Group 602): "blessed are those who mourn" — mourning is the defining condition, not a social group.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Transformation

**T5.1.1** — Transformation produced?

**E** — Yes. (1) From grief to capacity: 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) — the comforted person is transformed from affliction-sufferer to affliction-comforter. The transformation is not merely internal relief but a new capacity to serve. (2) From mourning to joy: Jer 31:13 (Group 1742) — "I will turn their mourning into joy; I will comfort them and give them gladness for sorrow" — the transformation of inner state is explicit. (3) Hope: Rom 15:4 (Group 3074) — the Scriptures' encouragement produces hope — a stable inner orientation toward the future.

**T5.1.2** — Reversible or irreversible?

**E** — The immediate transformation (grief → relief) is potentially reversible — new grief can recur (Psa 94:19 — Group 3079 names repeated cares of the heart requiring repeated consolations). The deeper transformation (from sufferer to comforter — 2Cor 1:4) appears to be a lasting capacity rather than reversible. The eternal comfort (2Th 2:16 — Group 3074) is by definition irreversible.

**T5.1.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

---

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**T5.2.1** — Sequence of inner states?

**E** — 2Cor 1:3-7 (Groups 602, 3073) provides the most complete sequence: affliction → receiving God's comfort → comforting others in affliction → those comforted endure → their endurance produces hope in the comforter. This is a multi-step sequence in both directions — the comforter and the comforted move through the sequence together.

**T5.2.2** — States and what they reveal?

**E** — Before: affliction/grief/distress (Groups 1741, 602). During comfort: encounter with the comforting presence/word (Groups 1742, 3073). Immediately after: relief/cheer/heart-refreshment (Groups 3079, 3075). Sustained: capacity to comfort others (Group 602), hope (Group 3074), endurance (Group 3074). Eschatological: eternal comfort (Group 3074). The sequence reveals that comfort is a transformative process, not a single event — it moves the person through multiple stages toward a lasting state of hope and comforting capacity.

**T5.2.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**T5.3.1** — Mechanism?

**E** — Three mechanisms: (1) **Divine presence and initiative**: "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742) — the divine presence itself is the mechanism. Psa 23:4 (Group 1741): "your rod and your staff, they comfort me" — the signs of the Shepherd's presence produce comfort. (2) **Scripture engagement**: Rom 15:4 (Group 3074) — "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures." (3) **Shared suffering and human presence**: 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602) — the comforter's own experience of affliction is the mechanism that qualifies them to comfort others.

**T5.3.2** — Distinct mechanisms in different contexts?

**E** — Yes: divine presence is the primary OT mechanism (Groups 1741, 1742); Scripture engagement is the primary NT-epistolary mechanism (Group 3074); shared suffering and relational presence is the primary apostolic mechanism (Group 602); mutual community practice is the mechanism in the communal register (Group 3075).

**T5.3.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**T5.4.1** — Operating in relation to suffering?

**E** — Yes — this is the characteristic most explicitly constituted by its relationship to suffering. Suffering is not merely a context for this characteristic; it is its defining trigger. "The God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction" (2Cor 1:3-4 — Groups 602, 3073) — the affliction is what calls forth the comfort. The entire na.cham corpus (Groups 1741, 1742) is situated in grief, loss, exile, oppression, or desolation. There is no comfort without a suffering that calls it forth.

**T5.4.2** — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic?

**E** — Produces and deepens. Suffering produces the need for comfort (the distressed person becomes a candidate for receiving it) and deepens the comforter's capacity (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602: the affliction of the comforter is what equips them to comfort others). The suffering-to-comforter pathway (2Cor 1:3-7) is the most theologically developed formation arc in M05-F: suffering produces comfort-received, which produces comfort-given, which produces endurance in the comforted, which produces hope in the comforter.

**T5.4.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**T5.5.1** — Participation in the longer arc?

**E** — Yes, through the comfort-to-comforter pathway. The person who is repeatedly comforted through affliction acquires a deepening comforting capacity and credibility over time. The community that practices mutual encouragement (Group 3075; 1Th 5:11 — "build one another up") undergoes a corporate formation into a community of mutual care. Rom 15:4 (Group 3074) — "endurance and encouragement of the Scriptures" — names a formation process that produces hope as a stable character orientation.

**T5.5.2** — What does the evidence reveal?

**E** — Comfort and encouragement are formation agents that work specifically through the suffering-and-consolation cycle. The person is formed into deeper relational capacity, hope, and endurance through the repeated experience of being comforted in affliction and extending comfort to others. This is a specific pathway of formation — not available through prosperity but only through the suffering-comfort cycle.

**T5.5.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**T5.6.1** — Eschatological fullness?

**E** — Yes, prominently. "Eternal comfort and good hope through grace" (2Th 2:16 — Group 3074) — comfort has an eternal extent. Mat 5:4 (Group 602): "blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted" — the comfort is future and eschatological. Luk 2:25 (Group 3074): "waiting for the consolation of Israel" — Simeon's entire life was oriented toward the eschatological comfort that the Messiah would bring. Isa 66:13 (Group 1742): "you shall be comforted in Jerusalem" — the eschatological city as the place of final consolation.

**T5.6.2** — What does present experience anticipate?

**E** — Present comfort is a foretaste and a ground for eschatological hope. The "eternal comfort" of 2Th 2:16 (Group 3074) qualifies the present comfort as a participation in what will be completed. The consolation of Israel (Group 3074 — Luk 2:25) that Simeon was "waiting for" arrived in Christ but awaits its fullness. Present comfort is real but partial; the eschatological fullness is its completion.

**T5.6.3** — If silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

**T5.7.1-3** — Deposit consequence.

**S** — T2.8 found no bodily deposit. The formative deposits of M05-F are inner and relational: the comforter-capacity (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602), the stable hope (Rom 15:4 — Group 3074), and the deepened relational attentiveness to the suffering of others. These are inner and relational rather than somatic. T5.7 closes.

## T6 — Structural Relationships with Other Characteristics

### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**T6.1.1** — Adjacent characteristics appearing alongside?

**E** — Primary co-occurrences:
- **Compassion (M05-B)**: Isa 49:13 (Group 1742): "the Lord has comforted his people and will have compassion on his afflicted" — comfort and compassion paired. Judg 2:18 (Group 1742): na.cham and compassion appear in the same verse.
- **Love (M05-A)**: Phile 7 (Group 3075): "I have derived much joy and comfort from your love" — love as the source of comfort. 2Cor 1:3-4 (Groups 602, 3073): the comfort flows from the "Father of mercies" in the context of the mercy-love cluster.
- **Peace (eirēnē)**: Act 9:31 (mentioned in BOUNDARY section): "the church had peace and was being built up. And walking in the fear of the Lord and in the comfort of the Holy Spirit" — peace and comfort paired.
- **Hope**: Rom 15:4 (Group 3074): encouragement and hope are explicitly linked as sequential outcomes.

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — Primary terms and roots.

**E** — Three Hebrew root families and one Greek root family. ### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — Function of primary term in primary verse?

**E** — In 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602 anchor: "who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction"), parakaleō functions as the key verb twice — once in the participial form (the one who comforts) and once in the infinitive (to be able to comfort). The verse reveals that suffering is not merely the context for comfort but the necessary precondition for the comforter's qualification. No other verse in M05-F makes this with such precision.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Most useful framework?

**E** — Three frameworks are relevant. (1) **Grief psychology** (Kübler-Ross, Bowlby, Worden): the human comfort of the bereaved is one of the best-evidenced areas of psychological research — presence, acknowledgement of grief, and time are the primary comfort mechanisms. (2) **Trauma theory and post-traumatic growth**: the 2Cor 1:4 structure of suffering → comfort received → comforting capacity is structurally parallel to post-traumatic growth research (Tedeschi and Calhoun), which finds that people who have processed traumatic suffering develop a unique relational competence for supporting others through similar suffering. (3) **Social support research**: the community encouragement register (Group 3075) corresponds to social support research on the health benefits of perceived community belonging and mutual encouragement.

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates?

**E** — Post-traumatic growth research illuminates 2Cor 1:4 (Group 602): the finding that suffering-processed-through-comfort produces a unique comforter capacity corresponds to PTG research's finding that post-traumatic growth includes increased compassion for others who suffer. The framework makes the 2Cor 1:4 claim empirically credible: it is not merely a theological aspiration but corresponds to a documented human psychological phenomenon. Grief psychology illuminates the refusal-of-comfort pattern (Gen 37:35 — Group 1741): some grief is too great for social comfort and requires a different intervention — this is well-documented in bereavement research.

**T7.3.3** — Where the evidence diverges?

**E** — The sharpest divergence is in the source of comfort. Psychological frameworks treat comfort as primarily a human relational resource — the comforting person provides the primary resource. The verse evidence grounds comfort in God as the primary source and the human comforter as a secondary channel: "God of all comfort... who comforts us... so that we may be able to comfort others" (2Cor 1:3-4 — Groups 602, 3073). The cascading structure begins with God, not with the human comforter. No psychological framework has a structural equivalent to the divine source of comfort that flows through human channels. The eschatological dimension (eternal comfort — Group 3074; Mat 5:4's promise of future comfort — Group 602) has no psychological equivalent — grief psychology does not orient the grieving person toward an eschatological consolation.

**T7.3.4** — Aspects not addressed by verse evidence?

**E** — Grief psychology raises the question of the process of grief before comfort becomes possible — the stages or tasks of grief that must be engaged before comfort can be received. The verse evidence presents the comfort as something the person can receive or refuse but does not elaborate the inner process of grief-moving-toward-readiness-for-comfort. This is a gap for further verse investigation: what does the transition from refused comfort (Gen 37:35 — Group 1741) to received comfort (Gen 38:12 — Group 1741, same person Judah) look like internally?
