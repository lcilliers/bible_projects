## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1.1 — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty?

**[A]** Yes — phileō and fileō engage the person as perceiving and known: John 21's triple question ("do you love me?" — Group 1553) centres on Peter's perception of himself; the perceiving of the beloved's loveliness (Song — Groups 1574, 1575) is perceptive engagement.

**[B]** Yes — emphatically and primarily. Compassion is triggered by perception of suffering: sight (Jesus "saw the crowds" — Group 1601); hearing (God "heard their groaning" — Judg 2:18 — Group 1742). Perception of the other's need is the compassion trigger.

**[C]** Partial — the mercy cry (Group 1629) involves the perception of one's own need; the pitiable condition (eleeinos — Group 328) names what is visible to external perception.

**[D]** Partial — 1Pe 2:3 (Group 1469): "you have tasted that the Lord is good" — the sensory taste-image for encountering divine goodness. Perception is a minor register.

**[E]** Silent — gentleness is not perceptively triggered but contextually responsive; no dominant perceptive trigger evidenced.

**[F]** Yes — hearing of the other's suffering triggers the movement toward them: "when Job's three friends heard of all this evil that had come upon him... they came to comfort him" (Job 2:11 — Group 1741). Auditory perception triggers the comfort-orientation.

**[G]** Partial — the apostolic witness is grounded in "what we have seen and heard" (1Jo 1:3 — Group 1055) — eyewitness perception as the credential for proclamation that creates fellowship. Foundational but not ongoing.

---

### T3.1.2 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair perception?

**[A]** Deepens — love opens the cognitive access to the inner world of the beloved (Joh 15:15 — Group 1553: friendship constituted by knowledge-disclosure). Love enables a form of perception that strangers cannot access.

**[B]** Triggers — compassion is triggered by perception and in turn deepens the comforter's perceptive sensitivity to suffering (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602 parallel: the one who has been comforted in affliction perceives others' affliction more acutely).

**[F]** Deepens — the person who has been comforted in affliction perceives others' distress more precisely. The suffering-and-comfort pathway sharpens the perception of suffering in others.

---

### T3.1.3 — What does engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal?

**[CLUSTER]** Perception is the primary trigger for M05-B (compassion) and M05-F (comfort) — both are activated by the perceptive encounter with another's suffering. The other sub-groups engage perception more peripherally. This cluster-level pattern confirms that the cluster's characteristics are constitutively relational — they are not self-generated in isolation but activated by the encounter with another person's inner reality (suffering, need, invitation to fellowship). The characteristics require the perceptive faculty to be activated, even when perception is not the primary or ongoing constitutional engagement.

---

### T3.2.1 — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?

**[A]** Yes — love requires and produces knowledge (Hos 6:6 — "knowledge of God" as the companion of chesed; Joh 15:15 — love/friendship as mutual knowledge-disclosure). The renewed mind enabling discernment of the good (Rom 12:2 — Group 1098) is primarily a M05-D finding but applies to love's evaluative dimension.

**[B]** Silent on cognitive engagement as primary — compassion is pre-cognitive in its triggering. The visceral stirring precedes deliberation.

**[C]** Silent on cognitive engagement as primary — mercy operates in the judicial-relational frame rather than through cognitive reasoning.

**[D]** Yes — Rom 12:2 (Group 1098): "discern what is the will of God, what is good." Pronoeō (Group 2290): "give thought to do what is honourable." Goodness requires cognitive discernment of what goodness demands in each situation.

**[E]** Yes — Jam 3:13 (Group 1262): "the meekness of wisdom" — gentleness is the proper mode of wisdom's expression. The cognitive faculty's output is governed by the inner disposition of meekness.

**[F]** Yes in the encouragement register — Rom 15:4 (Group 3074): "through endurance and through the encouragement of the Scriptures" — Scripture is received cognitively and its encouragement operates through cognitive engagement.

**[G]** Yes — homofrōn (Group 1813 — 1Pe 3:8): "unity of mind" — cognitive-evaluative alignment as the mind-faculty expression of fellowship. Phile 6 (Group 1056): "full knowledge of every good thing that is in us" — koinōnia oriented toward expanded cognitive apprehension.

---

### T3.2.2-3 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair? Pattern reveals?

**[CLUSTER]** The cognitive faculty is engaged across all sub-groups but is the primary faculty only for M05-D (goodness requires cognitive discernment) and M05-G (fellowship produces unity of mind). In the other sub-groups, cognition is a support faculty rather than the primary engagement. M05-B is notable for its pre-cognitive triggering — compassion explicitly precedes deliberation. The cluster's characteristics are not primarily cognitive but they all require or produce cognitive content. The cluster-level finding: these characteristics are not intellectual virtues but they all have a cognitive dimension through which they are discerned, expressed, or deepened.

---

### T3.3.1–3 — Memory engagement?

**[A]** Yes — memory is a support faculty. God's remembrance of his chesed (Psa 98:3 — Group 1583-a context: "he has remembered his steadfast love") is an act of memory. The chasid's sustained faithfulness through time requires memory of the covenant.

**[B]** Silent — memory is not evidenced as a significant faculty for compassion.

**[C-E]** Silent — memory is not a significant faculty engagement for mercy, goodness, or gentleness.

**[F]** Yes, partially — Psa 119:52 (Group 1741): "when I think of your rules from of old, I take comfort, O Lord" — memory of God's past ways produces present comfort. Memory enables comfort by making available the evidence of God's past faithfulness.

**[G]** Silent — memory is not evidenced as significant for fellowship.

**[CLUSTER]** Memory is a peripheral faculty across M05. It is evidenced for M05-A (sustaining love through time) and M05-F (enabling comfort through remembrance of past faithfulness). The cluster does not primarily engage memory — its characteristics are constituted by present inner qualities and relational bonds rather than by the retrieval of past states. This is a cluster-level finding: M05 is not a memory-dependent cluster.

---

### T3.4.1 — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty?

**[A]** Yes — love has a rich affective dimension. Ahavah and phileō are constitutively affective; the yearning of God's inner being for Ephraim (Jer 31:20 — Group 1596 context) is affective; Song 5:6 (Group 1575) — "my soul failed me" at the beloved's absence — is intense affective experience.

**[B]** Yes — centrally and constitutively. Compassion is the most affective characteristic in M05. The somatic-visceral stirring (splanchnizō — Group 1601) is an affective event at the gut level. The womb-root (ra.cham) is itself an affective image.

**[C]** Partial — mercy produces joy (Phile 7 — Group 3075 parallel; Rom 5:11 — Group 2069 parallel with M05-G). The judicial frame of mercy is not primarily affective but mercy received generates affective responses of joy.

**[D]** Partial — the generous giving of haplotēs (Group 688) carries an affective quality of gladness (2Cor 8:2 — Group 688). But affect is not the primary register of M05-D.

**[E]** Yes, partially — the "gentle and quiet spirit" (1Pe 3:4 — Group 1261) names inner settledness — an affective quality of non-agitation. Anexikakos (Group 867) requires management of the affective response to wrong (suppressing resentment).

**[F]** Yes — centrally. Comfort is constituted by addressing and transforming the inner affective state: from grief/distress toward relief/cheer. Psa 94:19 (Group 3079): "your consolations cheer my soul." The affective transformation is the characteristic's primary operational goal.

**[G]** Partial — joy as the affective response to reconciliation received (Rom 5:11 — Group 2069). The fellowship bond itself generates belonging as an inner state. But fellowship is primarily constituted by the relational bond rather than by the affective state it produces.

---

### T3.4.2–3 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair? Pattern reveals?

**[CLUSTER]** Affect is the primary faculty for M05-B (compassion) and M05-F (comfort), the most important secondary faculty for M05-A (love), and a real but tertiary faculty for M05-C, D, E, G. The cluster's characteristics engage the affective faculty differentially: the suffering-triggered characteristics (B, F) are constitutively affective; the moral-quality characteristics (D, E) are peripherally affective; the relational characteristics (A, G) span both. This pattern reveals that M05 is not uniformly affective — it ranges from the constitutively somatic-affective (M05-B) to the substantially non-affective (M05-D, G in their primary registers).

---

### T3.5.1–3 — Creativity engagement?

**[CLUSTER]** Silent across all seven sub-groups. The creativity faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — is not evidenced as engaged by any characteristic in M05. This is a cluster-level finding. The cluster's characteristics engage perception, cognition, memory, affect, volition, agency, moral evaluation, conscience, conscientiousness, and relational capacity in various degrees — but not creativity. This may reflect the relational-ethical character of the cluster: M05's characteristics are constituted by their engagement with others rather than by origination of new things.

### T3.6.1 — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**[A]** Yes — centrally. Love is commanded (Mat 22:37 — Group 1542): a commandment requires volitional response. Enemy-love (Mat 5:44 — Group 1565) is the most demanding volitional love — it operates against natural preference. The ranking of loves (Mat 10:37 — Group 1551) requires deliberate volitional choice.

**[B]** Partially — compassion's primary stirring is pre-volitional (the visceral trigger precedes deliberation). Volition operates primarily in the judicial-withholding register: the decision to withhold compassion appropriately (Ezekiel's "eye shall not pity" — Groups 338, 630) is an overriding of the compassion-impulse through deliberate judicial volition.

**[C]** Partial — human mercy requires the deliberate volitional choice to show it (Mat 18:33 — Group 1630 — the servant chose not to show mercy). The sovereignty of divine mercy (Rom 9:15-16 — Group 1628) is itself a divine volitional act.

**[D]** Yes — centrally. Goodness is commanded and requires sustained volitional commitment: "hold fast to what is good" (Rom 12:9 — Group 1098); "overcome evil with good" (Rom 12:21 — Group 1098); "do good to those who hate you" (Luk 6:35 — Group 1093).

**[E]** Yes — significantly. The characteristic is commanded and pursued: "pursue gentleness" (1Ti 6:11 — Group 1261); "put on meekness" (Col 3:12 — Group 1104); "seek humility" (Zep 2:3 — Group 1272). Each context where gentleness is commanded is one where the coercive alternative is available — the choice of gentleness requires volitional override of the natural reactive impulse.

**[F]** Yes — the giving of comfort is a volitional act: "I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742); Job's choosing to sit like one who comforts mourners (Job 29:25 — Group 1741). The apostolic appeal (Group 604-a) is addressed to the will of the recipient.

**[G]** Yes — fellowship in the horizontal register involves deliberate choice: the Macedonians "begged earnestly for the favor of taking part" (2Cor 8:4 — Group 1056). Heb 13:16 (Group 1056): "do not neglect to do good and to share" — volitional command against neglect.

---

### T3.6.2–3 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair? Pattern?

**[CLUSTER]** Volition is engaged across all seven sub-groups, making it the most universally engaged inner faculty in M05. The cluster's characteristics are not merely felt or perceived but consistently require volitional engagement — they must be chosen, maintained, practiced, and directed. The one significant variation is M05-B (compassion), where the primary stirring is pre-volitional (the visceral trigger precedes deliberation), though volition is engaged in the judicial-withholding register. The cluster as a whole requires the will — these characteristics cannot be merely passive. This is one of M05's most important faculty findings: love, compassion, mercy, goodness, gentleness, comfort, and fellowship are all volitional commitments, not only experiential states.

### T3.7.1 — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty?

**[A]** Yes — love generates agency: "love does... love is... love gives" (1Cor 13 — Group 1542). The agapaō verbal form (to love — Group 571) names love as an active engagement. The love-your-enemies command (Mat 5:44 — Group 1565) requires active agentic expression.

**[B]** Yes — compassion generates action without gap: seeing the crowd, Jesus "had compassion on them, and healed their sick" (Mat 14:14 — Group 1601). The compassion-stirring leads immediately to the healing act. The disobedient compassion of 1Sa 15:9 (Group 629-b) also evidences agency — Saul chose to spare what should have been destroyed.

**[C]** Yes — the act of showing mercy (eleeō — Group 1630); the ministry of mercy in Rom 12:8 (Group 1630 — "the one who does acts of mercy"); the Samaritan's actions in the Lukan context (Group 1630 context).

**[D]** Yes — extensively. The agathopoieō compound family (Group 1093) is constitutively agentic: doing good, working good, good-doing as ongoing practice. Eph 2:10 (Group 1098): "created in Christ Jesus for good works to walk in" — agency as the telos of re-creation.

**[E]** Yes — gentleness defines the mode of agency rather than producing it. Restoring (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104), correcting (2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104), making a defence (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262) are active engagements where the characteristic defines how rather than whether.

**[F]** Yes — comfort is given through specific acts: coming to the person, speaking kindly, writing encouragingly (multiple Group 1741 instances). The cascading comfort structure (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602) produces a new agentic capacity in the comforted person.

**[G]** Yes — epoikodomeō (Group 2849) is constitutively agentic: building, investing, constructing the community on its foundation. Financial sharing (Group 1056) is agentic.

---

### T3.7.2–3 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair? Pattern?

**[CLUSTER]** Agency is engaged across all seven sub-groups, making it co-equal with volition as the most universally engaged faculty in M05. The cluster's characteristics are not merely inner states but characteristically produce outward action. The pattern across all seven: the inner quality generates outward agency. Love generates acts of love; compassion generates the healing, feeding, protecting act; mercy generates the act of showing mercy; goodness generates the doing of good; gentleness defines the mode of all corrective and relational action; comfort generates the act of comforting; fellowship generates sharing, building, and reconciling. The cluster's characteristics are inherently agentic — they cannot remain merely inner without expressing outward.

### T3.8.1 — Does this characteristic engage moral evaluation?

**[A]** Yes — love is the evaluative standard: "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Rom 13:10 — Group 1542). The question of whether love is rightly directed (toward God and neighbour versus the world or disordered objects — Groups 1565, 1565) requires moral evaluation. Filagathos (love of good — Group 1558) is itself a moral evaluation term.

**[B]** Yes — the judicial-withholding register requires moral evaluation of when compassion is appropriate and when it should be withheld (Groups 338, 630, 629-b). The disobedient compassion of 1Sa 15:9 (Group 629-b) is the clearest evidencing: compassion shown where it should have been withheld is a moral failure.

**[C]** Yes — mercy is a moral virtue and a moral standard: "blessed are the merciful" (Mat 5:7 — Group 1631); "mercy triumphs over judgment" (Jam 2:13 — Group 1633); "I desire mercy, and not sacrifice" (Mat 9:13 — Group 1633). The assessment of what mercy requires is a moral evaluation act.

**[D]** Yes — centrally. Goodness is a moral-quality term: the not-good verdict (Group 1100), the comparative wisdom (Group 1097 — "the good person out of his good treasure"), the renewed mind discerning what is good (Rom 12:2 — Group 1098). Moral evaluation is one of goodness' most extensively engaged faculties.

**[E]** Yes — Jam 3:17 (Group 1103): the wisdom from above is characterised by epieikēs as a moral quality. Gal 6:1 (Group 1104): restoring someone in a spirit of gentleness requires moral evaluation of their failure and the appropriate response. Moral evaluation is both a prerequisite of genuine gentleness and a capacity deepened by its practice.

**[F]** Partial — the distinction between genuine and false comfort (Job 16:2 — Group 1741; Job 21:34 — Group 1741) requires moral evaluation. The pastoral exhortation register (Group 604-a) engages moral evaluation of conduct.

**[G]** Yes — moral solidarity (Group 1058) is the most morally weighty dimension of fellowship: participating with someone constitutes moral alignment with their cause. The question of what one participates in is a profound moral evaluation question.

---

### T3.8.2–3 — Pattern?

**[CLUSTER]** Moral evaluation is a significant faculty across all sub-groups. The cluster's characteristics are morally evaluative in multiple senses: they are moral qualities themselves (subject to moral assessment); they require moral evaluation to be rightly practiced (discerning when compassion should be withheld, whether fellowship is genuine); and they serve as moral standards (love as the fulfilling of the law). The cluster-level finding: M05's characteristics are not merely relational or affective — they are constitutively moral, requiring and enabling the moral evaluation faculty.

---

### T3.9.1 — Conscience engagement?

**[A]** Partial — the heart as the seat of both love and conscience (1Ti 1:5 — Group 1097 context: "love from a pure heart and a good conscience and sincere faith") creates an implicit connection. But the conscience is not explicitly named as a primary faculty for M05-A.

**[B-C]** Silent — conscience is not evidenced as a significant faculty for compassion or mercy in M05-B or M05-C.

**[D]** Yes — explicitly and distinctively. Good conscience is named multiple times in M05-D: Act 23:1 (Group 1097); 1Ti 1:5 (Group 1097); 1Ti 1:19 (Group 1097 — shipwreck of faith by rejecting good conscience); 1Pe 3:16 (Group 1097); 1Pe 3:21 (Group 1097). Conscience as the inner registrar of goodness' faithfulness is the sub-group-distinctive finding of M05-D.

**[E-G]** Peripheral — conscience is not a central faculty for gentleness (M05-E), comfort (M05-F), or fellowship (M05-G) in their primary verse evidence.

**[CLUSTER]** Conscience is the distinctive faculty of M05-D. No other sub-group evidences good conscience as explicitly or consistently. This is a cross-sub-group visible finding: when all seven passes are read together, M05-D stands out as the conscience-engaged characteristic in the cluster. The conscience is to M05-D what the bowel/womb vocabulary is to M05-B — the sub-group's distinctive inner faculty contribution to the cluster.

### T3.10.1 — Conscientiousness engagement?

**[A]** Yes — chesed as sustained covenant faithfulness maintained through time, cost, and adversity (Ruth's chesed — Rut 3:10 — Group 1584; Jacob's refusal to abandon his promise of covenant loyalty) is the integrated moral-volitional-agentic expression of conscientious love.

**[B]** Partial — the judgment-withholding register (Groups 338, 630) requires conscientious integration of moral awareness, volition, and restraint. But compassion's primary mode (the visceral trigger) is not conscientious in the integrated-deliberative sense.

**[C]** Partial — the reciprocal mercy practice (Mat 18:33 — Group 1630) requires conscientious integration of having-received and obligation-to-show.

**[D]** Yes — prominently. The agathopoieō group (Group 1093) is constituted by the integrated doing-of-good under cost: "if when you do good and suffer for it you endure" (1Pe 2:20 — Group 1093). This is the fullest conscientious expression: knowing what good requires, choosing it, and acting on it even under suffering.

**[E]** Yes — the endurance-of-evil-without-resentment (2Ti 2:24 — Group 867): knowing evil is being suffered (moral awareness), choosing not to retaliate (volition), and enduring without resentment (sustained action) — the three elements of conscientiousness converging.

**[F]** Partial — the pastoral exhortation toward faithful living (Group 604-a) engages conscientiousness by calling the recipient toward the integrated moral-volitional-agentic response.

**[G]** Yes — Heb 13:16 (Group 1056): "do not neglect to do good and to share" — the command against neglect requires conscientious integration of knowing, willing, and doing.

**[CLUSTER]** Conscientiousness is significantly engaged across M05, particularly in M05-A (chesed's sustained faithfulness), M05-D (goodness under suffering), and M05-E (gentleness under provocation). The cluster's characteristics are not occasional responses but sustained integrated commitments — they require the ongoing alignment of moral awareness, volition, and action that conscientiousness describes.

### T3.11.1 — Does this characteristic engage relational capacity?

**[A]** Yes — constitutively. Love is the relational characteristic par excellence: it is constituted by directed attachment toward another person. The great commandment (Mat 22:37 — Group 1542) names love toward God and neighbour as the two primary relational orientations of the person.

**[B]** Yes — compassion is constitutively relational: triggered by the perceptive encounter with another's suffering, it produces action toward the sufferer. The directional asymmetry (compassion flows from greater to lesser; human never shows compassion toward God — Group 1596 analysis) confirms its relational structure.

**[C]** Yes — mercy is inherently relational in its judicial frame: it always involves the greater (authority) and the lesser (guilty/helpless) in a defined relational position. Mat 5:7 (Group 1631): "the merciful shall receive mercy" — the reciprocal relational structure.

**[D]** Yes — kindness (chrēstotēs) is explicitly relational: "he is kind to the ungrateful and the evil" (Luk 6:35 — Group 1469) — the relational reach extends to enemies. Goodness builds relational capacity (Rom 15:14 — Group 1097: "full of goodness, able to instruct one another").

**[E]** Yes — gentleness is the most relationally-defined characteristic in its operational structure: it exists only as the quality of engagement with another person. Every major evidencing is relational in context (restoration, correction, defence, endurance, courtesy).

**[F]** Yes — comfort is constitutively relational: the comforter moves toward the distressed person. The cascading structure of 2Cor 1:3-7 (Groups 602, 3073) is a relational chain.

**[G]** Yes — irreducibly so. Fellowship and participation is the characteristic that cannot exist except as the bond between persons. Every term in M05-G requires at least two parties.

---

### T3.11.2–3 — Enable, deepen, bypass, or impair? Pattern?

**[CLUSTER]** Relational capacity is the most universally and centrally engaged faculty across all seven sub-groups — equal in universality with volition and agency. This is the primary cluster-level faculty finding: M05's characteristics all constitute, enable, or express the relational capacity of the inner person. The cluster as a whole is the relational-capacity cluster — its characteristics are the inner equipment for genuine engagement with God and with other human persons. The variations between sub-groups are in the mode of relational engagement: love as directed attachment; compassion as responsive inner movement; mercy as authority-toward-helplessness; goodness as moral-generative expression; gentleness as non-coercive mode; comfort as grief-directed relief; fellowship as shared participation.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1.1 — Does this characteristic operate from God toward the human?

**[A]** Yes — the most extensively evidenced directional axis in M05-A. God's love is the prior and originating form: "we love because he first loved us" (1Jo 4:19 — Group 1542). The chesed groups (1583-a, 1583-b) document God's chesed toward Israel across 115 verses.

**[B]** Yes — Groups 1594-1596 document divine compassion as the dominant directional register: God's compassion is the attribute (ra.chum), the inner movement (Jer 31:20 — Group 1596), and the eschatological gathering (Isa 54:7 — Group 1596).

**[C]** Yes — God's mercy is the ground of salvation, new birth, and election: "according to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again" (1Pe 1:3 — Group 1632); Rom 9:15-16 (Group 1628).

**[D]** Yes — God's kindness in Christ (Eph 2:7 — Group 1111); God's doing of good through providence (Act 14:17 — Group 1092); God's goodness as the exclusive standard (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096).

**[E]** Yes — God's gentleness making the person great (Psa 18:35 — Group 1271); Christ's meekness and gentleness as the ground of Paul's appeal (2Cor 10:1 — Groups 1102, 1104).

**[F]** Yes — extensively. The divine comfort corpus (Group 1742): "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12); "Comfort, comfort my people" (Isa 40:1); "as one whom his mother comforts, so I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13).

**[G]** Yes — God calls into fellowship (1Cor 1:9 — Group 1055); God reconciles through Christ (2Cor 5:18 — Group 2069); God grants participation in the divine nature (2Pe 1:4 — Group 1057).

**[CLUSTER]** The God-to-human direction is the most extensively evidenced directional axis across all seven sub-groups. This is the primary relational interface in M05. The cluster's characteristics are all first and most fully evidenced as divine dispositions toward the human person — before they are human dispositions toward others.

---

### T4.1.2 — On what basis does God extend this characteristic?

**[A]** Covenantal and unconditional in its deepest form — "with everlasting love I have loved you" (Jer 31:3 — Group 1542). Responsive to covenant-love: "to a thousand generations of those who love me" (Exo 20:6 — Group 1583-a).

**[B]** Compassion (ra.chum) in Exo 34:6 (Group 1594) is stated as divine constitutive character rather than conditional on Israel's merit. Triggered by their groaning (Judg 2:18 — Group 1742) — responsive to need.

**[C]** Sovereignly — "not because of works but because of him who calls" (Rom 9:11-12 — Group 1628 context). Mercy's basis is divine sovereign will, not human merit.

**[D]** Grace — "immeasurable riches of his grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus" (Eph 2:7 — Group 1111). Unconditional in character, directed toward repentance in purpose.

**[E]** Constitutive character — God's gentleness is not conditional but is what he is toward the creature. The warrior-king who comes meekly (Mat 21:5 — Group 1261) images the unconditional divine mode.

**[F]** Covenantal faithfulness and responsive compassion — "let your steadfast love comfort me according to your promise to your servant" (Psa 119:76 — Group 1741). Both promised and responsive.

**[G]** Faithfulness and grace — "God is faithful, by whom you were called into the fellowship of his Son" (1Cor 1:9 — Group 1055). "Not counting their trespasses against them" (2Cor 5:19 — Group 2069) — the basis is non-accounting of trespasses, i.e., grace.

---

### T4.1.3 — What does God's extension reveal about his disposition?

**[CLUSTER]** Across all seven sub-groups, God's extension of the cluster's characteristics reveals a consistent divine disposition: unconditional, initiatory, covenantal, and transformatively purposive gracious engagement with the human person. God does not merely respond to human goodness; he precedes, initiates, and grounds the possibility of the human's receiving and expressing these characteristics. The cluster reveals a God who is constitutively for the human person — who loves, compassionates, mercifully overrides condemnation, gives gracious goodwill, stoops in gentleness, actively comforts, and calls into fellowship with himself.

---

### T4.1.4 — If silent on God-to-human, note explicitly.

Not applicable — the God-to-human direction is evidenced across all seven sub-groups.

### T4.2.1 — Human-to-God direction?

**[A]** Yes — the great commandment (Mat 22:37 — Group 1542) is primarily a human-toward-God direction: "you shall love the Lord your God." The entire love-toward-God register is the human-to-God direction of M05-A.

**[B]** Absent as a giving direction — humans never show compassion toward God in the verse evidence. The human-to-God direction for compassion is petitionary only: appealing to God's compassion, not showing it toward him. This is a structural asymmetry: compassion flows only downward (from greater to lesser), and the human is never the greater in relation to God.

**[C]** Absent — the mercy cry (Group 1629) is human petitioning God's mercy, not showing mercy toward God. The directional asymmetry of mercy (flows from authority toward helplessness) structurally prevents the human-to-God direction.

**[D]** Partial and limited — the evidence in M05-D does not focus on goodness or kindness as a mode of the person's inner movement toward God. The characteristic primarily operates horizontally toward others.

**[E]** Yes — a.na.vah/an.vah (Groups 1272, 1271) is constitutively oriented toward God: "humility and fear of the Lord" (Pro 22:4 — Group 1272); "seek humility" before God (Zep 2:3 — Group 1272). The Hebrew humility-meekness vocabulary is primarily the creature's appropriate vertical orientation before God.

**[F]** Partial — the seeking of comfort from God (Psa 119:82 — Group 1741: "when will you comfort me?") is the human-to-God direction in the seeking/petitionary sense. Human comfort-giving toward God is structurally impossible (same asymmetry as compassion and mercy).

**[G]** Yes — walking in the light as the condition for fellowship with God (1Jo 1:7 — Group 1055); sharing in Christ's sufferings (Phili 3:10 — Group 1055). The human actively participates in the vertical fellowship through moral orientation and willing suffering-sharing.

### T4.2.2–4 — Inner posture required? What does the direction reveal? If silent?

**[A]** Total inner mobilisation — "with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength" (Mat 22:37 — Group 1542). Love toward God requires complete inner engagement.

**[E]** Humility as the creature's accurate self-location before God — not self-diminishment but appropriate recognition of who God is and who the creature is.

**[CLUSTER]** The human-to-God direction is the most structurally asymmetric dimension in M05. M05-B and M05-C are structurally silent — the directional asymmetry of compassion and mercy (flowing only from greater to lesser) means the human can never be the compassion-giver or mercy-giver toward God. M05-A (love toward God) and M05-E (humility before God) are the strongest human-to-God directions. M05-G (walking in the light, sharing sufferings) is present but constitutively responsive to divine initiative. The cluster teaches that the human's primary relational movement is not self-generated love toward God but a responsive, enabled participation in the love God has first shown.

### T4.3.1 — Human giving of this characteristic?

**[A]** Yes — the horizontal love commands (love your neighbour, love your enemies — Mat 5:44; Lev 19:18 — Groups 1564, 1565) require the human to extend love outward. The chesed of Ruth (Rut 3:10 — Group 1584) and Jonathan-David (1Sa 20:14 — Group 1584) are the primary human-giving instances.

**[B]** Yes — Judg 21:6, 15 (Group 1742): "the people of Israel had compassion for Benjamin their brother." Human compassion toward another is evidenced though less prominent than divine compassion.

**[C]** Yes — Mat 5:7 (Group 1631): "blessed are the merciful"; Mat 18:33 (Group 1630): "should you not have had mercy on your fellow servant?" — human mercy-giving is explicitly commanded and required.

**[D]** Yes — extensively. The agathopoieō compound family (Group 1093) is entirely about human doing of good toward others: "love your enemies and do good" (Luk 6:35 — Group 1093); "do good to those who do good to you" and beyond (Group 1093).

**[E]** Yes — gentleness given through restoration (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104), correction (2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104), defence (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262), endurance toward those who do evil (2Ti 2:24 — Group 867), courtesy toward all (Tit 3:2 — Groups 1103, 1262).

**[F]** Yes — Group 1741 human comfort: Joseph comforting brothers (Gen 50:21 — Group 1741); Job's friends coming to comfort him (Job 2:11 — Group 1741); the mutual comfort of believers (Group 3075).

**[G]** Yes — financial contribution (Group 1056); ministry of reconciliation (2Cor 5:18 — Group 2069 — entrusted to humans); moral solidarity in persecution (Heb 10:33 — Group 1058).

### T4.3.2 — Conditions enabling genuine extension?

**[CLUSTER]** Across all sub-groups, the consistent condition for genuine giving is having first received from God: the person who loves has received God's prior love (1Jo 4:19 — Group 1542); the person who comforts in affliction has been comforted by God (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602); the person who reconciles has received reconciliation (2Cor 5:18 — Group 2069); the person who does good has a good heart treasury (Mat 12:35 — Group 1097). The cluster teaches that genuine giving of the characteristics requires prior reception from God — the human cannot generate any of these characteristics from their own resources without the divine prior act.

---

### T4.3.3 — What must the person have received before they can genuinely give?

**[CLUSTER]** The divine prior act specific to each characteristic: love received from God (M05-A); compassion from God's own compassion-character (M05-B); mercy received before mercy can be shown (M05-C); Spirit-produced goodness before good-doing (M05-D); Christological model of gentleness and Spirit-production (M05-E); comfort from God before comforting others (M05-F); divine calling into fellowship before extending fellowship to others (M05-G). The pattern is invariant: reception precedes genuine giving across all seven.

### T4.4.1–4 — Human receiving direction?

**[CLUSTER]** The receiving direction is evidenced across all sub-groups but is unevenly documented. M05-A (love received), M05-B (compassion received — the cry for divine compassion), M05-C (mercy cry — Group 1629 — is the most vivid and extensive receiving evidencing in M05), M05-F (comfort received — extensively evidenced in both OT grief contexts and NT apostolic contexts) are the most receiver-prominent sub-groups. M05-D and M05-E are the most giver-oriented in their verse evidence — the receiver's immediate inner experience is underspecified. M05-G documents receiving reconciliation (Rom 5:11 — Group 2069) and being called into fellowship (1Cor 1:9 — Group 1055) as the primary receiving events.

**Conditions enabling reception** are consistent across sub-groups: humility, need, seeking, honest mourning, walking in the light — the posture of the person who acknowledges their need and opens themselves to receive.

**Non-receiving inner states** are also consistent: hardness (M05-A, D), refusal (M05-F), self-deception (M05-G), mercilessness (M05-C), disordered love priority (M05-A).

### T4.5.1–4 — Relational scope and boundaries?

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster's characteristics consistently transcend relational boundaries — they are not restricted to existing bonds but extend toward strangers, enemies, the world. The evidence for universal scope: "do good to everyone" (Gal 6:10 — Group 1098); "kind to the ungrateful and the evil" (Luk 6:35 — Group 1469); "comfort all who mourn" (Isa 61:2 — Group 1742); "God was reconciling the world to himself" (2Cor 5:19 — Group 2069); "show perfect courtesy toward all people" (Tit 3:2 — Groups 1103, 1262). The cluster teaches that these characteristics have a universal relational scope defined by the person's condition of need rather than by pre-existing relational bonds. The community of faith retains priority (Gal 6:10 — Group 1098: "especially to those of the household of faith") but the scope is not restricted to it.

### T4.6.1 — Spiritual beings interface?

**[G]** Yes — uniquely and in a negative register. 1Cor 10:20 (Group 1057): "I do not want you to be participants with demons" — koinōnos with demons is explicitly named as a possibility to be avoided. This is the only verse in M05 that explicitly names an adversarial spiritual being as a koinōnos of the human person.

**[A-F]** The other sub-groups are silent on adversarial spiritual being engagement in their primary verse evidence.

**[CLUSTER]** The adversarial spiritual beings interface is evidenced only in M05-G, and exclusively in the negative register. The fellowship characteristic is the most spiritually dangerous in M05 — it names the possibility of participation with demonic powers (through idol worship) alongside the highest form of participation (in the divine nature — 2Pe 1:4 — Group 1057). The range of M05-G's koinōnos is from the lowest (participation with demons) to the highest (participation in divine nature) — the widest spiritual register of any characteristic in M05.
