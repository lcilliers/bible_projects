## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A] Love:** God's constitutive being is love — the divine self-disclosure formula at Exo 34:6 (Group 1583-a/b) names chesed as his defining inner attribute; 1Jo 4:8 establishes that God is love ontologically. Love is not one attribute among many but the characterisation of what God is. The specific love-forms (chesed, agapē, rachamim) all name God before naming the human: the divine instance is the original; the human instance is participatory.

**[B] Compassion:** God is constitutively compassionate — ra.chum in Exo 34:6 (Group 1594) names his compassion alongside chesed in the divine self-disclosure formula. The maternal imagery of Isa 49:15 (Group 1596) — "can a woman forget her nursing child?" — establishes that God's compassion exceeds the most reliably tender human compassion. The character revealed: one who is moved by the encounter with his people's suffering as a mother is moved by the cry of her infant.

**[C] Mercy:** God is "rich in mercy" (Eph 2:4 — Group 1632) — mercy is a constitutive divine abundance, not a reluctant concession. The Lukan birth narrative deploys eleos as the content of God's covenant-faithfulness in action (Luk 1:50, 54, 72, 78 — Group 1632). The character revealed: one whose authority is exercised as merciful toward the lesser party who stands in guilt or helplessness.

**[D] Kindness and Goodness:** "No one is good except God alone" (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096) — goodness is God's exclusive constitutive attribute from which all creaturely goodness is derivative. God's kindness is purposive and active: "God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance" (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111). The character revealed: one whose inner disposition is generative gracious goodwill that is not passive but transformatively directed.

**[E] Gentleness:** God's gentleness makes the person great — "your gentleness made me great" (Psa 18:35 / 2Sa 22:36 — Group 1271). Christ's meekness and gentleness are named as the ground of apostolic appeal (2Cor 10:1 — Groups 1102, 1104). The character revealed: one who holds all coercive power and chooses not to exercise it destructively — the stooping of the infinite toward the finite that produces greatness in the recipient.

**[F] Comfort and Encouragement:** God names himself as comforter — "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12 — Group 1742). He is the "Father of mercies and God of all comfort" (2Cor 1:3 — Group 3073). The maternal comfort image (Isa 66:13 — Group 1742) identifies his comforting with the most instinctive human tender care. The character revealed: one who actively moves toward the distressed with the specific intent to relieve — not passively available but initiatorily present.

**[G] Fellowship and Participation:** The "fellowship of the Holy Spirit" (2Cor 13:14 — Group 1055) names koinōnia as a quality of the divine inner life. God is "faithful, by whom you were called into the fellowship of his Son" (1Cor 1:9 — Group 1055). He initiates katallagē — "God, who through Christ reconciled us to himself" (2Cor 5:18 — Group 2069). The character revealed: a God whose inner communal life (Trinitarian fellowship) overflows into restoration of broken relationships and constitution of a shared community.

**[CLUSTER]** Across all seven sub-groups, the verse evidence establishes God as the constitutive origin of every characteristic in M05. Not one characteristic is presented as naturally human or autonomously generated: love is what God is (M05-A); compassion is his defining attribute (M05-B); mercy is his inexhaustible richness (M05-C); goodness is his exclusive quality (M05-D); gentleness is his mode of exercising power (M05-E); comfort is his self-identification (M05-F); fellowship is his Trinitarian inner life (M05-G). The cluster as a whole images a God of relational, gracious, generous, non-coercive engagement with his creation.

### T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**[A]** Love equips the person to be the relational image-bearer — the creature whose fundamental inner orientation is directed attachment toward God and neighbour. The great commandment (Mat 22:37 — Group 1542) names love as the vocation for which the person is constituted.

**[B]** Compassion equips the person to respond to suffering with a visceral inner movement that generates action — to be a source of relief for those who suffer. The compassionate hearts commanded at Col 3:12 (Group 1638) establish this as the vocation of the new humanity.

**[C]** Mercy equips the person to participate in the mercy-economy that structures the divine-human relationship — to be a recipient and conduit of mercy: "blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy" (Mat 5:7 — Groups 1629, 1631).

**[D]** Goodness equips the person to be a source of benefit toward others and toward creation — "created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand" (Eph 2:10 — Group 1098). Goodness is the vocation for which re-creation is purposive.

**[E]** Gentleness equips the person to engage difficult situations — correction, opposition, reception of God's word, witness under pressure — without becoming coercive or destructive. It equips the person to be a restorer (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104) and a witness (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262).

**[F]** Comfort equips the person to become a channel of the comfort they have received — "so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God" (2Cor 1:4 — Group 602). The purpose is the cascading of received comfort outward.

**[G]** Fellowship equips the person to participate in the shared life that the gospel creates — "so that you too may have fellowship with us; and indeed our fellowship is with the Father and his Son" (1Jo 1:3 — Group 1055). The purpose is vertical and horizontal participation simultaneously.

**[CLUSTER]** The cluster's characteristics collectively equip the person for the full range of relational engagement: in love (constitutive orientation), compassion (response to suffering), mercy (response to guilt/helplessness), goodness (moral expression), gentleness (response to provocation and conflict), comfort (response to grief), and fellowship (shared communal life). Together they constitute the relational equipment for life as an image-bearer in a world where suffering, guilt, conflict, grief, and broken fellowship are constant realities.

### T0.2.2 — Original design, response to fallen condition, or both?

**[A]** Both. The Song of Songs presents love in a creation-delight register without reference to sin. But chesed in its most evidenced form addresses human failure and covenant rupture — it persists through what the fallen condition produces.

**[B]** Primarily a response to the fallen condition. The trigger (encounter with suffering) presupposes post-fall realities. However, the divine attribute of compassion (Exo 34:6 — Group 1594) is constitutive rather than remedial.

**[C]** Response to the fallen condition. Mercy presupposes guilt and condemnation — its judicial frame requires a broken relationship that needs gracious override. No pre-fall mercy evidence appears.

**[D]** Both. Goodness as the purpose of re-creation (Eph 2:10 — Group 1098) implies a prior created design; Rom 7:18-19 (Group 1100) shows the fallen disruption of that design; Spirit-produced kindness (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112) is the restoration.

**[E]** Both likely, though the evidence is primarily post-fall in its explicit contexts. God's gentleness (Psa 18:35 — Group 1271) is constitutive; the meekness commanded in conflict contexts (Gal 6:1, 2Ti 2:24-25 — Groups 1104, 867) presupposes the fallen world.

**[F]** Primarily a response to the fallen condition. Grief, distress, and the need for comfort presuppose post-fall realities. The la.vav heart-stirring (Song 4:9 — Group 2741) may be an original-design dimension.

**[G]** Both. The Trinitarian fellowship (2Cor 13:14 — Group 1055) is constitutive; katallagē (Group 2069) is constitutively remedial — reconciliation presupposes estrangement.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called, and what does the name signal?

**[A]** "Love" — signals constitutive directed attachment; the name implies an object (love is always toward something) and a bond. It does not capture the full semantic range of chesed (covenantal loyalty), agapē (willed self-giving), or rachamim (compassionate yearning) but names the relational orientation they share.

**[B]** "Compassion" — signals a movement toward the suffering person; the com- prefix (with) implies relational entry. The name undersells the somatic depth of the ra.cham (womb) root and the bowel/visceral register of splanchnizō.

**[C]** "Mercy" — signals the response of authority toward guilt/helplessness; the name correctly captures the judicial-covenantal register that distinguishes mercy from compassion.

**[D]** "Kindness and Goodness" — compound name covering two registers: kindness (relational disposition of gracious goodwill) and goodness (moral quality of the inner person). The compound is apt because the evidence spans both poles without one subsuming the other.

**[E]** "Gentleness" — signals yielding non-coercive strength. The name undersells the power dimension — meekness in the evidence is restrained power, not the absence of power. The king who comes meekly (Mat 21:5 — Group 1261) has all royal authority.

**[F]** "Comfort and Encouragement" — compound covering grief-relief (comfort) and calling-forward (encouragement). The compound accurately represents the parakaleō vocabulary's semantic range across both registers.

**[G]** "Fellowship and Participation" — compound covering the relational bond (fellowship) and the active sharing-in (participation). The compound is apt — the koinōn- root family spans both the state and the act.

### T1.1.2 — What do the primary terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[A]** Hebrew chesed (covenantal loyalty/kindness), ahavah (intense personal love), rachamim (womb-derived compassion); Greek agapē (willed self-giving), phileō (natural affection), philia compounds (love directed by specified object). Together: love is a family with a clear centre (directed attachment) and multiple modes.

**[B]** Hebrew ra.cham/ra.cha.mim (womb-root — maternal inner movement), ra.chum (compassionate as divine attribute); Greek splanchnizō (bowel-located visceral stirring), oiktirmos/oiktirmōn (compassion as inner character quality), sumpathēs/sumpatheō (fellow-feeling). Together: compassion is constitutively somatic at its root.

**[C]** All Greek — eleos (mercy as covenantal-judicial favour), eleeō (to have mercy), eleēmōn (mercifulness as character). The exclusively Greek vocabulary (no Hebrew terms in M05-C groups) is structurally significant — mercy in this sub-group is framed by the NT judicial-covenantal vocabulary without the Hebrew root-family.

**[D]** Greek chrēstotēs/chrēstos/chrēsteuomai (kindness — what is useful/serviceable/fitting); agathos and compound family (goodness — what is morally excellent). Together: kindness as gracious goodwill; goodness as moral excellence — two poles of the same outward-oriented inner disposition.

**[E]** Greek praus/praotēs/prautēs (tamed strength); epieikeia/epieikēs (gracious reasonableness willing to yield); anexikakos (patient endurance without resentment); Hebrew a.na.vah/an.vah (humility/meekness before God). Together: the vocabulary names restrained power across multiple registers.

**[F]** Hebrew na.cham (breathing deeply in relief/regret — the emotional sigh of comfort); tan.chum (consolation as received gift); la.vav (captivating/stirring the heart); Greek parakaleō/paraklēsis (calling alongside). Together: comfort is constitutively directional toward the distressed person.

**[G]** Greek koinōnia (shared-life bond), koinōnos (participant/sharer), katallagē (restoration of broken bond), homofrōn (same-minded), isopsuchos (same-souled), epoikodomeō (building upon a foundation). Together: fellowship is the irreducibly communal characteristic — every term requires at least two parties.

**[B]** Primarily relational and directional — compassion flows from greater to lesser, triggered by encounter with suffering.

**[C]** Primarily relational and directional — mercy flows from the one with authority toward the one in helplessness or guilt.

**[F]** Directional and relational — comfort is always addressed toward a specific distressed person; the parakaleō root (calling alongside) is directional by definition.

**[G]** Constitutively relational — koinōnia cannot exist in a single person; every term requires the shared bond between parties.

**[F]** A compound of inner orientation (giver's movement toward the distressed) and inner experience (receiver's relief, cheer, heart-refreshment). Both sides are constitutive of the characteristic.

**[G]** A relational-constitutive condition — the state of shared participation in a common life. Koinōnia is something one "has" (1Jo 1:3 — Group 1055) — a state of shared inner belonging, not primarily an act or a feeling.

### T1.2.3 — Best working description of each characteristic?

**[A]** Love is the inner orientation of directed attachment — stable, covenantally constituted, constitutively relational, and constitutively directional (defined by its object). Primary location: the heart. Structural opposite: hatred and disordered-directedness.

**[B]** Compassion is the inner movement triggered by encounter with suffering — visceral-somatic in its primary register (womb/bowel vocabulary), generating action without deliberative gap. Primary location: the gut/bowel level of the inner being. Structural opposite: judicial withholding.

**[C]** Mercy is the relational-judicial disposition of the greater toward the lesser — operating in the frame where guilt, condemnation, or helplessness is the operative context, and where the mercy of the authoritative party is what determines the outcome. Primary location: the moral-judicial register of the person. Structural opposite: mercilessness as character failure.

**[D]** Kindness and goodness is the inner quality of gracious generous goodwill rooted in God's own character — constitutive moral excellence (goodness), relational disposition toward the other (kindness), ongoing practice under cost (doing good), and undivided giving orientation (haplotēs). Primary location: the heart. Structural opposite: evil/ponēros and severity/apotomia.

**[E]** Gentleness is the settled inner disposition of the heart — strength under control — that yields where it could assert, endures where it could retaliate, and receives rather than resists. Primary location: the heart ("hidden person of the heart" — 1Pe 3:4 — Group 1261). Structural opposite: quarrelsomeness and coercive self-assertion.

**[F]** Comfort and encouragement is the inner orientation directed specifically toward the distressed, grieving, or faltering person — bringing relief to inner grief, consolation as a sustained gift, and the calling-alongside that strengthens for continued living. Primary location: soul and heart level. Structural opposite: the absent comforter and the false comforter.

**[G]** Fellowship and participation is the inner-relational bond of shared life — holding things in common, participating together in a shared reality, belonging to one another through a mutually constitutive bond. Vertical (with God) and horizontal (with one another) simultaneously constituted. Primary location: spirit (Trinitarian ground), soul (isopsuchos), mind (homofrōn). Structural opposite: darkness and estrangement.

### T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not?

**[A]** Not merely sentiment (it is commanded — Mat 22:37); not natural affection only (agapē exceeds phileō in reach); not the same as compassion, mercy, or goodness though it overlaps with all.

**[B]** Not a static disposition (it is an inner movement — triggered, not permanent); not the same as love (love is the prior orientation; compassion is its activation under suffering); not the same as mercy (compassion is affective-somatic; mercy is judicial-relational).

**[C]** Not the same as compassion (compassion is triggered by suffering; mercy operates in the frame of guilt/authority); not an emotion (it is a disposition exercised in a judicial frame); not unconditional in its human economy (Mat 18:33 — Group 1630 — the mercy-received generates mercy-obligation).

**[D]** Not merely performance (goodness is a heart-treasury that produces; not external conduct without inner ground); not the same as love (love is the relational orientation; goodness is the moral quality of the inner person).

**[E]** Not weakness or passivity (the meek king has royal authority — Mat 21:5 — Group 1261); not indifference (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104 requires active engagement); not the same as patience/makrothumia (which appears alongside praotēs as a distinct quality in Eph 4:2 — Group 1104).

**[F]** Not the same as compassion (compassion is the inner stirring; comfort is the specific directed response to grief); not all parakaleō is comfort (Group 604-b — social requests — is parakaleō in a non-comfort register); not passive presence (the comforter actively moves toward the distressed person).

**[G]** Not mere proximity or social co-presence (1Jo 1:6 — Group 1055 names false fellowship where presence exists without inner reality); not the same as love (love is the relational orientation; fellowship is the shared-participation bond); not friendship (friendship is love-oriented attachment; fellowship is holding-in-common).

**[D]** Constitutive quality mode (agathos as what the person is — Group 1097); purposive mode (pronoeō — Group 2290); Spirit-production mode (chrēstotēs — Gal 5:22 — Group 1112); practice mode (agathopoieō — Group 1093).

**[A]** Yes — covenantal love operates differently in the God-to-human direction (initiatory, unconditional) versus the human-to-God direction (responsive, commanded) versus the human-to-human direction (costed, self-giving).

### T1.4.3 — Communicative or speech-based mode?

**[A]** Yes — love has a speech-based mode: the good heart produces good speech (Mat 12:34 — Group 1097); chrēstologia (counterfeit kindness-speech — context of Group 1469) reveals that the counterfeit exists, confirming that genuine love-speech is real. **[C]** Yes — the mercy cry (Group 1629) is a speech act; the sovereign declaration of mercy (Rom 9:15 — Group 1628) is a divine speech act; "mercy triumphs over judgment" (Jam 2:13 — Group 1633) is a wisdom-speech declaration.

**[D]** Yes — Eph 4:29 (Group 1098): "only such as is good for building up... that it may give grace to those who hear." Good speech flows from the good inner character. Mat 12:34 (Group 1097) links heart-goodness directly to speech.

**[E]** Yes — correction (2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104) and defence of hope (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262) are speech acts governed by gentleness. The communicative mode is secondary but real.

**[F]** Yes — prominently. **[G]** Yes — the gospel proclamation creates koinōnia (1Jo 1:3 — Group 1055); the message of reconciliation is entrusted as a speech act (2Cor 5:19 — Group 2069). Both the creation of fellowship and the restoration of broken fellowship are mediated through proclamation.

### T1.5.2 — Consistent immediate response?

**[A]** Consistent in direction (toward God, toward relief, toward responsive love) but varies by sub-register.

**[B]** The compassion-stirring itself is consistent — it is the characteristic's primary constitutive event.

**[C-G]** Generally consistent in direction: positive inner states (joy, relief, cheer, belonging) across all sub-groups where receiving-experience is evidenced.

**[CLUSTER]** The immediate responses across M05 consistently name positive inner movements — joy, relief, rest, cheer, heart-refreshment, responsive love — as the experiential consequence of receiving these characteristics. The one exception is compassion (M05-B), whose immediate response is the inner movement in the giver rather than in the receiver — compassion's immediate response is the stirring of the one who perceives suffering, not the relief of the one who suffers.

### T1.6.3 — Sustained effect differs from immediate response?

**[CLUSTER]** Yes, consistently across all sub-groups. The immediate responses are primarily affective and experiential (joy, relief, cheer, rest, belonging). **[B]** Need/suffering is the condition — compassion flows toward those who are suffering. The cry of distress (Judg 2:18 — Group 1742) triggers the divine compassion-movement.

**[C]** The mercy cry (Group 1629) — the petition that acknowledges helplessness and need. The inner posture of the tax collector (Luk 18:13 — Group 1629 context) is the clearest evidencing.

**[D]** Asking/seeking (Mat 7:11 — Group 1098; Luk 11:13 — Group 1099); honest and good heart (Luk 8:15 — Group 1097); the renewed mind that can discern the good (Rom 12:2 — Group 1098).

**[E]** Seeking the characteristic (1Ti 6:11 — Group 1261 — "pursue gentleness"; Zep 2:3 — Group 1272 — "seek humility"); fear of the Lord as companion orientation (Pro 22:4 — Group 1272).

**[F]** Mourning — Mat 5:4 (Group 602) — "blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted." The honest acknowledgement of inner grief is the enabling condition for receiving comfort.

**[G]** Walking in the light (1Jo 1:7 — Group 1055); faithful participation in the gospel (Phili 1:5 — Group 1056).

### T1.7.3 — Inner state of the non-receiver?

**[A]** The person who claims love but does not receive it remains in darkness and self-deception (1Jo 2:15 — Group 1565 context — the world-lover).

**[B]** The one whose compassion has been appropriately withheld (judicial register) is under judgment. The one who disobediently shows compassion where it should be withheld suffers moral culpability (1Sa 15:9 — Group 629-b).

**[C]** The merciless person who forfeits mercy (Jam 2:13 — Group 1626 — anileōs). The person who does not receive God's sovereign mercy is in the condition of hardness (Pharaoh — Group 1628 context).

**[D]** Hardness and continued unrepentance (Rom 2:4-5 — Group 1111 context). The person who encounters God's kindness without receiving it treats the kindness as ambient background rather than as the transformative address it is.

**[E]** Haughtiness and contentiousness — the self-asserting, quarrelsome person (Pro 18:12 — Group 1272; 2Ti 2:24 — Group 867 contrast).

**[F]** Sustained grief and desolation — Lam 1:16 (Group 1741): "a comforter is far from me, one to revive my spirit." The person who refuses comfort remains in the unreleved state.
