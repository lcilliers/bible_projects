## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — God's goodness is named as exclusive and constitutive: "No one is good except God alone" (Mar 10:18; Luk 18:19; Mat 19:17 — Group 1096). Goodness is not one attribute among many but the defining ontological quality from which all creaturely goodness is derivative. God's kindness (chrēstotēs) is similarly constitutive: "God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance" (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111), and it is inexhaustibly displayed — "immeasurable riches of his grace in kindness toward us in Christ Jesus" (Eph 2:7 — Group 1111). The divine character revealed here is gracious generative goodwill that is active, purposive, and transformative rather than passive benevolence. God gives good gifts to those who ask (Mat 7:11 — Group 1098); his providential works toward good for those who love him (Rom 8:28 — Group 1099); he does good by giving rains and fruitful seasons (Act 14:17 — Group 1092). The image is of a God whose innermost character overflows outward in goodness and kind generosity.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**E** — Yes, explicitly and emphatically. Mar 10:18 names goodness as God's exclusive attribute, which means every human instance of goodness is participatory rather than originary. The human person does not generate goodness autonomously; they draw from and reflect the single source. Rom 2:4 similarly names chrēstotēs as a divine disposition with purpose — kindness that leads. Eph 2:7 names it as displayed in Christ and directed toward humanity. The attribution reveals that goodness and kindness in the human person are image-expressions of what God is in himself; their presence in a person is evidence of what they are drawing from and being shaped by.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**S** — There is no silence on divine possession of this characteristic. Both goodness (agathos) and kindness (chrēstotēs) are explicitly attributed to God. This prompt does not apply.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**E** — The most explicit statement of created purpose is Eph 2:10: "we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand, that we should walk in them" (Group 1098). Goodness is not an optional achievement but the vocation for which the human person is constituted — it is the telos for which they are made and re-made. The good person produces good from the good treasure of the heart (Mat 12:35; Luk 6:45 — Group 1097): the characteristic equips the person to be a source and generator of good toward others. Kindness' purpose is explicitly transformative: God's kindness leads to repentance (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111) — the characteristic serves the formation of others, not merely the gratification of the giver.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**P** — The evidence is suggestive rather than explicit. Eph 2:10 ("created in Christ Jesus for good works") speaks of new creation, not the original; it names re-creation as the context for goodness' purpose. Rom 7:12-19 (Group 1100) reveals that goodness is what the person wills under the law but cannot perform in the flesh — this implies goodness as the original design's orientation, now frustrated by the fallen condition. The trajectory is: original design → fallen inability → new creation restoration. The kindness family (chrēstotēs) appears primarily as Spirit-produced (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112), suggesting that in its fullest form it belongs to the restored condition. The evidence does not resolve the question of original creation completely.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**E** — Yes. The eschatological dimension of goodness is explicit in the agathos verb compounds: "the good things to come" (Heb 10:1 — Group 1099) and "high priest of the good things that have come" (Heb 9:11 — Group 1099) name goodness as the eschatological content — what is coming and what has broken in through Christ. Phili 1:6 names the good work begun as completing toward "the day of Jesus Christ" (Group 1098). The eschatological orientation is not separable from the present characteristic; the good work is directed toward the day it will be completed and revealed.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?

**E** — The treasury metaphor (Mat 12:35; Luk 6:45 — Group 1097) is the most precise evidencing: the good person carries an inner deposit (the good treasure of the heart) that produces good from within. This is an image of the person as analogous to God — as one who generates and gives outward from an inner abundance of goodness. Act 11:24 ("he was a good man, full of the Holy Spirit and of faith" — Group 1097) confirms that goodness as an inner quality is inseparable from Spirit-fullness: the image is expressed through the Spirit's inhabitation. The image-bearing function of this characteristic is the capacity to be a source of good toward others — to extend, as God extends, outward from inner abundance.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?

**E** — Shared, but asymmetrically. Goodness is God's exclusive constitutive attribute (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096); human goodness is participatory and derivative. The human good person produces good from the good treasure of the heart (Group 1097), but this is not independent origination — it is the person drawing from and expressing what God has deposited. Eph 2:10 (created for good works prepared by God — Group 1098) makes the divine preparation explicit: even the good works are pre-formed by God. The sharing is real but not symmetrical.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**E** — The Rom 7 passage is the most diagnostic: "nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh" (Rom 7:18 — Group 1100) reveals that the absence of goodness is the defining mark of the fallen condition — not a peripheral failure but the core incapacity. Mat 12:34 ("how can you speak good, when you are evil? For out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks" — Group 1097) connects goodness directly to the inner condition: the absence of good speech reveals the absence of a good inner treasury. Rom 3:12 ("no one does good, not even one" — Group 1112) names universal goodness-absence as the mark of the fallen condition. The presence of goodness, conversely, is evidence of renewal — Act 11:24 (Barnabas, good man and full of Holy Spirit — Group 1097).

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**E** — Yes. The Eph 2:7 display of divine kindness "in Christ Jesus" (Group 1111) is typologically significant: Christ as the event in which God's kindness toward humanity is supremely enacted. The "good things that have come" and "good things to come" in Heb 9–10 (Group 1099) use agathos as the eschatological content — the Levitical system is a shadow of the agathos that Christ brings and that awaits consummation. Mat 11:30 ("my yoke is easy/kind" — Group 1469) uses chrēstos of Christ's character as the content of the yoke — the kindness of Christ as the mode of his governance.

**T0.4.2** — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology?

**E** — The direction is primarily divine-to-human: God's goodness and kindness are the pattern and source; the human expression is derivative and responsive. Eph 2:7 names divine kindness as the model displayed in Christ toward which human kindness is an echo. The "good things to come" in Hebrews run from the divine end (what God has prepared) toward the human end (what the worshipper receives). The typology establishes divine goodness as the original, creaturely goodness as the image, and Christ as the point where the two intersect.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.

Not applicable — typological use is evidenced above.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**E** — "Kindness and Goodness." The compound name signals that the sub-group holds two related but distinguishable orientations: kindness (the relational disposition of gracious goodwill toward another) and goodness (the moral quality of the inner person oriented toward what is right and beneficial). The compound name reflects the Greek evidence: chrēstotēs/chrēstos name the kind, gracious quality; agathos names the good, morally excellent quality. The name indicates that these are not identical — kindness is more relational, goodness more moral-constitutional — but they share an inner orientation toward the beneficial for others.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**E** — The Greek terms are primary for this sub-group (no Hebrew terms appear in M05-D groups). Chrēstotēs/chrēstos root carries the sense of what is useful, serviceable, and fitting — "what serves well." Agathos carries the sense of what is morally excellent and productive of benefit. The grammatical range is broad: agathos appears as adjective (the good person), noun (the good), and in compound verbs (agathopoieō — to do good; agathoergeō — to work good). Chrēstotēs/chrēstos appear as noun and adjective; chrēsteuomai as a verb (love is kind — 1Cor 13:4, Group 1467). Haplotēs (singleness/generosity) adds the undivided-openhandedness dimension. Pronoeō adds the purposive-intentionality dimension.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**E** — Directional and relational. Kindness (chrēstotēs) is explicitly directional: God's kindness is directed toward humanity (Eph 2:7 — Group 1111), toward repentance (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111). Goodness (agathos) is primarily constitutional (what the person is) but also directional in the good-works compounds: agathopoieō directs the person outward toward others even in adverse conditions (1Pe 2:20 — Group 1093). The characteristic as a whole is outward-facing — it is an inner quality that generates outward acts toward others.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**E** — A compound of disposition and quality. Goodness (agathos in Group 1097) is primarily an inner quality — "the good person out of his good treasure brings forth good" names a constitutive inner character that produces rather than merely performs. Kindness (chrēstotēs in Group 1112) is a Spirit-produced disposition — fruit of the Spirit (Gal 5:22), a quality put on deliberately (Col 3:12). The agatho- verb compounds (Groups 1092–1094) show goodness operating also as an ongoing disposition and practice, not only a character quality. Haplotēs (Groups 687–688) is an inner orientation of undivided generosity — a condition of the will. Pronoeō (Group 2290) is an intentional inner purposing. The sub-group is therefore a compound: inner character quality + Spirit-produced disposition + volitional orientation + purposive intentionality.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?

**E** — Complex in structure. The 16-group structure across 10 terms reveals at minimum four constituent registers: (1) kindness as divine attribute and Spirit-produced disposition (chrēstos/chrēstotēs family — Groups 1111–1112, 1469, 1467); (2) goodness as constitutive inner character quality producing good outward (agathos in Groups 1096–1100); (3) the practice of doing good as inner commitment maintained under suffering (agathopoieō/agathoergeō/agathopoiia/agathopoios — Groups 1092–1095); (4) singleness and generosity as the undivided inner orientation (haplotēs — Groups 687–688) and purposive care as intentional inner direction (pronoeō — Group 2290).

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

**E** — Kindness and goodness is the inner quality of gracious, generous goodwill rooted in God's own character — expressed as constitutive moral excellence (goodness), as relational disposition toward the other (kindness), as ongoing practice of good under cost (doing good), and as undivided generous orientation (haplotēs). Its primary constitutional location is the heart (Mat 12:35; Luk 8:15; 1Ti 1:5 — Groups 1097, 1097). It is Spirit-produced in its fullest form (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112) and is ontologically grounded in God's exclusive goodness (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096). The characteristic both constitutes the person (who they are) and generates their acts (what they do).

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**E** — Multiple structural opposites emerge from the evidence. For goodness (agathos), the primary opposite is evil/ponēros — named explicitly in Mat 12:35 ("evil person out of evil treasure" — Group 1097), Rom 12:21 ("do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good" — Group 1098). For kindness (chrēstotēs), the explicit opposite is severity/apotomia — named directly in Rom 11:22: "the kindness and the severity of God" (Group 1111). The Rom 7 conflict names the flesh as the internal structural opponent: "nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh" (Rom 7:18 — Group 1100). Rom 3:12 ("no one does good, not even one" — Group 1112) names the condition of total goodness-absence as the mark of fallen humanity.

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**E** — Goodness resists evil both passively (not bearing bad fruit — Mat 7:17 — Group 1097) and actively ("overcome evil with good" — Rom 12:21 — Group 1098). The agathopoieō verb family resists suffering-induced retreat: goodness persists in doing good even when beaten for it (1Pe 2:20 — Group 1093), even when it generates hostility (Luk 6:33 — Group 1093). Kindness resists severity and harshness (Rom 11:22 — Group 1111). Haplotēs in its sincerity register (Group 687) resists duplicity and divided allegiance (2Cor 11:3 — "led astray from sincere and pure devotion").

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**P** — Two distinctions are clear from the evidence. (1) Goodness is not mercy: the cross-sub-group verse Jam 3:17 names mercy and good fruits as adjacent but distinct qualities of wisdom (Groups 1093 and 1633). (2) Kindness (chrēstos) is not love: 1Cor 13:4 places chrēsteuomai as a mode of love ("love is kind") rather than love itself (Group 1467) — kindness is the expression of love's inner character rather than the same phenomenon. The evidence does not sharply resolve where goodness ends and related characteristics (faithfulness, righteousness) begin — these remain to be distinguished in cross-sub-group review.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**E** — Four distinct operational modes are evidenced. (1) **Constitutive mode**: goodness as what the person is at the inner level — the good treasure of the heart that produces good (Mat 12:35 — Group 1097). (2) **Purposive mode**: the person deliberately aims toward the honourable (pronoeō — Rom 12:17; 2Cor 8:21 — Group 2290). (3) **Fruit/production mode**: kindness and goodness are Spirit-produced — given rather than self-generated (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112; 2Cor 6:6 — Group 1112). (4) **Practice mode**: the agathopoieō compounds name ongoing doing-of-good as the characteristic's outward operation even under adverse conditions (1Pe 2:15, 2:20, 3:17 — Group 1093).

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**E** — Yes. God's kindness operates in a covenantal-judicial frame — its operation toward the unrepentant is transformative pressure (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111); its operation toward the fallen is comparison with severity (Rom 11:22 — Group 1111). Human kindness appears primarily in interpersonal and community registers — put on (Col 3:12 — Group 1112), expressed in community virtue (1Pe 3:8 — cross-sub-group). Goodness in the divine direction (God's agathos) is eschatological and providential (Rom 8:28; Heb 9–10 — Group 1099); goodness in the human direction is moral-constitutional (Groups 1096–1100) and practical-ethical (Groups 1093–1095).

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation?

**E** — Partial evidence. Eph 4:29 names good talk as the speech expression of goodness: "only such as is good for building up, as fits the occasion, that it may give grace to those who hear" (Group 1098) — the good inner character produces speech that builds up. Mat 12:34 explicitly connects good speech to a good heart ("out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks" — Group 1097). The speech-based mode is therefore grounded in the inner goodness of the person and flows outward through speech as one of its forms.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty?

**E** — Yes, centrally. Rom 12:21 ("overcome evil with good" — Group 1098) names a volitional act of goodness as resistance. Haplotēs' sincerity register (Group 687) engages volition as undivided commitment.

**T3.6.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**E** — The characteristic both requires and deepens volition. It requires volition because goodness under cost cannot occur without a volitional choice to persist (1Pe 2:20 — Group 1093; 1Pe 4:19 — Group 1094). It deepens volition by providing the inner conviction (good conscience — 1Ti 1:5 — Group 1097) that sustains the will. The Rom 7 passage (Group 1100) reveals that in the fallen condition, the will wills the good but cannot perform it — the characteristic exercises the will beyond what the fallen will can deliver, exposing the volitional limitation.

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Goodness is volitionally demanding — more so than compassion (which is triggered) or mercy (which is called upon). Goodness must be chosen and maintained under adverse conditions. The volitional faculty is the primary operative faculty through which goodness is practiced. This is confirmed by the quantity of imperatival goodness in the evidence: "do good," "hold fast to good," "overcome evil with good," "seek good" — the characteristic is commanded because it requires volitional commitment.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — What is the function of the primary term in its primary verse?

**E** — In Mar 10:18 (Group 1096 anchor: "no one is good except God alone"), agathos functions as a predicate adjective establishing ontological exclusivity — "good" names what only God is in himself. The term grounds the enquirer's assumption and redirects it: the question "Good Teacher, what must I do?" is examined through the exclusive attribution of goodness to God alone, which both relativises human goodness and establishes the divine standard.

**T7.2.2** — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence?

**E** — The evidence is distributed across multiple literary forms: wisdom-saying (Mat 12:35 — Group 1097), dominical saying (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096), epistolary virtue catalogue (Col 3:12 — Group 1112; Gal 5:22 — Group 1112), epistle of pastoral instruction (1Pe 2:15, 2:20, 3:17 — Group 1093), apostolic letter (Rom 2:4 — Group 1111; Rom 7:12-19 — Group 1100). The diversity of literary forms confirms that goodness and kindness are not restricted to a single theological register — they appear in dominical teaching, Pauline ethics, Petrine practical instruction, and Pauline theological argument.

**T7.2.3** — What is the logical structure of key arguments?

**E** — Rom 2:4 (Group 1111): Premise 1: God has shown kindness; Premise 2: that kindness has a purpose (leading to repentance); Conclusion: the person who does not repent in response is presuming on the kindness rather than receiving it. The argument is about the teleological structure of divine kindness — it is not ambient goodwill but purposive grace.

Rom 7:18-19 (Group 1100): Premise 1: nothing good dwells in the flesh; Premise 2: the person wills the good; Premise 3: but does not do the good; Conclusion: a power other than the person's will is operating in the flesh against the good. The argument diagnoses the structural inability of the fallen will to perform the good it wills.

**T7.2.4** — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence?

**E** — The primary settings are: ethical/formation (Col 3:12; Gal 5:22; 1Pe 2:15-20 — the character garments of the new humanity); anthropological-diagnostic (Rom 7 — the fallen condition's relation to goodness); theological-judicial (Rom 2:4 — God's kindness in the frame of judgment and repentance); eschatological (Heb 9-10; Phili 1:6; Group 1099). The dominant contextual settings are ethical-formation and anthropological-diagnostic — goodness is both the standard the person is measured by and the character the person is being formed toward.

**T7.2.5** — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic?

**E** — Yes: Mat 12:35 (Group 1097): "The good person out of his good treasure brings forth good" is the most compact and definitive statement of goodness as an inner-treasury phenomenon that produces outward good. It captures the constitutive inner quality, the productive movement outward, and the person-type language simultaneously. Runner-up: Mar 10:18 (Group 1096) for the divine grounding; Rom 2:4 (Group 1111) for the active-purposive divine kindness; Gal 5:22 (Group 1112) for the Spirit-production.

**T7.2.6** — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?

**E** — Mat 12:35 reveals the treasury structure of goodness: goodness is not a performance but a deposit. The person does not produce good by effort from nothing — they draw it from what is stored in them. This means goodness is prior to its expression — the inner treasury must exist before good can be brought forth. The treasury metaphor also implies that the treasury can be exhausted (bad treasure produces bad, not silence) — the inner character actively produces according to what it contains.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?

**E** — Virtue ethics (Aristotle and its contemporary descendants, including contemporary character-based moral psychology) is the most natural framework: agathos as an inner quality, agathopoios as a character-type, the good conscience as habituated inner moral awareness — all correspond to virtue ethics' concern with stable character dispositions that produce consistent good action. Positive psychology (character strengths tradition — VIA Character Strengths, Peterson and Seligman) also overlaps significantly with the kindness, goodness, and generosity dimensions.

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates the verse evidence, what does it reveal?

**E** — Virtue ethics illuminates the treasury metaphor (Mat 12:35 — Group 1097) by providing the concept of habituated character: the good person has practiced goodness until it is an inner deposit from which good action flows naturally. This coheres with Aristotle's account of virtue as acquired by practice until it becomes character. Positive psychology illuminates the generosity dimension of haplotēs (Groups 687–688): the finding that generosity is self-reinforcing (the enriched person is enriched to be generous — 2Cor 9:11 — Group 688) coheres with research on giving as both expressive and constitutive of flourishing.

**T7.3.3** — Where the verse evidence and human science diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

**E** — The sharpest divergence is in the origin of goodness. Virtue ethics (both ancient and modern) treats character as the person's own acquisition through practice — the person habituates themselves to goodness. The verse evidence makes goodness derivatively divine: "no one is good except God alone" (Mar 10:18 — Group 1096) and "created for good works that God prepared beforehand" (Eph 2:10 — Group 1098). Human virtue ethics has no structural place for the claim that all goodness is derivative from a single divine source and must be received before it can be practiced. The Spirit-production (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112) is also beyond the framework: kindness as an organic product of indwelling Spirit has no human-science analogue.

**T7.3.4** — Does the human science framework surface aspects the verse evidence has not addressed?

**E** — Virtue ethics raises the question of how the good character is formed — what role does practice play in the acquisition of the good treasury? The verse evidence names practice (agathopoieō — Group 1093), Spirit-production (Gal 5:22 — Group 1112), and divine pre-preparation (Eph 2:10 — Group 1098) as formation agents, but does not elaborate a full pedagogy of goodness-formation. The virtue tradition's account of habituation as the formation mechanism would benefit from being tested against the verse evidence more thoroughly — this is a gap for further investigation.
