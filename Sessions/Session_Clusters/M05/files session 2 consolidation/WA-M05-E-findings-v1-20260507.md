## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — Two explicit divine attributions of gentleness emerge from the evidence. (1) An.vah as a divine quality: "your gentleness made me great" (2Sa 22:36 / Psa 18:35 — Group 1271) attributes gentleness directly to God — a divine disposition of condescension toward the creature that produces greatness rather than crushing. (2) Praus/praotēs as a Christological attribute: "by the meekness and gentleness of Christ" (2Cor 10:1 — Groups 1102, 1104) names Christ's gentleness as the ground of Paul's pastoral appeal. The nature of God revealed here is one who holds all coercive power and chooses not to deploy it destructively — the divine character of yielding strength, of the greater stooping to benefit the lesser.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**E** — Yes. Psa 18:35 / 2Sa 22:36 (Group 1271): God's gentleness/condescension is the direct cause of the person's greatness. 2Cor 10:1 (Groups 1102, 1104): Christ's meekness and gentleness are named as the basis for apostolic appeal — not dominical power. The attribution reveals that gentleness in the human person images the divine mode of exercising authority and power: the person who is gentle does not mimic weakness but mimics the divine restraint that chooses not to impose itself coercively. This is a high-status characteristic — it requires power in order to restrain it.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**S** — Not applicable — divine attribution is present. The evidence is not silent.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**E** — Three purposes are evidenced. (1) **Reception of God's word**: "receive with meekness the implanted word" (Jam 1:21 — Group 1262) — gentleness equips the person to receive rather than resist what God speaks. (2) **Restoration of the fallen**: "restore him in a spirit of gentleness" (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104) — the person who is gentle can restore others without destroying them. (3) **Witness and defence**: "make a defence... with gentleness and respect" (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262) — gentleness equips the person for non-coercive witness. These purposes share a common structure: the person equipped with gentleness can engage difficult situations (resistant person, fallen brother, hostile questioner) without escalating them.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**P** — The evidence does not directly address the original-creation question for gentleness. The Hebrew terms (a.na.vah — Group 1272; an.vah — Group 1271) appear in OT contexts that presuppose human finitude before God (Pro 22:4: "humility and fear of the Lord" — pre-fall design or post-fall orientation is not specified). The NT contexts are overwhelmingly in a world of conflict, opposition, and sin — suggesting gentleness functions as a response to the fallen condition. However, the divine attribution (God's gentleness in Psa 18:35) pre-dates the fall in terms of the divine character — suggesting the characteristic is part of the created design insofar as God always had it.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**E** — Yes, though muted. Mat 5:5 ("the meek shall inherit the earth" — Group 1261) is an eschatological promise: the earth-inheritance is future and eschatological. Phili 4:5 ("let your reasonableness/epieikēs be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand" — Group 1103) explicitly grounds the public display of gentleness in the imminence of the Lord's return — the characteristic's visible practice is oriented toward the eschatological horizon. The meek's inheritance is not merely spiritual but the fullness of a restored creation.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image?

**E** — Gentleness expresses the divine image in the mode of power-that-yields. The divine pattern (God's gentleness making the person great — Group 1271; Christ's meekness as the mode of his approach — Groups 1102, 1104) establishes that the image-bearing expression of gentleness is the restraint of coercive power in favour of the other. The human person who is gentle enacts a version of what God does in stooping to the creature — not eliminating the difference in power but choosing not to weaponise it.

**T0.3.2** — Is this characteristic shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?

**E** — Shared. Psa 18:35 / 2Sa 22:36 (Group 1271) attribute an.vah to God; 2Cor 10:1 (Groups 1102, 1104) attributes praotēs and epieikeia to Christ. The human characteristic is therefore a genuine participation in a divine quality, not a compensatory creaturely substitute.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**E** — 1Pe 3:4 (Group 1261 anchor: "the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which in God's sight is very precious") names gentleness as a constitutive inner quality of deep worth before God. Its absence is implicitly the quarrelsome, coercive, self-asserting person — the one who imposes rather than yields. 2Ti 2:24 (Group 867: "the Lord's servant must not be quarrelsome but kind to everyone, patiently enduring evil") names quarrelsomeness as the explicit opposite — a mode of relation that refuses the gentle restraint.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?

**E** — "Gentleness." The name signals the disposition of yielding, non-coercive engagement — strength that does not assert itself destructively. The name is accurate to the evidence but slightly under-represents the power dimension: gentleness in the evidence is not softness but restrained power. The meek king on a donkey (Mat 21:5 — Group 1261) has all royal authority; the meekness is in the mode of approach, not in the absence of power.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary terms reveal at the definitional level?

**E** — Four Greek term clusters and two Hebrew term clusters span the sub-group. Praus/praotēs/prautēs: the Greek root carries the sense of tamed, domesticated strength — not wild but disciplined. Epieikeia/epieikēs: the sense of what is fitting, reasonable, equitable — the disposition willing to yield where strict enforcement would be technically justified. Anexikakos: patient endurance of evil without resentment — forbearance under provocation. A.na.vah/an.vah: humility/meekness before God — the disposition of the creature appropriately small before the Creator.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**E** — Primarily constitutional and relational. Constitutional: the characteristic is located "in the hidden person of the heart" (1Pe 3:4 — Group 1261) — it is a depth-level inner quality. Relational: it operates in the space between persons — restoration (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104), correction (2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104), witness (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262), communal life (Eph 4:2 — Group 1104). The characteristic is constituted as an inner quality that defines the person's mode of relational engagement.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is this characteristic?

**E** — A settled disposition of the inner person — not an act, not a condition, but a stable constitutional orientation. 1Pe 3:4 (Group 1261) is the definitional verse: "the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit." The terms "hidden person," "heart," and "imperishable" all signal depth and permanence — this is a settled inner character quality, not a momentary response.

**T1.2.2** — Is it simple or does it have constituent elements?

**E** — Compound. The nine terms cover distinguishable registers: (1) meekness as settled inner orientation (praus/praotēs/prautēs — Groups 1261, 1104, 1262); (2) gracious reasonableness — the disposition willing to yield rather than insist on rights (epieikeia/epieikēs — Groups 1102, 1103); (3) humble orientation before God (a.na.vah/an.vah — Groups 1272, 1271); (4) patient forbearance under provocation (anexikakos — Group 867). These are not identical; they are aspects of the same fundamental disposition — the inner quality of non-coercive, non-retaliatory, yielding strength.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description?

**E** — Gentleness is the settled inner disposition of the heart — strength under control — that yields where it could assert, endures where it could retaliate, and receives rather than resists. Its primary constitutional location is the heart (1Pe 3:4 — Group 1261). It is Christologically grounded (2Cor 10:1 — Groups 1102, 1104; Mat 21:5 — Group 1261), Spirit-produced (Gal 5:23 — Group 1104), and eschatologically secured (Mat 5:5 — Group 1261; Phili 4:5 — Group 1103). It is most evidenced in contexts of conflict, correction, suffering, and opposition — where the coercive alternative is available but refused.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite?

**E** — Quarrelsomeness (eristic/contentious spirit): 2Ti 2:24 (Group 867) directly pairs "the Lord's servant must not be quarrelsome" with "patiently enduring evil" — the explicit opposite of anexikakos is the contentious person who fights back. For epieikēs (Group 1103), the opposite is implied by 1Ti 3:3 ("not violent but gentle, not quarrelsome") — violence and quarrelling. For a.na.vah (Group 1272), the implicit opposite is the proud or haughty heart: Pro 18:12 explicitly pairs "a man's heart is haughty" before destruction with "humility comes before honour" — haughtiness is the structural opposite of humility/meekness.

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**E** — It excludes: coercion (the rod in 1Cor 4:21 — Group 1104, the alternative Paul refuses); harshness (the severity that epieikeia does not exercise); retaliation (what anexikakos absorbs — Group 867); self-assertion under pressure (what prautēs resists — Jam 1:21: not receiving the word with proud resistance — Group 1262). It explicitly resists the impulse to insist on legal rights where yielding would serve better (epieikeia — Group 1102).

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**E** — Gentleness is not weakness or passivity: Mat 21:5 (Group 1261) — the king who comes meekly has royal authority; 2Cor 10:1 (Groups 1102, 1104) — Paul who appeals by Christ's meekness is also "bold" in his authority. Gentleness is not indifference: Gal 6:1 ("restore him in a spirit of gentleness" — Group 1104) requires active engagement, not withdrawal. The boundary with forbearance/long-suffering (makrothumia) is proximate but distinct: makrothumia appears alongside praotēs in Col 3:12 (Group 1104) and Eph 4:2 (Group 1104) as a paired but separate quality — patience under delay, while gentleness is the mode of relational engagement.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**E** — Four operational modes are evidenced: (1) **Receptive mode**: receiving God's word without resistance (Jam 1:21 — Group 1262); (2) **Restorative mode**: restoring fallen persons without harshness (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104; 2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104); (3) **Defensive mode**: making a defence without aggression (1Pe 3:15 — Group 1262); (4) **Endurance mode**: patiently absorbing evil without resentment (anexikakos — 2Ti 2:24 — Group 867). Each mode names a situation where coercive or reactive alternatives are available and the characteristic chooses the non-coercive path.

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context?

**E** — Yes. Before God: a.na.vah is the humble orientation of the creature before the Creator (Pro 22:4 — Group 1272) — the vertical dimension. Toward fallen persons: praotēs in restoration contexts (Gal 6:1 — Group 1104; 2Ti 2:25 — Group 1104) — the corrective dimension. In community: epieikēs in communal virtue lists (Phili 4:5 — Group 1103; Jam 3:17 — Group 1103) — the civic/communal dimension. Under suffering and unjust treatment: epieikēs toward unjust masters (1Pe 2:18 — Group 1103) — the endurance dimension. The characteristic adapts its expression to the relational context while maintaining the same inner orientation.

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?

**E** — Partial. 2Ti 2:25 (Group 1104): "correcting his opponents with gentleness" — correction is a speech act governed by the inner quality of gentleness. 1Pe 3:15 (Group 1262): "make a defence... with gentleness and respect" — apologetic speech is shaped by gentleness. Eph 4:29 (Group 1098 — M05-D cross-reference) makes the connection of inner character to speech explicit for goodness; for gentleness the same pattern holds: the speech act expresses the inner quality rather than simply conveying information.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**E** — Honour and life: Pro 22:4 (Group 1272) — "the reward for humility and fear of the Lord is riches and honour and life." Greatness: Psa 18:35 (Group 1271) — sustained encounter with God's gentleness produces greatness in the person. Inheritance of the earth: Mat 5:5 (Group 1261) — the eschatological outcome of meekness is not inner peace alone but the inheritance of the earth. Repentance in opponents: 2Ti 2:25 (Group 1104) — "God may perhaps grant them repentance" in response to gentle correction. The sustained effect of gentleness is a paradox: the disposition that yields produces enduring outcomes — honour, inheritance, transformation of opponents.

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, or capacities does sustained possession establish?

**E** — Sustained gentleness establishes the capacity to receive increasing wisdom: Jam 3:13 (Group 1262) — "Who is wise and understanding among you? By his good conduct let him show his works in the meekness of wisdom." Wisdom and gentleness are constitutively linked — the wise person demonstrates their wisdom through meekness, not through assertive display. Sustained gentleness also establishes freedom from anxiety: Phili 4:5 (Group 1103) — "let your reasonableness be known to everyone. The Lord is at hand" — the eschatological grounding of epieikēs produces a non-anxious presence in the world.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**G** — Only partial immediate-response evidence available. What can be said: the immediate encounter with God's gentleness produces greatness (Group 1271); the sustained practice of gentleness produces honour, wisdom capacity, and the inheritance of the earth — these are clearly progressive rather than instantaneous.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty?

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Which human science framework is most useful?

**E** — Two frameworks are relevant. (1) Virtue ethics: gentleness as a stable character disposition requiring practice and acquired through habituation — the prau- family is classical virtue language. (2) Emotional regulation psychology: the characteristic requires the management of reactive emotional responses (anger, resentment, desire for retaliation) in favour of non-coercive alternatives — this is the domain of emotional regulation research (Gross, Linehan's dialectical behaviour therapy framework for distress tolerance). The epieikēs dimension (reasonableness, willingness to yield) has resonance with the literature on conflict de-escalation.

**T7.3.2** — Where the framework illuminates the evidence.

**E** — Emotional regulation illuminates anexikakos (Group 867): patient endurance of evil without resentment is the characterisation of a person who has regulated the reactive emotional response to wrong — not suppressing emotion but not allowing it to generate retaliation. Research on forgiveness and rumination is relevant here. Virtue ethics illuminates the pursuit of the characteristic (1Ti 6:11 — Group 1261; Zep 2:3 — Group 1272): the seeking and practice of gentleness as a virtue that deepens through habituation.

**T7.3.3** — Where the verse evidence and framework diverge.

**E** — The sharpest divergence is in the source and ground of the characteristic. Emotional regulation frameworks treat the capacity for non-reactive engagement as a skill developed through training and practice — self-generated through effort. The verse evidence grounds gentleness in the character of Christ (2Cor 10:1 — Groups 1102, 1104), in the Spirit's formation (Gal 5:23 — Group 1104), and in the eschatological conviction that the Lord is at hand and will vindicate (Phili 4:5 — Group 1103). The eschatological grounding — the person is gentle because they do not need to insist on their rights, because the Lord's return is the final vindication — has no structural equivalent in emotional regulation frameworks, which have no category for eschatologically-grounded non-reactivity.

**T7.3.4** — Does the framework surface aspects the evidence has not addressed?

**E** — Emotional regulation research surfaces the question of the internal process of gentleness — what happens inside the person between the provocation and the gentle response? The verse evidence shows the inner quality (the settled heart disposition — 1Pe 3:4 — Group 1261) and the outcome (non-retaliation, gentle correction), but does not describe the inner process of transforming provocation into gentle response. This is a gap for further investigation — the internal mechanism by which the settled heart disposition converts provocation into patience is not elaborated in the evidence.
