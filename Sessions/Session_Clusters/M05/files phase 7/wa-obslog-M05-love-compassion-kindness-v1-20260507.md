### M05-A — Love (500 verses, 475 G + 25 P)

*Observation:* The love vocabulary across 23 terms spans Genesis to Revelation with extraordinary density and breadth. What emerges from reading the full body is not a single uniform characteristic but a structured family with a clear centre and multiple directed expressions.

**Summary:** The love characteristic, as evidenced by M05-A's verses, is fundamentally the inner orientation of directed attachment — the person turned toward another with loyal, committed goodwill. The Hebrew centre is che.sed: a covenantal faithfulness that never ceases (Lam 3:22), that is inscribed on the heart (Pro 3:3), that God names as his defining attribute (Exo 34:6). The Greek centre is agapē: the love that is patient and kind (1 Cor 13:4), that God first showed in Christ (1 Joh 4:19), that is poured into hearts by the Spirit (Rom 5:5), and that binds the community (Joh 13:35). The philo- compound family names the specific relational directions of the same orientation: brotherly love (filadelfia), family affection (filostorgos), love of children (filoteknos), love of the stranger (filoxenia), love of what is good (filagathos). These are not peripheral varieties — they are the same inner orientation expressing itself through the specificity of the relationship. The person-types (cha.sid, a.hav) confirm that love is constitutive of character: the chasid is not someone who occasionally shows che.sed but someone whose inner life is structured by it. The disordered register — love of the world (filia in Jas 4:4), love of money (implied by its absence in afilarguros), Amnon's love that became hatred (2 Sam 13:15) — reveals that love operates as the inner mechanism of directedness and that its direction, not its mere presence, determines its moral quality. The structural opposite is not mere absence but active directional reversal: hatred is love running backwards. Constitutional location: the heart is named repeatedly as love's primary site (heart of Jacob longing for Absalom; Pro 3:3 inscription; the great commandment — with all your heart, soul, mind, strength).

### M05-B — Compassion (188 verses, 183 G + 5 P)

*Observation:* The compassion vocabulary is dominated by the divine register — God's compassion toward Israel in her suffering, exile, and need. The human compassion instances are fewer but analytically significant.

**Summary:** The compassion characteristic, as evidenced by M05-B's verses, is a visceral inward movement triggered by the encounter with suffering — a stirring that precedes and motivates the responding act. The somatic grounding is explicit: ra.cham is womb-derived (Isa 49:15 — "can a woman forget the nursing child?"), splanchnizō is bowel-located (Mat 9:36 — "he had compassion on them"), and the sumpathēs/sumpatheō family names the capacity to enter into and share another's experience (Heb 4:15; 1 Pet 3:8). What distinguishes compassion from love is its trigger — compassion is activated by the encounter with the need or suffering of the other, not simply by the relationship. Jesus's compassion in the gospels is consistently triggered by seeing crowds, disease, grief, or hunger. The OT divine compassion is similarly triggered by Israel's affliction, her cry, her exile. The judicial withholding of compassion (chus in Ezekiel's repeated "my eye shall not pity") reveals that compassion's presence is a deliberate restraint from automatic judgment — its removal is the activation of strict justice without mercy. The most acute instance is Lamentations: God has withdrawn compassion, and this withdrawal is itself described as an act (Lam 2:17, 3:43), showing that compassion is the default mode of relational engagement that must be actively suspended in judgment. The racham family names the womb-root of this disposition in God: he is constitutively one who is moved by the sight of his people's suffering — "he will again have compassion" (Mic 7:19) as the eschatological certainty. Constitutional location: compassion appears to operate at the gut-visceral level of the inner being — the splanchn- root is somatic, and even the Hebrew womb-image is somatic. But its activation produces soul-level responses (Joseph's heart yearning — 1 Ki 3:26) and issues in acts.

### M05-C — Mercy (60 verses, 59 G + 1 P)

*Observation:* M05-C's verses are overwhelmingly NT, with eleos appearing in Lukan and Pauline writings as a covenant-colouring word. The mercy cry (eleē me — have mercy on me) is a distinctive feature.

**Summary:** The mercy characteristic, as evidenced by M05-C's verses, is the compassionate disposition of the greater toward the lesser — operating within a covenantal-judicial frame where guilt or helplessness is the operative context. It is most visible as what is called upon in the mercy cry: Bartimaeus, the ten lepers, the Canaanite woman, the publican in the temple — all address Jesus with eleē me as a petition to the one with authority to grant clemency. This judicial-covenantal register distinguishes mercy from compassion: compassion is triggered by suffering; mercy operates in the frame of guilt, helplessness, or inequality. The beatitude "blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy" (Mat 5:7) identifies mercy as a reciprocal characteristic — the one who shows it participates in the mercy-logic that structures the divine-human relationship. Mat 18:33 makes this structural: having received mercy, the servant was obligated to show it. Mercy triumphs over judgment (Jas 2:13) — mercy is not the absence of judgment but its override in favour of the lesser party. The Lukan birth narrative uses eleos extensively (Luk 1:50, 54, 58, 72, 78) as the content of God's covenantal faithfulness in action — mercy as the form that divine chesed takes when it encounters human need and helplessness. The negative forms (aneleēmōn in Rom 1:31 as a mark of moral collapse; anileōs in Jas 2:13 as the judgment that awaits the unmerciful) confirm that mercilessness is a moral failure of the inner character, not merely a behavioural lapse. Eleeinos (pitiable: 1 Cor 15:19, Rev 3:17) names the condition that calls forth mercy, confirming that mercy's characteristic presupposes an asymmetry of condition between giver and receiver.

### M05-D — Kindness and Goodness (127 verses, 126 G + 1 P)

*Observation:* The agathos family is the most prolific term in M05-D, spanning OT and NT extensively. The chrēstos/chrēstotēs cluster is a tighter set but analytically dense.

**Summary:** The kindness and goodness characteristic, as evidenced by M05-D's verses, is the inner quality of gracious generous goodwill — oriented outward toward others and rooted constitutively in God's own character. God's kindness (chrēstotēs) is the agent that leads to repentance (Rom 2:4) — active and purposive, not passive; it is displayed in Christ (Eph 2:7) as the supreme expression of what God is constitutively like toward humanity. In the human person, kindness is Spirit-produced (Gal 5:22) — the fruit of the Spirit rather than a natural acquisition — and is put on as a deliberate character garment (Col 3:12). Goodness (agathos) is the most broadly evidenced characteristic in M05-D: the good person produces good from the good treasure of the heart (Mat 12:35; Luk 6:45); no one is good except God alone (Mar 10:18) — making divine goodness the ontological ground of creaturely goodness. The agatho- verb compounds confirm that goodness is not passive: agathopoieō (to do good) appears in conflict and suffering contexts — "it is better to suffer for doing good" (1 Pet 3:17), "do good to those who hate you" (Luk 6:33). Haplotēs (singleness/generosity) reveals that goodness has an undivided inner orientation — the openhanded person gives without reservation or hidden agenda (Rom 12:8; 2 Cor 8:2; 9:11). The kindness/goodness characteristic's structural opposite is cruelty/severity (apotomia alongside chrēstotēs in Rom 11:22) — revealing that kindness and harshness are two possible faces of the same relational power. Constitutional location: the heart is named as the treasury from which goodness flows (Mat 12:35) and the site of good conscience (1 Tim 1:5; Act 23:1).

### M05-E — Gentleness (30 verses)

*Observation:* M05-E is the smallest characteristic sub-group in verse count but has a highly cohesive set. The verses cluster around specific high-evidence contexts.

**Summary:** The gentleness characteristic, as evidenced by M05-E's verses, is the inner disposition of yielding, non-coercive, non-retaliatory strength — the power that chooses not to impose itself. The praus/praotēs family provides the clearest evidencing: the meek inherit the earth (Mat 5:5), the king comes meek on a donkey (Mat 21:5), Paul appeals by the meekness and gentleness of Christ (2 Cor 10:1), the Spirit-bearer restores the fallen in a spirit of gentleness (Gal 6:1). What the verses reveal is that gentleness operates precisely in contexts of potential force: correction (Gal 6:1; 2 Tim 2:25), response to opposition (1 Pet 3:15), reception of God's word (Jas 1:21), wisdom displayed in conduct (Jas 3:13). Gentleness is not weakness — the epieikeia family reveals its character as gracious reasonableness, the willingness to yield where legal strictness would prevail (Phili 4:5; Jas 3:17; Tit 3:2). God's gentleness is itself evidenced: "your gentleness made me great" (2 Sam 22:36; Psa 18:35) — the divine disposition of condescension toward the creature produces greatness in the recipient. The Hebrew an.vah/a.na.vah family confirms the OT grounding: humility before God and gentleness before others are a single disposition (Zep 2:3; Pro 15:33; 22:4). Anexikakos (patient endurance of evil without resentment) adds the relational-endurance dimension: the servant of the Lord is not quarrelsome but patient in evil-endurance (2 Tim 2:24). Structural opposite: harshness, coercion, insistence on legal rights. Constitutional location: 1 Pet 3:4 is definitive — "the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit" — placing gentleness explicitly at the heart level as constitutive inner character.

### M05-F — Comfort and Encouragement (206 verses, all G)

*Observation:* M05-F is large primarily because parakaleō has a very wide semantic range across NT usage. This creates an evidential challenge: many parakaleō instances are appeals/urgings rather than comfort proper.

**Summary:** The comfort and encouragement characteristic, as evidenced by M05-F's verses, is the inner orientation directed specifically toward the distressed, faltering, or grieving person — bringing relief, strengthening, and calling alongside. The Hebrew na.cham corpus is richest in grief and desolation contexts: Jacob refusing to be comforted after Joseph's disappearance (Gen 37:35); Rachel weeping with refusal of comfort (Jer 31:15); Lamentations' repeated lament "there is no one to comfort her" (Lam 1:2, 9, 16, 17, 21). The comfort that is absent from these scenes reveals what it is when present: a bringing of inner relief that changes the person's inner state from grief to something liveable. God's comfort is eschatological and unconditional: "I, I am he who comforts you" (Isa 51:12); "as one whom his mother comforts, so I will comfort you" (Isa 66:13) — the mother-image paralleling the father-image of compassion in Ps 103:13, but with the specific comfort orientation. The NT parakaleō family is wider: comfort in affliction (2 Cor 1:3-7, where the comforter's own experience of suffering is the credential for comforting others), exhortation toward faithful living (Acts 14:22; 1 Thes 4:1), mutual building-up in community (1 Thes 5:11). The 2 Cor 1:3-7 passage is the richest single evidencing: comfort flows from God through the suffering of the comforter into those who suffer, with a cascading, participatory structure. La.vav (Song 4:9) reveals the intimate register: the captivation of the heart through intimate presence. Tan.chum reveals the received form: consolations that cheer the soul amid inner anxiety (Psa 94:19). Structural opposite: the failed comforter (Job's "miserable comforters" — Job 16:2) and the absence of any comforter (Lamentations).

### M05-G — Fellowship and Participation (40 verses, 31 G + 9 P)

*Observation:* M05-G is a small but analytically distinctive sub-group. The koinōnia family is NT-only; katallagē is NT-only; homofrōn, isopsuchos, epoikodomeō are NT-only. The sub-group is entirely NT.

**Summary:** The fellowship and participation characteristic, as evidenced by M05-G's verses, is the inner-relational bond of shared life — holding something in common, participating together, belonging to one another in a shared reality. The koinōnia vocabulary establishes the semantic centre: Act 2:42's fellowship as the fundamental community pattern; 1 Cor 10:16's "participation in the blood/body of Christ" as the vertical-divine dimension; 2 Cor 13:14's "fellowship of the Holy Spirit" as the Trinitarian grounding; 1 Joh 1:3, 6-7 as the most explicit statement — fellowship with us, with the Father and the Son, and with one another as inseparable co-realities. The horizontal and vertical dimensions of koinōnia are not alternatives but simultaneous: walking in the light produces fellowship with one another and with God at the same moment (1 Joh 1:7). The koinōnos (participant) family names the person constituted by this shared participation: partner in suffering (2 Cor 1:7), partner in the gospel (Phile 17), partaker of divine nature (2 Pet 1:4). The most analytically significant verses are 2 Pet 1:4 — participation in divine nature as the inner transformation that the characteristic produces — and Mat 23:30 — the negative instance (not wanting to have been participants in the killing of the prophets) confirming that participation constitutes moral solidarity, not merely physical co-presence. Katallagē (reconciliation) evidences the restoration of the broken fellowship bond: 2 Cor 5:18-19 as the ministry of reconciliation being the outworking of God's act of not counting trespasses. Epoikodomeō confirms the formative dimension: the community is built up on its foundation, and this building-up is an ongoing participatory formation process (1 Cor 3:10-14; Eph 2:20). Structural opposite: exclusion, darkness, partnership with unrighteousness (2 Cor 6:14).
