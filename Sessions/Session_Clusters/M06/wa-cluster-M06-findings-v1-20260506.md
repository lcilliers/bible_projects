_T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected_

**T0.1.1** `[finding]` What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

The verses reveal that God himself is extensively the subject of hatred. Psa 11:5 locates it in his soul: "his soul hates the wicked and the one who loves violence." The pattern is consistent: God's hatred is a real inner-being disposition, located in his soul, always directed at wickedness and covenant violation — never arbitrary. This reveals that in God, hatred is the other side of his absolute commitment to righteousness. What he loves perfectly, he opposes perfectly. When the human person hates what God hates (Pro 8:13, Psa 97:10, Psa 139:21), they are aligning their inner being with God's own.

**T0.1.2** `[finding]` Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

Yes, extensively. The same term (sa.ne) used for human hatred is used for God's hatred across multiple verses. The explicit attribution reveals that hatred of evil is not inherently a disordered or creaturely characteristic — it belongs to God's inner life. When the human person hates what God hates, the verses treat this as alignment with God, not as moral failure. Righteous hatred is therefore not merely a permitted human response but a participation in the divine inner life.

**T0.1.3** `[finding]` Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

Scripture is not silent — God's hatred is extensively attested. This prompt does not apply to M06-A in the sense of silence. The positive attribution is the finding.

Psa 97:10 commands it: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" Psa 45:7 grounds the anointed king's authority in it: "you have loved righteousness and hated wickedness; therefore God your God has anointed you." The evidence shows that hatred of evil is not an incidental response but a necessary function of the properly ordered inner life — the negative pole of moral orientation that makes love of the good coherent. A person who loves righteousness without hating wickedness has an incomplete and unstable moral orientation.

Both. Yes. Psa 81:15: "those who hate the Lord would cringe toward him, and their fate would last forever" — hatred of God reaches its eschatological terminus in permanent reckoning. Constitutional implication: Lev 19:17 locates it "in your heart"; 2Sa 5:8 locates it "in David's soul." The characteristic is at home in both heart and soul.

_T1.2 — Kind_

**T1.2.1** `[finding]` What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

Primarily a **disposition** — a settled, durable inner orientation. Gen 37:5 shows hatred intensifying over time. Gen 27:41 shows sa.tam settling and persisting. Pro 10:12 treats sin.ah as a force that "stirs up strife" — acting from within as ongoing pressure. Also has a **condition** form (sa.ni — being hated as a lived condition) and an **act** form (the momentary triggering). The dominant kind across the corpus is disposition.

**T1.2.2** `[finding]` Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

Has constituent elements: (1) triggering perception of injury or injustice; (2) inner settlement of the hostile disposition; (3) surface manifestation in changed behaviour (inability to speak peacefully); (4) intensification with new provocations; (5) concealment or expression; (6) volitional organisation toward action.

**T1.2.3** `[finding]` What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

Hatred (M06-A) is a settled inner disposition of the person oriented against another person, quality, or reality — constitutionally located in the heart and soul, engaging the relational and volitional faculties, producing either sustained enmity (when directed at persons) or moral alignment (when directed at evil in the pattern of God's own hatred). It is inherently relational and directional, always requiring an object, intensifying when not resolved, and concealing itself when expression is dangerous.

_T1.3 — Boundary_

**T1.3.1** `[finding]` What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

Love (a.ha.vah). Pro 10:12: "hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offences." 2Sa 13:15: "the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her." Ecc 9:6: "their love and their hate...have already perished." The pairing is explicit and structural throughout. The opposite is not the absence of hatred but the positive disposition of love.

**T1.3.2** `[finding]` What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

In person-directed form (group 1601): peaceful speech (Gen 37:4 — "could not speak peacefully to him"), forgiveness and reconciliation, covenantal solidarity. In hatred of discipline (Pro 12:1, 15:10): growth, correction, and formation. The characteristic resists reconciliation — the verses show no pathway from hatred to restoration that does not first pass through the cessation of the hatred.

**T1.3.3** `[finding]` What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

Anger is not hatred. Hatred is settled; anger can be momentary. Gen 27:41 shows sa.tam (settled hatred) persisting after the heat of anger has passed — the grudge remains when the emotion dissipates. Deu 19:6 distinguishes judicial killing from hateful murder by the absence of prior hatred: "he had not hated his neighbour in the past." Contempt (M06-B) is adjacent: contempt is a worth-evaluation (treating as beneath regard), hatred is relational hostility (being against). One can despise without hating; one can hate without despising.

_T1.4 — Modes of Operation_

**T1.4.1** `[finding]` In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

Four modes: (1) **Concealment mode** — hatred operates within while the person presents a false exterior (Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips"; Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart"). (2) **Accumulation mode** — hatred intensifies when fed by new provocations (Gen 37:5, 8: "they hated him even more"). (3) **Settlement mode** — sa.tam: the hatred settles into a plan (Gen 27:41: Esau says internally "then I will kill my brother Jacob"). (4) **Expression mode** — hatred breaks through into visible behaviour when restraint fails (Eze 35:11: "the anger and envy that you showed because of your hatred against them").

**T1.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

Significantly. When directed at evil (group 550-NEW-02): hatred operates as moral discernment in expression — it stabilises the inner orientation toward truth and against falsehood. When directed at persons (group 1601): hatred operates as relational enmity — isolating, concealing, corrosive. When hatred of God (group 3596): hatred operates as a constitutive orientation that defines the person's entire covenantal standing. When communal-atmospheric (group 1199 — mas.te.mah): hatred operates as a shared inner climate that destroys collective spiritual capacity.

**T1.4.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

Yes. Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips" — speech as the instrument of concealment. Psa 109:3: "they encircle me with words of hate" — speech as the vehicle of hostile expression. Zec 8:17: "love no false oath, for all these things I hate" — God's hatred of false speech shows that covenant-faithful speech is what hatred of deception produces. The characteristic operates through speech in both its concealed (deceptive) and expressed (hostile) forms.

_T1.5 — Immediate Response_

**T1.5.1** `[finding]` What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

From the person who is hated: Psa 69:20: "reproaches have broken my heart, so that I am in despair. I looked for pity, but there was none." The immediate inner response to being hated is a shattering of the inner self — heart-breaking — followed by the cry for pity. Gen 37:4: the person who is hated finds that peaceful speech becomes impossible ("could not speak peacefully to him") — relational closure is immediate. From the one who receives a triggering injury and begins to hate: settlement of a hostile inner posture (Gen 37:4 — hatred immediate upon perceiving favouritism).

**T1.5.2** `[finding]` Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

Consistent in direction: hatred consistently produces rupture or closure. The specific form varies — acute (Gen 37:4 — immediate inability to speak peacefully) or sustained (Gen 27:41 — immediate internal murderous resolution) — but the pattern of inner breaking or inner organisation around a hostile intention is consistent.

**T1.5.3** `[finding]` Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

The process by which hatred takes hold internally — how it moves from triggering perception to settled disposition — is not described in the verses.

_T1.6 — Sustained Effect_

**T1.6.1** `[finding]` What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

In the hater: progressive narrowing of the inner life around the object of hatred. Pro 10:18: concealed hatred requires lies — the person becomes complicit in self-deception. Hos 9:7: accumulated communal hatred produces "the prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad" — the spiritual perceptive capacity is obliterated by the accumulated inner climate. In the hated: sustained hatred from others produces despair (Psa 69:20), fear of abandonment (Gen 50:15), and the chronic condition of disfavour (group 550-NEW-01 — the unloved wife across Deuteronomy and Genesis).

**T1.6.2** `[finding]` What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

Readiness for retributive action (Gen 50:15 — anticipated). Corruption of speech (Pro 10:18, 26:24). In the communal register (group 1199): spiritual blindness — the capacity for prophetic discernment destroyed.

_T1.7 — Conditions of Reception_

**T1.7.1** `[finding]` What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

Perceived injustice or loss (Gen 37:4 — brothers' perception of favouritism). Violated trust or honour (Gen 27:41 — perceived theft of blessing; 2Sa 13:22 — Amnon's violation of Tamar). For hatred of God: moral and spiritual drift (Hos 9:15 — "there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds").

**T1.7.2** `[finding]` What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

Love as the primary blocking condition: Pro 10:12 — "love covers all offences" — what could become the seed of hatred is covered rather than nursed. Lev 19:17's command to frank confrontation prevents the inner settlement of hatred by keeping the relational channel open.

**T1.7.3** `[finding]` What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

Job 31:29: "if I have rejoiced at the ruin of him who hated me" — the righteous person does not move into reciprocal hatred when hated. Exo 23:5: "if you see the donkey of one who hates you lying down under its burden, you shall rescue it with him" — the command assumes it is possible to act for the benefit of the person who hates you without reciprocating. The inner state is not described directly but implied: the person maintains a different inner-being orientation — grounded in something beyond the relational exchange — that enables acting for the enemy's good.

_T1.8 — Dimension Classification_

**T1.8.1** `[finding]` What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**Relational Disposition.** Hatred is constitutively relational — it always requires an object, always involves the positioning of the self against another. Every group in M06-A involves a relationship: person against person, spouse against spouse, person against evil (in alignment with God's relational stance), God's relational stance toward sin.

**T1.8.2** `[finding]` What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

Every group in M06-A involves a relationship: person against person (1601), spouse against spouse (550-NEW-01), person against evil in alignment with God (550-NEW-02), God's relational stance toward sin (3595, 3596).

**T1.8.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

Secondary: **Moral Character** (groups 550-NEW-02 and 3595 — hatred as moral quality and divine inner-being reality) and **Emotion — Negative** (the affective quality is present throughout, particularly in group 1200's sa.tam and 2Sa 13:15's "very great hatred").

#### T2

_T2.2 — Soul-Level Location_

**T2.2.1** `[finding]` Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?

Yes, explicitly. Psa 11:5: "his soul (nephesh) hates the wicked and the one who loves violence" — God's hatred located in the soul. 2Sa 5:8: "hated by David's soul (nephesh)" — David's hatred is located in his soul. Isa 1:14: "my soul (nephesh) hates" — God's hatred again explicitly soul-located. The soul-location is direct and recurring.

**T2.2.2** `[finding]` What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?

The soul (nephesh) in these verses is the seat of the person's deepest personal experience — it is where the person's most fundamental orientations are located. When Psa 11:5 says God's soul hates, it names hatred as a disposition of God's innermost personal being, not merely a judicial decision or external stance. When David's soul hates (2Sa 5:8), the hatred is not merely a social attitude but an inner-personal reality. Soul-level location makes hatred a characteristic of the person's deepest self — which is why hatred in the heart (Lev 19:17) is treated as a sin even when it never produces an act.

_T2.3 — Heart_

**T2.3.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?

Yes, explicitly and commandingly. Lev 19:17: "you shall not hate your brother in your heart (levav)" — the heart is named as the constitutional location of hatred in the foundational prohibitory verse. Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart (levav)" — hatred concealed in the heart while lips present otherwise. Zec 8:17: "do not devise evil in your hearts (levav) against one another" — the heart as where hostile schemes originate.

**T2.3.2** `[finding]` What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?

The heart in Lev 19:17 is the integrating centre where intention, planning, and relational orientation converge. The prohibition "not in your heart" is precise: hatred at the heart level is the problem, even before any act. This means hatred is culpable as an inner state, not only as a behaviour. Pro 26:24 shows the heart as the hiding place — hatred can be active in the heart while the lips present false peace, revealing that the heart is where the real disposition lives, prior to and independent of speech or action. The heart's hatred is the primary moral problem; the act is its secondary expression.

_T2.4 — Mind_

**T2.4.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?

Not directly. No M06-A verse explicitly names the mind as the constitutional location of hatred. However, group 1199 (mas.te.mah, Hos 9:7): the accumulated communal hatred produces "the prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad" — the characteristic has destroyed the community's cognitive-prophetic capacity. Psa 36:2: "he flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated" — the mind's self-perception has been distorted by the hatred-avoidance.

**T2.4.3** `[finding]` If the evidence is silent on mind-location, note this explicitly.

Direct mind-location is not evidenced for hatred (M06-A), abhorrence (M06-C), or malice (M06-G). Cognitive effects of these characteristics are evidenced as consequences but not as constitutional locations.

### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

_T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets_

**T2.5.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?

The volitional sub-dimension of the soul. Gen 27:41: Esau's hatred produces a resolution — "then I will kill my brother Jacob" — the volitional resolution is explicit and internal. The settled hatred has organised the volitional capacity around a future act. The hatred does not merely exist as feeling; it has become an intention. The will as a soul-subset is engaged by hatred — hatred recruits the will into a hostile plan.

**T2.5.2** `[finding]` If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?

The will as the soul-subset engaged by settled hatred reveals that hatred is not merely a passive state — it actively recruits the volitional faculty. The movement from inner hostile disposition to volitional resolution (Gen 27:41: "then I will kill") shows that hatred does not remain inert but presses toward action. This is why Lev 19:17's prohibition of hatred "in the heart" addresses not only an emotion but a volitional orientation.

_T2.6 — Body — Significance_

**T2.6.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?

Several body-part links. Job 16:9 (sa.tam context): "he has gnashed his teeth at me" — the teeth as expressive instrument of the inner hostile state. The hand appears frequently as the instrument through which hatred's expression operates violently: 2Sa 22:18 — "those who hated me, for they were too mighty for me" — the strength of the haters' hands is what overwhelms. The body-part links in M06-A are moderate and indirect.

**T2.6.2** `[finding]` If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?

The gnashing of teeth (Job 16:9) is expressive — it names the body's involuntary participation in the inner hostile state. It shows that hatred, at its most intense, cannot be fully contained internally but erupts into somatic expression. The inner disposition erupts outward through the body before it has been consciously planned.

_T2.7 — Body — Direction_

**T2.7.1** `[finding]` Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?

Soul → body. The inner disposition of hatred produces somatic expression (gnashing of teeth, violent hands). No evidence in M06-A of the body feeding hatred back into the soul — the verses consistently show the inner disposition driving outward expression.

**T2.7.2** `[finding]` What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?

The consistent soul-to-body direction confirms that M06 characteristics are constitutionally inner-being phenomena that express outward — not behavioural patterns that create inner states. This is the methodological ground for the programme's approach: the inner state is the primary moral concern, and the behaviour is its secondary expression. Addressing the behaviour without addressing the inner state leaves the characteristic intact (as Lev 19:17's prohibition of inner hatred demonstrates — it targets the root, not the fruit).

_T2.8 — Body — Deposit_

**T2.8.1** `[finding]` Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?

Yes. Exo 20:5 and Deu 5:9 (group 3596, anchor Exo 20:5): "visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation of those who hate me." The sustained inner disposition of hating God produces a consequence that extends beyond the individual into the generations — not merely as social inheritance but as covenantal consequence. The iniquity is "visited," meaning God himself acts on the generational connection. The deposit is both sociological (children raised in a household of God-hatred) and covenantal-theological (the covenant holds the generations together under the consequence of the ancestors' inner orientation).

**T2.8.2** `[finding]` What evidence supports or contradicts this?

Exo 20:5 and Deu 5:9 are structural — the generational consequence is named as a constitutive element of the divine covenant, not an incidental observation. The pairing of "those who hate me" with the generational consequence appears in both the Exodus and Deuteronomy Decalogue texts, confirming its structural weight. Psa 105:25 provides a second dimension: "He turned their hearts to hate his people" — God redirected Egypt's inner orientation toward hatred of Israel as part of his purposes, and the subsequent Pharaoh was formed in a social and inner environment shaped by that generational hostility.

_T2.9 — Origin and Source_

**T2.9.1** `[finding]` Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?

Multiple origins: (1) **From perceived injury or injustice** — Gen 37:4 (brothers' jealousy of father's favour → hatred of Joseph); Gen 27:41 (perceived theft of blessing → Esau's hatred); 2Sa 13:22 (Amnon's violation of Tamar → Absalom's hatred). (2) **From sustained moral corruption** — Hos 9:15 ("there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds") — divine hatred originates in the accumulation of covenant violation. (3) **From divine action** — Psa 105:25 ("He turned their hearts to hate his people") — hatred can originate from outside the person through divine action, introduced into the heart for his purposes. (4) **From righteous formation** — group 550-NEW-02: righteous hatred of evil originates from the person's love of God and alignment with his character (Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil").

**T2.9.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?

Multiple — four distinct origin-paths evidenced (perceived injury, moral corruption, divine action, righteous formation). The origin determines the direction and moral quality of the hatred.

**T2.9.3** `[finding]` Does the origin of this characteristic change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?

Yes — the same term (sa.ne) names hatred originating from jealousy (Gen 37:4), from violated honour (Gen 27:41), from moral alignment with God (Pro 8:13), and from divine action (Psa 105:25). The term carries no origin-information; the context supplies it entirely.

_T2.10 — Constitutional Movement_

**T2.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?

Evidence of movement within the inner being: Pro 5:12 — "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof" — the hatred at the heart level produced a despising of reproof, which is a moral evaluation faculty operation. The hatred at heart level activated a contempt at the evaluation faculty level. The movement is not across body-soul-spirit levels but within the soul from disposition to evaluation and then to volitional intention (Gen 27:41: settled hatred → murderous intention).

**T2.10.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?

The pattern visible from the verses: perceived injury → inner settlement (heart) → volitional organisation (plan/intention) → concealment in speech → eventual expression or action. Pro 26:24-26 shows the full internal sequence most clearly: hatred active in the heart (v.24), concealed in speech (v.24-25), eventually exposed by the assembly (v.26).

#### T3

_T3.1 — Perception_

**T3.1.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?

The perceptive faculty is engaged at the point where hatred enters the inner life. Gen 37:4: "when his brothers *saw* that their father loved him more than all his brothers, they hated him" — the perception of favouritism is the trigger. Each new perception (the dream, the words — Gen 37:5, 8) intensifies the hatred. The perception does not merely register facts; it reads relational signals and feeds them to the disposition. In group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred): Psa 119:104 — "through your precepts I get understanding; therefore I hate every false way" — perception of truth actively generates hatred of its opposite. Understanding of what is true makes the encounter with falsehood more immediately and viscerally intolerable.

**T3.1.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?

Impairs in the disordered register — Psa 36:2: hatred-avoidance distorts self-perception; group 1199's mas.te.mah destroys prophetic perception entirely. Enables and sharpens in the righteous register — righteous hatred sharpens perception of moral reality (Psa 119:104).

_T3.2 — Cognition_

**T3.2.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

In the righteous register: Psa 119:104 — "through your precepts I get understanding; therefore I hate every false way" — cognition (understanding through the precepts) produces directed hatred. The cognitive sequence runs: learning → understanding → hatred of the false. In the disordered register: Pro 26:24 — "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart" — cognition is recruited into the service of the hidden hatred, managing the concealment.

**T3.2.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?

Righteous register: deepens and clarifies — hatred of falsehood is the product of sharpened understanding. Disordered register: corrupts — cognition is bent to produce false communication and manage the concealment of hatred.

_T3.3 — Memory_

**T3.3.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?

Memory is central to the persistence of hatred, particularly in group 1200 (sa.tam — grudge-bearing). Gen 27:41: Esau remembers the blessing theft. Gen 50:15: Joseph's brothers remember what they did to him — and fear he remembers it too. The grudge lives in memory; without memory it cannot persist. Sa.tam is essentially hatred preserved in memory across time — the grudge is the memory of the wound kept alive in the inner being.

**T3.3.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?

Deepens in a specific direction: the hater's memory of the injury or perceived wrong is preserved and returned to repeatedly. The characteristic selectively preserves injury-memory while suppressing reconciling-memory. Gen 50:15 shows the brothers assuming Joseph has been doing the same — holding the memory of what they did to him.

**T3.3.3** `[finding]` What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

Hatred is a memory-organising characteristic — specifically in its sa.tam (grudge-bearing) form. It preserves the memory of injury and supplies it as justification for the ongoing disposition.

_T3.4 — Affect_

**T3.4.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?

Yes, substantially. 2Sa 13:15: "Amnon hated her with very great hatred" — the intensity qualifier "very great" names the affective weight. Psa 25:19: "with what violent hatred they hate me" — violent names the affective character. Job 16:9 (sa.tam): "he has gnashed his teeth at me" — the somatic expression signals the affective intensity beneath. Ecc 9:6: hatred is listed alongside love and envy as one of the constitutive affective orientations of the living person. The affective dimension is present across all groups, though it varies in intensity.

**T3.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?

In the concealment register: impairs honest affective expression — the person must suppress the true affective state and perform its opposite (false peace). In group 1200 (sa.tam): transforms affect from acute anger into sustained smouldering state — the heat of the moment becomes the cold persistence of the grudge.

_T3.5 — Creativity_

**T3.5.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?

Yes, in the disordered register. Gen 27:41: Esau's hatred generates a plan — "then I will kill my brother Jacob" — the hatred has recruited imagination to construct a future scenario. Psa 41:7: "all who hate me whisper together about me; they imagine the worst for me" — the verb names a cognitive-creative act of projecting negative futures for the hated person. Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips" — the concealment requires creative construction of a false persona.

**T3.5.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity in the person?

In the disordered register: distorts — channels the creative capacity into destructive planning and deceptive self-presentation. In the righteous register: no evidence of creativity being engaged; righteous hatred is more receptive (downstream of understanding) than creative.

_T3.6 — Volition_

**T3.6.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

Deeply. Gen 27:41: Esau decides internally "then I will kill my brother Jacob" — the volitional resolution is explicit and internal. Num 35:20: "if he pushed him out of hatred or hurled something at him, lying in wait" — the "lying in wait" names a volitional act of prepared hostility. In the righteous register: Psa 26:5 — "I hate the assembly of evildoers, and I will not sit with the wicked" — the volitional "I will not" arises from and expresses the righteous hatred.

**T3.6.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?

Both enables (toward hostile action in the disordered register; toward righteous refusal in the ordered register) and constrains (righteous hatred closes off volitional options — "I will not sit with the wicked," Psa 26:5).

_T3.7 — Agency_

**T3.7.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

Yes. Hatred is named as the inner ground from which hostile action originates. Num 35:20: "if he pushed him out of hatred or hurled something at him" — the hatred is the legal criterion that distinguishes murder from manslaughter. The act of pushing or hurling is the agency expression; the hatred is the inner-being ground that makes it murder. Gen 27:41: Esau's murder plan is the agency expression of his sa.tam.

**T3.7.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?

Enables and directs agency toward harmful ends in person-directed hatred; toward just ends in righteous hatred (Amos 5:15: "hate evil, and love good, and establish justice in the gate").

_T3.8 — Moral Evaluation_

**T3.8.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?

This is the faculty most centrally engaged in the righteous register. Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil" — the moral evaluation of evil as evil is what produces the hatred. Psa 45:7: "you have loved righteousness and hated wickedness" — the moral evaluation of righteousness (as worthy of love) and wickedness (as worthy of hatred) is the operative faculty. In the disordered register: hatred bypasses or corrupts moral evaluation of the hated person (the hater cannot evaluate the hated person fairly because the hostile disposition prejudices the evaluation). Psa 36:2: the wicked "flatters himself in his own eyes" — his moral self-evaluation is corrupted by a related avoidance of having his iniquity "found out and hated."

**T3.8.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?

Righteous register: deepens and sharpens. Disordered register: corrupts — prevents fair evaluation of the hated person.

_T3.9 — Conscience_

**T3.9.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?

Psa 36:2: the wicked flatters himself that his iniquity cannot "be found out and hated" — the conscience (the faculty that would find out and hate iniquity) is suppressed or evaded. Pro 5:12: "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof!" — hatred of discipline is a suppression of the conscience's external channel. In the righteous register: the person who hates evil (group 550-NEW-02) has a functioning conscience that produces sustained opposition to what violates moral truth.

**T3.9.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?

Righteous hatred deepens and sharpens conscience. Disordered hatred suppresses conscience (hatred of discipline = refusing the conscience's external channel).

_T3.10 — Conscientiousness_

**T3.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?

In the righteous register (group 550-NEW-02): Exo 18:21 — men who "hate a bribe" are named as qualified for leadership. The hatred of bribery is not merely a moral attitude but a reliable predictor of conscientious conduct. The integrated response is: moral awareness (bribery is wrong) + volitional commitment (I hate it) + action (therefore I will not take it). Amo 5:15: "hate evil, and love good, and establish justice in the gate" — the commanded hatred of evil is the inner spring of conscientious public conduct.

**T3.10.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?

Righteous hatred enables and sustains conscientiousness — the person who hates bribes (Exo 18:21) is reliably conscientious in contexts where bribery is possible. Disordered hatred produces performed conscientiousness that eventually breaks (Pro 26:26: "his wickedness will be exposed in the assembly").

_T3.11 — Relational Capacity_

**T3.11.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?

Most comprehensively affected faculty. Gen 37:4: "they hated him and could not speak peacefully to him" — the direct consequence of hatred is the loss of peaceful speech, which is the basic instrument of relational connection. Pro 15:17: "a fattened ox and hatred with it" — the presence of hatred in a household makes even abundance miserable. The relational capacity, when organised by hatred, is not destroyed but inverted — the capacity for connection remains but is directed against rather than toward the other.

**T3.11.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?

Impairs deeply in person-directed register; the righteous register (550-NEW-02) may enable genuine relational capacity toward the righteous by clarifying who shares one's moral orientation.

#### T4

_T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human_

**T4.1.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?

Yes. God's hatred is directed toward the human person in two registers: (1) toward wickedness and those whose inner disposition is hatred of him (groups 3595, 3596 — 33 verses). Psa 5:5: "you hate all evildoers." Psa 11:5: "his soul hates the wicked." Deu 7:10: "repays to their face those who hate him, by destroying them." (2) The divine-to-human movement is consistently a response to what the human has done or become. Hos 9:15: "there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds" — the divine hatred is responsive to human choices.

**T4.1.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

God's hatred is consistently responsive and morally grounded — never arbitrary. Hos 9:15: "because of the wickedness of their deeds." Jer 12:8: "she has lifted up her voice against me; therefore I hate her." Every instance of divine hatred in M06-A has an explicit moral grounding.

**T4.1.3** `[finding]` What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

God's hatred of evil and of those who persistently choose it reveals the depth and seriousness of his moral commitments. He is not indifferent to wickedness — his inner being (nephesh) is set against it. This means that when God acts in judgment against evil, he is not merely applying an external rule but expressing an inner disposition. Psa 5:5-6 shows the dual face: God hates evildoers and abhors the bloodthirsty — this is paired with God's grace toward those who seek him (v.7). The hatred and the welcome exist in the same God, directed toward different objects.

_T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God_

**T4.2.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?

Two directions. (1) Human hatred of God (group 3596 — 14 verses): "those who hate me" (Exo 20:5) — hating God is the defining inner orientation of those in covenant rupture. (2) Human hatred of evil as covenant alignment (group 550-NEW-02): Psa 97:10: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" — hating what God hates is an act of covenant participation. These are opposite expressions of the same relational orientation toward God.

**T4.2.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

For righteous hatred (alignment): the fear of the Lord as the prior orienting posture (Pro 8:13). For hatred of God (rupture): the person has turned against God in their innermost orientation — no specific prior posture is named, but the sustained covenant violation that produces it is documented.

**T4.2.3** `[finding]` What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?

Names a state of total covenantal rupture. Psa 81:15: "those who hate the Lord would cringe toward him, and their fate would last forever" — the hatred has become so settled that even eventual forced acknowledgment cannot restore what has been lost.

_T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving_

**T4.3.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?

Yes, extensively in groups 1601 and 1200. Hatred is extended as an ongoing orientation shaping all interaction: Gen 37:4 — brothers give hatred to Joseph through their inability to speak peacefully. 2Sa 13:22 — Absalom gives his settled hatred to Amnon through years of silent enmity before acting. Pro 26:24 — the hater gives hatred through disguised speech. The giving of person-directed hatred is not typically a discrete act but an ongoing orientation.

**T4.3.2** `[finding]` What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?

An unresolved perceived injury, loss, or violation that has not been covered (Pro 10:12: love covers offences; hatred keeps them open). Sa.tam (group 1200) requires the wound's memory to be actively preserved as the fuel.

**T4.3.3** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?

The person must have received (or perceived) an injury that they have not released. In the righteous register: the person must have received the instruction that enables accurate moral evaluation (Psa 119:104).

_T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving_

**T4.4.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?

Yes — the receiving of hatred is documented across many verses. Gen 29:31: "Leah was hated" — the condition of being hated named as a state she inhabits. The receiving of hatred is experienced as a relational condition — not just a series of hostile acts but a state the person is placed in.

**T4.4.2** `[finding]` What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?

For the recipient of hatred: the absence of divine advocacy intensifies the damage. The presence of a relationship with God enables bearing it without reciprocating.

**T4.4.3** `[finding]` What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?

Job 31:29: "if I have rejoiced at the ruin of him who hated me" — the righteous person does not enter into reciprocal hatred. Exo 23:5: the command to rescue the enemy's donkey assumes it is possible to act for the benefit of the person who hates you without reciprocating the hatred.

**T4.5.1** `[finding]` Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?

Yes — the distinction is stark. The most intense and damaging hatred in M06-A operates within existing bonds: brothers hating a brother (Joseph's brothers), son hating father's heir (Esau/Jacob), husband withdrawing love from wife (group 550-NEW-01), Amnon's post-violation hatred of Tamar. Thin. Job 16:9 (sa.tam) attributes hatred/grudge-bearing to God in a lament context ("he has torn me in his wrath and hated me; he has gnashed his teeth at me; my adversary sharpens his eyes against me") — Job's language moves between human and divine adversary, and the adversary figure is significant. Psa 105:25 is the strongest pointer: "He turned their hearts to hate his people" — God used the Egyptians' hatred as an instrument of his purposes. #### T5

_T5.1 — Nature of Transformation_

**T5.1.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?

Both. In the disordered register: hatred transforms the person's condition by progressively narrowing the inner life around the object of hatred. Gen 37:4-8 shows the transformation unfolding in steps — each provocation deepens the hatred ("they hated him even more"), and what begins as a response to perceived favouritism settles into an inner organising principle that makes peaceful speech impossible and leads eventually to the decision to sell Joseph into slavery. The person has not merely acquired an attitude; they have been reorganised differently inside. In the righteous register: hatred of evil transforms the person's orientation toward the entire moral landscape. **T5.1.2** `[finding]` Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?

In the disordered register: no verse in M06-A describes a person moving from sustained hatred of a person to love or reconciliation using these terms. The transformation hatred produces appears highly resistant to reversal. Eschatologically: Psa 81:15 shows that the person whose defining inner orientation is hatred of God will "cringe toward him" at the final horizon — that is exposure and collapse, not reconciliation. The fate "lasts forever."

_T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States_

**T5.2.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?

Yes — the most complete sequence in M06. From the Joseph narrative and Proverbs wisdom material:
1. **Triggering perception** — Gen 37:4: "when his brothers saw that their father loved him more." Something is perceived as injury or injustice.
2. **Inner settlement** — Gen 37:4: "they hated him." The perception has produced a settled inner state.
3. **Surface manifestation** — Gen 37:4: "they could not speak peacefully to him." The inner state affects outward behaviour before any deliberate act.
4. **Intensification** — Gen 37:5, 8: "they hated him even more." Each new provocation deepens the disposition.
5. **Concealment** — Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips." As social pressure requires, the surface presentation diverges from the inner state.
6. **Volitional organisation** — Gen 27:41 (sa.tam): "then I will kill my brother Jacob." The hatred recruits the will into a plan.
7. **Action or exposure** — Pro 26:26: "his wickedness will be exposed in the assembly." The inner state eventually surfaces through action or exposure.

_T5.3 — Mechanism of Change_

**T5.3.1** `[finding]` What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?

Progressive inner organisation around the object of hatred. The characteristic works by recruiting each inner faculty toward the hostile orientation: perception is narrowed to register injury-evidence, memory preserves the wound, cognition is recruited into concealment, imagination generates plans of harm, volition is organised toward a hostile end. The Joseph narrative shows this across time — the brothers' inner lives become increasingly organised around their hatred of Joseph.

**T5.3.2** `[finding]` Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?

In the person-directed register: largely self-sustaining through the person's own perception and memory. In the communal register (group 1199): socially self-reinforcing — communal hatred maintains itself across the community. In Psa 105:25: externally introduced by divine action — the mechanism can come from outside the person.

_T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction_

**T5.4.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?

Extensively. Hatred is one of the primary causes of suffering in the M06-A corpus. The Psalms of lament are suffused with the experience of being hated: Psa 69:4 ("those who hate me without cause"), Psa 25:19 ("many are those who hate me"), Psa 38:19 ("many are those who hate me wrongfully"). In the Job material: Job 16:9 names God as the perceived source of persistent hostile treatment — "he has torn me in his wrath and hated me" — the experience of suffering can produce the perception of being hated even when that is not the cause.

**T5.4.2** `[finding]` Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?

Suffering precipitates hatred where an underlying wound is not resolved: 2Sa 13:22 — Absalom's sustained hatred of Amnon, rooted in the suffering of Tamar's violation. Suffering distorts perception toward reading the world through hatred (Deu 1:27 — group 1202: Israel in the wilderness attributes their suffering to divine hatred, falsely). Suffering can produce the perception of being hated where actual hatred is not the cause.

_T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification_

**T5.5.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?

In the righteous register: directly and positively. **T5.6.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?

Yes. Two trajectories. The present hatred of God anticipates a final confrontation it cannot survive — Psa 81:15's cringing is not repentance but the forced acknowledgment of what the hatred opposed. The present hatred is moving toward its own exposure and permanent consequence.

The iniquity is "visited" — God himself acts on the generational connection. _T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation_

**T7.2.1** `[finding]` What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

Sa.ne functions as the object of the prohibition: "you shall not hate your brother in your heart." The argument: inner hatred is morally culpable before it produces any act; the alternative is honest relational engagement, not merely behavioural restraint.

**T7.2.3** `[finding]` What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?

Inner state (hatred) + failure to confront honestly = moral culpability (sin). The premise: inner states are morally binding before they produce acts. The conclusion: the alternative to hatred is honest confrontation, not merely behavioural restraint.

**T7.2.4** `[finding]` What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?

Legal/judicial (Deu 19, Num 35 — hatred as legal criterion), covenantal (Exo 20:5 — hatred within the Decalogue), liturgical/Psalmic (Psa 139:21-22, Psa 97:10 — righteous hatred as worship-declaration), wisdom (Pro 8:13, 10:12), prophetic (Isa 1:14, Amos 5, Hos 9).

**T7.2.5** `[finding]` Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?

Lev 19:17 — the only verse in M06-A that simultaneously names the constitutional location (heart), the moral culpability of the inner state before any act, the alternative (frank confrontation), and the consequence (incurring sin). It is the most definitionally complete verse for the whole sub-group.

**T7.2.6** `[finding]` What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?

Uniquely reveals: (1) heart as explicit constitutional location; (2) the alternative (frank confrontation) — what replaces hatred; (3) moral culpability of the inner state before any act. No other M06-A verse combines all three.

_T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks_

**T7.3.1** `[finding]` Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?

Primary: clinical and social psychology — specifically the psychology of hatred, hostility, and aggression, and moral emotion research. Secondary: developmental psychology (formation of hatred and prejudice over time) and neuroscience (neural substrates of sustained hostile states).

**T7.3.2** `[finding]` Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?

Three convergences: (1) Clinical psychology distinguishes acute anger (episodic) from chronic hostility (dispositional, sustained) — mapping precisely onto the verse evidence's distinction between sa.ne (settled disposition) and momentary anger (a.phon). (2) Rumination — the repetitive mental rehearsal of perceived injury — is the mechanism sustaining chronic hostility; this maps onto sa.tam (grudge-bearing preserved in memory). (3) Developmental psychology and epigenetics document that hostile relational environments in childhood shape attachment patterns and inner-life architecture; this maps onto Exo 20:5's generational consequence of sustained hatred of God.

**T7.3.3** `[finding]` Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

Clinical psychology has no category for morally required or virtuous hatred. All sustained hostile dispositions are treated as pathological or problems to be reduced. The verse evidence's positive space for righteous hatred (Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil"; Psa 97:10: "hate evil!"; Amo 5:15: commanded hatred) is beyond the science's framework. This is a genuine divergence — not register divergence but a difference in what constitutes the properly ordered inner life.

**T7.3.4** `[finding]` Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?

The science of prejudice and in-group/out-group hostility (social psychology) is highly developed, documenting how group identity shapes the direction and intensity of hostile dispositions. The verse evidence documents this phenomenon (Egypt hating Israel, surrounding nations hating Zion) but does not articulate the mechanism by which group-identity-based hatred operates at the inner-being level. The social psychology of prejudice would add mechanistic description to the communal-level hatred documented in groups 1199 and 3596.

### §3.2 `M06-B` — Contempt

**Findings recorded:** 108

#### T0

_T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected_

**T0.1.1** `[finding]` What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

God holds minimal contempt as an originating characteristic. **T0.1.2** `[finding]` Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

Minimally and only in a reactive/judicial form (Deu 32:19, Lam 2:6). The limited and reactive nature of divine contempt-attribution reveals that contempt as an originating inner posture is not a divine characteristic. Its significance in the human person is therefore as a disorder — an inversion of what should be reverence and honour — rather than as an expression of something God shares with them.

**T0.1.3** `[finding]` Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

The relative silence (only reactive, judicial uses of na.ats attributed to God) suggests that contempt as a settled inner posture of treating what is sacred as beneath regard is not a characteristic of the divine nature. Since human beings are made in God's image, and contempt is absent from God's originating character, contempt is a distortion of the image — an inversion of the reverence and honour-giving that belongs to the properly ordered creature.

**T0.2.1** `[finding]` What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

No verse in M06-B suggests contempt serves a created purpose. Unlike righteous hatred, there is no commanded or praised contempt in M06-B. The characteristic appears entirely as a disordering of the inner being — the inversion of reverence and honour-giving. Whether contempt could theoretically have a righteous form (appropriate disregard for what is genuinely worthless) is not addressed by the verse evidence. The corpus treats contempt as a characteristically fallen disposition.

**T0.2.2** `[finding]` Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

Fallen condition only. Every instance in M06-B is named as sin (1Sa 2:17), as covenant violation (Deu 31:20), or as the inner speech of the wicked (Psa 10:13). No verse presents contempt as a capacity serving the created design.

**T0.2.3** `[finding]` Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?

The eschatological trajectory is reversal, not completion. Isa 60:14: "all who despised you shall bow down at your feet." Num 14:23: "none of those who despised me shall see it." Those who held contempt meet eschatological reversal; those who were despised are vindicated. The characteristic's future orientation is its exposure and defeat, not its fulfilment.

_T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression_

**T0.3.1** `[finding]` In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?

Contempt distorts rather than expresses the divine image. The image of God in the human person involves the capacity for accurate moral evaluation — assigning worth in accordance with what things actually are. Contempt inverts this: it assigns low worth to what has high worth (God's name, covenant, word). The capacity for worth-evaluation exists in the image-bearer; contempt is that capacity running in the wrong direction.

**T0.3.2** `[finding]` Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?

Not shared as an originating disposition. God's contempt-responses (Deu 32:19, Lam 2:6) are judicial and reactive, not expressions of an originating inner character. Contempt is a creaturely distortion — the inversion of reverence that belongs to the properly ordered creature before the Creator.

**T0.3.3** `[finding]` What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

Its presence is a direct indicator of image-disorder. Isa 1:4: "they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — the contempt and the estrangement from God are sequential. Contempt of the sacred marks a person in whom the image's capacity for appropriate reverence and honour-giving has been inverted. The image is in disorder when the person despises what God values.

_T0.4 — Typological Significance_

**T0.4.1** `[finding]` Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate (covenantal, eschatological, christological)?

Yes. Isa 53:3 (group 1280-001, anchor for ba.zah): "he was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief; and as one from whom men hide their faces he was despised, and we esteemed him not." The experience of being despised becomes the central experience of the Servant's mission — the contempt directed at him is the contempt of those who do not recognise what they are dismissing.

**T0.4.2** `[finding]` If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?

Human-to-revelation. The human contempt directed at the Servant reveals, through its wrongness, the reality of who is being dismissed. The direction runs from the human act of despising toward the theological reality of what is being missed.

#### T1

_T1.1 — Name and Naming_

**T1.1.1** `[finding]` What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

Called "Contempt." The name signals an evaluative act — the inner posture of treating something as beneath regard. It signals downward assessment: the person renders a verdict of low or no worth against its object.

**T1.1.2** `[finding]` What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

Six terms across distinct registers:
- **H5006 na.ats** — the dominant term (25 verses); spurning, rejecting as insignificant. Almost exclusively vertical: human person treating God, his word, or his servants as beneath regard.
- **H0959 ba.zah** — to despise; broader object range (birthright, Servant, God's name). Names the inner act of treating as valueless.
- **H0937 buz** — contempt as experienced; most present in contexts where contempt is received. The most socially-relational of the terms.
- **H0963 biz.za.von** — contempt as social contagion; one act spreading the disposition through a community (Est 1:18).
- **H5007B ne.a.tsah** — blasphemous contempt expressed in speech; the speech-act of reviling that reveals the heart's rejection.
- **H7590 shut** — contempt from surrounding nations toward Israel; always from the receiving side and always met by divine judgment.

**T1.1.3** `[finding]` Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

Strongly directional: almost universally directed upward (creature toward Creator, covenant, sacred things) or received downward (from nations toward Israel). Constitutional: Psa 10:13 — "say in his heart" — the heart is the constitutional location.

_T1.2 — Kind_

**T1.2.1** `[finding]` What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

Primarily an **inner act of evaluation** — the heart assigning low worth to what deserves high worth. But contempt can become habitual (Mal 1:6-12 shows priests in sustained patterns). It operates as both a discrete inner act and a habitual orientation depending on the verse context.

**T1.2.2** `[finding]` Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

Has constituent elements: (1) perception of the object; (2) moral evaluation rendering a low-worth verdict; (3) volitional response — acting as if freed from the object's claim; (4) optional speech-expression of the inner verdict.

**T1.2.3** `[finding]` What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

Contempt (M06-B) is an inner act and habit of the moral evaluation faculty in which the person assigns low or no worth to what is sacred, covenantally significant, or inherently worthy — constitutionally located in the heart, engaging the moral evaluation and cognitive faculties, producing a closed inner posture that excludes the despised object from its proper claim on the person's honour, compliance, or reverence.

_T1.3 — Boundary_

**T1.3.1** `[finding]` What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

Honour and reverence (ka.vod, yir.ah). Mal 1:6: "a son honours his father...If then I am a father, where is my honour? And if I am a master, where is my fear?" The structural opposite of contempt is the active attribution of appropriate worth — honouring what deserves honour.

**T1.3.2** `[finding]` What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

Compliance with what is despised (Num 15:31: contempt precedes commandment-breaking). Correction — group 172-002 (contempt of reproof) names the closing of the person to the formation that would come through instruction. Covenantal participation: Deu 31:20 — "despise me and break my covenant" — contempt and covenant-breaking are sequential.

**T1.3.3** `[finding]` What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

Not hatred: hatred is sustained relational enmity; contempt is a worth-evaluation that can be directed at things and words as much as persons. Not abhorrence (M06-C): abhorrence is visceral moral recoil; contempt is cool evaluative dismissal. Not anger: contempt can be cool and comfortable (Job 12:5 — "in the thought of one who is at ease"). Contempt is the cool verdict; abhorrence is the visceral recoil; hatred is the sustained enmity.

_T1.4 — Modes of Operation_

**T1.4.1** `[finding]` In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

Four modes: (1) **Inner speech mode** — the contempt is articulated privately (Psa 10:13: "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'"). (2) **Behavioural mode** — the inner verdict produces action in accordance with it (Num 15:31: "because he has despised the word...he has broken his commandment"). (3) **Speech-act mode** — ne.a.tsah: blasphemous contempt expressed outward in speech (Neh 9:18, 9:26, Eze 35:12). (4) **Contagion mode** — biz.za.von: contempt spreading from one person's act through a community (Est 1:18).

**T1.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

Yes. When directed at God and the sacred (groups 172-001, 172-003, 1567-001): contempt is the inner posture of rebellion — devaluing the covenantal relationship itself. When operating between persons (group 316-001): contempt is social dismissal experienced by the low-status person. When operating at the national level (groups 1281-001, 1277-001): contempt functions as collective inner disposition toward another people.

**T1.4.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

Yes, two speech modes: (1) **Inner speech** (Psa 10:13 — "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'") — the private verdict articulated internally. (2) **Public speech-act** (ne.a.tsah — Neh 9:18, 9:26, Eze 35:12) — blasphemous contempt expressed audibly.

_T1.5 — Immediate Response_

**T1.5.1** `[finding]` What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

For the recipient: Psa 123:3-4: "our soul has had more than enough of the scorn...of the contempt of the proud" — the soul registers saturation and cries for relief. Job 31:34: "the contempt of families terrified me" — fear is the immediate affective response to received contempt. For the holder: the inner verdict is rendered — a private assessment of worthlessness applied to the object.

**T1.5.2** `[finding]` Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

For the recipient: consistently the burden on the soul and the cry for relief. For the contemner's interior: the inner verdict is consistently private and unexpressed publicly.

_T1.6 — Sustained Effect_

**T1.6.1** `[finding]` What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

Progressive estrangement from the object of contempt. Isa 1:4: "they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — the estrangement is the sustained effect. In group 172-002 (contempt of reproof): the sustained effect is the closure of the formation pathway — the person becomes permanently closed to correction. In the communal register (Jer 23:17): the community that despises God's word becomes receptive to false prophets — the contempt has corrupted the capacity to distinguish true from false.

**T1.6.2** `[finding]` What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

Covenantal closure — the person is no longer capable of receiving what God or wisdom offers because the evaluative faculty has rendered those things as beneath regard.

_T1.7 — Conditions of Reception_

**T1.7.1** `[finding]` What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

Pride and comfort (Job 12:5: "in the thought of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune"; Psa 123:4: "the contempt of the proud"). Cognitive misreading of God's accountability (Psa 10:13: "you will not call to account" as the inner premise from which contempt of God follows).

**T1.7.2** `[finding]` What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

Fear of the Lord blocks contempt (Mal 1:6 — its absence is what makes contempt possible). The posture of openness to formation blocks contempt of reproof (Pro 15:5 — "whoever heeds reproof is prudent").

**T1.7.3** `[finding]` What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

Isa 49:7: the Servant is "deeply despised, abhorred by the nation" yet the promise of divine vindication is the inner resource that enables the despised to bear contempt without being destroyed or reciprocating it.

_T1.8 — Dimension Classification_

**T1.8.1** `[finding]` What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**Moral Character.** Contempt is an act of moral evaluation — the heart assigning low or no worth to what deserves high worth. It operates through the moral evaluation faculty rather than primarily through the relational capacity. The moral failure is in the evaluation (wrong verdict about worth), not in the relational orientation.

**T1.8.2** `[finding]` What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

1Sa 2:17: contempt of the Lord's offering named as "very great sin" — a moral failure. Gen 25:34: Esau's despising of the birthright is an act of wrong moral evaluation (valuing stew above covenant inheritance).

**T1.8.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

Secondary: **Relational Disposition** (contempt has relational consequences — estrangement, Isa 1:4) and **Cognition** (the cognitive grounding of contempt — the prior verdict that God will not call to account, Psa 10:13).

### T2.3 — Heart

_T2.3 — Heart_

**T2.3.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?

Yes. Psa 10:13: "why does the wicked renounce God and say in his heart (levav), 'You will not call to account?'" — the heart is explicitly named as the location of the inner speech of contempt. Pro 5:12: "how I hated discipline, and my heart (lev) despised reproof" — the heart is the constitutional location of the despising of reproof. The heart is where the contemptuous verdict is formed before it is expressed in speech or behaviour.

_T2.4 — Mind_

**T2.4.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?

Yes — the cognitive faculty is the ground of contempt. Psa 10:13: the contempt rests on a cognitive conviction ("you will not call to account") — the mind has evaluated the situation and produced an assessment that grounds the dismissal. Contempt is not unreasoned; it is a verdict the mind renders. The cognitive verdict then feeds the heart's evaluative posture.

**T2.4.2** `[finding]` What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?

The cognitive grounding of contempt reveals that it is not merely affective — it is a reasoned dismissal. The person who holds contempt has produced a cognitive assessment (this person/thing is beneath regard; God will not call to account) that functions as the premise from which the dismissal follows. This makes contempt particularly resistant to correction: to challenge the contemptuous person's behaviour without challenging their cognitive premise leaves the premise intact.

_T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets_

**T2.5.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?

The volitional faculty: Gen 25:34 (Esau's despising of the birthright) — "and he ate and drank and rose and went his way" — the will acts in accordance with the contemptuous evaluation. Once the worth-verdict is rendered, the will acts as if the thing truly has no claim. Job 12:5: "in the *thought* of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune" — the word "thought" (Hebrew: 'ashton) names a cognitive-reflective faculty: the person's comfortable thought-life generates contempt.

_T2.6 — Body — Significance_

**T2.6.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?

Thin. The primary body connection is through the speech organs — ne.a.tsah (group 1567-001) names blasphemous contempt expressed in speech. Isa 37:22: "she wags her head behind you" — contemptuous body language as the physical expression of the inner posture of dismissal. The body-part links in M06-B are primarily in the speech register.

_T2.7 — Body — Direction_

**T2.7.1** `[finding]` Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?

Soul → body. Inner contempt produces speech acts (ne.a.tsah) and body language (head-wagging). No evidence of reverse direction.

**T2.7.2** `[finding]` What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?

The consistent soul-to-body direction confirms that M06 characteristics are constitutionally inner-being phenomena that express outward — not behavioural patterns that create inner states. This is the methodological ground for the programme's approach: the inner state is the primary moral concern, and the behaviour is its secondary expression. Addressing the behaviour without addressing the inner state leaves the characteristic intact (as Lev 19:17's prohibition of inner hatred demonstrates — it targets the root, not the fruit).

_T2.8 — Body — Deposit_

**T2.8.1** `[finding]` Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?

No explicit generational deposit evidenced. The social propagation of contempt (group 1281-001, biz.za.von — Est 1:18) shows that contempt spreads across a social system from one person's act, but this is lateral social contagion, not generational deposit. No verse names contempt as producing a constitutional bodily or generational consequence.

**T2.8.3** `[finding]` If the evidence is silent, note this explicitly. This finding feeds directly into T5.7.

Generational or constitutional bodily deposit is not evidenced for contempt (M06-B), abhorrence (M06-C), cruelty (M06-D), hostility/enmity (M06-F), or malice (M06-G). The generational deposit finding in M06-A (hatred of God) is characteristic-specific, not a cluster-wide finding.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

_T2.9 — Origin and Source_

**T2.9.1** `[finding]` Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?

Two enabling origins: (1) **Pride and comfort** — Job 12:5: "in the thought of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune"; Psa 123:4: "the contempt of the proud" — the person's comfortable superiority generates contempt. (3) **Accumulated spiritual drift** — Deu 31:20: prosperity leads to spiritual satiation leads to turning to other gods leads to contempt of the covenant God.

**T2.9.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?

Multiple (pride, cognitive misreading, spiritual drift).

_T2.10 — Constitutional Movement_

**T2.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?

The movement from inner cognitive verdict (mind) to heart-level evaluative posture to volitional action (will) to speech expression (mouth) to social contagion (biz.za.von — Est 1:18). Within the soul, contempt moves from cognitive assessment through evaluative heart to expressed behaviour.

**T2.10.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?

Cognitive conviction (mind) → heart's evaluative verdict → volitional de-obligation (the will acts as if freed from the object's claim) → behavioural expression → social propagation.

#### T3

_T3.1 — Perception_

**T3.1.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?

Contempt distorts perception in a specific way: it causes the person to see the despised object through the lens of the low-worth verdict already rendered. Once Esau has despised the birthright, he cannot perceive it accurately — its value is invisible to him. Psa 10:3: the wicked "curses and renounces the Lord" — the perception of God has been filtered through a prior verdict of his irrelevance. Contempt is a perception-filter: what is despised becomes imperceptible in its actual worth.

**T3.1.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?

Impairs — contempt filters out the actual worth of the despised object. It produces a systematically distorted perception: what has been designated as worthless cannot be perceived as it actually is.

_T3.2 — Cognition_

**T3.2.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

The cognitive faculty is both the origin and the instrument of contempt. Psa 10:13: the cognitive conviction ("you will not call to account") is what generates the contemptuous inner speech. The mind renders a verdict that then operates as the ground of the dismissal. Sustained contempt also impairs cognition: Jer 23:17 — the community that despises God's word becomes receptive to false prophets. The contempt has corrupted the cognitive capacity to distinguish true from false prophetic address.

**T3.2.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?

Impairs — sustained contempt destroys the capacity to distinguish true from false address (Jer 23:17). The community that despises God's word cannot discern between true and false prophets.

_T3.3 — Memory_

**T3.3.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?

Memory is not prominently engaged in M06-B. Contempt does not characteristically persist through memory of a specific injury; it persists through a habitual inner posture of evaluation. The retrospective lament of Pro 5:12 ("how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof") — here memory perceives retrospectively what the contempt cost. Memory is the faculty that looks back on the contempt and names its damage, but not the faculty that sustains the contempt itself.

_T3.4 — Affect_

**T3.4.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?

Characteristically affectively cool rather than hot. Job 12:5: "in the thought of one who is at ease" — the ease is the affective register: contempt is comfortable, relaxed, indifferent. Est 1:18 (biz.za.von) pairs contempt with wrath ("contempt and wrath in plenty") — but the wrath is a separate phenomenon. The distinctive affective register of contempt is cold indifference or comfortable dismissal, not heated hostility.

**T3.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?

Contempt is characteristically affectively flat — the cool dismissal. It does not intensify affect but evacuates it: what should produce respect and reverence produces nothing. It impairs the affective registering of worth.

_T3.5 — Creativity_

**T3.5.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?

Not evidenced. Contempt is characteristically unreflective — it dismisses rather than plans. The creative faculty is not visibly engaged.

_T3.6 — Volition_

**T3.6.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

Contempt organises the will away from the despised object. Gen 25:34: Esau's despising of the birthright produces a volitional act — he eats, drinks, rises, goes his way. The will acts in accordance with the verdict that the birthright has no binding claim. Num 15:31: "he has despised the word of the Lord and has broken his commandment" — the contempt produces the volitional choice to break.

**T3.6.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?

Enables a specific volitional direction — away from the despised object — while closing off the volitional response of compliance and obedience. Contempt de-obligates the will from the claims of what has been despised.

_T3.7 — Agency_

**T3.7.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

Yes — contempt enables agency in a specific direction: it removes the felt obligation to act in a particular way toward the despised object. The priests who despise God's name (Mal 1:6) do not feel bound to honour the offering — their agency operates as if God's name has no claim. The characteristic de-obligates the agent from the demands the despised thing would otherwise make.

**T3.7.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?

Enables agency in the direction of non-compliance; removes the moral constraint that would limit agency. Contempt liberates the will from the perceived claim of the despised object.

_T3.8 — Moral Evaluation_

**T3.8.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?

The moral evaluation faculty is the primary operative faculty of contempt. Contempt is an act of moral evaluation — specifically, a wrong verdict rendered by the moral evaluation faculty. Every instance involves a wrong assessment of worth: Esau wrongly assesses the birthright as worth less than food; the priests wrongly assess God's name as not demanding honour; the comfortable person wrongly assesses the sufferer's misfortune as not deserving attention.

**T3.8.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?

Corrupts in both directions — the contemner evaluates the sacred as worthless (wrong verdict) and becomes unable to evaluate accurately because the verdict has already been rendered.

_T3.9 — Conscience_

**T3.9.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?

Group 172-002 (contempt of reproof) shows conscience's relationship to contempt most clearly. Pro 5:12: "my heart despised reproof" — the contempt of reproof is the contempt of the mechanism through which conscience operates. Reproof is conscience made external; despising reproof is the inner act of closing the conscience's external channel. The characteristic attacks the conscience's access to the person.

**T3.9.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?

Impairs — contempt of reproof attacks conscience's primary access mechanism.

_T3.10 — Conscientiousness_

**T3.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?

Contempt systematically dismantles conscientiousness in its domain. The priests of Mal 1 are the clearest example: their role requires conscientious attention to the honour of God's name; their contempt has made this conscientiousness impossible. They go through the motions while having internally rendered a verdict that the motions do not matter. Contempt produces performed conscientiousness — the appearance without the inner reality.

**T3.10.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?

Impairs — contempt dismantles the inner ground of conscientiousness toward the despised object.

_T3.11 — Relational Capacity_

**T3.11.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?

Contempt closes the relational capacity toward the despised object entirely. Isa 1:4: "they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — contempt produces total estrangement. Unlike hatred which organises the relational capacity against the hated person, contempt removes the person from the relational field altogether — they are beneath regard, which means beneath relational engagement.

**T3.11.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?

Impairs by removing the despised person from the relational field entirely.

**T4.1.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

Reactive and judicial — in response to Israel's prior contempt of God.

**T4.2.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?

The dominant movement is the human's contempt directed toward God: despising his name (Mal 1:6), treating his table as despicable (Mal 1:7, 12), spurning his counsel (Psa 107:11), saying in the heart "you will not call to account" (Psa 10:13). The inner posture required to sustain this is covenantal presumption — the assumption that God's claims are negotiable. Contempt toward God operates from within the covenantal relationship, making it a form of covenant betrayal from within.

**T4.2.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

#### T0

Not as a held characteristic. God imposes the condition covenantally (Eze 5:14) but does not himself inhabit the condition of being reproached as an inner-being state. God does not inhabit the condition of reproach as one who is reproached (he imposes and removes it). Fallen condition only. Reproach arises from the disordered inner lives of those who hate, despise, and mistreat others (M06-A through D). In a world without disordered inner lives, there would be no reproach.

Honour and vindication (ka.vod). Isa 60:15: "whereas you have been forsaken and hated...I will make you majestic forever, a joy from age to age." The structural opposite is not merely the absence of reproach but the positive attribution of honour, dignity, and public recognition of worth.

**T1.3.2** `[finding]` What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

Honour (Mal 2:9 — the opposite of reproach). Confident public presence (Psa 31:11 — "those who see me in the street flee from me"). Pro 6:33: "his disgrace will not be wiped away" — the most severe reproach excludes even the possibility of restoration without divine intervention.

**T1.3.3** `[finding]` What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

Not guilt: guilt is an inner conviction of wrongdoing; reproach is the social-inner condition that may or may not correspond to actual wrongdoing. Many verses document unjust reproach (Psa 69:4: "those who hate me without cause"). Not shame: shame can be an inner state independent of social designation; reproach is specifically the social-outer designation felt inwardly. They often co-occur (Psa 69:19: "my reproach, and my shame and my dishonor") but are distinguishable.

_T1.4 — Modes of Operation_

**T1.4.1** `[finding]` In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

Four modes: (1) **Personal acute mode** — ne.a.tsah: the overwhelming shame-experience in a moment of crisis (2Ki 19:3). (2) **Personal chronic mode** — cher.pah (individual): the sustained inner-social condition of the person publicly designated as shameful (Psa 69:7). (3) **Corporate-covenantal mode** — cher.pah (communal): reproach as the condition of a whole people among the nations (Eze 5:14). (4) **Dignity-violation mode** — hubrizō: reproach as the experience of having one's dignity actively stripped (1Th 2:2).

**T1.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

Yes — varies by duration (acute vs chronic), scope (individual vs communal), and source (active dignity-stripping vs passive social designation). The inner experience varies correspondingly.

**T1.4.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

Yes, extensively. Reproach is characteristically communicated through speech: the taunt, the mockery, the insult. Lam 3:61: "you have heard their taunts, O Lord." Psa 79:12: "the taunts with which they have taunted you." Jer 20:8: "the word of the Lord has become for me a reproach and derision all day long." Speech is the primary vehicle through which reproach is delivered and maintained.

_T1.5 — Immediate Response_

**T1.5.1** `[finding]` What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

Psa 69:20: "reproaches have broken my heart, so that I am in despair." The immediate response is heart-breaking — a shattering of the inner self. The secondary immediate response: "I looked for pity, but there was none, and for comforters, but I found none" — the reaching out for relational support, and the compounding of the condition when that support is absent.

**T1.5.2** `[finding]` Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

Consistent in direction: inner breaking followed by the impulse toward appeal. The form varies — acute crisis (2Ki 19:3), sustained lament (Psa 69), corporate appeal (Joel 2:17) — but the pattern holds.

_T1.6 — Sustained Effect_

**T1.6.1** `[finding]` What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

In the individual: sustained reproach restructures the person's inner life — their perception of themselves (Psa 22:6: "I am a worm and not a man"), their relation to others (Psa 31:11: "those who see me in the street flee from me"), and their volitional capacity (Job 31:34: "I kept silence, and did not go out of doors"). In the corporate register: sustained reproach produces communal shame that becomes a defining identity (Jer 24:9 — "a reproach, a byword, a taunt, and a curse in all the places where I shall drive them"). Isa 54:4 shows that restoration from deep reproach requires active forgetting — the reproach has been laid down in memory so deeply that removal is not automatic.

**T1.6.2** `[finding]` What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

Fear: Isa 51:7 — "fear not the reproach of man" — the command implies sustained reproach has produced fear as a settled inner state. Identity fragmentation: Psa 22:6 — the sustained social designation has been internalised and restructures self-perception.

_T1.7 — Conditions of Reception_

**T1.7.1** `[finding]` What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

Social isolation (Psa 69:20b — "I looked for pity, but there was none") — when the reproached person has no relational resource, reproach takes hold most completely. For corporate reproach: the community's own faithlessness (group 322-002) removes the divine protection that would have prevented it.

**T1.7.2** `[finding]` What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

Relationship with God as the source of identity and worth: Isa 51:7 — "you who know righteousness, the people in whose heart is my law; fear not the reproach of man." The identity established in God cannot be overwritten by the social designation of reproach.

**T1.7.3** `[finding]` What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

1Th 2:2: Paul had "boldness in our God to declare the gospel" despite shameful treatment. Jer 15:15: "for your sake I bear reproach" — the prophet holds the reproach in the context of his relationship with God, which prevents identity destruction. The inner state involves the pain of reproach alongside a groundedness in God's regard that keeps the reproach from colonising the person's identity.

_T1.8 — Dimension Classification_

**T1.8.1** `[finding]` What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**Emotion — Negative.** Reproach is primarily an affective-experiential condition — the felt inner state of being publicly shamed. Psa 69:20's "broken my heart...in despair" names the affective dimension as the primary inner-being impact.

**T1.8.2** `[finding]` What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

"Broken my heart" (Psa 69:20), "in despair," "I am a worm and not a man" (Psa 22:6), "great trouble and shame" (Neh 1:3) — consistently affective vocabulary throughout M06-E.

**T1.8.3** `[finding]` Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

Secondary: **Relational Disposition** (reproach fundamentally alters relational standing). **Dependence/Creatureliness** (the corporate mode — Israel's corporate reproach marks their dependence on God for vindication and restoration). The extensive appeal literature of M06-E (Psa 69:13, Jer 15:15, Joel 2:17, Lam 5:1) is the voice of creatureliness in its most vulnerable form.

#### T2

_T2.2 — Soul-Level Location_

**T2.2.1** `[finding]` Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?

From the recipient's perspective: Psa 123:3-4 — the soul is the organ that bears and registers the weight of received contempt and reproach. The soul is the receiving location for the condition, not named as holding the characteristic actively.

_T2.3 — Heart_

**T2.3.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?

Yes, as the organ that bears and is broken by reproach. Psa 69:20: "reproaches have broken my heart (lev), so that I am in despair" — the heart is explicitly named as the organ where reproach does its deepest damage. Psa 89:50: "I bear in my heart (levav) the insults of all the many nations" — the heart as the carrying-place of received insults. Unlike M06-A where the heart holds a hostile disposition, in M06-E the heart bears the weight of what others' hostility has done to it.

**T2.3.2** `[finding]` What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?

The heart as the organ of integration means that reproach does not merely damage the person's social standing — it damages the integrating centre of who they are. "Reproaches have broken my heart" (Psa 69:20) names the shattering of the person's inner coherence, not merely their surface emotions. When the heart is broken, the person's capacity to integrate experience, maintain identity, and orient toward the future is compromised. This explains why reproach requires active forgetting for full restoration (Isa 54:4) — the damage is at the integrating level.

_T2.4 — Mind_

**T2.4.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?

Indirectly. Job 19:5: "if indeed you magnify yourselves against me and make my disgrace an argument against me" — reproach is being deployed cognitively as an argument against the person. The reproached person must cognitively resist that argument from inside the condition. Psa 22:6: "I am a worm and not a man" — the person has internalised the social designation and now holds it as a cognitive self-assessment.

_T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets_

**T2.5.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?

The identity-level of the soul. Sa.ni (group 1664-001, Deu 21:15) names reproach as a total relational identity condition — the disfavoured wife is not merely experiencing episodes of reproach but inhabiting a position of permanent relational identity that shapes every legal and relational dimension of her existence. Isa 54:4 — "you will forget the shame of your youth, and the reproach of your widowhood you will remember no more" — the memory faculty (as a soul-subset) is named as the location where reproach persists after the external condition has changed, requiring active forgetting for restoration.

**T2.5.2** `[finding]` If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?

The memory faculty bearing reproach (Isa 54:4) reveals that the condition is not merely experiential but has been encoded at the deepest level of the person's inner history. This is why restoration from deep reproach requires not merely the cessation of the external condition but active new identity formation — the memory carries the reproach forward even when the situation has changed.

_T2.6 — Body — Significance_

**T2.6.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?

Strong. Job 16:10: "they have struck me insolently on the cheek" — the physical blow as the bodily delivery of reproach. Luk 18:32: "he will be mocked and shamefully treated and spit upon" — spitting and shameful treatment as the bodily delivery of reproach upon Christ. Psa 22:6: "I am a worm and not a man, scorned by mankind and despised by the people" — the image of the worm is the image of the bodily posture of the crushed and reproached person.

_T2.7 — Body — Direction_

**T2.7.1** `[finding]` Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?

Both directions evidenced. The bodies of others deliver reproach to the recipient (blows on the cheek, spitting — Job 16:10, Luk 18:32) — body → soul direction from outside. But the soul's reception of reproach then affects the person's own bodily posture: "I am a worm and not a man" (Psa 22:6) — the inner-soul condition of reproach is expressed through the person's own bodily self-perception and posture.

**T2.7.2** `[finding]` What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?

The bidirectional evidence for reproach confirms that it operates at the intersection of the inner being and the social-relational: others' bodies deliver it inward, and the person's inner state is then expressed through their own body. Reproach is a socially mediated inner-being condition — it is not purely internal or purely external but involves a loop between the social world and the inner self.

_T2.8 — Body — Deposit_

**T2.8.1** `[finding]` Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?

A personal constitutional deposit (not generational). Isa 54:4: "you will forget the shame of your youth, and the reproach of your widowhood you will remember no more" — the fact that the reproach must be *forgotten* implies it has been deposited at a level where it persists in memory and shapes ongoing experience. The reproach has left a constitutional deposit in the person's inner life requiring not merely cessation of the external condition but active new identity formation and forgetting.

**T2.8.2** `[finding]` What evidence supports or contradicts this?

Isa 54:4's requirement of active forgetting is the evidential basis for the personal deposit. The need to *forget* the reproach — not merely to cease experiencing it — implies it has been encoded in the memory at a level that persists independently of the external condition. Restoration is therefore not automatic but requires new formation.

_T2.9 — Origin and Source_

**T2.9.1** `[finding]` Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?

Three sources: (1) **From human hostility** — the disordered inner lives of M06-A through D produce the condition in their recipients. (2) **From divine covenantal judgment** — God imposes reproach as judgment on covenant violation (Eze 5:14, Jer 23:40). (3) **From voluntary acceptance** — Jer 15:15: "for your sake I bear reproach" — the prophet consciously takes up the condition in service of God.

**T2.9.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?

Multiple (human hostility, divine judgment, voluntary acceptance).

_T2.10 — Constitutional Movement_

**T2.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?

From external social designation (others designate the person as shameful) → social delivery (taunts, insults, physical contempt) → inner reception at the heart (Psa 69:20) → restructuring of self-perception (Psa 22:6) → sustained condition of fear and diminishment. In the restoration direction: divine intervention → social reversal → restoration of identity (Isa 54:4 — forgetting the reproach).

#### T3

_T3.1 — Perception_

**T3.1.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?

Reproach restructures perception. Psa 22:6: "I am a worm and not a man" — the person perceives themselves through the lens of others' contempt. Isa 51:7: "fear not the reproach of man" — the command implies that reproach has reorganised the person's perception of threat, making every potential reproach-encounter something to be feared. The characteristic shapes how the person perceives themselves, others' regard, and the social environment.

**T3.1.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?

Distorts — reproach reorganises perception of the self (as worthless) and of the social environment (as threatening further reproach). The person under sustained reproach perceives threat where there may be none.

_T3.2 — Cognition_

**T3.2.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

Job 19:5: "if indeed you magnify yourselves against me and make my disgrace an argument against me" — reproach is being deployed cognitively as an argument against the person. The cognitive dimension of reproach is its use as evidence — others use the condition of being reproached as proof of unworthiness. The reproached person must cognitively resist that argument from inside the condition.

**T3.2.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?

Distorts cognitive self-assessment — the reproached person internalises the social verdict and holds it as a cognitive self-understanding ("I am a worm").

_T3.3 — Memory_

**T3.3.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?

Memory is the faculty through which reproach persists after the external condition has changed. Isa 54:4: "you will forget the shame of your youth, and the reproach of your widowhood you will remember no more" — the explicit naming of forgetting as what restoration requires shows that memory is the organ through which reproach is carried beyond its immediate experience. Psa 89:50: "I bear in my heart the insults of all the many nations" — the bearing is a memory-carrying function.

**T3.3.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?

Both — reproach is deposited in memory (deepening it in the direction of the wound) and ultimately requires the active operation of forgetting (for restoration to be complete). The memory faculty is the organ through which the reproach persists beyond its occasion.

**T3.3.3** `[finding]` What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

Reproach is a memory-depositing characteristic — it leaves its mark in the memory faculty, requiring divine intervention and active new formation to complete the healing.

_T3.4 — Affect_

**T3.4.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?

M06-E has the most extensive affective evidence in the cluster. Psa 69:20: "broken my heart...in despair" — not sadness but despair. Psa 31:11: "an object of dread to my acquaintances" — the reproached person produces dread in others; their affective condition is visible externally. Neh 1:3: "great trouble and shame." Jer 51:51: "we are put to shame, for we have heard reproach" — shame as the inner-affective response to received reproach. The affective register of M06-E is despair, shame, and fear.

**T3.4.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?

Reproach impairs — it produces a sustained state of shame, fear, and despair that reshapes the whole affective orientation of the person toward their social environment.

_T3.5 — Creativity_

**T3.5.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?

Not evidenced from the recipient's perspective. The person bearing reproach is not characteristically creative in response — they are more typically constrained and paralysed (Job 31:34). Silent.

_T3.6 — Volition_

**T3.6.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

The volitional impact of reproach is primarily in the paralysis or constraint it produces. Job 31:34: "because I stood in great fear of the multitude, and the contempt of families terrified me, so that I kept silence, and did not go out of doors" — the reproach-fear produced volitional paralysis. The counter-volitional response: 1Th 2:2: Paul had "boldness in our God to declare the gospel" despite shameful treatment — the volitional choice to continue speaking despite reproach is grounded in God.

**T3.6.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?

Impairs — reproach produces volitional paralysis (Job 31:34). The counter-movement toward boldness (1Th 2:2) is possible but requires a specific resource (boldness in God) to overcome the paralysis.

_T3.7 — Agency_

**T3.7.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

Reproach reduces agency — the person under the condition has less effective agency because their social standing has been stripped. Psa 22:6: "I am a worm and not a man" — the image of the worm is the image of something without effective agency. But the restorative dimension shows agency being restored: 1Sa 17:26: David takes up the challenge against Goliath specifically to "take away the reproach from Israel" — the restoration of honour is an act of agency. God is the primary agent of reproach-removal (Gen 30:23, Jos 5:9, Isa 25:8).

**T3.7.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?

Impairs agency (paralysis through reproach) until restored by divine intervention or grounded boldness.

_T3.8 — Moral Evaluation_

**T3.8.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?

The moral evaluation faculty is engaged in cognitive resistance to reproach. Isa 51:7: "you who know righteousness, the people in whose heart is my law; fear not the reproach of man" — the internal moral knowledge (knowing righteousness) is the evaluative resource that enables the person to resist internalising the false social verdict. The faculty is called to evaluate the reproach itself as unjust and to maintain that evaluation against social pressure.

**T3.8.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?

The person bearing unjust reproach must actively maintain an accurate moral evaluation against social pressure — the faculty is strengthened when it successfully resists the false social verdict.

_T3.9 — Conscience_

**T3.9.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?

Reproach and conscience interact in two directions. When reproach is deserved (group 322-002): Jer 31:19 — "I was ashamed, and I was confounded, because I bore the disgrace of my youth" — the conscience confirms the reproach as appropriate, producing shame and genuine penitence. When reproach is undeserved (group 322-001): the conscience is what enables the person to know the reproach is unjust and to appeal against it to God.

**T3.9.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?

The conscience is what distinguishes deserved from undeserved reproach — it is the internal moral compass that enables the person to navigate the condition accurately.

_T3.10 — Conscientiousness_

**T3.10.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?

Jer 15:15: "for your sake I bear reproach" — the prophet's conscientiousness in his call produces reproach. The conscientious person (faithful to their calling, truthful in their speech) is precisely the one most exposed to reproach in a community that despises faithfulness. M06-E shows that conscientiousness does not protect from reproach — in a morally disordered context, it produces it.

**T3.10.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?

Conscientiousness in the face of reproach is possible but requires the specific resource of relationship with God (1Th 2:2).

_T3.11 — Relational Capacity_

**T3.11.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?

Reproach assaults the relational capacity from the outside: Psa 31:11: "those who see me in the street flee from me" — the social isolation reproach produces is total. But the relational capacity toward God intensifies: the extensive appeal literature of M06-E (Psa 69:13, Jer 15:15, Joel 2:17, Lam 5:1) shows the relational capacity toward God intensifying as the relational capacity toward other humans is stripped. The characteristic strips horizontal relationships and drives the person toward the vertical relationship.

**T3.11.2** `[finding]` Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?

Impairs horizontal relational capacity; intensifies vertical (God-ward) relational capacity.

The eschatological promise (Isa 25:8 — taking away reproach from all the earth) shows that the removal of this condition is part of God's ultimate purpose for creation.

**T4.2.1** `[finding]` Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?

**T4.2.2** `[finding]` What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

For appeal: awareness of need and trust that God can and will act. Jer 15:15: "for your sake I bear reproach" — the prophet's relationship with God is the inner posture from which the appeal is made.

**T4.2.3** `[finding]` What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?

The appeal-posture reveals a person who, even in the condition of reproach or facing an adversary, maintains their fundamental relational orientation toward God as the source of justice and vindication. The horizontal relational field has been damaged; the vertical relational capacity intensifies in response.

_T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving_

M06-E is the receiving sub-group — it documents what is given to the person rather than what the person gives. **T4.3.4** `[finding]` If the evidence is silent on the giving direction, note this explicitly.

M06-E is structurally the receiving end of the cluster. The giving direction is minimally evidenced — primarily through what others give to the person. Psa 15:3 shows the righteous person not giving reproach, but the giving of reproach is not M06-E's primary evidential register.

#### T5

_T5.1 — Nature of Transformation_

**T5.1.1** `[finding]` Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?

Reproach produces transformation in both directions.
