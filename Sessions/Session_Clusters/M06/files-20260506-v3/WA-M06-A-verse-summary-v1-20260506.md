## Summary

Across 164 verse rows, these five terms name a single sustained inner orientation of the person turned against another. The verses show hatred functioning in three directions.

**Hatred between persons.** The most populated register is hatred among human beings — brothers, spouses, neighbours, enemies. The verses show hatred as something that can build over time (Joseph's brothers hated him and then hated him *even more*), be concealed behind lying lips, stir up strife, motivate ambush, and drive men to murder. Esau's hatred of Jacob and Amnon's hatred of Tamar after violation are the clearest illustrations: hatred is not momentary but settles into a persistent inner posture that shapes all subsequent action. Sa.tam names the grudge-bearing form specifically — hatred that persists as a smouldering enmity.

**Hatred directed toward God.** A significant number of verses name those who hate God or whom God hates. Hating God is defined by refusing his commands, making covenant violation the inner ground of the hostility. God's hatred is directed at wickedness, falsehood, injustice, and disordered worship — never at persons arbitrarily, always in response to moral and covenantal failure.

**Righteous hatred.** The Psalms and wisdom literature show hatred that is aligned with love — the psalmist hates falsehood because he loves God's law; hating evil is commanded of those who love the Lord. This is not incidental: hatred appears as the necessary negative pole of moral orientation.

In all three registers, the terms name an inner disposition, not merely an act. Hatred lives in the heart before it produces behaviour.
