## Opening structural observation

M06-D has no righteous form. M06-A (Hatred) had commanded righteous hatred of evil (group 550-NEW-02, 21 verses). M06-C (Abhorrence) had commanded righteous abhorrence of falsehood (group 252-001). M06-B (Contempt) had no righteous form. M06-D (Cruelty/Ruthlessness) has no commanded, praised, or ordered expression in this corpus. The one possible exception — Isa 13:9 applying akh.za.ri to the day of the Lord itself ("the day of the Lord comes, cruel, with wrath and fierce anger") — describes a divine act using a cruelty-term but does not name cruelty as a divine characteristic in the same way God's hatred and abhorrence are named.

This structural absence is a finding about the characteristic's nature: cruelty and ruthlessness are not capacities that have a rightly-directed form. Unlike hatred (which can be directed at evil) or abhorrence (which can be aligned with God's own moral recoil), cruelty is constitutively characterised by the absence of a limiting good — the absence of mercy, restraint, and the fear of God. It is not a capacity that can be directed rightly; it is a condition defined by the removal of what ought to constrain it.

Additionally, M06-D is the only sub-group so far with a substantial **recipient register** fully built into its group structure: group 1775-002 (13 verses) documents the experience of being under ruthless domination and longing for deliverance. The sub-group formally carries both the agent's inner character (groups 1775-001, 6966-001, 6967-001, 6968-001) and the recipient's inner experience (group 1775-002). This agent/recipient dual structure within a single sub-group was observed across the cluster but has not until now been formalised within one sub-group's group architecture.

## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — Isa 13:9 is the only verse in M06-D that applies a cruelty-term to God: "the day of the Lord comes, cruel (akh.za.ri), with wrath and fierce anger, to make the land a desolation." This is not God being named as a cruel person by character, but the day of the Lord's judgment being described by its severity using the term akh.za.ri. The usage is descriptive of the intensity and unsparing quality of the judgment, not definitional of God's character.

God is never named as cruel or ruthless in M06-D as a characteristic of his inner being the way he is named as a hater of evil or as one who abhors. The cruelty-vocabulary is applied to God's action only in the context of final judgment — and even there, Jer 30:14 makes clear that God's severe dealing has a ground ("because your guilt is great, because your sins are flagrant"). The severity is not arbitrary but responsive.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute cruelty as a characteristic to God?

**S (borderline)** — Isa 13:9 uses the term for the day of the Lord, but this is not the same as naming cruelty as a divine characteristic. No verse in M06-D names God as cruel or ruthless by inner character. The term is applied to divine action in judgment but not to the divine inner being as a settled disposition.

**T0.1.3** — What does this silence suggest?

**E** — The silence is structurally significant. God hates evil (M06-A), abhors corruption (M06-C), and holds contempt-responses (M06-B, reactive). But God is not cruel or ruthless — these terms name the absence of what God characteristically has: mercy, restraint, compassion. Cruelty is defined by the removal of the limiting goods that God possesses fully. It is therefore an anti-divine characteristic — it names the inner-being condition of persons who have removed from themselves the characteristics that reflect God's image. God's own severity in judgment is not cruelty because it is always constrained, grounded, and purposive.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What purpose does cruelty serve in the human person as created?

**S** — No verse in M06-D suggests cruelty serves a created purpose. Unlike hatred and abhorrence which have commanded forms, cruelty has none. The characteristic appears entirely as a fallen condition — the absence of mercy, restraint, and fear of God rather than the presence of a positive inner capacity. Cruelty cannot be directed rightly because it is constitutively the absence of what directing rightly requires.

**T0.2.2** — Part of original design, response to fallen condition, or both?

**E** — From the verse evidence, cruelty is entirely a fallen phenomenon. It is defined by the absence of fear of God (Psa 54:3: "they do not set God before themselves") — which implies that when God was properly set before the person, the cruelty was not present. The condition is the product of removing the inner constraint that God-consciousness provides.

**T0.2.3** — Orientation toward future fullness?

**E** — The future orientation is not the fulfilment of cruelty but its removal. Isa 29:20: "the ruthless shall come to nothing." Isa 13:11: "I will lay low the pompous pride of the ruthless." Isa 25:3-5: the ruthless nations are subdued by God and strong peoples glorify God in their place. The eschatological trajectory for cruelty is its elimination, not its completion. Unlike abhorrence (which has an eschatological completion as the permanent moral verdict on the unrighteous), cruelty simply ceases.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — How does cruelty relate to the divine image?

**E** — Cruelty expresses the **negation** of the divine image. Lam 4:3 names this precisely: "even jackals offer the breast; they nurse their young; but the daughter of my people has become cruel, like the ostriches in the wilderness." The comparison to ostriches — creatures notorious in ancient understanding for abandoning their young — names cruelty as the failure of the most basic nurturing impulse. Even animals express what the cruel person has lost. The divine image includes mercy, restraint, and care for the vulnerable; cruelty is the condition of having lost these.

**T0.3.2** — Shared with God?

**E** — No. Cruelty is not shared between God and the image-bearer. It names the absence of what God has (mercy, restraint, purposive severity). It is exclusively a creaturely failure condition, not a creaturely expression of a divine attribute.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence of cruelty reveal about the condition of the image?

**E** — Psa 54:3's diagnostic is precise: "they do not set God before themselves." The image is in profound disorder when the person has removed God from their inner governing framework — when the consciousness of divine presence and accountability that would restrain destructive impulses is gone. Cruelty is the inner-being condition produced by the removal of God-consciousness from the person's operational centre.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What does "Cruelty/Ruthlessness" signal?

The double label signals that M06-D covers two related but distinguishable phenomena. Cruelty (akh.zar / akh.za.ri / akh.ze.riy.yut) names an inner character quality — the disposition of merciless destructiveness. Ruthlessness (a.rits) names the inner moral character of those who operate without God before themselves — the disposition of the oppressor. Both name the absence of mercy and restraint, but cruelty focuses on the quality of the destructive act while ruthlessness focuses on the character of the person who acts without divine constraint.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary terms reveal?

**H6184 a.rits** — "ruthless, violent, terrible." The dominant term by verse count (20 verses across two groups). The root carries the sense of terrifying power exercised without restraint. Psa 54:3 makes the inner-being ground explicit: ruthlessness is the disposition of those who "do not set God before themselves." The term names a character orientation defined by the absence of the divine constraint.

**H0394 akh.za.ri** — "cruel" as an inner quality characterising persons and nations. Pro 12:10: "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — the term names how the cruel person's attempts at mercy are themselves perverted by the underlying disposition. The inner quality colours everything the person does.

**H0393 akh.zar** — "cruel" as a disposition named in specific acts: Job 30:21 (cruel persecution) and Lam 4:3 (cruelty named through its contrast with animal nurturing).

**H0395 akh.ze.riy.yut** — "cruelty" as an abstract noun. Pro 27:4: "wrath is cruel (akh.ze.riy.yut), anger is overwhelming, but who can stand before jealousy?" The abstract noun form names cruelty as a quality that wrath possesses — the cruelty is the quality of wrath that has no mercy in it.

**T1.1.3** — Directional, relational, constitutional implications?

**E** — Constitutional: Psa 54:3 names the ground of ruthlessness as "not setting God before themselves" — the inner absence of God-consciousness as the operating principle. This is the most precise constitutional statement in M06-D. Directional: cruelty is outward-directed, toward others as its victims. Relational: the characteristic is the disposition of the oppressor — it names an inner quality that expresses in the relationship between the powerful and the vulnerable, the dominant and the subject.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — Kind of inner-being phenomenon?

**E** — M06-D is primarily a **character quality** rather than a disposition, state, or act. The akh.zar root family names cruelty as a constitutive quality of the person — the kind of person they are, not merely what they do in a moment. Pro 12:10: "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — the character quality is so pervasive it colours even the acts that appear merciful. This is different from hatred (a disposition that can be triggered and sustained) and contempt (an evaluative act that can become habitual). Cruelty is constitutive of character.

Ruthlessness (a.rits) is similar: Psa 37:35: "a wicked, ruthless man, spreading himself like a green laurel tree" — the image is of an established, flourishing character quality, not a momentary state.

**T1.2.2** — Constituent elements?

**E** — The constituent elements from the verse evidence:
- **Absence of God-consciousness** (Psa 54:3, 86:14: "they do not set God before themselves") — the inner governing framework that would restrain destructive impulses is absent
- **Absence of mercy** (Jer 6:23, 50:42: "they are cruel and have no mercy") — mercy is explicitly named as what is missing
- **Capacity for unrestrained destructive power** — the cruelty is the exercise of power without the limiting goods
- **Self-harm component** (Pro 11:17: "a cruel man hurts himself") — the cruel person's character ultimately turns against themselves

**T1.2.3** — Working description:

Cruelty/Ruthlessness (M06-D) is a constitutive inner character quality of the person — not a disposition triggered by circumstance but a settled quality that defines what the person is — characterised by the absence of mercy, restraint, and God-consciousness, and expressed in the unrestrained exercise of destructive power toward others. Unlike the other M06 characteristics, cruelty has no righteous form: it is constitutively defined by the absence of what would make it ordered. Its presence marks a person in whom the image-bearing capacities that would limit destructiveness have been removed.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — Structural opposite?

**E** — Mercy (chesed / H2617) and kindness. Pro 11:17: "a man who is kind (chesed) benefits himself, but a cruel man hurts himself." The antithesis is direct — kindness and cruelty are structural opposites in this verse. The one who is kind does good to himself; the cruel does harm to himself. The structural opposite of cruelty is not merely the absence of cruelty but the active presence of mercy and kindness.

**T1.3.2** — What does cruelty explicitly exclude?

**E** — Mercy ("have no mercy" — Jer 6:23, 50:42). Fear of God (Psa 54:3, 86:14). Care for the vulnerable (Lam 4:3 — the nurturing that even jackals provide). Regard for life (Pro 12:10: "the righteous has regard for the life of his beast"). Cruelty excludes every inner capacity that would hold destructive power in check.

**T1.3.3** — What is cruelty not?

**E** — Cruelty is not hatred: hatred is a relational hostility toward a specific person or thing; cruelty is a character quality that operates independently of any specific relational object. A cruel person is cruel even to those they do not hate. It is not anger: Pro 27:4 distinguishes cruelty from wrath and anger as related but distinct phenomena — wrath and anger are emotional states; cruelty is what remains when mercy is absent from those states. It is not contempt: contempt dismisses worth; cruelty exercises power without restraint over those it may not even evaluate as worthless.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — Distinct modes?

**E** — Three modes visible:

1. **Character-quality mode (groups 6966-001, 6967-001, 6968-001):** Cruelty as the persistent inner quality that characterises the person — what they are, not just what they do. Pro 12:10: "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — the quality pervades all the person's acts, including apparent acts of mercy.

2. **Oppressor-disposition mode (group 1775-001):** Ruthlessness as the inner character of those who do not set God before themselves — the powerful exercising their power without divine constraint. Psa 54:3: ruthless men seek the psalmist's life, characterised by the diagnostic "they do not set God before themselves."

3. **Terror-as-instrument mode (group 1775-002):** The ruthless nations used by God as instruments of judgment — Eze 28:7, 30:11, 32:12: "the most ruthless of nations" deployed against Egypt, Tyre. The ruthless become instruments of divine judgment while remaining characterised by their inner ruthlessness.

**T1.4.2** — Operates differently by context?

**E** — Yes. In personal contexts (Pro 11:17, 12:10): cruelty operates as a character quality corrupting interpersonal relationships. In national/military contexts (Jer 6:23, Eze 28:7-32:12): ruthlessness operates at the level of national character and military conduct. In the cosmic-judicial context (Isa 13:9): the quality is applied to the day of the Lord's judgment itself as a description of its severity. The same vocabulary covers personal character, national disposition, and the quality of divine judgment — without being identical in each case.

**T1.4.3** — Speech mode?

**S** — No speech-specific mode of cruelty is evidenced in M06-D. The characteristic operates through action and through the absence of restraint, not through verbal expression. Silent on speech mode.

**T1.5.1** — Immediate response to encountering this characteristic?

**E** — From the recipient's perspective (group 1775-002):

Job 6:23: the cry for deliverance — "deliver me from the adversary's hand...redeem me from the hand of the ruthless." The immediate inner response of those under ruthless domination is appeal, specifically appeal to God as the only power that can counteract the ruthless. The pattern across group 1775-002 is consistent: the immediate inner response to ruthless domination is the cry for divine deliverance and the recognition that only God can contend with the ruthless.

**T1.5.2** — Consistent across verse evidence?

**T1.5.3** — Where silent, note explicitly.

The inner experience of the cruel/ruthless person — what cruelty feels like from within — is entirely silent in M06-D. Pro 11:17's "a cruel man hurts himself" names a consequence but not an inner experience. The phenomenology of cruelty from the inside is not accessible from this corpus.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does cruelty produce over time?

**E** — In the perpetrator: Pro 11:17: "a cruel man hurts himself." The cruelty turns back on its practitioner — the sustained effect is self-destructive. Job 15:20: "the wicked man writhes in pain all his days, through all the years that are laid up for the ruthless" — the ruthless person's inner life is characterised by sustained inner anguish, though the verse describes this from outside. The sustained effect of cruelty on the cruel person is their own undoing.

In the recipient: sustained exposure to ruthless domination produces the experience described in Isa 25:4 — those under the ruthless seek shelter as from a storm. The sustained effect on the recipient is the stripping of all relational and social shelter.

**T1.6.2** — What states does sustained cruelty establish?

**E** — In the perpetrator: a character disposition that has become constitutive — the cruelty is not situational but settled. Pro 12:10's "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" shows that the character quality has so thoroughly organised the person that even their mercy-attempts are corrupted. In the recipient: the state of subjection and longing for deliverance documented in group 1775-002 — the inner condition of the oppressed under ruthless domination is one of sustained appeal and hope for divine intervention.

**T1.6.3** — Sustained effect vs immediate response?

**E** — The immediate response (appeal for deliverance) and the sustained effect (the condition of ongoing subjection) differ: the appeal is acute; the subjection is chronic. The chronic state is what group 1775-002 documents — not the moment of crying out but the sustained condition of living under ruthless domination.

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What conditions enable cruelty to take hold as inner character?

**E** — The primary enabling condition is the removal of God-consciousness from the inner governing framework. Psa 54:3: "they do not set God before themselves" — this is the diagnostic ground of ruthlessness. The removal of the awareness that God is present, attentive, and will call to account is what enables the restraint of mercy to be removed. Secondary: Pro 27:4 suggests that wrath and anger, when they lack mercy, become cruel — the enabling condition for cruelty within emotional states is the absence of the limiting counterweight of mercy.

**T1.7.2** — What blocks cruelty?

**E** — Fear of God (which is the same as setting God before oneself) blocks cruelty. Mercy and kindness as settled inner character qualities block cruelty (Pro 11:17's antithesis: the kind man benefits himself). The righteous person's regard for life (Pro 12:10) is what prevents the cruel person's pattern from taking hold.

**T1.7.3** — Inner state of person who encounters cruelty but does not become cruel?

**E** — Group 1775-002 documents this most fully. The recipient of ruthlessness who does not reciprocate it is characterised by appeal (Job 6:23), trust in God as deliverer (Isa 25:4), and prophetic confidence in God's contention against the ruthless (Isa 49:25, Jer 15:21). The inner state is not passivity but active sustained appeal grounded in a different understanding of where ultimate power lies.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — Primary dimension?

**E** — **Moral Character** is the primary dimension — but with a distinctive character different from M06-B's contempt (also Moral Character). Where contempt is the moral evaluation faculty rendering wrong verdicts, cruelty is the **moral character void** — the condition of being constituted without the character qualities that would restrain destructive impulses. It is not a wrong act of moral evaluation but the absence of a positive moral quality (mercy, fear of God, restraint). Cruelty is character-by-absence.

**T1.8.2** — Evidence?

**E** — Pro 12:10: "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — the cruel character quality is so pervasive that it corrupts even acts that appear merciful. This is character-level operation, not situational. Psa 54:3's "they do not set God before themselves" names the absent character quality (God-consciousness and its associated restraints) as the ground of the ruthlessness.

**T1.8.3** — Secondary dimensions?

**E** — **Agency/Power** is a secondary dimension — cruelty operates through the exercise of power, and ruthlessness is characteristically the disposition of those who have power (oppressors, nations). The characteristic is not passive; it acts through its destructive power. **Emotion — Negative** is a secondary dimension for the recipient register (group 1775-002) — the inner experience of being under ruthless domination has a strong affective dimension of fear, distress, and longing for deliverance.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**E** — Cruelty does not merely express through perception; it distorts it. Pro 12:10: "the righteous has regard for the life of his beast, but the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — the righteous person perceives the life of even an animal as worthy of regard; the wicked person does not perceive it. The cruel person's perception has been reorganised so that what should trigger mercy and restraint does not register as such. The perception of vulnerability, suffering, and life-worth has been dulled or inverted.

---

### T3.2 — Cognition

**E** — The cognitive element is present in Psa 54:3's "they do not set God before themselves." This is a cognitive-dispositional failure: the person's cognition does not include God as an operative reality. The ruthlessness is downstream of a cognitive framework from which God has been excluded. The mind functions, but it functions without the governing premise that would restrain the character.

---

### T3.3 — Memory

**S** — Memory is not visibly engaged in M06-D. Cruelty is not characteristically sustained by the memory of past injury (that is sa.tam's mechanism). The cruel person is not cruel because they remember a wound — they are cruel because of a character condition that operates independently of specific memories. Silent.

---

### T3.4 — Affect

**E** — Pro 27:4: "wrath is cruel, anger is overwhelming" — cruelty is named as the quality that wrath possesses when unrestrained by mercy. The affective register of cruelty is the heat of wrath without the cooling of mercy. It is not the cool indifference of contempt or the visceral recoil of abhorrence; it is the unrestrained destructive force of affective intensity (wrath, anger) when mercy is absent. The affect is present and intense; what is absent is the limiting counterweight.

---

### T3.5 — Creativity

**S** — No evidence of the creative faculty being engaged by cruelty in M06-D. Unlike hatred (which recruits imagination into planning harm), cruelty appears to operate more directly — its destructiveness does not require creative planning. Silent.

---

### T3.6 — Volition

**E** — Cruelty organises the will toward unrestrained destructive action. Deu 19:11's definition of murder (hatred + lying in wait + attack) maps onto the ruthless person's volitional pattern. The cruel person's will is not inhibited by mercy, restraint, or fear of God — the result is volition that acts without the limiting goods. Isa 13:9: the day of the Lord comes "to make the land a desolation and to destroy its sinners" — the ruthless volitional pattern (destroy without mercy) is what gives the judgment vocabulary of Isa 13 its intensity.

---

### T3.7 — Agency

**E** — This is the faculty most visibly engaged in M06-D. Cruelty and ruthlessness are characteristically agentic — they exercise power without restraint. Group 1775-002 is almost entirely about the agency of the ruthless: "the most ruthless of nations" drawing swords, filling the land with the slain, cutting down and leaving. The agency of the ruthless is unrestrained, massive, and directed toward destruction. This is the most agency-saturated sub-group in M06.

---

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**E** — The moral evaluation faculty is characteristically absent or inverted in M06-D. Pro 12:10: "the righteous has regard for the life of his beast" — the righteous person evaluates animal life as worthy of regard; the wicked person does not make this evaluation. The cruel person's moral evaluation faculty has ceased to register the worth of what would otherwise trigger mercy. The faculty is not functioning in its rightly-ordered form — not inverted like M06-B's contempt of justice, but simply absent in its mercy-triggering function.

---

### T3.9 — Conscience

**E** — The conscience's restraining function is what cruelty has removed. The cruel person is not someone whose conscience recoils from their cruelty — Pro 12:10's "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" shows that the conscience's alarm signal is not operative in restraining the person's conduct. Lam 4:3's contrast with the jackals shows that even the animal nurturing instinct (a kind of creaturely conscience-analog) is present in animals but absent in the cruel person. The conscience that would say "this is too far" has been silenced.

---

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**E** — Pro 17:11: "an evil man seeks only rebellion, and a cruel messenger will be sent against him" — the cruel person is characterised by a disposition toward rebellion and the exercise of power in the wrong direction. Their conscientiousness is entirely absent in the direction of mercy and restraint. The ruthless nations (group 1775-002) exercise conscientiousness in their military campaigns — they are thorough and effective — but this conscientiousness is directed entirely toward destruction rather than toward the goods that would limit it.

---

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**E** — Cruelty destroys the relational capacity in a specific way: it removes the person from the relational register of mercy and care, replacing it with domination and destruction. Group 1775-002 documents the result: the relationship between the ruthless and their victims is one of oppressor and oppressed, with no relational quality of mutual regard. Lam 4:3: the daughter of the people has become "like the ostriches in the wilderness" — the nurturing relational capacity that even animals express has been removed. The relational capacity remains in a functional sense (the cruel person relates to others) but has been stripped of the qualities that make relation genuinely human.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**E** — God does not extend cruelty toward the human person as a characteristic. Isa 13:9 applies the term to the day of the Lord's judgment but as a description of the judgment's severity, not of God's character. The divine movement in M06-D is consistently toward the **removal of the ruthless** (Isa 29:20, 13:11), the **deliverance of the oppressed** (Isa 49:25, Jer 15:21), and the **judgment of the cruel** (Job 27:13). God is the answer to cruelty, not its practitioner.

---

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**S** — No verse in M06-D shows cruelty operating in the human person's movement toward God. The characteristic is entirely outward-directed (toward other humans and nations). The human-to-God interface for M06-D is not cruelty but the appeal that cruelty generates (group 1775-002 — the oppressed cry to God). The characteristic generates the cry to God rather than being directed at God.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Transformation

**E** — The transformation that cruelty produces is the progressive removal of limiting inner goods. The person does not become cruel in a single moment; the character quality consolidates through the sustained practice of acting without mercy and without setting God before themselves. Pro 12:10's "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" is the description of cruelty at its consolidated state — even mercy-attempts are shaped by the underlying character. The transformation is complete when even the person's most apparently merciful acts are expressions of their cruelty.

Reversal: Pro 11:17 implies reversal is possible (the contrast with kindness suggests kindness is an alternative orientation). But no verse in M06-D describes a person moving from cruelty to mercy — the movement described is always cruelty toward its consequences and ultimate removal.

---

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**E** — From the verse evidence:

1. **Removal of God-consciousness** (Psa 54:3 — "do not set God before themselves") — the governing framework is evacuated
2. **Absence of restraining qualities** (mercy, fear, regard for life) — the limiting goods have no ground
3. **Exercise of unrestrained power** — the character quality expresses through destructive agency
4. **Self-destructive consequence** (Pro 11:17 — "a cruel man hurts himself") — the characteristic turns on its practitioner
5. **Eventual divine removal** (Isa 29:20 — "the ruthless shall come to nothing") — the eschatological terminus

For the recipient:
1. **Subjection to ruthless domination** — the starting condition
2. **Appeal to God** (Job 6:23, Jer 15:21) — the characteristic inner response
3. **Divine deliverance** (Isa 49:25, Jer 20:11) — the promised reversal

---

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**E** — The mechanism is the **removal of the inner governing framework** — specifically the fear of God and the practice of setting God before oneself (Psa 54:3). When this framework is removed, the restraining qualities (mercy, regard for life) have nothing to anchor them, and the destructive impulses that God-consciousness would have checked are free to operate. The mechanism is not the arrival of a new positive inner force but the departure of the limiting goods.

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**E** — Group 1775-002 is almost entirely a documentation of suffering under ruthless domination. The cruel person is the primary cause of suffering in M06-D. Job 6:23: the sufferer cries for deliverance from the ruthless. Isa 25:4: God is the shelter for the poor and needy from the storm of the ruthless. The characteristic's relationship to suffering is as its primary producer — cruelty generates the suffering documented in group 1775-002.

Job 15:20: "the wicked man writhes in pain all his days, through all the years that are laid up for the ruthless" — the cruel person also suffers, but the verse presents this from outside without describing the inner experience.

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**E** — M06-D participates in the formation question negatively only. No verse shows cruelty contributing to sanctification. The characteristic is the anti-formation condition — the state of the person in whom the governing framework (fear of God, mercy, restraint) has been removed. ### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**E** — The eschatological trajectory of cruelty is its removal, not its completion. Three verses name this directly:

Job 27:13: "this is the portion of a wicked man with God...the heritage that oppressors receive from the Almighty."

The eschatological direction is the elimination of cruelty from the created order — a removal of what does not belong in the order God intends. Unlike de.ra.on (M06-C) which names an eternal condition of being abhorrent, cruelty simply comes to nothing. The ruthless cease.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical

**T7.1.1-3** — Primary terms and semantic range:

H6184 a.rits is the dominant term by verse count (20 verses). The root carries the sense of "terrifying, awe-inspiring in a destructive sense" — the ruthless person produces terror in those under their power. The word is used almost exclusively for persons and nations whose power is exercised without mercy or restraint. The root does not carry the moral quality in itself but the consistent usage across the corpus shows that the ruthless person is always characterised by the absence of the limiting goods.

The akh.zar root family (H0393, H0394, H0395) is the cruelty-vocabulary proper — the noun, adjective, and abstract noun forms of the same root. The root's sense is hard, severe, relentless. H0395 akh.ze.riy.yut (Pro 27:4) is the most abstract form: "cruelty" as a quality that wrath possesses. The root family is internally coherent and covers the full grammatical range of a single quality.

**T7.1.4** — Vocabulary distinctions: The vocabulary covers the agent's character quality (akh.za.ri — characterising persons), the concrete experience of the quality (akh.zar — describing specific acts), the abstract quality itself (akh.ze.riy.yut), and the person-type of the ruthless (a.rits). **T7.1.6** — Person-type: A.rits functions as a person-type throughout — "the ruthless" as a category of person defined by their inner character. The ruthless person (a.rits) is the person-type for this sub-group.

### T7.2 — Literary

**T7.2.1** — Primary anchor: **Psa 54:3** (group 1775-001): "ruthless men seek my life; they do not set God before themselves." The term a.rits functions here as a person-type designation, and its grounding diagnostic ("do not set God before themselves") is what makes this the primary anchor. The verse names both the phenomenon (ruthless men) and its inner-being ground (the absence of God-consciousness). No other verse in M06-D names both the characteristic and its constitutional ground with equal precision.

**T7.2.2** — Literary forms: Psalmic/lament (Psa 54:3, 86:14, 37:35, Job 6:23), Prophetic (Isa 13:9, 25:3-5, 29:20, Jer 6:23, Eze 28:7), Wisdom (Pro 11:17, 12:10, 27:4). The psalmic form carries the experiential register — the cry from under ruthless domination. The prophetic form carries the judgment register — ruthlessness as what God will remove. The wisdom form carries the character-quality register — cruelty as what the wicked person is.

**T7.2.3** — Key arguments: Pro 11:17's argument is the most structurally significant: "a man who is kind benefits himself, but a cruel man hurts himself." The argument is that character qualities produce consequences that return on the person — kindness is self-beneficial; cruelty is self-destructive. The logic is not primarily about external judgment but about the inner coherence of character: what the person is eventually produces what the person receives.

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: Military/national (group 1775-002 — the ruthless nations), judicial/eschatological (Isa 13:9, 29:20), wisdom/moral (Pro 11:17, 12:10, 27:4), personal appeal/lament (Psa 54:3, 86:14). The range across settings confirms that cruelty is not a situationally specific characteristic but a character quality that operates across all contexts.

**T7.2.5-6** — Primary anchor unique revelation: Psa 54:3 is the only verse in M06-D that names the inner-being ground of ruthlessness explicitly ("do not set God before themselves"). This verse reveals what no other verse in the corpus reveals: that ruthlessness is not primarily a choice to be cruel but the consequence of a prior choice to exclude God from the inner governing framework. The cruelty is downstream of the God-absence.

### T7.3 — Science

**T7.3.1** — Primary field: **Clinical and social psychology** — specifically the psychology of psychopathy, callousness, and unemotional traits (what clinical psychology calls "CU traits" — Callous-Unemotional). Secondary: **evolutionary biology and developmental psychology** (the development of empathy and its absence; the formation of callous traits through developmental conditions).

**T7.3.2** — Convergences:

**Convergence 1 — Callous-Unemotional traits:** Research on psychopathy identifies the absence of empathy, guilt, and fear as the core of callous-unemotional functioning — the person acts without the emotional responses that would normally restrain harmful behaviour toward others. This maps onto M06-D's structure: cruelty is defined by the absence of mercy, fear of God, and regard for life. The scientific model names the absent emotional-regulatory responses; the verse evidence names the absent inner character qualities (mercy, God-consciousness). They are describing the same inner-being condition from different angles.

**Convergence 2 — Pro 11:17's self-destructive mechanism:** Research in social psychology documents that callous and cruel interpersonal patterns reliably damage the practitioner's own relationships, social standing, and wellbeing over time. The "cruel man hurts himself" (Pro 11:17) finding has substantial empirical support — social rejection, relational isolation, and eventual social consequences are documented outcomes of callous conduct. The wisdom literature's insight is empirically confirmed.

**Convergence 3 — The absence of empathy as the ground:** Research on cruelty and violence consistently identifies empathy failure — the inability to register another's pain as a claim on one's conduct — as the primary internal mechanism. This maps onto Pro 12:10's "the righteous has regard for the life of his beast" — the righteous person's empathic registration of animal life as worthy of regard is what the cruel person lacks. The science names the mechanism (empathy failure); the verse evidence names the moral quality (mercy, regard for life).

**T7.3.3** — Divergences:

**Divergence 1 — The God-consciousness ground:** Psa 54:3's "they do not set God before themselves" names the primary constitutional ground of ruthlessness as the exclusion of divine accountability. Clinical psychology has no category for God-consciousness as a restraining inner-being mechanism. The science works with empathy, fear of consequences, and social norms as restraining mechanisms — all of which are secondary to what the verse evidence names as the primary ground.

**Divergence 2 — The self-destructive consequence:** Pro 11:17's "a cruel man hurts himself" makes a character-based argument about the coherence of inner character and its consequences. Clinical psychology can document the social and relational consequences, but the verse evidence is making a deeper claim: the inner character itself is self-destructive as a quality of the person's being, not merely in its social consequences.

**T7.3.4** — Science gap: Research on **moral injury and its repair** — how callous and cruel patterns can be reversed — is developed in clinical psychology and trauma research. The verse evidence in M06-D does not describe the reversal of cruelty from within (only its removal from without, by divine judgment). The science's work on the conditions under which callous traits can be modified or reformed would be relevant to understanding whether the characteristic is reversible — a question the verse evidence leaves largely unanswered.
