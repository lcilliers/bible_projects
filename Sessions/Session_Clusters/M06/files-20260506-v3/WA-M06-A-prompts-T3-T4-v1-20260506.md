## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**T3.1.1** — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses — and if so, which and how?

**E** — The perceptive faculty is engaged primarily at the point where hatred enters the inner life. Gen 37:4: "when his brothers *saw* that their father loved him more than all his brothers, they hated him." The perception of favouritism is the trigger — hatred enters through seeing. Gen 37:5, 37:8: each new perception (the dream, the words) intensifies the hatred. The perception does not merely register facts; it reads relational signals and feeds them to the disposition. In group 550-NEW-02: Psa 119:104 — "through your precepts I get understanding; therefore I hate every false way" — understanding (cognitive perception of truth) produces the directed hatred of falsehood. Perception of truth actively generates hatred of its opposite.

**T3.1.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?

**E** — Impairs, significantly. Psa 36:2: "he flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated" — the wicked person's hatred-avoidance has distorted his self-perception; he cannot see his own iniquity. Group 1199 (mas.te.mah, Hos 9:7): "the prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad, because of your great iniquity and great hatred" — communal hatred has destroyed the perceptive capacity entirely; those whose role is spiritual sight have become blind and mad. Conversely, in group 550-NEW-02: righteous hatred sharpens perception — the person who hates falsehood (Psa 119:163) is the person who has understood truth. The direction of the hatred determines whether it enables or impairs perception.

**T3.1.3** — What does the pattern reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**E** — Hatred is profoundly selective in what it perceives and how it processes what it sees. Person-directed hatred (group 1601) distorts perception of the hated person — all signals are read through the frame of the hostile disposition. Righteous hatred (group 550-NEW-02) sharpens perception of moral reality — what is false, corrupt, or unjust becomes more visible to the one who hates it. The characteristic is therefore a perception-organiser: it determines what the inner senses attend to and how those signals are interpreted.

### T3.2 — Cognition

**T3.2.1** — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

**E** — Engaged in both directions. In group 550-NEW-02: Psa 119:104 — "through your precepts I get understanding; *therefore* I hate every false way" — cognition (understanding through the precepts) produces directed hatred. The cognitive sequence runs: learning → understanding → hatred of the false. Psa 119:128: "therefore I consider all your precepts to be right; I hate every false way" — the word "consider" names an explicit cognitive act that grounds the hatred. In group 1601 (person-directed hatred): the cognition is present but distorted — Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart" — the hater must cognitively manage the concealment, which requires active misrepresentation. Cognition is recruited into the service of the hidden hatred.

**T3.2.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**E** — In group 550-NEW-02: deepens and clarifies — hatred of falsehood is the product of sharpened understanding. In group 1199 (mas.te.mah): destroys — "the prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad" (Hos 9:7) — the capacity for prophetic understanding is obliterated by accumulated communal hatred. In group 1601 (concealed hatred): corrupts — Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips" — cognition is bent to produce false communication. Hatred bypasses honest cognition by requiring the inner life to maintain two registers simultaneously: the real (hatred) and the performed (false peace).

**T3.2.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred and cognition are in a reciprocal relationship. Rightly-directed hatred is downstream of clear understanding; misdirected hatred corrupts understanding. The characteristic does not bypass cognition — it works through it, either clarifying it (in the righteous register) or bending it to serve concealment and distortion (in the person-directed register).

### T3.3 — Memory

**T3.3.1** — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — holding and retrieving inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?

**E** — Memory is central to the persistence of hatred, particularly in group 1200 (sa.tam — grudge-bearing). Gen 27:41: Esau remembers the blessing theft. Gen 50:15: Joseph's brothers remember what they did to him — and fear he remembers it too. The grudge lives in memory; without memory it cannot persist. Eze 35:11: "I will deal with you according to the anger and envy that you showed because of your hatred against them" — God remembers Edom's hatred and will act on that memory in judgment. Memory is the faculty that enables hatred to survive beyond the triggering moment and sustain itself over time.

**T3.3.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**E** — Deepens in a specific direction: the hater's memory of the injury or perceived wrong is preserved and returned to repeatedly. Gen 50:15 shows the brothers assuming Joseph has been doing the same — holding the memory of what they did to him. The hatred keeps the wound fresh. But it may impair memory of counter-evidence — there is no verse in M06-A where the person-directed hater recalls positive aspects of the relationship that might qualify the hatred. The characteristic appears to selectively preserve injury-memory while suppressing reconciling-memory.

**T3.3.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred is a memory-organising characteristic. It preserves the memory of injury and supplies it as justification for the ongoing disposition. Sa.tam (persistent grudge-bearing) is essentially hatred preserved in memory across time — the grudge is the memory of the wound kept alive in the inner being.

### T3.4 — Affect

**T3.4.1** — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?

**E** — Yes, substantially. The affective quality of hatred is named directly in several verses. 2Sa 13:15: "Amnon hated her with very great hatred" — the intensity qualifier "very great" names the affective weight of the hatred. Psa 25:19: "with what violent hatred they hate me" — violent names the affective character. Job 16:9 (sa.tam): "he has gnashed his teeth at me" — the somatic expression signals the affective intensity underneath. Ecc 9:6: hatred is listed alongside love and envy as one of the constitutive affective orientations of the living person. The affective dimension is present across all groups, though it varies in intensity.

**T3.4.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**E** — In group 1601 (concealed hatred): impairs honest affective expression. Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips" — the person must suppress the true affective state and perform its opposite. This is an affective split: the inner affect (hatred) and the expressed affect (false peace) are in tension. In group 1200 (sa.tam): hatred transforms affect from acute anger (the original wound) into a sustained, lower-intensity smouldering state — the heat of the moment becomes the cold persistence of the grudge. The affect does not extinguish; it settles.

**T3.4.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred is not simply an emotion but a disposition that organises affect. The initial affective trigger (anger, jealousy, betrayal) is transformed by hatred into a sustained inner orientation. The affect is present throughout but subordinated to the disposition — the characteristic is not primarily felt but primarily held. This is why Pro 10:18's concealment is possible: the affective expression can be suppressed even when the disposition remains active.

### T3.5 — Creativity

**T3.5.1** — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty — imagination and the capacity to originate — and if so, how?

**E** — Engaged in a specific and troubling way. Gen 27:41: Esau's hatred generates a plan — "then I will kill my brother Jacob" — the hatred has recruited imagination to construct a scenario of future action. Psa 41:7: "all who hate me whisper together about me; they imagine the worst for me" — the Hebrew for "imagine the worst" names a cognitive-creative act of projecting negative futures. Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips" — the concealment requires creative construction of a false persona. The creative faculty is engaged by person-directed hatred primarily in the service of destructive planning and deceptive self-presentation.

**T3.5.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?

**E** — Distorts — it channels the creative capacity into destructive and deceptive ends. In group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred), there is no evidence of creativity being engaged; the characteristic in its righteous form is more receptive (hatred arising from understanding) than creative.

**T3.5.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Person-directed hatred is not passive; it recruits imagination and planning capacity into its service. This makes it dangerous not merely as an inner state but as a productive force — it actively generates scenarios of harm and strategies of concealment. The creative faculty, when organised by hatred, produces plans rather than possibilities.

### T3.6 — Volition

**T3.6.1** — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

**E** — Deeply. Two distinct modes. First, hatred of persons (group 1601, 1200) recruits volition toward hostile action: Gen 27:41 — Esau decides, in himself, "then I will kill my brother Jacob" — the volitional resolution is explicit and internal. Num 35:20: "if he pushed him out of hatred or hurled something at him, lying in wait" — the "lying in wait" names a volitional act of prepared hostility. Second, group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred) shows volition as the choice to maintain moral alignment: Psa 26:5 — "I hate the assembly of evildoers, and I will not sit with the wicked" — the "I will not" is a volitional act arising from and expressing the hatred.

**T3.6.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**E** — Both enables and constrains. In the hater: enables action toward a hostile end (Gen 27:41: murder plan; Num 35:20: lying in wait). In the righteous register: constrains certain choices — "I will not sit with the wicked" (Psa 26:5) — the righteous hatred closes off options the person might otherwise take. For the person experiencing hatred from another: Exo 23:5 commands the person to act for the benefit of the one who hates them, requiring a volitional act against the natural impulse. The characteristic therefore both drives volition (toward harm) and can be resisted by a contrary volitional act.

**T3.6.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred is not involuntary passivity — it actively organises the volitional faculty. The settled inner disposition (Lev 19:17's "in your heart") becomes a volitional orientation that drives choices. This is why the prohibition "you shall not hate" in Lev 19:17 is a command — it treats hatred as something the will can and must resist. The volitional dimension is what makes hatred morally culpable.

### T3.7 — Agency

**T3.7.1** — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

**E** — Yes, consistently in group 1601 and 1200. Hatred is named as the inner ground from which hostile action originates. Num 35:20: "if he pushed him out of hatred or hurled something at him" — the hatred is the legal criterion that distinguishes murder from manslaughter. The act of pushing or hurling is the agency expression; the hatred is the inner-being ground that makes it murder. Gen 27:41: Esau's murder plan is the agency expression of his sa.tam. Pro 26:26: "his wickedness will be exposed in the assembly" — the hatred eventually produces public actions that expose it. Hatred drives toward agency — it is not content to remain inner.

**T3.7.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?

**E** — Enables and directs agency toward harmful ends in person-directed hatred. In group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred): directs agency toward just ends — Amo 5:15: "hate evil, and love good, and establish justice in the gate" — the command pairs hatred of evil with the agency act of establishing justice. The hatred of what is wrong is what motivates the active work of making things right. Agency shaped by righteous hatred is constructive; agency shaped by person-directed hatred is destructive.

**T3.7.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred is inherently agency-seeking. The inner disposition is directed outward toward a consequence. This is visible in the legal use of "hatred" as the criterion for murder (Deu 19:11: "if anyone hates his neighbour and lies in wait for him and attacks him") — the law treats hatred not as mere feeling but as the inner cause of a violent act. The characteristic has an inherent trajectory from inner disposition toward outer action.

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**T3.8.1** — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?

**E** — This is the faculty most centrally engaged by M06-A, particularly in group 550-NEW-02. Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil" — the moral evaluation of evil as evil is what produces the hatred. The hatred follows from the evaluation; the evaluation is what gives the hatred its direction. Psa 45:7: "you have loved righteousness and hated wickedness" — the moral evaluation of righteousness (as worthy of love) and wickedness (as worthy of hatred) is the operating faculty. In group 3595 (divine hatred of sins and practices): God's hatred of child sacrifice, false oaths, robbery, and hollow worship is grounded in his moral evaluation of these as violations of his character. The hatred is the inner-being response to the moral evaluation.

**T3.8.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?

**E** — In the righteous register (550-NEW-02): deepens and sharpens moral evaluation. The person who has genuinely evaluated evil as evil, and hatred has settled as the response, is not easily moved toward accommodation of that evil. The hatred is the felt firmness of the moral evaluation. In the disordered register (1601, 1200): bypasses or corrupts moral evaluation of the hated person. The hater does not evaluate the hated person fairly — the hostile disposition prejudices the evaluation. Psa 36:2: the wicked "flatters himself in his own eyes" — his moral self-evaluation is corrupted by a related avoidance of having his iniquity "found out and hated."

**T3.8.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Moral evaluation and hatred are structurally linked — they are not independent faculties operating in parallel but a sequential pair. The evaluation comes first; the hatred follows as its affective and dispositional expression. When the evaluation is accurate (evil is evil), the hatred that follows is righteous. When the evaluation is distorted (by jealousy, by injury, by self-interest), the hatred that follows is disordered. The moral quality of hatred is entirely downstream of the quality of the moral evaluation that grounds it.

### T3.9 — Conscience

**T3.9.1** — Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?

**E** — Engaged, though often in the negative direction. Psa 36:2: "he flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated" — the conscience (the faculty that would find out and hate iniquity) is suppressed or evaded by the wicked person. The verse names an inner-being dynamic: the person's self-flattery is designed to prevent the conscience from doing its work of exposing moral failure. Pro 5:12: "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof!" — the retrospective confession names a prior state in which the hatred of discipline was a suppression of conscience. The person who hates reproof is the person who resists the conscience's work.

**T3.9.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?

**E** — In the righteous register (550-NEW-02): enables and sharpens conscience. Hatred of falsehood (Psa 119:163) is what makes the conscience sensitive to its own departures from truth. The person who hates lying is the person whose conscience is alert to dishonesty. In the disordered register (1601, 1200): impairs. Person-directed hatred suppresses the conscience's capacity to evaluate the hated person fairly, and concealed hatred (Pro 10:18, 26:24) requires the conscience to be overridden in order to maintain the false exterior.

**T3.9.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Hatred and conscience are in a mirroring relationship. Rightly-directed hatred of evil is the expression of a functioning conscience — the conscience has assessed something as sinful and hatred is the settled inner response to that assessment. Disordered hatred of persons suppresses the conscience — the person cannot afford to let the conscience evaluate the situation fairly because it would undermine the hatred. The direction of the hatred reveals the condition of the conscience.

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**T3.10.1** — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action?

**E** — In the righteous register (550-NEW-02), yes. Exo 18:21: men who "hate a bribe" are named as qualified for leadership — the hatred of bribery is not merely a moral attitude but a reliable predictor of conscientious conduct. The integrated response is: moral awareness (bribery is wrong) + volitional commitment (I hate it, meaning I am set against it) + action (therefore I will not take it). Amos 5:15: "hate evil, and love good, and establish justice in the gate" — the sequence moves from inner orientation (hate/love) through to public action (establish justice). The hatred of evil is the inner spring of conscientious public conduct.

**T3.10.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?

**E** — In the righteous register: enables and sustains conscientiousness. The person who genuinely hates bribes (Exo 18:21) is reliably conscientious in contexts where bribery is possible — the inner disposition provides the moral energy for consistent conduct. In the disordered register (person-directed hatred): bypasses conscientiousness. The concealed hater (Pro 26:24) is performing conscientiousness (the "fair" exterior) while the actual inner orientation is hostile. The appearance of conscientiousness masks the absence of it.

**T3.10.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Conscientiousness built on righteous hatred is stable — it is grounded in a settled inner opposition to what is wrong. Conscientiousness performed over concealed hatred is unstable — it will eventually break (Pro 26:26: "his wickedness will be exposed in the assembly"). The inner-being ground of conscientiousness matters.

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**T3.11.1** — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?

**E** — This is the faculty most comprehensively affected by M06-A. Hatred is constitutively relational (it always has an object) and it consistently degrades the relational capacity between persons. Gen 37:4: "they hated him and could not speak peacefully to him" — the direct consequence of hatred is the loss of peaceful speech, which is the basic instrument of relational connection. Pro 15:17: "a fattened ox and hatred with it" — the presence of hatred in a household makes even abundance miserable; it is the relational atmosphere that determines the quality of shared life. The relational capacity, when organised by hatred, is not destroyed but inverted — the capacity for connection remains but is directed against rather than toward the other.

**T3.11.2** — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?

**E** — Impairs deeply in the person-directed register. Deu 21:15-17 (group 550-NEW-01): the hated wife lives under a legal and relational condition of permanent disfavour — the relational capacity of her husband is organised away from her. The law's provision (protecting the disfavoured wife's son's inheritance) is a legal compensation for the relational impairment hatred has caused. In the righteous register (550-NEW-02), hatred of evil may enable genuine relational capacity — the person who hates what destroys others is capable of genuine care for them. Psa 97:10: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil! He preserves the lives of his saints" — the two are connected; hating evil is part of what enables the Lord to be in genuine protective relationship with his people.

**T3.11.3** — What does the pattern reveal?

**E** — Person-directed hatred closes the relational space between persons — the one who is hated is effectively excluded from the full relational capacity of the hater. The hater can no longer see, evaluate, or respond to the hated person through the full range of relational equipment. The relational capacity has been narrowed to the hostile channel only. This is why Lev 19:17's alternative ("you shall reason frankly with your neighbour") is not merely practical advice but a relational restoration — frank speech reopens the relational channel that hatred has closed.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?

**E** — Yes, but in a specific direction. God's hatred is not extended toward the human person as a general posture; it is extended toward wickedness and toward those who have persistently chosen to hate him and violate his covenant. Group 3595 (divine hatred of sins and practices): God hates what humans do — child sacrifice (Deu 12:31), hollow worship (Isa 1:14, Amo 5:21), injustice (Isa 61:8), false oaths (Zec 8:17). Group 3596 (divine response to those who hate God): God directs the consequence of his settled opposition toward those whose inner disposition is hatred of him (Deu 7:10: "repays to their face those who hate him, by destroying them"). God's hatred operates toward the human person as a response to the human person's choices and direction.

**T4.1.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?

**E** — It is consistently responsive, not arbitrary. Hos 9:15: "there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds" — the "because" is explicit. Jer 12:8: "my heritage has become to me like a lion in the forest; she has lifted up her voice against me; therefore I hate her" — the divine hatred follows from Israel's hostility. God's hatred is never presented in the M06-A verses as capricious or without moral grounding. Even Mal 1:3 ("Esau I have hated") — which appears unconditional in its brevity — is grounded in Paul's theological use of it in Romans 9 (outside M06-A) in the context of election, not presented in Malachi itself as arbitrary.

**T4.1.3** — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**E** — God's hatred reveals the depth and seriousness of his moral commitments. He is not indifferent to wickedness — his inner being (nephesh) is set against it. This means that when God acts in judgment against evil, he is not merely applying an external rule but expressing an inner disposition. Psa 5:5-6 shows the dual face: God hates evildoers and abhors the bloodthirsty — this is paired with God's grace toward those who seek him (v.7). The hatred and the welcome exist in the same God, directed toward different objects.

**T4.1.4** — If the evidence is silent on God-to-human operation, note explicitly.

Not applicable — God-to-human operation is evidenced.

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**T4.2.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant?

**E** — Two directions visible. First, human hatred of God — group 3596 names this extensively. The one whose inner disposition is hatred of God is in covenant rupture (Exo 20:5: "those who hate me" are those on whom iniquity is visited generationally). Hating God is the defining inner orientation of the person who has turned away from covenant relationship. Second, human hatred of evil as an act of worship and covenant alignment — group 550-NEW-02. Psa 97:10: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" — the command pairs loving God with hating evil; they are two expressions of the same relational orientation. Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil" — the hatred of evil is the negative expression of what fearing God means positively. Hating what God hates is an act of covenant participation.

**T4.2.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

**E** — For righteous hatred (movement toward God through hating evil): the inner posture required is the fear of the Lord (Pro 8:13) — a prior covenantal orientation toward God that produces the aligned hatred. For hatred of God (movement away from God): the verses do not describe an inner posture so much as a direction — these are people who have turned against God and whose inner disposition reflects that turning. Deu 31:20: "they will turn to other gods...and despise me" — the turning produces the hatred, which then reinforces the turning.

**T4.2.3** — What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?

**E** — Hatred of God names a state of total relational rupture. It is not a momentary failure but a constitutive orientation — Psa 81:15: "those who hate the Lord would cringe toward him, and their fate would last forever" — the hatred has become so settled that even eventual forced acknowledgment (cringing) cannot restore what has been lost. Hatred of evil toward God, by contrast, names the person as aligned — Psa 139:21: "do I not hate those who hate you, O Lord?" — the psalmist's relational orientation is so aligned with God's that he shares God's inner disposition toward those who oppose him.

**T4.2.4** — If the evidence is silent on human-to-God operation, note explicitly.

Not applicable — both directions are evidenced.

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**T4.3.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how?

**E** — Yes, extensively in groups 1601 and 1200. Hatred is given in these verses primarily as a relational act of turning against. Gen 37:4: the brothers extend their hatred toward Joseph through their inability to speak peacefully. 2Sa 13:22: Absalom extends his settled hatred toward Amnon through years of silent enmity before acting. Pro 26:24: the hater extends hatred through disguised speech — the giving of hatred is concealed in false friendliness. The giving of hatred in person-directed cases is not usually a discrete act but an ongoing orientation that shapes every interaction with the hated person.

**T4.3.2** — What inner conditions in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?

**E** — A perceived injury, loss, or violation that has not been resolved or covered. Gen 37:4: the brothers' perception of paternal favouritism is the enabling condition. Gen 27:41: the perceived theft of blessing. 2Sa 13:22: the violation of Tamar. The enabling condition is consistently an unresolved wound — something that has been done (real or perceived) that the inner being has not released. Sa.tam (group 1200) shows that the wound's memory is the persistent fuel.

**T4.3.3** — What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?

**E** — The person must have received (or perceived) an injury that they have not released. The giving of person-directed hatred requires first having held the wound — letting it settle rather than covering it (Pro 10:12: love covers offences; hatred keeps them open). In the righteous register (group 550-NEW-02): the person must have received genuine moral understanding — Psa 119:104's sequence is: precepts → understanding → hatred of the false. To give righteous hatred, the person must first have received the instruction that enables accurate moral evaluation.

**T4.3.4** — If the evidence is silent on the giving direction, note explicitly.

Not applicable — the giving direction is extensively evidenced.

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**T4.4.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another?

**E** — Yes — and uniquely in this corpus, the receiving of hatred is one of the major evidential themes of M06-A. Gen 29:31: "Leah was hated" — the condition of being hated named as a state she inhabits. Gen 37:4: Joseph receives his brothers' hatred. Psa 69:4: "those who hate me without cause" — the psalmist as recipient. Isa 66:5: "your brothers who hate you and cast you out for my name's sake" — being hated for bearing God's name. The receiving of hatred is experienced as a condition — not just a series of hostile acts but a relational state the person is placed in.

**T4.4.2** — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another?

**E** — The verses show that being hated without cause (Psa 69:4, Psa 35:19) is experienced as injustice — the person has not done what would make the hatred comprehensible. The inner condition that makes hatred more bearable appears from the evidence to be: appeal to God (Psa 9:13: "be gracious to me, O Lord! See my affliction from those who hate me"), and the maintenance of integrity (Psa 139:21-22: the psalmist can receive the hatred of God's enemies because his own inner orientation is aligned with God). The inner condition that makes hatred most damaging appears to be isolation without appeal — Psa 69:20: "I looked for pity, but there was none, and for comforters, but I found none" — the breaking of the heart occurs when the hatred is received without any relational resource.

**T4.4.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?

**E** — Job 31:29: "if I have rejoiced at the ruin of him who hated me" — the righteous person does not move into reciprocal hatred when hated. Exo 23:5: "if you see the donkey of one who hates you lying down under its burden, you shall rescue it with him" — the command assumes it is possible to act for the benefit of the person who hates you without reciprocating the hatred. The inner state required is not described directly in M06-A but implied: the person who does not receive hatred back maintains a different inner-being orientation — one that can hold the experience of being hated without reorganising the self around hostile reciprocation.

**T4.4.4** — If the evidence is silent on the receiving direction, note explicitly.

Not applicable — the receiving direction is richly evidenced.

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**T4.5.1** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance?

**E** — Yes, and the distinction is stark. The most intense and damaging hatred in M06-A operates within existing bonds: brothers hating a brother (Joseph's brothers), son hating father's heir (Esau/Jacob), husband withdrawing love from wife (group 550-NEW-01), Amnon's post-violation hatred of Tamar. These cases show that hatred within existing relational bonds is uniquely destructive — it operates in a space where trust and expectation existed, and its violation of that space multiplies the damage. Psa 55:12-13 (outside M06-A but adjacent): "it is not an enemy who taunts me... but it is you, a man, my equal, my companion" — the betrayal by a close associate is named as qualitatively worse than hostility from a stranger.

**T4.5.2** — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?

**E** — It crosses all boundaries. Hatred operates between brothers (within family covenant), between spouses (marital covenant), between Israel and surrounding nations (across covenant boundaries), and between the human person and God (the ultimate covenant). The covenantal context does not prevent hatred but makes it more consequential — divine hatred of Israel's covenant violation (groups 3595, 3596) is grounded in the prior covenant relationship. Hate within covenant is treated as more serious precisely because it violates the relational terms that the covenant established.

**T4.5.3** — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and excluded?

**E** — Person-directed hatred appears to include anyone who becomes the object of the inner hostile disposition — there are no relational categories that are immune. The verses show hatred directed at family members, neighbours, strangers (enemies), God's servants (prophets — 1Ki 22:8), and even God himself (group 3596). Righteous hatred (550-NEW-02) is directed at evil and falsehood, not at persons categorically, though Psa 26:5's "I hate the assembly of evildoers" shows it can have persons as objects when those persons are identified with evil. The scope is effectively unlimited.

**T4.5.4** — If the evidence is silent on relational boundaries, note explicitly.

Not applicable — relational boundary evidence is present.

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**T4.6.1** — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial?

**E (limited)** — Group 1200 (sa.tam): Job 16:9 attributes hatred/grudge-bearing to God in a lament context ("he has torn me in his wrath and hated me; he has gnashed his teeth at me; my adversary sharpens his eyes against me"). Job's language moves between human and divine adversary, and the adversary figure is significant. The Satan figure in Job's framing is the hostile spiritual being whose activity Job is experiencing. Within the M06-A evidence itself, the spiritual-beings interface is thin — no verse explicitly names a spiritual being other than God as the subject of hatred in M06-A.

**T4.6.2** — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — can it be attacked, distorted, or weaponised?

**E** — The evidence from Psa 105:25 is the strongest pointer: "He turned their hearts to hate his people, to deal craftily with his servants" — God himself used the Egyptians' hatred as an instrument of his purposes. This shows that the characteristic can be directed or redirected by a higher agency. Whether adversarial spiritual beings do the same is not directly evidenced in M06-A, but the mechanism is visible: hearts can be turned toward hatred by a power external to the person.

**T4.6.3** — Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry?

**S** — No evidence in M06-A of angelic mediation of hatred.

**T4.6.4** — If the evidence is silent on the spiritual beings interface, note explicitly.

**S (partial)** — The adversarial dimension is present in Job's lament and implicit in Psa 105:25, but explicit spiritual-beings interface is largely silent in M06-A. The interface with the divine being (God as subject of hatred) is extensively evidenced and covered in T4.1.
