## M06-B — Contempt

**Terms (6):** H5006 na.ats · H0937 buz · H7590 shut · H0959 ba.zah · H0963 biz.za.von · H5007B ne.a.tsah
**Verse rows:** 55

These terms name the inner posture of treating something or someone as beneath regard. The verses show contempt operating in three directions.

The most consistent pattern is contempt directed at what is sacred — God's word, God's name, God's servants, and the covenant. Esau despises his birthright; Israel despises the Lord's offering; priests despise his name; fools say in their hearts that God does not call to account. The verb consistently names an inner act of evaluation in which the sacred is deemed worthless by the one who spurns it. The consequences are severe: covenant rupture, divine judgment, and a closing of the person to any further growth or correction.

Contempt directed at reproof or discipline appears as a distinct pattern. Na.ats and buz name the inner posture of the person who refuses correction — who despises his father's instruction, hates reproof, and shuts down the very capacity by which wisdom enters. The verses frame this closing as a self-destructive inner act.

Contempt received from others appears especially in buz — the person who experiences being held in disregard by the proud and the comfortable, and in shut — the nations treating Israel with contempt, which God sees and judges. Est 1:18 shows contempt as socially contagious: one act of defiance spreads the disposition through a whole court.

Contempt, across these verses, is fundamentally an inner act of de-valuation. It is not primarily anger or hostility — it is the inner refusal to assign worth.

## M06-C — Abhorrence

**Terms (5):** G0948 bdelussomai · H8263 she.qets · H8374 ta.av · H8581 ta.av · H1860 de.ra.on
**Verse rows:** 36 (several SA — set aside as ritual classification)

These terms name visceral inner recoil from what is morally or ritually repugnant. Two primary directions appear.

The first is the person's moral revulsion from evil and falsehood. Ta.av (H8581) names this in both directions: the morally alert person abhors lying and evil (Psa 119:163: "I hate and abhor falsehood, but I love your law"), and the person given over to wickedness has inverted this capacity — abhorring justice and truth instead. The abhorrence capacity itself is morally neutral; its direction defines the person. Bdelussomai names a similar inner revulsion — what a person claims as morally repugnant, whether genuinely or hypocritically (Rom 2:22: the person who abhors idols but robs temples).

The second direction is divine sustained abhorrence toward persistent moral corruption. God abhorred his heritage (Psa 106:40), abhorred the proud (ta.av H8374, Amo 6:8), abhorred those who had deeply corrupted themselves. This is not momentary revulsion but settled divine inner response to the accumulation of covenant violation.

De.ra.on (abhorrence) appears in its most severe form — the eschatological abhorrence of those who have rebelled, described in Dan 12:2 and Isa 66:24 as an everlasting condition. Abhorrence here becomes the permanent relational state between the unrighteous and all who see them.

She.qets in its active form (commanded inner detestation) belongs here; its ritual-object uses are set aside.

## M06-D — Cruelty/Ruthlessness

**Terms (4):** H6184 a.rits · H0394 akh.za.ri · H0393 akh.zar · H0395 akh.ze.riy.yut
**Verse rows:** 33 (2 SA)

These terms name a settled inner character quality — not an emotion or a momentary act but the disposition of a person or nation that operates without mercy or restraint. The primary identification is Psa 54:3: ruthless men seek the psalmist's life, "they do not set God before themselves." The absence of God-consciousness is named as the inner-being ground of ruthlessness.

Akh.za.ri names the inner character quality in its sharpest form — cruelty as the operative principle of a person's dealings. Pro 11:17 states it plainly: a cruel man hurts himself, as a kind man benefits himself. The cruel person's destructiveness is reflexive. Pro 12:10 is striking: "the mercy of the wicked is cruel" — even when the wicked attempt what looks like mercy, cruelty is what comes out, because the disposition has corrupted the expression.

The a.rits (ruthless) verses show the ruthless primarily as oppressors. The Psalms position them as those who threaten life (Psa 54:3, 86:14); Isaiah names their pride and arrogance as what God will bring low (Isa 13:11); the prophets describe ruthless nations as instruments and objects of divine judgment. The verses also describe the inner experience of being under ruthless domination — the longing for deliverance from those who do not restrain their destructive power.

Isa 13:9 describes the day of the Lord as "cruel, with wrath and fierce anger" — applying the term to divine action in judgment, showing the term carries the force of absolute, unrestrained severity.

## M06-E — Reproach

**Terms (5):** H8103 shim.tsah · H2781 cher.pah · G5195 hubrizō · H5007A ne.a.tsah · H8146 sa.ni
**Verse rows:** 81

Reproach is the condition experienced by the person who has become an object of public contempt and shame. These terms name what is done to a person, not what a person holds as an inner disposition. The inner-being content is in the recipient.

The verses show reproach operating at two levels. At the personal level, it is the felt inner experience of being despised and mocked — Psa 69:20 names the interior impact precisely: "reproaches have broken my heart, so that I am in despair." Isa 51:7 commands the person who knows righteousness not to fear "the reproach of man" — the command assumes that reproach produces inner fear, and that it requires an inner counterweight. The reproach experience is not merely social embarrassment but an inner shattering.

At the corporate/national level, reproach is a condition God imposes as judgment — Israel becomes a reproach among the nations when covenant faithfulness fails. This usage is extensive across the prophets. But the same verses also show God removing and avenging reproach — Gen 30:23 (God takes away Rachel's reproach of barrenness), Jos 5:9 (God rolls away the reproach of Egypt), Isa 25:8 (God will take away the reproach of his people from all the earth). Reproach is not the final word.

Sa.ni (the disfavoured wife, Deu 21:15) names the lived inner relational condition of being unloved — a social and legal status with profound inner consequences.

Hubrizō names dignitary violation — insolent treatment that strips the person of honour and produces shame.

## M06-F — Hostility/Enmity

**Terms (4):** G0476 antidikos · H3401 ya.riv · H7009 qim · H0340 a.yav
**Verse rows:** 9

These nine verses name the relational structure of being in opposition — the adversary as a structural position in relation to the self. The inner-being content is not primarily in the adversary's disposition but in the response generated in the person who faces structured opposition.

Exo 23:22 is the pivotal verse: God himself says "I will be an enemy to your enemies and an adversary to your adversaries" — adopting the adversary posture on behalf of Israel. This shows enmity as a relational stance that God can take, which gives the posture theological weight.

The ya.riv verses (Psa 35:1, Isa 49:25, Jer 18:19) show the adversary relationship as the occasion of appeal — the person in contest calls out to God to contend on their behalf. The inner-being content is in the urgency and trust of that appeal.

Antidikos in Matthew and Luke names the legal opponent who calls forth urgent response — come to terms quickly, settle on the way. The inner urgency produced by facing a legal adversary is the registerable inner-being dimension. In 1Pe 5:8, the adversary is the devil, and the inner-being response commanded is sobriety and watchfulness — heightened vigilance as the characteristic inner response to facing this adversary.

Job 22:20 uses qim for adversaries cut off by God — the inner response is relief and vindication.

These terms name enmity as relational structure more than as inner disposition, but they consistently generate characteristic inner responses: appeal, urgency, vigilance, and relief.

## M06-G — Malice

**Terms (1):** H7589 she.at
**Verse rows:** 3

Three verses, all from Ezekiel, and all involving the same phenomenon: the soul's active rejoicing in another's destruction.

Eze 25:6 names it most precisely: "you have clapped your hands and stamped your feet and rejoiced with all the malice within your soul against the land of Israel." The term is located explicitly within the soul (nephesh). The malice is not merely an attitude — it expresses itself in physical celebration of another's destruction. The soul takes pleasure in what destroys the other.

Eze 25:15 names the Philistines who "took vengeance with malice of soul to destroy in never-ending enmity." Malice here is described as relentless and purposive — it does not cease because it derives satisfaction from the ongoing destruction. The "never-ending enmity" suggests a self-perpetuating inner orientation that renews itself by dwelling in the destruction of the other.

Eze 36:5 names "wholehearted joy and utter contempt" — two inner-being dimensions running simultaneously. The malice in she.at is not cold hatred but a compound of heated pleasure at another's fall and contempt for their worth. The nations gave God's land to themselves "with utter contempt" — the contempt and the pleasure are inseparable.

Across all three verses: malice is the inner state of the soul that takes pleasure in destruction, driven by deep enmity and contempt operating together. This is why she.at was distinguished from simple hatred and simple contempt — it is a compound that produces something qualitatively different from either alone.

## BOUNDARY

**Terms (4):** H0887 ba.ash · H2778A cha.raph · G0865 afilagathos · H7850 sho.tet
**Verse rows:** 56 (many SA)

These four terms sit at the edge of M06 scope. Their inner-being relevance is real but each operates differently from the core sub-groups.

**H2778A cha.raph (to taunt, 38 verses active)** names the verbal act of contemptuous defiance — what contempt and hostility produce when they are spoken. The verses are extensive: Goliath defying Israel, Assyrian messengers mocking the living God, Sennacherib's letter casting contempt on the Lord, enemies taunting the psalmist daily. The inner-being content is in both the agent (contempt expressed through speech) and the recipient (the inner impact of being taunted). The term is not itself a disposition but the act that dispositions produce. Its presence in M06 is appropriate — it shows what hatred and contempt look like in expression — but it belongs to a different level of the inner-being account.

**H0887 ba.ash (to stink, 16 verses, many SA)** uses stench as a metaphor for moral-reputational odium — the quality that wickedness and folly give off, perceptible to others. Where the term carries inner-being content (2Sa 10:6; 2Sa 16:21; Pro 13:5; Ecc 10:1), it names the quality that moral failure produces in the perception of others. It is a quality term, not a disposition term.

**G0865 afilagathos (hating good, 1 verse)** appears in 2Ti 3:3 as part of a catalogue of the character of the last days — alongside heartless, unappeasable, brutal. The Mounce definition is "not loving good." The term names the absence of orientation toward the good as a character condition. It is a character-disorder term adjacent to M06 but more naturally belonging to the love/disordered-love register.

**H7850 sho.tet (scourge, 1 verse)** names the whip used as a metaphor for hostile domination in Jos 23:13. It is a metaphorical term with minimal inner-being content.
