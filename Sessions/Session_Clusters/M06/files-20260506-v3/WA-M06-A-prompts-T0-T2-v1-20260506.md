## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — The verses show God himself as a subject of hatred. Psa 11:5 locates God's hatred in his soul: "his soul hates the wicked and the one who loves violence." Isa 1:14 has God saying "my soul hates" disordered worship. Jer 12:8 records God saying "I hate her" of his heritage. Hos 9:15 records God saying "there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds." The pattern is consistent: God's hatred is a real inner-being disposition, located in his soul, directed at wickedness, covenant violation, and those who persistently reject him. The hatred is never arbitrary — it is always grounded in what the object of hatred has done or become. This reveals that in God, hatred is the other side of his absolute commitment to righteousness: what he loves perfectly, he opposes perfectly.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?

**E** — Yes, extensively and directly. The groups 3595 (divine hatred of sins/practices, 19 verses) and 3596 (divine response to those who hate God, 14 verses) document this across the corpus. The explicit attribution is not metaphorical hedging — the verses use the same term (sa.ne) for God's hatred as for human hatred. The significance for the human person: hatred is not inherently a disordered or creaturely characteristic. It belongs to God's inner life. When the human person hates what God hates, the verses treat this as alignment with God, not as a moral failure.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**S** — Scripture is not silent here; God's hatred is extensively attested. This prompt does not apply to M06-A.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**E** — Group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred, anchor Pro 8:13) shows this most directly. "The fear of the Lord is hatred of evil" — the verse makes hatred of evil constitutive of what it means to fear God. Psa 97:10 commands it: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" Psa 45:7 grounds the anointed king's authority in it: "you have loved righteousness and hated wickedness; therefore God your God has anointed you." The evidence shows that hatred of evil is not an incidental response but a necessary function of the properly ordered inner life — the negative pole of moral orientation that makes love of the good coherent. A person who loves righteousness without hating wickedness has an incomplete and unstable moral orientation.

**T0.2.2** — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**E / G** — The righteous hatred of evil (group 550-NEW-02) appears to belong to the original created design — it reflects God's own inner life and is described as the substance of fearing God, not as a compensatory response to sin. However, the bulk of the verse evidence (human-to-human hatred, group 1601, and grudge-bearing hatred, group 1200) documents hatred operating in the fallen condition — where it drives murder (Gen 27:41), rape-aftermath (2Sa 13:15), and social violence. The evidence shows both: hatred as a created moral capacity (properly directed toward evil) and hatred as a corrupted expression of that capacity (misdirected toward persons). The verses do not explicitly theorise the pre-fall state of hatred. **G** — whether hatred in its person-directed form existed before the fall, or is entirely a fallen phenomenon, is not directly addressed by the verses in this corpus.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?

**E** — Psa 81:15 shows an eschatological dimension: "those who hate the Lord would cringe toward him, and their fate would last forever" — the person whose inner disposition is hatred of God meets a final reckoning at which that disposition is exposed and its consequence is permanent. Dan 12:2 (in M06-C, de.ra.on) shows "everlasting contempt" as the final condition of those who rebel. Within M06-A specifically, the righteous hatred of evil (group 550-NEW-02) implies a future fullness: when evil is finally eliminated, the hatred directed at it will have served its purpose completely.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?

**E** — The evidence from group 550-NEW-02 shows hatred expressing the divine image specifically through moral alignment — hating what God hates, in the same direction and for the same reasons. Pro 8:13 makes this structural: Wisdom speaks and says "Pride and arrogance and the way of evil and perverted speech I hate" — the hatred of evil is the signature of wisdom, which is itself the expression of the divine image in the human person. Exo 18:21 shows it as a qualification for leadership: men who "hate a bribe" are the ones fit to govern. The image-bearing function of hatred is its role as moral discernment made into an orientation — not just knowing that evil is wrong, but being constitutionally set against it.

**T0.3.2** — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or exclusively creaturely?

**E** — Shared. God hates (extensively evidenced, groups 3595 and 3596). The human person is commanded to hate what God hates (group 550-NEW-02). The sharing is explicit in Psa 139:21: "Do I not hate those who hate you, O Lord?" — the psalmist's hatred is consciously aligned with and derived from God's own. This is not analogy but participation: the human person takes on God's own disposition as an act of alignment.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

**E** — The verses show two directions. The presence of rightly-directed hatred (group 550-NEW-02) reveals the divine image operating — the person's inner life is aligned with God's. Job 34:17 shows the inverse: "shall one who hates justice govern?" — hatred of justice is named as the disqualifying inner disposition, the sign that the image is disordered. In group 1601 (human-to-human hatred), particularly in Lev 19:17 ("you shall not hate your brother in your heart"), person-directed hatred reveals image-disorder: the human person has turned the capacity meant to oppose evil against a fellow image-bearer instead.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**E** — Psa 45:7, cited in Heb 1:9, is the clearest typological use: "You have loved righteousness and hated wickedness; therefore God, your God, has anointed you." The verse is applied in Hebrews to the Son — the perfect hatred of wickedness as a defining characteristic of the Messianic King. The human instance (the righteous king of Psa 45) points toward and is fulfilled in the divine instance (the Son). The typological direction is from the human-royal toward the divine-messianic.

**T0.4.2** — What is the direction of the typology?

**E** — Human-to-divine. The Davidic king's hatred of wickedness establishes the pattern; the Son fulfils it perfectly. The divine instance does not merely repeat the human — it is the completion of what the human instance only partially realised.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note explicitly.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What is this characteristic called in the programme, and what does the name signal about its essential nature?

The sub-group is labelled "Hatred." The name signals a sustained inner orientation — not a momentary reaction but a settled disposition of the person. The grouping under M06-A brings together five terms that share this quality of settlement and direction: they all name the inner being turned against something or someone in a durable way.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

The primary terms are: **sa.ne** (H8130, verb — to hate), **sin.ah** (H8135, noun — hatred as condition), **sa.tam** (H7852 — grudge-bearing persistence), **mas.te.mah** (H4895 — accumulated hostile atmosphere), **se.na** (H8131 — Aramaic cognate, person-category use). At the definitional level: sa.ne is the broadest and most attested — it names the act and state of hating across all registers. Sin.ah names hatred as a thing-that-exists in a person or relationship, giving it noun-weight as a constitutive condition. Sa.tam adds the dimension of persistence and smouldering — hatred that does not let go and drives toward action. Mas.te.mah adds the communal-atmospheric dimension — hatred as a climate that pervades a community. Se.na is used only as a person-type designation (those-who-hate as a category). Together they show that the Hebrew vocabulary distinguishes hatred as act, as condition, as persistence, and as atmosphere.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**E** — Directional and relational. All five terms are inherently relational — they require an object. The verb sa.ne takes a direct object in nearly every verse: one hates *someone* or *something*. The noun sin.ah names hatred as it subsists in a relationship. The direction of the hatred is its defining feature: toward God, toward persons, toward evil, toward discipline. Constitutional implication: Lev 19:17 locates hatred "in your heart" — the heart is the constitutional seat. 2Sa 5:8 has David's soul as the location. The characteristic is at home in both heart and soul levels.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**E** — Primarily a **disposition** — a settled, durable inner orientation — rather than a momentary act or state. Gen 37:5 shows hatred intensifying over time ("they hated him even more"). Gen 27:41 shows sa.tam settling after an event and persisting until an opportunity for action arises. Pro 10:12 treats sin.ah as a force that "stirs up strife" — it acts from within as an ongoing pressure. However, the characteristic also has a **condition** form (sa.ni, group 1664-001 — being hated as a lived condition) and an **act** form (the momentary triggering of hatred in response to a stimulus). The dominant kind across the corpus is disposition.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple or does it appear to have constituent elements?

**E** — It has constituent elements visible across the groups. Hatred in group 1601 (relational human-to-human) involves: a triggering injury or perception ("when his brothers saw that their father loved him more" — Gen 37:4), an inner settlement (the brothers "could not speak peacefully to him"), and a sustained orientation that shapes all subsequent interaction. Hatred in group 1200 (sa.tam, grudge-bearing) adds persistence and the concealment of intent until action is possible (Gen 27:41: Esau plans internally while the mourning period provides waiting time). The constituent elements appear to be: perception of a cause, inner settlement, concealment or expression, and orientation toward a consequence.

**T1.2.3** — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

Hatred (M06-A) is a settled inner disposition of the person oriented against another person, quality, or reality — constitutionally located in the heart and soul, engaging the relational and volitional faculties, and producing either sustained enmity (when directed at persons) or moral alignment (when directed at evil in the pattern of God's own hatred). It is inherently relational and directional, always requiring an object, and it intensifies when not resolved and conceals itself when expression is dangerous.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**E** — Love. This is explicit across the corpus. Pro 10:12: "Hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offences." Pro 15:17: "Better a dinner of herbs where love is than a fattened ox and hatred with it." 2Sa 13:15: "the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her." Ecc 9:6: "their love and their hate...have already perished." Psa 97:10: "O you who love the Lord, hate evil!" — love and hatred are structurally paired throughout. Lev 19:17: "you shall not hate your brother in your heart" — the implicit alternative is love of neighbour (v.18). The structural opposite is not merely the absence of hatred but the positive disposition of love.

**T1.3.2** — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**E** — In group 1601 (relational hatred), hatred excludes peaceful speech (Gen 37:4: "could not speak peacefully"), forgiving response (Gen 50:15: fear of being "paid back"), and covenantal solidarity. In group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred), it excludes accommodation of evil, tolerance of falsehood, and complicity with injustice. Hatred of discipline (Pro 12:1, 15:10) excludes growth and correction. The characteristic resists reconciliation — the verses show no pathway from hatred to restoration that does not first pass through the cessation of the hatred.

**T1.3.3** — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**E** — Anger is not hatred. Hatred is settled; anger can be momentary. Gen 27:41 shows sa.tam (settled hatred) after the anger of the moment has passed — the grudge remains when the heat dissipates. Deu 19:6 distinguishes judicial killing from hateful murder precisely by the absence of prior hatred: "he had not hated his neighbour in the past." Contempt (M06-B) is adjacent but distinct — contempt is primarily a worth-evaluation (treating as beneath regard), while hatred is primarily a relational orientation (being against). One can despise without hating and hate without despising, though both often co-occur.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**E** — Four modes are visible from the verses:

1. **Concealment mode** (Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips"; Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart"). Hatred operates within while the person presents a false exterior. This is an inward-active, outward-hidden mode.

2. **Accumulation mode** (Gen 37:5, 8: "they hated him even more" — incremental intensification). Hatred does not remain static; it grows when fed by new provocations.

3. **Settlement mode** (sa.tam, Gen 27:41: Esau says internally "then I will kill my brother Jacob"). The hatred has settled into a plan — it has found a future direction without yet acting.

4. **Expression mode** (Eze 35:11: "the anger and envy that you showed because of your hatred against them"). Hatred breaks through into visible behaviour when restraint fails or opportunity arrives.

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**E** — Significantly differently:

- **Directed at evil (group 550-NEW-02):** Hatred operates as moral discernment in expression — it functions as a stabilising inner orientation aligned with God's own character.
- **Directed at persons (group 1601):** Hatred operates as relational enmity — it is isolating, concealing, and corrosive.
- **Directed at God (group 3596, the haters of God):** Hatred operates as a constitutive orientation that defines the person's entire covenantal standing and determines their eschatological fate.
- **As atmosphere (mas.te.mah, group 1199):** Hatred operates at a communal level — it is not one person's disposition but the shared inner climate of a community, producing collective spiritual confusion.

**T1.4.3** — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode?

**E** — Yes. Pro 10:18: "the one who conceals hatred has lying lips" — speech is the instrument of concealment. Psa 109:3: "they encircle me with words of hate" — speech as the vehicle of hostile expression. Zec 8:17: "love no false oath, for all these things I hate, declares the Lord" — God's hatred of false speech shows the inverse: that covenant-faithful speech is what hatred of deception produces. The characteristic operates through speech in both its concealed (deceptive) and expressed (hostile) forms.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering hatred?

**E** — From the recipient's perspective: Psa 69:20 (in M06-E, but directly relevant): "reproaches have broken my heart, so that I am in despair." The immediate inner response to being hated is a shattering of the heart. From the group 1202 verses (attributed divine hatred): Israel's immediate response to perceived divine hatred is murmuring and accusation (Deu 1:27). From group 1601 (human relational hatred): the immediate response of the hated person is isolation — "could not speak peacefully to him" (Gen 37:4) names the mutual withdrawal that hatred produces. The immediate responses vary: despair, accusation, withdrawal, or fear (Gen 50:15: brothers feared retribution).

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence?

**E** — Not fully consistent. The response depends on who is doing the hating and who is receiving it. But across the evidence, a pattern of **internal rupture** is consistent — something breaks or closes in the person who encounters hatred directed at them. The peaceful speech that was possible becomes impossible (Gen 37:4); the confidence that was present becomes fear (Gen 50:15); the heart breaks (Psa 69:20). The immediate response is consistently a closing or fracturing of the relational and inner space.

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note explicitly.

The immediate inner response of the one who is being turned into a hater (Psa 105:25: "He turned their hearts to hate his people") is not described — only the outcome is named. The process of how hatred takes hold internally is not detailed in this corpus.

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**E** — In the hater: sustained hatred produces a narrowing of the inner life. Pro 10:18: hatred that is concealed requires lies — the person becomes complicit in self-deception. Pro 26:26: "though his hatred be covered with deception, his wickedness will be exposed" — the sustained concealment eventually produces exposure, but before that it produces a corrupted inner coherence. Gen 27:41: Esau's hatred produced a sustained murderous intention that shaped his entire relationship with Jacob across years. In the hated: sustained hatred from others produces despair (Psa 69:20), fear of abandonment (Gen 50:15), and the chronic condition of disfavour (group 550-NEW-01, the unloved wife across Deuteronomy and Genesis).

**T1.6.2** — What states, qualities, or capacities does sustained possession of this characteristic establish?

**E** — For the hater: readiness for retributive action (Gen 50:15 shows this as an assumption — that sustained hatred will eventually seek repayment). Corruption of speech (Pro 10:18, 26:24). Closure of conscience in certain directions — particularly in group 1199 (mas.te.mah): sustained community-level hatred produces "prophetic madness" (Hos 9:7) — the capacity for spiritual discernment is destroyed by the accumulated inner climate.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**E** — Yes. The immediate response is rupture or closure; the sustained effect is consolidation and corruption. Hatred, sustained, does not remain as pain — it settles into a structural orientation that reshapes the whole inner life around it. The hater becomes organised around the object of hatred in a way that distorts perception (Psa 36:2: the wicked flatters himself, unable to perceive his iniquity "found out and hated"), speech (Pro 26:24), and eventually spiritual capacity (Hos 9:7).

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**Note:** This prompt was designed for characteristics that are received as a gift (like grace). For M06-A (Hatred), the relevant question is: what conditions enable hatred to take hold and settle in the inner person?

**E** — The verses show several entry conditions. Perceived injustice or loss: Gen 37:4 — the brothers' hatred entered when they perceived their father's favouritism. Violated trust or honour: Gen 27:41 — Esau's hatred entered at the moment of perceived theft of blessing. Moral and spiritual drift: Hos 9:15 — God's hatred of Israel began "there" (at Gilgal, a place of disobedience) "because of the wickedness of their deeds." The condition that enables hatred to settle is a prior injury — real or perceived — to something the person values or claims. The injury then provides the object and the justification.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block or prevent hatred from settling?

**E** — The verses show love as the primary blocking condition: Pro 10:12: "love covers all offences" — what could become the seed of hatred is covered rather than nursed. Lev 19:17's command to reason frankly with a neighbour rather than hating in the heart suggests that honest confrontation prevents the inner settlement of hatred. The verses in group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred) show that hatred of evil, when properly oriented, prevents hatred of persons — the person who hates what God hates is not free-floating in their capacity to hate.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters hatred from another but does not receive it (does not become hateful in return)?

**E** — The evidence here is thin within M06-A itself. Job 31:29 shows the negation: "If I have rejoiced at the ruin of him who hated me..." — the righteous person does not rejoice when their hater suffers, implying they have not entered into a reciprocal hatred. Exo 23:5: "If you see the donkey of one who hates you..." — the command assumes the person can act for their enemy's good despite being hated, which requires an inner state where the hatred of the other has not colonised the self.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?

**E** — From the verse evidence: the primary dimension is **Relational Disposition**. Hatred is constitutively relational — it always requires an object, always involves the positioning of the self against another. The heart and soul are its constitutional locations, but the faculty most consistently engaged is the relational capacity — the capacity for connection and orientation toward others, which hatred organises in a hostile direction.

**T1.8.2** — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

**E** — Every group in M06-A involves a relationship: 1601 (person against person), 550-NEW-01 (spouse against spouse), 550-NEW-02 (person against evil, in alignment with God's relational stance), 3595 and 3596 (God's relational stance toward sin and toward those who reject him), 1200 (sustained relational grudge), 1201 (hatred as the noun-quality of a relational condition), 1199 (communal relational climate). The only group with reduced relational content is 1602 (se.na as person-type designation), and even there the relational adversary structure is present.

**T1.8.3** — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?

**E** — Yes. **Moral Character** is a secondary dimension — particularly in groups 550-NEW-02 and 3595, where hatred functions as a moral quality (the righteous person's inner alignment with God's opposition to evil). **Emotion — Negative** is a secondary dimension — the affective quality of hatred is present throughout, particularly in group 1200 (sa.tam: grudge-bearing with anger) and in the visceral intensity of 2Sa 13:15 (Amnon's hatred "greater than the love"). The emotion is present but it is not the primary dimension — the relational disposition is more fundamental than the affective experience.

### T2.2 — Soul-Level Location

**T2.2.1** — Is this characteristic identified as a soul-level phenomenon?

**E** — Yes, explicitly. Psa 11:5: "his soul (nephesh) hates the wicked and the one who loves violence" — God's hatred located in the soul. 2Sa 5:8: "hated by David's soul (nephesh)" — David's hatred is located in his soul. Isa 1:14: "my soul (nephesh) hates" — God's hatred again explicitly soul-located. The soul-location is direct and recurring.

**T2.2.2** — What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in innermost personal experience?

**E** — The soul (nephesh) in these verses is the seat of the person's deepest personal experience — it is where the person's most fundamental orientations are located. When Psa 11:5 says God's soul hates, it names hatred as a disposition of God's innermost personal being, not merely a judicial decision or external stance. When David's soul hates (2Sa 5:8), the hatred is not merely a social attitude but an inner-personal reality. Soul-level location makes hatred a characteristic of the person's deepest self — which is why hatred in the heart (Lev 19:17) is treated as a sin even when it never produces an act.

**T2.2.3** — If the evidence is silent on soul-level location, note explicitly.

Not applicable — soul-level location is evidenced.

### T2.3 — Heart

**T2.3.1** — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?

**E** — Yes, explicitly. Lev 19:17: "you shall not hate your brother in your heart (levav)" — the heart is named as the constitutional location of hatred. Pro 26:24: "whoever hates disguises himself with his lips and harbours deceit in his heart (levav)" — hatred concealed in the heart while lips present otherwise. Zec 8:17: "do not devise evil in your hearts (levav) against one another" — the heart is where hostile schemes originate.

**T2.3.2** — What does the heart-location reveal?

**E** — The heart in these verses is the integrating centre where intention, planning, and relational orientation converge. Lev 19:17's command "not in your heart" is precise: hatred at the heart level is the problem, even before any act. This means hatred is culpable as an inner state, not only as a behaviour. Pro 26:24 shows the heart as the hiding place — hatred can be active in the heart while the lips present false peace, revealing that the heart is where the real disposition lives, prior to and independent of speech or action.

**T2.3.3** — If the evidence is silent on heart-location, note explicitly.

Not applicable — heart-location is evidenced.

### T2.4 — Mind

**T2.4.1** — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?

**S / E (borderline)** — No verse in M06-A explicitly names the mind (lev in its cognitive function, or the Greek nous) as the location of hatred. However, group 1199 (mas.te.mah, Hos 9:7-8) shows hatred producing a corruption of the prophetic/cognitive capacity — "the prophet is a fool; the man of the spirit is mad, because of your great iniquity and great hatred." The accumulated inner climate of hatred has destroyed the community's capacity for spiritual discernment and understanding. This is a cognitive consequence of hatred, not a mind-location of hatred itself.

**T2.4.2** — What does mind-location reveal?

**E (indirect)** — While hatred is not explicitly located in the mind, the evidence shows it corrupts cognitive function. Psa 36:2: the wicked person "flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated" — hatred (or the avoidance of it) has distorted self-perception. The characteristic impairs the mind's capacity for accurate moral evaluation of the self.

**T2.4.3** — If the evidence is silent on mind-location, note explicitly.

**S** — Direct mind-location is not evidenced. The cognitive effects of hatred are evidenced but not a mind-location per se.

### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**T2.5.1** — Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind?

**E** — The will/volition dimension of the soul is surfaced implicitly in the sa.tam verses. Gen 27:41: Esau's hatred produces a resolution — "then I will kill my brother Jacob." The settled hatred has organised the volitional capacity around a future act. The hatred does not merely exist as feeling; it has become an intention. This is a volitional sub-dimension of the soul engaged by hatred.

**T2.5.2** — If so, what does it reveal?

**E** — It reveals that hatred, when it settles, does not remain as affect — it recruits the volitional faculty and converts itself into a plan. The will becomes organised around the object of hatred and oriented toward a response. This is why Lev 19:17's prohibition of hatred "in the heart" is not merely about emotion but about the inner organisation of the person's will around a hostile intention.

**T2.5.3** — If the evidence is silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable — some surfacing is evidenced.

### T2.6 — Body — Significance

**T2.6.1** — Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?

**E** — Several body-part links appear in the corpus. Psa 55:3 (sa.tam): "in anger they bear a grudge against me" — no specific body part. But in the broader M06-A verse set: Eze 25:6 (M06-G malice, but adjacent): "you have clapped your hands and stamped your feet" — body as expressive instrument of inner hostility. Within M06-A: the hand appears frequently as the instrument of hatred's expression (2Sa 22:18: "those who hated me, for they were too mighty for me" — deliverance from the hand of the hater). The gnashing of teeth (Job 16:9: sa.tam context, "he has gnashed his teeth at me") names a body-part expression of hostile inner state.

**T2.6.2** — What is Scripture doing by making that link?

**E** — The gnashing of teeth (Job 16:9) is expressive — it names the body's involuntary participation in the inner hostile state. It shows that hatred, at its most intense, cannot be fully contained internally but erupts into somatic expression. The hand as instrument of the hater's violence shows the characteristic moving from inner disposition to bodily action.

**T2.6.3** — If no body-part link is evidenced, note explicitly.

Not applicable — some body-part linkage is evidenced.

### T2.7 — Body — Direction

**T2.7.1** — Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run?

**E** — The direction is soul/heart → body. The inner disposition of hatred produces somatic expression (gnashing, physical violence, stamping). There is no evidence in M06-A of the body feeding hatred back into the soul — the verses consistently show the inner disposition driving outward expression, not the reverse.

**T2.7.2** — What is the consequence of that directionality?

**E** — It confirms that hatred is constitutionally an inner-being phenomenon that expresses outward, not a behavioural pattern that creates an inner state. This is consistent with Lev 19:17's prohibition of inner hatred: the problem is in the heart, and the body's actions are downstream of that. Addressing the behaviour without addressing the inner disposition leaves the characteristic intact.

**T2.7.3** — If no body-characteristic link is evidenced, note explicitly.

Not applicable.

### T2.8 — Body — Deposit

**T2.8.1** — Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit?

**E** — Exo 20:5 (and Deu 5:9), the anchor for group 3596: God "visits the iniquity of the fathers on the children to the third and fourth generation of those who hate me." The sustained inner disposition of hating God produces a consequence that extends beyond the individual into the generations — not merely as social inheritance but as covenantal consequence. This is the clearest statement in M06-A of a generational deposit: hatred of God carried long enough by a person or community generates a consequence that their descendants inherit.

**T2.8.2** — What evidence supports or contradicts this?

**E** — Supports: Exo 20:5, Deu 5:9 — both explicitly name generational visitation of iniquity as the consequence of hating God. The pairing of "those who hate me" with the generational consequence is structural, not incidental. Psa 105:25 (in the 1601 group): "He turned their hearts to hate his people" — divine action producing a generational pattern of hostility in Egypt toward Israel. The deposit from one generation's hatred shapes the next generation's inner orientation.

**T2.8.3** — If the evidence is silent, note explicitly.

Not applicable — generational deposit is evidenced, specifically in the context of hatred of God.

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**T2.9.1** — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally?

**E** — The verses show multiple origins:

- **From perceived injury or injustice:** Gen 37:4 (brothers' jealousy of father's favour → hatred of Joseph); Gen 27:41 (perceived theft of blessing → Esau's hatred); 2Sa 13:22 (Absalom's hatred of Amnon originating from Amnon's violation of Tamar).
- **From sustained moral corruption:** Hos 9:15 ("there I began to hate them. Because of the wickedness of their deeds") — divine hatred originates in the accumulation of covenant violation.
- **From divine action:** Psa 105:25 ("He turned their hearts to hate his people") — hatred can originate from outside the person through divine action, introduced into the heart by God for his purposes.
- **From righteous formation:** Group 550-NEW-02 — righteous hatred of evil originates from the person's love of God and alignment with his character (Pro 8:13: "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil").

**T2.9.2** — What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?

**E** — Multiple. The origins are genuinely diverse and the verses do not reduce them to one source. Hatred can arise from injury, from moral corruption, from divine action, or from righteous alignment. The origin determines the direction and moral quality of the hatred.

**T2.9.3** — Does the origin change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?

**E** — Yes, as described above. The same term (sa.ne) names hatred originating from jealousy (Gen 37:4), from violated honour (Gen 27:41), from moral alignment with God (Pro 8:13), and from divine action (Psa 105:25). The term itself does not carry origin-information — that is determined by context.

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**T2.10.1** — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels?

**E** — The evidence shows movement from soul/heart → expression in speech and body (as noted in T2.6-2.7). But there is also evidence of movement within the inner being: Pro 5:12 — "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof" — the hatred in the heart produced a despising of reproof, which is a different inner faculty (contempt/moral evaluation). The hatred at heart level activated a contempt at the evaluation faculty level. The movement is not across body-soul-spirit levels but within the soul from disposition to evaluation and then to volitional intention (Gen 27:41).

**T2.10.2** — What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?

**E** — The pattern visible from the verses: perceived injury → inner settlement (heart) → volitional organisation (plan/intention) → concealment in speech → eventual expression or action. Pro 26:24-26 shows the full internal sequence most clearly: hatred active in the heart (v.24), concealed in speech (v.24-25), eventually exposed by the assembly (v.26).

**T2.10.3** — If no constitutional movement is evidenced, note explicitly.

Not applicable — movement is evidenced.
