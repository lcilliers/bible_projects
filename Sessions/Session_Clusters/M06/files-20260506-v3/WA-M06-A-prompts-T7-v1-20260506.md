## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1** — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?

**E** — Five terms constitute M06-A. Reading what the verse data and group descriptions reveal about their roots and meanings:

**H8130 sa.ne** — the primary verb, "to hate." The root carries the sense of sustained hostility — it is not the vocabulary of momentary displeasure but of settled opposition. Its grammatical range includes both active (hating) and passive (being hated) forms, showing that the term covers the full relational axis. The sheer frequency (the dominant term in the sub-group with 139 active verses across all groups) confirms sa.ne as the lexical anchor of the whole characteristic.

**H8135 sin.ah** — the noun form of the same root. "Hating" as a condition or quality — hatred as a thing that exists, subsists, and can be named as a substance in a relationship. Pro 10:12: "Hatred stirs up strife" — sin.ah acts as a causal force in the sentence. The noun-form gives hatred a kind of weight and persistence that the verb alone does not carry.

**H7852 sa.tam** — a distinct root from sa.ne, carrying the sense of bearing a grudge, maintaining a settled hostile disposition across time. Gen 27:41 shows its character: the hatred does not discharge in a single act but persists, waits, and continues to orient the person toward a future harm. The root's distinctiveness from sa.ne is evidenced by its consistent use in contexts of smouldering, sustained enmity rather than immediate hostile response.

**H4895 mas.te.mah** — "hatred, hostility, enmity" as an inner quality or atmosphere. Group 1199 (Hos 9:7-8) shows it functioning as a communal inner climate — not the hatred of one person toward another but the accumulated inner disposition of a whole people. The term names hatred as an atmosphere rather than an act.

**H8131 se.na** — Aramaic cognate of sa.ne, appearing only in Dan 4:19. Used as a person-category designation ("those who hate you") rather than as a developed inner-being term in its own right. Its Aramaic form confirms the root's cross-linguistic reach without adding distinct semantic content.

**T7.1.2** — What is the grammatical range of the primary term — and what does that range reveal?

**E** — H8130 sa.ne operates across multiple grammatical forms visible from the verse data:

- **Verb (qal active):** "they hated him" (Gen 37:4), "I hate" (Isa 1:14) — the act of hating as an ongoing state.
- **Verb (qal passive):** "Leah was hated" (Gen 29:31), "hated by David's soul" (2Sa 5:8) — the condition of being the object of hatred.
- **Participle as noun (agent noun):** "those who hate me" (Exo 20:5), "those who hate you" (Deu 33:11) — the hater as a person-type, a person defined by their inner disposition.
- **Infinitive construct:** used in legal texts as the criterion of murder — "having hated him in the past" (Deu 19:4), "without being at enmity with him" (Deu 4:42).

This range reveals that hatred is not merely an act that happens and passes. The grammatical forms treat it as a condition (passive), a defining characteristic of a person-type (agent noun), and a legally operative prior state (infinitive construct). The grammar itself encodes the sustained, dispositional character of the term.

**T7.1.3** — What is the semantic range of the primary term?

**E** — From the thirteen groups in M06-A, the semantic range of sa.ne spans:

- Relational enmity between persons (group 1601 — the dominant use)
- Marital disfavour with legal consequence (group 550-NEW-01)
- Commanded righteous orientation away from evil (group 550-NEW-02)
- Divine sustained opposition to sin, practices, and objects (group 3595)
- Divine response to those whose inner disposition is hatred of him (group 3596)
- Underlying "enemy/foe" as a category term (group 550-002)

The breadth is significant: the same term covers human relational hatred, legal disfavour, moral-alignment hatred of evil, and divine inner dispositions. The common thread across all uses is the settled hostile orientation — the relational capacity organised against something or someone.

**T7.1.4** — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?

**E** — Yes, explicitly:

- **Disposition (ongoing state):** sa.ne in its active form, sin.ah as noun-quality — both name the inner state as it persists.
- **Act:** sa.ne in legal contexts where it names the prior disposition that produces a specific act (Deu 19:11: "hates his neighbour and lies in wait").
- **Received condition:** sa.ne passive (Gen 29:31: "Leah was hated"), sa.ni (H8146, group M06-E: "the disfavoured/hated wife" — the state of being the object of hatred).
- **Quality / atmosphere:** mas.te.mah (H4895) — hatred as the inner quality of a community's spiritual climate, not a specific act or state of one person.
- **Persistent grudge-form:** sa.tam (H7852) — adds the temporal-persistence dimension to the basic hatred vocabulary.

The five terms together cover the full spectrum: act, disposition, received condition, quality, and persistence. This is an unusually complete vocabulary for a single characteristic.

**T7.1.5** — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?

**E** — Love (a.ha.vah / H0160) is the structural opposite, evidenced explicitly and repeatedly in the verse data: Pro 10:12, Pro 15:17, 2Sa 13:15, Ecc 9:6, Psa 97:10, Judg 14:16. The pairing is grammatically and structurally explicit throughout the corpus. A.ha.vah is in a different registry cluster (C17, the love cluster) but its structural relationship with sa.ne is the most persistent cross-cluster connection in M06-A.

**T7.1.6** — Does the vocabulary include a person-type term — for the one who habitually possesses this characteristic?

**E** — Yes. The agent-noun form of sa.ne functions as a person-type: "those who hate me" (sa.ne + suffix), "those who hate you" — these designate a category of person defined by their inner disposition. It is not a separate lexeme but a grammatical form of the primary term. Group 1602 (se.na, Dan 4:19) uses the Aramaic equivalent purely as a person-type designation. The "enemy" (o.yev / H0341) appears consistently in parallel with the hater across many verses (2Sa 22:18, Psa 18:40, Psa 44:7) — the enemy is functionally the person-type for one whose inner disposition toward another is hatred.

**T7.1.7** — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — for seeking this characteristic from another?

**S** — No term in M06-A names the act of seeking hatred from another, nor would one expect it. Hatred is not the kind of characteristic that is sought; it is experienced as received (in its passive form) or held (in its active form). The absence of a seeking-term is structurally appropriate to the characteristic's nature.

**T7.1.8** — What does LXX use reveal about continuity or development across the Testaments?

The Greek miseo (to hate) is the standard LXX and NT equivalent for sa.ne, and it appears in NT texts beyond this corpus. The continuity appears strong — the same relational-hostility vocabulary carries through. Precise LXX mapping flagged as G for CC query.

**T7.1.9** — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic?

**G** — The NT introduces G0865 afilagathos ("not loving good / hating good") in 2Ti 3:3 — this is a compound negative form that names the absence of love-for-good as a form of the hatred characteristic. Whether this represents genuine coinage in the NT period or draws on Hellenistic moral vocabulary requires lexical database query. What the verse evidence shows is that this term applies the characteristic in a vice-catalogue context alongside heartlessness, unappeasability, and brutality — the company it keeps in 2Ti 3:3 clarifies its register as a character-disorder term.

**T7.1.10** — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?

**E** — The five M06-A terms together reveal that hatred in the biblical vocabulary is not a simple emotion but a complex inner-being phenomenon spanning:

- Acts and conditions
- Individual and communal registers

The vocabulary arc moves from the fundamental verb (sa.ne — to hate) through the noun-quality (sin.ah — hatred as thing-that-subsists), the persistence-form (sa.tam — grudge-bearing), the communal-climate form (mas.te.mah — accumulated hostility), and the person-type designation (se.na — those who hate). This is a vocabulary that has been differentiated by use over time to cover the full range of the characteristic's operation. The range is not conceptually constructed but emerges from the literary evidence across genres and centuries.

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

**E** — The anchor verse for M06-A is Lev 19:17: "You shall not hate your brother in your heart, but you shall reason frankly with your neighbour, lest you incur sin because of him."

Sa.ne functions here as the prohibited inner-being state — the object of the prohibition. The sentence structure is: prohibition (do not hate) + location (in your heart) + alternative command (reason frankly) + consequence (lest you incur sin). Sa.ne carries the full weight of the prohibition's object. By naming hatred specifically in the heart, the verse makes the inner state — not the external act — the locus of the prohibition. The argument of the verse is that the inner state of hatred is morally culpable before it produces any act, and that the alternative to it is honest relational engagement, not merely behavioural restraint.

**T7.2.2** — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?

**E** — M06-A's evidence is distributed across five primary literary forms:

**Legal text (Torah, Deuteronomy):** Lev 19:17, Deu 19:4, 19:11, Num 35:20 — hatred as a legal criterion. The legal form requires reading the term precisely within its legislative function: what distinguishes intentional from unintentional killing, what defines the state of enmity that makes an act murder. The legal precision is not metaphorical.

**Narrative (Genesis, Samuel):** Gen 37:4-8, Gen 27:41, 2Sa 13:15, 13:22 — hatred embedded in plot. The narrative form requires tracking the term across a developing sequence: hatred is introduced at one point in the story and its consequences unfold across subsequent events. The narrative is not primarily about the term but the term reveals character and drives action within the story.

**Psalm / lament:** Psa 25:19, 69:4, 109:3-5, 139:21-22 — hatred as the experience of the speaker and as the psalmist's own moral orientation. The lament form requires distinguishing between the psalmist's description of being hated (which is the recipient's inner experience) and the psalmist's statement of their own righteous hatred (which is a moral declaration). Both are first-person but they name different inner-being phenomena.

**Wisdom / Proverb:** Pro 10:12, 10:18, 26:24-26, 8:13 — hatred as a structural force in the moral landscape. The wisdom form requires reading the term in its aphoristic compression: what is stated as a generalisation ("hatred stirs up strife") has been observed as a pattern across many cases, not described from one instance. The wisdom form licenses the structural observation but requires testing against the narrative evidence.

**Prophetic:** Isa 1:14, Amos 5:10-15, Hos 9:7-8, Jer 12:8 — hatred in divine address and national diagnosis. The prophetic form requires attending to the speaker (God or the prophet on God's behalf) and the addressee (Israel or specific communities) and the covenantal context in which the hatred-language functions.

**T7.2.3** — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence?

**E** — Three key arguments visible from the verse evidence:

**Argument 1 — Lev 19:17:** If you harbour hatred in your heart toward your brother without frank confrontation, you bear sin. The argument's structure: inner state (hatred) + failure to confront (silence) = moral culpability (sin). The logical premise is that the inner state is morally binding before any act; the responsible path is honest engagement.

**Argument 2 — Pro 8:13:** The fear of the Lord IS hatred of evil. The logical structure is an identity claim — not "the fear of the Lord leads to hatred of evil" but "the fear of the Lord is hatred of evil." This is a definitional argument: righteous hatred of evil is not a downstream consequence of fearing God but a constitutive part of what that fear is.

**Argument 3 — Exo 20:5:** The person whose inner disposition is hatred of God is the person on whom iniquity falls generationally. The argument's structure: inner disposition (hating God) = covenantal consequence (generational iniquity-visitation). The premise is that inner orientation determines covenantal standing; the conclusion is that the orientation has consequences extending beyond the individual.

**T7.2.4** — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence — and what does that setting reveal?

**E** — The primary verse evidence spans multiple settings, each revealing a different dimension:

**Legal/judicial:** Deu 19, Num 35 — hatred as the inner criterion distinguishing murder from manslaughter. This setting reveals that hatred is treated by the law as an objectively determinable prior state, not merely a subjective feeling. The law treats inner disposition as legally operative.

**Covenantal:** Exo 20:5, Deu 5:9 — hatred as the defining inner orientation of covenant-breakers, with generational consequence. This setting reveals hatred's place in the entire theological structure of covenant relationship.

**Liturgical/Psalmic:** Psa 139:21-22, Psa 97:10 — the worshipping person's declaration of righteous hatred as an act of alignment with God. This setting reveals that hatred of evil is not merely a private disposition but can be voiced before God as an act of worship and self-definition.

**Wisdom:** Pro 8:13, Pro 10:12 — hatred as a structural force in the moral order. This setting reveals hatred's place in the architecture of the inner life — how it relates to wisdom, to love, to strife.

**T7.2.5** — Does any verse function as the primary anchor — the verse that most fully and directly expresses the essential character of this characteristic?

**E** — Lev 19:17 has been confirmed as the anchor for group 1601 and functions as the primary definitional verse for the whole M06-A sub-group. It directly names the constitutional location (the heart), the relational form (toward a brother), the moral status (sin), and the alternative (frank confrontation). No other verse in the corpus covers all four of these dimensions simultaneously.

For the righteous register, Pro 8:13 is the anchor for group 550-NEW-02 and functions as the definitional verse for that form of the characteristic: it makes righteous hatred constitutive of the fear of God, not merely associated with it.

**T7.2.6** — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?

**E** — Lev 19:17 reveals three things uniquely:

First, it names the **constitutional location precisely**: "in your heart" — this is the only verse in M06-A that explicitly specifies the heart as the location of hatred in a commandment. It makes the inner location the operative legal ground.

Second, it names the **alternative explicitly**: "you shall reason frankly with your neighbour." The verse does not merely prohibit hatred; it prescribes what replaces it. No other M06-A verse specifies what the inner transformation from hatred requires in relational practice.

Third, it **links the inner state to moral culpability before any act**: "lest you incur sin because of him." The structure makes the concealed inner state of hatred itself the sin — not only the act that hatred might eventually produce. This is the verse that most directly grounds the programme's understanding of hatred as culpable at the inner-being level.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?

**E** — The primary field, following the programme's science-in-action framework, is **clinical and social psychology** — specifically the psychology of hatred, hostility, and aggression, and the broader area of moral emotion research. Secondary fields are **developmental psychology** (formation of hatred and prejudice over time) and **neuroscience** (the neural substrates of sustained hostile states and their somatic expression).

The programme's science framework positions clinical and social psychology as the primary lens for soul operations — emotion, moral psychology, the psychology of will. Hatred, as evidenced in M06-A, is a sustained inner disposition with affective, volitional, cognitive, and relational dimensions — precisely the territory clinical and social psychology covers.

**T7.3.2** — Where does the human science framework illuminate the verse evidence?

**E** — Three convergence points are visible from the evidence:

**Convergence 1 — The distinction between anger and hatred:** Clinical psychology distinguishes acute anger (episodic, stimulus-bound, temporally limited) from chronic hostility (dispositional, sustained, characterological). This maps precisely onto what the verse evidence shows: the Joseph narrative (Gen 37:4-8) and the sa.tam material (Gen 27:41, Psa 55:3) both show hatred as sustained, dispositional, and independent of the initial trigger — which corresponds to the chronic hostility category, not acute anger. The scientific distinction illuminates why the verse evidence distinguishes hatred (sa.ne, sa.tam) from anger (a.phon / H0639) as different phenomena.

**Convergence 2 — The rumination mechanism:** Research in clinical psychology identifies rumination — the repetitive mental rehearsal of perceived injury — as the primary mechanism sustaining chronic hostility. Group 1200 (sa.tam — grudge-bearing) is evidenced by Gen 50:15 (brothers' fear that Joseph has been rehearsing the injury) and Job 16:9 (the perceived grudge maintained across time). The science names the mechanism; the verse evidence names the phenomenon. The convergence is mechanistic: the biblical grudge-bearing corresponds to what clinical psychology calls rumination-sustained hostility.

**Convergence 3 — The generational transmission:** Developmental psychology and epigenetics research documents that early exposure to hostile relational environments shapes the developing person's attachment patterns and inner-life architecture. Psa 105:25 (God turning the Egyptians' hearts toward hatred of Israel) and Exo 20:5 (iniquity visited to the third and fourth generation of those who hate God) name generational effects of sustained hostility. The science provides mechanistic description (epigenetic transmission, attachment disruption in subsequent generations) of what the verse evidence names as covenantal consequence.

**T7.3.3** — Where do the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge?

**E** — Two divergences:

**Divergence 1 — The moral quality of hatred:** Clinical psychology treats hatred as pathological — a dysfunction of the hostility system. The verse evidence does not treat all hatred as pathological. Group 550-NEW-02 (righteous hatred of evil) is presented as commanded, praised, and constitutive of the fear of God (Pro 8:13). The scientific framework has no category for morally required or virtuous hatred; it treats all sustained hostile dispositions as problems to be reduced. The verse evidence explicitly contradicts this by naming hatred of evil as an ordered capacity essential to the inner life. This is a genuine divergence — not register divergence but a difference in what constitutes the properly ordered inner life.

**Divergence 2 — The divine subject:** Clinical and social psychology treats hatred as a human phenomenon explicable by social, developmental, and neurological factors. The verse evidence extensively documents God as the subject of hatred (groups 3595, 3596) — God's soul hates, God's inner disposition is set against wickedness. The science has no category for this. This is not a conflict to resolve but a boundary of the scientific lens — it describes the human phenomenon but cannot engage the divine subject.

**T7.3.4** — Does the human science framework surface any aspect not yet addressed by the verse evidence?

**E** — Yes, one specific gap: the science of **prejudice and in-group/out-group hostility** is highly developed in social psychology, documenting how group identity shapes the direction and intensity of hostile dispositions — how people come to hate particular categories of persons based on group membership. The verse evidence documents this phenomenon (Egypt hating Israel, surrounding nations hating Zion) but does not articulate the mechanism by which group-identity-based hatred operates at the inner-being level. The social psychology of prejudice would surface this mechanism explicitly. This gap does not require additional verse investigation (the phenomenon is evidenced) but the mechanistic description from science would add explanatory depth to the communal-level hatred documented in groups 1199 (mas.te.mah) and 3596 (those who hate God as a category).
