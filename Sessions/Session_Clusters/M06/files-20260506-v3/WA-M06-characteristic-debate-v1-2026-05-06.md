## Purpose

This document records the debate and reasoning on whether M06 represents a single inner-being characteristic or a family of distinct characteristics — and if multiple, how they are distinguished. The debate begins by grounding the definition of a characteristic in the programme prose, then works through the gloss list systematically.

---

## Step 1 — What is a characteristic? Reading from the programme prose

The programme prose does not use the word "characteristic" as a defined technical term in a single place. It is used throughout as a natural descriptor. Drawing from the programme prose, a characteristic can be identified from the following sources:

**From the working definition of inner-being (section: Defining Inner Being):**

> *Inner-being characteristics are the non-physical, internal states, capacities, and expressions that constitute a person's invisible life — encompassing how a person thinks, feels, chooses, relates, and orients themselves toward meaning, others, and God.*

This is the programme's formal definition. A characteristic is therefore:
- Non-physical and internal
- A *state*, a *capacity*, or an *expression*
- Part of how a person thinks, feels, chooses, relates, or orients themselves

**From T1 (Definition tier):**

T1 asks three things about a characteristic: its **Name**, its **Kind**, and its **Boundary**. Kind is described as:

> *a description that encapsulates the characteristic within the constitutional location it occupies, the faculties it engages, and the impact it produces in the person.*

This is the closest the programme comes to defining what distinguishes one characteristic from another: two things are distinct characteristics if they differ in constitutional location, in which faculties they engage, or in the impact they produce in the person.

**From T6 (Structural Relationships):**

> *No characteristic exists in isolation. Every characteristic has structural relationships — some characteristics precede others, some produce others, some exclude others, some co-occur consistently.*

This implies that characteristics can be distinguished by their causal and sequential relationships to one another — one can precede or produce another, which means they are distinguishable.

**From the methodological principles:**

> *Words are not forced into preconceived categories or assumptions. Dimensions of the inner being are identified from the verse evidence; groups are formed from what the verses engage; findings emerge from the questions applied to the data.*

This means the question of whether M06 is one characteristic or several must be answered from the verse evidence, not from conceptual tidiness.

**Synthesised working definition of a characteristic for this debate:**

A distinct inner-being characteristic is an internal state, capacity, or expression that:
1. Has an identifiable **constitutional location** (where it lives in the person)
2. Engages a distinguishable set of **inner faculties** (what inner capacities it activates)
3. Produces a recognisable **impact** in the person (what it does to or in the person)
4. Has a **structural opposite** that defines its boundary
5. Can be distinguished from adjacent characteristics by its causal direction, sequential position, or directional object

If two glosses differ in *all five* of these dimensions, they are almost certainly distinct characteristics. If they share all five, they are most likely variant forms of one. The interesting cases are those that share some but not all.

## Step 3 — Is this one characteristic or several?

### The case for one characteristic

There is a surface reading that sees this whole cluster as a single unified characteristic: the capacity of the inner person to be *against* something or someone — to orient the self in opposition, rejection, or hostility. Under this reading, hatred, contempt, hostility, cruelty, reproach, and scorn are all expressions of the same fundamental inner orientation: the self turned against.

This reading has some support. Many of the terms co-occur in the same verses. The dominant group description in the cluster data (group 1601) frames even the primary term sa.ne as naming "hatred as the inner moral disposition opposed to love" — a single dispositional orientation.

### The case against — where the glosses begin to separate

Reading the glosses more carefully, they do not all describe the same inner-being phenomenon. The following distinctions emerge:

**Observation 1: Direction of the characteristic differs.**

Some glosses name a disposition *held* within the person — an inner state the person carries:
- hatred (mas.te.mah, sin.ah, sa.ne) — the inner orientation of the hater
- contempt (buz, ba.zah, na.ats) — the inner posture of the one who despises
- cruelty (akh.zar, akh.za.ri, akh.ze.riy.yut) — the inner character of the cruel person
- ruthlessness (a.rits) — the inner disposition of the ruthless

Other glosses name a condition *experienced* by a person — what is done to them from outside:
- reproach (cher.pah) — the condition of being an object of shame and public contempt
- derision (shim.tsah) — the condition of being mocked
- scorn (she.at) — the condition of being scorned
- to mistreat (hubrizō) — the act of subjecting another to insolent treatment

This is not a trivial distinction. The first group describes an inner state *in the agent*. The second group describes a condition *experienced by the recipient*. They engage different faculties and produce different impacts. The agent's hatred engages the agent's volition, affect, and moral evaluation. The recipient's reproach engages the recipient's affect, identity, and social orientation. These are genuinely different inner-being phenomena.

**Observation 2: Some glosses name acts or postures, not states.**

Some glosses name what a person *does*, not what they *are*:
- to taunt (cha.raph) — an act of contemptuous speech directed outward
- to mistreat (hubrizō) — an act of insolent treatment of another
- to watch with envy (ra.tsad) — a posture of hostile surveillance

These are expressive acts. They may flow from an inner state (contempt, hostility), but the term itself names the act, not the state. Under the programme's distinction between state, capacity, and expression, these are expressions — they belong to the third category of the definition. They may be derivative characteristics (flowing from the state) rather than primary ones.

**Observation 3: Relational category differs.**

Some glosses name a relational category — the position of another person in relation to the self:
- adversary (qim, antidikos) — the person positioned against me
- opponent (ya.riv) — the person in legal or relational contest with me

These terms name the relational *other*, not an inner state of the person. They tell us about the external relational structure, not the inner orientation. Whether they qualify as inner-being characteristics at all is a programme-level question. Their inclusion in M06 suggests the prior classification placed them here because of their relational-hostility register, but they may be better understood as the *object* of a characteristic (the person toward whom hostility is directed) rather than the characteristic itself.

**Observation 4: Cruelty and ruthlessness are not the same as hatred and contempt.**

Cruelty and ruthlessness (akh.zar/akh.za.ri/akh.ze.riy.yut, a.rits) describe the *quality of inner character* — the disposition of a person who does not restrain destructive impulses, who does not set God before themselves, who acts without mercy. This is not the same as hatred. A person can be cruel without hating — cruelty is a character disposition regarding how one treats others, independent of the specific emotion of hatred. The group descriptions confirm this: ruthlessness is described as "the disposition of those who do not set God before themselves" — a moral character quality with its own constitutional shape.

**Observation 5: Detestation and abhorrence differ from hatred.**

Detestation (she.qets), abhorrence (de.ra.on, ta.av, bdelussomai), and to abhor/loathe describe a visceral inner recoil from something perceived as morally or ritually repugnant. This is qualitatively different from hatred in its directional and affective character. Hatred is sustained hostile orientation toward a person. Abhorrence/detestation is visceral revulsion at a quality, act, or thing. They can coexist (one can abhor and hate the same object), but they are distinguishable: abhorrence is primarily a moral evaluation response (the conscience recoiling from what violates it), while hatred is primarily a relational disposition (the self oriented against another person or thing).

**Observation 6: Reproach operates as a social-relational phenomenon, distinct from a purely inner state.**

Reproach (cher.pah) is extensively attested and carefully grouped: it names both an inner-social condition experienced by individuals (felt shame of being despised) and a corporate condition imposed as divine judgment. This is a different inner-being register from hatred. Reproach is what hate and contempt *produce* in the recipient — it is a consequence-state, not an originating disposition. It is more closely related to shame than to hatred as such. Its presence in M06 may reflect historical registry placement rather than genuine characteristic family membership.

## Step 4 — Proposed characteristic groupings from the gloss evidence

Based on the above observations, the following distinct characteristics appear to be present within M06:

### Characteristic A — Hatred / Hostile orientation
*The sustained inner disposition of the self turned against a person, group, or quality — a settled enmity of the inner being.*

Primary terms: sa.ne (to hate), sin.ah (hating), mas.te.mah (hatred), sa.tam (to hate — settled grudge form), se.na (to hate — variant), sa.ni (hated — passive/received form)

Distinguishing features:
- Constitutional location: soul/heart level (inner orientation)
- Faculty engagement: relational capacity (directed against), affect, volition
- Impact: produces sustained inner enmity; motivates hostile acts; structurally opposite to love
- Directional: primarily the disposition of the *agent* toward a person

### Characteristic B — Contempt / Despising
*The inner posture of regarding another as beneath regard — treating the sacred, the person, or the word of God as of no worth.*

Primary terms: ba.zah (to despise), na.ats (to spurn), buz (contempt), biz.za.von (contempt), shut (to despise), she.at (scorn)

Distinguishing features:
- Constitutional location: soul/heart level (posture of the heart)
- Faculty engagement: moral evaluation (assigning low worth), volition, affect
- Impact: the one who despises closes themselves to correction, growth, and covenantal relationship; produces a hardened inner disposition
- Directional: the *agent's* posture toward something or someone valued
- Distinct from hatred: contempt is primarily about *worth-assignment* (treating as beneath regard); hatred is about *relational hostility* (sustained enmity). One can despise without actively hating; one can hate without despising.

### Characteristic C — Abhorrence / Moral revulsion
*The visceral inner recoil from what is morally or covenantally repugnant — the inner being's response of revulsion toward evil, idolatry, or wickedness.*

Primary terms: ta.av (to abhor / to loathe), bdelussomai (to abhor), de.ra.on (abhorrence), she.qets (detestation)

Distinguishing features:
- Constitutional location: soul/conscience level — the inner being's moral evaluation faculty
- Faculty engagement: moral evaluation (recoil from what violates the standard), conscience, affect
- Impact: separates the person from what is repugnant; the abhorrence of evil is commanded of those who love God — it is the negative pole of moral discernment
- Directional: the inner being recoiling *from* something perceived as morally repugnant (object-directed, not person-directed in the same way as hatred)
- Distinct from hatred: abhorrence responds to a moral quality (evil, idolatry, falsehood); hatred responds to a person or agent. Abhorrence can be righteous (God abhors pride; the righteous abhor falsehood); the same term applies to God's abhorrence of evil and the human abhorrence of the same. This double use (divine and human) is a structural feature absent from pure hatred vocabulary.

### Characteristic D — Cruelty / Ruthlessness
*The inner character disposition of merciless destructiveness — the absence of restraint, mercy, or the fear of God in how one treats others.*

Primary terms: akh.zar (cruel), akh.za.ri (cruel), akh.ze.riy.yut (cruelty), a.rits (ruthless)

Distinguishing features:
- Constitutional location: character/disposition level — a settled quality of the person's inner moral character
- Faculty engagement: moral evaluation (absence of), volition, agency
- Impact: the cruel person harms others without restraint; this is named as an inner character quality, not a momentary emotion
- Distinct from hatred: cruelty concerns the *manner* of treating others (without mercy, without restraint); one can be cruel to people one does not hate. Cruelty is an absence of a limiting inner quality (mercy/compassion) rather than the presence of a hostile orientation.

### Characteristic E — Reproach / Experienced shame and contempt
*The inner-social condition of being the object of public contempt, mockery, or disgrace — the subjective experience of being despised and its inner impact.*

Primary terms: cher.pah (reproach), shim.tsah (derision), cha.raph (to taunt — in its expressive dimension)

Distinguishing features:
- Constitutional location: primarily experienced at the soul/affect level — shame, social identity, inner distress
- Faculty engagement: affect, identity, social-relational capacity
- Impact: reproach breaks the heart (Psa 69:20 — "reproaches have broken my heart"), produces inner despair, and strips social standing; but it is also overcome by divine vindication
- Directional: primarily a *received* condition — what is done to the person by others
- Distinct from hatred: reproach is what the *recipient* experiences; hatred is what the *agent* carries. They are adjacent (hatred directed at a person produces reproach in that person) but occupy different positions in the inner-being landscape.

### Characteristic F — Hostility / Enmity as relational posture
*The relational orientation of being set against another — enmity as a posture that the inner being takes or receives in a structured contest.*

Primary terms: a.yav (be hostile), ya.riv (opponent), antidikos (opponent), qim (adversary)

Distinguishing features:
- These terms are on the boundary between characterising an inner state and naming a relational category. A.yav carries the clearest inner-being signal (hostile *disposition*); the adversary/opponent terms are more relational-structural.
- This grouping may not constitute a fully independent characteristic. It may be better understood as the *relational expression* of hatred or contempt — what hatred looks like when it is structured into a relationship of contest.
- **Flag for researcher decision:** whether Characteristic F warrants independent characteristic status, or whether it is better treated as a sub-dimension of Characteristic A (hatred), needs researcher input. The verse data for these terms is thin (1–4 verses each), which limits the analytical purchase available.

### Borderline terms not yet clearly assigned

- **to stink (ba.ash)** — Partially addressed in the UT review (previous session). The term spans literal stench, social-reputational odium, and inner-relational contempt. Its inner-being use (where it names the moral odium one has become in another's eyes) is adjacent to Characteristic E (reproach) and to Characteristic B (contempt). The term may be primarily a metaphorical expression rather than an independent characteristic.
- **to rival (tsa.rar)** — One verse only (Lev 18:18: rivalry as relational dynamic producing inner jealousy and distress). This may belong more naturally to a jealousy/envy cluster than to hate/contempt.
- **to watch with envy (ra.tsad)** — One verse (Psa 68:16). The envious gaze as hostile surveillance. This sits at the intersection of envy and hostility — likely a sub-expression of envy rather than an independent characteristic within M06.
- **scourge (sho.tet)** — One verse, not inner-being relevant (Jos 23:13). Already flagged in the UT review for reconsideration of cluster assignment.
- **hating good (afilagathos)** — One verse (2Ti 3:3). The inner disposition of not loving good. This is described in the group data as "an inner-being orientation that characterises the person given over to disordered loves." It is structurally closer to Characteristic B (contempt — treating the good as beneath regard) or to the love/disordered-love vocabulary of C17.

## Step 5 — Summary of the debate

### Is M06 a single characteristic?

**No.** The gloss list, read against the programme's definition of an inner-being characteristic, reveals at least four clearly distinct characteristics and two further candidate groupings. They are distinct because they differ in:
- The direction of the inner-being phenomenon (agent-held vs recipient-experienced)
- The faculty primarily engaged (relational disposition vs moral evaluation vs moral character quality vs affect/social identity)
- The impact produced in the person
- The structural opposite that defines each boundary
- The object toward which they are directed (persons vs moral qualities vs external agents)

### What are the distinct characteristics?

| Proposed label | Core description | Primary terms |
|---|---|---|
| A — Hatred | Sustained inner hostility directed at a person | sa.ne, sin.ah, mas.te.mah, sa.tam |
| B — Contempt | Inner posture of regarding as beneath regard | ba.zah, na.ats, buz, shut, she.at |
| C — Abhorrence | Visceral moral revulsion from evil or the repugnant | ta.av, bdelussomai, de.ra.on, she.qets |
| D — Cruelty/Ruthlessness | Character disposition of merciless destructiveness | akh.zar, akh.za.ri, akh.ze.riy.yut, a.rits |
| E — Reproach | Experienced condition of public contempt and shame | cher.pah, shim.tsah |
| F — Hostility/Enmity | Relational posture of structured opposition | a.yav, ya.riv, antidikos, qim |

### What requires researcher decision before proceeding?

1. **Is Characteristic F (hostility/enmity) a distinct characteristic or a sub-dimension of Characteristic A?** The verse evidence for these terms is thin and the terms may be better read as relational expressions of hatred rather than a separate inner-being phenomenon.

2. **Does Characteristic E (reproach) belong in M06 at all?** Reproach is the condition experienced by the recipient of contempt and hatred. Its inner-being content is real but it may belong more naturally in a shame/dignity cluster than in a hate/contempt/hostility cluster.

3. **How are the borderline terms (ba.ash, tsa.rar, ra.tsad, sho.tet, afilagathos) assigned?** Each has been flagged above with a provisional reading, but several are single-verse terms with insufficient evidence for confident placement.

4. **Is Characteristic B (contempt) sufficiently distinct from Characteristic A (hatred) to warrant separate treatment?** The debate above argues yes — they differ in directional object (person vs worth-evaluation) and in faculty engagement. But the researcher may judge the overlap sufficient to treat them as sub-dimensions of a single "hostile orientation" characteristic.

## Observations on what the verse data would need to show to resolve these questions

- **For question 3 (A vs B):** The verse data would need to show whether contempt and hatred co-occur consistently (suggesting one characteristic) or appear in systematically different contexts with different consequences (suggesting two). Hatred appears most frequently in relational/conflict contexts; contempt appears most frequently in moral-covenantal contexts (despising God's word, spurning correction). This is already visible in the group descriptions and supports treating them as distinct.

- **For question 1 (F):** The verse data for the adversary/opponent terms would need to show whether they carry the inner disposition of hostility within the *agent* (supporting independent characteristic status) or simply name the *external relational position* (supporting relegation to relational expression).

- **For question 2 (E):** The question is whether reproach's primary inner-being content is the shame experienced by the recipient (placing it closer to shame vocabulary) or the contempt-projection dynamic that produces it (keeping it in M06). Psa 69:20 — "reproaches have broken my heart" — suggests the primary inner-being impact is in the recipient's affect, not in the agent's hostility. This leans toward relocation.

## Methodological note

This debate has been conducted from the gloss list and the group descriptions visible in the report. It has not yet read individual verses systematically across all 36 terms. The observations above are therefore at the level of the programme's working definition and the group-level evidence — they are analytical starting points, not findings in the programme's full evidential sense. The verse-level evidence must be read to confirm, qualify, or overturn what the gloss-level debate has raised.

Per the programme's methodological principles: *a classification that cannot be grounded in at least one verse in a registry's corpus is not a classification the programme uses.* The proposed characteristics above are therefore provisional working hypotheses, not findings. They are ready to be tested against the verse data.
