## Opening observation — How M06-B differs structurally from M06-A

Before applying the prompts, the structural shape of M06-B must be noted because it determines how the prompts will land.

M06-A (Hatred) had 174 verses across 13 groups. Its primary term (sa.ne) had an enormous semantic range spanning six distinct registers, and the major analytical work was differentiating those registers from one another.

M06-B (Contempt) has 56 verses across 8 groups, and its structure is markedly more unified. Five of the six terms (na.ats, ne.a.tsah, ba.zah, biz.za.von, shut) have their primary or only group directed at the same object: **the sacred, the covenant, God's name, God's word, or God's people**. The characteristic in M06-B is almost exclusively directed upward — contempt of the human person toward God and his things — or experienced downward as received contempt from nations toward Israel. The horizontal (human-to-human) contempt is present but thin. This is the opposite of M06-A where human-to-human hatred was the dominant register.

This means several prompt categories will behave very differently in M06-B than they did in M06-A. In particular: T0.1 (divine nature reflected), T4.1 (God to human), and T4.2 (human to God) will be the most evidentially rich tiers. The human-to-human interface (T4.3-4.4) will be thin. This is not a gap — it is a structural finding about what contempt is in the biblical vocabulary.

## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

### T0.1 — Divine Nature Reflected

**T0.1.1** — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**E** — Unlike M06-A where God was extensively a *subject* of hatred, God is almost never the *subject* of contempt in M06-B. The one exception is Deu 32:19 (na.ats): "The Lord saw it and spurned them, because of the provocation of his sons and his daughters" — God spurning his people in response to their idolatry. And Lam 2:6: "the Lord has...spurned king and priest." These are responses to Israel's prior contempt of him, not an originating divine disposition. God does not hold contempt toward his creation as a characteristic; he responds to having his name, word, and covenant despised.

This is structurally significant: contempt as a settled inner posture of regarding-as-beneath-regard does not appear to be an attribute of God's character in the way that hatred-of-evil is. God does not despise persons the way he hates wickedness.

**T0.1.2** — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God?

**E (limited)** — In Deu 32:19 and Lam 2:6, na.ats is attributed to God as a judicial response to Israel's behaviour. This is different from the extensive divine hatred documented in M06-A. The divine contempt here is reactive and covenantal — God's spurning is a consequence of being spurned, not an independent inner disposition toward created beings.

**T0.1.3** — Where Scripture is silent about God possessing this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?

**E** — The relative silence is itself significant. Contempt — the posture of treating what is sacred as beneath regard, of assigning low worth to what deserves honour — is not a characteristic of God toward his creation. God takes his own name, covenant, word, and people with full weight and seriousness. The characteristic of contempt belongs primarily to the creature-in-rebellion, not to the Creator. This is the mirror-image of what M06-A found: hatred of evil is shared between God and the righteous person; contempt of the sacred is the exclusive territory of the disordered creature.

### T0.2 — Created Purpose

**T0.2.1** — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?

**S / G** — No verse in M06-B suggests that contempt serves a created purpose in the properly ordered inner life. Unlike righteous hatred (M06-A, group 550-NEW-02), there is no commanded or praised contempt in M06-B — no equivalent of "despise evil." The characteristic as evidenced is entirely a disordering of the inner being. **G** — whether contempt could theoretically have a righteous form (e.g., appropriate disregard for what is genuinely worthless) is not addressed by these 56 verses. The corpus treats contempt as a characteristically fallen disposition.

**T0.2.2** — Is this characteristic part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**E** — From the verse evidence, contempt appears entirely as a fallen phenomenon. Every instance in M06-B is either named as sin (1Sa 2:17: "the sin of the young men was very great...they treated the offering with contempt"), as covenant violation (Deu 31:20: "they will...despise me and break my covenant"), or as the inner speech of the wicked (Psa 10:13: "the wicked...say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'"). There is no verse in this corpus that presents contempt as a capacity serving the created design.

**T0.2.3** — Is there evidence of orientation toward a future fullness?

**E** — Yes, but from the receiving side. Isa 60:14: "all who despised you shall bow down at your feet" — those who held contempt toward Zion will be reversed eschatologically. Isa 49:7: the servant who is "deeply despised, abhorred by the nation" will be honoured when "kings shall see and arise." The eschatological trajectory for contempt is its reversal — not its fulfilment. The characteristic that despised will be exposed and the despised will be vindicated.

### T0.3 — Image-Bearer Expression

**T0.3.1** — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image?

**E** — Contempt *distorts* the divine image rather than expressing it. The image of God in the human person involves the capacity for accurate moral evaluation — assigning worth in accordance with what things actually are. Contempt inverts this: it assigns low worth to what has high worth (God's name, his word, his covenant, his servants). Gen 25:34: "Esau despised his birthright" — the birthright was of extraordinary worth; Esau's inner act of despising it was a failure of moral evaluation. The capacity for worth-evaluation exists in the image-bearer; contempt is that capacity running in the wrong direction.

**T0.3.2** — Is this characteristic shared between God and the human person?

**E** — Not as an originating disposition. God does not hold contempt for sacred things (he is the source of their sacredness). The spurning God occasionally shows toward Israel (Deu 32:19) is a judicial response, not a characteristic posture. This contrasts sharply with hatred where God and the righteous person share the same directed disposition. Contempt is not shared — it is a creaturely distortion.

**T0.3.3** — What does the presence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?

**E** — Its presence is a direct indicator of image-disorder. Isa 1:4: "they have forsaken the Lord, they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — the sequence is precise: forsaking → despising → estrangement. The contempt of the sacred is not an isolated act but the middle step in a progression from abandonment to full estrangement from God. The image is in disorder when the person despises what God values; it has become incompatible with the covenantal relationship.

### T0.4 — Typological Significance

**T0.4.1** — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically?

**E** — Yes, in Isa 53:3 (group 1280-001, anchor): "He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief; and as one from whom men hide their faces he was despised, and we esteemed him not." Ba.zah here names the contempt directed at the Servant of the Lord. The verse is typologically charged — the experience of being despised by those who should have recognised and honoured him becomes the central experience of the Servant's mission.

**T0.4.2** — What is the direction of the typology?

**E** — In Isa 53:3, the direction is from the human-level contempt directed at the Servant toward the divine reality that the Servant is bearing. The contempt experienced is a human act; what it reveals about the one who is despised (and about those who despise) is the theological depth. The typological movement is from the experience of contempt received → the reality of what is being missed by those who despise. Those who despise the Servant are despising what they do not recognise.

**T0.4.3** — If no typological use is evidenced, note explicitly.

Not applicable — typological use is evidenced.

## T1 — Definition

### T1.1 — Name and Naming

**T1.1.1** — What does the name "Contempt" signal about its essential nature?

The sub-group label "Contempt" captures the primary phenomenon: the inner posture of treating something as beneath regard — assigning it a worth so low that it does not deserve attention, honour, or compliance. The name signals an evaluative act: contempt is the internal downward assessment of worth, not a relational hostility (which is hatred) but a moral-evaluative dismissal.

**T1.1.2** — What do the primary terms reveal at the definitional level?

Six terms, differentiated by their specific register:

**H5006 na.ats** — the dominant term (25 verses across 3 groups). The root conveys spurning, rejecting as worthless, treating as insignificant. It operates almost exclusively in the vertical direction: the human person treating God, his word, his servants, or his covenant as beneath regard. The frequency and vertical orientation make this the core term of the sub-group.

**H0959 ba.zah** — "to despise, to hold in contempt." Operates with a slightly broader object range — Esau despises his birthright (Gen 25:34, a sacred inheritance), the Servant is despised (Isa 53:3), priests despise God's name (Mal 1:6). The term names the inner act of treating something as valueless when it has significant value.

**H0937 buz** — "contempt, scorn." The most experiential of the terms — it appears frequently in contexts where contempt is experienced by the person rather than held (Psa 123:3-4: "our soul has had more than enough of the scorn of those who are at ease, of the contempt of the proud"). Job 12:5 (anchor): contempt as the inner attitude of the comfortable toward those who suffer.

**H0963 biz.za.von** — "contempt" as a social force. Single verse (Est 1:18). The distinctive feature is that it names contempt as a contagion — one act of defiance spreading the disposition through a whole society.

**H5007B ne.a.tsah** — "blasphemous contempt" as the speech-act of rebellion (Neh 9:18, 9:26, Eze 35:12). The term specifically names contempt expressed in speech — the reviling, blaspheming that reveals an inner disposition of rejection of the holy.

**H7590 shut** — "to despise, treat with contempt." Three verses, all in Ezekiel, naming the contempt of surrounding nations toward Israel. God's response is to remove and avenge this contempt.

**T1.1.3** — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**E** — Strongly directional: contempt in M06-B is almost universally directed upward (creature toward Creator, people toward covenant) or outward (nations toward God's people). It almost never appears as the inner posture of a superior toward an inferior — which would be patronising dismissal. Constitutional implication: Psa 10:13 locates the contempt in the heart ("say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'"). Pro 5:12 names the heart as the location of the despising of reproof. The characteristic lives in the heart's evaluative centre.

### T1.2 — Kind

**T1.2.1** — What kind of inner-being phenomenon is this?

**E** — Contempt is primarily an **inner act of evaluation** — the heart assigning low worth to what deserves high worth. It is not primarily a disposition in the sense of a sustained relational orientation (that is hatred); it is primarily an evaluative posture — a specific inner movement of the moral evaluation faculty that renders a negative verdict on the worth of its object. Gen 25:34: "Esau despised his birthright" — ba.zah names the single inner act of evaluation Esau performed. But contempt can also be sustained: Mal 1:6-12 shows priests in a sustained pattern of treating the Lord's table as despicable. It operates as both a discrete act and a habitual orientation.

**T1.2.2** — Is this characteristic simple or does it have constituent elements?

**E** — It has constituent elements visible across the groups:
- A **perception** of the object (what the thing is)
- A **moral evaluation** (the thing is assessed as beneath regard)
- A **volitional response** (the person acts in accordance with that evaluation — ignoring, spurning, violating)

Psa 10:13 shows the innermost constituent: "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'" — the contempt is grounded in a prior cognitive evaluation (God will not hold me accountable) that produces the dismissal. The contempt is not merely affective but cognitively grounded.

**T1.2.3** — Working description of this characteristic?

Contempt (M06-B) is an inner act and habit of the moral evaluation faculty in which the person assigns low or no worth to what is sacred, covenantally significant, or inherently worthy — constitutionally located in the heart, engaging the moral evaluation and cognitive faculties, and producing a closed inner posture that excludes the despised object from its proper claim on the person's honour, compliance, or reverence. Unlike hatred, contempt is directed at worth rather than at persons as such; it is a devaluation of what should be valued.

### T1.3 — Boundary

**T1.3.1** — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic?

**E** — Honour, reverence, and fear (of the Lord). Mal 1:6: "a son honours his father, and a servant his master. If then I am a father, where is my honour? And if I am a master, where is my fear?" — the structural opposite of despising God's name is honouring it. The same verse positions fear of the Lord as the positive counterpart. Isa 60:14: the reversal of contempt is bowing down — the posture of honour replacing the posture of dismissal. The structural opposite is not merely absence of contempt but the positive attribution of worth.

**T1.3.2** — What does contempt explicitly exclude or resist?

**E** — It excludes compliance with what is despised (Num 15:31: "because he has despised the word of the Lord and has broken his commandment" — despising precedes and explains breaking). It excludes correction — group 172-002 (contempt of reproof): Pro 15:5: "a fool despises his father's instruction" — the contempt closes the person to the formation that would come through instruction. It excludes covenantal participation: Deu 31:20: "they will despise me and break my covenant" — contempt and covenant-breaking are sequential.

**T1.3.3** — What is contempt not — where does it end and something else begin?

**E** — Contempt is not hatred. Hatred is sustained relational enmity toward a person; contempt is a worth-evaluation that can be directed at things, words, offices, and names as much as at persons. One can despise a birthright (Gen 25:34) — that is not hatred of Jacob. One can despise a reproof (Pro 15:5) — that is not hatred of the father who gives it. The boundary is between relational hostility (hatred) and evaluative dismissal (contempt). Contempt is also not the same as abhorrence (M06-C) — abhorrence is visceral moral recoil from what is morally repugnant; contempt is the cool dismissal of worth. One can be contemptuous without being repulsed; one can be repulsed without being contemptuous.

### T1.4 — Modes of Operation

**T1.4.1** — In what distinct ways does contempt operate?

**E** — Four modes from the verse evidence:

1. **Inner speech mode (group 172-003):** Psa 10:13: "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account.'" The contempt is first articulated internally — a private verdict about God's power or relevance. This is contempt's most intimate form: the heart's secret dismissal.

2. **Behavioural mode:** The contempt produces action in accordance with its verdict. Num 15:31: "because he has despised the word...he has broken his commandment." The inner devaluation becomes the ground for non-compliance. Esau eats and drinks and goes his way — he acts as if the birthright had no claim on him.

3. **Speech-act mode (group 1567-001):** Ne.a.tsah names blasphemous contempt expressed outward in speech — the reviling, the verbal contempt of Neh 9:18, 9:26, Eze 35:12. The inner disposition breaks through into audible expression.

4. **Contagion mode (group 1281-001):** Biz.za.von (Est 1:18): contempt as a social force spreading from one act of defiance through a community. One person's contemptuous act communicates a permission-structure to others — if the queen can despise the king's command with impunity, the noble women will draw the same conclusion.

**T1.4.2** — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context or direction?

**E** — Significantly. When directed at God and the sacred (groups 172-001, 172-003, 1567-001, 1280-001): contempt is the inner posture of rebellion — it operates as the devaluation of the covenantal relationship itself, producing disobedience and eventual estrangement. When operating between persons (group 316-001, buz): contempt is the social dismissal of persons considered to be of low worth — experienced by the poor, the suffering, and the righteous. When operating socially/nationally (groups 1281-001, 1277-001): contempt functions at the level of collective inner disposition — nations treating Israel as beneath regard.

**T1.4.3** — Does contempt have a speech-based mode?

**E** — Yes, explicitly in group 172-003 (inner speech: "say in his heart") and group 1567-001 (ne.a.tsah: blasphemous speech-act). These are two distinct speech modes: the private inner verdict and the public verbal expression. Eza 35:12: "I have heard all the revilings that you uttered against the mountains of Israel" — God explicitly names the speech-acts of contempt as what he has heard and will judge.

### T1.5 — Immediate Response

**T1.5.1** — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to encountering this characteristic?

**E** — From God's perspective as recipient: Num 14:11: "How long will this people despise me?" — the divine response is named as grieved questioning. Mal 1:6: "If then I am a father, where is my honour?" — the response is a challenge that exposes the contradiction. The immediate response to receiving contempt (even from God's perspective) is the naming of what has been withheld.

From the human perspective of receiving contempt (group 316-001): Job 31:34: "because I stood in great fear of the multitude, and the contempt of families terrified me" — the immediate inner response to social contempt is fear, specifically the fear of exposure and judgment by those who hold you in contempt. Psa 123:3-4: "our soul has had more than enough of contempt" — the soul registers saturation — the accumulation of received contempt produces an inner cry for relief.

**T1.5.2** — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence?

**E** — For the one who holds contempt: no immediate inner response is named — the characteristic is described from the outside, as observed behaviour and its consequences. The inner experience of contempt-holding is visible only through its products (disobedience, dismissal, inner speech). For the one who receives contempt: the immediate response is consistent — the soul is burdened (Psa 123:3-4), fear arises (Job 31:34), there is a cry for divine relief.

**T1.5.3** — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note explicitly.

**S** — The immediate inner experience of the person *holding* contempt — what it feels like to despise — is not described in the M06-B verse evidence. The characteristic is visible in its expression and its consequences, but the phenomenology of contempt from the inside is silent.

---

### T1.6 — Sustained Effect

**T1.6.1** — What does contempt produce in the inner being over time?

**E** — In the contempt-holder: sustained contempt produces progressive estrangement from the object of contempt. Isa 1:4: "they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — the estrangement is the sustained effect. Deu 31:20: the pattern is predictive — material prosperity will produce contempt, and contempt will produce covenant-breaking. Contempt, held over time, closes the inner being to any claim the despised object might make.

In group 172-002 (contempt of reproof): Pro 15:5: "a fool despises his father's instruction" — the sustained effect is the closure of the formation pathway. The person who holds contempt for correction cannot be corrected; the very mechanism by which the inner being might grow is shut down by the contempt that dismisses it.

**T1.6.2** — What states, capacities, or orientations does sustained contempt establish?

**E** — Sustained contempt establishes an inner state of **covenantal closure** — the person is no longer capable of receiving what God or wisdom or covenant offers because the evaluative faculty has rendered those things as beneath regard. Jer 23:17 shows this at a community level: those who despise the word of God are told by false prophets "it shall be well with you" — the community's contempt of the word has made them receptive to false comfort and closed to genuine prophetic address.

**T1.6.3** — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?

**E** — Yes. The immediate response is an inner act of devaluation (the verdict rendered). The sustained effect is structural — the person has organised their inner life around the verdict, and the object of contempt can no longer gain access to them. What began as an evaluative posture becomes a constitutional closure.

---

### T1.7 — Conditions of Reception

**T1.7.1** — What inner conditions enable contempt to take hold?

**E** — Two enabling conditions visible from the verses:

**Pride and comfort:** Job 12:5 (anchor for group 316-001): "in the thought of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune" — the person at ease, comfortable, and secure is the one whose inner life generates contempt for those who suffer or struggle. The comfort produces a false sense of superiority that finds expression in contempt. Psa 123:4: "the contempt of the proud" — pride is named as the generative condition.

**Cognitive self-deception:** Psa 10:13: "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'" — the contempt of God is grounded in a prior cognitive conviction that accountability is absent or God is inattentive. The evaluative dismissal ("God doesn't matter") is grounded in a prior misreading of reality.

**T1.7.2** — What inner conditions block contempt?

**E** — Fear of the Lord blocks contempt. Mal 1:6: "if then I am a master, where is my fear?" — the absence of fear of the Lord is what makes the contempt of God's name possible. Where fear of the Lord is genuinely present, the evaluative faculty assigns appropriate worth to God and his things. Pro 15:5: "whoever heeds reproof is prudent" — the one who receives correction is the opposite of the contemptuous; the enabling condition is a posture of openness to formation.

**T1.7.3** — What is the inner state of the person who encounters contempt from another but does not receive it?

**E** — Isa 53:3 provides the most significant evidence here. The Servant receives contempt from those who despise him, but the verse does not describe the Servant's inner response as becoming contemptuous in return. The verse names the experience ("despised and rejected") but not an inner collapse. Isa 49:7 shows the eschatological reversal awaiting the despised Servant — the promise of vindication is the inner resource that enables the despised to receive contempt without being destroyed by it or reciprocating it.

### T1.8 — Dimension Classification

**T1.8.1** — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?

**E** — **Moral Character** is the primary dimension of M06-B. Unlike M06-A where the primary dimension was Relational Disposition, contempt operates primarily through the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess worth against a standard. Contempt is the inner-being's moral evaluation faculty running in the wrong direction: assigning low worth to what deserves high worth. The constitutional location (heart) and the faculty engaged (moral evaluation) both point to Moral Character as the primary dimension.

**T1.8.2** — What evidence supports this?

**E** — Group 172-001 (na.ats): Israel's contempt of God is the inner expression of their wrong moral evaluation of God's worth relative to their own desires. 1Sa 2:17: the young men's contempt of the Lord's offering is named as "very great sin" — a moral failure, not merely a relational rupture. Gen 25:34: Esau's despising of the birthright is an act of wrong moral evaluation — he assessed the bowl of stew as worth more than the birthright, which is an inversion of the proper worth-hierarchy.

**T1.8.3** — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions?

**E** — **Relational Disposition** is a secondary dimension — contempt has a relational consequence (the despised person or thing is excluded from the proper relational engagement). The estrangement that contempt produces (Isa 1:4) is a relational outcome. **Cognition** is also secondary — the cognitive grounding of contempt (the prior verdict that God will not call to account, Psa 10:13) shows that the cognitive faculty is engaged in producing the contemptuous posture.

## T2 — Constitutional Location and Boundaries

### T2.1 — Spirit Level

**S** — No verse in M06-B explicitly locates contempt at the spirit (ruach) level. Spirit-level location is silent.

---

### T2.2 — Soul Level

**E (limited)** — Psa 123:3-4: "our soul has had more than enough of the scorn of those who are at ease, of the contempt of the proud." The soul (nephesh) is named here as the organ that registers the weight of received contempt — it is the recipient's soul that is burdened, not named as the location where contempt is held by the agent. There is no verse in M06-B that locates the holding of contempt explicitly in the soul, in contrast to M06-A where God's soul was named as the location of hatred (Psa 11:5). **Partial E** — soul is named as the register of received contempt; not named as the constitutional seat of held contempt.

---

### T2.3 — Heart

**E** — Psa 10:13: "say in his heart, 'You will not call to account'" — the heart is explicitly named as the location of the inner speech of contempt. Pro 5:12: "my heart despised reproof" — the heart is the constitutional location of the despising act. The heart is where the contempt is formed as an inner verdict before it is expressed in speech or behaviour.

---

### T2.4 — Mind

**E** — The mind (cognitive faculty) is engaged as the ground of contempt. Psa 10:13: the contempt rests on a cognitive conviction ("you will not call to account") — the mind has evaluated the situation and produced an assessment that grounds the dismissal. Contempt is not unreasoned; it is a verdict the mind renders. The cognitive verdict then feeds the heart's evaluative posture.

---

### T2.5 — Other Soul Subsets

**E** — Job 12:5: "in the *thought* of one who is at ease there is contempt for misfortune" — the word translated "thought" names a cognitive-reflective faculty: the person's comfortable thought-life generates contempt. The volitional faculty is also implicated: Esau ate and rose and went his way (Gen 25:34) — the volition acts in accordance with the contemptuous evaluation. Once the worth-verdict is rendered, the will acts as if the thing truly has no claim.

---

### T2.6-2.7 — Body

**E (limited)** — Ne.a.tsah (group 1567-001) names contempt that is expressed through speech — the blasphemous reviling of Neh 9:18, 9:26 and Eze 35:12. The mouth and speech organs are the body-part through which this form of contempt is expressed. The direction is inner → outer: the inner disposition expresses through speech. Isa 37:22: "she wags her head behind you" — contemptuous body language as the physical expression of the inner posture of dismissal. No evidence of the body feeding contempt back into the inner being.

---

### T2.8 — Body Deposit

**S** — No verse in M06-B names a generational bodily deposit from sustained contempt. In contrast to M06-A's explicit generational consequence (Exo 20:5), M06-B's evidence is silent on this. The corporate consequences of national contempt are named (covenantal judgment falls on Israel when they despise God) but not as a constitutional bodily deposit in the way M06-A's hatred produces generational iniquity-visitation.

---

### T2.9 — Origin and Source

**E** — Three origins visible from the verses:

**Pride and prosperity:** Job 12:5, Psa 123:4 — contempt originates in the inner state of comfortable superiority. The person at ease generates contempt for those who suffer.

**Cognitive misreading of God's accountability:** Psa 10:13 — contempt of God originates in the false inner conviction that God will not call to account. The evaluative dismissal is grounded in a wrong reading of reality.

**Accumulated spiritual drift:** Deu 31:20 — prosperity leads to spiritual satiation leads to turning to other gods leads to contempt of the covenant God. Contempt is the arrival point of a progressive drift, not usually the starting point.

---

### T2.10 — Constitutional Movement

**E** — The movement visible from the verses: cognitive misreading (mind) → evaluative verdict (heart) → volitional action in accordance with the verdict → speech expression (mouth) → social contagion (biz.za.von, Est 1:18 — the movement from one person's act to a community's inner climate). The sequence moves from inner to outer and from individual to communal. Contempt is mobile — it does not stay in the individual heart but tends toward expression and propagation.

## T3 — The Inner Faculties

### T3.1 — Perception

**E** — Contempt distorts perception in a specific way: it causes the person to see the despised object through the lens of the low-worth verdict already rendered. Once Esau has despised the birthright, he cannot perceive it accurately — its value is invisible to him. Psa 10:3: the wicked "curses and renounces the Lord" — the perception of God has been filtered through a prior verdict of his irrelevance. Contempt is a perception-filter: what is despised becomes imperceptible in its actual worth.

---

### T3.2 — Cognition

**E** — The cognitive faculty is both the origin and the instrument of contempt. Psa 10:13: the cognitive conviction ("you will not call to account") is what generates the contemptuous inner speech. The mind renders a verdict that then operates as the ground of the dismissal. But sustained contempt also impairs cognition: Jer 23:17 — the community that despises God's word becomes receptive to false prophets. The contempt has corrupted the cognitive capacity to distinguish true from false prophetic address.

---

### T3.3 — Memory

**S / E (limited)** — Memory is not prominently engaged in M06-B the way it is in M06-A's grudge-bearing. Contempt does not characteristically persist through memory of an injury; it persists through a habitual inner posture of evaluation. The one exception is the retrospective lament of Pro 5:12: "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof!" — the speaker looks back on a prior state of contempt that shaped their formation. Memory here is the faculty that retrospectively perceives what the contempt cost.

---

### T3.4 — Affect

**E (limited)** — Contempt is characteristically affectively cool rather than hot. Unlike hatred (which has intense affective markers — "very great hatred," "violent hatred"), the M06-B terms do not carry strong affective intensity markers. Job 12:5: "in the thought of one who is at ease" — the ease is the affective register: contempt is comfortable, relaxed, indifferent. Est 1:18 (biz.za.von) pairs contempt with wrath ("contempt and wrath in plenty") — but the wrath is a separate phenomenon. The characteristic's distinctive affective register is **cold indifference** or **comfortable dismissal**, not heated hostility.

This is a structurally significant distinction from M06-A. Hatred is affectively intense; contempt is affectively cool. They may co-occur, but their affective registers differ.

---

### T3.5 — Creativity

**S** — No evidence that contempt engages the creative faculty. Unlike hatred (which recruits imagination into planning harm), contempt is characteristically unreflective — it dismisses rather than plans. The creative faculty is not visibly engaged by this characteristic.

---

### T3.6 — Volition

**E** — Contempt organises the will away from the despised object. Gen 25:34: Esau's despising of the birthright produces a volitional act — he eats, drinks, rises, goes his way. The will acts in accordance with the verdict that the birthright has no binding claim. Num 15:31: "he has despised the word of the Lord and has broken his commandment" — the contempt produces the volitional choice to break the commandment. The will is liberated from the claim of what has been despised — or rather, it believes itself liberated.

---

### T3.7 — Agency

**E** — Contempt enables agency in a specific, distorted sense: it removes the felt obligation to act in a particular way toward the despised object. The priests who despise God's name (Mal 1:6) do not feel bound to honour the offering — their agency operates as if God's name has no claim. The characteristic de-obligates the agent from the demands the despised thing would otherwise make. This is different from hatred's agency (which drives toward harm); contempt's agency is a removal of restraint.

---

### T3.8 — Moral Evaluation

**E** — This is the faculty most centrally and essentially engaged by M06-B. Contempt is an act of moral evaluation — specifically, a wrong verdict rendered by the moral evaluation faculty. Every instance in the corpus involves a wrong assessment of worth: Esau wrongly assesses the birthright as worth less than food; the priests wrongly assess God's name as not demanding honour; the comfortable person wrongly assesses the sufferer's misfortune as not deserving attention. The moral evaluation faculty is the operative faculty of contempt; when it renders an accurate verdict, it produces reverence; when it renders an inaccurate verdict, it produces contempt.

---

### T3.9 — Conscience

**E** — Group 172-002 (contempt of reproof) shows conscience's relationship to this characteristic most clearly. Pro 5:12: "how I hated discipline, and my heart despised reproof" — the contempt of reproof is the contempt of the very mechanism through which conscience operates. Reproof is conscience made external; despising reproof is the inner act of closing the conscience's external channel. The characteristic attacks the conscience's access to the person.

---

### T3.10 — Conscientiousness

**E** — Contempt systematically dismantles conscientiousness. Where hatred of evil enables reliable conscientious conduct (M06-A), contempt of what is sacred or right dismantles it. The priests of Mal 1 are the clearest example: their role requires conscientious attention to the honour of God's name in the offering; their contempt has made this conscientiousness impossible. They go through the motions while having internally rendered a verdict that the motions do not matter.

---

### T3.11 — Relational Capacity

**E** — Contempt closes the relational capacity toward the despised object specifically. Isa 1:4: "they have despised the Holy One of Israel, they are utterly estranged" — the estrangement is total. The relational capacity toward God has been closed by the contemptuous inner verdict. Group 1277-001 (shut, nations despising Israel): the contempt of the nations closes any relational possibility between them and Israel except hostility. Unlike hatred which organises the relational capacity against the hated person, contempt removes the person from the relational field altogether — they are beneath regard, which means beneath relational engagement.

---

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

### T4.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human

**T4.1.1-4** — Does God extend this characteristic toward the human person?

**E (reactive, not originating)** — As noted in T0.1: God's contempt/spurning of his people appears in Deu 32:19 and Lam 2:6 as a judicial response to their prior contempt of him. It is reactive and covenantal, not an originating divine disposition. The stronger divine movement visible in M06-B is God's *response* to contempt being directed at him — divine judgment on those who despise his name, word, and covenant. Mal 2:9: "I make you despised and abased before all the people" — God uses contempt as an instrument of judgment, returning upon the priests the very condition they had produced in their worship.

---

### T4.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God

**E** — This is the dominant relational interface in M06-B. The vast majority of the verse evidence describes the human person directing contempt toward God:
- Despising God's name (Mal 1:6)
- Treating the Lord's table as despicable (Mal 1:7, 1:12)
- Spurning God's counsel (Psa 107:11)
- Treating the offering with contempt (1Sa 2:17)
- Despising the word of the Holy One (Isa 5:24)
- Saying in the heart "you will not call to account" (Psa 10:13)

The inner posture required to sustain this movement is one of **covenantal presumption** — the assumption that God's claims on the person are negotiable or ignorable. Contempt toward God operates from within the covenantal relationship (these are Israel and Israel's priests) which makes it a form of covenant betrayal from within, not rebellion from without.

---

### T4.3 — Human Interface — Giving

**E (limited)** — Horizontal contempt between persons appears primarily in group 316-001 (buz): the comfortable contemning the suffering (Job 12:5), the proud contemning the righteous (Psa 123:4, Psa 31:18). Pro 18:3: "when wickedness comes, contempt comes also" — contempt is a characteristic companion of wickedness in horizontal human relationships. The giving of contempt between humans is thinner in M06-B than the giving of hatred in M06-A — the sub-group is weighted toward the vertical interface.

---

### T4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving

**E** — Isa 53:3 is the most significant receiving-verse: the Servant is the paradigmatic recipient of contempt — "despised and rejected by men...we esteemed him not." The verb ba.zah carries the full weight of the contemptuous inner evaluation of those who reject the Servant. Group 1277-001 (shut): Israel as recipient of the contempt of surrounding nations. Group 316-001 (buz): the sufferer, the poor, the righteous as recipients of the contempt of the comfortable and proud.

Being the recipient of contempt places the person outside the circle of the contemner's regard — not in the position of being a feared enemy but in the position of being beneath notice. This is experienced as social erasure.

---

### T4.5 — Human Interface — Boundaries

**E** — Contempt crosses covenantal boundaries: the nations hold contempt for Israel (shut, group 1277-001). It also operates within the covenant: Israel holds contempt for God (na.ats, group 172-001). And it operates in wisdom contexts across social boundaries: the comfortable hold contempt for the suffering (buz, Job 12:5). The characteristic is not bounded by covenant or social proximity — it is generated wherever a wrong worth-verdict is rendered.

---

### T4.6 — Spiritual Beings Interface

**S** — No evidence in M06-B of contempt operating in relation to angelic or adversarial spiritual beings. Silent.

## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

### T5.1 — Transformation

**E** — Contempt produces a specific transformation: the progressive closure of the inner being to the claim of what has been despised. Deu 31:20's prediction shows the arc: prosperity → contempt → covenant-breaking. The contempt is not the endpoint but the middle step — once the inner verdict of worthlessness has been rendered, the behaviour that follows the verdict becomes the natural next step. The person is not transformed dramatically; they are progressively organised around a false evaluation.

Reversal: Isa 60:14 names eschatological reversal — "all who despised you shall bow down at your feet." The reversal of contempt is not a reform of the contemner's inner evaluation but the eschatological exposure of their verdict as wrong. Repentance from contempt would require a radical re-evaluation — the admission that the verdict rendered was false, and the restoration of honour to what was despised.

---

### T5.2 — Sequence of Inner States

**E** — The sequence from the verse evidence:

1. **Prosperity or comfort** (Job 12:5, Deu 31:20) — the outer condition that enables the inner posture
2. **Cognitive misreading** (Psa 10:13 — "you will not call to account") — the false evaluation of accountability
3. **Inner verdict** (heart's despising, Pro 5:12) — the moral evaluation faculty renders its decision
4. **Volitional de-obligation** (Esau eats and goes his way) — the will acts as if freed from the claim
5. **Behavioural expression** (commandment-breaking, offering-pollution, blasphemous speech) — the inner verdict produces external acts
6. **Social propagation** (biz.za.von, Est 1:18) — one person's contempt spreads through a community
7. **Covenantal estrangement** (Isa 1:4 — "utterly estranged") — the sustained effect of contempt carried to completion

---

### T5.3 — Mechanism of Change

**E** — The mechanism is the false verdict becoming the operative framework for the inner life. Once the moral evaluation faculty has rendered "this is not worth my reverence," the entire inner life reorganises around that verdict. The mechanisms of growth (instruction, reproof, correction) are shut out — group 172-002 shows this directly: despising reproof closes the formation pathway. The characteristic works by making the person internally immune to the inputs that would correct the evaluation.

---

### T5.4 — Suffering and Affliction

**E** — Contempt is both a *cause* and a *context* of suffering. As cause: the nations' contempt of Israel (group 1277-001) produces Israel's suffering — being treated as beneath regard produces the experience of social erasure and dishonour. As context: Job 12:5 shows that suffering is the *occasion* for contempt by those who are at ease — the sufferer becomes the object of the comfortable person's contemptuous evaluation.

Isa 53:3 is the most penetrating verse: the Servant's suffering and the contempt are inseparable — being despised is part of the experience of suffering, and the suffering is the occasion of the despising.

---

### T5.5 — Formation and Sanctification

**E** — Group 172-002 (contempt of reproof) is the key formational finding: despising instruction and reproof is the inner-being mechanism by which the formation process is blocked. Pro 15:5: "a fool despises his father's instruction, but whoever heeds reproof is prudent." The contrast names two inner-being orientations toward the formation process: the one who despises instruction has closed the channel through which formation comes; the one who receives reproof is being formed. Contempt, in this register, is the anti-formation posture — it is what prevents sanctification rather than what participates in it.

---

### T5.6 — Eschatological Trajectory

**E** — Two trajectories:

**For the contemner:** Num 14:23: "none of those who despised me shall see it" — exclusion from the promised inheritance as the eschatological consequence of contempt. Psa 81:15-implied parallel to M06-A: those who persist in contempt face a final reckoning. Mal 2:9: "I make you despised and abased" — the contempt is returned upon the contemner.

**For the despised:** Isa 49:7: "kings shall see and arise; princes...shall prostrate themselves" — the one deeply despised is vindicated and honoured at the eschatological horizon. Isa 60:14: those who despised shall bow before Zion. The eschatological reversal of contempt is the restoration of honour to what was wrongly despised.

---

### T5.7 — Deposit Consequence

**S** — Unlike M06-A (Exo 20:5, generational iniquity from hating God), M06-B has no explicit generational deposit from contempt. The corporate/national consequences of contempt are named (covenant judgment falls on Israel as a people) but not as a constitutional bodily deposit. T2.8 found no generational deposit; T5.7 closes accordingly.

---

## T6 — Structural Relationships

### T6.1 — Co-occurrence

**E** — From the span data of M06-B verses:

**Iniquity / sin (a.von, chet):** 1Sa 2:17: "the sin of the young men was very great...they treated the offering with contempt." Isa 1:4: "sinful nation...they have despised the Holy One of Israel." Contempt and iniquity consistently co-occur — contempt is named as sin, or sin's accompanying inner posture.

**Rebellion (ma.rah, pa.sha):** Psa 107:11: "they had rebelled against the words of God, and spurned the counsel of the Most High." Neh 9:26: "they were disobedient and rebelled...and committed great blasphemies." Contempt and rebellion co-occur as sequential or simultaneous inner dispositions.

**Covenant-breaking (paraq berit):** Deu 31:20: "despise me and break my covenant" — contempt and covenant-breaking are explicitly linked as sequential acts.

**Pride (ga.on / H1347, ge.ah / H1344):** Psa 31:18: "lying lips...in pride and contempt." Psa 123:4: "the contempt of the proud." Pride is named as the generative condition of contempt in multiple verses.

**Honour/dishonour (ka.vod / H3519, bo.shet):** Mal 1:6: "where is my honour?" — contempt and the withholding of honour are paired as the same dynamic from opposite angles.

---

### T6.2 — Sequential Relationships

**E** — Three sequences:

1. **Contempt → covenant-breaking:** Deu 31:20 and Num 15:31 — the inner verdict of worthlessness produces the volitional act of breaking. The sequence is causal.

2. **Pride → contempt:** Psa 123:4, Job 12:5 — comfort/prosperity/pride generates contempt as its inner expression. Contempt is downstream of pride.

3. **Contempt → estrangement:** Isa 1:4 — despising the Holy One produces utter estrangement. The inner verdict, held over time, produces a relational rupture that becomes a condition.

---

### T6.3 — Causal Relationships

**E** — Contempt **produces**: covenant-breaking (Deu 31:20, Num 15:31), estrangement (Isa 1:4), closed inner life (group 172-002 — contempt of reproof closes the formation channel), blasphemous speech (group 1567-001 — ne.a.tsah).

Contempt **is produced by**: pride and comfort (Job 12:5, Psa 123:4), cognitive misreading of divine accountability (Psa 10:13), prosperity-induced spiritual satiation (Deu 31:20).

**Constitutive relationship:** Pro 18:3 ("when wickedness comes, contempt comes also") — contempt is presented as a co-constitutive companion of wickedness, not merely a consequence of it. The two travel together as co-expressions of the same disordered inner life.

---

### T6.4 — Vocabulary Sharing

**E** — The term ba.zah (H0959) appears in Isa 49:7 alongside ta.av (H8581, abhorrence — M06-C): "deeply despised, abhorred by the nation" — contempt and abhorrence co-occur as paired descriptions of the Servant's reception. The vocabulary of contempt (ba.zah) and abhorrence (ta.av) overlaps in the suffering-servant context. The two characteristics share verse-space here in the same way that hatred and abhorrence shared verse-space in M06-A — they describe the same experience from different angles (the evaluative dismissal and the visceral recoil).

---

### T6.5 — Distinctions

**E** — The nearest neighbour is **M06-A (Hatred)**. The distinction has been established: contempt is an evaluative dismissal (worth-verdict), hatred is relational hostility. They can co-occur (one can both hate and despise) but they engage different faculties. The second nearest neighbour is **M06-C (Abhorrence)** — abhorrence is visceral moral recoil from what is morally repugnant. Contempt is affectively cool dismissal; abhorrence is affectively intense recoil. The distinction in affect and faculty (moral evaluation in contempt; conscience/abhorrence-response in M06-C) is the boundary.

---

### T6.6 — Shared Anchors

**E** — **Isa 53:3** is the most significant shared anchor. It will function as a primary verse in studies of suffering, rejection, the Servant of the Lord, and any Christological study. It brings contempt into contact with the programme's deepest theological territory. **Gen 25:34** (Esau despising the birthright) will share with the love cluster and the covenantal themes. **Mal 1:6** will share with studies of honour, worship, and priestly formation.

The distinction is that those characteristics represent the properly functioning moral evaluation faculty; contempt represents its disordered form.

## T7 — Evidential and Methodological Foundation

### T7.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis

**T7.1.1-3** — Primary terms and semantic range:

The six M06-B terms together reveal that contempt in the biblical vocabulary operates across a spectrum from the private inner verdict (Psa 10:13 — "say in his heart") through habitual evaluation (priests despising God's name, Mal 1:6) through blasphemous speech (ne.a.tsah) through social contagion (biz.za.von). The root of na.ats carries the sense of treating as a disgrace or worthless thing. Ba.zah carries the sense of treating as light/thin — the metaphor of thinness naming the removal of weight and substance from the object. These root metaphors confirm that contempt is fundamentally about worth-removal: the object is made thin, light, insubstantial in the evaluator's inner register.

**T7.1.4** — Vocabulary distinctions:

The vocabulary covers: inner act (ba.zah — the despising act), sustained habitual orientation (na.ats — spurning as a characterological posture), speech expression (ne.a.tsah — blasphemous contempt in speech), social-contagion form (biz.za.von), experienced condition from the receiving side (buz — contempt as what is experienced), and corporate-national contempt (shut). This is a slightly less complete vocabulary architecture than M06-A but covers the main registers.

**T7.1.5** — Structural opposite: **Honour (ka.vod) and fear of the Lord (yir.ah).** Both are named explicitly in the anchor verse for Mal 1:6: "where is my honour...where is my fear?" The opposite of contempt is not merely the absence of contempt but the active attribution of honour and the inner posture of reverent attention.

**T7.1.6** — Person-type: The one who holds contempt is named implicitly by na.ats's recurring "those who despise me" pattern. The proud person (Psa 123:4: "the contempt of the proud") is the person-type of the contemner.

**T7.1.7** — Seeking-term: **S** — not applicable. Contempt is not sought.

**T7.1.8-9** — LXX and NT: **G** — requires database query. The Greek exoutheneo (to treat as nothing) is the standard LXX/NT equivalent for several of these terms and will be relevant to NT studies of contempt, particularly in Paul (Rom 14 — "do not despise the one who abstains").

**T7.1.10** — Full vocabulary arc: The arc reveals that contempt is the inner-being's evaluative faculty producing a negative verdict on worth — from the private heart-speech through habitual orientation through blasphemous expression through social propagation. The arc is consistent: all forms name the removal of proper worth from what deserves it.

---

### T7.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation

**T7.2.1** — Primary anchor function: **Num 14:11** (group 172-001): "How long will this people despise me?" Na.ats functions as the description of Israel's persistent inner posture toward God. The term carries the weight of the historical accusation — this is not a single act but a sustained orientation ("how long") that God names as the characteristic inner reality of the wilderness generation. The form is divine speech, making the naming authoritative.

**T7.2.2** — Literary forms: The M06-B evidence spans narrative (Gen 25:34), prophetic address (Isa 1:4, Mal 1:6, Eze 35:12), lament psalm (Psa 123:3-4), wisdom/narrative (Job 12:5), legal proclamation (Num 15:31), and historical prayer (Neh 9:18, 9:26). The dominant form is **prophetic address** — contempt is primarily a characteristic that the prophets name and diagnose in Israel, which means the verse evidence is shaped by the rhetorical purpose of prophetic confrontation.

**T7.2.3** — Key argument: The clearest logical argument is in Mal 1:6: "a son honours his father, and a servant his master. If then I am a father, where is my honour? And if I am a master, where is my fear?" The argument is analogical: the natural relational order (son/father, servant/master) establishes a norm of honour-giving; God's covenantal relationship with Israel is of the same kind; therefore Israel's failure to honour God is contempt. The argument grounds contempt's wrongness in the violation of a relational norm that everyone recognises as valid.

**T7.2.4** — Contextual settings: The dominant setting is **covenantal** — contempt in M06-B is almost always addressed within the covenant relationship. The prophetic form carries this setting: the prophets address Israel as covenant partners who have despised what the covenant requires them to honour.

**T7.2.5-6** — Primary anchor verses: **Num 14:11** (group 172-001 anchor — the divine naming of Israel's persistent contempt) and **Isa 53:3** (group 1280-001 anchor — the paradigmatic experience of being despised) function as the two poles of M06-B's evidential base: the act of despising from the inside, and the experience of being despised from the outside.

### T7.3 — Human Science Frameworks

**T7.3.1** — Primary field: **Social and moral psychology** — specifically the psychology of contempt, moral emotion research, and the social functions of disgust and contempt. Secondary: **developmental psychology** (formation of contemptuous attitudes) and **social psychology** (contempt's social propagation, in-group/out-group dynamics).

**T7.3.2** — Convergences:

**Convergence 1 — The three-component contempt model:** Social psychology (notably the work of researchers distinguishing anger, disgust, and contempt as the "CAD triad") identifies contempt as the emotion of moral hierarchy violation — directed at persons judged to be beneath one's level in a moral or social hierarchy. This maps onto the M06-B verse evidence's consistent upward-direction of contempt (creature toward Creator, lower social strata despising what should be above them). The science illuminates the social-hierarchical dimension of what the verses name as a covenantal violation.

**Convergence 2 — Contempt as relationship-ending emotion:** Research in relationship psychology (particularly John Gottman's work on the "Four Horsemen" of relationship dissolution) identifies contempt as the single most destructive relational force — it signals the complete withdrawal of respect and is the strongest predictor of relationship breakdown. This maps precisely onto what M06-B's evidence shows: estrangement (Isa 1:4), covenant-breaking (Deu 31:20), exclusion from inheritance (Num 14:23). The science confirms that contempt is structurally relationship-terminating.

**Convergence 3 — Contempt's cognitive grounding:** Cognitive psychology identifies contempt as requiring a prior cognitive evaluation — one must judge something as genuinely beneath regard before contempt can arise. This maps onto Psa 10:13's inner speech: the cognitive verdict ("you will not call to account") precedes and grounds the contemptuous dismissal. The science names the mechanism; the verse evidence names the phenomenon.

**T7.3.3** — Divergences:

**Divergence 1 — The object of contempt:** Social psychology treats contempt as a primarily horizontal, person-directed social emotion. M06-B's evidence shows contempt directed primarily at God, his word, his covenant, and sacred things — objects that social psychology has no category for as objects of this emotion. The science cannot engage the theological dimension of contempt directed upward.

**Divergence 2 — The social contagion dimension:** Biz.za.von (Est 1:18) names contempt as a social contagion — one act spreading the disposition through a community. While social psychology documents conformity and norm-following, the specific mechanism by which one person's contemptuous act permission-structures a whole community's inner disposition is not fully addressed in the standard contempt literature.

**T7.3.4** — Science gap: The science of **authority and legitimacy** (why people come to despise legitimate authority) is developed in social psychology and political science. The M06-B evidence is full of the contempt of legitimate authority — God as father/master, priests who should honour the covenant, Israel who should honour God's law. The science of how legitimate authority loses its perceived legitimacy in the minds of those under it would add explanatory depth to the Deuteronomy and Malachi material. This gap does not require additional verse investigation but the science would illuminate the mechanism.
