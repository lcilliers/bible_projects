## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design (12 prompts)

**T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic reveals God's nature primarily through the divine response: active revulsion, loathing, and rejection directed at specific inner moral conditions. Pro 6:16 (vc=65952 — VCG-05) frames God as the one who "hates" and finds "abomination" in seven specific attitudes and acts — establishing that God's holiness is not merely a passive state of purity but an active repugnance toward moral corruption. Pro 3:32 (vc=1686 — VCG-02) contrasts the abomination of the devious person with divine intimacy: "the devious person is an abomination to the Lord, but the upright are in his confidence" — revealing that God's moral character involves both rejection of the crooked and intimate sharing with the upright. Pro 11:20 (vc=1677 — VCG-01) and Pro 16:5 (vc=1689 — VCG-02) confirm that God's revulsion is specifically directed at inner-being conditions (crooked heart, arrogant heart). Luk 16:15 (vc=21 — VCG-02) reveals that what humans exalt as admirable is an abomination in God's sight — God's evaluative standard is categorically different from human social evaluation. The evidence collectively reveals God as morally sensitive at the inner-being level, responding to the quality of the heart with either revulsion or delight.

---

**T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Scripture does not attribute the experience of being abominable to God — God is not the one who becomes an abomination. But Scripture does explicitly attribute the response-side of the characteristic to God: God is the primary agent of the revulsion that names and constitutes something as abominable. Pro 8:7 (vc=65953 — VCG-02): "wickedness is an abomination to my lips" — spoken in Wisdom's voice, implying a divine-moral faculty of revulsion. Pro 11:1 (vc=65954 — VCG-01): "a false balance is an abomination to the Lord" — God's reaction is the defining standard. The divine attribution reveals that the characteristic's constitutive element (the revulsion response) is divine in origin: what makes something an abomination is specifically God's active moral response to it. This is analytically significant — the characteristic is not self-certifying in the human sphere but is defined by reference to the divine inner response. God's revulsion is the measure.

---

**T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Scripture is not silent but actively declarative: God is the one who reviles, not the one who is reviled. The relevant silence concerns the human experience of being abominable to oneself — the characteristic's subjective inner dimension is almost entirely framed from the divine-perspective side (what God finds abominable) rather than as a first-person inner experience of the abominable person. Where the human inner experience does appear — Eze 36:31 (vc=1681 — VCG-04): "you will loathe yourselves for your iniquities and your abominations" — it appears as an awakened human conscience finally registering the divine verdict. The silence about God possessing the experience of abomination (being repugnant) reveals the characteristic as a purely creaturely failure-condition: God has no moral corruption that would make him abominable. What God has is the morally sensitive faculty of revulsion that names and responds to abomination in the creature.

---

**T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The evidence does not address what purpose abomination as a category of inner moral failure serves in the originally designed human. The characteristic is framed entirely as a judgment on fallen moral conditions — the devious heart, the arrogant heart, dishonest commerce, silenced conscience — not as a designed element. The evidence's consistent framing is diagnostic and prophylactic: it names what is repugnant to God so that people may be warned away from it (Pro 6:16-19 listing the seven; the Proverbs antithetical contrasts). The purpose served by the characteristic in the created human is absent from the evidence — the evidence concerns what must be avoided and what happens when it is not, not what designed function it serves.

---

**T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of original created design, fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence presents abomination as a category of fallen moral failure, not original design. The Proverbs evidence identifies specific inner-being conditions that are abominable: the crooked heart (Pro 11:20 — VCG-01), the arrogant heart (Pro 16:5 — VCG-02), thoughts of the wicked (Pro 15:26 — VCG-02) — all of which presuppose a departure from an intended inner orientation. The conscience evidence (VCG-04 — Jer 6:15; Jer 8:12; Eze 9:4; Eze 36:31) shows a faculty (conscience) that should respond to abomination with shame and grief but has in some cases been wholly silenced — the designed conscience reacting to abomination is the original equipment; the silence of conscience is the fallen deviation. The characteristic therefore describes conditions that arise within the fallen human inner life, not conditions designed into the original person.

---

**T0.2.3 — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence orients the characteristic toward eschatological exclusion rather than forward fullness. Rev 21:27 (vc=22 — VCG-05): "nothing unclean will ever enter it, nor anyone who does what is detestable or false, but only those who are written in the Lamb's book of life" — the eschatological end-state is one in which abomination is permanently excluded from the divine presence. The trajectory of the characteristic is toward final separation, not toward a positive fulfillment. Rev 17:5 (vc=65878 — VCG-05): the identity constituted by generating abominations ends in judgment. The one redemptive trajectory in the evidence is Eze 36:31 (vc=1681 — VCG-04): restored memory producing self-loathing, which is the conscience-awakening that enables repentance — but that trajectory moves toward departure from the characteristic, not its fulfillment.

**T0.3.1 — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Abomination as a category of moral failure does not express the divine image — it violates it. The evidence consistently frames the abominable inner conditions as antithetical to the relational intimacy with God that image-bearing enables: Pro 3:32 (vc=1686 — VCG-02) explicitly contrasts the devious person as abomination (excluded from divine confidence) with the upright who are in God's confidence — the image-bearing relational intimacy is foreclosed by the abominable inner condition. Rev 21:27 (vc=22 — VCG-05) names the abominable as permanently excluded from the city of the divine presence — the characteristic represents the image-bearer in a state of incompatibility with the God in whose image they were made. Luk 16:15 (vc=21 — VCG-02): what humans exalt (self-justifying pride) is abomination in God's sight — the characteristic marks the inversion of the image's proper orientation from God-exalting to self-exalting.

**T0.3.2 — Does the evidence suggest this characteristic is shared between God and human, or exclusively a creaturely analogue?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic as experienced by the person (being abominable) is exclusively creaturely — God has no moral corruption that would make him an abomination. What the evidence shows shared is the capacity for moral revulsion: Eze 9:4 (vc=1698 — VCG-04) marks those who "sigh and groan over all the abominations" — humans who share in the divine moral response of revulsion and grief at abomination. Pro 29:27 (vc=65959 — VCG-01): "an unjust man is an abomination to the righteous" — the righteous person shares the divine response of moral revulsion toward abominable inner conditions. This suggests that the capacity for moral revulsion at abomination (as opposed to being abominable) is a shared capacity between God and image-bearing humans, particularly those whose conscience is functioning rightly.

**T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic reveal about the condition of the divine image?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The presence of the abominable inner conditions (crooked heart, arrogant heart, silenced conscience, dishonest dealings) reveals the divine image in a state of severe corruption — specifically at the moral-relational interface with God. Pro 3:32 (vc=1686 — VCG-02) reveals the consequence: the devious person (whose inner character is abominable) is excluded from God's confidence — the relational intimacy of the image-bearing relationship is severed. The absence of abominable conditions (the upright, the blameless) reveals the image more functional: Pro 11:20 (vc=1677 — VCG-01) — "those of blameless ways are his delight" — the image in its intended orientation toward God produces delight rather than revulsion. Tit 1:16 (vc=27 — VCG-02): those who profess God but deny him by their works are "detestable" — the gap between verbal profession and inner/practical reality is the specific image-corruption this characteristic diagnoses.

## T1 — Inner Being Faculty in Operation (24 prompts)

**T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name signal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic is named "Abomination — divine revulsion on moral character." Three components carry analytical weight. "Abomination" signals a category of extreme moral repugnance that violates a sacred boundary — not merely wrongdoing but a quality of inner being or conduct that produces active revulsion. "Divine revulsion" specifies the agent of the response: the revulsion is God's, not merely human social disapproval, establishing the characteristic as a relational category defined at the God-human interface. "On moral character" limits the scope: this is the moral-character sub-register of abomination, distinct from M10b-D (idolatrous abomination). The name signals that the characteristic operates at the intersection of divine holiness (God's capacity for revulsion) and human moral character (the inner conditions that trigger it): Pro 11:20 (vc=1677 — VCG-01 — "those of crooked heart are an abomination"), Pro 16:5 (vc=1689 — VCG-02 — "everyone who is arrogant in heart is an abomination").

---

**T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Primary Hebrew: to.e.vah (H8441, ~35V in this characteristic) — the term names that which is ritually or morally abhorrent; its root may connect to a verb of loathing or turning away in disgust. At the definitional level, to.e.vah designates a category of violation so severe that it provokes active revulsion rather than mere disapproval. The term is consistently relational — "an abomination to the Lord" or "to the righteous" — signalling that abomination is defined in relationship (the revulsion of the other), not self-referentially. Primary Greek: bdelugma (G0946, 4V — Luk 16:15; Rev 17:4; 17:5; 21:27) — from bdeo, to stink; the root names what is repugnant through its putrid, offensive quality. bdeluktos (G0947, 1V — Tit 1:16) — the adjectival form: the detestable or loathsome person. The Greek root's derivation from the concept of stench adds a sensory-disgust dimension: abomination is that which morally stinks in the divine nostrils.

---

**T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The name carries all three, with the relational dimension most prominent. Relational: to.e.vah is structurally relational — it names the inner condition from the perspective of the one who is revolted (God or the righteous). Every instance is "an abomination to the Lord" or "an abomination to the righteous" — the characteristic is defined by the divine-relational response, not by an internal self-assessment. Pro 29:27 (vc=65959 — VCG-01): the mutual repulsion of wicked and righteous confirms the relational definition. Constitutional: Pro 11:20 (vc=1677 — VCG-01) and Pro 16:5 (vc=1689 — VCG-02) locate abomination in the heart (crooked heart, arrogant heart) — constitutional inner conditions, not merely surface acts. Directional: Eze 14:6 (vc=65984 — VCG-04): "turn away your faces from all your abominations" — the characteristic names the direction the will is currently facing, and repentance requires a directional reversal.

---

**T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic is analytically a compound phenomenon: it is simultaneously a quality of the inner person (what makes them abominable), a condition of the inner person (the state of their heart/conscience), and a relational judgment (the divine verdict of revulsion). The Proverbs evidence (VCG-01, VCG-02) treats abomination primarily as a quality — the quality of dishonest practice (Pro 11:1 — vc=65954), of the crooked heart (Pro 11:20 — vc=1677), of the arrogant heart (Pro 16:5 — vc=1689). The VCG-04 conscience evidence treats it as a condition — a state the inner person is in relative to their conscience's engagement with abominable acts (Jer 6:15 — vc=1680: conscience wholly silenced). The relational verdict (divine revulsion as response) is the frame within which both quality and condition are assessed. The most analytically precise classification is: a quality of the inner moral character that provokes divine relational revulsion, constituting a sacred-boundary violation.

**T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The 5-VCG structure reveals multiple constituent elements. The characteristic has: (a) the commercial/ethical-practice register — dishonest weights, false balances, perverted judgment (VCG-01, 10V: Deu 25:16; Pro 11:1; Pro 11:20; Pro 15:9; Pro 16:12; Pro 17:15; Pro 20:10; Pro 20:23; Pro 24:9; Pro 29:27); (b) the inner-character register — pride, crooked heart, hidden corruption, lying lips, wicked thoughts (VCG-02, 9V: Pro 3:32; Pro 8:7; Pro 12:22; Pro 15:26; Pro 16:5; Pro 26:25; Luk 16:15; Tit 1:16; Rev 17:4); (c) the corrupt worship register — religious acts offered by the inwardly corrupt person (VCG-03, 4V: Pro 15:8; Pro 21:27; Pro 28:9; Isa 1:13); (d) the conscience register — silenced, awakening, and grieving conscience in relation to abomination (VCG-04, 11V); (e) the identity-constituted register — choice, identity, and being wholly constituted by abomination (VCG-05, 6V: Pro 6:16; Pro 13:19; Isa 41:24; Isa 44:19; Rev 17:5; Rev 21:27). These are constituent registers within a single characteristic.

**T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Abomination as divine revulsion on moral character is the verdict and response of divine holiness to specific inner moral conditions and their expressions — a verdict of active revulsion that names certain qualities of the heart, will, conscience, and conduct as fundamentally incompatible with the divine moral order. The evidence shows the characteristic operating at two poles: the divine-inner response (God's revulsion — Pro 11:1; Pro 16:5; Luk 16:15) and the human-inner state (the abominable condition — crooked heart, arrogant heart, silenced conscience, dishonest dealing). Between these poles lies the three-part mechanism: the act or inner condition that defiles, the sacred boundary it transgresses, and the divine revulsion that responds. The 40-verse corpus documents the characteristic across commercial practice (VCG-01), inner character (VCG-02), worship (VCG-03), conscience (VCG-04), and identity (VCG-05), showing it as a comprehensive category of divine moral response to human inner-being failure.

**T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response?**

The difference is between the immediate moral recoil (possible early) and the settled non-response (the outcome of sustained unaddressed abomination).

**T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Moral psychology and emotion science — specifically the psychology of disgust and moral condemnation — are the most useful frameworks. The characteristic is constitutively affective-relational: it names divine revulsion (an emotion-analogue) in response to specific moral violations. The contemporary psychology of disgust (Paul Rozin, Jonathan Haidt) is highly relevant: disgust is the emotion characterised by a specific sensory-revolting quality (nausea, repulsion), a motivation to expel or distance oneself from the disgusting object, and a judgment of the disgusting object as contaminated or polluted. The Greek bdelugma's derivation from bdeo (to stink) maps directly onto disgust psychology's oral-sensory-disgust origin. The OT to.e.vah's relational (to the Lord) framing maps onto moral disgust — the disgust response directed at moral violations rather than sensory contaminants. Moral foundations theory (Haidt) identifies disgust/purity as one of six moral foundations — CHAR-3 is the biblical expression of the purity/sanctity moral foundation.

**T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence, what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Disgust psychology illuminates the characteristic at several points. (2) The social-contamination aspect (Ezr 9:11 — VCG-04: abominations filling the land) is illuminated by disgust psychology's finding that disgust involves a contagion-sensitivity — the disgusting thing spreads its contaminating quality to what it contacts. (3) Jer 6:15 and 8:12 (VCG-04 — no shame or blushing) are illuminated by the psychology of shame suppression: sustained commission of morally disgusting acts can desensitise the shame response, eventually rendering it non-functional.

**T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The most significant divergence: the human science framework (Haidt's moral foundations theory) treats the purity/sanctity foundation as one cultural variable among six — different cultures weight the disgust/purity foundation differently, and many contemporary Western moral frameworks consider this foundation as less morally fundamental than harm or fairness. The verse evidence presents the divine abomination verdict as absolute and non-culturally-relative: Pro 16:5 (vc=1689 — VCG-02) — "everyone who is arrogant in heart is an abomination to the Lord; be assured, he will not go unpunished" — the verdict is not culturally contingent. This divergence reveals the characteristic as operating on a different plane than cultural moral psychology: it names not what humans find disgusting (which varies culturally) but what God finds abominable (which is presented as absolute and invariant). The disgust-science framework is a useful illuminating lens but cannot account for the absolute-divine character of the verdict.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The disgust psychology framework surfaces the question of how the disgust response relates to in-group/out-group dynamics. The science framework surfaces the in-group/out-group dimension as a potentially interesting analytical question, but the verse evidence's universal scope limits how far that framework applies.
