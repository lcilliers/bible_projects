## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

---

### T5.1.1 — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?

**[A]** E — M01-A reverential fear produces transformation in both dimensions. It changes **orientation**: Exo 14:31 (Israel's witness of God's power over Egypt produces fear of the Lord and belief — the people's inner orientation shifts from terror of Egypt to reverent trust in God); Jon 1:16 (sailors' storm-terror converts into reverential worship — "fear becomes the inner ground of devotion" — a transformation of orientation from threat-directed to God-directed fear). It also produces change in **condition**: Pro 10:27 (fear of the Lord extending life — a condition-change); Pro 19:23 (fear leading to life and "restful inner satisfaction" — a settled new condition of security); Jer 32:40 (God implanting fear as a permanent disposition "preventing them from turning away" — a condition of inner stability replacing prior instability). Both dimensions are evidenced.

**[B]** E — M01-B's commanded-release register (M01-B-VCG-06, anchor Isa 43:1) produces transformation in orientation: the person who receives "do not fear" moves from a grip of acute dread to a posture of trust grounded in divine presence. Dan 10:19 (M01-B-VCG-01: "fear is commanded away and replaced by peace and strength — the inner person moves from fearful weakness to fortified courage as the heavenly messenger speaks"). This is primarily **orientation-change** — the person's relationship to their threatening circumstance shifts without the circumstance itself changing.

**[C]** E — M01-C's divinely deployed terror produces **condition-change** in enemy peoples: Gen 35:5 (God-sent terror paralysing the will of surrounding cities — their condition of active hostility becomes one of paralysis); Est 9:2-3 (dread of the Jews redirecting the will of provincial officials — their condition of opposition converts to active assistance). The condition of the enemy is transformed by the terror imposed.

**[D]** E — M01-D dismay produces **condition-change** through its destructive dimension (inner collapse) and through its commanded-against-dismay corpus (M01-D-VCG-04): Jer 1:17 (the prophet commanded not to let dismay grip him — the implied transformation is from potential collapse to sustained composure grounded in divine commission). Dismay unchecked is a condition-worsening transformation; commanded against, it is resisted as the precondition of a transformed stable condition.

**[E]** E — M01-E-VCG-02 (trembling at God's word, anchor Isa 66:2) produces transformation in orientation: those who tremble at God's word form a defined community whose orientation toward divine communication is permanently marked by reverential sobriety (Isa 66:5: their trembling reverence marks their identity and sets them apart, with God's vindication promised to this inner posture — an orientation-change producing a stable identity).

**[F]** E — M01-F's primary transformative movement is from chronic anxiety to rest. Isa 12:2 (M01-F-VCG-04: "trust in God displacing dread from the inner person — the speaker declares that because God is his strength, fear no longer has a hold on his heart"); 1Jo 4:18 (M01-A-VCG-06/F-VCG-04: "perfect love expels fear entirely" — a transformation of inner condition in which the characteristic is removed). Psa 34:4 (anchor M01-F-VCG-02: "the psalmist sought the Lord and was delivered from all his fears — these fears were inner terrors and anxieties; divine response removed them entirely"). Both condition and orientation change.

**[G]** E — M01-G's transformation is the spirit-level move from timidity to the spirit of power, love, and self-control (2Ti 1:7 anchor: the contrast implies that the gift of God's spirit displaces the spirit of timidity — a spirit-level condition-change). 2Ti 1:6 context (the call to stir up the gift) implies the transformation is not automatic but involves the person's own engagement.

**[CLUSTER]** E — M01 produces transformation in both condition and orientation across all sub-groups. The most consistent transformation pattern is: fear directed toward God (M01-A) produces stable new conditions (wisdom, life, security) and transformed orientations (from threat-directed to God-directed). Fear commanded away (M01-B-VCG-06) transforms orientation without necessarily changing circumstance. Chronic fear overcome (M01-F, G) transforms condition (from bondage to freedom). The cluster confirms that fear is formatively powerful in both directions — ordered fear builds the person up; unaddressed disordered fear tears the person down.

### T5.1.2 — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?

**[A]** E — M01-A evidences both reversible and irreversible transformation. The **irreversible** dimension: Jer 32:40 (God implanting the fear as a "permanent inner disposition preventing them from turning away" — permanence is explicit); the "Fear of Isaac" formula (Gen 31:42; 31:53 — anchors A-VCG-06) suggests a defining characterisation that became Isaac's identity, not a reversible episode. The **reversible** dimension: 2Ki 17 corpus (M01-A-VCG-05: the settlers' syncretistic fear shows that genuine fear of God can be corrupted — a reversal of the right inner orientation). Isa 29:13 (fear becoming hollow external formula — a degradation of what was once genuine). The evidence shows transformation can be reversed through corruption, hardened heart (Isa 63:17), and competing allegiance.

**[B]** E — M01-B's acute-fear transformations are primarily **reversible** in the moment (fear released when divine reassurance comes: Mat 17:7; Luk 5:10) but can calcify into permanent states (1Sa 18:29: Saul's fear of David intensifies and "becomes permanent — the inner root of his sustained enmity"). Reversibility depends on whether the fear is addressed or left to solidify.

**[G]** E — M01-G: 2Ti 1:7 (timidity is not from God) implies it is a reversible condition — the gift of power, love, and self-control is available (2Ti 1:6). But Rev 21:8 (cowardice in the lake of fire) suggests that sustained unchecked timidity culminates in irreversible judgment-state.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The transformation produced by M01 is: reversible in its acute form (M01-B: fear addressed and released); permanently formative in its sustained ordered form (M01-A: Jer 32:40 implantation); reversible through corruption or hardening (M01-A: Isa 29:13; 2Ki 17 corpus); and eschatologically irreversible in its final expression (M01-G: Rev 21:8; M01-A: Rev 11:18 — the redeemed fear God permanently).

### T5.2.1 — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?

**[A]** E — M01-A-VCG-04 (fear arising from witnessing God's deeds) shows a clear before-during-after sequence. Before: complacency or absence of fear (Exo 9:30: Pharaoh and servants do not yet fear the Lord). During: encounter with God's power (Exo 14:31: witnessing God's victory at the sea). After: fear and belief (Exo 14:31: "Israel feared the Lord and believed" — a settled new inner state following the encounter). Jon 1:5-16 shows the most complete sequence: before (mariners fearing the storm, crying to their own gods), during (encounter with Jonah's testimony and the storm's cessation), after (Jon 1:16: "the men feared the Lord greatly" and offered sacrifice and vows — a transformed reverential state).

**[B]** E — M01-B-VCG-06 (commanded release of fear, anchor Isa 43:1) shows a before-during-after sequence structurally: before (fear gripping the inner person), during (divine word of identity and assurance spoken), after (the commanded release taking effect as trust replaces dread — implicit in the corpus as the intended outcome of each "do not fear" command). Dan 10:19 (M01-B-VCG-01: fear gripping Daniel → angel touching him and speaking → "inner person moves from fearful weakness to fortified courage") is the most explicit sequence.

**[E]** E — M01-E-VCG-02 (trembling at God's word) shows a sequence: before (the condition of those who do not tremble — spiritual insensitivity), during (encountering God's word in a posture of humility and contrition), after (trembling as the stable inner-somatic posture of the community defined by it, Isa 66:5: "God's vindication promised to this inner posture"). Ezr 9:4-10:3 shows the sequence more concretely: before (covenant unfaithfulness discovered), during (Ezra and the trembling community gathering in appalled grief), after (covenant decision made from the posture of trembling — a transformed community orientation toward God's commandments).

**[F]** E — M01-F-VCG-02 (anchor Job 3:25) shows the dread-sequence: before (dread sitting within as anticipated fear), during (the dreaded event materialising), after (the correspondence between fear and event — confirmation of the anticipatory dread). Psa 34:4 (anchor F-VCG-02: fear → seeking the Lord → deliverance from all fears) shows the redemptive sequence.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The verse evidence describes clear before-during-after sequences primarily in M01-A (complacency → encounter → reverential fear), M01-B-VCG-06 (fear → divine word → release), M01-E-VCG-02 (insensitivity → humility → trembling identity), and M01-F's redemptive form (dread → seeking → deliverance). The sequences across the cluster share a common structure: an initial state, a transformative encounter or word, and a new settled inner condition.

### T5.2.2 — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?

**[A]** E — The states in M01-A's sequence are: (1) absence of reverential fear / competing fear-objects; (2) encounter with God's power or word; (3) settled reverential orientation. The sequence reveals that fear-of-God is formed through encounter and then becomes stable disposition — it is not achieved by effort alone but by genuine meeting with God's reality. The Jer 32:40 implantation sequence (divine implantation → permanent disposition) shows the divine-initiative version of the same structure.

**[B-VCG-06]** E — The states in M01-B-VCG-06's sequence are: (1) acute fear gripping the will; (2) divine word of identity and presence ("you are mine," Isa 43:1); (3) trust replacing dread. The sequence reveals that the characteristic works through personal address — the release of fear requires not just information but relational claim. God's "you are mine" is the operative mechanism, not a generic reassurance.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across the evidenced sequences, the operative mechanism is consistent: encounter with a superior reality (divine presence, God's word, divine identity) transforms the inner person's prior state. The characteristic works through encounter, not through mere cognitive recalibration.

### T5.3.1 — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?

**[A]** E — M01-A uses multiple mechanisms: (1) **encounter** — Exo 14:31 (witnessing God's power at the sea); Hag 1:12 (hearing the prophetic word); Jon 1:16 (storm + testimony producing sudden reverential shift); (2) **gradual formation** — Deu 14:23; 31:12-13 (regular worship and Torah-reading designed to cultivate the fear over time — explicitly long-term formative process); Pro 2:5 (fear as "fruit of earnest seeking" — arrived at through diligent pursuit rather than sudden encounter); (3) **divine implantation** — Jer 32:40 (God pledging to place the fear directly in the hearts of his people — a divine act of immediate transformation rather than gradual process); (4) **social transmission** — Psa 34:11 (psalmist teaching fear to children — interpersonal formation mechanism).

**[B]** E — M01-B's acute fear is produced by **encounter** (sudden, involuntary): 1Sa 17:24 (sight of Goliath immediately triggering overwhelming fear); Luk 2:9 (angelic appearance immediately filling the shepherds with great fear). The release of acute fear (B-VCG-06) uses the mechanism of **divine word** — the spoken command "do not fear" grounded in divine identity and presence.

**[E]** E — M01-E-VCG-02's trembling-at-word is produced by **gradual formation** in community: Ezr 9:4; 10:3 (the community of those who tremble at God's word — a formed posture, not a sudden event). The M01-E-VCG-01 theophanic trembling is produced by **sudden encounter** (Exo 19:16: the Sinai phenomena producing immediate involuntary trembling).

**[F]** E — M01-F's chronic anxiety operates as a sustained **condition** rather than a mechanism producing change. Its resolution (Psa 34:4 anchor: seeking the Lord → deliverance from all fears) uses the mechanism of **divine response to seeking** — not discipline or self-effort but relational engagement with God producing the resolution.

**[G]** E — M01-G's transformation from timidity to the spirit of power uses the mechanism of **stirring up the divine gift** (2Ti 1:6-7 context: "stir up the gift of God" — implying active engagement with what God has already given, not a passive waiting for sudden transformation).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The mechanisms producing change in M01 are: encounter (M01-A from God's acts, M01-B from angelic/divine appearance), gradual formation (M01-A through Torah and worship practices), divine implantation (M01-A, Jer 32:40), divine word (M01-B-VCG-06 commanded release), divine response to seeking (M01-F resolution), and active engagement with divine gift (M01-G, 2Ti 1:6-7). The cluster does not evidence discipline as a primary mechanism for forming reverential fear; formation is primarily through encounter and sustained practice.

The mechanism is context-dependent, with all pathways converging on the same inner disposition.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Different canonical contexts deploy different mechanisms for forming the same reverential fear. The consistency of the destination (reverential fear as governing orientation) with the diversity of the mechanisms confirms that the characteristic is convergently achievable through multiple pathways.

### T5.4.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?

**[A]** E — M01-A operates in the context of suffering. Job 1:8; 2:3 (God affirming Job's fear-of-God character after intense suffering — the suffering does not destroy the reverential posture; rather, the fear is shown to hold through it). Job 2:3 anchor: "God again upholds Job's God-fearing character after intense suffering, showing that **genuine reverence is a disposition that holds fast even under severe trial**." Fear-of-God is tested by suffering and shown to be the characteristic that does not yield to affliction.

**[B]** E — M01-B operates extensively as a **response to suffering**: 2Cor 7:5 (M01-F-VCG-05 context: "fear is located 'within' as an inward experience alongside external conflicts, describing an internal climate of dread or anxiety that the body itself endured with no rest" — suffering producing acute inner fear). 1Pe 3:14 (M01-B-VCG-03: believers who suffer for righteousness are commanded not to let fear of persecutors grip their inner being — suffering is the context in which the command against fear is necessary).

**[D]** E — M01-D dismay is routinely produced by catastrophic suffering: Job 4:5 (M01-D-VCG-01: "when suffering touched Job personally, dismay overtook him — adversity reaching one's own self breaks inner composure in a way advising others could not prepare one for"); Psa 6:2-3 (anchor M01-D-VCG-01: the psalmist's bones deeply troubled and soul greatly dismayed — physical suffering producing inner collapse). Suffering is the primary context and producer of M01-D.

**[F]** E — M01-F-VCG-01 (chronic pervasive anxiety) is explicitly a product of sustained suffering and threat: Eze 4:16; 12:18-19 (anxiety accompanying eating bread by weight in besieged Jerusalem — siege conditions as the producing context for chronic anxiety). Heb 2:15 (fear of death holding people in lifelong inner slavery — mortality itself as the suffering context for this anxiety).

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity's context is explicitly persecution and threat: Rev 21:8 (cowardice in the context of apostasy under pressure); 1Pe 3:14-15 (M01-B-VCG-03/A-VCG-06: believers commanded not to fear persecutors — the threat of persecution is the suffering context for the timidity-temptation).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Suffering and affliction are the primary context for M01's disordered sub-groups (B's social fear, D's dismay, F's chronic anxiety, G's timidity). Suffering is the **test** for M01-A's reverential fear (Job corpus) and the **context** in which the characteristic either holds (Job 2:3) or yields (Isa 63:17; Jer 3:8). The cluster confirms that suffering is the crucible in which the inner fear-disposition is revealed and tested.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster divides on this question: suffering tests and reveals the ordered fear (M01-A), while it produces the disordered forms (M01-B's severity, D, F, G). This asymmetry is a significant formative finding: the person whose inner life is governed by reverential fear of God (M01-A) has a fear that can withstand suffering; those governed by other fears find that suffering intensifies and reveals the inadequacy of those fear-objects.

### T6.2.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic consistently preceding, following, or accompanying another characteristic in a sequence?

**[A]** E — M01-A consistently **precedes** wisdom and knowledge as their epistemological root: Pro 1:7; 9:10; Psa 111:10; Job 28:28 (fear is the beginning — it comes before wisdom as its precondition). **[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's primary sequences are: perceptive encounter → acute fear → volitional response (M01-B); fear of God → wisdom/ethics (M01-A); catastrophic stimulus → dismay → bodily collapse (M01-D). These sequences are consistent and directional across the evidence.

### T6.2.2 — What does the sequence reveal — is the relationship causal, developmental, or correlational?

**[A]** E — The M01-A → wisdom sequence is **causal and constitutive**: Pro 1:7 establishes that without fear of the Lord the mind is disoriented (fools "despise wisdom" in the same verse). **[B]** E — The encounter → M01-B → volitional response sequence is **causal**: the fear is the direct causal result of the perceptive encounter, and the volitional response is the direct causal result of the fear. The chain is causal at both joints.

**[D]** E — The stimulus → M01-D → bodily collapse sequence is **causal**: the overwhelming stimulus causes the inner dismay which produces the somatic expression. Dan 7:28 ("dismay works through Daniel's thoughts and changes his color" — thought-disruption as the causal intermediary between stimulus and somatic expression).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The sequential relationships in M01 are primarily **causal** rather than merely correlational or developmental. Fear of God causes wisdom; encounter causes acute fear; overwhelming stimulus causes dismay; dismay causes bodily collapse. The cluster is characterised by causal chains running through the volitional and perceptive faculties.

### T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?

**[A]** E — The primary M01-A terms and their root meanings: (1) **ya.re (H3372G, H3372H, H3373)** — root meaning: to fear, to be afraid, to stand in awe. The same root generates the stative verb (to be in a state of fear/reverence), nominal form, and adjective. Stative root significance: the characteristic is a state of being, not primarily an act — it is what the person is, not only what the person does. (2) **yir.ah (H3374)** — nominal form of the ya.re root: "the fear" as a possessed quality, a stable disposition. (3) **pa.chad (H6343)** — root meaning: to dread, to tremble in dread. Derived from a root suggesting the quaking that accompanies overwhelming encounter. (4) **pach.dah (H6345)** — a feminine noun from the pa.chad root — "dread" as a named inner state. (5) **mo.rah / mo.ra (H4172A, H4172B)** — from the ya.re root: "that which causes fear/awe" — the fear-inspiring quality of God or his instruments. (6) **fobeō (G5399)** — root meaning: to flee from, to be put to flight; by extension, to fear (what one flees from). The Greek root's flight-connotation is significant: fear-as-flight is the underlying image. (7) **fobos (G5401)** — the noun form, spanning fear, reverence, and awe. (8) **deos (G6015, Heb 12:28)** — a more refined Greek term for reverential fear distinct from the fobos family — awe in the presence of the sacred.

**[B]** E — M01-B's distinctive primary terms: (1) **de.chal (H1763, Aramaic)** — dread, terror in visionary contexts (Dan 2:31 anchor). (2) **za.chal (H2119B)** — to shrink back, crawl in fear (Mic 7:17 anchor — nations crawling out in fearful retreat). (3) **ya.gor (H3016)** — to be afraid of a specific object, anticipatory object-directed dread. (4) **ra.hah (H7297)** — to be afraid, in a specific Isaianic context. (5) **ekthambeo (G1568), ekthambos (G1569)** — to be greatly amazed/alarmed; the ek- prefix intensifies the thambeo root (amazement/astonishment) — extreme awe-shock. (6) **ekfobos (G1630)** — frightened out of one's senses; extreme terror. (7) **emfobos (G1719)** — in a state of fear; afraid, trembling within. (8) **ptoeō (G4422), ptoēsis (G4423), pturomai (G4426)** — roots meaning to be put to sudden flight, startled, alarmed — the image is of startled birds or sudden fright.

**[C]** E — M01-C's distinctive terms: (1) **e.mah (H0367)** — terror as an overwhelming force; derived from a root suggesting the effect of dread rather than the feeling itself. **[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's primary Hebrew terms are: ya.re / yir.ah (the broadest fear root, spanning reverential to acute), pa.chad / pach.dah (dread with intensity), mo.rah / mo.ra (the fear-inspiring quality), ba.hal / cha.tat (dismay/shattering), cha.rad / ra.gaz (trembling), and de.a.gah (anxiety/worry). The term inventory confirms the cluster's broad semantic range across the fear-spectrum.

### T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?

**[A]** E — The primary M01-A term ya.re functions as: **verb** (ya.re — to fear/revere, stative; the ongoing state of being in fear/reverence), **noun** (yir.ah — the fear as a possessed quality), and **adjective / participle** (ya.re — the one who fears; Job 1:1: "a man who feared God" — the present participle as a character-defining description). This grammatical range reveals that the characteristic operates as: a state of being (stative verb), a stable possession of the person (noun), and a defining character quality (participle). The stative verbal form is especially significant: ya.re describes an ongoing state rather than a completed action, confirming that M01-A is dispositional rather than episodic.

**[B]** E — M01-B's Greek terms: fobeō functions as a verb (episodic — "feared, was afraid") and fobos as a noun. The verb form in M01-B contexts is predominantly aorist or imperfect (completed past or ongoing past event) rather than the present participle (defining quality). This grammatical pattern confirms M01-B as episodic rather than dispositional.

**[D]** E — M01-D's ba.hal functions as a Piel verb (intensive action stem in Hebrew — the characteristic is intensive, not gentle), and cha.tat functions as a Niphal (passive — "to be dismayed, to be shattered") — confirming that dismay is something that happens to the person rather than something they do.

**[E]** E — M01-E's cha.rad functions as a Qal verb (basic stem, often reflexive in context — the body naturally trembles), cha.red as a Qal participle / adjective (the trembling one — character description), and cha.ra.dah as a noun (the trembling as an event). The participle form cha.red (Isa 66:2 anchor: "the one who trembles at my word") functions as a character-defining description, confirming that the trembling-at-word tradition (E-VCG-02) is dispositional like M01-A.

**[G]** E — M01-G's Greek terms (deilia, deiliaō, deilos) are predominantly: noun (deilia — the quality of timidity), verb (deiliaō — to be timid), and adjective (deilos — the cowardly one). The adjective deilos is used in evaluative character-judgments (Mat 8:26; Rev 21:8) — it describes what the person is, not merely what they experience.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The grammatical range across M01 reveals: stative verbs and nouns cluster in the dispositional sub-groups (M01-A, G); aorist and event verbs cluster in the episodic sub-groups (M01-B, C); passive Niphal forms cluster in the experience-of-imposition sub-groups (M01-D: "to be dismayed/shattered"). The participle as character-defining form appears in M01-A (ya.re — the God-fearer) and M01-E-VCG-02 (cha.red — the one who trembles at God's word) and M01-G (deilos — the cowardly one). Grammar confirms the dispositional vs. episodic distinction structurally.

### T7.1.3 — What is the semantic range of the primary term — across what breadth of meaning does it operate?

### T7.1.5 — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?

**[A]** E — The M01 vocabulary does not include a dedicated antonym term. The structural opposites are expressed through: (1) **negative constructions** — "no fear of God before their eyes" (Psa 36:1; Rom 3:18); "do not fear" (the entire M01-B-VCG-06 corpus); "not afraid" (numerous instances); (2) **paired contrasting terms** — cha.tat in M01-D (dismay/shattering) is used in M01-D-VCG-04 as the opposite of composure and courage; (3) **positive opposites** — the vocabulary of trust (batach) and faith (emunah) function as the structural opposites of fear in multiple passages but are not M01 cluster terms. **[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster lacks a dedicated antonym vocabulary. The absence is expressed through negation ("no fear," "not afraid"), through commanded-against states ("do not fear"), and through named positive alternatives (trust, faith, love). This pattern is consistent with a characteristic whose primary expression is positive (the reverential orientation) and whose absence is expressed as a diagnostic failure rather than an achieved counter-state.

### T7.1.8 — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?

**[A]** E — M01-A shows substantial **continuity** across Testaments. The Greek **fobeō / fobos** translates the Hebrew **ya.re / yir.ah** consistently across the Septuagint and NT. The NT picks up the same dispositional usage: Col 3:22 (fearing the Lord as governing orientation for conduct — identical to Lev 19:14 and M01-A-VCG-01 usage); Heb 12:28 (reverence and awe as the fitting disposition for approaching God in worship — identical to the Psalms worship corpus); 1Pe 1:17; 2:17 (fear as governing posture for conduct in exile — identical to Deu 6:2 and M01-A-VCG-02 usage). The NT does not introduce a new concept but extends and deepens the OT's reverential fear vocabulary into the new-covenant context.

**[A]** E — M01-A also shows **development** across Testaments. The NT introduces the tension between fear-as-slavery and fear-as-adoption: Rom 8:15 (the spirit of slavery associated with fear — the fear that adoption displaces is a different kind from the reverential awe that remains); 1Jo 4:18 (perfect love casting out the punishment-anticipatory fear — an eschatological-teleological development beyond the OT wisdom corpus's treatment of fear-of-God). The NT develops the inner discrimination between kinds of fear (servile vs. filial vs. reverential) more explicitly than the OT does.

**[B]** E — M01-B shows development in the NT through the **christological intensification** of theophanic fear: the disciples' fear at Jesus's miracles, transfiguration, and resurrection appearances (M01-B-VCG-01 and 02) use the same vocabulary (fobeō, fobos) as OT theophanies but now applied to the incarnate Son. The development is christological — the OT's ya.re at divine theophanies becomes fobeō at Christ's acts and presence.

**[G]** E — M01-G shows development: the **Greek evaluative vocabulary** (deilia, deiliaō, deilos) is a distinctively NT contribution. The OT does not have an exact equivalent evaluative person-type term for the coward; the closest is the "fearful and fainthearted" soldier of Deu 20:8 (ya.re — the same root as reverential fear) and the "fear of man" (cha.ra.dah) of Pro 29:25. The NT's deilia-family provides a dedicated evaluative-negative vocabulary for timidity that the OT addressed through contextual usage of shared roots.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The OT → NT trajectory across M01 shows: (1) **Continuity** — the same dispositional reverential fear (ya.re/yir.ah → fobeō/fobos) operates across both Testaments; (2) **Christological development** — NT theophanies are Christ-theophanies, concentrating the fear-encounter vocabulary around Jesus's identity; (3) **Discriminatory development** — the NT develops the inner taxonomy of fear (servile/adoption, punishment-anticipatory/love-perfected) more explicitly than the OT; (4) **Evaluative vocabulary addition** — the Greek deilia-family provides a dedicated negative-evaluative vocabulary that the OT lacked. Overall: strong continuity of concept, development of inner discrimination, and christological intensification.

### T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?

**[B]** E — The NT's Greek compound intensifiers represent vocabulary newly coined or specifically deployed for the fear register in NT contexts: **ekthambeo (G1568), ekthambos (G1569), ekfobos (G1630)** — these ek- prefixed compound forms intensify the base fear-terms and appear specifically in the gospel literature describing reactions to Jesus's acts and presence. The ekthambeo root in particular appears to be used distinctively for the awe-shock produced by Jesus's supernatural acts (Mar 9:15 anchor: the crowd's amazement at seeing Jesus; Mar 14:33: Jesus himself ekthambeo in Gethsemane). The coinage of these intensified compound forms in the NT context reveals that the encounters with Jesus produced an intensity of awe-shock that the existing vocabulary of fobeō/fobos did not fully capture — a new vocabulary was deployed to describe what it was like to encounter the incarnate Son of God directly.

**[G]** E — The **deilia / deiliaō / deilos** vocabulary, while existing in classical Greek, is newly deployed in a theological-moral context in the NT. 2Ti 1:7's use of deilia as a named spirit-level disposition not from God, and Rev 21:8's use of deilos as the heading term for the condemned, represents a theological appropriation of the classical Greek ethical vocabulary into the NT's moral-spiritual framework. The coinage reveals that the NT treats timidity as a moral-theological category equivalent in seriousness to idolatry — an evaluation that goes beyond the classical Greek understanding.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The NT's distinctive coinages in M01 are: the ekthambeo / ekfobos compound-intensifiers (for christological encounter-fear) and the theological appropriation of deilia / deilos (for the evaluative-moral category of cowardly apostasy). Both reveal that the NT needed new or newly-deployed vocabulary to describe the intensity of the christological encounters and the seriousness of the failure of courage under pressure.

### T7.2.1 — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?

**[A]** E — In Pro 1:7 (anchor M01-A-VCG-02): "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction." The term yir.ah (fear of the Lord) functions as the **subject and predicate nominative** of the main clause — it IS the beginning of knowledge. The grammatical function is definitional and constitutive: the verse does not say fear leads to knowledge but that fear IS the beginning. This elevates fear from a precondition to a constitutive element. The contrast clause ("fools despise") makes the argument by antithesis: the absence of fear = despising wisdom = foolishness. The argument is: right inner orientation (fear) = knowledge; its absence = folly.

**[A]** E — In Lev 19:14 (anchor M01-A-VCG-01): "You shall not curse the deaf or put a stumbling block before the blind, but you shall fear your God: I am the Lord." The term ya.re (fear your God) functions as the **grounding imperative** — the theological basis for the ethical prohibitions. The argument structure is: prohibition (do not exploit the vulnerable) + grounding command (fear your God) + divine identity (I am the Lord). The fear-term grounds the ethics in the divine reality rather than in consequentialist or social reasoning.

**[B]** E — In Mar 4:41 (anchor M01-B-VCG-02): "And they were filled with great fear and said to one another, 'Who then is this?'" The term fobos functions as the **content of their inner state** that produces the christological question. The fear functions argumentatively as the evidence that what they have witnessed exceeds natural categories — it is the inner-being sign that points toward Jesus's identity.

**[D]** E — In Dan 5:6 (anchor M01-D-VCG-03): "Then the king's color changed, and his thoughts alarmed him; his limbs gave way, and his knees knocked together." The term be.hal (alarmed/dismayed) functions as the **verbal expression of the inner event** from which all somatic consequences flow — the be.hal of his thoughts produces the color change, the limb-failure, the knocking knees. The term is causal in the argument's logic.

**[G]** E — In 2Ti 1:7 (anchor M01-G-VCG-01): "For God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control." The term deilia (fear/timidity) functions as the **named negative** in a contrast structure — God gave NOT this but THAT. The argument structure is: what God gives (positive triple) defined by contrast with what he does not give (deilia). The term carries the full evaluative weight of the sentence.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The primary terms function in their anchor verses as: subject/predicate nominative (constitutive definition — Pro 1:7), grounding imperative (theological basis for ethics — Lev 19:14), inner state pointing outward (christological evidence — Mar 4:41), causal inner event (producing somatic collapse — Dan 5:6), and contrast-term defining what God does not give (evaluative negation — 2Ti 1:7). The diversity of grammatical functions across anchor verses reflects the cluster's diversity of phenomenological registers.

### T7.2.2 — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?

**[A]** E — M01-A's evidence is carried primarily by **wisdom literature** (Pro 1:7; 9:10; 14:26-27; 15:33; Job 28:28) and **psalm literature** (Psa 25:14; 34:7; 103:11-17; 111:10; 112:1). Psalm form requires: reading the psalmist's own experiential testimony as shaped by liturgical use; attending to the historical setting of specific psalms (individual lament, communal praise) while recognising their appropriation as generalisable patterns.

**[B]** E — M01-B's evidence is carried primarily by **narrative** (OT: Gen, Exo, 1Sa, 2Ki corpus) and **gospel narrative** (NT: Mar, Luk, Mat, Joh — the miracle and resurrection stories). Narrative form requires: reading events as historically grounded particular instances rather than universal principles; attending to the narrative context and the characters' roles; recognising that narrative fear responses are descriptive (this is what happened) rather than prescriptive (this is what should happen), except where the narrator explicitly evaluates.

**[C]** E — M01-C's evidence is carried primarily by **prophecy and apocalyptic** (Jer 20:10; Eze 32:23-32; Isa 19:17) and **narrative** (Gen 35:5; Exo 23:27). Prophetic form requires: attending to the oracular address (God speaking, "I will send my terror"), the historical addressees, and the eschatological horizon; recognising that prophetic deployment of terror is typically set within the covenant framework of divine governance.

**[D]** E — M01-D's evidence is carried primarily by **psalm lament** (Psa 6:3; 30:7), **wisdom** (Job 4:5; 21:6; 23:15-16), and **Aramaic narrative** (Dan 4:5; 5:6; 7:15). Each form requires different interpretive care: lament psalms express experiential truth in hyperbolic emotional language; wisdom (Job) is dialogic and requires attending to the speaker (Job, friends, God — whose perspective is being expressed?); Daniel's Aramaic narrative requires attention to the visionary-apocalyptic genre.

**[E]** E — M01-E's evidence is carried by **narrative** (Exo 19:16; 1Sa 28:5), **prophetic oracle** (Isa 66:2; Eze 26:16), and **NT epistle** (Phili 2:12; 1Cor 2:3; 2Cor 7:15). Epistolary form requires: reading as occasional correspondence addressed to specific communities; recognising that Paul's use of tromos/entromos is embedded in pastoral and parenetic contexts.

**[F]** E — M01-F's evidence is carried by **legal/Deuteronomic corpus** (Deu 28:66-67), **psalm** (Psa 34:4; 94:19), **prophetic** (Eze 12:18-19), and **NT epistle** (Heb 2:15; 10:27; 1Jo 4:18; 2Cor 12:20). The legal corpus requires reading Deu 28 within the covenant-curse framework — the consequences are conditional on covenant violation, not universal psychological states.

**[G]** E — M01-G's evidence is carried primarily by **NT epistle** (2Ti 1:7) and **apocalyptic** (Rev 21:8), with **gospel narrative** (Mat 8:26; Mat 25:25). Apocalyptic form (Rev 21:8) requires: attending to the genre's symbolic vision structure; recognising that the lake of fire is eschatological judgment, and the list of condemned types is not a sociological survey but an eschatological warning; the "cowardly" heading the list intensifies the evaluative charge.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's evidence is distributed across all major biblical literary forms: wisdom, psalm, narrative, prophecy, apocalyptic, and epistle. This breadth is itself a finding: fear is not a genre-specific characteristic but one attested across the full literary range of Scripture. Responsible interpretation requires form-sensitive reading — treating wisdom axioms as universal principles, narrative events as specific instances, prophetic oracles within their covenant-address context, and epistolary instruction within its pastoral address.

### T7.2.3 — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?

**[A]** E — Pro 1:7 (anchor M01-A-VCG-02) logical structure: Premise 1: The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge. Premise 2: Fools despise wisdom and instruction. Conclusion (implicit): Those who despise wisdom lack the fear of the Lord; those who have knowledge have its fear as its ground. The argument is a definition plus its negation — establishing the characteristic as constitutive by excluding its alternative.

**[A]** E — Lev 19:14 (anchor M01-A-VCG-01) logical structure: Premise 1: You shall not exploit the vulnerable (deaf and blind). Grounding: You shall fear your God. Premise 2: I am the Lord (the identity that makes the fear meaningful). Conclusion: The ethical prohibition is grounded in the divine relationship, not in consequentialist reasoning or social accountability. The argument moves: prohibition → divine-fear ground → divine identity claim.

**[G]** E — 2Ti 1:7 (anchor M01-G-VCG-01) logical structure: Premise: God gave us a spirit of power, love, and self-control. Implicit premise: Timidity is not consistent with this spirit. Conclusion: Timidity is not from God; what God gives is courage, love, and discipline. The argument is a definitional contrast: divine gift (positive triple) excludes its opposite (deilia).

### T7.3.1 — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?

**[A]** E — For M01-A (reverential fear of God as governing orientation): the most useful science framework is **psychology of religion / positive psychology of awe** (science extract Section 4: Keltner and Haidt's awe research; Otto's numinous experience operationalised in Hood's Mysticism Scale). This framework identifies awe's core features — perceived vastness and need for accommodation — and its downstream effects: self-diminishment, slowed time perception, prosocial orientation, openness to new information. These downstream effects partially overlap with what M01-A describes: the God-fearer is characterised by humility (not self-exalting), ethical other-orientation (Pro 14:2; Lev 19:14), and teachability (Pro 15:33). The framework illuminates the experiential phenomenology of M01-A; it cannot adjudicate the normativity of the posture (see T7.3.3).

**[B]** E — For M01-B (acute fear and alarm): **neuroscience — affective neuroscience** (science extract Section 1: LeDoux amygdala model, Panksepp FEAR system, Barlow fear/anxiety distinction). The amygdala-centred model of fear processing establishes fear as potentially bypassing cortical processing — a subcortical rapid-response system. This is directly relevant to M01-B's "without deliberation" pattern (1Sa 17:24: all men of Israel flee immediately). Öhman's preparedness theory (some stimuli more readily activate fear circuitry) is relevant to M01-B-VCG-01's theophanic fear — the repeated biblical pattern suggests that divine presence is a maximally preparedness-activating stimulus.

**[C]** E — For M01-C (terror-as-force) and the military-threat dimension of M01-B: **behavioural science / social psychology — stress and coercion research** (science extract Section 5: Lazarus and Folkman's stress/appraisal/coping model; Seligman's learned helplessness). The stress-appraisal model identifies perceived uncontrollability and unpredictability as primary determinants of whether a stressor produces overwhelming fear. M01-C's terror-as-force is characterised precisely by its uncontrollability (God's terror cannot be resisted, Gen 35:5) and its unpredictability (Exo 23:27: God's terror sent as an advance weapon, unexpected by enemies). Seligman's learned helplessness (Seligman's model of uncontrollable negative stimuli producing passivity) maps onto M01-C's paralysis effect.

**[D]** E — For M01-D (dismay and inner collapse): **affective neuroscience and psychophysiology** (science extract Sections 1 and 2) in its freeze-response dimension. Porges's polyvagal theory (science extract Section 2: the freeze response distinct from fight-or-flight) is the most applicable framework for M01-D's characteristic inner collapse, colour change, limb-failure, and speechlessness. These map onto the dorsal vagal shutdown response (parasympathetic freeze/collapse) rather than the sympathetic arousal of M01-B. Porges's framework makes M01-D anatomically intelligible as a distinct physiological state.

**[E]** E — For M01-E (trembling / fear made somatic): **psychophysiology** (science extract Section 2: Cannon's sympathetic nervous system description; James-Lange theory; Damasio's somatic marker theory). The somatic vocabulary of M01-E maps directly onto the psychophysiology of the fight-flight-freeze response. Damasio's somatic marker theory (bodily states as constitutive of emotional experience, not merely expressive) is directly applicable to M01-E's somatic primacy — the body's trembling is not just a symptom of inner fear but is constitutive of the fear-experience itself, consistent with what Damasio proposes.

**[F]** E — For M01-F (anticipatory dread and anxiety): **clinical psychology — anxiety research** (science extract Section 3: Beck's cognitive model; Borkovec's worry research; Barlow's unified anxiety model). Borkovec's characterisation of worry as "verbal-linguistic, repetitive, and future-oriented" is the closest scientific analogue to M01-F's de.a.gah (H1674 — the cognitive-ruminating anticipatory anxiety). Beck's appraisal-model (threat appraisal + perceived inability to cope) maps onto M01-F-VCG-01's pervasive daily anxiety and VCG-02's dread that materialises.

**[G]** E — For M01-G (timidity and cowardly shrinking): **moral philosophy** (the deilia / andreia framework in Aristotle's virtue ethics — though not in the science extract) is the nearest framework. The science extract does not specifically address cowardice as a psychological category; the nearest is the clinical anxiety research (M01-F framework) applied to situations of moral decision-making under threat. The programme notes that M01-G's evaluative-moral dimension exceeds what clinical psychology addresses.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The most useful primary framework for the cluster as a whole is **affective neuroscience** (for the acute fear, somatic trembling, and freeze-collapse dimensions — M01-B, E, D), with **clinical psychology / anxiety research** as the major secondary framework for the anticipatory dimension (M01-F). **Psychology of religion / awe research** is the most relevant framework for M01-A but is limited by its inability to adjudicate normativity.

### T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?

**[E]** E — **Somatic marker theory (Damasio)** illuminates M01-E significantly: the cluster's unusually dense somatic vocabulary — bones, flesh, hair, lips, heart, knees, hands registering fear involuntarily — is not merely expressive description but points to the bodily states as constitutive of the fear-experience itself. Damasio's position (somatic states are not merely the expression of emotions but are integral to emotional experience) makes M01-E analytically significant rather than merely illustrative. The density of somatic vocabulary in M01-E (the science extract notes it "exceeds what is typical in ancient Near Eastern emotional vocabulary compilations") is illuminated by somatic marker theory as reflecting a recognition of the body's constitutive role in fear-experience.

**[B]** E — **LeDoux's subcortical fear-processing model** illuminates M01-B-VCG-03's "without deliberation" pattern (1Sa 17:24: all men of Israel flee without deliberation): the subcortical pathway bypassing conscious appraisal is exactly what this verse describes. The science explains mechanistically why M01-B fear overrides the deliberative will in ways that M01-A reverential fear does not — M01-A operates through the prefrontal-cortical pathway (deliberate, appraised, formative); M01-B's acute register operates through the subcortical-amygdala pathway (fast, bypass, reflexive).

**[F]** E — **Borkovec's worry model** illuminates M01-F-VCG-01 and VCG-05: the characterisation of worry as verbal-linguistic, future-oriented, and repetitive maps onto the de.a.gah (H1674) anxiety terms and onto Paul's anticipatory dread (2Cor 12:20: "rooted in thought about what he may find — engaging the mind with anxious expectation"). The science makes the cognitive dimension of M01-F's anxiety more precise: it is not merely affective but involves the mind in repeated forward-projection — exactly what Borkovec describes.

**[D]** E — **Porges's polyvagal theory / freeze response** illuminates M01-D's bodily collapse pattern (Dan 5:6: color change, limb-failure, knees knocking; Job 32:15: complete speechlessness). The dorsal vagal shutdown response — characterised by immobility, colour changes (due to blood flow redistribution), muscle weakness, and loss of coordinated motor function — is the physiological mechanism that makes M01-D's somatic pattern coherent. The freeze response distinguishes M01-D's immobility-collapse from M01-B's arousal-flight.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Science illuminates the cluster primarily in three areas: (1) the somatic vocabulary of M01-E (somatic marker theory making it constitutive rather than merely expressive); (2) the subcortical-bypass mechanism of M01-B's extreme register (LeDoux's model explaining why deliberation fails under acute fear); (3) the cognitive-ruminating structure of M01-F's anxiety (Borkovec's worry model mapping onto de.a.gah). Science makes the cluster's inner-being description more anatomically and psychologically precise without replacing the theological interpretation.

### T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?

**[A]** E — The most significant divergence is M01-A's **normativity claim**: science (psychology of religion) describes the phenomenology of awe and fear before the numinous (self-diminishment, openness, prosocial orientation), but science cannot and does not adjudicate whether such fear is right, fitting, or obligatory. Scripture treats fear-of-God as **categorically right** — not adaptive or maladaptive (science's functional assessment), but obligatory and virtuous. The science extract states this explicitly: "The theological distinctive of this cluster is not that fear falls outside science, but that the object and normativity of certain fears (fear of God as right response to covenant relationship) are theological categories that science does not adjudicate." This is a fundamental divergence: science asks whether a fear is functional; Scripture asks whether a fear is fitting before God.

**[A]** E — The second divergence is the **commanded fear that is simultaneously a virtue**: science has no category equivalent. Fear of God in M01-A is simultaneously commanded (ethical obligation) and virtuous (character excellence) and formative (producing wisdom). Clinical psychology would classify any commanded emotional state as potentially pathological (fear induced by external demand). Scripture treats fear of God as the normal, healthy, ordered inner state of the image-bearer before the Creator.

**[F]** E — In M01-F, the divergence concerns the **covenantal structure of anxiety**: Deu 28:66-67 (chronic anxiety as a covenant curse) has no psychological equivalent. Clinical psychology addresses anxiety as a functional disorder arising from appraisal failures or neurobiological dysregulation. The covenant-curse form of anxiety (imposed by God as a consequence of covenant violation) is a theological category — purposively sent, not merely occurring. Science cannot address whether God sends anxiety as judgment; it can only describe the phenomenology of chronic dread.

**[G]** E — In M01-G, the divergence concerns the **moral equivalence of cowardice with idolatry and murder** (Rev 21:8). Clinical psychology treats anxiety and avoidance (which underlie cowardice) as disorders requiring treatment. Scripture treats sustained cowardice-under-pressure as a morally equivalent failure to idolatry — a categorical judgment that exceeds science's evaluative framework entirely.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The primary divergences are: (1) normativity — science cannot adjudicate the fittingness of fear of God; (2) commanded virtue — science has no category for a commanded affective disposition that is simultaneously a virtue; (3) covenantal structure — anxiety as divine judgment is outside science's framework; (4) moral-eschatological equivalence — cowardice as morally equivalent to murder is a theological category science cannot evaluate. The divergence is most pronounced at M01-A's theology-of-fear-as-virtue and M01-G's eschatological judgment of cowardice.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The science framework surfaces three modest gaps: (1) the dissociative dimension of M01-D's freeze-collapse (Porges); (2) the verbal-self-talk dimension of M01-F's anxiety (Borkovec); (3) the body-to-soul feedback direction in M01-E (Damasio).
