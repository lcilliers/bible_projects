### T3.1.1 — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?

**[A]** E — M01-A engages the perceptive faculty primarily through **sight** and **hearing** as triggers of reverential awe. Exo 14:31 (Israel witnessed God's power over Egypt and feared the Lord — sight of divine action triggers reverential fear); Psa 145:6 (God's deeds produce fear-inducing wonder in those who "speak of them" — verbal-cognitive perception); Hag 1:12 (hearing the prophetic word produces fear — auditory trigger). Also spiritual discernment: Job 1:1 (Job's fear of God is the inner moral quality that discerns rightly across all circumstances — a discernment-based orientation rather than a perception-based response).

**[B]** E — M01-B engages the perceptive faculty extensively, primarily through **sight**: 1Sa 17:24 (sight of Goliath produces extreme fear), Mat 14:26 (sight of Jesus walking on water triggers fear), Dan 5:6 (sight of the writing on the wall produces dismay/fear), Luk 2:9 (sight of the angel and divine glory fills the shepherds with fear). **Hearing** also triggers B-fear: 2Ki 19:6 (fear triggered by Assyrian threatening words), Jer 51:46 (fearfulness from successive alarming reports). Spiritual discernment: 1Sa 18:12 (Saul's fear of David "rooted in theological perception — he recognises that God's presence has transferred from himself to David").

**[C]** E — M01-C engages **sight** as the primary perceptive trigger: Dan 2:31 (the great image's appearance produces fear), Job 41:14 (Leviathan's face radiates terror outward — sight of Leviathan produces terror in beholders). Also the inner sense of **hearing**: Jer 20:10 (Jeremiah hears whispers of "terror on every side" — auditory trigger for the encircling-terror experience).

**[D]** E — M01-D engages **sight** and **hearing**: Isa 21:3 (the vision the prophet sees produces dismay so overwhelming he cannot see), Dan 7:7 (the fearful beast — its appearance produces fear as the immediate inner response to sight of its dreadful strength). The distortion of sight and hearing (cannot see, speechlessness) is the distinctive pattern of D's perceptive engagement.

**[E]** E — M01-E engages **hearing** as a primary perceptive trigger: Exo 19:16 (thunder and trumpet at Sinai produced involuntary trembling — the auditory trigger is explicit). Amos 3:6 (a trumpet blast naturally produces trembling — auditory). Job 37:1 ("Elihu's heart trembles and leaps at the **thunder** of God's voice" — auditory trigger for somatic trembling). Also sight: Eze 26:16 (trembling seizes the sea-princes at **witnessing** Tyre's fall — visual trigger for somatic trembling).

**[F]** E — M01-F engages **hearing** as a primary trigger: Jer 49:23 (anxiety seizes Damascus "on hearing bad news"); 2Cor 12:20 (Paul's anticipatory dread rooted in "thought about what he may find" — imaginative rather than actual perceptive engagement). Also **sight**: Deu 28:67 ("dread explicitly located in the heart and tied to what **the eyes see**" — visual experience driving anticipatory dread).

**[G]** S — M01-G timidity's perceptive triggers are not explicitly described in the verse evidence. The threat situations (storm, master's harshness, persecution) are implied but the specific inner-sense engagement is not named.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The perceptive faculty is engaged across all sub-groups of M01. The dominant inner senses are **sight** (primary across B, C, D, E-VCG-03, A) and **hearing** (primary in A, E-VCG-01, F). Spiritual discernment is evidenced in M01-A (Job 1:1) and M01-B (1Sa 18:12). The perceptive faculty is the trigger-gateway for fear across the cluster — what is seen or heard initiates the fear-state. This confirms that M01 is heavily perception-driven: the characteristic is routinely initiated by perceptive engagement with external reality.

### T3.1.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?

**[A]** E — M01-A reverential fear **enables and deepens** perception in the ordered direction. Pro 2:5 ("fear of the Lord is the fruit of earnest seeking after wisdom — an inner understanding arrived at through diligent pursuit") implies that fear sharpens spiritual discernment. Psa 25:14 (fear positions the person to receive God's friendship and knowledge of his covenant — covenant knowledge opened by fear-posture). Fear of God appears to enable a kind of receptive attentiveness to God's communication.

**[B, C, D]** E — Acute fear (M01-B), terror-as-force (M01-C), and dismay (M01-D) routinely **impair** the perceptive faculty: Isa 21:3 (dismay so overwhelming the prophet "cannot see"); Job 32:15 (friends dismayed into speechlessness — the verbal-perceptive loop shut down); Dan 5:6 (color change and limb-failure as perceptive-system overwhelm); Mat 14:26 (disciples mis-perceive Jesus as a ghost — fear distorting perceptive identification). M01-D-VCG-03 is the most explicit: dismay systematically shuts down perception and speech.

**[E]** E — M01-E somatic trembling — where it is the response to theophany — does not impair but rather **registers** divine reality through the body. The E-VCG-02 trembling-at-word tradition may **deepen** receptive perception: Ezr 9:4; 10:3 (those who tremble at God's word gather for covenant action — the trembling marks a community whose perceptive attentiveness to God's word is heightened).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The perceptive faculty is: **enabled/deepened** by M01-A reverential fear; **bypassed** by the involuntary character of M01-B's theophanic fear (the disciples cannot choose not to fear the risen Jesus); **impaired** by M01-D dismay (systematic shutdown) and by M01-B's distorting social-fear (Isa 8:12: social fear "distorts perception of true danger"). The pattern suggests that ordered fear (M01-A) enhances perception while disordered fear (M01-B's social register, M01-C, M01-D) impairs it.

### T3.1.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

Ordered fear (M01-A) enables a clearer perception of God and reality; disordered fear (M01-D's dismay, M01-B's social fear) systematically distorts or shuts down perception. The cluster confirms that fear is not merely an inner experience but a perceptual orientation — it determines what the person is able to register about the world.

### T3.2.1 — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A is the most cognitively engaged sub-group in the cluster. Reverential fear is the **root of knowing**: Pro 1:7 (anchor A-VCG-02: "the foundational inner disposition from which all knowledge begins"); Psa 111:10 ("fear of the Lord is the foundational inner disposition from which wisdom begins — constitutive of understanding"); Job 28:28 ("fear of the Lord is identified as wisdom itself"). Fear of God is not merely a precondition for knowledge but is identified as the inner posture that constitutes genuine understanding. It is epistemologically foundational.

**[B]** E — M01-B's acute fear engages cognition in a disruptive mode: 1Sa 17:11 (Saul and all Israel "dismayed and deeply afraid — Goliath's words produce a two-layered inner impact: shock that **disorganises thought** and fear that disables action"); Mar 9:6 ("Peter's inability to know what to say at the transfiguration is explained by the terror gripping the disciples — the fear so overwhelms their inner faculties that thought and speech are disabled").

**[D]** E — M01-D dismay is a cognitive-shutdown characteristic: Isa 21:3 (cannot see — cognitive-perceptive shutdown); Job 32:15 (friends "dismayed into complete speechlessness — the rational and verbal faculties entirely shut down"); Dan 7:28 (dismay "works through Daniel's thoughts" but Daniel resolves to keep the matter inward — a last cognitive act before the colour changes).

**[F]** E — M01-F anticipatory dread is the cognitively richest disordered sub-group. De.a.gah (H1674) as anxiety carries a cognitive-ruminating quality (Borkovec's verbal-linguistic worry model is directly applicable — see T7.3). 2Cor 12:20 (anchor F-VCG-05: "Paul's fear is an anticipatory dread rooted in **thought** about what he may find — engaging the mind with anxious expectation"). The cognitive engagement is anticipatory and future-oriented.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cognitive faculty is engaged across M01 in two opposed directions: M01-A **enables** cognition (fear-of-God as the root of knowledge), while M01-B, D, and F (in different degrees) **disrupt or distort** cognition. This is a structural finding: ordered fear (M01-A) is epistemologically constitutive; disordered fear (M01-D, M01-B's paralysing dimension) is epistemologically destructive.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern: M01-A enables/deepens cognition; M01-D impairs it most severely; M01-F distorts it toward anxious anticipation; M01-B impairs it acutely in moments of overwhelming encounter; M01-G suppresses it volitionally. The ordered fear (M01-A) and the disordered fears (B-severe, D, F, G) are cognitively opposed.

**[F]** E — M01-F engages memory in a specific way: Job 21:6 (M01-E-VCG-05: "shuddering takes hold of Job's flesh the **moment memory brings dismay**" — memory triggering somatic fear response); Deu 28:60 (M01-F-VCG-02: "Israel's former terror of Egypt's diseases is recalled — God will bring back the very afflictions they once dreaded, **exploiting that deep-seated fear**" — memory as the site of embedded anticipatory dread).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Memory is engaged across M01 in three distinct modes: (1) **formative** — fear of God is built by cumulative memory of his acts (M01-A); (2) **corrective** — memory of past divine deliverance counters present acute fear (M01-B); (3) **triggering** — memory of past fear retrieves that fear in the present (M01-F-VCG-02). Memory is not a dominant faculty engagement in M01 but it is consistently present as a mediating element.

### T3.4.1 — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty — feeling and emotional experience — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A engages the affective faculty in a complex way. Reverential fear itself is an affective state — it is a felt orientation, not merely a cognitive assessment. Fear-as-reverence is an affective state that is not mutually exclusive with positive affect.

**[B]** E — M01-B engages the affective faculty centrally — acute fear is by definition an intense affective experience. The range includes: alarm (Gen 32:7), awe-mingled-dread (Mar 4:41), social anxiety (Joh 7:13), and relief-producing fear (Psa 105:38: Egypt's terror "produced gladness at their departure"). Mat 28:8 (women departing "holding **fear and great joy simultaneously**") is the most vivid example of complex affective coexistence.

**[D]** E — M01-D engages the affective faculty through the dismay-experience: Psa 6:3 (the psalmist's soul "greatly dismayed — profound turmoil driving the anguished cry to God"). The affective content is anguish, horror, and internal upheaval.

**[F]** E — M01-F's affective content is sustained apprehension: de.a.gah (H1674) anxiety as a weighing-down burden on the heart (Pro 12:25); the anticipatory dread of M01-F-VCG-03 as unrelenting anguish that "distorts the experience of time" (Deu 28:67).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The affective faculty is the primary dimension of M01 across all sub-groups. Fear is constitutively affective. The affective range across the cluster is wide: from reverent delight (M01-A, Psa 2:11; Heb 12:28) through acute alarm (M01-B) to overwhelming anguish (M01-D, F) to horror (M01-C). The characteristic's affective diversity reflects the cluster's seven-sub-group structure.

### T3.6.1 — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A engages the volitional faculty pervasively. Reverential fear governs the will's choices across all ethical and covenant contexts: Lev 19:14 (fear preventing the will from exploiting the vulnerable — the will restrained by the inner reverential disposition); Deu 6:24 (fear as the inner posture "that produces obedience" — directly governing the will); Pro 16:6 ("fear of the Lord is an operative inner force that **turns the person away from evil**, functioning as the moral agent that drives ethical avoidance"). The will is fear-of-God's primary operating theatre.

**[B]** E — M01-B engages the volitional faculty through will-governance: Gen 32:7 (fear "immediately drives strategic action" — the will is channelled by fear); 1Sa 17:24 (all men of Israel flee "without deliberation" — fear overrides the deliberative will); Joh 19:38 (Joseph's discipleship was hidden because "fear of the Jews **governed** his public behavior"). Fear in M01-B systematically governs or overrides the will.

**[C]** E — M01-C engages the volitional faculty by paralyzing or redirecting it: Gen 35:5 (terror "paralyzing the will to pursue Jacob's sons"); Est 9:3 (dread of Mordecai "redirected the will of those in authority" — the will of officials redirected by fear of Jewish power); Deu 11:25 (dread of Israel "placed in enemies' hearts so no one can withstand them" — the will to resist neutralized by divinely-imposed dread).

**[D]** E — M01-D engages the volitional faculty by dissolving it: Isa 21:3 (dismay so complete that the prophet cannot see — the will to perceive has collapsed); Eze 7:27 ("dismay so overwhelms the people that **their hands are paralyzed**" — the will to act physically disabled); Gen 45:3 (brothers "cannot answer" — the will to speak is shut down).

**[G]** E — M01-G is definitionally volitional: timidity is a failure of the will to act faithfully under pressure. Mat 25:25 (the will choosing passive self-preservation over faithful risk); Joh 14:27 (the heart commanded not to "yield to that fearful recoiling" — the will choosing to resist or surrender to timidity); Rev 21:8 (the will choosing apostasy under sustained threat).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The volitional faculty is the most consistently engaged inner faculty across all M01 sub-groups. Fear governs, channels, overrides, paralyses, or dissolves the will across every sub-group. The cluster's unifying feature is volitional impact: all seven sub-groups describe how fear determines what the person does or fails to do. The volitional faculty is M01's primary operating theatre.

### T3.6.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person?

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity **impairs** volition by channelling it toward passive self-preservation. The will is not absent but misdirected — toward burying, hiding, and shrinking rather than action.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern: M01-A enables/deepens volition (governance toward right action); M01-B and C bypass or override volition (urgency overwhelming deliberation); M01-D dissolves volition (hands paralyzed, speech gone); M01-G impairs volition (misdirection toward passivity). Ordered fear enriches volitional life; disordered fear degrades it.

### T3.7.1 — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?

**[B]** E — M01-B's relationship to agency is complex: acute fear can either enable agency (Gen 32:7: Jacob's fear immediately drives strategic action — splitting the camp, sending gifts ahead) or disable it (1Sa 17:24: all men of Israel flee without deliberation). **[CLUSTER]** E — The agency faculty is: **enabled** by M01-A (courage to act against tyranny, prayer in the face of disaster, ethical restraint as a form of moral agency); **conditionally engaged** by M01-B (enabling or disabling depending on the fear-object and intensity); **disabled** by M01-C, D (paralysis, hands immobilized, flight); and **suppressed** by M01-G (passive self-preservation rather than initiative). **[CLUSTER]** E — Ordered fear enables agency; disordered fear suppresses or destroys it. The pattern is consistent across all sub-groups.

### T3.7.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern confirms that fear is an agency-shaping characteristic at its core: what the person fears determines what they are willing to do and what they are too afraid to do. Ordered fear (M01-A) produces the most courageous agency in the cluster — the midwives defy Pharaoh, Nehemiah builds in the face of death threats. Disordered fear produces the most paralysed or suppressed agency (M01-D hands paralysed; M01-G burying the talent). The inverse relationship between ordered fear of God and suppression of human-threat agency is a significant structural finding: the more a person genuinely fears God, the less they fear human opposition (Deu 7:21; 1Ch 22:13; Neh 4:14).

### T3.8.1 — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A is the most morally evaluative sub-group in the cluster. Fear of God provides the interior moral standard against which the person's conduct is assessed in the absence of external accountability: Lev 19:14 (fear as the moral evaluator operating where "no human court can see"); Neh 5:9 (fear of God as "the inner ethical standard that should have restrained the leaders from oppressing their own people"); 2Ch 19:9 (judicial conduct governed by fear of the Lord "paired explicitly with faithfulness and whole-heartedness"). The moral evaluation faculty is the primary channel through which M01-A operates in practical contexts.

**[A-VCG-05]** E — M01-A-VCG-05 (reorientation of fear) engages moral evaluation directly: the person must morally assess what they are fearing and judge whether the object is appropriate (God) or not (crowds, idols, human power). Isa 8:12-13 (the prophet commanded not to adopt the crowd's fear-object — a moral assessment of fear's direction is required).

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity **impairs** moral evaluation: Rev 21:8 (cowardice listed alongside idolatry and murder — timidity is a moral failure); Mat 25:25 (the servant's misjudgment of the master's character drives the wrong decision — moral evaluation distorted by fear).

**[B]** E — M01-B's social fear (B-VCG-04) **bypasses** moral evaluation: Gal 2:12 (Peter's social fear driving withdrawal "against conviction" — the person's own moral assessment is overridden by fear-of-crowd-opinion); 1Sa 15:24 (Saul confesses that "fear of the people was the inner motive behind his disobedience — human social dread displaced his fear of God and drove him to comply with the crowd" — moral evaluation suppressed by social fear).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The moral evaluation faculty is: **enabled and deepened** by M01-A (fear of God as the interior moral standard); **bypassed** by M01-B's social fear (crowd opinion overriding moral judgment); **impaired** by M01-G timidity (cowardly failure of moral discernment under pressure). Ordered fear activates and grounds moral evaluation; disordered fear bypasses or suppresses it.

### T3.8.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?

**[B, G]** E — M01-B's social fear and M01-G timidity **bypass and impair** moral evaluation. Social fear substitutes crowd opinion for moral principle (Gal 2:12; 1Sa 15:24). Timidity substitutes self-preservation for moral faithfulness (Mat 25:25; Rev 21:8).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern is the clearest moral-evaluation finding in the cluster: M01-A reverential fear is the foundation of interior moral evaluation; social fear (M01-B-VCG-04) and timidity (M01-G) are the systematic bypasses of it.

### T3.8.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern reveals that fear-of-God is the constitutive interior moral faculty in Scripture's account of the human person. Psa 36:1 (absence of fear as "fundamental moral blindness"), Rom 3:18 (absence of fear as defining godless humanity's moral condition), and the Lev 19 corpus (fear as the operative moral restraint where no court sees) all confirm that ordered fear is not merely adjacent to morality but is its interior ground. This is a significant cluster-level finding for the programme's governing question: the inner moral faculty is, at its root, fear-of-God.

### T3.9.1 — Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?

**[B]** E — M01-B engages conscience through the social-fear corpus: 1Sa 15:24 (Saul confesses that social fear displaced his conscience — the fear of people overrode the fear of God that should have governed); 2Sa 1:14 (David's rebuke frames the absence of fear as a moral failure — "the messenger lacked the reverential dread that should have prevented him from touching God's anointed, exposing a void in **his inner conscience**").

**[F]** E — M01-F-VCG-04 engages conscience through anticipatory guilt: Act 24:25 (Felix became alarmed as Paul reasoned about righteousness, self-control, and coming judgment — "moral and eschatological argument penetrated his inner being and produced a **frightened, avoidant response**" — conscience stirred by fear of judgment producing avoidance rather than repentance). **[CLUSTER]** E — The conscience is engaged across M01 in three modes: (1) **activated** by reverential fear of God (M01-A: fear as interior moral witness); (2) **suppressed** by social fear displacing it (M01-B-VCG-04: crowd-fear overriding conscience); (3) **haunted** by anticipatory judgment-dread (M01-F: the conscience as the site of anticipated divine judgment).

**[F-VCG-04]** E — Anticipatory judgment-dread (M01-F-VCG-04) **engages** conscience in its avoidant mode: Act 24:25 (Felix alarmed but avoidant — conscience stirred but not yielded to). **[CLUSTER]** E — The conscience is enabled by M01-A, bypassed by M01-B's social fear register, and haunted but not necessarily responsive in M01-F's judgment-anticipation material. Ordered fear of God is the ground of an active, functioning conscience; social fear (M01-B-VCG-04) is one of its primary suppressors.

### T3.9.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

This structural relationship means that the diagnostic of a person's inner life (is the conscience functioning or suppressed?) is closely tied to whether they fear God or fear human opinion. **[CLUSTER]** E — M01-A is the most conscientiousness-enabling sub-group. Timidity (M01-G) is the most conscientiousness-impairing. The pattern: ordered fear integrates moral awareness, volition, and action; disordered fear disintegrates them.

### T3.10.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?

**[B-VCG-04, G]** E — Social fear (M01-B-VCG-04) and timidity (M01-G) **bypass and impair** conscientiousness. Social fear displaces moral awareness with crowd-preference (Gal 2:12; 1Sa 15:24). Timidity suppresses the volitional-action dimension of the conscience-volition-action triad.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Ordered fear (M01-A) is one of the programme's clearest conscientiousness-enabling characteristics. It integrates the inner person's moral awareness, will, and action into a coherent pattern of faithful living.

### T3.10.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]** E — The pattern reveals that ordered fear of God is not an isolated inner experience but an integrating principle of the inner life. It enables the person to act consistently with their moral awareness and will across contexts — including contexts of power asymmetry (Lev 19:14: power over the deaf and blind), sustained difficulty (Neh 5:9-15), and crisis (Job 2:3: fear holding through intense suffering). Conscientiousness is one of fear-of-God's most distinctive outputs. The cluster confirms that the inner life of the God-fearer is characterised by integrated wholeness, while the inner life of those governed by social fear or timidity is characterised by fragmentation.

### T3.11.1 — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A engages the relational capacity directly and pervasively. **[CLUSTER]** E — Ordered fear (M01-A) enables relational depth; social fear (M01-B-VCG-04) impairs relational authenticity; dismay (M01-D) ruptures relational bonds; pastoral anxiety (M01-F-VCG-05) flows from and expresses genuine relational care. ### T4.1.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A shows God operating toward the human person by: (1) **commanding** fear — Deu 6:13; 10:20; Ecc 12:13 (God directly requiring the reverential posture); (2) **implanting** it — Jer 32:40 (God pledging to place fear directly in the hearts of his people as a permanent disposition); (3) **forming** it through encounter — Deu 4:10 (God intending the Horeb encounter to produce lasting fear); (4) **rewarding** it — Exo 1:21 (God rewarding the midwives' fear-of-God-based disobedience with families).

**[B]** E — M01-B shows God operating toward the human person through: (1) **angelic appearances** producing fear (Luk 1:12-13; Luk 2:9-10); (2) **Jesus's acts** producing fear (Mar 4:41; Act 5:5-11); (3) **divine commands to release fear** (Isa 43:1 anchor B-VCG-06; "do not fear" as God speaking directly to the person's fear-state); (4) **theophanic encounters** (Exo 19:16 — God's descent at Sinai producing involuntary trembling).

**[C]** E — M01-C shows God deploying terror toward enemy peoples: Exo 23:27 (anchor C-VCG-01: "God's terror is sent as an advance weapon" — God actively deploying terror into enemy inner lives); Jer 32:40 / Deu 11:25 (God placing dread of Israel in surrounding nations).

**[D]** E — M01-D shows God producing dismay as judicial action: Psa 2:5 (God's wrath producing dismay in rebel kings); Jer 49:37 (anchor D-VCG-02: "God will terrify Elam before their enemies, making dismay the inner condition through which he breaks their will — his fierce anger is the divine source").

**[F]** E — M01-F-VCG-03 shows God imposing covenantal-curse dread: Deu 28:66-67 (anchor: "continual dread without assurance of life is a covenantal curse" — God imposing the chronic dread as a covenant consequence of unfaithfulness).

**[CLUSTER]** E — God operates toward the human person across M01 in at least five distinct ways: commanding fear (M01-A), implanting it (M01-A, Jer 32:40), deploying it as weapon against enemies (M01-C), imposing it as judicial instrument against rebels (M01-D), and imposing covenantal-curse dread on the unfaithful (M01-F). God is uniquely active across the entire cluster — more so than in most other programme clusters examined to date.

### T4.1.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?

**[A]** E — M01-A shows a primarily **covenantal** basis: the fear God implants (Jer 32:40) is a covenant promise; the fear God commands is grounded in the covenant relationship (Deu 6:13-14; Mal 2:5). **[C, D]** E — M01-C and D show a **conditional-responsive** basis: God deploys terror against Israel's enemies in response to their opposition to his covenantal purposes (Exo 23:27; Deu 11:25); he dismays rebels in response to their defiance (Psa 2:5; Jer 49:37). The basis is conditional on the enemies' opposition.

**[F-VCG-03]** E — M01-F-VCG-03 (covenantal curse-dread) is explicitly **conditional** on covenant violation: Deu 28:66-67 is the direct consequence of failing to obey the terms of the covenant.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The basis of God's operation across M01 is primarily **covenantal and responsive**: God extends reverential fear-formation to covenant members (M01-A), deploys terror against covenant enemies (M01-C), and imposes dread as covenant-curse on the unfaithful (M01-F). The characteristic's operation from God toward the human person is consistently embedded in the covenant structure.

### T4.1.3 — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**[A]** E — God's command to fear him, combined with his pledge to implant that fear (Jer 32:40), reveals a disposition of **covenant commitment and pastoral care**: God is not indifferent to whether the human person has the right inner orientation — he actively works to produce it. Psa 25:14 (offering covenant friendship to those who fear him) reveals God's disposition as intimate and relational — the fear-posture opens access to God's friendship.

**[B]** E — God's repeated "do not fear" commands in M01-B-VCG-06 (Isa 43:1 anchor: "you are mine" as the ground for releasing fear) reveal a disposition of **possessive care and relational nearness**: God addresses the frightened person directly, names himself as their ground of security, and commands the release of fear from a position of personal commitment.

**[C, D]** E — God's deployment of terror against enemies (M01-C-VCG-01) and imposition of dismay as judgment (M01-D-VCG-02) reveals his disposition as **sovereignly just**: he uses fear as an instrument of historical governance, not arbitrarily but in response to opposition to his covenantal purposes.

**[CLUSTER]** E — God's disposition toward the human person as revealed through M01 is: (1) intimately relational and care-ful toward those who fear him; (2) actively formative — working to produce the right inner orientation; (3) sovereignly just — deploying fear as an instrument of historical governance. The cluster reveals God as both the object of human fear and the sovereign producer of it.

The absence of this fear (Rom 3:18; Psa 36:1) is the diagnostic of a broken relationship. ### T4.3.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?

**[F-VCG-05]** E — M01-F-VCG-05 (pastoral anxiety) shows one person extending concern toward another: Paul's anticipatory dread for his churches (2Cor 12:20; Gal 4:11) flows from his care. The anxiety is in Paul, motivated by love for others — a one-directional giving of concerned attention.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Fear is not typically "given" as a gift from one person to another. The human-to-human dimension of M01 is primarily indirect: fear of God produces just treatment of others (M01-A), the command to release fear is extended from one to another (M01-B-VCG-06), and pastoral anxiety flows from loving concern (M01-F-VCG-05).

### T4.3.2 — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?

**[B-VCG-06]** E — For the extension of "do not fear" release commands: the person who extends this must stand in a position of genuine authority or covenant assurance relative to the other (Jesus extending release from a position of divine authority; Jonathan extending it to David grounded in divine promise).

**[F-VCG-05]** E — For the pastoral anxiety that flows from love: Paul's anxiety is enabled by his genuine love for his churches (Gal 4:11 context: "I am afraid I may have labored in vain for you" — the anxiety flows from the labour of love already invested).

### T4.3.3 — What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?

**[A]** E — To transmit reverential fear through instruction (Psa 34:11), the instructor must themselves have received and been formed by the fear. Deu 31:12-13 implies a multi-generational formation chain: each generation hearing and forming the fear, then transmitting it to the next. The person cannot give what they have not received and been formed by.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The general principle confirmed by M01: genuine giving of the characteristic's effects requires genuine possession of the characteristic. The God-fearer who treats others justly does so from the interior ground of their own reverential fear. The person who releases another from fear does so from the ground of their own courageous trust. The person whose pastoral anxiety flows from love does so from the ground of genuine relational investment.

### T4.3.4 — If the evidence is silent on the giving direction, note this explicitly.

**[C, D, E, G]** S — M01-C (terror-as-force), M01-D (dismay), M01-E (trembling), and M01-G (timidity) are largely silent on the human-to-human giving direction. Terror, dismay, and trembling are experienced and not typically given. Timidity has no giving direction at all.

### T4.4.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?

**[A]** E — Reverential fear can be received through instruction: Deu 31:12-13 (the assembled community receiving the formation of fear through hearing Torah — the community is positioned to receive); 2Ki 17:28 (a priest sent to teach the settlers "how to fear the Lord" — reception through instructed formation); Psa 34:11 (the psalmist's offer to "instruct children in fear of the Lord" — explicit receptive formation context).

**[B-VCG-06]** E — The "do not fear" command is received by the person who is gripped by acute fear: Lam 3:57 (divine response to the inner cry: "God drew near and spoke 'Do not fear' — the divine response to the inner cry is direct personal reassurance, addressing fear with the intimacy of God's nearness"). The reception requires the will's openness to the divine word.

**[C-VCG-01]** E — Terror-as-weapon is "received" involuntarily by enemy peoples: Gen 35:5 (terror falling on surrounding cities — no choice in reception); Exo 23:27 (God's terror working on enemy peoples' inner being — involuntary imposition).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The receiving direction shows three modes: (1) voluntary formed reception — reverential fear received through instruction (M01-A); (2) commanded-release reception — the frightened person receiving the command that releases acute fear (M01-B-VCG-06); (3) involuntary imposition — terror received against the will of the recipients (M01-C-VCG-01).

### T4.4.2 — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?

**[A]** E — Reception of reverential fear through instruction is enabled by: openness of will (Deu 31:12-13 implies that gathering and hearing are necessary — the person must present themselves); not yet having competing allegiances (2Ki 17 corpus shows that syncretistic prior allegiances block genuine reception of fear-of-God). Reception is blocked by: divided heart (Psa 86:11), hardened heart (Isa 63:17), and prior commitment to other fear-objects (2Ki 17:33 — fearing the Lord and serving other gods simultaneously, which prevents genuine reception).

**[B-VCG-06]** E — Reception of the "do not fear" command is enabled by: trust in the one speaking (Isa 43:1 anchor: the "you are mine" ground for releasing fear requires trust in the speaker's authority), and the will's openness to release the fear (the command is addressed to the will, which must choose to comply). It is blocked by: distrust (Act 9:26: Jerusalem disciples not trusting Paul's conversion), or by fear so overwhelming that the command cannot penetrate (1Sa 4:20: the dying mother unable to receive the reassurance because grief overwhelms everything).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The enabling conditions for receiving fear-related communication (whether instructed formation or commanded release) are: will-openness, trust in the speaker, and freedom from prior competing commitments. The blocking conditions are: divided or hardened heart, distrust, competing allegiances, and overwhelming grief or dismay.

### T4.4.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?

**[A]** E — The person who encounters instruction in the fear of the Lord but does not receive it is in the state of: moral blindness (Psa 36:1; Rom 3:18), defiant self-sufficiency (Psa 55:19), or syncretistic compromise (2Ki 17:33 — fearing the Lord and other gods simultaneously, which is the non-reception of genuine fear). Jer 5:24 (people who do not say in their hearts "let us fear the Lord" — ingratitude and irreverence are one, the inner state is a closed heart toward God).

**[B-VCG-06]** E — The person who hears "do not fear" but does not receive it remains in paralysis, flight, or speech-suppression. 1Sa 4:20 (dying mother unable to receive the reassurance — grief so total that the word cannot penetrate).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Non-reception of the commanded-or-instructed forms of M01 leaves the person in the state they were in: if in acute fear, still gripped; if in moral blindness, still blind; if in divided allegiance, still divided. The characteristic must be received by the will for it to take effect — outer exposure without inner reception leaves the person unchanged.

### T4.4.4 — If the evidence is silent on the receiving direction, note this explicitly.

**[D, E, G]** S — M01-D (dismay), M01-E (trembling, except E-VCG-02's instructed dimension), and M01-G (timidity) do not have extensive evidence of the receiving-from-another direction. These sub-groups describe primarily involuntary or inner-constitution-based characteristics rather than received ones.

### T4.5.1 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?

**[A]** E — M01-A operates consistently across relational distance — fear of God applies to the person's treatment of strangers (Lev 25:36-37: not exploiting a stranger's poverty), servants (Lev 25:43: no ruthless domination over a servant who is a fellow Israelite), enemies (Deu 25:18: Amalekites' attack on the weak is evidence of absent God-fear), and foreigners (Act 10:35: "anyone who fears God and does what is right is acceptable to him regardless of nationality"). The characteristic operates across all relational distance and difference. It does not narrow to existing bonds.

**[B]** E — M01-B's social fear (B-VCG-04) operates across relational difference in the direction of crowd-management: the leaders' fear of the crowd is fear across social distance (the crowd as mass, not individuals in relationship). M01-B's self-preservation fear (B-VCG-05) also operates across relational distance — Jacob's fear of Esau (Gen 32:7-11), the Gibeonites' fear of the covenant community (Jos 9:24).

**[A-VCG-01]** E — M01-A's ethical dimension operates specifically in power-asymmetry relationships: Lev 19:14 (the vulnerable deaf and blind), Lev 25:43 (the servant). Fear of God operates precisely where power difference would otherwise enable exploitation. The characteristic does not narrow to relational equals.

**[CLUSTER]** E — M01-A operates consistently across all relational bonds and distances. The characteristic's ethical dimension specifically addresses power-asymmetric relationships where relational exploitation would otherwise be possible. This confirms that fear-of-God is not a parochial characteristic limited to in-group relationships but operates as a universal governing orientation.

### T4.5.2 — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?

**[A]** E — M01-A crosses covenantal boundaries. The Gen 20:11 material (Abraham assuming absence of God-fear in Gerar's people — implying fear-of-God is expected in non-Israelites), Act 10:35 ("anyone who fears God and does what is right is acceptable to him regardless of nationality"), and the nations-fearing-God material (Psa 102:15; Isa 25:3; Rev 14:7; 19:5) all show that reverential fear of God is not limited to covenant members. God demands and expects it of all peoples; the goal is universal fear-of-God (Mal 1:14: "God's name will evoke genuine reverent fear among all nations").

**[C, A-VCG-07]** E — M01-C and M01-A-VCG-07 show God deploying fear across covenantal boundaries as a historical governance instrument: God instilling fear of Israel in surrounding nations, God producing fear in Egyptian overseers at the Exodus. The covenantal-cross operation is explicit.

**[CLUSTER]** E — M01-A's reverential fear of God crosses covenantal boundaries and is presented as universally required of all peoples. The characteristic is not parochial. The covenant community is the primary locus of its formation, but it is directed toward and required of all.

---

### T4.5.3 — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?

**[A]** E — M01-A's scope includes: all Israel (the primary covenant community throughout the Deuteronomy and Psalms corpus), the messianic king (Isa 11:2-3), individual leaders and judges (Exo 18:21; 2Ch 19:9), foreigners who come to know God (Solomon's prayer for foreigners, 1Ki 8:43; Cornelius, Act 10:2), and ultimately all nations (Rev 14:7; 19:5). The scope is universal in aspiration. Exclusions are not based on ethnic identity but on the inner disposition: those whose hearts are hardened (Isa 63:17), whose allegiance is divided (2Ki 17:33), or who do not fear God at all (Psa 36:1) are excluded not from God's demand but from receiving the blessing that fear-of-God confers.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The relational scope of M01-A is genuinely universal: God requires reverential fear of all peoples and rewards it wherever it is found (Act 10:35). The characteristic's scope is as wide as God's sovereignty — which is total.

### T4.5.4 — If the evidence is silent on relational boundaries, note this explicitly.

**[D, E, F]** S — M01-D (dismay), M01-E (trembling), and M01-F (anticipatory dread) do not have distinct relational boundary evidence. These sub-groups describe primarily individual inner experiences rather than relationally scoped characteristics.

### T4.6.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — M01-A shows fear of God operating in the angelic realm: Psa 89:7 (God is "greatly to be feared among the holy ones in the divine council — his awesomeness surpasses all heavenly beings, making reverential fear the only fitting inner response **even in heaven**"). Fear of God is not merely a human posture but the appropriate posture of the divine council before God. Gen 22:12 (the angel of the Lord affirming Abraham's fear of God as evidenced by his willingness to sacrifice — the fear tested and confirmed through angelic mediation).

**[B]** E — M01-B shows angelic mediation of fear extensively: the angelic appearances throughout the NT that produce fear in human persons (Luk 1:12-13; Luk 2:9-10; Mat 28:4-5; Luk 24:5; Act 10:4; Rev 1:17) are the most concentrated evidence. Angels both produce acute fear and command its release ("do not be afraid" from angels across the corpus). Angels operate as the interface through which divine holiness makes itself felt in the human inner life.

**[B-VCG-01]** E — M01-B-VCG-01 (fear before supernatural encounters) shows specific instances of adversarial encounter producing fear: Dan 7:7 (the fourth beast — terrifying in appearance, producing fear — in the visionary realm); Mar 14:33 (Jesus in Gethsemane, ekthambeo — though this is the suffering Son before the Father's judgment rather than adversarial encounter). The adversarial spiritual dimension is present but not dominant in the evidenced material.

**[F-VCG-04]** E — M01-F-VCG-04's fearful expectation of judgment (Heb 10:27) operates in the context of divine judgment as the eschatological reality — a spiritual-being interface in the sense that it anticipates encountering God as judge.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Angelic beings are prominent mediators of M01-B fear (appearances producing fear, commands releasing fear). The divine council evidences reverential fear of God (Psa 89:7 — M01-A). Adversarial spiritual operations are less prominent in the evidenced material: the beasts of Daniel's visions (M01-B-VCG-01) and the general spiritual-resistance context of M01-G (Rev 21:8's apostasy-under-pressure) are the nearest instances.

### T4.6.2 — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?

**[A]** E — M01-A shows fear being displaced by false-object attachment — a pattern consistent with adversarial distortion: 2Ki 17 corpus (reverential fear of God replaced by fear of idols — idols as adversarial alternatives to the living God); Isa 8:12 (social fear being substituted for fear of God — Isaiah commanded to resist adopting the crowd's conspiratorial dread). The systematic displacement of fear-of-God by fear-of-idols or fear-of-crowd represents a pattern in which the characteristic's proper direction is corrupted.

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity (2Ti 1:7: "God has not given us a spirit of timidity") implies that the spirit of timidity has a spiritual source distinct from God. This is the nearest statement to adversarial spiritual activity against the characteristic.

**[B]** E — M01-B's social fear (B-VCG-04) and self-preservation fear can be used as tools of adversarial pressure: Neh 6:9-14 (enemies deliberately engineering fear in Nehemiah and his workers as a weapon — "fear used as a tool to corrupt his will"). The intentional use of fear-induction as a weapon against God's servants is evidenced.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The characteristic is a site of adversarial activity in at least three forms: (1) the displacement of God-directed fear by false-object fear (M01-A-VCG-05: idols, crowds, political powers); (2) the use of fear-induction as a weapon against God's servants (M01-B-VCG-03: Neh 6:9-14); (3) the spirit of timidity as a non-divine inner disposition that may have adversarial origin (M01-G, 2Ti 1:7).

### T4.6.3 — Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry in the evidence?

**[B]** E — Yes, extensively in M01-B. Angels both produce fear (Luk 2:9 anchor; Mat 28:4) and release it ("do not be afraid" — Luk 1:13; Luk 1:30; Luk 2:10; Mat 28:5; Rev 1:17). The angelic role is to mediate divine presence (producing appropriate fear) and divine reassurance (releasing inappropriate fear). Angels function as the two-directional interface between divine holiness and human inner life in the fear register.

**[E-VCG-01]** E — The theophanic trembling at Sinai (Exo 19:16 anchor, E-VCG-01) is mediated through divine presence itself rather than specifically through angels.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Angelic ministry is the primary mediation structure for M01-B's theophanic fear (producing it) and for the commanded release of that fear (commanding it away). Angels are the agents through which divine holiness crosses the threshold between the divine and the human, producing both fear and its commanded release.
