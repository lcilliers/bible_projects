### M01-A — Reverential Fear / Fear of God as Governing Orientation

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers the meanings that describe fear-of-God as an enduring inner orientation rather than a reactive event. What distinguishes these verses from the rest of the cluster is the *constitutive* character of the fear: it is a disposition that governs the whole inner life — informing conduct, motivating worship, grounding covenant relationship, and forming identity. The fear here is not triggered by a threat or shock; it is the settled posture of a person who recognises who God is and orients their inner life accordingly. Across hundreds of verses this characteristic is expressed with remarkable consistency: fear of God is the root from which obedience grows (Deu 6:2; 10:12), the criterion that qualifies for divine guidance and provision (Psa 25:12; 111:5; 34:7), the foundation from which wisdom begins (Pro 1:7; 9:10; yir.ah), the inner force that restrains cruelty and injustice (Lev 19:14; 25:17; 25:43), and the defining character quality of the righteous person (Job 1:1; 1:8; Neh 7:2). The same characteristic appears in NT texts as the motivating awe that governs obedience to Christ and to earthly authorities (fobeō: Col 3:22; Eph 5:21; 1Pe 1:17; 2:17). The fear is learnable (Deu 14:23; 31:12-13; Psa 34:11), transmissible across generations (Deu 6:2; Psa 103:17), and can be implanted by God directly (Jer 32:40). When absent, it marks a hardened will and moral blindness (Psa 36:1; Jer 5:24; 44:10; Rom 3:18).

### M01-B — Acute Fear and Alarm

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers the meanings that describe fear as a reactive, situational event — the sudden inner state of alarm, dread, or terror triggered by a specific threat, an unexpected encounter, a supernatural appearance, or the display of divine or extraordinary power. What marks these verses is the *episodic* character of the fear: it arises at a moment, overwhelms the inner person, and governs the will's immediate response. In the OT, this acute alarm appears before armies (Num 22:3 — Moab's visceral, paralyzing dread of Israel; Deu 20:1-3 — soldiers commanded not to let fear grip them before battle), before divine theophanies (Exo 34:30 — the radiance of Moses's face producing involuntary awe-driven recoil; Judg 6:23 — Gideon's terror at seeing the Lord face to face), and before inexplicable events (Gen 42:35 — brothers afraid at the inexplicable return of silver). In the NT the same pattern appears repeatedly: disciples overwhelmed by fear at Jesus walking on water (fobos, Mat 14:26; fobeō, Joh 6:19), at the transfiguration (fobeō, Mat 17:6; Luk 9:34), at the calmed storm (fobos, Mar 4:41; fobeō, Luk 8:25), and at the resurrection appearances (emfobos, Luk 24:5; 24:37; fobeō, Rev 1:17). The acute fear-alarm also encompasses social fear: the fear of crowds governing rulers' decisions (fobeō, Mat 21:26; Luk 20:19), fear of the Jews suppressing speech (fobos, Joh 7:13; 19:38; 20:19). Common across all these meanings is the *reactive and episodic* character of the fear — it is not a chronic orientation but an acute event that overwhelms the will in a specific moment.

### M01-C — Terror as Overwhelming Force

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers meanings where terror is characterised as an active, assaulting force — something that comes upon the inner person from outside, encircles them, pursues them, or is projected by one power onto another. The distinctive quality is not the sufferer's reaction but the *agency and force* of the terror itself: it hunts (bal.la.hah, Job 18:11), it encircles on every side (ma.gor, Jer 6:25; 20:10; Lam 2:22), it is sent as a weapon by God against enemies (e.mah, Exo 23:27; chit.tah, Gen 35:5; pa.chad-noun, Deu 2:25), and it is the characteristic power projected by military empires over the living (chit.tit, Eze 32:23-32). The terror here strips identity and dignity (bal.la.hah, Job 30:15), paralyses the will to resist (chit.tah, Gen 35:5; e.mah, Jos 2:9), and renders even formidable enemies motionless as stone (e.mah, Exo 15:16). In many of these verses the terror is explicitly divine in origin — God deploys dread as a weapon on behalf of his people, or as judgment against the wicked. Several verses describe this from the other side: the awe-inspiring terror-force that powerful entities project (a.yom, Song 6:4; 6:10; Hab 1:7; chit.tit and mo.rah). The terror of Leviathan (e.mah, Job 41:14) and of the war horse (e.mah, Job 39:20) illustrate the same register: terror as a quality radiating outward that overwhelms those who encounter it.

### M01-D — Dismay and Inner Collapse

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers meanings where the characteristic is the sudden dissolution of inner coherence — what happens to the inner person when shock, catastrophe, or overwhelming news shatters their composure and disables their capacity to function. The distinctive quality is *collapse*: the dismayed person cannot answer (ba.hal, Gen 45:3), cannot see (ba.hal, Isa 21:3), cannot speak (cha.tat, Job 32:15), cannot resist (ba.hal, Judg 20:41), or cannot act (cha.tat, Jer 17:18). The inner collapse is often visible on the body — color changes (be.hal, Dan 5:6; 5:9), limbs give way (be.hal, Dan 5:6), hands are paralysed (cha.tat, Eze 7:27). The dismay can be divinely imposed as judgment (ba.hal, Psa 2:5; 90:7; cha.tat, Jer 49:37), the natural response to perceiving catastrophe (ba.hal, 2Sa 4:1), or the inner effect of grasping a terrible message (be.hal, Dan 4:19; Dan 7:15). What distinguishes dismay from acute fear-alarm (M01-B) is the emphasis on *dissolution of the inner person's coherence* rather than the reactive alertness of alarm. Dismay is the state *after* fear has overwhelmed — the shattering, not just the shock.

### M01-E — Trembling / Fear Made Somatic

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers meanings where the central focus is the involuntary bodily expression of fear — the trembling, shuddering, and quivering that registers fear in the body. The distinctive quality is *somatic*: these meanings consistently describe the body trembling, bones shaking, flesh quivering, hair bristling, or knees failing as the outward sign of an inner fear state. The trembling is involuntary — it overcomes the person rather than being chosen (cha.rad, Exo 19:16 — all Israel trembles at Sinai; entromos, Act 7:32 — Moses trembles and cannot look; tromos, Mar 16:8 — the women flee trembling). In many of these meanings, the bodily trembling is the only indicator of the inner state — the person is shaken physically before they can articulate what they feel (1Sa 28:5 — Saul's trembling is located in his heart; Job 37:1 — Elihu's heart trembles at the thunder). A theologically distinctive subset carries the "trembling at God's word" register — the inner posture of solemn reverence before God's communication, where trembling is a mark of genuine responsiveness (cha.red, Isa 66:2; 66:5; Ezr 9:4; 10:3). This gives the trembling cluster a double dimension: physical terror response and reverential responsiveness to the divine word.

### M01-F — Anticipatory Dread and Anxiety

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers meanings where the characteristic is a sustained, forward-looking inner burden of dread — fear that is not triggered by an immediate present threat but by what is anticipated, imagined, or foreknown. The distinctive quality is *chronicity and anticipation*: the anxiety weighs down the heart as an ongoing burden (de.a.gah, Pro 12:25), it saturates every moment of existence so that even eating and drinking are done in dread (de.a.gah, Eze 12:18-19), it is described as unrelenting and consuming by night and day (pa.chad, Deu 28:66-67), or it is an anticipatory anguish that precedes and then corresponds to the feared event (ya.gor, Job 3:25; pa.chad, Job 3:25). The fear here is also the fear of death as a lifelong enslaving burden (fobos, Heb 2:15) and the fearful expectation of judgment lodged in the mind (foberos, Heb 10:27). It includes the conscientious dread of divine accountability that shapes pastoral conduct (fobeō, 2Cor 12:20; Gal 4:11 — Paul's anxious apprehension about his churches).

### M01-G — Timidity and Cowardly Shrinking

**Core description:**

This sub-group gathers meanings where fear is characterised as an inner failure of constitution — a cowardly, faithless recoiling that is condemned or identified as a moral and spiritual defect. The distinctive quality is *evaluative*: unlike M01-B (acute alarm, which is a natural response) or M01-F (chronic anxiety, which is a burden), timidity here is presented as something the inner person *ought not* to have — a disposition that should yield to faith, courage, or the peace Christ gives. The most pointed of these meanings is 2Ti 1:7 (deilia): timidity is explicitly named as a spirit-level disposition God has not implanted, making it a deficiency of inner constitution. Joh 14:27 (deiliaō) commands the heart not to yield to cowardly shrinking, positioning the will against such inner collapse. Rev 21:8 (deilos) places cowardice alongside idolatry and murder as vices heading the lake of fire — likely denoting apostasy under pressure. Pro 29:25 (cha.ra.dah) names "fear of man" as an inner trap that ensnares the person. These meanings are not simply about fear being present — they evaluate the fear as the opposite of genuine faith, trust, or inner integrity.

## 5. Note on Deu 32:27 (gur, mti_id=290)

The meaning for Deu 32:27 reads: "God refrains from full destruction of Israel partly out of dread of how enemies would misinterpret it; this inner apprehension about enemy pride shapes his restrained judgment." This is the divine inner life, not a human inner state. The programme's governing question is the inner workings of the human person. This verse may require a set-aside or special handling flag. CC to note for researcher.
