## T0 — Divine Image and Created Design

---

### T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A]** E — M01-A reveals that God is himself the supreme object and generator of reverential fear: his holiness, incomparable greatness, awesome acts, and sovereign authority are what produce the reverential posture in the human person. The character of God that M01-A reflects is his absolute worthiness of awe — he is not merely powerful but uniquely holy, so that fear-of-God is the only fitting inner response to who he is. The anchor Exo 15:11 names this directly: "awesome in glorious deeds, doing wonders," with the fear arising from witnessing God's incomparable holiness. The Proverbs wisdom cluster (M01-A-VCG-02, anchor Pro 1:7) reveals that the fear God generates is epistemologically constitutive — he is the ground of all genuine knowledge. The covenant dimension (M01-A-VCG-03, anchor Psa 25:14) reveals that God is a relational being who offers friendship and covenant intimacy to those whose inner posture is reverential. The Mal 2:5 material reveals that God structured an entire covenant relationship around this response.

**[B]** E — M01-B reveals God's majesty through the terror encounters produce. The repeated pattern of angelic appearances followed by "do not be afraid" (Luk 2:9-10; Luk 1:12-13; Mat 17:6-7; Rev 1:17) shows that divine presence is intrinsically overwhelming — God's holiness, mediated through angels or through Jesus's own acts, produces fear as an involuntary reflex. The Transfiguration (Mar 9:6) and the storm-calming (Mar 4:41) reveal that Jesus's character includes the same awe-inducing dimension as the OT God of Sinai. The pattern of Jesus addressing fear with self-disclosure ("it is I") reveals God's disposition toward the frightened person: he does not leave the fear unaddressed but names himself as the ground of release.

**[C]** E — M01-C reveals God as the sovereign deployer of terror as a covenantal instrument. The Exo 23:27 anchor ("God's terror is sent as an advance weapon") shows God deploying dread purposively, not arbitrarily — it is a feature of his governance of history. The Ezekiel 32 corpus (M01-C-VCG-03) shows that God alone controls the terror that nations deploy: "God himself declares he spread terror in the land of the living" (Eze 32:32), so even imperial terror is ultimately under divine authority. This reveals God as the final sovereign over all fear-inducing power.

**[D]** E — M01-D reveals God as one who both produces and commands dismay. He dismays rebels and enemies as a judicial act (Psa 2:5; Jer 49:37 anchor), but he also directly commands against dismay in his people (Jer 1:17 anchor D-VCG-04). This double agency — causing dismay in enemies, forbidding it in his servants — reveals a God who governs inner composure as part of his covenantal relationship. The Job corpus (M01-D-VCG-01, anchor Psa 6:3) shows that even God's hidden face produces dismay, revealing that divine presence is constitutive of inner stability in the human person.

**[E]** E — M01-E reveals God as the source of the trembling that creation instinctively enacts before holiness. The Sinai material (Exo 19:16 anchor, E-VCG-01) shows that God's presence produces involuntary whole-body trembling even in a community of people — the mountain itself trembles (Exo 19:18). This reveals God's holiness as a reality that the physical world registers. The E-VCG-02 corpus (anchor Isa 66:2) shows that God actively looks for a specific kind of person: one who trembles at his word, paired with humility and contrite spirit. This reveals God's character as responsive to reverential somatic gravity in the human person.

**[F]** E — M01-F reveals God as judge and as the ground of freedom from anxiety. The dread-of-judgment corpus (M01-F-VCG-04, anchor Heb 10:27) reveals God's absolute judicial authority: "fearful expectation of judgment" is a real inner experience grounded in God's actual judgment-power. The 1Jo 4:18 material (fear rooted in punishment-anticipation, expelled by perfect love) reveals that God's own character — love perfected — is the counter to fearful anticipation. The M01-F-VCG-05 pastoral anxiety material (anchor 2Cor 12:20) shows that concern for God's people, modeled by Paul, participates in God's own pastoral character.

**[G]** E — M01-G reveals God by contrast: the spirit God gives is not timidity but "power, love, and self-control" (2Ti 1:7 anchor). Timidity as inner constitutional failure reveals by negation what God's character is — generative, courageous, loving. The Rev 21:8 material, listing cowardice alongside idolatry and murder, reveals that God's character is definitively non-cowardly: he is the one who acts, sustains, and does not shrink from judgment or from love.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, fear as a human inner-being characteristic reflects and images the holiness, sovereignty, and relational depth of God. The cluster reveals that God is: (1) the supreme object of reverential fear by virtue of his holiness and incomparability (M01-A); (2) one whose presence is intrinsically overwhelming (M01-B, E); (3) sovereign over all terror as a historical instrument (M01-C); (4) the ground of inner stability whose withdrawal produces dismay (M01-D); and (5) the final judge before whom anticipatory dread is rational (M01-F). The characteristic images God not as a property God shares with the human person (God does not fear — see T0.1.2) but as the One before whom the human person rightly orients in fear.

### T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[A]** S — M01-A does not attribute reverential fear to God. The set-aside of Deu 32:27 (divine apprehension) was confirmed at Phase 7 — outside programme scope. The Fear of Isaac (Gen 31:42; 31:53, M01-A-VCG-06) uses "fear" as a divine name, signifying that God is the object of the fear, not its subject. No M01-A verse attributes God-fearing to God himself.

**[B]** S — M01-B's acute fear is not attributed to God. The Mar 14:33 material (Jesus in Gethsemane, ekthambeo) describes Jesus experiencing anguished horror at approaching suffering — this is the nearest evidence, though the term there names awe-laden distress rather than fear of threat. It is routed to M01-B-VCG-01. Its significance is noted: if authentic, it suggests acute alarm is not alien to the incarnate Son, but the evidence is a single instance of the ekthambeo term and warrants caution about over-reading.

**[C]** S — Terror as a quality is attributed to God's acts (e.g. Exo 23:27 "God's terror") but as an instrument God deploys, not a state God experiences. The characteristic is God's weapon, not God's experience.

**[D, E, F, G]** S — Dismay, trembling, anticipatory dread, and timidity are not attributed to God in any M01 verse.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Scripture does not attribute fear as an inner experience to God within M01's evidence base. The set-aside of Deu 32:27 removed the one possible instance of divine inner apprehension. Fear is consistently and exclusively a creaturely response — to divine holiness, to threat, to judgment. This silence is itself significant: it establishes fear as definitively creaturely in structure. The significance for the human person is that fear is the appropriate posture of the creature before the Creator — not a shared property between them, but the asymmetrical relational posture that marks the creature's proper position.

### T0.1.3 — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?

**[A, B, C, D, E, F, G]** E — The silence across all sub-groups on God possessing fear (apart from the single set-aside) reveals that fear belongs to the creaturely dimension of the divine image rather than to the divine nature itself. It is not a characteristic the image-bearer shares with its Source, but one that marks the proper orientation of the image-bearer toward its Source. The significance: fear-of-God in the human person is the inner structural acknowledgment of the asymmetry between Creator and creature. It is the inner posture that orients the person rightly within that asymmetry. The M01-A material (Pro 1:7; Psa 111:10; Ecc 12:13) confirms this: fear is the foundational inner disposition from which all right knowing and living begin — not because God fears, but because the human person, made in God's image, exists in a constitutive relation of creaturely dependence that fear truthfully registers.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The silence on God possessing fear is consistent and total across M01's 941 verses. This establishes fear as a creaturally-distinctive characteristic — part of the divine image not as a reflection of a divine quality but as the creaturely response to the divine reality. The cluster's evidence suggests that the created purpose of fear is to orient the whole inner person rightly before God, enabling wisdom (M01-A-VCG-02), ethical restraint (M01-A-VCG-01), covenant relationship (M01-A-VCG-03), and ultimately worship (M01-B-VCG-02).

### T0.2.1 — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?

**[A]** E — In M01-A the created purpose of reverential fear is comprehensive: it equips the person to know rightly (Pro 1:7 anchor: "foundational inner disposition from which all knowledge begins"), act ethically (Lev 19:14 anchor: the operative moral restraint in situations of power over the vulnerable), receive divine guidance (Psa 25:14 anchor: reverential fear "positions a person for intimate covenant relationship"), and sustain life in covenant relationship (Deu 6:2; Ecc 12:13: "the whole duty of man"). Fear equips the person to be wise, just, covenant-faithful, and relationally aligned with God.

**[B]** E — M01-B's acute fear serves a situational purpose: it produces immediate inner responsiveness to genuinely dangerous or genuinely divine realities. Gen 32:7 (anchor B-VCG-05, Jacob's fear driving strategic action) shows fear equipping the person for responsive decision-making. The "do not fear" pattern (B-VCG-06, anchor Isa 43:1) implies that acute fear's created purpose is not suppression but redirection — the person is equipped to release fear when its object is mastered by God.

**[C]** E — M01-C's terror-as-force serves the purpose of establishing divine authority over human history. The Exo 23:27 material shows God's terror equipping Israel to occupy the land not through military strength but through a divine inner state in their enemies. The created purpose is to make human history legible as a domain governed by God, where resistance to his purposes collapses from within.

**[D]** E — M01-D's dismay serves a created purpose as an inner signal of genuine catastrophe — it is the inner collapse that accurately registers the breaking of what cannot hold. The D-VCG-04 material (Jer 1:17 anchor) shows that dismay is something a person of God can resist when God has spoken — suggesting its created purpose is honest registration of threat, checked by divine word.

**[E]** E — M01-E's trembling serves the double created purpose of: (1) registering divine holiness somatically (Exo 19:16 anchor: "whole-body fear response to divine presence") and (2) marking genuine reverence for God's word (Isa 66:2 anchor: "the trembling is located in the spirit and shows reverence that orients the whole inner person toward God's communication"). The person who trembles at God's word is specifically identified as the one God looks for (Isa 66:2). Trembling equips the person to receive divine communication with appropriate gravity.

**[F]** E — M01-F's anticipatory dread serves the purpose of honest forward-looking engagement with consequences. Job 3:25 (anchor F-VCG-02) shows dread that corresponded accurately to what actually came — the dread was not irrational. The covenantal-curse form of dread (Deu 28:66-67, anchor F-VCG-03) shows fear serving as a covenant-consequence indicator. The pastoral anxiety material (2Cor 12:20 anchor F-VCG-05) shows anticipatory dread serving responsible concern for others.

**[G]** E — M01-G's evidence defines the purpose of fear by negating timidity: the created purpose of the inner constitution is courage, faith, and trust — not cowardly shrinking. 2Ti 1:7 (anchor) specifies the spirit God gives: "power, love, and self-control." Timidity's absence equips the person to act faithfully under pressure (Mat 25:25; Mat 8:26).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, fear as a created capacity serves the following purposes: (1) orienting the person toward God as the supreme reality worthy of awe (M01-A); (2) enabling accurate inner registration of genuine threat and genuine holiness (M01-B); (3) establishing divine sovereignty over the inner states of peoples and nations (M01-C); (4) providing honest signal of genuine collapse (M01-D); (5) registering divine holiness in the body (M01-E); (6) enabling forward-looking moral responsibility (M01-F); and (7) establishing faith and courage as the created positive (M01-G). The capacity for fear is integral to the created inner life.

### T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A]** E — M01-A's reverential fear of God is presented as an original created purpose. Ecc 12:13 ("fear God, keep his commandments: the whole duty of man") presents this as constitutive of the human person without reference to the fall. The Deu 14:23 material ("cultivate an enduring inner reverence") and the Jer 32:40 material (God "pledges to implant the fear of himself") both treat it as a design feature capable of formation. The wisdom root (Pro 1:7) grounds knowledge itself in fear-of-God — this is not a fallen-condition repair but a created epistemological structure.

**[B]** E — M01-B's acute fear engages both dimensions. Gen 3:10 (B-VCG-05: Adam's shame-triggered dread of divine presence) is explicitly post-fall: "I was afraid because I was naked." The fear of divine presence in naked shame is a fallen-condition response. However, the acute-fear capacity itself — the rapid inner response to threat — serves created purposes (Jacob's strategic response to Esau's approach, Gen 32:7) and cannot be attributed solely to the fall.

**[C]** E — M01-C's terror-as-force (God sending terror as a weapon) operates within fallen human history as a covenantal instrument. No M01-C verse suggests this dimension existed before the fall; the Exo 23:27 material presupposes the conquest context, which presupposes human sin and displacement. M01-C is primarily a fallen-condition instrument of divine governance.

**[D]** E — M01-D's dismay similarly operates in fallen-condition contexts: catastrophe, divine judgment, enemy threat, the hiding of God's face as covenant consequence. No M01-D evidence suggests dismay as an original creation design feature; it appears to be a response to the broken condition of the world and human sin.

**[E]** E — M01-E's trembling spans both. The theophanic trembling at Sinai (Exo 19:16 anchor, E-VCG-01) and the "trembling at God's word" tradition (Isa 66:2 anchor, E-VCG-02) suggest that somatic reverence before God's presence and word is a created appropriate response — not fallen-condition damage. The fear-trembling of military crisis (E-VCG-04) operates in the fallen world but may engage a created capacity.

**[F]** E — M01-F's chronic anxiety and dread are presented in primarily fallen-condition terms. Deu 28:66-67 (anchor F-VCG-03) names chronic unrelenting dread as a covenant curse — it is punishment, not design. Heb 2:15 (fear of death holding people in "lifelong inner slavery") names the fear of death as a condition Christ delivers from — implying it is a fallen state, not a created one. The pastoral anxiety of M01-F-VCG-05 (2Cor 12:20 anchor) is more ambiguous — concern for others is creational; the form it takes as anxiety may be fallen-condition.

**[G]** E — M01-G's timidity is explicitly not from God (2Ti 1:7): "God has not given us a spirit of timidity." This implies it is a condition of the fallen inner constitution, not original design. The Rev 21:8 judgment on cowardice confirms it as a failure state, not a created purpose.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The evidence across M01 supports a differentiated answer: (a) The capacity for reverential fear of God (M01-A) and for somatic reverence before holiness (M01-E-VCG-01, E-VCG-02) appear to be original created design features. (b) Acute situational fear (M01-B) engages a created capacity but is shaped by fallen-condition contexts including shame-triggered dread of God (Gen 3:10). (c) Terror as a divine weapon (M01-C), dismay as inner collapse (M01-D), chronic anxiety as curse (M01-F), and timidity as constitutional failure (M01-G) are primarily fallen-condition phenomena.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster as a whole points toward a future fullness that includes: perpetuated, deepened reverential fear of God (M01-A, confirmed by Rev corpus); release from all fear of threat, judgment, and death (M01-B, F, G); and the complete absence of terror-as-domination and trembling-as-threat (M01-C, D, E). The fullness is not the elimination of fear but its purification: what remains is the fear that is rightly placed — fear of God as the organizing inner posture of the fully redeemed person.

### T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

The absence of fear marks a person whose inner orientation is fundamentally disordered.

**[G]** E — M01-G is the most direct: timidity (deilia) is named as "not from God" (2Ti 1:7 anchor) — it reveals a spirit-level deficiency in the person, a departure from the inner constitution God gives. Rev 21:8 (cowardice listed alongside idolatry and murder) shows that persistent timidity-as-apostasy reveals a catastrophic failure of the inner life, not merely a temperamental weakness.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The condition of the divine image is most clearly diagnosed through M01-A (presence or absence of reverential fear), M01-G (presence or absence of courage over timidity), and M01-A-VCG-05 (whether fear is rightly directed toward God or displaced onto false objects). The image is intact when fear-of-God governs the inner person; it is disordered when fear is absent (Psa 36:1; Rom 3:18), misplaced (2Ki 17 corpus), or replaced by timidity under pressure (2Ti 1:7; Rev 21:8).

**[B]** E — M01-B has christological typological use. The Transfiguration fear (Mar 9:6; Mat 17:6-7; Luk 9:34) points beyond the immediate encounter to Jesus's divine identity — the disciples' prostration is the typological enactment of creaturely response to the incarnate Son's divine nature. **[C]** E — M01-C has covenantal-typological use in the conquest narratives: God's terror-weapon (Exo 23:27 anchor; Deu 2:25; Deu 11:25) functions typologically as a sign that Israel's possession of the land is by divine gift, not human might. The Ezekiel 32 corpus (nations' terror stripped in Sheol) participates in the larger eschatological reality of divine sovereignty over all human power.

**[E]** E — M01-E's "trembling at God's word" tradition (Isa 66:2 anchor, E-VCG-02) participates typologically in the new-covenant promise: those who tremble at God's word are the ones God vindicates (Isa 66:5). This points toward a community defined by somatic reverence before God's communication — a typological anticipation of the Spirit's work producing this posture in the new-covenant community (Ezr 9:4; 10:3 as the community of those who tremble at God's word).

**[G]** E — M01-G has christological typological use: Jesus's own "do not yield to cowardly shrinking" command (Joh 14:27 anchor) is set against the peace he gives — which is itself christological gift. The Rev 21:8 condemnation of cowardice participates in the eschatological separation of the faithful from the apostate.

**[D, F]** S — No explicit typological use identified in M01-D or M01-F beyond the general eschatological framing of judgment.

**[CLUSTER]** E — M01 has typological deployment in at least four registers: (1) the Levi-covenant fear as a type of new-covenant implanted fear (M01-A); (2) the conquest-terror as a type of divine sovereignty over human history (M01-C); (3) the messianic king who bears fear-of-God as Spirit-gift (M01-A-VCG-02, Isa 11:2-3); and (4) the christological encounters of the NT where theophanic fear identifies Jesus as the divine Son (M01-B).

**[C]** E — In M01-C's conquest typology, the direction is from divine act (God's terror-weapon) → confirming divine ownership of history. The human instance (terror in Israel's enemies) points to the divine sovereignty established over all powers.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Where typology is present in M01, the dominant direction is from human instance upward to eschatological or divine confirmation. The exception is M01-A's Isa 11:2-3, where the divine gift of fear-of-the-Lord to the messianic king establishes a pattern — divine instance → human fullness.

### T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[A]** E — M01-A is called "Reverential Fear / Fear of God as Governing Orientation." The name signals three things: (1) "reverential" — the fear is morally and relationally appropriate, not merely visceral; (2) "fear of God" — the fear is directed, relational, and object-specific; (3) "governing orientation" — the fear is not an episodic event but a sustained inner disposition that governs the whole person. The name accurately captures the dominant characteristic of the sub-group's 364 verses: a settled, life-shaping reverence toward God.

**[B]** E — M01-B is called "Acute Fear and Alarm." "Acute" signals the episodic, situationally-triggered character — the fear arises at a moment and addresses a specific stimulus. "Alarm" adds the reactive, involuntary dimension. The name distinguishes B sharply from A: where A is chronic and orienting, B is acute and responsive.

**[C]** E — M01-C is called "Terror as Overwhelming Force." The name signals the distinctive structural feature: the terror is not primarily a state of the human inner person but a force acting upon or through the inner person. It has agency, direction, and overwhelming intensity.

**[D]** E — M01-D is called "Dismay and Inner Collapse." "Dismay" signals sudden disruption of inner coherence; "inner collapse" signals the disintegration of the functional capacity to act, think, or speak. The name points to the end-state of the characteristic, not its trigger.

**[E]** E — M01-E is called "Trembling / Fear Made Somatic." The compound name signals both the physical register (trembling) and its inner ground (fear). "Made somatic" captures the direction: inner fear expressing through the body, not bodily sensation generating the fear-emotion.

**[F]** E — M01-F is called "Anticipatory Dread and Anxiety." "Anticipatory" is the defining modifier — the fear precedes its object rather than responding to a present threat. "Dread" signals intensity; "anxiety" signals the more diffuse, chronic form. Both dimensions are evidenced in the VCG corpus.

**[G]** E — M01-G is called "Timidity and Cowardly Shrinking." "Timidity" signals a disposition-level weakness; "cowardly shrinking" names the evaluative dimension — this is not merely fear being present but fear evaluated as a moral-volitional failure. The name carries the evaluative charge of the sub-group's evidence.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster is called "Fear, Dread and Terror" — a three-part name that signals the full semantic range from measured inner orientation (fear) through intensified anticipatory pressure (dread) to overwhelming assault (terror). The name correctly implies that the cluster spans a spectrum rather than a single uniform experience.

### T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?

**[A]** E — The primary M01-A terms reveal at definitional level: (1) *ya.re* (H3372H, H3372G, H3373 — the core fear root, verb) — fear as a relational inner state that governs conduct; (2) *yir.ah* (H3374 — nominal form) — fear as a possession, a stable inner disposition; (3) *pa.chad* (H6343 — dread/awe) — fear with intensity and weight; (4) *mo.rah/mo.ra* (H4172A, H4172B — fear-inspiring object) — the quality of inspiring awe; (5) *fobeō* (G5399) / *fobos* (G5401) — the Greek equivalents carrying the same relational-fear range; (6) *deos* (G6015, Heb 12:28) — reverence as worshipful respect. The root *ya.re* is a stative verb, which signals that the characteristic is a stable inner state, not merely a momentary reaction.

**[B]** E — M01-B introduces: *de.chal* (H1763 Aramaic — fear/dread), *za.chal* (H2119B — to shrink in fear), *ya.gor* (H3016 — to dread, fear specific objects), *ekthambeo* (G1568 — to be amazed/alarmed), *ekthambos* (G1569), *ekfobos* (G1630 — terrified), *emfobos* (G1719 — afraid, alarmed), *ptoeō* (G4422 — to terrify/frighten), *ptoēsis* (G4423), *pturomai* (G4426 — to be startled/alarmed). These Greek terms are compounds (ek-, em- prefixes) that intensify the fear — they signal terror or alarm at an extreme level rather than measured reverential posture.

**[C]** E — M01-C's primary terms are all nouns — *e.mah* (H0367 — terror, dread), *bal.la.hah* (H1091 — sudden terror, consternation), *chit.tah* (H2847 — terror that paralyzes), *chit.tit* (H2851 — the terror projected by military power), *ma.gor* (H4032 — encircling terror), *me.chit.tah* (H4288 — crushing terror/ruin). The noun-dominance signals that M01-C's characteristic is less about the inner state and more about the terror as an entity with its own character and agency.

**[D]** E — M01-D's primary terms: *ba.hal* (H0926 — to dismay, alarm, agitate, rush), *be.hal* (H0927 Aramaic — to alarm, frighten), *cha.tat* (H2865 — to be dismayed, shattered, broken), *ze.va.ah* (H2113 — trembling horror), *re.tet* (H7374 — trembling panic), *tim.ma.hon* (H8541 — bewilderment, confusion). *ba.hal* uniquely captures both the shock-state and the rash-urgency that follows (evidenced in the dual character of M01-D-VCG-01 and VCG-05).

**[E]** E — M01-E's primary terms are all motion/state terms: *cha.rad* (H2729 — to tremble, quake), *cha.red* (H2730 — trembling as posture), *cha.ra.dah* (H2731 — trembling noun), *a.rats* (H6206 — to be in dread, tremble), *pal.la.tsut* (H6427 — shuddering, horror), *ra.gaz* (H7264 — to tremble, quake), *ra.ad* (H7460/H7461A — to tremble), *re.a.dah* (H7461B), *sa.ar* (H8175A, H8178A — to shudder, bristle), *entromos* (G1790 — trembling), *tromos* (G5156 — trembling). The term proliferation signals that M01-E's territory is highly differentiated — Hebrew and Greek both attend to specific kinds and registers of somatic trembling.

**[F]** E — M01-F's primary terms: *de.a.gah* (H1674 — anxiety, worry), *ya.gor* (H3025 — to fear, dread anticipatorily), *me.go.rah* (H4034 — fear, terror of what may come), *me.gu.rah* (H4035 — fear harbored, anticipated), *pa.chad* (H6342 — dread, with a pa.chad noun form), *foberos* (G5398 — fearful, dreadful). The *de.a.gah* root signals the cognitive-worrying dimension (cf. Borkovec's verbal-linguistic anxiety); the *ya.gor* root signals the dread of what has not yet occurred.

**[G]** E — M01-G's primary terms: *deilia* (G1167 — timidity, cowardice), *deiliaō* (G1168 — to be timid), *deilos* (G1169 — cowardly). These are evaluative terms — they carry moral weight, not merely descriptive content. The Greek *deilos* was a technical term in Greek ethics for the coward, the person who fails under pressure.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The Hebrew vocabulary is anchored in the *ya.re* root (the broadest fear term, spanning M01-A through B and beyond) and the *pa.chad* family (dread with intensity). The Greek vocabulary is anchored in *fobeō/fobos* (the primary NT fear terms, spanning M01-A through G) with specialized intensifiers for extreme and somatic registers. The term range confirms that "fear" in Scripture is a multi-register characteristic, not a single experience.

### T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?

**[A]** E — The name "Reverential Fear / Fear of God as Governing Orientation" carries all three implications: (1) directional — the fear is directed toward God, not diffuse or undirected; (2) relational — it is the inner posture within a specific God-human relationship; (3) constitutional — "governing orientation" signals that this is a stable constitutional disposition, not a passing event. The name orients the enquiry toward the person's relational standing before God as the primary question.

**[B]** E — "Acute Fear and Alarm" implies a directional dimension (fear of a specific triggering object or event) and a constitutional implication (the inner constitution is responsive to genuine threat). The relational dimension is present but secondary: the fear is relational in that it responds to a person, encounter, or display of power.

**[C]** E — "Terror as Overwhelming Force" carries a directional implication: the force comes from outside and acts upon the inner person. This orients the enquiry toward the source and agency of the terror rather than the inner state per se.

**[D]** E — "Dismay and Inner Collapse" carries constitutional implications: the inner person is not holding together but dissolving. The name orients the enquiry toward the structural integrity of the inner person under stress.

**[E]** E — "Trembling / Fear Made Somatic" carries a directional implication (from inner to body) and a constitutional implication (the somatic register of the inner life). The name orients the enquiry toward the body-soul interface.

**[F]** E — "Anticipatory Dread and Anxiety" carries a strong directional implication (forward-looking in time) and a relational implication (the dread has an object — something feared). The name orients the enquiry toward the temporal structure of fear, not just its intensity.

**[G]** E — "Timidity and Cowardly Shrinking" carries a moral-evaluative implication from the outset: this is fear being named as a failure. The constitutional implication (the inner constitution should not be like this) orients the enquiry toward the norm from which timidity departs.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, the sub-group names collectively orient the enquiry from the outset toward: object-direction (what the fear is of), temporal structure (present-responsive vs. future-oriented), constitutional depth (orientation vs. event vs. collapse), and moral evaluation (appropriate vs. failed). These four orienting dimensions are the cluster's analytical framework.

### T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?

**[A]** E — M01-A is a disposition — a stable, sustained inner orientation that governs conduct, cognition, and relationship without being triggered afresh by specific events. Pro 1:7 ("foundational inner disposition from which all knowledge begins"), Deu 14:23 ("cultivate an enduring inner reverence"), Jer 32:40 (God implants it as a "permanent inner disposition preventing them from turning away") all confirm the dispositional character. The nominal Hebrew forms (*yir.ah*, *pa.chad* as "Fear of Isaac") confirm this — fear-of-God functions as a noun, a possession, a stable feature of the person.

**[B]** E — M01-B is a condition — an inner state that arises in response to a specific stimulus and passes when the stimulus is resolved or addressed. Gen 32:7 (fear arising acutely, driving strategic action) and the "do not fear" corpus (fear being commanded away after it arises) both confirm that B is an episodic condition, not a stable disposition. It is more like an acute emotional event than a character trait.

**[C]** E — M01-C is a quality attributed to entities (terror is a quality of God's acts, of Leviathan, of the war horse, of the Babylonians) and a condition imposed on recipients (enemies are struck by terror, it falls upon them). It has dual nature: a quality in the source, a condition in the recipient.

**[D]** E — M01-D is a condition — the state of inner collapse that follows overwhelming shock, news, or judgment. It is not a disposition (not stable) and not primarily an act (not chosen). It is something that happens to the inner person.

**[E]** E — M01-E is primarily a condition (the body's involuntary trembling in response to fear or reverence) with one sub-group (E-VCG-02, "trembling at God's word") that approaches a disposition — the cha.red terms (Isa 66:2; Ezr 9:4; 10:3) describe a habitual, qualitative inner posture of reverence.

**[F]** E — M01-F is a condition with dispositional features. The chronic pervasive anxiety (Eze 12:18-19, anchor F-VCG-01) and the covenantal-curse dread (Deu 28:66-67, anchor F-VCG-03) are conditions — states the person is in. The pastoral anxiety (2Cor 12:20 anchor, F-VCG-05) and the fear-of-judgment anticipation (Heb 10:27, anchor F-VCG-04) approach a disposition — a settled forward-looking inner state.

**[G]** E — M01-G is a disposition that constitutes a failure — timidity (deilia, 2Ti 1:7 anchor) is named as a spirit-level disposition, a settled inner quality of the person rather than a momentary event. The evaluative character of G confirms this: it is a character trait (or its failure), not an episode.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster spans the full phenomenological range: stable disposition (M01-A, G), acute condition (M01-B, D), attributed quality (M01-C), somatic condition with dispositional subset (M01-E), and condition with dispositional features (M01-F). The cluster's complexity is intrinsic to its scope.

### T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?

**[A]** E — M01-A is structurally complex. Its constituent elements include: (a) the cognitive element — recognizing God's incomparable greatness (Pro 1:7; Psa 111:10); (b) the volitional element — choosing to maintain the reverential posture (Deu 14:23; Pro 23:17: "directed to continue in it all day"); (c) the ethical element — the fear expressing in ethical restraint (Lev 19:14; Neh 5:15); (d) the relational element — the fear positioning the person for covenant intimacy (Psa 25:14); (e) the formative element — the fear being learned, transmitted, and implanted (Deu 31:12-13; Jer 32:40). These five constituent elements are all evidenced in M01-A.

**[B]** E — M01-B has constituent elements in that its 275 verses span six distinct VCG phenomena (theophany fear, Jesus-miracle fear, military threat fear, social fear, self-preservation fear, commanded-release fear). But within any single VCG, the acute fear appears structurally simpler: it arises involuntarily, grips the will, and resolves.

**[C, D]** E — M01-C and D are relatively simple in structure — terror-as-force has the constituent elements of source (external) and recipient (inner person), but this is structural rather than constituent in the sense of M01-A. M01-D's dismay appears simple: disruption → collapse.

**[E]** E — M01-E is complex in having two structurally distinct kinds of trembling: involuntary-terror trembling (E-VCG-01, 03, 04, 05) and reverential-sobriety trembling (E-VCG-02, 06). These are phenomenologically distinct.

**[F]** E — M01-F has constituent elements: anticipatory cognition (the forward-looking temporal structure), affective intensity (the dread), and the somatic expression (anxiety saturating daily acts, Eze 12:18).

**[G]** E — M01-G is structurally simple: timidity is a single dispositional failure, the opposite of faithful courage.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster is structurally complex, with seven phenomenologically distinct sub-groups, each containing between one and nine constituent VCG phenomena. The cluster is not a simple single structure but a multi-dimensional characteristic with coherent unity across its diversity.

### T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?

**[A]** E — M01-A: Reverential fear of God is a dispositional inner orientation, primarily located in the heart and will, that aligns the person's cognitive, ethical, and relational life toward God as supreme reality. It engages the cognitive faculty (orienting the mind toward genuine knowledge, Pro 1:7), the volitional faculty (governing conduct and allegiance), the moral evaluation faculty (providing inner ethical restraint in situations without external accountability, Lev 19:14), and the relational capacity (positioning the person for covenant intimacy, Psa 25:14). Its impact on the person is comprehensive: it produces wisdom, ethical restraint, relational alignment with God, and community identity among those who share it.

**[B]** E — M01-B: Acute fear is an episodic inner condition, primarily operating through the perceptive faculty (triggered by sight of threat or supernatural encounter), that immediately engages the volitional faculty (governing the will's response through fight, flight, paralysis, or submission). Constitutional location is primarily in the heart (Psa 27:3: "heart will not fear"), the inner person generally, and the body as the fear's expressive vehicle. Its impact on the person is immediate and directive: it governs what the person does at the moment of encounter, and (in the "do not fear" corpus) it is the thing the divine word addresses, releases, and redirects.

**[C]** E — M01-C: Terror as overwhelming force is a condition imposed on the inner person from an external source (divine, military, or natural), primarily engaging the volitional faculty by paralysing, melting, or redirecting the will. It is not primarily located in a constitutional level but operates as an overwhelming force whose impact on the person is dis-agentive: it removes the person's effective will to resist.

**[D]** E — M01-D: Dismay is an acute inner collapse, primarily located in the soul (Psa 6:3 anchor: "the psalmist's soul itself is greatly dismayed") and the heart (Dan 5:10: "dismay originates in the inner life of thought and then visibly destabilizes the body"), engaging the cognitive faculty (shutting down perception and speech) and the volitional faculty (paralyzing action). Its impact is dissolution of inner coherence — the person cannot function normally in thought, speech, or action.

**[E]** E — M01-E: Trembling as fear made somatic is a condition located primarily in the body as the expression of an inner state, operating as the body's involuntary register of fear or reverence. The E-VCG-02 subset (trembling at God's word) engages the spirit-level (Isa 66:2: "the trembling is located in the spirit") and orients toward God's communication as its object. The broader trembling vocabulary engages the body's fear-response systems directly. Impact: the inner fear becomes visibly manifest, either as a warning signal of genuine threat or as a marker of genuine reverence.

**[F]** E — M01-F: Anticipatory dread and anxiety is a sustained inner condition, primarily located in the heart (Deu 28:67: "dread is explicitly located in the heart"; Pro 12:25: "anxiety weighs down a man's heart") and the cognitive faculty (de.a.gah: worry as ruminating future-oriented thought). It engages the volitional faculty by generating avoidance or flight behaviors (Deu 28:67: "constantly wishes the present moment away") and the affective faculty through pervasive emotional weight. Its impact is temporal distortion and the colonisation of ordinary activities by dread.

**[G]** E — M01-G: Timidity is a dispositional inner failure, located at the spirit level (2Ti 1:7 anchor: "spirit-level disposition that God has not implanted"), primarily engaging the volitional faculty by suppressing the capacity to act faithfully under pressure. Its impact is paralysis, passive self-preservation, and ultimately apostasy under sustained threat.

**[CLUSTER]** E — M01 as a whole: Fear is a multi-register inner characteristic that spans stable disposition (A, G), acute condition (B, D), imposed force (C), somatic expression (E), and chronic burden (F). Its primary constitutional homes are the heart (dominant across B, D, F), the will (primary operative faculty across all sub-groups), and the body (E). Its cognitive dimension is strongest in A and F; its somatic dimension is strongest in E; its moral-evaluative dimension is strongest in A and G. The cluster's unity is the common thread of the inner person's registering of power — whether divine, threatening, or overwhelming — and the volitional impact that registration produces.

### T1.3.1 — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?

**[A]** E — In M01-A, the structural opposite of reverential fear of God is: (1) the absence of fear as moral blindness (Psa 36:1: "no dread of God before their eyes — a fundamental moral blindness"; Rom 3:18); (2) misplaced fear — fear directed toward other objects (2Ki 17 corpus; Isa 8:12-13: "do not fear what they fear"); and (3) hollow fear — external form without inner reality (Isa 29:13: "hearts far from him while lips perform reverence"). The positive opposite is trust/confidence (Psa 14:26: "fear of the Lord functions as an inner foundation of strong confidence"), which fear-of-God produces rather than opposing.

**[B]** E — In M01-B, the structural opposite of acute fear is faith/trust: Mar 5:36 ("do not fear, only believe"); Mar 4:40 (timidity equated with no-faith); Luk 8:50 ("do not fear, only believe"). Also divine presence: "I am with you" (Isa 43:1; Isa 41:10) is presented as the structural counter to fear. Courage in M01-G overlaps here.

**[C]** E — In M01-C, the structural opposite of terror-as-force is the melting away of the force itself — the eschatological stripping of terror in Sheol (Eze 32:23 corpus) and the divine removal of all terror-power from enemies (Deu 11:25 material).

**[D]** E — In M01-D, the structural opposite is composure — specifically, the inner steadiness that the divine word produces (Jer 1:17: courage commanded as the opposite of dismay-before-opponents). Job 39:22 (war horse "laughs at fear and is not dismayed") defines courage as the complete absence of dismay.

**[E]** E — In M01-E, the structural opposite of trembling as fear-response is the covenant security in which no one makes one afraid (Lev 26:6; Eze 34:28; Mic 4:4 anchor E-VCG-04). The structural opposite of trembling-as-reverence (E-VCG-02) is the hardened heart that does not respond to God's word.

**[F]** E — In M01-F, the structural opposite is: (1) trust/rest — Isa 12:2 ("trust in God displaces dread from the inner person"); (2) wisdom — Pro 1:33 ("the one who listens to wisdom lives without dread of disaster"); (3) perfect love — 1Jo 4:18 ("perfect love expels fear entirely"). Dread and wisdom/love/trust occupy mutually exclusive inner space.

**[G]** E — In M01-G, the structural opposite is courage — specifically, the spirit of power, love, and self-control (2Ti 1:7 anchor). Faith (Mar 4:40) and the peace Christ gives (Joh 14:27) are also named as structural opposites.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, the structural opposites of fear in its various forms are: faith/trust (opposite of acute fear and timidity), wisdom (opposite of anxious dread), perfect love (opposite of punishment-anticipatory fear), divine presence (the ground that removes fear in all sub-groups), and courage (the volitional opposite of timidity). The most consistent structural opposite across the whole cluster is trust in God as the inner posture that displaces, absorbs, or reorients the person's fear.

### T1.3.2 — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?

**[A]** E — M01-A explicitly excludes: (1) pride and self-reliance (Pro 3:7: fear-of-God is "set against self-reliant pride"); (2) exploitation of the vulnerable (Lev 25:43: fear of God "prohibits ruthless domination"); (3) misplaced reverence toward false gods (2Ki 17 corpus; Judg 6:10); (4) moral blindness (Psa 36:1: absence of fear allows transgression to speak freely). It also excludes the panic-fear that would disable action (Deu 20:3: "soldier commanded not to let fear grip them before battle" — reverential fear of God displaces the panic-fear of human enemies).

**[B]** E — M01-B in its commanded-release form (B-VCG-06) explicitly resists the paralysis and flight that un-countered acute fear would produce. The "do not fear" corpus (Isa 43:1 anchor through the full corpus) shows the characteristic being contested and resisted.

**[D]** E — M01-D's commanded-against form (D-VCG-04, Jer 1:17 anchor) explicitly excludes prophetic and pastoral capitulation — dismay "must not grip" the prophet because it produces paralysis before opponents.

**[F]** E — M01-F, in its covenantal-curse form (Deu 28:66-67 anchor), excludes rest and settled trust — it is the experience of having no secure inner resting point.

**[G]** E — M01-G explicitly excludes faithful stewardship (Mat 25:25: timidity produces burying rather than investing) and honest witness (Rev 21:8: cowardice in the context of apostasy under pressure).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, the characteristic in its ordered forms excludes: self-reliance, exploitation, moral blindness, and misplaced reverence. In its disordered or acute forms, fear excludes: rest, trust, courageous action, and faithful witness. The ordered fear (M01-A) and the disordered fear (M01-B excessive; M01-F, G) exclude different things — this asymmetry is a structural finding.

### T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[A]** E — M01-A reverential fear is not: (1) mere external compliance (Isa 29:13: "lips perform reverence but hearts far from him" — this is not genuine fear); (2) servile dread of punishment (Rom 8:15: "the Spirit of adoption displaces the spirit of slavery and its associated fear" — the fear that remains is not servile but filial); (3) ordinary acute emotional alarm (Exo 20:20: Moses distinguishes "panic-fear — do not fear" from the God-given reverential fear that remains as moral restraint). The boundary between M01-A and M01-B is directness and disposition vs. episode.

**[B]** E — M01-B acute fear is not: (1) the reverential orientation of M01-A (it is episodic, not dispositional); (2) the shattering collapse of M01-D (it is alarmed but still responsive; dismay is the state after fear has overwhelmed); (3) the chronic burden of M01-F (it is triggered by present threat, not future anticipation).

**[D]** E — M01-D dismay is not M01-B fear: the sub-group description explicitly notes "dismay is the state after fear has overwhelmed — the shattering, not just the shock." Dismay involves the dissolution of capacity to function; acute fear may preserve the person's responsive capacity.

**[E]** E — M01-E trembling is not identical to M01-D collapse. Trembling is the somatic expression of an inner state; dismay is the dissolution of inner coherence. The body trembles while the person may still function (Ezr 10:9 anchor: trembling from two sources simultaneously but still making a covenant decision). Collapse in D involves functional dissolution; trembling in E may coexist with continued action.

**[F]** E — M01-F anxiety is not M01-D dismay. Anxiety is forward-looking and sustained; dismay is immediate and reactive. Anxiety anticipates; dismay registers what has arrived.

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity is not M01-B acute fear. Acute fear is an involuntary episodic condition; timidity is a dispositional failure. The evidence distinguishes them at Mat 8:26 / Mar 4:40 where the disciples' storm fear is called *deilos* (timidity, evaluative) rather than merely *fobeō* (acute alarm): Jesus evaluates the inner disposition, not just the momentary state.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster ends where: (1) other characteristics begin — grief (M03), guilt/shame (M10), anger (M02) all share adjacency with fear but are analytically distinct; (2) the characteristic becomes purely external — the cluster's set-aside pattern consistently removed uses where the term named physical terrain or cosmic events rather than inner states. The inner-being scope is the cluster's boundary.

### T1.4.1 — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?

**[A]** E — M01-A operates in at least five distinct ways: (1) as moral restraint — substituting for external accountability where no human observer is present (Lev 19:14; Lev 25:43); (2) as epistemic foundation — ordering the cognitive life toward genuine knowledge (Pro 1:7; Pro 9:10; Psa 111:10); (3) as relational posture — positioning the person for covenant intimacy with God (Psa 25:14; Psa 103:11-13); (4) as identity marker — defining who the person is within a covenant community (Job 1:1; 1Ki 18:3; Neh 7:2); (5) as moral compass — redirecting the will away from evil and toward wisdom's path (Pro 8:13: "fear of God is the inner hatred of evil"; Pro 16:6; Pro 14:27).

**[B]** E — M01-B operates as: (1) a will-governing impulse — driving flight, deception, or submission in response to threat (Gen 32:7 anchor; Gen 3:10; 1Sa 17:24); (2) a social moderator — shaping what is said and done in public through fear of crowd opinion (Mat 21:26 anchor; Joh 7:13; Gal 2:12); (3) an awe-register — producing worship-adjacent responses to divine or miraculous power (Mar 4:41 anchor; Act 2:43).

**[C]** E — M01-C operates as: (1) a divine weapon — actively deployed by God to paralyse enemy resistance (Exo 23:27 anchor); (2) an encircling force — surrounding and pursuing the wicked with no escape (Job 18:11 anchor); (3) a quality projected by powerful entities that radiates outward (Hab 1:7 anchor; Song 6:4); (4) an instrument of imperial domination — the defining power of military empires over their subjects (Eze 32:23 anchor).

**[D]** E — M01-D operates as: (1) a response to catastrophic news — collapsing confidence when the assumed order shatters (Psa 6:3 anchor; Gen 45:3); (2) a divine judicial instrument — God imposing inner collapse on rebels (Psa 2:5; Jer 49:37 anchor); (3) a bodily-manifest collapse — the inner shattering becoming visible on the body (Dan 5:6 anchor); (4) a rash agitation — the ba.hal register of impulsive urgency (Pro 28:22 anchor, D-VCG-05).

**[E]** E — M01-E operates as: (1) involuntary theophanic trembling — the body's response to divine presence (Exo 19:16 anchor); (2) reverential somatic sobriety before God's word (Isa 66:2 anchor); (3) body-register of witnessed catastrophe (Eze 26:16 anchor); (4) military panic trembling — spreading contagiously through groups (1Sa 14:15 anchor); (5) visceral shuddering of flesh (Job 21:6 anchor); (6) NT somatic gravity in worship and ministry (Phili 2:12 anchor).

**[F]** E — M01-F operates as: (1) pervasive daily anxiety — saturating ordinary activities (Eze 12:18 anchor); (2) anticipatory dread that materializes — the feared event arriving (Job 3:25 anchor); (3) covenantal curse-dread — chronic unrelenting anxiety as divine punishment (Deu 28:66 anchor); (4) dread of divine judgment — anticipatory fear of condemnation (Heb 10:27 anchor); (5) pastoral anxiety — protective forward-looking concern for others (2Cor 12:20 anchor).

**[G]** E — M01-G operates as: (1) spirit-level constitutional deficiency — a settled inner posture of cowardly shrinking (2Ti 1:7 anchor); (2) will-suppression — paralysing faithful action (Mat 25:25; Luk 19:21); (3) apostasy-under-pressure — the social/political dimension of yielding to threat (Rev 21:8).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, fear operates in at least ten distinct modes: as moral restraint, epistemic foundation, relational posture, identity marker, moral compass (M01-A); as will-governing impulse, social moderator, and awe-register (M01-B); as weapon, encircling force, and projected quality (M01-C); as catastrophe-response, judicial instrument, and bodily collapse (M01-D); as theophanic trembling, reverential sobriety, and panic-contagion (M01-E); as chronic anxiety, anticipatory dread, and pastoral concern (M01-F); and as constitutional failure under pressure (M01-G). The volitional faculty is engaged across every mode.

### T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A]** E — M01-A operates differently depending on: (1) context — in judicial settings it becomes ethical restraint (Lev 19:14; 2Ch 19:7-9); in wisdom settings it becomes the epistemic root (Pro 1:7); in worship settings it becomes the posture of approach (Psa 5:7; Heb 12:28); in communal identity settings it becomes the marker of the covenant community (Mal 3:16; Act 10:2); (2) direction — when directed toward God it is reverential fear; when directed toward false gods it becomes idolatrous misplacement (2Ki 17 corpus); (3) constitutional level — the Jer 32:40 material (fear implanted in the heart as permanent disposition) and the 2Ti 1:7 material (spirit-level deficit for G) suggest that M01-A operates at the heart-level primarily, with the spirit-level the depth from which it flows.

**[B]** E — M01-B operates differently depending on: (1) the object — fear of God's power (B-VCG-01, B-VCG-02) produces worship-adjacent awe; fear of human threat (B-VCG-03) produces self-preservation responses; fear of social consequence (B-VCG-04) governs speech; (2) the ground of release — fear is released differently depending on whether the ground is divine presence (Isa 43:1), divine promise (Isa 41:10), or human reassurance (Gen 43:23).

**[C]** E — M01-C operates differently depending on source: divinely deployed terror (C-VCG-01) has purposive character; naturally radiating terror from powerful entities (C-VCG-05) is an intrinsic quality; encircling terror (C-VCG-02) is relentless and inescapable.

**[D]** E — M01-D operates differently in: (1) the rash-agitation form (D-VCG-05 ba.hal in Ecclesiastes) where it produces impulsive action; (2) the shattering-collapse form (D-VCG-01, D-VCG-03) where it produces paralysis; (3) the divine-judicial form (D-VCG-02) where it is imposed.

**[E]** E — M01-E's two sub-group types (theophanic/military trembling vs. reverential trembling-at-word) operate from different constitutional levels: the theophanic trembling is involuntary and primarily somatic-driven; the trembling-at-word (E-VCG-02, Isa 66:2 anchor) is located in the spirit and engages volitional reverence.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Context, direction, and constitutional level all differentiate how fear operates across M01. The most significant contextual variable is the object of fear (God vs. human threat vs. divine judgment). The most significant directional variable is whether the fear is oriented toward God or displaced onto false objects. The constitutional level varies from spirit (2Ti 1:7; Isa 66:2) to soul (Psa 6:3) to heart (dominant across the cluster) to body (E-VCG corpus).

### T1.4.3 — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?

**[A]** E — M01-A has a significant speech-based dimension. Fear of God shapes testimony: Psa 66:16 ("those who fear God are qualified to hear testimony"), Psa 118:4 ("their inner reverence becomes the source of public proclamation"), Mal 3:16 (God records those who fear his name as they speak to one another). The 1Pe 3:15 material (reverence/fobos as the "inner manner in which believers are to give reasoned account of their hope") shows fear-of-God shaping even apologetic speech.

**[B]** E — M01-B has a prominent speech-inhibiting mode: social fear (B-VCG-04) governs what people will say publicly — Joh 7:13 ("fear of the Jews suppresses open speech"), Gal 2:12 (Peter's social fear drives withdrawal from table fellowship), Mar 9:32 (disciples afraid to ask Jesus). Also 2Sa 3:11 (Ish-bosheth's fear shutting down his capacity to speak entirely). Fear silences.

**[C]** S — M01-C terror does not have a primary communicative mode. It is imposed, not communicated. The one possible exception is the "terror on every side" (ma.gor) formula (Jer 20:10) used by enemies against Jeremiah — speech as a terror-weapon — but this is marginal.

**[D]** E — M01-D has a speech-collapse mode: Job 32:15 (friends "dismayed into complete speechlessness"), Gen 45:3 (brothers "cannot answer" Joseph), Job 23:15 (standing in God's presence produces an "overpowering inner upheaval" that collapses speech). Dismay systematically shuts down verbal capacity.

**[E]** S — M01-E trembling does not have a primary communicative mode, though the E-VCG-02 tradition (trembling at God's word) involves receiving communication rather than generating it.

**[F]** E — M01-F anxiety has some speech-shaping effects: Pro 12:25 ("anxiety weighs down a man's heart — relieved only by an encouraging word") implies that speech is one mechanism through which anxiety is addressed. The pastoral anxiety material (2Cor 12:20; 2Cor 11:3) involves Paul's communicative concern for his churches.

**[G]** E — M01-G has a speech-suppression mode analogous to B: Mat 25:25 (servant's fear producing hiding rather than investing) and the Rev 21:8 apostasy context both involve the failure to testify under pressure.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Fear has a consistent speech-shaping effect across M01: reverential fear shapes testimony (M01-A), acute fear and timidity suppress honest speech (M01-B, G), dismay collapses verbal capacity (M01-D). The pattern is that fear governs what can and cannot be said — ordered fear (M01-A) enables testimony; disordered fear (M01-B's social register, M01-G) silences it.

### T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

**[A]** E — The immediate inner response to fear-of-God in M01-A is obedience and worship. Hag 1:12 (anchor A-VCG-04 context: "after hearing the prophetic word, the remnant obeyed and the people feared the Lord") shows fear issuing immediately in obedience. Exo 14:31 ("feared the Lord and believed") shows fear immediately producing faith. Mat 9:8 ("the crowds' fear moves inward immediately into glorifying God") shows fear as gateway to worship.

**[B]** E — The most immediate response to acute fear in M01-B is the will's impulse toward flight or protection: Gen 32:7 (fear immediately drives strategic action — splitting the camp), Gen 3:10 (Adam's fear immediately drives him to hide), 1Sa 17:24 (all men of Israel flee "without deliberation, driven by overwhelming dread"). The immediacy is extreme — fear produces action before deliberation.

**[C]** E — The immediate response to M01-C terror is paralysis or melting: Gen 35:5 (terror "paralyzing the will to pursue"), Exo 15:16 ("rendering enemies motionless as stone"), Jos 2:9 ("causes the people to melt inwardly, dissolving capacity to resist"). The will collapses immediately.

**[D]** E — The immediate response to M01-D dismay is inner speechlessness or cognitive shutdown: Gen 45:3 (brothers "cannot answer"), Isa 21:3 ("cannot see"), Job 32:15 (friends in "complete speechlessness"). The cognitive and verbal faculties are the first to fail.

**[E]** E — The immediate response to M01-E terror-trembling is involuntary bodily trembling that precedes conscious processing: Exo 19:16 (the trembling is the direct response to the overwhelming sensory phenomena at Sinai — there is no gap between encounter and trembling).

**[F]** E — M01-F anxiety does not have a single "immediate first response" — it is a sustained condition. But when anticipatory dread materializes (F-VCG-02, Job 3:25), the immediate experience is the correspondence between feared event and actual event.

**[G]** E — The immediate response to the situation that provokes timidity (M01-G) is passive inaction: Mat 25:25 (servant buries the talent rather than acts), Joh 14:27 (heart yields to cowardly shrinking). Timidity's first response is the suppression of action.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, fear's most immediate inner-being responses are: flight/action (M01-B), paralysis (M01-C), speechlessness (M01-D), involuntary trembling (M01-E), and passive inaction (M01-G). In the ordered register (M01-A), the immediate response is obedience and worship. The immediate responses confirm that fear's primary operating theatre is the volitional faculty — it governs what the person does or fails to do in the first instance.

### T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

**[A]** E — The immediate response in M01-A varies by context: in ethical contexts the response is restraint from wrongdoing (Lev 19:14); in wisdom contexts it is the opening of the cognitive faculty to knowledge (Pro 1:7); in worship contexts it is approach to God (Psa 5:7; Heb 12:28); in the corporate miracle context it is glorifying God (Mat 9:8). There is variation, but all responses are characterised by alignment with God's reality rather than resistance to it.

**[B]** E — M01-B's immediate response varies significantly: theophany fear produces prostration (Mat 17:6; Luk 24:5); Jesus-miracle fear produces wonder and questioning (Mar 4:41); military threat fear produces flight (Gen 32:7; 1Sa 17:24); social fear produces speech-suppression (Joh 7:13). The variation is a function of the object of fear.

**[C]** S — M01-C terror is consistently paralyzing or melting in its immediate effect — less variation than M01-B.

**[D]** E — M01-D varies between speechlessness (Job 32:15; Gen 45:3) and violent agitation (Dan 11:44: "alarming news converting inward alarm into violent external action"). The ba.hal root spans both responses.

**[CLUSTER]** E — The immediate response is not consistent across the cluster — it varies by object, sub-group, and context. This variation is itself a finding: fear is a highly context-sensitive characteristic whose immediate expression is governed by what triggered it and toward what it is directed.

### T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A]** E — M01-A reverential fear produces over time: (1) wisdom — Pro 2:5 ("fear of God is the fruit of earnest seeking after wisdom — an inner understanding arrived at through diligent pursuit"); (2) life — Pro 10:27 ("fear of the Lord extends life"); Pro 19:23 ("fear of the Lord leads to life and produces a restful inner satisfaction"); (3) confidence — Pro 14:26 ("fear of the Lord functions as an inner foundation of strong confidence, not anxiety"); (4) ethical character — the person who sustains this fear develops trustworthiness, integrity, and justice (Exo 18:21; 2Ch 19:9); (5) community identity — the God-fearers form a recognizable community (Mal 3:16; Act 10:2; Ecc 8:12-13).

**[B]** E — M01-B acute fear in its ongoing form (where dread persists) produces avoidance behaviors and sustained enmity: 1Sa 18:29 ("Saul's fear of David intensifies and becomes permanent — it is the inner root of his sustained enmity"). The commanded-release pattern (B-VCG-06) aims to prevent the sustained negative effect of unresolved fear.

**[F]** E — M01-F-VCG-03 shows covenantal-curse dread producing over time: temporal distortion ("the sufferer constantly wishes the present moment away," Deu 28:67), insomnia, and the total colonization of daily life by dread (Eze 12:18-19 anchor).

**[G]** E — M01-G timidity sustained produces apostasy (Rev 21:8: the coward is listed among those consigned to the lake of fire in a context suggesting sustained failure under pressure) and the paralysis of gifts and callings (2Ti 1:7).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Over time, fear in its ordered form (M01-A) produces wisdom, life, confidence, ethical character, and community. In its disordered forms (M01-F, G), fear produces: temporal distortion, life-colonization by dread, and apostasy. The sustained effect of fear depends entirely on whether it is ordered (directed toward God) or disordered (directed toward threat, punishment, or human opinion).

### T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?

**[A]** E — Sustained possession of M01-A reverential fear establishes: the capacity for genuine wisdom (Pro 1:7; 9:10); ethical reliability in positions of power (Exo 18:21; Neh 5:15); the relational posture that receives covenant love (Psa 103:11-17); multigenerational identity ("fear of the Lord" as the characteristic from which God's steadfast love runs across generations, Psa 103:17); and the inner integration that fear of God requires — Psa 86:11 ("fear of God requires inner integration; a divided heart is an obstacle to genuine reverence").

**[B]** E — Sustained acute fear (unresolved) establishes permanent inner states of dread (1Sa 18:29: Saul's fear becoming permanent inner root of enmity) and speech-suppression (Joh 20:19: disciples' fear causing them to lock their doors). Sustained exposure to fear as the commanded-release corpus produces: trust (Psa 56:3-4), courage (1Ch 22:13; 28:20), and the capacity for mission (Mat 28:10).

**[F]** E — Sustained M01-F dread establishes: the "spirit of slavery" (Rom 8:15 secondary: the spirit of slavery associated with fear, displaced by adoption) and lifelong inner bondage (Heb 2:15: "fear of death held people in lifelong inner slavery").

**[G]** E — Sustained M01-G timidity establishes failure of stewardship (Mat 25:25-26: the buried talent) and ultimately the full apostasy pattern of Rev 21:8.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Sustained ordered fear (M01-A) establishes wisdom, integrity, and covenant identity. Sustained disordered fear (M01-F, G) establishes inner bondage, paralysis, and apostasy. The sustained effects confirm that fear is a formative characteristic — what the person fears over time shapes who the person becomes.

### T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?

**[A]** E — In M01-A, the sustained effect (wisdom, life, confidence, identity) differs significantly from the immediate response (obedience, worship, restraint from wrong). The immediate response is situational; the sustained effect is constitutive. This confirms that M01-A is dispositional — it works cumulatively and formatively, not merely episodically.

**[B]** E — In M01-B, the sustained effect (permanent enmity, doors locked, speech suppressed) differs from the immediate response (flight, prostration, silence). The immediate response is situational; the sustained effect represents the calcification of the fear into a relational structure. Unresolved acute fear becomes a settled inner state — approaching the dispositional character of M01-F.

**[G]** E — In M01-G, the sustained effect (apostasy, failed stewardship) differs from the immediate response (inaction, burying) in scale and consequence. What begins as a momentary failure of nerve accumulates into a settled disposition of cowardly shrinking and ultimately full apostasy.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across M01, the sustained effect consistently differs from the immediate response in the dimension of scale and constitutional depth. The immediate response is situational; the sustained effect is formative. This is a significant cluster-level finding: fear is not merely an episodic experience but a formative characteristic whose sustained operation shapes the inner person over time.

### T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**[A]** E — In M01-A, genuine reverential fear is enabled by: (1) an undivided heart — Psa 86:11 ("fear of God requires inner integration; a divided heart is an obstacle"); (2) humility — Pro 15:33 ("humility naturally precedes honor" in the context of fear-based wisdom formation); Pro 22:4 (fear paired with humility as the inner disposition whose reward is concrete blessing); (3) hearing God's word — Deu 31:12-13 (fear is formed through regular hearing of the law); (4) divine implantation — Jer 32:40 (God himself pledges to implant the fear, suggesting that ultimately human reception is enabled by divine gift).

**[B]** E — M01-B acute fear's "reception" is involuntary — it is not received but struck. However, the commanded-release corpus (B-VCG-06) implies that for fear to be genuinely released, the person must have a receptive will — Isa 43:1's "do not fear" presupposes a will capable of receiving and acting on the divine word.

**[E]** E — M01-E-VCG-02's trembling-at-God's-word is enabled by a spirit of humility and contrition (Isa 66:2 anchor: trembling paired with "humility and a contrite spirit as the inner posture God looks for"). The trembling is not manufactured but flows from the contrite spirit.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Genuine reception of ordered fear (M01-A, E-VCG-02) is enabled by: humility, an undivided heart, openness to God's word, and ultimately divine gift (Jer 32:40). These are enabling conditions, not sufficient causes — the characteristic flows from the person's receptive posture before God.

### T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[A]** E — In M01-A, reception is blocked by: (1) a hardened heart — Isa 63:17 ("God himself has hardened their heart so that they do not fear him"); (2) a divided heart — Psa 86:11; (3) false-object attachment — 2Ki 17 corpus (fear misplaced on other gods blocks genuine fear-of-God); (4) external compliance without inner reality — Isa 29:13 ("hollow fear — learned external formula with no inner reality"); (5) pride and self-reliance — Pro 3:7 (fear-of-God set against self-reliant pride); (6) accumulated sinful patterns — Jer 3:8 ("Judah did not fear despite witnessing Israel's divorce-judgment — a will hardened against the corrective lesson before her").

**[B]** E — M01-B's disordering of acute fear is blocked or distorted by: prior experience producing distrust (Act 9:26: Jerusalem disciples' fear of Saul based on his history), misidentification (Mat 14:26: disciples misidentify Jesus as a ghost, generating fear), and the crowd's conspiratorial social fear that displaces genuine fear-of-God (Isa 8:12: "inner disposition of communal dread that distorts perception of true danger").

**[G]** E — M01-G's ordered courage is blocked by: insufficient investment in the spirit of power, love, and self-control (2Ti 1:7 implies that the blocking condition is the failure to stir up the gift God has given — 2Ti 1:6 context).

**[CLUSTER]** E — Reception of ordered fear (M01-A) is blocked by hardened hearts, divided allegiance, pride, accumulated sin, and the substitution of external compliance for inner reality. These are consistent with the programme's finding across other M-clusters that the heart's integrity is the primary enabling/blocking factor for inner-being characteristics.

### T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A]** E — The person who encounters the stimulus for reverential fear but does not receive it is in a state of moral blindness (Psa 36:1: "no dread of God before their eyes — fundamental moral blindness that allows transgression to speak freely in the heart"); defiant self-sufficiency (Psa 55:19: "not fearing God marks an inner disposition of defiant self-sufficiency"); and ultimately apostasy or judgment-bound hardness (Jer 5:22-24: "the people do not say in their hearts 'let us fear the Lord'" — ingratitude and irreverence are one; the absence of fear in the heart is the root of covenant failure). The NT is direct: Rom 3:18 ("absence of fear of God in their inner orientation — indicates that godless humanity lacks the reverential awareness that should govern all thought and conduct").

**[B]** E — The person who encounters acute fear's stimulus and is commanded "do not fear" but does not receive the release remains in the grip of paralysis, silence, or flight. The commanded-release corpus (B-VCG-06) assumes the person's ongoing struggle with the fear until the divine word penetrates.

**[G]** E — The person who encounters the call to courage but remains in timidity is in the state described by Rev 21:8: cowardice as a defining inner orientation, heading toward apostasy.

**[CLUSTER]** E — Non-reception of reverential fear (M01-A) produces moral blindness and defiant self-sufficiency. Non-reception of commanded-fear-release (M01-B's "do not fear") produces paralysis. Non-reception of courage-over-timidity (M01-G) produces apostasy. The common feature: non-reception leaves the inner person structurally misaligned — either toward wrong objects of fear or toward no genuine alignment at all.

### T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?

**[A]** E — M01-A's primary dimension is **affective-relational** — the characteristic is the inner feeling-posture that orients the person in relationship toward God. However, it is unusual in that it is equally **volitional** (governing conduct through constraint and redirection) and **cognitive** (being the foundation of genuine knowledge). This multi-dimensionality is a finding rather than a classification problem: M01-A genuinely inhabits the intersection of affect, volition, and cognition.

**[B]** E — M01-B's primary dimension is **affective** — it is an acute emotional experience triggered by an external stimulus. The secondary dimension is **volitional** — the acute fear immediately governs the will's response.

**[C]** E — M01-C's primary dimension is **force** or **dynamic** — terror as a quality/power that acts upon the inner person from outside. If the dimension vocabulary does not include a "force" category, the nearest is **affective** (the terror produces a felt state in the recipient) with **volitional** as secondary (paralyzing the will).

**[D]** E — M01-D's primary dimension is **affective** (the inner experience of dismay) with **cognitive** as a significant secondary (the dismay shuts down cognition — speechlessness, inability to see, inability to think). The bodily collapse dimension (D-VCG-03) adds a **somatic** secondary.

**[E]** E — M01-E's primary dimension is **somatic** — the characteristic is definitionally the body's expression of inner fear. The E-VCG-02 trembling-at-word subset adds a **spirit-level** and **volitional-reverential** secondary dimension.

**[F]** E — M01-F's primary dimension is **affective-temporal** — the characteristic is the inner experience of sustained anticipatory dread. The **cognitive** dimension is significant (de.a.gah as ruminating, forward-oriented thought). The **volitional** dimension is secondary (anxiety driving avoidance or flight).

**[G]** E — M01-G's primary dimension is **volitional-constitutional** — the characteristic is a failure of the will's constitutive courage, operating at the spirit level (2Ti 1:7).

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's primary dimension is **affective** across most sub-groups, with **volitional** as the most consistent secondary dimension across all sub-groups. M01-E is unique in its **somatic** primary; M01-G unique in its **volitional-constitutional** primary. The cluster confirms that fear is primarily a felt inner state (affective) with immediate volitional consequences.

### T1.8.2 — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?

**[A]** E — M01-A's affective-relational classification is supported by the consistent nominal forms (*yir.ah* as a possessed quality, "Fear of Isaac" as a name, Mal 2:5 as a covenant defined by fear-as-inner-posture) and by the relational framing (Psa 25:14: "positioned for intimate covenant relationship"). **[B]** E — M01-B's affective primary is supported by the consistent involuntary character of the acute fear (Gen 3:10; 1Sa 17:24; Luk 2:9). The volitional secondary is supported by the systematic descriptions of fear governing what the person does immediately after it strikes.

**[E]** E — M01-E's somatic primary is supported by the entire vocabulary corpus: cha.rad, cha.ra.dah, ra.gaz, ra.ad, pal.la.tsut, sa.ar, rog.zah, re.tet, tromos, entromos — all name bodily states. The sub-group's 98 verses consistently describe physical manifestation as the characteristic's primary mode.

**[G]** E — M01-G's volitional-constitutional primary is supported by 2Ti 1:7 ("spirit-level disposition") and by the consistent description of timidity as will-suppression (Mat 25:25: "paralyzed his will and produced passive self-preservation").

**[CLUSTER]** E — The affective classification is supported by the overwhelming dominance of experiential-state language across the cluster's 941 verses: fear is consistently described as something that fills the person, grips them, falls upon them, overwhelms them — the language of involuntary affective experience.

### T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?

**[A]** E — M01-A's secondary dimensions — **volitional** (governing conduct) and **cognitive** (grounding knowledge) — do not compete with the affective-relational primary but integrate into it. **[CLUSTER]** E — The secondary dimensions do not compete with but specify the primary across most sub-groups. The one genuine tension is M01-E-VCG-02, where the somatic primary and spirit-level secondary describe genuinely different originating levels for what is formally the same characteristic (trembling).
