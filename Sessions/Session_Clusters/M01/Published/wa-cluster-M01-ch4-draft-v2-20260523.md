## 4. Where each characteristic lives in the person

The characteristics that make up this study are not scattered uniformly across the inner life. Each lives in particular places, engages particular inner capacities, and moves in particular directions between the depths of the person and the surface of action and expression. This chapter maps that inner topography — where each characteristic sits in the person, which capacities it calls into action, and what the location reveals about the characteristic's nature.

### 4.1 Reverential Fear / Fear of God as Governing Orientation

The primary constitutional home of reverential fear is the heart. The evidence for this is extensive and runs across every form of the characteristic. Deuteronomy 5:29 names the location directly: God longs for Israel to have a heart always disposed to fear him — this reverence is located in the heart, seen as the seat of enduring moral orientation. Jeremiah 5:24 shows the same location in its failure mode: the people do not say in their hearts "let us fear the Lord." Jeremiah 32:40 makes the heart the site of divine implantation: God pledges to place the fear directly into the hearts of his people, preventing them from turning away. The heart is not one possible location among several — it is the governing constitutional site from which reverential fear operates.

What the heart-location reveals is equally clear. The heart in the Old Testament is the seat of the whole integrating person — the place of willing, knowing, feeling, and moral self-direction. Reverential fear engages all of these, but with its primary grip on the willing and moral-awareness functions. Deuteronomy 5:29 shows the heart as the seat of volitional disposition — the fear "always disposed" is a matter of the will's settled posture. Jeremiah 5:24 shows the heart as the seat of moral self-direction: the decision to fear or not to fear is made there. Psalm 86:11 adds another dimension: "a divided heart is an obstacle to genuine reverence" — the heart must be unified in its knowing and willing before authentic fear can take root.

Reverential fear is not located at the spirit level in any explicit verse. The closest approach is Jeremiah 32:40 (implantation in the heart) and Isaiah 11:2–3 (the fear-of-the-Lord as a Spirit-given endowment resting on the messianic king) — but in neither case is the fear itself described as located in the human spirit. The silence is notable: across more than 360 verses, reverential fear of God is not described as a spirit-level phenomenon. It is heart-level. Soul-level location is similarly absent; the heart dominates.

The inner capacities this characteristic engages are unusually broad. The cognitive faculty is engaged most distinctively. "The fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge; fools despise wisdom and instruction" (Pro 1:7) — fear-of-God is not merely a precondition for knowledge but is identified as the inner disposition that constitutes genuine understanding. Psalm 111:10 makes the same claim; Job 28:28 goes further, identifying fear of the Lord with wisdom itself. The implication is that cognition without this inner orientation is structurally deficient — not just lacking information but lacking the ordering principle that makes knowledge genuine.

The perceptive faculty is also engaged. Sight and hearing trigger reverential fear when they register God's acts: Exodus 14:31 (sight of divine power producing fear), Haggai 1:12 (hearing the prophetic word producing fear). The fear then sharpens perception — Psalm 25:14 ("The friendship of the Lord is for those who fear him, and he makes known to them his covenant") shows fear enabling a receptive attentiveness to God's covenant communication that is not available to those without it.

The volitional faculty is engaged pervasively. Leviticus 19:14 — "You shall not curse the deaf or put a stumbling block before the blind, but you shall fear your God: I am the Lord" — shows fear operating as an interior volitional restraint in situations where human oversight is absent. The will is not suppressed by reverential fear but governed: Proverbs 16:6 identifies fear as the operative inner force that turns the person away from evil. Deuteronomy 6:24 names fear as the inner posture that produces obedience — the will aligned with God's purposes through this orientation.

The moral evaluation faculty is the primary practical channel through which reverential fear operates. It provides the inner standard that continues functioning even when no external accountability is present: Leviticus 19:14 (fear as the moral evaluator where no court can observe), Nehemiah 5:15 (fear as the inner restraint against abusing authority). Second Chronicles 19:9 pairs the fear of the Lord explicitly with faithfulness and whole-heartedness in judicial conduct. Reverential fear deepens conscience — its absence produces the moral blindness that Psalm 36:1 describes: "no fear of God before his eyes" becomes the condition in which transgression speaks freely in the heart.

The affective and relational faculties are also engaged, and characteristically: reverential fear does not flatten affect but integrates it with joy. Psalm 2:11 shows fear coexisting with rejoicing in worship; Proverbs 14:26 presents fear as the foundation of confident security rather than anxiety. The relational capacity is directly activated — fear of God is the ground of Leviticus 19:14's ethical treatment of vulnerable others, of Joseph's treatment of his brothers in Genesis 42:18, of Cornelius's generous almsgiving in Acts 10:2. The person who fears God has an interior relational orientation that produces trustworthy conduct toward others precisely because its ground is not human approval.

The body carries reverential fear only in its somatic expression: Psalm 119:120 shows the flesh trembling in response to the inner reverential dread. The direction runs from soul outward to body — reverential fear is not generated by physical arousal but by the person's inner recognition of God's reality. The creative faculty is the one the evidence does not engage: reverential fear's formation is through hearing and receiving, not through imaginative origination.

### 4.2 Acute Fear and Alarm

Acute fear and alarm is located primarily in the heart. Psalm 27:3 names it directly: "even when war surrounds him, the psalmist's heart will not fear" — the heart is the inner locus of fear, explicitly named. First Samuel 28:5 (Saul seeing the Philistine army) locates trembling "explicitly in his heart." Nehemiah 2:2 shows fear "seated in the heart, visible in his countenance" — the heart as the originating site from which the fear then expresses on the face. Heart-location across these instances engages the feeling function: the heart registers threat as an intense affective experience, before the will responds.

Mind-location also appears, though as a secondary site. Daniel 4:5 shows fear produced by a dream that targets the mind — visions of the head alarming the inner person, making dread internally generated through the mind's nocturnal experience. This is a genuinely different pathway from the dominant external trigger: acute fear can be generated from within when the cognitive-imaginative faculty produces threatening scenarios in sleep.

The perceptive faculty is the primary entry point for acute fear. Sight is its dominant trigger: the sight of Goliath (1Sa 17:24: "All the men of Israel, when they saw the man, fled from him and were much afraid"), the sight of Jesus walking on water (Mat 14:26), the angel and divine glory seen by the shepherds in Luke 2:9. Hearing also triggers it: the Assyrian threatening words in 2 Kings 19:6, alarming successive reports in Jeremiah 51:46. Spiritual discernment marks the subtler register: Saul's fear of David in 1 Samuel 18:12 is rooted in theological perception — recognising that God's presence has transferred from himself to David.

What acute fear does to the faculties once triggered is typically impairing. Mark 9:6 captures it starkly: "For he did not know what to say, for they were terrified" — thought and speech are disabled together at the Transfiguration. The will is bypassed in the flight of all Israel's men before Goliath (1Sa 17:24: they fled "without deliberation"). The perceptive faculty is distorted: at Jesus walking on water the disciples misperceive him as a ghost (Mat 14:26). The agency faculty is either shut down or, in rarer instances, activated: Jacob's fear at Esau's approach immediately drives strategic action — splitting his camp (Gen 32:7). The dual effect — fear that disables and fear that organises — depends on the context and intensity.

The social register of acute fear (fear of crowds, of the Jews, of public opinion) runs through the same perceptive and volitional pathways but with a distinctive impairing effect on conscience: in Galatians 2:12, Peter's social fear overrides his own convictions, and in 1 Samuel 15:24, Saul confesses that fear of the people was the inner motive behind his disobedience to God. Social fear displaces the moral evaluation faculty by substituting crowd-preference for moral principle. Relational capacity is also impaired: social fear produces relational inauthenticity, withdrawal, and the failure of honest witness.

The memory faculty is engaged as a corrective resource. Deuteronomy 7:18 commands Israel not to be afraid of the nations — the inward dread of numerous enemies is to be replaced by memory of what God did to Pharaoh, grounding courage in historical experience. Memory of God's past acts is presented as the counter to present acute fear.

The affective engagement of acute fear is intense and complex. Matthew 28:8 — the women departing from the empty tomb "holding fear and great joy simultaneously" — is the most vivid instance of complex affective coexistence, showing that fear does not simply suppress all other affect. In extreme cases, as in Daniel 5:6, fear monopolises the inner life entirely: all other affect is displaced.

Acute fear is not located at the spirit level in any verse in this body of evidence. Spirit-level location remains silent across the entire acute-fear corpus, consistent with what the evidence shows: acute fear is an event that happens to the inner person through external triggers — it is not a constitutional disposition generated from within.

### 4.3 Boundary — Terms Held for Researcher Decision

The terms held pending researcher decision cannot be fully mapped in this chapter. Their verse evidence has either been set aside (meaning corpora empty) or carries unresolved analytical questions that prevent confident placement. What can be observed from the key verses is that these terms cluster around a field of inner states adjacent to fear, alarm, and dismay — including astonished bewilderment at the inexplicable (Gen 43:33: the brothers looking at one another in amazement), acute anguish that grips body and soul together (2Sa 1:9: "anguish has seized me, and yet my life still lingers"), the imagery of overwhelming mortal terror (2Sa 22:5: waves of death encompassing from all sides), the interior pressure of hostile oppression (Psa 55:3: the wicked dropping trouble upon the psalmist), and the Gethsemane distress that grips the soul (Mat 26:37: sorrowful and troubled). One term, ke.ra (Dan 7:15), carries the only explicit spirit-level location in this family outside of the trembling and timidity forms: Daniel's "spirit within me was anxious" places that distress directly in the spirit.

These terms are acknowledged here. They are not subject to full inner-topography analysis in this chapter because their classification remains open. A future pass will determine their placement and the inner-being mapping will be completed at that point.

### 4.4 Terror as Overwhelming Force

Terror as overwhelming force locates differently from the other fear characteristics because it is primarily described from the outside — as a power acting upon persons and peoples rather than as an experience located within a specific constitutional site. When the evidence does name inner location, the heart appears, but only in the terror-of-death material: "My heart is in anguish within me; the terrors of death have fallen upon me" (Psa 55:4). That is the clearest heart-location for terror as a received inner experience. In the main terror-as-weapon texts — Exodus 23:27 ("I will send my terror before you and will throw into confusion all the people against whom you shall come"), Genesis 35:5 (a terror from God falling upon the surrounding cities) — the terror is described as falling upon the person as a whole, not as entering a specific constitutional site. The inner location is presupposed rather than named.

The same pattern holds for spirit, soul, and mind — none of these is explicitly named as the constitutional site of terror-as-force in the core texts. The terror enters the person as an undifferentiated whole. The evidence is not silent because the characteristic has no inner reality — the will-paralysis and agency-collapse it produces are extensively documented — but because the biblical writers describe terror as a force that strikes from without, and what it strikes is the person entire.

The volitional faculty is terror's primary target. Genesis 35:5 shows it paralysing the will to pursue Jacob's sons — the enemies' hostile intent simply ceases to function under the imposed dread. Deuteronomy 11:25 describes the dread of Israel placed in the hearts of enemies so that no one can withstand them — the will to resist neutralised by a divinely-imposed inner state. Esther 9:3 shows officials' wills redirected by dread of Mordecai. Terror-as-force characteristically works on the volitional faculty in one of two modes: paralysis (the will to act ceases) or redirection (the will is reoriented toward flight or compliance). The deliberative process is bypassed entirely.

Agency follows the same pattern. Jeremiah 46:5 shows terror causing Egypt's warriors to abandon their posts and flee — inner resolve shattered by overwhelming dread. Ezekiel 7:27 (in the dismay/terror borderland) shows hands paralysed — agency physically disabled. Where terror-as-force operates, it operates against agency, not through it.

The perceptive faculty is engaged as the entry point: sight of Leviathan radiates terror from its face (Job 41:14); Daniel's great image with exceeding brightness is frightening by its visual appearance (Dan 2:31). Hearing also works: Jeremiah 20:10 shows the encircling terror triggered by hearing whispers of "terror on every side." Terror-as-force enters through the senses and then overwhelms the inner person from its external source.

No explicit mind-location and no spirit-level location are evidenced for terror as overwhelming force outside the dismay-adjacent material. The characteristic does not document constitutional deposit in the body. What it documents is the collapse of bodily function as its consequence: colour change, limb failure, flight — all expressions of what happens when the will and agency that normally maintain bodily composure are overwhelmed.

The one meaningful silence is the absence of image-bearing articulation. Unlike reverential fear, where the evidence grounds the characteristic in what it means to be rightly positioned before God, terror-as-force has no verse that explicitly connects it to the human person's created design. Its divine treatment is primarily instrumental — God deploys it — and its inner-topography evidence is primarily functional: what it does to the person, not what it reveals about the person's creaturely constitution.

### 4.5 Dismay and Inner Collapse

Dismay and inner collapse lives in two distinct places in the inner person — soul and heart — with the soul providing the most explicit single statement. Psalm 6:3: "My soul also is greatly troubled. But you, O Lord — how long?" — the psalmist's soul itself is greatly dismayed, named as the seat of the profound turmoil that drives the anguished cry to God. The nefesh (soul) in Hebrew anthropology is the seat of the person's own experienced aliveness — hunger, desire, vitality. When dismay seizes the nefesh, the capacity to sustain one's own inner life is disrupted. This is constitutionally deeper than heart-disturbance: the person's experienced existence itself is shaken.

The heart is also a primary site. Job 23:16 identifies the heart as where God's action produces dismay — "God has made Job's heart faint and the Almighty has terrified him." Daniel 5:10 (the queen speaking to Belshazzar) recognises that dismay "originates in the inner life of thought" — the inner life centred in the heart. The heart-location engages both the knowing and feeling functions: Daniel 5:6 shows thoughts alarmed (knowing) followed by limbs giving way and knees knocking (somatic expression of inner collapse).

The mind is a significant secondary site, distinctive to this characteristic. Daniel 4:19 shows "dismay holds Daniel for a moment as his thoughts race — the inner impact of understanding a terrible message temporarily overrides speech and composure, with alarmed thoughts pressing on the inner person." Daniel 7:28 places dismay through the thoughts. The pattern is: the mind processes the terrible message, and the processing produces the inner collapse. This is the reverse of many fear forms — dismay in these texts begins in cognition and flows outward to affect and body.

The constitutional movement is the most explicitly documented in this family. Daniel 5:6 traces the sequence in full: dismay strikes the inner person (thoughts alarmed) → colour changes (autonomic somatic response) → limbs give way → knees knock. Dismay follows a pattern of triggering event → inner cognitive disruption → somatic manifestation. The direction is inner-person to body.

Multiple body-parts are named as sites of dismay's manifestation: in Daniel 5:6, limbs giving way and knees knocking; in Isaiah 21:3, the inability to see — perception itself shut down; in Job 32:15, speechlessness — the verbal faculties failing. Scripture uses these body-part specifications expressively, to show the completeness of the inner collapse by its bodily consequence, and indicatively, to signal severity: when limbs fail and speech ceases, the person has been overwhelmed to their constitutional limit.

The inner capacities are uniformly impaired by dismay. Cognition is shut down: "As often as it passes through it will take you; for morning by morning it will pass through, by day and by night; and it will be sheer terror to understand the message" (Isa 28:19) — comprehension itself becomes the trigger of terror. Job 32:15 shows the friends "dismayed into complete speechlessness — the rational and verbal faculties entirely shut down." Volition is dissolved: Ezekiel 7:27 shows hands paralysed, the capacity to choose and act physically disabled. Agency is bypassed: Jeremiah 46:5 shows warriors abandoning posts under the dissolving force of overwhelming dread. Affect is monopolised: Psalm 6:3's soul-level anguish leaves no room for other inner states.

Dismay impairs relational capacity as well: Psalm 31:11 shows the sufferer becoming an object of dread to his acquaintances — the characteristic drives others away, operating as a social and relational rupture. Dismay does not enter through the body — there is no evidence of bodily experience generating dismay — but it exits through the body in full display.

Spirit-level location is absent. Dismay is not an experience the verse evidence places at the spirit level. Nor is it located at the mind-level as a constitutional site; mind engagement in dismay is the processing stage, not the home. The soul and heart are where dismay is received and held.

### 4.6 Trembling / Fear Made Somatic

Trembling is unique in the family because it is the one characteristic whose primary constitutional theatre is the body itself. The body is not merely the vehicle through which trembling expresses an inner state — it is the primary evidence that the inner state exists. "On the morning of the third day there were thunders and lightnings and a thick cloud on the mountain and a very loud trumpet blast, so that all the people in the camp trembled" (Exo 19:16). The trembling is the registration; the inner state and the somatic response are simultaneous, not sequential.

Within this dominant somatic character, the evidence distinguishes two streams. The first — theophanic and panic trembling — is not located at the spirit level. The trembling happens to the body in response to external overwhelming events. Bones shake (Job 4:14), flesh trembles (Job 21:6), hair bristles (Eze 27:35: "the hair of their kings bristles with horror"), lips quiver (Hab 3:16), knees knock (Dan 5:6). The body-part evidence is the richest in the entire family. The characteristic is expressive in all these cases: the body registers what the inner person cannot contain, with each body-part providing its own specific form of evidence.

The second stream — trembling at God's word — is constitutional at a different level. Isaiah 66:2 is the pivotal text: "He who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word." Here the trembling is located in the spirit. The spirit-level location of this form of trembling reveals that it is not a reflex triggered by sensory overwhelm but a posture of the deepest constitutional self — emerging from the same depth as humility and contrition. Ezra 10:9 shows the community trembling from two simultaneous sources: "the moral gravity of the matter of unfaithfulness and the physical distress of heavy rain" — an inner moral trembling and a somatic one occurring together. Psalm 2:11 — "Serve the Lord with fear, and rejoice with trembling" — shows trembling as a reverential qualifier of joy, not a terror-response at all.

The heart is an additional location, with specific verse support. Isaiah 21:4: "horror shudders through the prophet's heart, appalling him" — trembling explicitly in the heart. Job 37:1: "Elihu's heart trembles and leaps at the thunder of God's voice" — the heart as the inner organ that responds with physical agitation to overwhelming divine sound. First Samuel 4:13 locates Eli's trembling explicitly in his heart.

Constitutional movement is the most explicitly documented across this family for this characteristic. The spirit-to-body sequence is named for trembling-at-word (Isa 66:2: spirit-posture producing somatic trembling). The encounter-to-whole-person sequence is named for theophanic trembling (Exo 19:16: the divine encounter and the trembling appear simultaneously). And a bidirectional dynamic is at work in the panic and military trembling texts: the external trigger produces the inner state which produces the somatic response — but once the trembling begins, it may itself deepen the inner experience. The body's agitation feeds back to intensify the inner state.

The perceptive faculty is the characteristic's primary entry point in most forms. Hearing is especially prominent: the thunder and trumpet at Sinai (Exo 19:16), Amos 3:6 (a trumpet blast naturally producing trembling), Job 37:1 (the thunder of God's voice). Moses trembled and did not dare to look when God named himself at the burning bush: "I am the God of your fathers, the God of Abraham and of Isaac and of Jacob" (Act 7:32) — the self-identification is the auditory trigger, and the trembling and the refusal to look arrive simultaneously. Sight also triggers trembling: Ezekiel 26:16 shows the sea-princes trembling at witnessing Tyre's fall.

The trembling-at-word tradition deepens the perceptive dimension beyond mere sensory trigger. Ezra 9:4 and 10:3 show a community who tremble at God's word gathering for covenant action — the trembling marks heightened receptive attentiveness to God's communication, not perceptive shutdown. This is the only form of fear in the family that deepens rather than impairs perception.

Trembling as a sustained condition carries constitutional weight: Deuteronomy 28:65 names a trembling heart as a covenantal curse — "the Lord will give you there a trembling heart and failing eyes and a languishing soul" — the unceasing inner agitation paired with bodily and soul-level deterioration, the whole person being dismantled by sustained dread. No constitutional deposit in the body is documented; the silence on generational or DNA-level effects is consistent across this family.

### 4.7 Anticipatory Dread and Anxiety

The constitutional movement of anticipatory dread is the most explicitly documented of any characteristic in this family: mind → heart → body. The sequence is traced in the evidence: the mind projects forward (cognitive anticipation), the heart is weighed down (Pro 12:25: "anxiety weighs down a man's heart"), and the body is colonised in its most ordinary acts (Eze 12:18: "eat your bread with quaking, and drink water with trembling and with anxiety"). Dread begins in thought and saturates progressively deeper, eventually making even eating and drinking impossible to do without it.

The heart is the primary constitutional seat for this characteristic. Deuteronomy 28:67 locates dread explicitly in the heart and ties it to what the eyes see — it "produces such unbearable inner anguish that the sufferer constantly wishes the present moment away." Proverbs 12:25 names the heart as the site of anxiety's weight. The heart-location engages both the feeling function (Deu 28:67's unbearable anguish) and the moral-awareness function (Pro 12:25: the heart as capable of being relieved by an encouraging word — the heart's responsiveness to speech).

The mind is the distinctive secondary site, and for this characteristic, the mind's engagement is anticipatory. Second Corinthians 12:20 — "For I fear that perhaps when I come I may find you not as I wish, and that you may find me not as you wish" — names the anxiety as "rooted in thought about what he may find," explicitly engaging the mind with anxious expectation before the event arrives. The de.a.gah terms carry a cognitive-ruminating quality: anxiety as a churning restlessness the mind cannot still, circling around possible futures. Hebrews 10:27 — "a fearful expectation of judgment, and a fury of fire that will consume the adversaries" — locates anticipatory dread in the mind as anticipation directed at coming divine judgment.

The body is the third location, characteristically the end-point of a process that begins in cognition. Ezekiel 12:18 shows anxiety so pervasive it manifests in the hands and body during eating and drinking — the ordinary physical actions of daily survival taken over by inner dread. The functional link is the clearest in the family: the body is not expressing a moment of inner experience but is showing that anxiety has colonised the person's entire daily functioning.

Spirit-level location is absent. Anticipatory dread is not a spirit-level phenomenon in the verse evidence; its constitutional language points to mind, heart, and body, in that sequence.

The inner capacities engaged are distinctive. The creative-imaginative faculty is the most prominent, and this sets anticipatory dread apart from the other fear characteristics. By definition, anticipatory dread involves imagining a feared future that has not yet arrived. Job 3:25 — "For the thing that I fear comes upon me, and what I dread befalls me" — shows that Job carried the anticipated scenario internally before it materialised: the dread and the feared reality eventually converge, but the dread preceded the reality and was held in the imagination. Second Corinthians 12:20 shows the same imaginative structure: Paul is projecting possible disorder before the visit. De.a.gah as a term-type describes worry as fundamentally imaginative activity — the mind generating and dwelling in scenarios that are not yet real.

This imaginative engagement is also where the characteristic most clearly distorts cognition. The cognitive faculty in anticipatory dread is active but misdirected — fixated on anticipated negative futures rather than present reality, unable to rest on what is actually known. Memory is engaged in a specific way: Deuteronomy 28:60 shows embedded fears being retriggered — the person's earlier terror of Egypt's diseases is recalled by the covenant curse, exploiting the deep-seated fear memory as an instrument of ongoing dread.

The affective faculty is impaired through colonisation: Hebrews 2:15 ("fear of death holding people in lifelong inner slavery") shows the sustained dread occupying the entire affective space available to the person. Ezekiel 12:18–19 confirms it: anxiety saturates every moment, leaving no room for other affective engagement.

The conscience is engaged in an avoidant mode. Acts 24:25 shows Felix becoming alarmed as Paul reasons about righteousness and coming judgment — conscience stirred by anticipatory dread of divine assessment, producing avoidance rather than repentance. Hebrews 10:27's fearful expectation of judgment lodged in the mind shows conscience as the site of anticipated divine scrutiny — engaged, but in a way that produces shrinking rather than turning.

### 4.8 Timidity and Cowardly Shrinking

Timidity and cowardly shrinking has the most explicitly negative spirit-level location in this family. Second Timothy 1:7 is unambiguous: "for God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control" — timidity is identified as a spirit-level disposition that God has not implanted, making it a failure of inner constitution rather than circumstance. The spirit-level naming is not peripheral; it is the diagnostic that gives this characteristic its weight. Cowardly shrinking is not primarily a situational response or an emotional reaction — it is a constitutional failure at the person's deepest level.

What the spirit-level location reveals is significant. By naming timidity as a spirit-level disposition God has not implanted, Paul simultaneously defines the spirit-level positive: power, love, and self-control are what God does implant. Timidity is the wrong spirit where the right spirit should be. The absence is at the constitutional core, not a downstream effect of other inner states.

The heart is also engaged, by implication and by command. John 14:27 addresses the heart directly: "Let not your hearts be troubled, neither let them be afraid." The command is to the heart because the heart is where the yielding or resistance to timidity occurs. Jesus commands the will of the heart not to yield to the fearful recoiling — establishing the heart as the site of volitional decision about whether to permit or resist the characteristic.

The volitional faculty is the primary operational theatre. Timidity is definitionally a failure of will under pressure. Matthew 8:26 — "Why are you afraid, O you of little faith?" — links cowardly recoiling directly to a failure of trust: the inner cowardice reveals a failure of the will to rest in Jesus's presence. Matthew 25:25 shows the will choosing passive self-preservation over faithful risk. John 14:27's command addresses the will: the heart is not to "yield to that fearful recoiling" — the will can choose to resist. Revelation 21:8's condemnation shows the will choosing apostasy under sustained threat. In every instance, the will is present and operative — timidity does not bypass the will but misdirects it toward inaction, hiding, and shrinking.

Agency is the faculty timidity most directly suppresses. Matthew 25:25: the talent buried rather than invested — the action not taken. Luke 19:21: fear of the severe master producing inaction. Timidity is the failure of the agency-courage that would take faithful action, producing instead the absence of action, the closed door, the hidden life.

Moral evaluation is also impaired. Revelation 21:8 names cowardice as a moral failure alongside idolatry and murder, not as a temperamental weakness. Matthew 25:25 shows the servant's moral evaluation of the master's character distorted by fear — the wrong assessment driving the wrong action. Conscientiousness — the integrated moral-awareness, volition, and action of faithfulness — is fragmented: the servant has the moral awareness and the will for self-preservation, but not the integrated will-and-action of faithful stewardship.

Cognition is impaired in a specific mode: the servant's failure to think productively about how to use the talent (Mat 25:25) shows fear suppressing the cognitive action that would enable faithful investment. The mind is present but constrained toward self-protective calculation.

No body-part link is documented for timidity. The physical inaction it produces — burying the talent, locking the doors, hiding — is behaviourally evidenced but not body-part-specified. The spirit-level origin and the volitional-agency-conscience engagement are where this characteristic operates. Its perceptive triggers are not explicitly described in the verse evidence; the threat situations (storm, master's harshness, persecution) are implied but the specific inner-sense engagement through which timidity arises is not named.
