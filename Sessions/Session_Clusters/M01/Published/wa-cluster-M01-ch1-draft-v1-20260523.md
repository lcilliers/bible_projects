## 1. What this study is

Scripture uses the language of fear, dread and terror across a wider range than any single English word can hold. This study examines that full range — not as a uniform emotion, but as a family of distinct inner-being characteristics that together map the inner person's encounter with power, holiness, threat and the weight of what is to come. The eight characteristics gathered here span from reverential fear as a stable governing orientation of the whole life, through acute alarm, dismay, and trembling, to the chronic burden of anticipatory dread and the moral failure of cowardly shrinking. Their names signal the range: fear, dread, terror are not synonyms but registers on a spectrum, from measured inner disposition through intensified anticipatory pressure to overwhelming assault.

What holds this family together is that every characteristic in it concerns the inner person's registering of power — whether the power is divine, threatening, or simply overwhelming — and the impact that registration has on the will. The volitional faculty is engaged across every form: fear in its ordered form governs what the person does; fear in its disordered forms determines what the person cannot do or will not do. This is the common thread. And beneath the volitional dimension lies an affective one: across more than nine hundred verses, fear is consistently described as something that fills the person, grips them, falls upon them, overwhelms them — the language of inner states that are undergone rather than chosen.

Scripture is equally consistent on one structural feature: fear belongs to the creature, not the Creator. God is the supreme object of reverential fear by virtue of his holiness and incomparability; he is not himself a subject who fears. This asymmetry is not incidental. As Psalm 36:1 names the condition of the person who lacks reverential fear — "there is no fear of God before his eyes" — the divine image in the human person is most clearly located and most clearly diagnosed through the presence or absence of this characteristic. The capacity for fear is part of what it means to be made for right relationship with God. And the evidence points forward: the goal toward which this family of characteristics moves is not the elimination of fear but its purification — what endures is the fear rightly placed, the reverence before God that marks the inner life of the fully redeemed person, while every form of disordered fear is left behind.

Within that shared structure, variation matters. Several of these characteristics — acute fear, terror as overwhelming force, dismay, anticipatory dread — belong primarily to the fallen condition, engaging a created capacity in contexts shaped by threat, collapse and judgment. Reverential fear and the somatic trembling of theophanic encounter appear to be original created features: the creature's right registration of the holy. Timidity is presented not merely as a condition but as a moral failure, the constitutional recoil that faith is given to overcome. Each form carries its own inner logic, and treating them as a single undifferentiated phenomenon would misread the evidence.

The study moves through these characteristics in sequence, examining what each is, where it lives in the inner person, how it operates, how it is formed and transformed, and how the family's members relate to one another. It closes by accounting for what the verse evidence does not address.
