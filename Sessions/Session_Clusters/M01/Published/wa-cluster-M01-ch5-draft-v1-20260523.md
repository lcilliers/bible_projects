## 5. How each characteristic works

Where the previous chapter mapped where these characteristics live in the inner person, this chapter sets them in motion — tracing how each one moves between God and the human person and between persons, how it is formed and deepened over time, how it is tested, and where it is going.

### 5.1 Reverential Fear / Fear of God as Governing Orientation

Reverential fear of God moves along multiple channels simultaneously, and the multiplicity is itself a finding. God commands it — Deuteronomy 6:13 and 10:20 require the reverential posture directly. He forms it through encounter — Deuteronomy 4:10 records that God intended the Horeb encounter to produce lasting fear in those who stood there, and that intention was to run forward to their children. He implants it as a covenant gift — Jeremiah 32:40 records God pledging to put his fear into the hearts of his people as a permanent disposition preventing them from turning away. And he rewards it — Exodus 1:21 shows God giving families to the midwives whose fear of him governed their disobedience to Pharaoh. The characteristic flows toward the human person through command, encounter, and implantation; the basis is covenantal rather than transactional.

The human person's movement toward God through reverential fear is correspondingly rich. Worship is its most direct expression: Psalm 5:7 shows fear drawing the psalmist into the temple in a posture of bowing, and Hebrews 12:28 names "reverence and awe" as the fitting inner disposition for approaching God in worship, "prompted by gratitude for receiving an unshakeable kingdom." Prayer is another movement: Daniel 9:4 shows reverential fear as the inner disposition from which his confession and petition arise; Jeremiah 26:19 records Hezekiah's fear producing active prayer that averted national disaster. Covenant is a third movement: Malachi 2:5 reveals that God structured an entire covenant relationship around this response — "My covenant with him was one of life and peace, and I gave them to him. It was a covenant of fear, and he feared me. He stood in awe of my name."

The horizontal movement between persons is primarily pedagogical. The psalmist offers in Psalm 34:11 to teach fear of the Lord — it can be transmitted through instruction and example. Deuteronomy 31:12–13 shows the designed mechanism: public reading of the law, generation after generation, cultivating fear in those who hear, including children who have not yet heard. The formation chain is pedagogical rather than biological — Psalm 103:17 shows the covenantal benefit of the fear-posture running across generations, but the transmission is through teaching and practice, not constitutional deposit.

Opposition acts against reverential fear primarily through displacement rather than direct assault. The 2 Kings 17 corpus shows reverential fear systematically replaced by fear of local gods — competing objects of fear occupying the inner space where God alone should reign. Isaiah 8:12 shows social fear threatening to displace God-fear: the prophet must resist adopting the crowd's conspiratorial dread, actively maintaining the fear of God against the current of surrounding anxiety. Hollow fear is another form of corruption: Isaiah 29:13 names the inner condition in which the lips perform reverence while the heart stands at a distance — a formal external compliance that has displaced the genuine inner orientation.

Reverential fear is formed by multiple mechanisms depending on the canonical tradition. In the wisdom tradition, it is the fruit of earnest seeking — Proverbs 2:5 presents it as an inner understanding arrived at through diligent pursuit. In the Deuteronomic tradition, regular communal hearing of Torah produces it over time. In the prophetic tradition, God implants it directly in the heart (Jer 32:40). In the narrative tradition, witnessing God's acts produces it suddenly: Exodus 14:31 records that the sight of God's victory at the sea produced immediate fear and belief in Israel. Haggai 1:12 shows the same mechanism: hearing the prophetic word produces the reverential response immediately. The mechanism is context-dependent; all pathways converge on the same inner disposition.

Reverential fear is tested by suffering and shown to hold. Job 1:1 and 2:3 both record God affirming Job's character — "blameless, upright, fearing God, turning from evil" — after and through the intensity of his suffering. The affliction does not destroy the reverential posture; it reveals whether it is genuine. The eschatological arc is the most developed of any characteristic in the family. Revelation 11:18 names fearing God's name as the defining characteristic of those who receive reward at the final judgment; Revelation 14:7 commands fear of God in view of the hour of his judgment; Revelation 15:4 and 19:5 show fearing God as the universal posture of the consummated community. The fullness of reverential fear is not its abolition but its universalisation — every creature responding as the God-fearer now does in part, the present partial and contested experience reaching its permanent and uncontested form.

### 5.2 Acute Fear and Alarm

Acute fear and alarm moves primarily from the outside in and from God downward. Its dominant movement is the encounter — the sudden overwhelming of the inner person by something that exceeds its normal categories. God produces this movement through theophanic encounters: angelic appearances that fill the shepherds with great fear (Luk 2:9), Jesus's acts that leave the disciples "filled with great fear" asking "Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?" (Mar 4:41), the Transfiguration that leaves Peter unable to know what to say because "they were terrified" (Mar 9:6). Angels mediate this movement bidirectionally — they both produce acute fear through their appearances and command its release ("do not be afraid," Luk 1:13; Luk 2:10; Mat 28:5). The angel stands at the interface between divine holiness and human inner life, producing the fear that correctly registers divine reality, then releasing it.

The most structurally significant movement is God's repeated address to the frightened person. "Fear not, for I have redeemed you; I have called you by name, you are mine" (Isa 43:1) — the release of acute fear is grounded not in circumstances changing but in relational identity being named. The "you are mine" is the operative mechanism, not reassurance in general. What holds the release in place is not the disappearance of the threatening circumstance but the named belonging that makes the circumstance secondary. The same structure governs Jesus's calming of his disciples: "Rise, and have no fear" (Mat 17:7) follows his touch — the command is grounded in the relationship and the proximity. Reception of this release requires a will open to the divine word; distrust blocks it.

The human person's movement toward God through acute fear follows the theophanic pattern. When the storm fear and the testimony converge, the sailors in Jonah 1:16 move from threat-fear to reverential worship — they "feared the Lord greatly, and offered a sacrifice to the Lord and made vows." Acute fear, rightly redirected, is a gateway into worship. Matthew 27:54 shows the centurion's fear at the crucifixion producing confession — the experiencing of fear as the appropriate response to what has been witnessed moves directly into theological statement. The movement is not always smooth: the disciples in the Upper Room, after the resurrection, have locked the doors "for fear of the Jews" (Joh 20:19), and it is Jesus's appearance, his showing of his hands and side, that turns fear into joy.

Between persons, the "do not fear" command operates as a giving of release. David to Mephibosheth (2Sa 9:7), Elisha to his servant (2Ki 6:16), Jesus to the disciples — in each case the one who commands release of fear does so from a position of authority or covenant assurance relative to the one who is afraid. The giving of this release is not the giving of the fear itself but the provision of the ground on which the fear can be let go.

Formation in the disordered direction is the characteristic's main developmental path when acute fear is left unaddressed. First Samuel 18:29 shows Saul's fear of David intensifying into a permanent inner root of sustained enmity — what begins as an episodic reaction calcifies into a settled state approaching the dispositional character of anticipatory dread. The disciples' doors locked in John 20:19 show the same calcification. Against this, the "do not fear" corpus shows that when the fear is addressed — when the divine word penetrates and the person receives it — the result is trust, courage, and the capacity for mission (Mat 28:10).

Acute fear's eschatological orientation is freedom from all disordering fear. Revelation 2:10 commands the church at Smyrna not to fear the coming suffering — faithfulness through eschatological trial is the direction. Luke 21:26 identifies cosmic signs as producing fainting from fear, naming the eschatological context as precisely the place where the "do not be terrified" command applies (Luk 21:9). The fullness is a state in which no legitimate ground for acute alarm remains — not because the person has become insensate to reality but because all threatening realities have been resolved.

### 5.3 Boundary — Terms Held for Researcher Decision

The terms held pending researcher decision do not have sufficient analytical foundation for a full account of how they work in the inner person's movement, formation, and transformation. Their movement dynamics, formation patterns, and eschatological orientations cannot be described with confidence from the available evidence.

What the key verses reveal in aggregate is that these terms cluster around experiences of being seized, overwhelmed, and filled — anguish seizing the wounded man (2Sa 1:9: "anguish has seized me, and yet my life still lingers"), waves of death encompassing the psalmist (2Sa 22:5), amazement filling the crowd at the recognised beggar healed (Act 3:10). The directions of movement are primarily from outside in — these are states that arrive rather than states that are chosen or formed. The Gethsemane term (ademoneo, Mat 26:37) is the most theologically significant, engaging Jesus's soul as he faces the approaching suffering. Its movement is unique in the family — the Son, in the act of obedience, experiencing an acute inner distress that his prayer addresses.

A full movement-and-formation account must await the research decision that will determine where each term belongs. These terms are acknowledged here; they are not developed.

### 5.4 Terror as Overwhelming Force

Terror as overwhelming force moves almost entirely in one direction: from God or powerful entities outward and downward, landing upon peoples and inner lives as an imposed condition. The movement from within the human person toward God is largely absent. This characteristic does not document a human-to-God movement in the evidence — the terror is imposed, not offered; received involuntarily, not given.

God's movement through this characteristic is purposive and covenantal. "I will send my terror before you and will throw into confusion all the people against whom you shall come, and I will make all your enemies turn their backs to you" (Exo 23:27). The basis is conditional on enemy opposition to God's covenantal purposes — terror is his instrument of historical governance in response to resistance. Genesis 35:5 shows the same mechanism in the patriarchal narrative: a terror from God falls upon the surrounding cities so that they do not pursue Jacob's sons. The initiative is entirely divine; the inner states of the enemy peoples are the point of impact. Deuteronomy 11:25 generalises the pattern: God placing the dread of Israel in the hearts of peoples across the land, so that no one can withstand Israel.

The sustained effect of terror-as-weapon is condition-change in its recipients. Genesis 35:5 shows hostile intent becoming paralysis; Esther 9:2–3 shows opposition to the Jewish community converting under the dread of Mordecai into active assistance from provincial officials. The characteristic transforms the internal disposition of those it strikes, turning potential enemies into passive or cooperative subjects. This is its function in the covenantal narrative — it makes human history legible as a domain governed by God, where resistance to his purposes dissolves from within.

Eschatologically, the direction is toward the stripping of all such terror-power. The Ezekiel 32 corpus shows nations whose terror was their power lying in Sheol with that power entirely removed — Assyria, once spreading terror "in the land of the living" (Eze 32:23), lies powerless in the pit. The eschatological fullness for this characteristic is not an intensification but a termination: a world in which no human power deploys terror as a weapon over others, because the only legitimate dread is the fear of God himself.

The characteristic is largely silent on formation. Terror-as-force does not produce formative growth in those who experience it; it imposes a condition. No mechanism for developing terror-endurance or resilience is documented in the evidence. The exception, perhaps, is the transformation of those who receive God's covenantal dread: the paralysed enemy who cannot pursue becomes the defeated people through whom God's sovereignty is demonstrated. But even that is a political-historical transformation, not a character-forming one.

### 5.5 Dismay and Inner Collapse

Dismay and inner collapse moves from God outward as a judicial instrument, and from catastrophic events inward into the person. God produces dismay in his judicial capacity: Psalm 2:5 shows his wrath producing dismay in rebel kings; Jeremiah 49:37 records God declaring, "I will terrify Elam before their enemies" — the inner collapse of the enemy people is the instrument through which divine judgment breaks their resistance. The basis is conditional on their defiance; dismay is not arbitrary but responsive to opposition to God's covenantal purposes.

The same God who produces dismay in enemies also commands against it in his servants. Jeremiah 1:17 holds both movements together: "Do not be dismayed by them, lest I dismay you before them." The prophet is commanded not to let inner collapse grip him when facing opponents, with the sobering implication that if he yields to dismay, God himself will produce in him the very state he fears. Dismay is thus a characteristic over which God has sovereign authority in both directions — he can impose it and he can forbid it. The existence of the command against dismay implies that the person retains agency: the characteristic can be resisted, though only from the ground of divine commission and word.

The human-to-God movement through dismay is limited but present. Psalm 6:3's soul-level dismay drives the anguished cry to God — "But you, O Lord — how long?" The dismay is not a turning away from God but a turning toward him in extremity. The lament psalms show this pattern repeatedly: the inner collapse becoming the occasion of prayer, the dissolution of human capacity becoming the ground for divine address. Dismay does not produce structured worship but it can produce the cry that is itself a form of movement toward God.

Between persons, dismay has no substantial giving direction. It is not transmitted person-to-person in the way reverential fear is instructed or acute fear is released. Its movement is primarily individual — the person is struck by catastrophe, news, or divine action, and the inner collapse follows. The contagion evidenced in 1 Samuel 14:15 (panic spreading through the Philistine garrison as a wave) is the nearest approach — dismay propagating through a group — but even this is not the giving of a characteristic so much as the social amplification of a shared experience.

Formation is largely absent for dismay as positive growth. The evidence does not document a mechanism for developing dismay-resilience or terror-endurance; these are states experienced rather than formative processes. The commanded-against-dismay material (Jer 1:17; Jer 30:10: "salvation and quiet rest" as the antidote) implies that resistance to dismay is possible and commanded, but the mechanism is the divine word rather than a developmental process. The person who does not yield to dismay when God has spoken stands on the ground of divine commission; the formation involved is the formation of trust in that commission, not of dismay-endurance as such.

Suffering is the primary producer of dismay. Job 4:5 shows Eliphaz noting that when affliction reached Job personally, dismay overtook him; Psalm 6:2–3 shows physical suffering producing inner collapse. The characteristic arises directly from catastrophic contexts — it is the inner registering of what cannot be contained. Its eschatological direction is the promise of no-more-dismay: Jeremiah 30:10 names the antidote as "salvation and quiet rest," the eschatological state of undismayed composure grounded in God's deliverance.

### 5.6 Trembling / Fear Made Somatic

Trembling moves in two quite different patterns depending on which form is in view. Theophanic trembling — the body's involuntary response to divine presence — moves from the encounter outward, simultaneously inner and somatic. "On the morning of the third day there were thunders and lightnings and a thick cloud on the mountain and a very loud trumpet blast, so that all the people in the camp trembled" (Exo 19:16). There is no interval between encounter and trembling; the divine presence and the somatic response arrive together. "Moses trembled and did not dare to look" (Act 7:32) — the same simultaneity, with God's self-identification producing the trembling before any deliberation. This form of trembling has no instruction pathway, no formation mechanism, no human-to-human transmission. It is involuntary and unmediated.

Trembling at God's word operates from a different constitutional depth and through different pathways. Isaiah 66:2 names the posture: "he who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word." The trembling-at-word tradition is formed in community and sustained through covenant engagement. Ezra 9:4 and 10:3 show those "who tremble at the commandment of our God" gathering as a distinct community around covenant decision-making — the trembling posture leading to covenant action, the somatic gravity expressing the community's reverential attentiveness to God's communication. The formation sequence is: before (covenant unfaithfulness discovered), during (the community assembling in appalled grief and trembling), after (a covenant decision made from the posture of reverent gravity). The mechanism is community formation in the context of God's word, not sudden encounter.

The human movement toward God through trembling operates differently in the two forms. Theophanic trembling is not a movement toward God but a response to his proximity — the body's registration of what the inner person has encountered. Trembling at God's word, by contrast, is precisely the posture of receptive engagement with divine communication: the person who trembles at God's word is the person God looks for, the one to whom he turns. Isaiah 66:5 promises vindication to the community that trembles at his word. Psalm 2:11 shows trembling as the somatic qualifier of joy in service to God — "rejoice with trembling" — where the trembling marks the gravity that prevents casual approach.

Between persons, trembling has almost no giving direction. It is not transmitted or extended; it is experienced. The one near-exception is the reverential weight that Ephraim's speech once commanded (Hos 13:1: "when Ephraim spoke, there was trembling" in others) — an authoritative presence that produced trembling in those who heard. But this is the body's response to perceived authority rather than a transmitted characteristic.

The eschatological direction of trembling splits along the same two-form line. For the theophanic and panic forms, the eschatological fullness is freedom from them: Ezekiel 34:28 and Micah 4:4 promise that "none shall make them afraid" — the restoration as a state where the threat-driven trembling that afflicts the present has entirely ceased. For the trembling-at-word tradition, no corresponding elimination is promised; the posture of reverential somatic gravity before God's communication is not one of the things the eschaton removes. The community defined by trembling at God's word is the one Isaiah 66:5 vindicates. Philippians 2:12 — "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" — shows the trembling-gravity continuing as the appropriate inner-bodily tone of earnest eschatological engagement.

### 5.7 Anticipatory Dread and Anxiety

Anticipatory dread and anxiety is a characteristic that God imposes in its disordered form and releases in its resolution. Deuteronomy 28:66–67 shows covenantal-curse dread as a divine imposition — "night and day you shall be in dread and have no assurance of your life" — the chronic anxiety as a direct covenant consequence of unfaithfulness. The basis is conditional on covenant violation; God does not impose chronic dread arbitrarily but as a fitting consequence of the relationship's rupture. God will also bring upon the disobedient the very fears they have harboured (Isa 66:4) — turning their own anticipatory anxieties into the instrument of judgment.

But God also stands as the primary source of release from this characteristic. The sequence in Psalm 34:4 is one of the clearest in the evidence: "I sought the Lord, and he answered me and delivered me from all my fears." The mechanism of release is relational — seeking God produces encounter with God, and encounter with God produces deliverance from the inner terrors that had gripped the person. First John 4:18 names the ultimate mechanism: "perfect love casts out fear, because fear has to do with punishment." The love of God, when it is received in its fullness, displaces the punitive-anticipatory dimension of fear. God as judge is what makes anticipatory dread of judgment rational; God as perfect love is what displaces it.

The movement from person to person through this characteristic is limited and specific. Paul's pastoral anxiety in 2 Corinthians 12:20 — his anticipatory dread of what he may find when he visits his churches — flows from love already invested. The anxiety is relationally motivated; his concern for the community generates the forward-looking dread of discovering disorder. Philippians 2:26 shows the same relational structure in Epaphroditus's distress because the Philippians had heard of his illness — the anxiety arising from concern about how his condition has troubled those he loves. This form of anticipatory dread moves from the one who loves toward the ones loved; it is anxiety-as-care.

Formation and transformation in anticipatory dread follow no positive arc. Chronic anxiety is not a characteristic that deepens beneficially through exercise — it colonises. Ezekiel 12:18–19 shows it saturating every moment of eating and drinking; Hebrews 2:15 names the fear of death as something that held people in lifelong inner slavery. The characteristic is a condition produced by suffering and threat (siege conditions in Ezekiel, mortality itself in Hebrews), and its resolution is not through habituation but through deliverance. The partial deliverances the evidence documents — Psalm 34:4's seeking and being answered, Isaiah 12:2's declaration that dread no longer has a hold — are foretastes of the eschatological fullness in which perfect love has entirely displaced the fear of punishment (1Jo 4:18). Christ's defeat of death is the ground of Hebrews 2:15's liberation from lifelong fear-of-death slavery; the eschatological fullness of this characteristic's resolution is tied directly to his work.

The imagination is the primary operating theatre of anticipatory dread, and this makes it the one characteristic in the family that works most distinctively through the mind's forward-projecting capacity. Job 3:25 shows dread as an anticipated scenario held in the imagination before it materialised — the dreaded thing sitting within him as an inward anticipation before it arrived as an outer event. Paul's pastoral dread (2Cor 12:20) operates the same way: the feared relational disorder is projected and held in the mind before the visit. The characteristic distorts cognition not by shutting it down, as dismay does, but by fixing it on anticipated negative futures. Its resolution requires that the mind be redirected — not suppressed but given a different object. The promise "I sought the Lord" is the turn from the feared future to the reliable present reality of God's response.

### 5.8 Timidity and Cowardly Shrinking

Timidity and cowardly shrinking does not come from God. Second Timothy 1:7 states the negative directly: "God gave us a spirit not of fear but of power and love and self-control." The spirit of timidity is what God has not given; by implication it has a different source — a failed or corrupted inner constitution that stands in the place where God's gift should be. The characteristic does not move from God toward the person in any positive sense; God's movement toward the person in relation to this characteristic is the negation of it, the giving of its opposite.

The command against it operates through the relational ground that Christ provides. John 14:27: "Let not your hearts be troubled, neither let them be afraid." The command is to the heart — to the site of volitional decision — and it is grounded in the peace Christ gives ("my peace I give to you"). The mechanism is not willpower alone but will engaging with what has already been given. Second Timothy 1:6 adds the context: "stir up the gift of God that is in you." The transformation from timidity to the spirit of power, love, and self-control is not passive but actively engaged — the person must stir up what God has already placed within them.

The movement between persons is almost entirely absent for this characteristic. Timidity has no giving direction and no receiving direction between persons; it is not instructed or transmitted. What moves between persons is the command against it and the ground that releases it — Jesus commanding the disciples, Paul encouraging Timothy — but the characteristic itself is not a given or received thing. It is a condition of the inner constitution.

Opposition is what this characteristic most directly engages. Matthew 8:26 — "Why are you afraid, O you of little faith?" — shows Jesus naming the disciples' storm panic as timidity, linking it to the failure of trust in his presence. The opposition that produces timidity is specific: the storm, the master's perceived harshness, the threat of persecution (Rev 21:8 context). The characteristic arises as a response to pressure and threat, suppressing the volitional capacity that faithful action requires.

Formation is the characteristic's primary movement, but in a negative direction. What begins as a momentary failure of nerve can accumulate into a settled disposition of cowardly shrinking and ultimately into apostasy. Matthew 25:25–26 shows the buried talent — a single act of fearful self-preservation — producing the master's judgment of the servant as "wicked and slothful." Revelation 21:8 places cowardice alongside idolatry and murder as the failure state of those who apostatise under pressure. The trajectory of unaddressed timidity is not merely personal weakness but full covenant abandonment.

The transformation arc runs from timidity toward the spirit of power, love, and self-control (2Ti 1:7). The mechanism is stirring up the gift — an active engagement with what God has already given, not a passive waiting for sudden transformation. The eschatological stakes are explicit: the negative terminus is Revelation 21:8's judgment on the cowardly; the positive terminus is the company of those who fear God and receive his reward (Rev 11:18; 19:5). The formation arc for timidity is one of the most urgently motivated in the study — the eschatological distance between the coward and the God-fearer is the full distance between the lake of fire and the consummated community of the redeemed.
