The three aspects of volition (capacity, interaction with other characteristics, constraints) are all present: capacity is real, the interaction with moral evaluation and agency is evident, and the constraint is the divine prescription.

**T3.7.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic strongly enables agency in the mediator, and simultaneously bypasses agency in the recipient. The priest is the paradigmatic agent in M38-E: all action, initiative, and effectuation belong to the priestly role (Lev 9:7, 16:17, 16:33, Num 16:46–47, 1Ch 6:49). The atoned-for persons and community are recipients, not agents, of the atonement. This asymmetry is not incidental but structural: the characteristic is defined as Register B precisely because the person does not act for themselves but receives priestly mediation. The enabling of priestly agency and the bypassing of recipient agency are two sides of the same structural feature. There is no instance in the corpus where the atoned-for person's own agency effects the atonement itself.

**T3.8.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic enables moral evaluation by providing a structured framework within which the moral status of persons, spaces, and situations before God can be assessed and addressed. The recognition that bodily discharge, skin disease, childbirth impurity, and corporate transgression all generate real guilt before God (VCG-03, VCG-04) is a moral evaluation built into the system. The self-covering-first sequence (Lev 16:6, 9:7) requires the priest to apply moral evaluation to himself before mediating. However, the characteristic also partially bypasses personal moral evaluation by institutionalizing the verdict: the system defines what constitutes uncleanness and what resolves it, reducing the scope for individual moral assessment. The overall effect is: moral evaluation enabled and structured within prescribed categories, with individual discernment operating within those categories rather than defining them.

**T3.9.2 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic directly addresses and relieves conscience without bypassing it. The annual reset (Lev 16:30 — "the conscience unburdened from the full year's accumulated sin-guilt") enables the conscience to be genuinely cleared, not suppressed. The individual restoration sequences (VCG-03) show that the priest's mediation resolves real guilt-states that have been pressing upon the person's conscience. The provision for the poor (Lev 12:8, 14:21, 14:31) ensures that the conscience-clearing mechanism is accessible regardless of economic status — the inner-being need for resolution is universal. The characteristic does not impair conscience; it takes conscience seriously as a genuine moral reality and provides the mechanism for its resolution. The crisis-intercession cases (VCG-05) show that a community carrying acute conscience-weight (grave sin, divine wrath present) can have that weight addressed through priestly mediation.

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic both requires and deepens conscientiousness in the mediator. The self-covering-first principle demands that the priest be conscientious about his own moral state before proceeding (Lev 16:6, 11). The prescribed sequencing of the Day of Atonement requires sustained conscientious execution across a complex ritual. Aaron's crisis response (Num 16:46–47) and Moses's intercession (Exo 32:30) are expressions of heightened conscientiousness under pressure. The post-exilic community's commitment (Neh 10:33) represents institutionalized conscientiousness. For the recipient, the requirement to present oneself to the priest for atonement (Lev 12:7, 14:18, 15:15) involves a conscientious recognition of one's own state and the action of seeking priestly mediation. The characteristic does not impair conscientiousness; it exercises and deepens it in the mediator and evokes it in the recipient.

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The characteristic enables and deepens relational capacity in multiple directions. For the atoned-for individual, priestly mediation restores the broken relational standing with God (Lev 14:20, 15:15, 16:30 — "standing before God restored"). For the mediator, the characteristic develops and exercises a profound relational solidarity with both God and the people: the high priest must be relationally qualified before God (covered himself) and relationally identified with the community (bearing their standing). The crisis-intercession cases (VCG-05) show the extreme form: Moses and Aaron place themselves in the relational breach, and this relational solidarity is the vehicle through which atonement reaches the community. The characteristic does not impair relational capacity; rather, it is constituted by it.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern reveals that M38-E's characteristic is not primarily perceptive in nature but presupposes a functional perceptive faculty in the mediator. Perception appears only in crisis-intercession contexts (VCG-05), where the urgent recognition of divine wrath is a triggering condition for priestly action. The absence of explicit perceptive engagement in the procedural, institutional forms (VCG-01 through VCG-04) reveals that the characteristic's primary register is volitional and agentive (the priest enacting prescribed procedure) rather than perceptive. The characteristic does not generate perceptual experience; it responds to perceptual reality (divine holiness, communal guilt, active wrath). This reveals that M38-E is a responsive and mediating characteristic rather than a perceptive or revelatory one.

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern of constrained cognitive engagement reveals that M38-E is primarily an executive-procedural characteristic rather than a cognitive one. The priest applies a divinely-given system; he does not construct it. The relatively limited role of open cognition (sequencing, scaling, assessing) within a heavily prescribed framework reveals that the characteristic's power and efficacy does not derive from priestly understanding but from divine ordination. The atonement works because God has commanded it and the priest enacts it, not because the priest has understood it fully. This has a significant implication: the characteristic functions even where cognitive comprehension is incomplete, pointing to its nature as an objective, God-grounded mechanism rather than a cognitively-mediated one.

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The structural engagement of memory (annual cycle, calendar of offerings, covenant commitment) reveals that M38-E is a temporally extended, accumulation-and-reset characteristic rather than a one-time event. Sin accumulates over time in the community's consciousness and before God (Lev 16:16 — "accumulated uncleannesses and transgressions"), and the atonement mechanism addresses this accumulated weight. The characteristic is therefore not merely reactive to immediate, acute guilt but is designed to address the slow build of sin-memory in the community. The perpetual statute of Lev 16:34 reveals that the need for conscience-clearing memory-resolution is a permanent feature of Israel's covenant life, not an exceptional crisis response. This temporally-structured engagement with memory reveals the characteristic as a recurring, systemic provision for ongoing communal guilt-management.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern is instructive: affective engagement is concentrated in the mediator (especially in crisis-intercession, VCG-05) and in the outcome for the community (relief, unburdened conscience, Lev 16:30), but is largely absent from the procedural prescription itself (VCG-01, VCG-02, VCG-04). This reveals that M38-E is not an affectively-constituted characteristic (it does not require emotional experience to function), but that it addresses affective reality (guilt-weight, dread, relief) and, in its crisis form, is powered by affective energy (zeal, solidarity-burden). The distinction between the procedural and crisis forms of the characteristic maps onto a distinction between affect as outcome (all forms) and affect as motivating energy (crisis form only). The characteristic's ground is not affective but structural and divine-initiatory; affect is real but secondary.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** S — The complete absence of creativity-engagement in the M38-E corpus reveals that Characteristic 5 is constitutively non-creative in its structure. The priestly mediation machinery of atonement is entirely received, prescribed, and executed — never originated, imagined, or improvised. This reveals the characteristic as fundamentally heteronomous (rule-given, God-ordained) rather than autonomous or generative. The absence of creative faculty engagement is not incidental but definitional: the system works because it conforms exactly to the divine prescription, not because any human creative contribution has been added to it.

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern of constrained-but-real volitional engagement reveals a fundamental structural feature of M38-E: the characteristic operates within a divine framework that defines the available volitional options but does not eliminate genuine choice. The contrast between the procedural compliance of the Day of Atonement (where volition is exercised in obedience to prescribed sequence) and the crisis intercession of VCG-05 (where volition is exercised with urgency, risk, and uncertain outcome) reveals that the characteristic encompasses a spectrum of volitional register. The self-sacrificial willingness of Moses (Exo 32:30) and Aaron (Num 16:47) reveals that volitional engagement can reach the extreme of self-risking solidarity. This pattern reveals that volition is not suppressed by the priestly mediation system but channeled, and that within that channel it can reach profound depth and costly expression.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The concentrated priestly agency throughout M38-E reveals that this characteristic is structurally asymmetric: it is constituted by a high-agency mediator acting for a low-agency (recipient) community. This asymmetry is not a defect but the defining feature of Register B atonement as distinct from the atoned-for person's own inner-being response (M38-D). The pattern of priest-as-agent/community-as-recipient across all five VCGs reveals that Characteristic 5 is fundamentally a provided, conferred good rather than an achieved or self-generated one. The community cannot atone for itself; it requires an authorized agent to act on its behalf. This structural agency-asymmetry reveals the deepest logic of M38-E: that atonement's efficacy derives from the authorized mediator's action, not from the recipient's activity or inner transformation.

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern of diagnostic moral evaluation (assessing pre-defined categories of uncleanness and guilt) rather than deliberative moral evaluation (open-ended discernment of right and wrong) reveals that M38-E operates within a binary moral structure: clean/unclean, covered/uncovered, atoned/guilty. The characteristic does not generate new moral understanding but applies a given moral taxonomy to persons and situations. This reveals that the characteristic's moral engagement is executive rather than formative — it addresses moral states (guilt, uncleanness) within a pre-given framework rather than developing or expanding moral awareness. The one partial exception is the crisis form (VCG-05), where the moral evaluation of the community's sin-laden state and its peril before God is more acute and personal (Moses's recognition of grave sin, Phinehas's moral clarity that justifies priestly zeal). But even here the moral framework is given; the evaluation is its application.

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The deep and consistent engagement with conscience across all five VCGs reveals that M38-E is, at its core, a conscience-addressing characteristic. Every form of the characteristic — individual restoration from bodily uncleanness (VCG-03), corporate annual reset (VCG-02), regularized institutional provision (VCG-04), crisis intercession (VCG-05), and the self-covering-first sequence (VCG-01) — exists because there is a conscience that carries real guilt before a holy God. The characteristic is the divinely-provided mechanism for resolving that conscience-weight. The fact that the corpus never claims to eliminate the need for conscience-clearing (the annual cycle continues, the provision is perpetual) reveals that conscience is treated as a permanent and active faculty in M38-E's world: it accumulates guilt, it is burdened, and it requires regular clearing. The characteristic does not abolish conscience but addresses it structurally and repeatedly.

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pattern of conscientiousness required from the mediator (self-covering first, precise sequencing, crisis response, institutional sustaining) and evoked from the recipient (presenting oneself for atonement, sustaining the system) reveals that M38-E does not treat the atonement mechanism as passive or automatic. The system requires conscientious human participation at every point — both from the priest who executes it and from the community that sustains it. The failure to be conscientious (failing to provide the offerings: Neh 10:33 implies prior neglect; Hezekiah restoring worship after neglect: 2Ch 29:24) results in a lapse in corporate atonement-standing. This reveals that the characteristic, though divinely originated and priest-mediated, is not self-sustaining: human conscientiousness is the indispensable activation mechanism through which the divinely-given provision is realized.

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-5]** E — The pervasive and structurally central relational engagement throughout M38-E reveals that Characteristic 5 is relational in its essence. The priestly mediation machinery of atonement exists precisely because the relational standing between a holy God and a sin-laden people requires restoration, and because that restoration cannot be achieved by the people directly but requires a relationally-qualified mediator. The three-party relational structure (God — priest — people) is not merely the context for the characteristic but its defining form. The crisis-intercession cases (VCG-05) push this relational logic to its limit: the mediator's solidarity with the endangered people becomes so total that he places himself into the gap between divine wrath and human destruction. This pattern reveals that relational capacity is not incidental to M38-E but is the constitutional medium through which the entire mechanism operates — atonement is essentially a relational transaction enacted through a relationally-capable mediating person.
