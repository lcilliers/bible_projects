**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the evidence shows God operating as the rescuing agent directed toward the endangered human person across multiple VCGs. In MB-VCG-01, Mat 8:25 presents the disciples crying 'Save us' to a divine presence who then acts; Mat 14:30 shows Peter's cry 'Lord, save me' met with immediate divine response — the rescue is from God toward the sinking human. Psa 69:18 (H6299 pa.dah) calls for God to 'draw near and ransom the soul,' framing the divine movement as proximity-then-rescue directed personally at the endangered self. In MB-VCG-03, Heb 5:7 records that Jesus 'prayed with anguished cries to be saved from death and was heard' — the Father's hearing and response is the divine-toward-human vector, even where the subject is the Son. In MB-VCG-04, Heb 11:7's Noah receives divinely-warned deliverance, and Luk 1:71's Benedictus announces God's act of rescue from enemies for a corporate people. The operative mechanism is consistently responsive: God moves toward the human in the moment of mortal threat, and the rescue is enacted by divine agency rather than human capacity.

**T4.2.1 — Divine Interface — Human to God — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the human-to-God movement is the defining interior phenomenon of this characteristic in its purest registers. MB-VCG-01 provides the clearest data: Mat 14:30 (Peter crying 'Lord, save me') and Mat 8:25 (disciples crying 'Save us, we perish') both present the imperilled human turning urgently toward God in the sole available act of the creaturely will — supplication under mortal duress. Psa 69:18 frames this movement as pleading for divine nearness: 'draw near to my soul, redeem it.' MB-VCG-03 deepens this register: Joh 12:27 shows Jesus himself contemplating the prayer 'Father, save me from this hour,' and Heb 5:7 records his 'anguished cries' offered 'with reverence' — the human-to-God movement here is not panic alone but anguished petition combined with deep reverence and volitional surrender. MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7 presents Noah's sustained, obedient faith as the vehicle of the human-toward-God movement — not a single cry but embodied covenantal response over time.

**T4.3.1 — Human Interface — Giving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence for human-to-human giving is present but indirect. MB-VCG-04 provides the clearest instance: Heb 11:7 shows Noah's faith-driven construction of the ark saving his household — the rescue is physically extended by one person (Noah) to those entrusted to his care (his family). The giving operates through sustained, obedient action motivated by reverent faith rather than by direct intervention in another's crisis. Luk 1:71's corporate rescue also implies that the acts of faithful figures (Zechariah prophesying, Zechariah's priestly lineage) carry deliverance toward a wider people. MB-VCG-02's crucifixion verses ironically testify to Jesus having 'saved others' (Mar 15:31, Luk 23:35) — his prior healing and rescue acts constituting a giving of physical deliverance from one person to others. Outside these instances, the characteristic primarily runs along the God-to-human axis; human-to-human physical rescue is peripheral in the verse set.

**T4.4.1 — Human Interface — Receiving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Reception of physical rescue from another person is attested in MB-VCG-04: Noah's household receives the deliverance his faith-action provides; they are 'saved' through his obedience. MB-VCG-02's ironic mockers inadvertently testify that others had previously received physical rescue from Jesus (Mar 15:31 'he saved others'). The primary reception dynamic in the corpus, however, runs from God to the human: in MB-VCG-01, the disciples and Peter receive divine rescue in response to their cry — reception is immediate, bodily (rescued from drowning), and follows the faith-cry as its direct consequence. In MB-VCG-03, Heb 5:7 presents the Son as receiving a hearing from the Father — the rescue sought is received as answered prayer, though resolved through surrender rather than physical escape. Reception, where it appears, operates as the terminus of the faith-cry arc.

**T4.5.1 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows the characteristic operating predominantly within existing relational bonds. MB-VCG-01's storm cries occur within the disciple-Lord relationship; the disciples and Peter appeal to one they already follow. MB-VCG-03's Joh 12:27 and Heb 5:7 depict the Son appealing to the Father — the most intimate possible relational bond. MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7 operates within the household (Noah saving his family) and within the covenantal relationship of faith and divine warning. Luk 1:71's corporate rescue operates for a covenanted people. MB-VCG-02's crucifixion verse (Luk 23:39) is the sole case where the demand for rescue comes from a relational outsider — a criminal who demands rescue 'without repentance or faith.' The contrast between Luk 23:39 (faith absent, unbelief) and Luk 23:40–43 (the penitent criminal) suggests the characteristic is accessible to those outside existing bonds only where faith or genuine petition is present; Luk 23:39 shows it withheld from demands lacking this posture. Evidence for operation across relational difference is sparse and largely negative.

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The dominant basis revealed by the evidence is responsive: God extends physical rescue in direct response to the cry or petition of the endangered person. In Mat 14:30 Peter's cry is immediately answered; in Mat 8:25 the disciples' collective cry precedes the divine act of calming the storm. Psa 69:18 pleads for God to 'draw near' as though divine proximity must be invoked — rescue is responsive to supplication. MB-VCG-03 (Heb 5:7) specifies that Jesus was 'heard because of his reverence' — adding a qualitative condition (reverence, godly fear) to the responsiveness, suggesting the basis is not unconditional but is shaped by the posture of the petitioner. MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7 introduces a covenantal dimension: Noah acts on divine warning within an existing covenant relationship, and the rescue is the covenantal consequence of that obedient faith. MB-VCG-05's Act 27:20 presents the inverse — where no rescue is expected and none is yet extended, hope is abandoned — but Paul's subsequent reassurance (not in the verse set) implies rescue ultimately does come. Across the evidence: the basis is primarily responsive-to-faith-cry, with covenantal dimensions where sustained obedience is in view, and with reverence as a qualifier in the Son's own petition.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence reveals three distinct inner postures, each present in different registers of the sub-group. First, urgent, desperate faith-cry under terror: Mat 8:25 and Mat 14:30 (MB-VCG-01) present the posture as panic-driven but genuinely directed — the cry is to a specific rescuing presence, not mere screaming; faith is exercised even in its most elemental and terrified form. Second, reverent anguish with volitional surrender: Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03) specifies that Jesus was heard 'because of his reverence' (eulabeia) — the posture includes not only desperate petition but deep, reverential submission to the Father's will. Joh 12:27 shows the inner movement of contemplating the cry and then surrendering it: 'for this purpose I came to this hour.' Third, sustained, obedient faith acting over time: Heb 11:7 (MB-VCG-04) presents Noah's inner posture as 'reverent fear' that issues in embodied covenantal obedience — a prolonged rather than instantaneous posture. The contrast with Luk 23:39 (faith absent, demanding without reverence or repentance) implies that the absence of faith-posture is what renders the cry ineffective or unfruitful.

**T4.3.2 — Human Interface — Giving — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence for human-to-human giving is limited (primarily MB-VCG-04), but it is sufficient to identify enabling inner conditions. In Heb 11:7, Noah's giving of rescue to his household is enabled by 'reverent faith' (eulabeia + pistis) that receives divine warning as credible and acts upon it over time. The inner condition is not courage per se but obedient reverence — a faith that takes the divine word seriously enough to enact it embodied, structurally, and persistently (building the ark). The mockers of MB-VCG-02 ironically testify that Jesus's prior giving of rescue to others ('he saved others') flowed from a posture that included willingness to expend himself for others while refusing self-preservation — 'self withheld' and 'will surrendered' as inner orientations that enabled the giving. Direct evidence for human-to-human giving conditions is thin; the corpus primarily depicts God as the giver, with Noah as the single extended human-giving case.

**T4.4.2 — Human Interface — Receiving — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence is most explicit on blocking conditions. Luk 23:39 (MB-VCG-02) presents a criminal who demands physical rescue 'without repentance or faith' — the keywords 'faith absent' and 'unbelief' mark this as blocked reception. The contrast with the penitent criminal implies that a posture of genuine acknowledgement and trust is what enables reception. In MB-VCG-04, Noah's household receives rescue by being within the orbit of the faith-actor; no inner condition of their own is explicitly described — the evidence is silent on whether they themselves exercise faith or simply benefit from Noah's. In MB-VCG-01, Peter and the disciples receive divine rescue through the very act of crying out — the enabling condition is the faith-cry itself, however terrified. MB-VCG-05's Act 27:20 presents total abandonment of hope as the state in which rescue is neither sought nor received; whether the crew later received rescue is outside the verse set. Reception appears enabled by: directed cry to the rescuing presence, presence within the faith-actor's protective action, or genuine (even terrified) petition. Blocked by: demanding without faith, unbelief, or collapse into hopeless resignation without renewed cry.

**T4.5.2 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence suggests the characteristic primarily but not exclusively operates within covenantal contexts. MB-VCG-01 (disciples, Peter) and MB-VCG-03 (Jesus's own petition) are embedded within established covenantal-relational bonds. MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7 and Luk 1:71 are explicitly covenantal. Similarly, Mat 8:25's storm threatens the whole boat including Jesus; the rescue extends to all present. MB-VCG-02's crucifixion verses involve mockers and soldiers entirely outside covenant relationship. The evidence does not show the characteristic restricted to covenant insiders in its physical-rescue dimension; its availability to non-covenanted persons in extremis is at least implicit in Act 27:20 and the storm narratives.

**T4.1.3 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence reveals God's disposition as one of proximate, responsive attentiveness to the endangered human. Across the evidence, the divine disposition is: responsive to the faith-cry, near in crisis, personally attentive to posture, and willing to warn as an act of protective provision.

**T5.1.1 — Nature of Transformation — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows transformation operating primarily at the level of condition rather than orientation, but with orientation-change present in the higher-register cases. In MB-VCG-01, Mat 14:30 and Mat 8:25 present rescue that changes the person's physical condition — Peter is no longer sinking; the disciples are no longer perishing. The transformation is objective and external: rescued from mortal danger. In MB-VCG-03, however, Joh 12:27 and Heb 5:7 reveal a more complex transformation: Jesus's prayer for rescue from 'this hour' is resolved not by condition-change (he does die) but by orientation-change — the soul troubled by the prospect of suffering moves to embrace its redemptive purpose ('for this purpose I came to this hour'). Heb 5:7's 'heard because of his reverence' implies that the hearing produces not condition-escape but inner resolution through surrender. In MB-VCG-04, Heb 11:7's Noah undergoes no recorded transformation of inner orientation; the transformation is the household's physical survival. The evidence thus shows: condition-change in the panic-cry and household-rescue registers; orientation-change (or orientation-deepening) in the Son's own anguished petition register.

**T5.2.1 — Sequence of Inner States — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the evidence describes a clear before-during-after sequence, most explicitly in MB-VCG-01 and MB-VCG-03. The sequence in MB-VCG-01 (Mat 14:30 especially): Before — bold venture (Peter steps onto the water), normal existence disrupted by wind and waves; During — terror and beginning to sink, the faith-cry; After — rescue enacted, and in the broader Matthean account, awe and worship ('truly you are the Son of God,' Mat 14:33). The 'before' is ordinary existence or bold venture; the 'during' is the mortal-threat state including fear and cry; the 'after' is rescue and its aftermath. MB-VCG-03 (Joh 12:27): Before — arrival at the hour of suffering, soul 'troubled'; During — contemplating the cry for rescue, then the volitional surrender ('for this purpose I came'); After — the Father's voice, resolution. MB-VCG-05 (Act 27:20): the sequence is partial — before: voyage and storm building; during: all hope abandoned, inner despair; after: outside the verse, rescue comes. MB-VCG-04 (Heb 11:7): Before — divine warning received; During — reverent faith building the ark; After — household saved, Noah becomes heir of righteousness. The sequence is well-attested.

**T5.3.1 — Mechanism of Change — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence identifies two distinct mechanisms operating in different registers. First, sudden encounter: in MB-VCG-01, the mechanism is immediate — the crisis arrives suddenly (wind, waves, sinking), the cry is uttered in a single moment, and the rescue is enacted in direct response. The change (from perishing to rescued) is sudden and event-driven. This is the dominant mechanism in the panic-cry register. Second, gradual formation through obedient faith-action: in MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7, the mechanism is extended over time — Noah receives warning, and then builds the ark over a prolonged period, with reverent faith as the sustained inner orientation producing eventual deliverance. The change is accumulative rather than instantaneous. A third mechanism is present in MB-VCG-03: anguished petition resolved through volitional surrender — not sudden rescue but deepening of inner orientation through the act of bringing anguish before the Father and finding it heard and resolved through embrace of purpose. This is an interior transformation-through-encounter mechanism distinct from both sudden rescue and gradual formation.

**T5.4.1 — Suffering and Affliction — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Suffering and mortal affliction is the constitutive context of this entire characteristic — it is the defining precondition, not an occasional feature. Suffering is thus simultaneously context, occasion, and revealer of the faith-cry's depth.

**T5.1.2 — Nature of Transformation — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence is mixed and register-dependent. In the physical rescue register (MB-VCG-01), the rescue from drowning is a reversible-class transformation: Peter is rescued from the immediate danger of sinking, but the mortal frailty that made him sinkable remains. The rescue is real but does not permanently alter the person's susceptibility to danger. Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05) similarly concerns a temporal, physical rescue that, even if it had occurred and hope been maintained, would not permanently secure the persons against future mortal threats. In MB-VCG-04's Heb 11:7, Noah's household is saved from the flood — an irreversible event in that that specific destruction does not recur — but again, the persons remain mortal. The one case with an irreversibility marker is MB-VCG-04's statement that Noah 'became heir of the righteousness that comes by faith' — this inner-being consequence of the faith-rescue arc has a character of enduring deposit rather than mere event. In MB-VCG-03, the Son's volitional surrender (Joh 12:27) and the 'learning obedience' trajectory (Heb 5:7 in context) point toward an irreversible inner transformation — perfection through suffering — that is not undone. The evidence does not present a single answer: physical rescue is event-level and potentially reversible in kind; inner transformation in the surrender/formation registers has irreversible markers.

**T5.2.2 — Sequence of Inner States — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Drawing from T5.2.1 findings, the sequence of inner states most fully documented in the corpus is: (1) Ordinary existence or bold venture — the person is engaged with life as normal, or has stepped out in active faith (Mat 14:28–29 context for Peter; Noah's pre-warning state; Jesus's movement toward the hour). (2) Disruption by mortal threat — the threat breaks in (wind and waves, storm, the cross, enemies) and the inner being is seized by fear, terror, trouble, or despair ('soul troubled,' Joh 12:27; 'fear,' Mat 8:25/14:30; 'hope abandoned,' Act 27:20). (3) The crisis-point inner act — either the faith-cry ('Lord, save me,' Mat 14:30; 'Save us,' Mat 8:25; 'save me from this hour,' Joh 12:27 contemplated; 'anguished cries,' Heb 5:7) or, in the negative case, the collapse into hopeless resignation (Act 27:20) or faithless demand (Luk 23:39). (4) Resolution — rescue (Mat 14:30 aftermath; Noah's household; Luk 1:71), or volitional surrender with inner resolution (Joh 12:27 'for this purpose I came'), or continued despair without resolution yet (Act 27:20). What the sequence reveals: the characteristic works by bringing the endangered inner being to the crisis-point where the only available resource is the faith-cry, and the cry is the decisive inner act that either releases rescue or, in the higher register, transforms the inner being's relationship to the suffering itself.

**T5.3.2 — Mechanism of Change — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the evidence clearly distinguishes three mechanisms operating in different contexts (see T5.3.1): (1) Sudden-encounter rescue in the panic-cry context (MB-VCG-01: Mat 8:25, Mat 14:30): the crisis arrives without warning, the cry is instantaneous, and rescue is immediate. The mechanism is event-rupture → cry → divine act. No gradual formation is in view; the change is punctiliar. (2) Gradual-formation through sustained obedient faith in the household-rescue context (MB-VCG-04: Heb 11:7): the mechanism is divine warning → reverent reception → extended embodied obedience → rescue as culmination. The time-scale is long; the inner-being formation is cumulative. (3) Anguished-petition-and-surrender in the Son's own crisis context (MB-VCG-03: Joh 12:27, Heb 5:7): the mechanism is mortal-threat awareness → contemplation of rescue-cry → volitional reorientation → heard petition resolved through embrace of purpose. This is neither purely sudden nor purely gradual; it is a deliberate interior act of surrender in the face of known suffering. MB-VCG-05 (Act 27:20) presents a fourth degenerate case: no mechanism of change is enacted; despair without cry, and hope's extinction is the state recorded.

**T5.4.2 — Suffering and Affliction — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows suffering functioning in multiple roles relative to this characteristic: Revealing: Mat 14:30's panic and cry reveals the depth (and the limit) of Peter's faith — he steps out boldly but sinks when the wind is felt; the suffering-context of the waves reveals what was previously untested. Testing: the storm in Mat 8:25 tests the disciples' faith to its limit — they resort to the cry, which is itself a test-outcome revealing that even terror-driven faith-cry suffices. Producing (deepening): Heb 5:7 presents the Son's anguished cries as the condition through which his reverence and obedience were expressed and through which (in the Heb 5:8–9 context at the boundary of the verse set) perfection was produced. Suffering does not merely elicit the cry; it is the instrument through which the inner-being quality is forged. In Heb 11:7's Noah, the threat of destruction (revealed suffering) produces the sustained faith-action that constitutes the characteristic's deepest expression in that register. Act 27:20 presents a negative case: suffering at sufficient extremity produces hope's extinction rather than deepening of the faith-cry — suggesting that suffering can either deepen or overwhelm, depending on whether the inner cry is sustained.
