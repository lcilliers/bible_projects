**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics converge on a single, composite portrait of God: the one whose inner nature is constitutively oriented toward the rescue, covering, and restoration of the creature. This convergence is not merely thematic repetition but a structural finding — each characteristic approaches the same divine reality from a different angle, and together they triangulate a character that cannot be reduced to any single attribute. The dominant terms emerging across characteristics are: mercy (M38-A, M38-D), responsiveness to the creature's cry (M38-B, M38-C), initiative that precedes human action (M38-D, M38-E, M38-G), warrior-zeal sustaining the saving commitment (M38-F), and covenantal love that grounds ransoming in relational fidelity rather than merit (M38-G, M38-A).

A significant convergence appears across M38-A, M38-D, and M38-G: in all three, the operative divine attribute is explicitly not justice-requiring-merit but mercy, compassion, or love as the originating source of the saving act. Tit 3:5, Psa 78:38, and Deu 7:8 form a triangulated cluster — mercy, compassion-restrained-wrath, and freely given covenantal love — that establishes this as a structural divine-character claim, not merely a local textual note.

A significant divergence appears between M38-B and M38-F on one hand and M38-E on the other. M38-B and M38-C present God as responsive — drawing near when cried to, naming faith as the operative cause of healing. M38-E presents God as the designer of a system, initiating the machinery before the human creature has yet presented a specific need. These two modes (responsive and preemptive) are not contradictory but are genuinely distinct divine-character dimensions: God who responds to the cry and God who has already provided the mechanism before the cry is made.

The most structurally unexpected finding emerges from M38-F: God does not merely give salvation but is himself clothed in it, restless until it is complete (Isa 59:17; Isa 62:1; Hab 3:8). This moves the divine-character portrait beyond benevolent dispenser to committed warrior whose zeal for salvation is identity-constitutive. No other characteristic reaches this depth of divine self-involvement. M38-G adds a further dimension from the same angle: God ransoms to "make himself a name" (2Sa 7:23) — the divine character is disclosed and constituted by the ransoming act itself, not merely expressed through it.

Across all seven, the silence about God being rescued, atoned-for, or exercising faith toward another reveals the asymmetric shape of the portrait: God's nature is wholly on the giving, initiating, covering, and completing side of every salvific transaction.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across the seven characteristics, the verse evidence collectively identifies a coherent cluster of purposes that salvation-related inner-being capacities equip the human person to fulfill, even where individual characteristics are primarily responses to the fallen condition. These purposes are not identical across characteristics but form a pattern with several convergent axes and several structural divergences.

The strongest convergence is across M38-A, M38-C, M38-D, and M38-F: each equips the person to stand in full relational access to God — as one who reigns in life (Rom 5:17), receives integrated wholeness (Mar 10:52), is accepted in the divine presence (Lev 1:4), and orients the whole self toward the not-yet while remaining morally active in the present (Isa 56:1; 1Th 5:8). The common thread is that these characteristics equip the person for a quality of standing before God that is simultaneously receptive, active, and directional.

A second convergence appears across M38-B, M38-C, and M38-G: all three equip the person for *other-oriented* action flowing from their own experience of rescue. M38-B shows Noah's faith-under-threat protecting those in his household (Heb 11:7); M38-C shows Bartimaeus moving into discipleship after healing (Mar 10:52); M38-G shows ransomed memory generating the will to treat the vulnerable justly (Deu 15:15; 24:18) and to intercede for others (Deu 9:26). Rescue received does not terminate in the self but becomes the source of vicarious and ethical action.

The sharpest structural divergence is between M38-E and the remaining six. M38-E's created-purpose finding focuses on the mediatorial vocation — equipping a person to stand between holy God and guilty community in solidarity-burden, sequential ritual order, and wrath-intercepting intercession. This is a vocation-specific equipping, not a generic creaturely one. The six other characteristics describe inner-being faculties (faith-reception, hope-sustaining, gratitude-memory) that in principle belong to every human person; M38-E describes a structural role assigned to specific persons within the covenant community. This asymmetry — universal inner-being equipping versus specific vocational equipping — is the cluster's most significant divergence.

M38-F adds the dimension absent from all others: the characteristic equips the person to *endure the gap* between promise and fulfillment without collapsing (Rom 8:24). Other characteristics equip for reception, action, standing, or mediation; M38-F uniquely equips for sustained forward-orientation under non-arrival — a capacity for temporal perseverance that is structurally distinct from all others.

**T0.3.1 — Image-Bearer Expression — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The seven characteristics together instantiate at least four distinct dimensions of the divine image, and the cluster finding is that these dimensions are not uniform across characteristics but fall into genuinely different image-bearing modes.

The dominant convergence is the *relational-receptive* dimension: M38-A, M38-B, M38-C, and M38-F all locate the primary image-bearing expression in the human person's capacity to orient the self toward God in trust, petition, or hope. This is not a passive capacity but structured interior activity — forming interior conviction (M38-C, Mat 9:21), crying out under mortal threat (M38-B, Mat 14:30), sustaining forward-orientation toward a not-yet salvation (M38-F, Rom 8:24). Across these four, the image is expressed in the act of receptive, directional address toward God — something no sub-personal creature performs.

A distinct second dimension emerges from M38-D and M38-G: the *moral-relational* dimension of the image, expressed in guilt-awareness, contrition, and covenantally responsive memory. M38-D locates the image-bearing expression in the complex moral-emotional interiority of the atoned-for person — capable of shame, wordlessness, and remembered guilt alongside forgiveness (Eze 16:63). M38-G locates it in the capacity to receive a relational history as constitutive of the self, and to orient will and ethical treatment of others from ransomed identity. These two characteristics share the image-dimension of *moral responsiveness to God's action*.

The third dimension is *vicarious-representative* and belongs specifically to M38-E: the image expressed in the capacity to stand between holy God and guilty community, bearing the weight of others' standing before God. This is the most distinctive image-dimension in the cluster — it does not appear in any other characteristic in this form.

The structural asymmetry the cluster must name is this: M38-E, M38-D, and M38-G all contain explicit findings that these characteristics are *not* direct reflections of something God possesses in the same mode. God is not atoned-for, not the ransomed, not the one requiring a mediator. Their image-bearing expression therefore operates in the *response-and-reception* register of the image rather than the *mirroring* register. By contrast, M38-A, M38-B, M38-C, and M38-F each instantiate the image in a mode that mirrors — however asymmetrically — a divine attribute or divine posture (responsive salvific power, zeal for rescue, hearing the cry, warrior-commitment to completion). The cluster thus partitions: four characteristics mirror divine attributes in creaturely form; three characteristics express image-bearing in the distinctively creaturely register of reception, need, and response.

**T0.4.1 — Typological Significance — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics evidence typological deployment. This is one of the cluster's most striking convergence findings: no characteristic in M38 Salvation functions only at the level of the immediate. Every characteristic is pressed by the scriptural evidence into service as a pointer, a participation in, or an anticipation of a reality beyond itself. However, the *registers* of typological deployment differ markedly across characteristics, and this differentiation is the cluster's primary analytical contribution.

M38-A and M38-F share the widest typological range: both deploy OT rescue events as covenant-warning types (Jude 5 in M38-A), prophetic-to-christological types (1Pe 1:10 in M38-F), and present-salvation-as-anticipation-of-eschatological-completion (Rom 5:9 in M38-A; Heb 9:28 in M38-F). Their typological structure is multi-layered and operates simultaneously in covenantal, christological, and eschatological registers.

M38-D and M38-E share a more concentrated typological function: both operate primarily in the OT-to-christological register, with the Levitical atonement machinery (M38-D's VCG-01 and -02; M38-E's entire evidence base) pointing forward to Christ as the definitive hilastērios, the once-for-all high priest. Their typological freight is heavy and explicit in the NT (Rom 3:25; Heb 2:17; 1Jo 2:2) but the OT texts themselves carry it primarily through internal incompleteness — the annual reset, the mediator's own sinfulness requiring coverage, the sanctuary still defiled year after year.

M38-B and M38-C evidence typological use that is more concentrated and more ironic. M38-B's most distinctive deployment is the crucifixion mockery sequence, where the physical-rescue vocabulary is pressed into inverse service — the refusal of self-rescue enacts the rescue of others. This is typology in its most paradoxical form. M38-C's clearest typological moment is 1Pe 3:21 (Noahic rescue → baptism-as-conscience-appeal), which is explicit in the text.

M38-G's typological structure is foundational-to-progressive: the Exodus ransom functions as the originating type against which all subsequent ransom instances are calibrated, with the eschatological instances (Isa 35:10; Hos 13:14) representing the type's ultimate fulfillment rather than a new typological direction.

The cluster's integrative finding: across all seven characteristics, the Exodus event functions as a persistent typological reference point — appearing in M38-A (Jude 5), M38-B (Luk 1:71), M38-D (Levitical system presupposing Sinai covenant), M38-E (same), and M38-G (Deu 7:8; 9:26; Mic 6:4). The Exodus is the cluster's shared typological anchor.

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — A decisive structural partition runs through this question across the seven characteristics, and naming it is the cluster's primary task. The partition is between characteristics that Scripture *does* explicitly attribute to God and those it does not — and the theological significance of each side of the partition differs fundamentally.

Characteristics explicitly attributed to God: M38-A (salvation as God's act and desire: Rev 7:10; 1Ti 2:4; Tit 3:5), M38-D (atonement-giving as divine act: Psa 65:3; Psa 78:38; Lev 17:11; 1Jo 4:10), and M38-F (salvation as divine warrior-clothing and restless commitment: Isa 59:17; Isa 62:1; Hab 3:8). For all three, the human characteristic's significance is enhanced by the attribution: because God saves, desires salvation, gives atonement, and drives toward salvific completion, the human person's reception of these things is not merely fortunate but is participation in what God himself is and does.

Characteristics where the *rescue-capacity* is attributed to God but the *experiential phenomenology* is not: M38-B and M38-C. God hears, extends his hand, responds — but does not himself cry in panic-terror (M38-B) or exercise faith as a human inner faculty (M38-C). Here the attribution is asymmetric: the divine *response-side* is clearly attributable to God; the creaturely *exercise-side* is not. This asymmetry is informative: it locates the faith-cry and faith-exercise as specifically creaturely modes of engaging the divine responsiveness that is God's own.

Characteristics where explicit attribution is absent or structurally impossible: M38-E (priestly mediation machinery — God designs it but does not himself undergo it), M38-G (ransomed identity — God is the ransomer, not the ransomed). For M38-E and M38-G, the absence reveals that these characteristics exist in a uniquely creaturely register: they are not God's attributes analogized downward to the creature, but the creature's responses to and instruments of what God does. M38-G adds the distinctive finding that God ransoms *to make himself a name* — meaning the human characteristic of ransomed gratitude exists as the creaturely reception of an act that is also constitutive of divine self-disclosure.

Across all seven, the consistent finding is that God's side of each transaction is always present and primary; the human characteristic always exists in dependent relation to it. But the *form* of that dependence varies: participation (M38-A, M38-D, M38-F), creaturely-analogue (M38-B, M38-C), creaturely-instrument (M38-E), creaturely-response (M38-G).

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster finding across all seven characteristics is a consistent pattern of *dual structure*: none of the characteristics is purely original-design, none is purely fallen-condition response, but the *proportion* of each element and the *nature* of the duality differ significantly across characteristics.

The strongest original-design component is found in M38-C and M38-F. M38-C locates the *structural capacity* for interior trust directed toward God (the image-bearing relational-receptive faculty beneath the specific faith-exercise) in original design, even though the conditions that occasion its exercise (disease, blindness, death-threat) are fall-markers. M38-F similarly separates the *capacity* for transcendent forward-orientation from the specific *form* of longing-under-deprivation that is the fallen expression: the created person is equipped to orient the whole self toward a future fullness not yet received; the anguished groaning that salvation is far off belongs to the fallen condition.

The strongest fallen-condition weighting appears in M38-D and M38-E. M38-D's entire operative apparatus — sin offerings, guilt offerings, blood sacrifice, conscience burdened by transgression — presupposes sin's reality. M38-E's priestly mediation machinery presupposes a gap between holy God and sinful humanity that would not exist in an unfallen creation. Both characteristics are responses to the fallen condition in their operative form, though M38-E raises the possibility that the *vocation-capacity* for carrying others' burden in solidarity may be a latent created capacity.

M38-A and M38-G share a distinctive structure: the *divine intention* behind the characteristic is pre-temporal (M38-A: 2Ti 1:9, grace purposed before time; M38-G: God's ransoming identity is constitutive of his self-disclosure), while the *operative form* the characteristic takes in human experience is fully conditioned by the fall. Both point toward a grounding in the eternal divine will that precedes and exceeds the fallen condition, even as the human experience of it belongs entirely to it.

M38-B is the most exclusively fallen-condition characteristic in the cluster: no verse addresses a pre-fall state, and every instance — storm, crucifixion, plague, flood-judgment — is located in a world of threat and death that is post-fall in its conditions. The possible created-design residue is the *relational-vocative capacity* (the ability to address God under extremity), but the characteristic as exercised has no pre-fall analogue in this evidence.

The cluster's integrative finding: original-design *capacity* underlies all seven characteristics; fallen-condition *occasion* is the context for their operative expression in five of seven (M38-A, M38-B, M38-D, M38-E, M38-G). The exceptions are M38-C and M38-F, where the created capacity has a more visible structural independence from the fallen occasion.

**T0.3.2 — Image-Bearer Expression — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The seven characteristics distribute across a spectrum from *partial sharing* to *pure creaturely analogue*, and the cluster finding is that this spectrum is itself theologically structured rather than random.

The strongest case for partial sharing is M38-F: God wears salvation as armor (Isa 59:17), rides toward it militantly (Hab 3:8), and will not rest until it blazes forth (Isa 62:1). The same salvation-orientation characterizes both the divine actor and the human waiter, though in fundamentally different modes — God drives salvation; the creature awaits what God is bringing. This is the only characteristic in the cluster where the sharing is real and the difference is modal rather than categorical. M38-A approaches this: God saves, and the human person participates instrumentally in extending salvation to others (1Cor 9:22; Rom 11:14; Jam 5:20), though the primary salvific act remains exclusively divine.

The middle range — *asymmetric partial sharing* — includes M38-B and M38-C. In both, the divine *responsiveness* side is clearly God's (hearing the cry, naming the faith, extending the hand), while the creaturely *exercise* side (the panic-petition, the interior trust-formation) belongs exclusively to the creature. The sharing is therefore asymmetric: God and human are both involved, but on different sides of the transaction, and neither side is interchangeable with the other.

The strongest cases for *pure creaturely analogue* are M38-D, M38-E, and M38-G. God does not receive atonement (M38-D); God does not undergo the priestly mediation machinery, does not require coverage before mediating (M38-E); God is not the ransomed, does not possess ransomed identity gratitude (M38-G). In all three, the characteristic is exclusively creaturely — but it corresponds to a divine attribute from which it flows: God's compassion-atonement-giving flows into the human reception of conscience-cleansing (M38-D); God's design-and-authorization of the priestly system flows into the creature's mediatorial vocation (M38-E); God's ransoming covenantal fidelity flows into the creature's ransomed-identity gratitude (M38-G).

The cluster's integrative finding: the spectrum runs from modal-sharing (M38-F) through instrumental-participation (M38-A) through asymmetric transaction (M38-B, M38-C) to pure creaturely response-to-divine-action (M38-D, M38-E, M38-G). The dividing line is whether the human person's inner-being posture has any analogue on the divine side: for M38-F and M38-A it does; for M38-D, M38-E, and M38-G it does not. M38-B and M38-C occupy the intermediate position where the divine *responsiveness* is shared but the creaturely *need* is not.

**T0.4.2 — Typological Significance — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The dominant typological direction across all seven characteristics is from the divine instance downward to establish the pattern for the human — but the cluster contains two significant counter-currents and one genuinely bidirectional characteristic, which together produce the cluster's most nuanced finding.

The clearest *divine-to-human* direction appears in M38-C, M38-D, M38-G, and M38-F (primary register). In M38-C, the Noahic rescue (divine act) establishes the type; baptism-as-conscience-appeal is the antitype. In M38-D, Lev 17:11's divine provision of the blood-mechanism is the originating pattern; the Levitical priestly instances and the christological fulfillment both follow from it. In M38-G, the Exodus ransom (divine act) is the foundational type; all subsequent human experiences of ransomed identity are calibrated against it and flow from it. In M38-F, God's warrior armor of salvation (Isa 59:17) is the pattern; the believer's helmet of hope-of-salvation (1Th 5:8) is the derivative human form.

The strongest *human-instance-pointing-upward* movement appears in M38-E and M38-F (secondary register). M38-E is structurally distinctive: God designs and commands the human priestly system (Lev 8:34; 16:34), but the system itself — with its internal incompleteness, annual repetition, and mediator's own sinfulness — points forward and upward to a greater high priest. Here the human instance is the type pointing toward the divine-human antitype, rather than the divine instance establishing the human pattern. This makes M38-E the clearest case of upward-pointing typology in the cluster. M38-F's secondary register — prophetic human hope pointing toward the incarnate Savior's appearing (1Pe 1:10) — shares this upward direction.

M38-B is the genuinely bidirectional characteristic: the crucifixion mockery sequence (MB-VCG-02) presses the human rescue vocabulary into service as an ironic pointer toward the divine redemptive act (human pointing upward); but Heb 5:7's presentation of the Son's anguished petition as the archetype of creaturely prayer establishes the divine-human instance as the pattern for human petitionary prayer (divine establishing the human pattern).

M38-A is primarily divine-to-human (covenant promise fulfilled in Christ received by human faith), with a secondary human-to-eschatological movement where present salvific experience is itself an anticipatory type of final rescue.

The cluster's integrative finding: the divine-to-human direction is dominant but not universal. M38-E represents a genuine exception where God establishes a human institution that is itself typologically incomplete and thus points upward. The cluster's typological structure is therefore not simply hierarchical descent but contains an ascending counter-current, particularly concentrated in the priestly-mediation and prophetic-hope characteristics.

**T0.1.3 — Divine Nature Reflected — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The seven characteristics produce two structurally distinct categories of silence, and the cluster finding is that these categories carry different theological freight.

The *theologically informative silence* — where the absence of divine attribution directly marks the creature's creaturely particularity — appears most strongly in M38-B, M38-D, M38-E, and M38-G. God does not cry in panic-terror under mortal threat (M38-B); God is not atoned-for (M38-D); God does not require coverage before mediating (M38-E); God does not hold ransomed identity gratitude (M38-G). In all four cases, the silence marks the *boundary* of the image: the characteristic belongs to the image-bearer as image-bearer, precisely because the image-bearer is not the original. The creature's creaturely position — finite, guilty, threatened, dependent, rescued — generates inner-being states that God by nature cannot possess. These silences are not deficiencies but the proper shape of the creaturely image.

The *partial silence* — where God *is* attributed a related but not identical characteristic — appears in M38-A, M38-C, and M38-F. God saves but does not receive salvation by faith (M38-A). God is faithfully responsive but does not exercise faith as an inner faculty (M38-C). God is clothed in salvation and drives toward it, but does not wait for it in unfulfilled longing (M38-F). In these three cases, the silence is not a categorical absence but a modal one: the divine side of the characteristic exists, but the specifically creaturely *form* of it (faith-as-reception, faith-as-trust-exercise, hope-as-longing-under-deprivation) belongs exclusively to the creature. The silence marks not a boundary but a register difference — the same reality (saving, wholeness, salvation-orientation) exists in both God and creature but in irreducibly different modes.

The cluster's integrative finding: no characteristic in M38 is entirely absent from divine attribution — every silence is a silence about a specific *creaturely mode* of a divine reality, not about the reality itself. This is theologically significant: it suggests that the characteristics of this cluster instantiate the divine image not by copying divine attributes directly but by enacting creaturely-appropriate forms of realities that in God exist in uncreated, non-dependent forms. The image-bearer expresses what God is — saving, covering, rescuing, orienting toward wholeness — through the specifically creaturely modes of faith, need, dependence, petition, gratitude, and mediation. The silences collectively define the shape of the creature as distinct from but genuinely reflective of the Creator.

**T0.2.3 — Created Purpose — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The forward-orientation finding is the most uniformly positive across all seven characteristics: every characteristic in M38 Salvation is evidenced as oriented toward a future fullness that exceeds present possession. This is the strongest convergence finding in the entire T0 tier and merits precise articulation of both the convergence and the divergences within it.

The convergence is structurally grounded in the nature of salvation itself: because the cluster's central subject is rescue from death, guilt, and condemnation toward life, standing, and wholeness, every characteristic participates in a trajectory that by definition reaches beyond its present form. Rom 8:24 (M38-F) states this most nakedly: "saved in hope" — the present is already salvific but not yet complete. This structure appears, in varying degrees of explicitness, in M38-A (justification present, salvation from wrath future: Rom 5:9), M38-D (Dan 9:24's eschatological atonement-for-iniquity culminating in everlasting righteousness), M38-E (the annual reset pointing toward a definitive non-repeated atonement), M38-C (Bartimaeus following Jesus "on the way" — eschatological discipleship direction), M38-B (Noah's physical rescue oriented toward inherited righteousness: Heb 11:7), and M38-G (the ransomed returning with "everlasting joy": Isa 35:10; 51:11).

The divergence lies in the *nature* of the future fullness toward which each characteristic is oriented. For M38-A and M38-F, the future fullness is explicitly eschatological in the temporal sense — a coming completion of what is already present in part (final salvation from wrath; the appearing of the Savior; the full harvest of righteousness). For M38-D and M38-E, the future fullness is christological — the once-for-all atonement that fulfills and supersedes the repeated OT pattern. For M38-B and M38-C, the future fullness is simultaneously christological (discipleship after healing) and eschatological (inherited righteousness). For M38-G, the future fullness is both cosmic and eschatological — ransom from Death and Sheol themselves (Hos 13:14; Psa 49:15), not merely from historical bondage.

The cluster's most significant structural observation: M38-E's forward-orientation is unique in being primarily indicated by internal *inadequacy* — the annual reset, the mediator's own sinfulness, the sanctuary still being defiled — rather than by explicit eschatological promise. Its forward-pointing is figured in incompleteness, not in explicit promise. Every other characteristic's forward-orientation is indicated by explicit eschatological or christological language. This makes M38-E's future-orientation the most implicit in the cluster, carried by the logic of the system rather than by its direct assertions.

**T0.3.3 — Image-Bearer Expression — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The seven characteristics collectively map the condition of the divine image through both its functioning presence and its damaged or distorted absence, and the cluster finding is that the negative cases are as theologically coherent as the positive ones — they form a consistent diagnostic pattern.

The convergence on the positive side: across M38-A, M38-C, M38-D, M38-F, and M38-G, the presence of the characteristic signals an image functioning in its proper relational orientation — toward God, in dependence, trust, and receptivity. The person who receives salvation by faith (M38-A), exercises interior trust toward God for wholeness (M38-C), receives conscience-cleansing atonement (M38-D), sustains hope toward the not-yet-arrived salvation (M38-F), and holds ransomed identity as the anchor of self-understanding (M38-G) is a person whose image-bearing relational faculty is directed toward its proper object and functioning in its proper mode.

The convergence on the negative side is equally revealing. The absence of each characteristic produces a consistent pattern of *misdirected* or *collapsed* relational orientation: deliberate closure of the will against truth (2Th 2:10 in M38-A), hope extinguished without cry toward God (Act 27:20 in M38-B), inner-being fragmentation remaining in un-integrated bodily-spiritual disorder (M38-C), conscience burdened and permanently unrelieved with no access to covering (M38-D), self-rescue striving that produces nothing (Isa 26:18 in M38-F), forgetting the day of redemption and losing covenantal self-understanding (Psa 78:42 in M38-G). In every case the absence indicates not merely the lack of a capacity but a positive disorder — the image-bearer oriented away from God, collapsed inward, or grasping at self-sufficiency.

The divergence within this pattern: M38-B and M38-E reveal the condition of the image in a different diagnostic register. M38-B's diagnostic is primarily *relational-vocative*: the presence of the faith-cry under mortal threat signals an image still capable of dependent address to God; its replacement by faithless demand (Luk 23:39) or silent despair reveals relational-vocative collapse. M38-E's diagnostic is *vocational*: the priestly mediator who functions in proper sequence — self-covered before interceding for others — instantiates the image in its assigned role; the mediator who presumes to act for others without self-coverage represents the image distorted in its specific vocation. This vocational diagnostic is unique to M38-E and has no parallel in the other six characteristics.

The cluster's integrative finding: across all seven characteristics, the divine image is not a static property but a dynamic relational orientation. Its condition is revealed by the direction of the inner being's attention, dependence, and receptivity — toward God (image intact) or away from God toward self-rescue, self-sufficiency, or mute despair (image disordered). The cluster's characteristics thus function collectively as diagnostic instruments for the inner-being's relational orientation toward its Creator-Redeemer.

**T0.4.3 — Typological Significance — If no typological use is evidenced, note this explicitly.**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics evidence typological deployment. The cluster finding for this prompt is therefore not a silence-note but a convergence-observation of structural significance: in the M38 Salvation cluster, typological use is universal across characteristics. This universality is itself a finding about the cluster's nature — salvation is not merely a flat datum but a category that Scripture consistently deploys as pointing, as anticipating, as participating in realities beyond itself.

The absence of any silence in this prompt is meaningful because it stands in contrast to what might have been expected from M38-B and M38-G, where several individual characteristic findings approached an equivocal position. M38-B's physical rescue narratives (storm-cry, crucifixion mockery) might have been read as immediate and non-typological, but the evidence reveals that even these are pressed into christological and redemptive-historical service. M38-G's ransomed-identity gratitude might have been read as purely retrospective and non-typological, but the Exodus-as-type pattern and the eschatological ransom from Death (Hos 13:14; Isa 35:10) give it clear forward direction.
