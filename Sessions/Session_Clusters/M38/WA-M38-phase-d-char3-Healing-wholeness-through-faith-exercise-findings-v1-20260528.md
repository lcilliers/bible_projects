**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence consistently positions God — specifically Jesus as the agent of sōzō — as one whose healing power responds to faith exercised from within the human person. In Mat 9:22 and Mar 5:34, Jesus names faith as the operative cause: "your faith has made you well," revealing that God's saving-healing character is not capricious or arbitrarily selective but structurally responsive to interior trust. In Luk 8:50, Jesus commands "only believe" as the direct counter to mortal fear, indicating that God's character includes a disposition to save precisely at the boundary of death when faith is the presenting inner posture. Jam 5:15 (M38-C-VCG-03) reveals a further dimension: God is the one who, when addressed by the prayer of faith, heals body and forgives sin in a single integrated act, showing that the divine character holds physical and spiritual restoration together rather than separating them. 1Pe 3:21 deepens this: God is one who receives the inner appeal of conscience and answers it through resurrection power. Taken together, the evidence images a God whose nature is integrative wholeness — one who does not partition body from spirit in the act of rescue, and whose responsive character is activated by interior faith-movement in the creature.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence positions faith-exercised-toward-wholeness as the inner-being faculty that enables the human person to receive the full, integrated rescue God offers. The hemorrhaging woman's interior conviction in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 — forming before any outward act is complete — suggests that this faculty is the point of contact between human need and divine power: it equips the person to reach toward God at the place where bodily and spiritual need converge. Bartimaeus in Mar 10:52 and the blind man in Luk 18:42 illustrate that this characteristic also equips the person for discipleship — Bartimaeus "followed him on the way" after receiving sight, indicating that wholeness-through-faith does not end at physical cure but opens the person toward continued relational orientation to Christ. The Samaritan leper's return in Luk 17:19 further shows that the characteristic equips the person for grateful recognition of the giver of wholeness. In sum, the created purpose evidenced is: this characteristic equips the human person to move from fragmentation (disease, blindness, death-threat) through faith-exercise toward integrated personhood that is bodily, spiritual, and relationally directed toward God.

**T0.3.1 — Image-Bearer Expression — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The divine image dimension evidenced here is the capacity for responsive trust — the ability of the inner being to form an interior conviction directed toward God as the source of wholeness. The women in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 exhibit a pre-verbal, pre-contact interior speech — "if I touch his garment I will be made well" — which is an act of the person's image-bearing rationality and will oriented toward the divine. This is not animal instinct but structured interior faith: purposive, directional, and theologically oriented. The image-bearer expression is therefore the relational-receptive capacity: the human person, unlike other creatures, can form interior trust oriented toward God and, by that trust, become the site where divine saving power acts. 1Pe 3:21's "appeal to God for a good conscience" (M38-C-VCG-03) sharpens this further — conscience as image-bearing faculty is the organ capable of seeking right standing before God, and it does so through interior appeal rather than external rite. The characteristic thus instantiates the relational and volitional aspects of the image: the capacity to turn inwardly toward God in trust, which is itself a creaturely reflection of the covenantal relationality that belongs to God's nature.

The direction of typology is examined in T0.4.2.

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence does not explicitly attribute faith-exercised-as-inner-faculty to God (faith in the Pauline sense of God's faithfulness is a separate register not present in this sub-group's corpus). However, the evidence reveals an important asymmetry: faith is consistently located in the human person as the activating faculty, while God — through Jesus — is the one who names, confirms, and responds to it. Jesus's declarations in Mat 9:22, Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48, Luk 17:19, Luk 18:42, and Mar 10:52 all have the same structure: the human person exercises faith inwardly, and Jesus recognises and names it as the operative cause of sōzō. This asymmetry is itself revelatory: what it discloses is that God in his nature is responsive to faith — not as a constraint on divine freedom but as a covenantal disposition to honour the interior trust that the image-bearer directs toward him. The significance for the human person is that this characteristic is precisely that which aligns the creature with the responsive structure of God's saving character: by exercising faith, the human person enters into the relational dynamic that God's covenantal nature has prepared.

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence does not address the pre-fall state directly, but the internal logic of the evidence suggests a dual register. The conditions that occasion the faith-exercises in M38-C are uniformly conditions of bodily breakdown: haemorrhage (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28), blindness (Mar 10:52; Luk 18:42), leprosy (Luk 17:19), death-threat (Mar 5:23; Luk 8:50), and sickness requiring communal intercession (Jam 5:15). These are all markers of the fallen condition — mortality, decay, disease — suggesting that the operative expression of this characteristic is a response to the fallen condition. However, the structural capacity beneath the expression — the capacity to form interior trust directed toward God as source of wholeness — appears to belong to the created design as image-bearer rationality and will. The characteristic is therefore best described as both: the deep faculty is created-design (the relational-volitional capacity for interior faith directed toward God), while its particular form as faith exercised in the face of bodily dissolution is a response to fallenness. Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21 (M38-C-VCG-03) reinforce the fallen-response register by pairing healing with forgiveness and conscience-cleansing, indicating that sin's effects on the inner person are also part of what this characteristic addresses.

The characteristic is thus exclusively creaturely in its operational form while being relationally structured toward the divine.

Isa 35:5 — outside this corpus but setting the background). The typological direction is therefore divine → human throughout: God's pattern of integrated rescue, established in covenant and announced in prophecy, becomes the framework within which individual faith-exercised-wholeness events participate and point forward.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus is silent on God exercising faith as an inner faculty (see T0.1.2 and T0.3.2). This silence is structurally significant rather than merely negative. The divine image in the human person is not a simple copy of God's properties but includes capacities that arise from the creature's position as dependent being-in-relation-to-God. Faith-exercised is one such capacity: it only makes sense for a being who is not self-sufficient, who must reach toward another for wholeness. God possesses wholeness inherently; the image-bearer is constituted to reach for it. The silence therefore locates this characteristic squarely within the creatureliness dimension of the imago Dei — it is not a reflection of a divine attribute in the sense that love or justice are, but rather a distinctively creaturely form of the relational structure that the image is designed to sustain. Its place in the divine image is as the inner-being faculty by which the creature maintains its constitutive orientation toward God as source — an orientation that God himself does not need because he is the source.

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Healing wholeness through faith exercised." The name signals several structural commitments simultaneously. "Healing" locates the primary presenting register: the inner being is in a condition requiring restoration, not merely improvement. "Wholeness" signals that the outcome is integrated and total — not partial cure but a convergence of physical and spiritual restoration, which is the sōzō semantic field's distinctive contribution. "Through" indicates instrumentality and directional movement: the characteristic is not a state received passively but a result achieved by means of something. "Faith exercised" is the instrument named — not faith as disposition only, but faith in active, operative mode; the participle "exercised" marks intentional, directional inner-being action. The name as a whole therefore signals: this is an active inner-being phenomenon (not passive), its activity is faith directed toward an object (God/Christ), and its goal is the bridging of physical and spiritual need in a single integrated rescue event. The word "exercised" is the name's most decisive signal — it distinguishes this characteristic from faith as settled doctrinal assent and positions it as a dynamic, moment-bound inner act that produces ontological change.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence positions this characteristic primarily as an act — a specific, directional inner-being movement — rather than a stable disposition or quality. The most accurate classification is: faith exercised is an act with dispositional depth — a moment-bound inner-being movement that is possible because of an underlying trust-orientation, but which is itself distinct from that underlying orientation. It is the actualization of faith in a specific movement toward God for specific wholeness. In programme vocabulary, it is best described as an inner-being act arising from and expressing a deeper dispositional trust.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus directly names the structural opposite: fear. The primary structural opposite is therefore fear (phobos) as the inner-being reality of self-protective withdrawal in the face of mortal threat, which directly excludes the outward movement of trust toward God that this characteristic requires.

**T1.4.1 — Modes of Operation — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence reveals at least three distinct modes of operation. (1) Interior conviction mode (M38-C-VCG-01): the characteristic operates as pre-verbal, pre-contact inner monologue or formed conviction — the woman in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 forms the conviction inwardly before touching, and this interior act is itself causally efficacious; Jesus confirms it was operative ("your faith has made you well"). (2) Crisis-displacement mode (M38-C-VCG-02): the characteristic operates as active counter-movement against mortal fear — in Luk 8:50 and Mar 5:23, faith is not quiet conviction but urgent, desperate exercise that displaces fear as the governing inner state; the faith-act is precipitated by extreme threat and operates through intensity rather than composure. (3) Intercessory/conscience mode (M38-C-VCG-03): the characteristic operates through the conscience as primary organ (1Pe 3:21 — "appeal to God for a good conscience") and through intercessory faith on behalf of another (Jam 5:15 — "the prayer of faith will save the sick"). In this mode, the characteristic's operation is not self-directed but other-directed, and the restoration is explicitly conscience-level rather than somatic only. These three modes are genuinely distinct in their presenting dynamics, though all three share the structural feature of faith as the inner-being activating movement toward God.

**T1.5.1 — Immediate Response — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences two distinct immediate responses depending on the mode of encounter. (1) Where faith has been exercised and sōzō received, the immediate inner response is peace: Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 both close with Jesus granting the woman "peace" (eirēnē) — this is the direct inner-being marker of what the person first experiences upon receiving healing-wholeness through faith. Peace here functions not as a general well-being but as the specific inner-being state that attends the settling of the faith-crisis: the striving of faith has reached its object, and the inner being rests in that reception. (2) In the blindness-healing cases (Mar 10:52; Luk 18:42), the immediate response is discipleship-orientation — "followed him on the way" — suggesting that in the sight-restoration mode, the first inner response is not peace-as-rest but forward-movement toward Christ. (3) In the conscience-appeal mode (1Pe 3:21), the immediate inner response is not named explicitly but the structure implies conscience-cleared: the appeal to God is answered by resurrection, and the good conscience is the immediate inner result. Across all three modes, the immediate response involves an inner-being shift from the state that preceded the faith-exercise (fear, darkness, troubled conscience) to a new integrated state (peace, sight/following, clear conscience).

**T1.8.1 — Dimension Classification — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic's primary Greek term is sōzō (G4982), with the inner-faculty term being pistis/pisteuō (faith/believe) as the instrument named by Jesus in the healing declarations. At the definitional level, sōzō carries a semantic range that refuses separation of physical and spiritual rescue — it can denote rescue from physical danger, healing of bodily disease, and soteriological salvation, with these senses intersecting rather than being mutually exclusive. The programme's researcher direction explicitly notes a "sōzō sense-split" — this sub-group is held at the intersection where healing and saving are inseparable, which is sōzō's own semantic structure. Pistis/pisteuō at the definitional level denotes trust, reliance, and commitment directed toward an object — it is inherently relational and directional, presupposing both a truster and a trusted. The combination of sōzō and pistis in the standard Synoptic formula ("your faith has sōzō'd you") therefore produces at the definitional level: trust-directed-toward-God/Christ as the inner act that releases integrated rescue (physical-plus-spiritual). The Hebrew background of shālōm (peace, wholeness, completeness) — evoked by Jesus's "go in peace" in Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 — adds the dimension that the result of sōzō-through-pistis is wholeness as restored-right-order, not merely absence of disease.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic is composite at first encounter, not simple. Three constituent elements are immediately visible in the verse evidence. (1) An inner conviction or trust-forming act: the woman's interior monologue (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28) shows that faith must be formed as a specific directed conviction before it becomes operative — this is an epistemic-volitional element. (2) An outward expression or movement: touching the garment, crying out persistently (Bartimaeus, Mar 10:52), returning to give thanks (Luk 17:19), praying for the sick (Jam 5:15) — the inner conviction must pass through some expression or directional act; faith exercised is not purely interior but moves outward. (3) A reception and confirmation event: Jesus's naming of faith as the cause ("your faith has made you well") and the bestowing of peace or sight or cleansed conscience — the characteristic is only complete when the inner act has reached its object and been received as sōzō. These three elements — formation, expression, reception — are distinguishable but not separable in the evidence. A fourth element is present in M38-C-VCG-03: intercessory extension, where faith operates not only for the self but on behalf of another (Jam 5:15). The characteristic is therefore structurally composite with at least three to four constituent elements.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes or resists the following, drawn from direct verse evidence. (4) Self-sufficiency as alternative to faith: the act of forming interior conviction directed toward Christ as the source of wholeness excludes self-reliance as the operative inner posture — one cannot simultaneously rely on oneself and exercise faith toward Christ for the same wholeness. These exclusions collectively define the boundary: the characteristic ends where fear, external-only ritual, ungrateful passivity, or self-sufficiency begin.

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, the evidence supports contextual and directional variation in operation, as distinct from the three structural modes identified in T1.4.1. (1) Self-directed vs. other-directed: In M38-C-VCG-01 and VCG-02, faith is exercised by the healing-seeker for their own wholeness or by a parent for their child (Mar 5:23). (2) Crisis-intensity vs. composure-level: VCG-02's crisis-displacement mode (Luk 8:50; Mar 5:23) operates at higher intensity than VCG-01's quiet interior conviction mode (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28). The constitutional level at which faith engages the inner being appears to shift with urgency — both are genuine faith-exercises but with different phenomenological registers. (3) Bodily-healing register vs. conscience-register: The Synoptic healing narratives operate primarily in the somatic register that carries spiritual overtone, while 1Pe 3:21 operates primarily in the conscience register with the somatic (baptism) as outward correlate. The characteristic therefore differentiates in operation across these contextual-constitutional dimensions while retaining the same structural core.

(2) Divine enabling: the actual sōzō event is always attributed to Christ's power, and in Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21 the saving/healing is explicitly God's act in response to faith. These two origins are not competitive; the corpus presents them as structurally necessary to each other — faith without the divine respondent is inert, and divine power here is consistently accessed through faith as the activating human contribution. The origin is therefore irreducibly dual.

**[CHAR-3]** E — The sequence evidenced across M38-C VCG-01 and VCG-02 follows a consistent four-stage pattern: (1) *Formation*: interior faith/conviction arises in the heart or conscience of the seeker (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28, Mar 5:23, Luk 8:50). The frameworks are useful for understanding the inner-being dynamics of the faith-act and the phenomenology of receiving wholeness, but they reach their explanatory limit at the point where the data requires divine agency as a non-reducible cause.

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The human science frameworks surface two aspects insufficiently addressed in the 12-verse corpus:

(1) **The long-term inner-being trajectory after healing**: The verse evidence records the sōzō event and the immediate peace-grant or discipleship-initiation but does not address what happens to the inner being of the healed person over time. Health psychology raises questions about whether healing-faith produces ongoing dispositional change or is a one-time event. The woman's post-healing inner life, Bartimaeus's discipleship trajectory beyond Mar 10:52, and the Samaritan leper's subsequent inner-being development are all unaddressed. This suggests further investigation into NT passages that track the sustained inner-being life of those healed through faith.

(2) **The social-reintegration dimension**: Medical anthropology and illness-narrative studies emphasize that healing from stigmatized illness (hemorrhage, leprosy, blindness) requires social reintegration as much as physical cure. The verse evidence touches this in Mar 5:34 ("daughter" — public identity restoration) and Luk 17:14 (commanded to show priests — ritual-social verification), but does not develop the social-inner-being dimension systematically. Further verse investigation in the healing pericopes' aftermath and in Levitical purity contexts could address this gap.
