**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — **Hebrew primary term: כָּפַר (ka.phar, H3722B)**. The root is widely agreed to carry the core meaning of "to cover" (cognate with Arabic *kafara*, "to cover/conceal") or possibly "to wipe away" (cognate with Akkadian *kuppuru*). Both meanings converge on the same inner-being implication: the offending substance (guilt/sin) is either hidden from divine sight or wiped from the relational surface. The priestly ritual enacts this covering/wiping through sacrificial substitution. Every VCG-01 through VCG-07 verse uses this root.

**Greek primary terms** (a cluster, not one term):
- **ἱλάσκομαι (hilaskomai, G2433)**: Luk 18:13, Heb 2:17. Root meaning: "to propitiate, to render favorable, to appease." The root carries the relational sense of *moving an offended party from hostile to favorable disposition toward the offender*.
- **ἱλασμός (hilasmos, G2434)**: 1Jo 2:2, 1Jo 4:10. The noun form: "the propitiatory act/provision," the concrete means by which divine disposition is moved.
- **ἱλαστήριος (hilastērios, G2435)**: Rom 3:25. Adjectival/substantive: "propitiatory," the thing/person that enacts propitiation. Also the LXX translation of kapporet (mercy seat), creating direct lexical continuity between Hebrew cultic atonement and NT Christology.
- **ἵλεως (hileōs, G2436)**: Heb 8:12. "Merciful/propitious" — the *resultant divine disposition* after propitiation.

The Hebrew root reveals an *objective covering* mechanism; the Greek root reveals a *relational-disposition-moving* mechanism. Together they show that atonement operates on both the sin itself (covered/wiped) and the relationship (God rendered propitious).

**T7.2.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — **For Hebrew כָּפַר (ka.phar)**: The evidence base uses it almost exclusively as a **Piel verb** ("to make atonement," the intensive-active stem), with the priest as subject and the offerer's sin or soul as the object. This grammatical concentration reveals that atonement is fundamentally an *enacted act* rather than a state or disposition — something that must be *done*, by an agent, on behalf of another. The Piel voice intensifies the action, suggesting completeness and directed energy. There is no passive form dominant in this corpus — atonement is never something that simply happens or drifts; it is performed.

**For Greek hilaskomai/hilasmos/hilastērios/hileōs**: The range spans all grammatical forms — verb (hilaskomai, Luk 18:13; Heb 2:17), noun (hilasmos, 1Jo 2:2; 4:10), substantive adjective (hilastērios, Rom 3:25), and attribute adjective (hileōs, Heb 8:12). This wider range reveals that in NT usage the characteristic is not only enacted (verb) but *embodied in a person* (hilastērios applied to Christ in Rom 3:25) and eventuates in a *divine disposition* (hileōs in Heb 8:12). The grammatical arc from verb to noun to person-embodiment tracks the theological development from repeated ritual act to once-for-all incarnate provision.

**T7.1.2 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes. The vocabulary differentiates at least four distinct aspects:

(1) **Act versus state**: The Piel verb כִּפֶּר (kipper, "to make atonement") names the *act*; the passive outcome סָלַח (salach, "forgiven") names the *resultant state*. These are grammatically and semantically distinct throughout VCG-01.

(2) **Given versus received**: Lev 17:11 ("I have *given* it to you upon the altar to make atonement") marks God as the giver of the atoning mechanism; Rom 3:25 ("through faith") and the worshipper's hand-laying (Lev 1:4) mark the *receiving* side. The characteristic thus requires both a giving vocabulary and a receiving vocabulary to be complete.

(3) **Divine disposition resulting**: ἵλεως (hileōs, Heb 8:12) is specifically the *divine relational quality* — God being "merciful/propitious" — which is the relational condition that atonement produces on God's side. This distinguishes the human inner-being state (conscience cleansed) from the divine relational state (God rendered propitious) as two distinct outcomes of the same act.

(4) **Mechanism versus embodiment**: ἱλασμός (hilasmos, noun, "propitiatory provision") distinguishes the *mechanism* from ἱλαστήριος (hilastērios, substantive, "the propitiatory one/person") which marks the *embodiment* of that mechanism in Christ, a distinction Rom 3:25 and 1Jo 2:2/4:10 make visible.

**T7.2.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The OT-NT vocabulary relationship reveals both strong *structural continuity* and *significant developmental concentration*:

**Continuity**: The LXX translates כִּפֶּר (kipper, Piel of ka.phar) with ἱλάσκομαι and related forms throughout the Pentateuch, establishing direct lexical continuity. The hilastērios of Rom 3:25 explicitly echoes the kapporet (mercy seat, translated ἱλαστήριον in LXX Exo 25:17) — Paul's use places Christ in the structural position of the Yom Kippur mercy seat, maintaining the OT cultic architecture while reidentifying its content. The blood-atonement mechanism (Lev 17:11) finds direct NT continuity in Rom 3:25 ("through his blood").

**Development**: The NT vocabulary makes three developments OT Hebrew cannot: (1) *Once-for-all embodiment* — hilastērios applied to a *person* (Christ) rather than a repeated ritual act, collapsing all the repeated VCG-01 ka.phar-events into one; (2) *Universal scope* — 1Jo 2:2 extends the coverage to "the whole world," beyond Israel's covenant community; (3) *Faith as the receptor mechanism* — Rom 3:25 adds "through faith" as the inner-being act that appropriates propitiation, a precision the OT ritual system expressed through hand-laying (Lev 1:4) but not through an explicit pistis vocabulary. The OT emphasizes the *priestly mechanism*; the NT emphasizes the *faith-appropriation* of the once-for-all provision.

**T7.3.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The term **ἱλαστήριος (hilastērios)** as applied to a *person* (Rom 3:25: "whom God set forth as a propitiation/hilastērios") represents a significant semantic development if not an outright neologism. In classical Greek and LXX usage, hilastērios functions as an adjective ("propitiatory") or refers to the kapporet (mercy seat, the cultic *object*). Paul's application of the term to Christ as the subject who *is* the propitiation — a person rather than an object — is a distinctive NT development that transforms the word's referential range. This reveals that the NT authors understood Christ's atonement not as one more sacrifice within the OT system but as the *living embodiment* of the entire atonement-mechanism: the priest, the sacrifice, and the mercy seat all concentrated in a single person. The coinage-by-application reveals that no existing term was fully adequate for this unprecedented identification — a new referential use was required.

**T7.1.4 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The full vocabulary arc from ka.phar (OT) through hilasmos/hilastērios/hileōs (NT) reveals that Char-4's semantic range is *tripartite*:

(1) **Mechanism/act range**: Covering, wiping away, ransoming, substituting life-for-life, making propitious — all the ways in which the guilt-conscience is addressed from outside the self. This is the widest dimension of the characteristic, spanning ritual, relational, intercessory, and cosmic registers.

(2) **Status-change range**: Forgiven (salach), atoned (ka.phar Pual passive), accepted (Lev 1:4), justified (Luk 18:14) — the inner-being state produced by the mechanism. The conscience moves from burdened/condemned to cleared/accepted.

(3) **Relational-disposition range**: God rendered hileōs (propitious/merciful, Heb 8:12), wrath averted (Psa 78:38, Rom 3:25), divine love expressed as covering (1Jo 4:10) — the relational condition on God's side that mirrors and enables the inner-being change on the human side.

The full arc reveals a characteristic that is simultaneously *objective* (a mechanism enacted), *subjective* (a conscience transformed), and *relational* (a divine-human relationship restored). Its semantic range is wider than forgiveness (which is only relational), wider than sacrifice (which is only mechanical), and wider than guilt-relief (which is only subjective) — it holds all three simultaneously as one complex inner-being event.

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary anchor verse is **Lev 4:20** (VCG-01 anchor, vc_id=29586) with the primary term כִּפֶּר (kipper, Piel verb). Within the verse's syntactic structure, kipper functions as the **main predicate of the priestly act**: "the priest shall make atonement [kipper] for them, and they shall be forgiven." The verb is the hinge of the entire sentence — it is what the priest *does* (subject: the priest; verb: kipper; object: "for them/on account of the sin"), and the consequence clause ("they shall be forgiven") is grammatically subordinated to it as result. In the argument of Lev 4 as a whole, this verse functions as the **programmatic statement** of the sin-offering's purpose: after several verses establishing the correct procedure, v.20 delivers the guaranteed outcome. The kipper-verb thus carries the entire weight of the chapter's inner-being promise — it is the mechanism that activates the forgiveness guarantee.

For the NT anchor **Rom 3:25**, ἱλαστήριον (hilastērios) functions as a **predicate accusative or object complement** in the clause "whom God set forth as a propitiation through his blood, received through faith." It names what Christ *is* in his atoning function — the term carries Christological identification and functional definition simultaneously, making it the argumentative centre of Paul's justification argument in Rom 3:21–26.

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence base spans multiple literary forms, each requiring distinct interpretive handling:

**T7.1.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Three argumentative structures are identifiable:

(1) **Levitical priestly argument (VCG-01)**: Structure: *Premise 1*: This person has sinned and requires covering; *Premise 2*: God has designated the specific sacrificial procedure for this type of sin; *Premise 3*: The priest enacts the designated procedure on behalf of the offerer; *Conclusion*: The offerer is forgiven. This is a *conditional guarantee* structure — the outcome is certain given the premises. The logical force is judicial: compliance with prescribed mechanism yields guaranteed outcome.

(2) **Pauline propitiation argument (Rom 3:21–26)**: *Premise 1*: All have sinned and are under condemnation (3:23); *Premise 2*: God set forth Christ as hilastērios through his blood (3:25); *Premise 3*: This is received by faith (3:25); *Conclusion*: Former sins are passed over and God is shown to be both just and justifier (3:25–26). The logical structure is *satisfaction-of-divine-justice* through substitutionary provision.

(3) **Johannine love-atonement argument (1Jo 4:7–10)**: *Premise 1*: God is love (4:8); *Premise 2*: God's love was expressed in sending his Son as hilasmos (4:10); *Premise 3*: This was prior to any human love-initiative (4:10b); *Conclusion*: We are to love because God first loved us (4:11). The logical structure is *grace-prior-to-response*, placing conscience-cleansing atonement as the *ground of human ethical life* rather than its reward.

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — **Rom 3:25** reveals what no other verse in the corpus makes explicit: the **simultaneous justification of God and the sinner** as the dual outcome of propitiation. The verse states: "to show God's righteousness … so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus" (3:26). The inner-being conscience-cleansing of the receiver thus rests on a theologically complete foundation that only this verse articulates.

**Lev 4:20** (the OT anchor) reveals what no other verse quite captures: the *guaranteed, outcome-certain* structure of the priestly mechanism. The declarative "they shall be forgiven" (nisgal lahem) is not a conditional hope but a juridical guarantee — when the priest performs ka.phar correctly, forgiveness is certain. This certainty-structure is present in all VCG-01 verses but most clearly stated here as the programmatic principle.

**T7.1.7 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The most useful framework is **moral psychology**, specifically the contemporary psychology of *moral emotions* and *guilt-resolution*. Char-4 is fundamentally concerned with the inner-being state produced when guilt — an aversive moral emotion tied to specific transgression — is *objectively removed* through an act external to the self. Moral psychology's distinction between **guilt** (specific act-focused self-condemnation), **shame** (self-focused identity-condemnation), and **conscience** (the evaluative inner faculty) directly illuminates the characteristic's architecture. The verse evidence engages all three: guilt as the presenting state (VCG-01's sin-burdened offerers); shame as the post-atonement response (Eze 16:63, Luk 18:13); conscience as the cleansed faculty (the characteristic's definitional term).

Second most useful is **cultural anthropology of sacrifice**, particularly theories of substitutionary exchange (Rene Girard's mimetic theory, though contested, and older Hubert-Mauss functionalism). These frameworks illuminate why the substitutionary mechanism (Lev 1:4's hand-laying, Lev 17:11's blood-for-soul) functions as an inner-being resolution rather than merely a physical transaction.

Third: **relational/attachment psychology** — the atonement-received state is fundamentally relational (restored standing before God), making attachment theory's account of rupture-and-repair in secure relationships relevant to VCG-03 and VCG-07.

**T7.1.8 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Moral psychology's guilt-resolution framework illuminates Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) in a way that the verse alone does not fully explain. The verse depicts shame *persisting* after atonement has been fully granted: "you will be ashamed and confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you." Moral psychology distinguishes between **guilt-relief** (which ends when the transgression is forgiven) and **shame** (which can deepen when forgiveness is received precisely because the severity of what was forgiven becomes clearer). The verse evidences this psychological phenomenon — the conscience-cleansed state does not erase the memory or shame of the original transgression; it transforms it from burden-of-condemnation into humbled-gratitude. Moral psychology names this as the *integration of forgiven guilt into identity* — a secondary inner-being process distinct from the initial guilt-relief. Without this framework, the verse appears paradoxical (why is shame intensified by forgiveness?); with it, the verse reveals a psychologically coherent secondary phase of Char-4's inner-being movement.

Similarly, the ransom-equality principle in Exo 30:15–16 (VCG-03) is illuminated by **social psychology of fairness**: every soul requiring identical ransom regardless of status creates a moral-psychological equalizer — the rich person's conscience is no more securely covered than the poor person's, and vice versa. This equality-before-atonement dynamic reveals Char-4 as a social-equalizer in the inner-being landscape as well as a personal event.

**T7.1.9 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The most significant divergence occurs at the point of **objective versus subjective guilt resolution**. Contemporary moral psychology and psychotherapy operate primarily with *subjective* guilt — the felt sense of wrongdoing — and treat its resolution as primarily a matter of cognitive reframing, relational repair, or therapeutic processing. The verse evidence in VCG-01 through VCG-05 consistently treats guilt as *objective* — standing before God as a real condition requiring an objective external act for its removal, regardless of the offender's felt state. Lev 5:17–18 explicitly addresses *unintentional* sins where the person did not even know they were guilty (Lev 5:18: "he did not know it, and he is guilty") — the guilt is real and requires atonement irrespective of subjective awareness.

This divergence reveals that Char-4 is not primarily a psychological phenomenon in the modern therapeutic sense. The inner-being change is not produced by the person coming to *feel* forgiven or restructuring their self-narrative — it is produced by an *objective* event (the atoning act) that changes the person's *actual standing* before God. The psychological sensation of relief or shame may or may not accompany this, but Char-4's core is the *status-change*, not the feeling-change. Contemporary frameworks that reduce guilt to subjective experience cannot account for the unintentional-guilt of Lev 5:18 or the objectivity of atonement as a mechanism at all.

**T7.1.10 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Moral psychology surfaces one aspect that the verse evidence addresses only partially: **the persistence of the cleansed-conscience state over time**. The verse evidence establishes conscience-cleansing as an event (moment of atonement and forgiveness) and gives glimpses of its lasting effect (Heb 8:12: "I will remember their sins no more"; 1Jo 2:2: ongoing advocate). However, moral psychology would ask about *recurrence of guilt* — the well-documented phenomenon where guilt returns for already-forgiven transgressions, requiring either repeated reassurance or a deepening of the forgiveness event's internalization. The repeated ka.phar events of VCG-01 (covering different sins and different occasions) may implicitly address this through the provision of an ongoing ritual mechanism, but the corpus does not directly address the inner-being dynamics of *holding onto* the cleared-conscience state.

This absence *may* warrant further verse investigation into texts addressing assurance of forgiveness, the once-for-all finality of Christ's atonement (Heb 10:1–18 would be a relevant domain), and the pastoral theology of conscience-management in an already-atoned state. These are not represented in the current 39-verse corpus and their absence is a genuine gap.
