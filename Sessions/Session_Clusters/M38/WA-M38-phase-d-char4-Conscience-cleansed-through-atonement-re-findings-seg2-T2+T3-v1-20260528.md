**T3.6.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence suggests atonement-received enables volition in a specific way. Before atonement, the sin-laden state is one of diminished volitional freedom — the unresolved guilt is a weight and constraint (Psa 65:3: "iniquities overwhelm me"). After atonement, the freed conscience enables unimpeded approach to God and responsive action. Pro 16:6 (VCG-04) links atonement-coverage to moral turning — "fear of the LORD turns one from evil" — suggesting the atoned state releases the will toward moral obedience. Isa 6:7–8 shows the sequence: atonement received, then Isaiah's volitional response "Here I am, send me" is possible. Atonement enables volition by removing the conscience-weight that constrains it.

---

**T3.7.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic primarily enables agency by restoring the person's standing before God from which action becomes possible. Isa 6:7–8 shows the most direct evidence: atonement received, and immediately the prophet's agency is restored ("Who shall I send? Here I am"). The Levitical system (VCG-01) requires human agency (bringing sacrifice, confession) to activate the atonement-mechanism. VCG-06's negative pole (atonement withheld) implicitly shows agency constrained or condemned when guilt remains. The characteristic does not bypass agency — it requires initial human agency (approach, sacrifice, prayer) and then enables subsequent agency by removing the conscience-barrier. Divine agency (the act of covering) is the enabling foundation for restored human agency.

---

**T3.8.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Atonement-received presupposes and deepens moral evaluation without bypassing it. The system requires accurate moral self-evaluation as its precondition: one must recognize sin as real guilt (Lev 5:6 "guilt surfacing"; Lev 5:18 "unintentional but real guilt"; Luk 18:13 "I am a sinner"). VCG-06 shows that moral evaluation operating without atonement-coverage produces permanent condemnation (1Sa 3:14: the iniquity recognized but declared unatonable). After atonement-received, moral evaluation is not dissolved — Pro 16:6 links the covered state to continued fear of the LORD and turning from evil, and Eze 16:63 shows ongoing moral self-awareness in shame. The characteristic clears the guilt-verdict of moral evaluation without eliminating moral evaluation itself; if anything it deepens honest self-assessment.

---

**T3.9.2 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Atonement-received cleanses the conscience rather than bypassing or impairing it. The conscience is the primary site of operation: guilt is real and registered (Lev 5:6, Luk 18:13), and atonement addresses the guilt directly. The cleansing does not suppress conscience-function — Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) shows the atoned person remains silenced in shame, indicating an active, sensitized conscience. Heb 8:12 frames forgiveness as "sins remembered no more" — not that the conscience becomes insensitive, but that the divine record is cleared. The negative pole (VCG-06) shows conscience permanently burdened when atonement is withheld. The characteristic enables a cleared conscience that remains alert, not a deadened one. Conscience is enabled and its guilt-register resolved without its warning-function being disabled.

---

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Partially enables. Pro 16:6 (VCG-04) and Isa 27:9 (VCG-07) show atonement linked to moral transformation and turning from sin — the integrated moral-volitional-active response that constitutes conscientiousness. The atoned state is not a license for moral passivity but produces renewed moral engagement. However, the corpus does not evidence atonement-received directly deepening conscientiousness as a sustained character-disposition. The enabling is event-proximate: the cleared conscience creates the relational space in which conscientious response can follow. Bypassing is not evidenced — the system requires acknowledged guilt (moral awareness) before atonement is applied.

---

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Atonement-received enables and restores relational capacity. The fundamental relational breach is guilt before God; atonement closes the breach and restores the person to standing in God's presence. Exo 30:16 (VCG-03) frames atonement as bringing the person into divine remembrance — active relational connection. 2Ch 30:18 shows the heart-to-God relationship as the operative dimension of atonement-received. 1Jo 4:10 (VCG-04) grounds atonement in divine love — the relational act of God is the enabling source. Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) shows the relational consequence as a covenant-restored state producing shame and gratitude in the human partner. The characteristic restores and enables relational capacity toward God as its primary movement; human-to-human relational effects are not explicitly evidenced in this corpus.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The pattern reveals that atonement-received is not primarily a perceptive event but requires a prior perceptive act (recognition of sin, awareness of guilt) as its trigger and produces a cleared field for perception after. The perceptive faculty is preparatory rather than constitutive. Isa 6's sequence — sight of holiness, self-perception of uncleanness, coal-atonement, commission — shows perception framing the atonement act on both sides without being its content. This reveals that conscience-cleansed atonement is fundamentally a status-change and relational-restoration event, not a perceptive experience. The characteristic does not depend on extraordinary spiritual perception; it operates through objective mechanisms (sacrifice, blood, priestly declaration, Christ's propitiation) that are received, not primarily seen or heard.

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The minimal cognitive engagement reveals that atonement-received is not dependent on intellectual achievement, theological understanding, or cognitive capacity. The priestly system (VCG-01) operates for the economically humble (Lev 5:13: even a grain offering suffices), the accidentally sinning (Lev 5:18: unintentional), and the ritually impure (2Ch 30:18: heart sincere despite irregular preparation). Heb 8:12's frame of God "remembering sins no more" bypasses the cognitive dimension entirely — the forgiveness is divine action, not human comprehension. This reveals that the characteristic is accessible without cognitive sophistication: it operates through enacted ritual, received declaration, and faith-posture rather than understanding-achievement. The cognitive requirement is minimal: know you have sinned, know the mechanism exists, and approach.

---

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The memory-pattern reveals a theologically significant asymmetry at the heart of this characteristic. Divine memory is cleared (Heb 8:12: "remember sins no more") and redirected to positive remembrance (Exo 30:16: "brought into remembrance before God"), while human memory of past sin persists (Eze 16:63: shame and silence from remembered guilt). This asymmetry reveals that atonement-received is not psychological forgetting but relational status-change: the person's record before God is cleared while the person retains the memory that produces humility. The characteristic produces a person who is objectively covered but subjectively remembers — which is precisely the structure of gratitude. The memory-pattern reveals atonement as fundamentally covenantal-relational rather than therapeutic-psychological.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The affective pattern reveals that atonement-received produces a complex, layered affective state rather than simple positive emotion. The evidenced affective outcomes include: guilt-weight preceding (Psa 65:3; Luk 18:13), relief at burden-lifting (Psa 65:3), shame persisting after covering (Eze 16:63), contrition maintained (Luk 18:13), and awed gratitude at divine love (1Jo 4:10). The pattern reveals that this characteristic does not produce euphoria or emotional resolution that dissolves affective engagement. Instead it produces a chastened affective register — relief that holds shame, gratitude that holds awareness of what required covering. This rules out atonement-received as a merely sentimental or emotionally comfortable state. The affective engagement reveals a characteristic that transforms the valence of guilt (from condemned to covered) while preserving the memory and moral weight that make gratitude genuine.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** S — The consistent non-engagement with the creative faculty across this corpus reveals that atonement-received is not a generative or originating characteristic in the creativity-faculty sense. It does not create new inner-being content; it removes existing negative content (guilt, condemnation) and restores prior standing. The characteristic is restorative and relational, not creative and generative. This distinguishes it from other inner-being characteristics that produce new psychological states or capacities; atonement-received returns the person to a pre-guilt baseline of cleared standing before God.

---

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The volitional pattern reveals that atonement-received requires minimal volition for reception but enables significant volition as its consequence. Volition is engaged asymmetrically: the approach (bringing sacrifice, praying, hand-laying) requires volitional acts, but the atonement itself is received, not volitionally generated. The post-atonement evidence (Isa 6:7–8: Isaiah's volitional "here I am"; Pro 16:6: turning from evil; Isa 27:9: destruction of idols) shows volitional capacity released and directed after covering is received. This reveals the characteristic as volitionally enabling in its outcome: it removes the conscience-weight that constrained will-toward-God and frees the will for obedient response. Volition is not the engine but is both the access-point and the beneficiary.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The agency pattern reveals that this characteristic has a dual agency structure that is theologically defining. Divine agency is primary and causally sufficient (VCG-04: God himself atones; VCG-05: God sets forth Christ; Lev 17:11: God provides the blood). Human agency is necessary but not causally generative — it activates the reception channel (bringing sacrifice, praying, approaching) without producing the atonement itself. The negative pole (VCG-06) confirms this: when divine agency withdraws from atonement-making (1Sa 3:14: no sacrifice can atone), human agency is helpless. The pattern reveals that atonement-received is defined by divine-primary, human-secondary agency structure — a gift-reception structure where human agency orients and approaches but cannot produce the covering. This is the fundamental asymmetry of grace.

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The moral evaluation pattern reveals that this characteristic presupposes the full operation of moral evaluation rather than bypassing or suspending it. Every instance of atonement-received in this corpus is preceded by accurate moral evaluation: the sin is named, categorized, and assessed as genuinely guilty (Lev 5:18: real guilt even for unintentional sin; Luk 18:13: "I am a sinner"). VCG-06 (negative pole) shows moral evaluation operating without atonement-coverage producing permanent condemnation — the standard holds even when the remedy is refused. Pro 16:6 and Isa 27:9 show moral evaluation and moral transformation as integral outcomes of atonement. This reveals that the characteristic does not suspend judgment; it satisfies it. The moral standard remains fully operative; what changes is the verdict applied to the one who has received the covering. Atonement-received is the resolution of moral evaluation, not its bypass.

---

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The conscience-pattern reveals that this characteristic is definitively a conscience-event at its core. The conscience is the site of the problem (guilt registered, sin-burdened, conscience-struck — VCG-01 keywords throughout; Luk 18:13) and the site of the resolution (conscience cleansed, guilt removed — VCG-01, VCG-04). But Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) reveals that conscience-cleared does not mean conscience-inactivated: the atoned person remains conscious of what required covering and is humbled by it. VCG-06 provides the structural opposite: conscience permanently burdened when atonement is withheld. The pattern reveals that this characteristic is specifically designed to address the conscience-problem of guilt before God — not to eliminate moral sensitivity but to remove the condemnation-verdict while preserving the sensitivity. The conscience is activated, addressed, cleared, and yet maintained in an alert-humble state as the defining inner-being consequence of atonement-received.

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The partial conscientiousness-engagement reveals that atonement-received is not primarily a conscientiousness-event but operates at the more foundational level of relational-status and conscience-clearing. Conscientiousness (integrated moral awareness + volition + action) appears in the approach-to-atonement (bringing sacrifice, making restitution in Lev 5:16, voluntary offering in Num 31:50) and in the post-atonement moral-turning (Pro 16:6, Isa 27:9). This pattern reveals that conscientiousness is both a precondition for approaching atonement (one must act on one's guilt-awareness) and a consequence of receiving it (moral turning follows). But the characteristic itself — the cleared-conscience, covered-status, forgiven state — is not itself a conscientiousness-act. Atonement is received; conscientiousness is exercised in response to it. The pattern reveals the characteristic as foundational to, rather than coextensive with, conscientious moral living.

---

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The consistent, pervasive relational engagement across all seven VCGs reveals that atonement-received is intrinsically and irreducibly a relational characteristic. Every structural element — the breach (sin before God), the mechanism (priestly mediation, substitutionary sacrifice, Christ's propitiation), the outcome (standing restored, divine remembrance maintained, wrath averted), the consequence (shame, gratitude, covenant-renewal) — is relational in nature. The characteristic addresses the fundamental disruption in the person's relationship with God and restores it. This is not incidental to atonement-received; it is its definition. The relational pattern reveals that this characteristic cannot be understood in merely psychological terms (guilt-relief as internal emotional event) without the relational-covenantal framework that gives it meaning. The inner-being state of conscience-cleansed atonement is simultaneously and necessarily a statement about the person's standing in relation to God — the two are inseparable in every evidenced instance.
