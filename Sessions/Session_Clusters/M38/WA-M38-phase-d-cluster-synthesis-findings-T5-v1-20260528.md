**T5.1.1 — Nature of Transformation — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across all seven characteristics, transformation is universally attested, but a structural asymmetry organizes the cluster: five characteristics (M38-A, C, D, F, G) produce both condition-change and orientation-change; one (M38-E) produces primarily condition-change with orientation-change only marginally evidenced; and one (M38-B) produces condition-change in its dominant register but orientation-change as the primary resolution in its highest-register case (the Son's Gethsemane-type prayer). This asymmetry is not random — it maps onto the structural position of each characteristic. M38-E, as the priestly mediation *machinery*, is designed to effect objective status-change through external ritual act; the inner orientation of the person receiving atonement is not the mechanism's object. M38-B, as physical rescue, operates most visibly at the condition level (rescued or not rescued), yet the MB-VCG-03 evidence presses against this — when the condition is not changed (Jesus does die), orientation-change becomes the transformation that the characteristic nonetheless produces. This is the cluster's sharpest finding: orientation-change appears as the fallback transformation when condition-change is denied, which reveals that transformation of orientation is the more fundamental category. M38-F is the inverse case: it produces orientation-change in the present (the soul reoriented toward not-yet-received salvation) while condition-change is deferred to the eschatological horizon — transformation operates entirely through orientation restructuring until fullness arrives. M38-G, M38-A, and M38-C all produce both simultaneously, though M38-C concentrates condition-change in the event and orientation-change in the aftermath (following, worship, peace). The one finding that surfaces across all seven: no characteristic produces orientation-change without some anchoring in an objective condition — whether past (M38-G's memory of ransom), present (M38-D's declarative forgiveness), or anticipated (M38-F's promised salvation). Pure orientation-without-condition is absent from the cluster's evidence.

**T5.2.1 — Sequence of Inner States — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics evidence a before–during–after sequence, but the completeness and phenomenological richness of that sequence vary significantly across the cluster, and the variation is structurally informative. M38-A, M38-C, and M38-G provide the most fully articulated sequences, naming distinct inner-being states at each stage with enough specificity to map subjective experience. M38-B and M38-D provide clear tripartite sequences but with the "during" stage more mechanically described (the faith-cry; the priestly act) than phenomenologically explored. M38-E sequences the before–during–after primarily at the level of objective status rather than subjective experience — the schema exists but the inner texture of each stage is underdeveloped relative to other characteristics. M38-F presents a distinctive case: it evidences the before-state (groaning incapacity) and the after-state (eschatological arrival) extensively, but the "after" is structurally not-yet-arrived within present experience — the sequence is real but its third term is accessible only by anticipation, not by report. This creates a before–during–(anticipated after) pattern unique to M38-F in the cluster. A convergent finding across all seven: the "during" state — the moment of the atoning act, the faith-cry, the touch, the intercessory prayer, the active waiting — is the pivot on which every sequence turns, and in every case it involves a creaturely act of some kind (faith, cry, approach, endurance, ritual presentation, memory-exercise). No sequence in any characteristic runs directly from before to after without a creaturely pivot in the middle. The sequence thus reveals salvation-cluster logic: divine agency initiates and completes, but creaturely response occupies the transitional hinge.

**T5.3.1 — Mechanism of Change — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's seven characteristics together evidence a spectrum of mechanisms, and the spectrum's two poles are occupied by characteristics that share the same root cluster (salvation) but operate with nearly opposite mechanism-profiles. At one pole: M38-E (priestly mediation machinery) operates through external, authoritative, ritual mediation — a mechanism that is neither encounter nor formation nor discipline but prescribed structural enactment by a third party. At the other pole: M38-A and M38-F operate through sustained inner reorientation over time — the word received by faith, endurance through suffering, hope-shaped daily conduct. Between these poles: M38-B (sudden encounter, panic-cry), M38-C (sudden transformative encounter mediated through faith-act), M38-D (substitutionary-declarative moment received through faith), and M38-G (direct divine rescue plus commanded memory). The convergences are revealing: sudden encounter is evidenced in M38-A (crisis conversion), M38-B (drowning panic), M38-C (synoptic healings), and M38-D (declarative forgiveness) — four characteristics share this mechanism in at least one register. Gradual formation is evidenced in M38-A (endurance), M38-B (Noah's sustained faith-building), M38-F (hope-shaped conduct over time), and M38-G (commanded memory institutionalized across generations) — four characteristics also share this mechanism. The cluster thus does not choose between sudden and gradual: it holds both, and the mechanism varies by context within every characteristic that has been examined across multiple VCGs. The one mechanism absent from the cluster's evidence: discipline in the sense of divine corrective suffering as the primary mechanism of salvation — suffering appears extensively as context (T5.4) but is not named as the mechanism of change in any characteristic's primary findings.

**T5.4.1 — Suffering and Affliction — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Suffering and affliction appear across all seven characteristics, but their structural relationship to each characteristic differs in a way that maps cleanly onto the characteristic's inner logic. For M38-B, suffering is constitutive — the characteristic cannot exist without mortal danger as its precondition; suffering is not context but definition. For M38-C, suffering is the consistent presenting context — faith is always exercised against a backdrop of need, disease, or death-facing. For M38-D and M38-E, suffering functions primarily as the occasion that makes the need for atonement visible: it is neither the mechanism of atonement nor its product, but the condition of recognition that drives approach. For M38-A, suffering operates in a more complex relationship: as the context requiring endurance (persecution), as the path of the mediator (Christ's suffering founding salvation), and as voluntary self-surrender (a form of chosen suffering). For M38-F, suffering is the lived experience of the not-yet — groaning, disappointed labor, deferred hope — that makes salvation-anticipation necessary and formative. For M38-G, suffering appears as the pre-ransom state from which the ransoming act delivers, and also as the ongoing threat that keeps the memory of past ransom active. The cluster-level finding: only M38-B treats suffering as the constitutive precondition rather than the contextual occasion. In all other characteristics, suffering is the pressure that activates the characteristic's operation but does not define its nature. The inverse case is also present: in M38-F (2Cor 1:6), suffering is not merely context but becomes the communicative medium through which salvation-hope is transmitted to others — suffering as generative rather than merely receptive. This generative relationship between affliction and salvation-communication is unique in the cluster to M38-F.

**T5.5.1 — Formation and Sanctification — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The seven characteristics divide cleanly into three groups on the formation question. Group one (M38-A, M38-F, M38-G): formation is substantively evidenced. M38-A presents salvation as the origin-event of a holy calling and as an ongoing state requiring moral working-out; M38-F shows hope-sustained anticipation directly motivating righteous conduct and arming the mind toward sobriety; M38-G makes ransomed identity the explicit motivating ground of ethical formation (Deu 15:15 and 24:18 as the clearest bridge between soteriological identity and moral character). Group two (M38-C, M38-D): formation is partially evidenced, concentrated at specific points. M38-C contributes formation primarily through the aftermath of healing events (discipleship-following, return in worship, ongoing conscience-appeal) rather than the healing event itself; M38-D contributes through Pro 16:6, Isa 27:9, and Eze 16:63, where atonement received produces the fear of God and moral reorientation over time. Group three (M38-B, M38-E): formation is sparse or institutional rather than individual. M38-B's formation evidence is concentrated entirely in the Hebrews edge of its corpus (Noah's righteousness-formation, the Son's perfection-through-suffering); the dominant panic-cry register yields none. M38-E's formation is corporate-institutional — the machinery maintains the community's sanctified standing — rather than generating individual character development. The cluster asymmetry: M38-E sustains sanctification but does not generate it; M38-A generates it but requires M38-E's underlying atonement for the conscience-clearance on which formation depends. The two characteristics are complementary in function even though M38-E itself does not directly participate in individual formation.

**T5.6.1 — Eschatological Trajectory — Does the verse evidence point this characteristic toward an eschatological fullness — a future state toward which its present operation is oriented?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's most pronounced divergence surfaces here. M38-F is constitutively eschatological — the characteristic is defined by its forward orientation; to remove the eschatological trajectory would dissolve the characteristic entirely. M38-A is extensively eschatological, with present salvation explicitly framed as approaching, incomplete, and oriented toward a future telos (wrath-deliverance, final rescue, resurrection-life). M38-G contains a clear eschatological register in its VCG-05 and VCG-06 material (ransom from Death itself, olam-joy, reception by God beyond Sheol). M38-D contains eschatological orientation at significant but not dominant density (Dan 9:24's everlasting righteousness, Heb 8:12's new covenant never-remembered-again). M38-C is partially and implicitly eschatological through 1Pe 3:21 and the Kingdom-of-God resonance of the synoptic healing accounts, but this is not the characteristic's primary register. M38-B is deliberately bounded away from eschatological content by its definitional BOUNDARY resolution — the eschatological presence in Heb 11:7 and Luk 1:71 is at the literary edges, not the characteristic's core. M38-E is explicitly silent — the only characteristic in the cluster with confirmed absence of eschatological trajectory. This produces a finding about the cluster's internal architecture: M38-E is the one characteristic that operates entirely within the present-historical moment, effecting intra-historical status-change without anticipating its own supersession or fulfilment. Every other characteristic, to varying degrees, contains within its present operation some forward-reaching vector. The contrast between M38-E's silence and M38-F's complete eschatological constitution is the cluster's maximum polarity on this dimension.

**T5.1.2 — Nature of Transformation — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The reversibility question reveals the cluster's most complex internal tension. No characteristic presents a fully uniform answer, but the pattern of divergence is structured rather than random. M38-E presents the most unambiguous reversibility: the annual reset and periodic rituals are by design repeatable because the cleansed state does not permanently persist — reversibility is built into the mechanism. M38-B presents physical rescue as reversible-class: the rescued person remains mortal. M38-F presents a split: the anticipated eschatological transformation is explicitly irreversible (2Ti 1:10, Isa 51:6's outlasting of cosmic decay), but the present orientation-transformation requires active maintenance through faith and is evidenced as susceptible to erosion. M38-A presents the sharpest tension within a single characteristic: irreversibility is implied by Heb 7:25 and Eph 2:5, while genuine risk of reversal is attested by 2Pe 2:20, 1Cor 15:2, and Heb 2:3 — the evidence is genuinely mixed and the texts cannot be harmonised without loss. M38-G similarly splits along temporal lines: the eschatological register (olam-joy, Psa 49:15, Hos 13:14) implies irreversibility; the historical-identity register shows that memory can fail (Psa 78:42) and transformation of orientation can be functionally reversed. M38-C presents the strongest cluster-case for irreversibility: no healing is undone in the narrative evidence, and the resurrectional ground of 1Pe 3:21 is itself irreversible. M38-D splits by covenant register: OT repeated atonement implies functional reversibility of the cleansed state; New Covenant "never remembered again" (Heb 8:12) implies irreversibility. The cluster's synthesis: reversibility tracks covenant register and mechanism-type. Systems that require repetition (M38-E, OT atonement) are reversible by design. Systems grounded in Christ's unrepeatable act (M38-A, M38-C, M38-D in NT register) tend toward irreversibility, though the present reception of that irreversible ground still requires ongoing faith-response.

**T5.2.2 — Sequence of Inner States — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — When the per-characteristic sequences are set alongside each other, a composite phenomenological grammar of the salvation cluster emerges. Across M38-A, M38-B, M38-C, M38-D, M38-E, M38-F, and M38-G, the before-state is named with consistent vocabulary: spiritual death, enemy-status, sin-laden, guilt-bearing, unclean, groaning in experienced incapacity, enslaved, endangered. The common element is a before-state defined by the person's objective deficit and subjective recognition of inability. The transitional states diverge more: M38-A, M38-C, and M38-B share a crisis-pivot (the faith-cry, the touch, the panic); M38-D and M38-E share a presentation-pivot (bringing the offering, approaching with the required animal or ransom); M38-F has an extended transitional state unique in the cluster — sustained active waiting with no single crisis-point but an ongoing forward-tension as its operative mode; M38-G's transitional state includes both crisis-cry and commanded memory-enactment. The after-states converge most strongly: justified, forgiven, clean, whole, at peace, restored to joy, ransomed-and-rejoicing. The cluster's after-state vocabulary is more uniform than its transitional vocabulary, suggesting that the different pathways through the middle converge on a shared phenomenological destination. What the sequence reveals about how the cluster works: the mechanism of transformation is heterogeneous (crisis, ritual, sustained waiting, encounter, memory), but the direction of transformation is homogeneous — from deficient and unable to covered and whole. The sequence thus reveals a soteriological structure that is pluriform in means and uniform in telos.

**T5.3.2 — Mechanism of Change — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics distinguish mechanisms by context, and the contextual differentiation reveals a cluster-level pattern: the crisis context universally elicits a sudden-encounter or panic-cry mechanism (M38-A's jailer, M38-B's drowning Peter, M38-C's haemorrhaging woman, M38-D's tax collector, M38-E's plague-interrupting Aaron, M38-G's urgent cry), while the sustained, non-crisis context universally elicits either a gradual-formation mechanism or an extended ritual sequence. The distinction is not arbitrary; it maps onto the person's phenomenological situation — crisis strips away the accumulated structure of gradual formation and forces the mechanism down to its most elemental form: direct cry, direct touch, direct act. This crisis-stripping is itself informative: it reveals that every characteristic has an elemental mechanism that can operate without prerequisite formation or extended ritual, but that the fuller operation of each characteristic typically involves more extended processes. The cluster's one consistent contextual differentiation: the communal or intercommunity context introduces mediation by a third party as the mechanism — M38-E (priest for person), M38-G (intercessor for community, Deu 9:26), M38-F (prophets' sustained longing benefiting later generations), M38-A (apostle's suffering for the elect). Mediated mechanism appears across at least four characteristics in communal or corporate contexts, suggesting that salvation's mechanisms are not uniformly individual even when the transformation is experienced individually. The cluster thus resists a purely individual-encounter account of how change is produced.

**T5.4.2 — Suffering and Affliction — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across the seven characteristics, suffering plays all four named roles — deepening, testing, revealing, producing — but the roles are not evenly distributed. Revealing is the most widely attested function: M38-B (Peter's panic reveals faith's limit), M38-C (acute need reveals faith's necessity by clearing intermediate resources), M38-D (overwhelmed conscience reveals dependence), M38-G (the test of whether ransomed identity is genuinely held). Testing is nearly as widely attested: M38-A (persecution tests whether perseverance is real), M38-B (storm tests faith to its limit), M38-F (deferred hope tests whether anticipation remains operative). Deepening is attested more sparingly: M38-A (Paul's suffering deepens identification with others' salvation), M38-F (prolonged non-receipt may intensify longing rather than diminish it). Producing is the most contested role: M38-B (Heb 5:7-9's formation through suffering produces perfection in the Son), M38-F (2Cor 1:6 is the cluster's strongest evidence for suffering as generative — Paul's afflictions actively work salvation for others). The negative counter-evidence is also cluster-significant: M38-G's Psa 78:42 establishes that suffering experienced in the past does not automatically produce or even maintain the characteristic — memory can fail despite prior affliction; Hos 7:13 shows affliction not producing turning. The cluster finding: suffering reveals and tests universally, deepens frequently, but produces the characteristic in only the highest-register cases (the Son's perfection through suffering, Paul's apostolic affliction working salvation). Ordinary suffering occasions the characteristic; extraordinary suffering within a redemptive vocation can generate it in others.

**T5.5.2 — Formation and Sanctification — What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — When the seven characteristics' formation roles are mapped together, a division of labor within the cluster becomes visible. M38-E maintains sanctification (preservation and restoration of holy standing); M38-A originates sanctification (salvation as the beginning of a holy calling); M38-G motivates sanctification (ransomed identity as the identity-ground that makes ethical formation coherent); M38-F orients sanctification (the eschatological horizon that recalibrates present conduct); M38-D clears the foundation for sanctification (atonement as the prerequisite clearing that makes moral reorientation possible, Pro 16:6); M38-C thresholds sanctification (the healing-wholeness event as the initiating rupture from which formation proceeds); M38-B contributes to sanctification only at its Hebrews edge (Noah's righteousness-formation through faith-obedience). This division of labor suggests that the cluster functions systemically in the formation arc: no single characteristic fully accounts for the entire arc of formation from origination through maintenance through eschatological orientation. Each characteristic makes a distinct contribution — origination (M38-A), foundation-clearing (M38-D), maintenance (M38-E), threshold-opening (M38-C), motivation (M38-G), orientation (M38-F) — and the characteristics are complementary rather than redundant on this dimension. The cluster's formation logic is thus distributed across the seven characteristics in a way that mirrors the multi-stage structure of sanctification itself. The one absence: no characteristic evidences formation as its sole or primary purpose. Formation is always a consequence or context of salvation rather than its central aim.

**T5.6.2 — Eschatological Trajectory — What does the present experience of this characteristic anticipate about its future fullness?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster's eschatological content, read across all seven characteristics, describes a trajectory from partial to complete, from repeatable to once-for-all, from covered-but-remembered to never-remembered-again, from physical rescue to final rescue from death itself, from conditioned orientation to irreversible condition. M38-A anticipates the movement from present justification to final deliverance from wrath — the present is a real but preliminary state. M38-C's present healing anticipates final wholeness in which no sickness or exclusion remains — the healings are proleptic. M38-D anticipates the shift from repeated provisional covering (OT) to permanent eschatological "never remembered again" (Heb 8:12) — a qualitative change, not merely quantitative. M38-F anticipates face-to-face encounter with the Savior's person (Tit 2:13), abolition of mortality (2Ti 1:10), and richly provided entrance into the kingdom (2Pe 1:11) — the most specified eschatological content in the cluster. M38-G anticipates permanence (olam-joy displacing provisional joy), comprehensiveness (ransom from all iniquities, Psa 130:8), and community (the ransomed returning together, Isa 35:10 and 51:11). M38-B anticipates, at its literary edges, the inheritance of the patriarchs not yet received. M38-E is silent. The cluster's aggregate eschatological anticipation thus has three shared structural features: permanence (versus the provisionality of present experience), comprehensiveness (versus the partial coverage of present atonement and rescue), and personal encounter (versus the mediated access of present experience). The eschatological fullness that the cluster as a whole anticipates is characterized by the removal of every structure of mediation, repetition, and partiality that marks the cluster's present operation.

**T5.7.2 — Deposit Consequence — Does the evidence indicate generational consequence — a deposit carried forward beyond the individual?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Generational consequence is attested across five of seven characteristics, with clear evidence in M38-B (Noah's household rescue), M38-E (perpetual statute and priestly succession as generational institutional design), M38-F (prophets' longing benefiting later generations; Isa 51:8's salvation enduring "to all generations"; covenant memory carried across covenant generations), and M38-G (the firstborn-ransom ritual explicitly institutionalized for transgenerational formation, Exo 13:15; subsequent generations addressed as the Exodus-ransomed despite personal non-presence). M38-A shows relational-missional extension (household salvation, apostolic suffering for the elect) rather than strictly biological generational deposit. M38-C is explicitly silent — the individual, immediate character of the healing accounts yields no generational evidence. M38-D shows generational scope in its community-coverage texts (Deu 21:8, Exo 30:15-16, Psa 78:38) but does not name personal deposit carried biologically. The cluster finding: generational consequence is most robust in the characteristics where the salvific act is corporate or institutionally structured (M38-E's priestly system, M38-G's commanded memorial ritual, M38-F's prophetic longing). It is weakest in the characteristics that operate primarily through individual encounter (M38-C, and to a lesser extent M38-B in the panic-cry register). This maps onto a broader cluster principle: corporate and institutionally framed salvation characteristics naturally generate generational consequence through the structural mechanisms that sustain them, while individually-framed characteristics generate generational consequence only when the individual's faith-act is explicitly framed in household or communal terms.

**T5.6.3 — Eschatological Trajectory — If the evidence is silent on eschatological trajectory, note this explicitly.**

**[CLUSTER]** E — M38-E is the one confirmed silence: the priestly mediation machinery corpus contains no eschatological trajectory, and this silence is explicit rather than ambiguous. All other six characteristics provide positive eschatological evidence, ranging from constitutive (M38-F) through extensive (M38-A) through partial (M38-C) through present-at-significant-density (M38-D, M38-G) through present-at-literary-edges (M38-B). The single silence of M38-E against the positive evidence of six others produces the cluster's clearest structural asymmetry: the atonement machinery characteristic operates in a different temporal register from all its cluster partners. It is the one characteristic that does not reach forward. The cluster-level implication: M38-E's intra-historical operation is what makes it available to be completed, superseded, and fulfilled by the once-for-all atonement that M38-A, M38-D, and M38-F all gesture toward eschatologically.
