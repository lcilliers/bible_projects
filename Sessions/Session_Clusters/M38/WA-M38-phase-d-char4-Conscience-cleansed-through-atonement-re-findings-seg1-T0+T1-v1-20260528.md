**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus reveals God as fundamentally a covering, wrath-restraining, compassion-driven being whose inner nature is oriented toward the removal of human guilt rather than its permanent retention. Psa 78:38 describes God as repeatedly atoning for iniquity out of compassion and restraining wrath rather than destroying — the inner-being movement of atonement-giving flows from divine mercy as its source. Psa 65:3 shows God as the one who personally atones for overwhelming transgressions, acting when the human person is helpless. 1Jo 4:10 (M38-D-VCG-04) makes the structural claim explicit: the origin of propitiation is not human initiative but God's prior love — he is the sender of the sin-covering sacrifice. This characteristic therefore images a God whose holiness does not merely condemn but actively provides the means of its own satisfaction, and whose compassion drives him toward the covering of guilt as a core divine act.

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic is named "Conscience cleansed through atonement received." The name is structurally composite and signals several things about its essential nature. First, the primary inner-being locus is the conscience — not the intellect, will, or emotion as such, but the moral-relational faculty that registers guilt and standing before God. Second, the operative verb is "cleansed" — a passive transformation verb, indicating that the conscience does not cleanse itself but is cleansed by an act done to or for it. Third, "through atonement" names the mechanism — the characteristic is not simply forgiveness-as-abstract-declaration but is specifically mediated through the atonement structure (sacrifice, substitution, propitiation). Fourth, "received" confirms the directional asymmetry: the person is recipient, not agent, of the atonement. The name therefore signals at the definitional level: this is a passively-received, mechanism-mediated, conscience-located transformation from guilt-laden to cleared — and it is structurally dependent on an external atoning act.

**T1.4.1 — Modes of Operation — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The verse evidence supports at least five distinct modes of operation within the inner person:

1. **Forensic-declarative mode** (M38-D-VCG-01): The priest declares forgiveness as the direct outcome of sacrifice — guilt is objectively removed, and the inner person receives a declared standing-change. Lev 4:20, 4:26, 5:18 all follow the pattern: act → pronouncement → inner state altered.

2. **Substitutionary-identification mode** (M38-D-VCG-02): Through hand-laying (Lev 1:4), the person's inner being is linked to the sacrifice — the covering comes not through declaration alone but through personal identification with the substituted life. The conscience is cleared because another life has been accepted in the person's place.

3. **Ransom-sustaining mode** (M38-D-VCG-03): Atonement operates as ongoing relational coverage — keeping the person "remembered" before God (Exo 30:16), sustaining the person in divine awareness and protection rather than resolving a discrete sin-event.

4. **Gift-reception mode** (M38-D-VCG-04): God himself is the agent — the inner person receives atonement as pure gift, without initiating or completing the mechanism (Psa 65:3, Psa 78:38, 1Jo 4:10). The conscience is cleared by divine action alone.

5. **Transformative-shame mode** (M38-D-VCG-07): Eze 16:63 uniquely shows that received atonement operates not only to clear but to humble and silence — the inner person is left in wordless contrition, shame, and remembered guilt coexisting with forgiveness. This is a secondary mode where the clearing itself becomes a transformative moral event.

The most consistent immediate inner-being response across the full corpus is transition from burdened to relieved — guilt-laden to forgiven — but the texture of that relief ranges from shame-mixed-with-gratitude (Eze 16:63) to readiness-for-action (Isa 6:7) to dependent-contrite-expectation (Luk 18:13).

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary Hebrew term is H3722B ka.phar (the atoned-state use), and the primary Greek terms are G2433 hilaskomai (verb), G2434 hilasmos (noun), G2435 hilastērios (noun), and G2436 hileōs (adjective). At the definitional level, before deeper lexical work:

- **Ka.phar** carries the basic sense of covering — something placed over guilt so that it is no longer exposed to divine view or wrath. The inner-being state named is one of covered-ness: the person is no longer naked before judgment.
- **Hilaskomai** is to propitiate or make propitious — to effect a change in the standing between a holy party and a guilty party, removing the obstacle (guilt/wrath). The inner-being register is relational-positional: the person's standing has shifted.
- **Hilasmos** names the propitiation as a thing — the sin-covering provision that produces the changed standing. Christ himself is the hilasmos (1Jo 2:2; 1Jo 4:10).
- **Hilastērios** names the place or person as the mercy-seat/propitiation-locus — Christ set forth as the place where wrath meets blood and is satisfied (Rom 3:25).
- **Hileōs** is the adjectival state — God being propitious/merciful, favorably disposed toward rather than opposed (Heb 8:12).

Together these terms define the characteristic as: covered, propitiated, standing in a divinely-shifted relational position — guilt hidden, wrath averted, God favorably disposed. The definitional core is relational-positional covering.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic is composite rather than simple at first encounter. The characteristic is therefore structurally composite: it involves (1) a sin-burdened inner state, (2) an external atoning act, (3) an inner act of reception (faith, contrition, identification), and (4) the resulting inner state of cleared conscience. These are not alternative descriptions but constituent phases of a single phenomenon.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes and resists:

1. **Self-generated moral improvement as a substitute for atonement**: The Levitical corpus makes clear that specific sacrificial acts are required — personal effort, good intentions, or moral reform alone do not produce the inner state of guilt-removed. Isa 22:14 and 1Sa 3:14 (M38-D-VCG-06) show that even religious activity (sacrifice and offering) is insufficient where the atonement-pathway is closed — much less unaided moral effort.

2. **Persistent self-accusation after atonement received**: Heb 8:12's promise that sins will be "remembered no more" structurally resists ongoing self-condemnation in the person who has genuinely received atonement — the conscience-cleared state excludes continued guilt-bearing for covered sin.

3. **Pride and self-sufficiency in approach**: Luk 18:13 (M38-D-VCG-05) implicitly excludes the Pharisaic self-justifying posture (the immediate narrative foil) — the characteristic resists approaching the atonement-mechanism from a position of self-righteousness.

4. **Defiance and hardened indifference**: Isa 47:11 (M38-D-VCG-06) implies that the defiant city's proud self-reliance is precisely what puts atonement out of reach — the characteristic resists the inner posture of "I have no need of covering."

5. **Ritual compliance without sincere heart-seeking**: 2Ch 30:18's positive case (God covers sincere but ritually irregular seekers) implies the exclusion of its negative: ritual compliance without sincere heart-orientation does not guarantee genuine reception.

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, the corpus evidence supports context- and level-dependent variation in operation:

**By context (individual vs. corporate)**: M38-D-VCG-01 operates primarily at the level of individual sin-event resolution (Lev 4:20–6:7 — named individuals, specific offences, personal forgiveness). M38-D-VCG-03 operates at corporate and communal levels: Exo 30:15–16 covers all Israel through the ransom-levy; Deu 21:8 (VCG-07) addresses community blood-guilt through intercession; 2Ch 30:18 covers an entire worshipping assembly through royal intercession.

**By direction (human-initiated vs. divine-initiated)**: In M38-D-VCG-01 and VCG-02, the mechanism is human-initiated — the offerer brings the sacrifice, the priest performs the act. In M38-D-VCG-04, the direction reverses — God acts first as the agent of atonement (Psa 65:3, Psa 78:38, 1Jo 4:10), and the human person is purely receptive.

**By constitutional level (immediate vs. eschatological)**: The Levitical instances (VCG-01) operate at the level of immediate, event-specific guilt resolution. Dan 9:24 and Heb 8:12 (VCG-07, VCG-04) operate at the eschatological-constitutional level — atonement that reshapes the entire moral status of the person permanently, not addressing a discrete sin but the person's whole standing before God.

**T1.5.2 — Immediate Response — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The immediate response varies meaningfully across the verse evidence. The dominant response across M38-D-VCG-01 is the declared state of "forgiven" — a positional change registered without elaboration of inner phenomenology beyond guilt-removal. This is the most consistent pattern but also the most spare. The variation is significant:

- **Eze 16:63** (VCG-07): immediate response is shame and silenced contrition — not peace or relief but wordless humility.
- **Isa 6:7** (VCG-04): immediate response is readiness and vocation — the cleared conscience immediately activates responsive service.
- **Luk 18:13** (VCG-05): the immediate posture is contrition and plea-making; the declared response (the man going home justified) implies an immediate relational shift, but no elaborated inner phenomenology is given.
- **Exo 29:33** (VCG-02): the immediate response is ordination-sealing — the atoned-for state enables the priest to function in his role.
- **2Sa 21:3** (VCG-03): the immediate register is guilt-seeking-resolution — the question "what shall I do?" shows the inner person actively reaching toward the atonement act rather than having received it.

The variation reflects the multi-modal nature of the characteristic (see T1.4.1). The only consistent element across all instances is the directional movement: from uncovered/guilt-laden toward covered/cleared — but the inner phenomenology of that transition differs by context, mode, and constitutional level.

**T1.6.2 — Sustained Effect — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus evidence supports the following sustained inner-being states and capacities established by this characteristic:

1. **Cleared-conscience relational access**: The person who has received atonement retains standing before God — Heb 8:12 (VCG-04) promises permanent memory-clearing of sin, establishing an unobstructed relational orientation toward God.

2. **Moral reorientation**: Pro 16:6 (VCG-04) and Isa 27:9 (VCG-07) both show that sustained atonement-received state is associated with turning from evil and destruction of idolatry — the inner person's will is reoriented by the experience of being covered.

3. **Vocation and service capacity**: Isa 6:7–8 establishes that lips atoned → sustained prophetic service; the cleared conscience is the permanent condition for effective God-ward speech and action.

4. **Humble contrition as a permanent posture**: Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) uniquely establishes that sustained possession of atonement produces not comfort-born forgetting but ongoing remembered shame alongside freedom — a permanently humbled inner disposition.

5. **Eschatological righteousness**: Dan 9:24 (VCG-07) — the ultimate sustained effect is "everlasting righteousness," a permanent righteous standing before God that transcends the repeated cycles of sin and covering.

6. **Fear of the LORD**: Pro 16:6 pairs atonement with "fear of the LORD" as an associated sustained orientation — the atoned-for person maintains a reverent, God-conscious inner posture.

**T1.7.2 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The negative-pole VCG-06 and several other corpus verses identify specific blocking conditions:

1. **Persistent, unrepentant sin**: 1Sa 3:14 (VCG-06) — the iniquity of Eli's house cannot be atoned for because it is persistent and the household has not turned. The inner condition of entrenched, unacknowledged sin closes the atonement-pathway.

2. **Pride and self-sufficiency**: Isa 47:11 (VCG-06) — the proud city finds atonement unavailable. The inner posture of "I have no need" is a blocking condition. Luk 18:13's narrative foil (the Pharisee who lists his own righteousness) implies self-justification as a distorting condition.

3. **Defiance and contempt**: Isa 22:14 (VCG-06) — God's declaration that iniquity will not be atoned for in this lifetime follows the people's self-indulgent defiance in the face of judgment. Willful contempt of the divine warning is a blocking condition.

4. **Hardened indifference to the atoning mechanism**: Num 35:33 (VCG-06) — murder's blood-guilt is not coverable by standard sacrifice because the gravity of the sin exceeds the mechanism's scope. While this is partly a category distinction, it also implies that treating the atoning mechanism as universally available without regard to the nature of the sin distorts reception.

5. **Absence of sincere heart-orientation**: 2Ch 30:18 (VCG-03) implicitly identifies lack of genuine heart-seeking as a blocking condition — sincere seeking enables reception even through ritual imperfection, implying that ritual compliance without sincerity distorts or prevents genuine reception.

**T1.1.3 — Name and Naming — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, all three types of implication are present in the name "Conscience cleansed through atonement received":

**Directional**: "Received" establishes the directionality immediately — the characteristic moves from outside the person inward, from divine-or-priestly act toward the human conscience. This is not a self-generated state; the enquiry is oriented from the outset toward the question of source and mechanism rather than personal cultivation or development.

**Relational**: "Conscience" is inherently a relational faculty — it registers standing in relation to a moral authority (ultimately God). "Atonement" is structurally relational — it presupposes a ruptured relationship between guilty party and holy party, and its function is relational repair. The name orients the enquiry toward the God-human relational axis as the primary register.

**Constitutional**: "Conscience cleansed" locates the characteristic constitutionally in the deep moral-inner faculty of the person, not in behavior, external status alone, or emotion. The name signals that the transformation occurs at the level of the person's inner moral self-awareness, not merely their legal standing in an abstract register. This orients the enquiry toward the inner-being constitutional question of what kind of inner faculty is engaged and transformed.

Together, the name establishes from the outset: this is a divinely-directed (inward from outside), relational (God-person repair), constitutionally-located (conscience) phenomenon.

**T1.2.3 — Kind — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Working description: **Conscience cleansed through atonement received is a passively-received, mechanism-mediated moral-relational condition in which the inner person's guilt-bearing conscience is transformed from sin-laden and wrath-facing to covered, cleared, and standing-restored before God — produced by an external atoning act (priestly sacrifice, divine initiative, or christological propitiation) received through faith, contrition, and self-presentation, and carrying both immediate relief from guilt-burden and sustained effects including moral reorientation, humble contrition, eschatological righteousness, and capacity for vocation.**

Constitutional location: the moral-relational conscience — the faculty that registers standing before God in terms of guilt, right-standing, and wrath-exposure.

Faculties primarily engaged: conscience as primary site; will engaged in self-presentation and moral turning (Lev 1:4, Pro 16:6); faith as the receiving-orientation (Rom 3:25); emotional capacity engaged in shame and gratitude (Eze 16:63).

Impact on the person: positional (standing before God changed), moral (will reoriented away from evil), relational (access to God restored and sustained), and eschatological (oriented toward everlasting righteousness).

**T1.3.3 — Boundary — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The verse evidence permits the following boundary demarcations:

1. **Not mere emotional peace or psychological relief**: The characteristic is a conscience-cleared standing before God, not a subjective feeling of peace. No verse in the corpus describes the inner state in terms of emotional comfort alone — "forgiven" (Lev 4:20 etc.) is a moral-relational declaration, not an emotional description. Where emotion appears (shame in Eze 16:63, contrition in Luk 18:13), it is secondary to and shaped by the conscience-cleared state.

2. **Not self-generated moral improvement**: The entire structure of the characteristic — "received," "through atonement" — excludes the idea that the person produces their own clear standing by becoming morally better. This distinguishes it from repentance (M38's adjacent characteristics) and sanctification.

3. **Not identical to forgiveness as a cognitive knowledge alone**: The characteristic involves conscience transformation, not merely intellectual awareness that one is forgiven. Eze 16:63's shame-response and Luk 18:13's bodily-posture of distance both indicate that genuine reception operates at a deeper level than cognitive assent.

4. **Not permanent liturgical access per se**: The characteristic grants inner-being cleared standing, not automatically all the privileges of holy-place access — Exo 29:33 (VCG-02) shows ordination is required for priestly holy-space activity; atonement is its precondition, not its equivalent.

5. **Not propitiation of an offended human party**: All atonement in this corpus is directed God-ward — even 2Sa 21:3 (VCG-03, David and the Gibeonites) ultimately aims at restoring divine blessing to the land. The characteristic ends at the God-human axis; horizontal human reconciliation is an adjacent but distinct characteristic.

**T1.4.3 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, a communicative mode is evidenced, though it is not primary. Two distinct speech-based operations appear:

1. **Priestly declaration as constitutive speech-act** (M38-D-VCG-01): The priest's pronouncement of forgiveness ("he shall be forgiven," repeated across Lev 4:20, 4:26, 5:10, 5:13, 5:18, 6:7) is a performative speech act — not describing a pre-existing state but constituting the forgiven state through authorized declaration. The inner person's conscience is cleared partly through the priest's verbal pronouncement. This is communicative operation in the declarative-pronouncement mode.

2. **Prayer-as-atonement-seeking** (M38-D-VCG-03, VCG-07): 2Ch 30:18 shows Hezekiah praying for God to atone for the people — speech as the vehicle of corporate atonement-seeking. Deu 21:8 involves a formal verbal plea ("accept atonement, O LORD, for your people Israel"). Psa 79:9 is a communal speech-act requesting atonement alongside deliverance. In these cases, speech mediates the reception of atonement by naming the need before God.

3. **Inverse-communicative mode** (VCG-06): Jer 18:23 uses the prophetic speech-act to invoke atonement's absence — prayer can also call for guilt not to be covered. This is communicative operation in the curse/withholding mode.

The characteristic does not have a mode where the person simply speaks themselves into a cleansed state — speech is the vehicle of priestly declaration or prayer, not the source of atonement itself.

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, the sustained effect demonstrably differs from the immediate response across the corpus evidence. The difference is in two directions:

**Direction 1 — Deepening and moral integration**: The immediate response in M38-D-VCG-01 is the declared state of "forgiven" — a positional change. The sustained effects evidenced in Pro 16:6 (moral turning), Eze 16:63 (permanent shame-humility), and Dan 9:24 (everlasting righteousness) show that over time the characteristic moves from a received positional state to an integrated moral-dispositional transformation. The immediate forgiveness becomes a sustained moral reorientation and eschatological righteousness. This is qualitative deepening, not mere continuation.

**Direction 2 — From specific guilt-relief to comprehensive standing**: The immediate response addresses a specific sin or guilt-event (Levitical VCG-01 pattern: specific offence → specific covering → specific forgiveness). The sustained effect moves toward comprehensive standing before God — Heb 8:12's "I will remember their sins no more" names not the resolution of one sin but a permanent total clearing. Dan 9:24's eschatological framing names the culmination of all atonement as cosmic everlasting righteousness.

The immediate response is thus event-specific, positional, and relief-from-burden; the sustained effect is comprehensive, dispositional, and constitutive of permanent new standing. They are not contradictory but represent temporal deepening: the immediate relief is the entry point into a transformed inner-being state that becomes more fully integrated and permanently established over time.

**T1.7.3 — Conditions of Reception — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — M38-D-VCG-06 names this inner-being state most directly. The person who encounters atonement but does not receive it — whether through blocked pathway, defiance, or permanent judicial closure — remains in the state this characteristic is designed to resolve:

- **Guilt permanent and unresolved**: 1Sa 3:14 — iniquity cannot be atoned for; the inner person carries guilt that no mechanism can reach. The conscience remains condemned rather than cleared.
- **Wrath-facing without relief**: Isa 22:14, Isa 47:11 — the inner person stands before divine judgment with no covering available. The God-ward relational posture is exposed, helpless, judgment-facing.
- **Moral pollution that spreads**: Num 35:33 — blood-guilt not only burdens the individual conscience but pollutes the land; the uncovered inner person's state has communal consequence.
- **Defiant helplessness**: Isa 47:11 describes the proud city as unable to "atone" for the coming disaster by any charm or magical act — the inner state combines pride (still unwilling to seek atonement properly) with helplessness (no mechanism available). This is a particularly acute form of the non-receiving state: the person is both too proud to seek and too helpless to self-rescue.
- **Guilt actively invoked rather than covered**: Jer 18:23 shows that the person who has refused atonement becomes the object of prayers for their guilt's exposure rather than its covering — the non-receiving state can become the target of inverse intercession.

The inner-being state of the non-receiving person is therefore: conscience condemned, wrath retained, guilt accumulated, relational access to God closed — the structural mirror image of conscience-cleansed atonement received.

**T1.8.3 — Dimension Classification — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, secondary dimensions are evidenced, and they do not compete with the primary classification but enrich it:

**Secondary dimension 1 — Emotional-affective**: Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) evidences shame and contrition as inner-being responses to atonement received; Psa 78:38 (VCG-04) involves divine compassion as the source, and the human response implied is relief and gratitude. These are affective states secondary to the primary conscience-clearing. They do not compete because the shame of Eze 16:63 is itself conscience-shaped (moral memory activating affective response), not independent.

**Secondary dimension 2 — Volitional-transformative**: Pro 16:6 (VCG-04) and Isa 27:9 (VCG-07) both show the will reoriented by atonement received — turning from evil, destroying idolatry. This is a volitional secondary dimension. It does not compete with the primary moral-relational conscience classification because the will's reorientation is downstream of the conscience-cleared state, not prior to it.

**Secondary dimension 3 — Relational-covenantal**: Exo 30:16 (VCG-03) — atonement keeps the person "remembered before God," sustaining an active relational covenant-bond. Dan 9:24 (VCG-07) frames atonement within a covenantal-eschatological trajectory. This relational-covenantal dimension is closely adjacent to the primary classification but distinguishable from it — the conscience dimension is about inner moral standing; the relational-covenantal dimension is about ongoing divine-human relationship maintenance.

**Secondary dimension 4 — Vocation/service capacity**: Isa 6:7–8 (VCG-04) shows the cleared conscience enabling prophetic service — a functional-vocational secondary dimension. This does not compete with the primary classification; it is a downstream capacity unlocked by conscience-clearing.

None of these secondary dimensions displace the primary moral-relational conscience classification; they represent the radiation of the characteristic's effects into adjacent inner-being domains.
