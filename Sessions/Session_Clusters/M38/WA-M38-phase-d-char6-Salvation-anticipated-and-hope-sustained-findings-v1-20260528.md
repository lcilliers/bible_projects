**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence reveals God as a being whose nature is irreversibly committed to saving his creatures — a commitment so deep it functions as identity, not merely as action. In M38-F-VCG-04, God arms himself with a helmet of salvation (Isa 59:17), rides a chariot of salvation into cosmic battle (Hab 3:8), and declares he will not rest until Jerusalem's salvation blazes forth (Isa 62:1). These images portray saving intent as warrior-zeal built into the divine character. In M38-F-VCG-02, God's salvation is contrasted with a perishing cosmos (Isa 51:6) and with decaying enemy power (Isa 51:8) — it outlasts all created things because it is grounded in the eternal character of God himself. The human characteristic of salvation-anticipated-and-hope-sustained is therefore a creaturely image of this divine constancy: the soul that waits in hope for salvation is oriented toward a God whose very nature cannot fail to save. The inner posture of expectant trust reflects, in finite and dependent form, the inexhaustible saving energy that belongs to the divine nature.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence suggests that hope-sustained-toward-salvation equips the person to endure the structural gap between promise and fulfillment without collapsing. This is most explicit in Rom 8:24 (M38-F-VCG-05): "saved in hope" — the inner being lives in a present salvation sustained by forward-looking hope, not by sight of completed rescue. The characteristic equips the person to remain morally and volitionally oriented toward God even when the fullness of rescue is not yet visible: Isa 56:1 (M38-F-VCG-01) shows that imminent salvation motivates righteous conduct, aligning the will with justice in anticipation. In 1Th 5:8 (M38-F-VCG-01), hope of salvation arms the inner being for sobriety and vigilance, showing that the characteristic equips for active, disciplined living under eschatological tension. In Heb 9:28 (M38-F-VCG-03), the soul is sustained in "eager waiting" for Christ's return — equipping it for the long arc of patient perseverance. The characteristic therefore serves the person as the faculty of sustained orientation: enabling the person to be a creature capable of living faithfully between the already and the not-yet, anchored in divine promise rather than present circumstance.

**T0.4.1 — Typological Significance — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, the evidence shows typological deployment at multiple registers. At the prophetic-to-christological register, 1Pe 1:10 (M38-F-VCG-01) explicitly constructs a typological sequence: the prophets' anticipatory longing for salvation was itself prophesying "the grace that was to come to you" — their inner posture of hope was typologically oriented toward Christ's appearing, so the characteristic's earlier instances point forward to the Savior's arrival. At the OT-to-eschatological register, the warrior imagery of Hab 3:8 and Isa 59:17 (M38-F-VCG-04) — God riding a chariot of salvation, wearing a helmet of salvation — is absorbed into Paul's armor of God language (Isa 59:17 → 1Th 5:8 in M38-F-VCG-01), where the helmet of salvation is worn by the believer. This is typological transfer: the divine warrior's equipment becomes the creaturely equipment of the hope-sustained soul, signaling participation in the divine saving battle. At the eschatological-christological register, Tit 2:13 (M38-F-VCG-03) and Heb 9:28 make the Savior's appearing the explicit telos toward which the characteristic points, transforming all prior instances of waiting into typological anticipations of the Parousia.

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, and the attribution is striking. In M38-F-VCG-04, God himself wears a helmet of salvation (Isa 59:17) and rides a chariot of salvation (Hab 3:8) — salvation is not merely something God dispenses but something God is clothed in and moves by. In Isa 62:1, God declares he will not be silent or rest until Jerusalem's salvation blazes forth — a declaration of divine restlessness oriented toward the completion of his own saving work, analogous in structure to the human soul's sustained hope. This means that when a human soul sustains hope toward salvation, it is participating in a trajectory that God himself inhabits from the other side: the creature's waiting corresponds to the Creator's not-yet-resting. The human characteristic is thus not merely creaturely emotion but a divinely-anchored posture with an archetype in the divine character. The significance for the human person is profound: hope-sustained is not a deficiency in the person (as if mature persons would need no hope) but a creaturely reflection of divine salvific energy — to hope for salvation is to be aligned with God's own unstoppable movement toward completing it.

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence is mixed and warrants careful distinction. The longing dimension of the characteristic — groaning that salvation is far off (Isa 59:11, M38-F-VCG-01), human effort producing nothing (Isa 26:18, M38-F-VCG-07), Job's peril requiring hope before God (Job 13:16, M38-F-VCG-05) — presupposes the fallen condition. These forms of hope-sustained arise from the anguish of unreceived deliverance and would have no purchase in an unfallen world. The eschatological forward-orientation dimension, however, appears to belong to created design: the capacity to orient the whole self toward a future fullness not yet received (Rom 8:24; Heb 9:28) and the image-bearing reach toward transcendent reality beyond the visible are creaturely capacities that preexist the fall even if the fall intensified their deployment. The prophets' searching (1Pe 1:10) also suggests that anticipatory orientation toward salvation is built into the structure of created historicity — creatures who exist in time are creatures who reach forward toward completion. The most accurate finding is that the characteristic has a dual origin: its structural form (eschatological-forward orientation) belongs to created design; its content as rescue from peril and despair is foregrounded by the fallen condition.

**T0.3.2 — Image-Bearer Expression — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence supports a shared-but-asymmetrical structure rather than either pure identity or pure analogue. The sharing is real: God wears salvation as armor (Isa 59:17), moves toward it militantly (Isa 62:1), and the divine warrior rides by it (Hab 3:8) — the same salvation-orientation characterizes both the divine actor and the human waiter. However, the modes differ fundamentally: God's orientation toward salvation is the movement of the one who accomplishes it, while the human's is the posture of the one who receives it. God does not wait for salvation; he drives it. The human soul awaits what God is irresistibly bringing. The creaturely analogue is therefore the receptive mirror of the divine active form: the same salvation-orientation exists in both, but in God as sovereign warrior-movement and in the human as dependent hope. This asymmetry prevents collapsing the shared element into identity — the human is not a co-savior but an image-bearer whose hope reflects, in dependent receptivity, the divine commitment that funds it.

**T0.4.2 — Typological Significance — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Both directions are evidenced, operating at different registers. The primary direction in M38-F-VCG-04 and the armor imagery is divine-to-human: God's warrior equipment (Isa 59:17) establishes the pattern that is then transferred to the believer (1Th 5:8), so the divine instance grounds and authorizes the human. God's salvific posture is the archetype; the believer's hope-of-salvation is the antitype. In this direction, the typology runs: divine pattern → human participation. The secondary direction in 1Pe 1:10 and the prophetic corpus is human-to-divine-fulfillment: the prophets' inner posture of longing anticipation points forward toward the incarnate Savior's appearing. Here the human instances (prophetic hope, covenantal waiting) are typologically oriented toward the Christ-event as their fulfillment. The typology runs: human anticipation → divine arrival. Both directions co-exist because salvation-as-characteristic is fundamentally relational and bidirectional: God moves toward it from above, the creature reaches toward it from below, and Christ's appearing is the meeting point where both trajectories converge (Heb 9:28; Tit 2:13).

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The characteristic is called "Salvation anticipated and hope sustained." The name has two structural components held in tension. "Salvation anticipated" signals that the object is specific and concrete — not a generic hopefulness but hope directed at a particular divine act (salvation/yeshuah/soteria). The word "anticipated" carries temporal weight: what is anticipated is real and incoming but not yet received; anticipation is oriented toward a future that is already determined. "Hope sustained" signals the inner-being posture required while the anticipation is unfulfilled — the self must actively maintain its orientation against the attrition of delay, suffering, and apparent absence. "Sustained" is significant: it implies duration, effort, and stability under pressure. Together the name signals that this characteristic is not a momentary emotional state but a durable inner posture requiring active maintenance of orientation toward an object (salvation) whose arrival is certain but whose timing creates the necessity for sustained hope. The name also implicitly distinguishes this characteristic from M38-A (salvation already received): the defining feature is precisely the not-yet structure of anticipation rather than the already-received structure of possession.

**T1.2.1 — Kind — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence supports classifying this characteristic as a compound inner-being phenomenon combining dispositional and conditional elements, with recurring volitional acts as its expression. The dispositional element is primary: across VCGs 01–06, the soul is consistently characterized by a settled orientation toward salvation as its object — this is not a series of punctual decisions but a stable stance of the inner being (see the "helmet" imagery of 1Th 5:8, the "citizenship in heaven" of Phili 3:20, the "guarded through faith" of 1Pe 1:5). The conditional element is also present: the soul exists in a specific condition of not-yet-received salvation, which is not chosen but given by the creature's temporal location between promise and fulfillment (Rom 8:24; Heb 9:28). Volitional acts emerge from this disposition-in-condition: Gen 49:18 ("I wait"), Isa 33:2 (the community pleads), Hab 3:8 (confidence in divine warrior action). The VCG-07 evidence (Isa 26:18) introduces a condition-of-failure state that the characteristic by contrast excludes. The most accurate working classification is: a sustained dispositional orientation (hope-directed-toward-salvation) that exists as a condition of creaturely temporality and expresses itself in recurring volitional acts of waiting, trust, and anticipation.

**T1.3.1 — Boundary — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence from M38-F-VCG-07 and the negative cases within other VCGs points to two structural opposites, which may be understood as the two failure modes of this characteristic. The first is self-reliant striving toward self-achieved deliverance: Isa 26:18 (VCG-07) shows the community laboring at deliverance and producing nothing — wind, not salvation. This is hope directed toward the wrong object (human capacity) rather than sustained toward divine salvation. The second is despair-under-deferred-hope: Isa 59:11 (VCG-01) shows the inner being groaning with salvation far off, with no divine anchor to sustain the waiting. This is the collapse of sustained orientation into hopeless mourning. These two failure modes together define the boundary: on one side, the soul that turns from waiting on God toward self-rescue; on the other, the soul that loses orientation entirely and sinks into despair. Salvation-anticipated-and-hope-sustained stands between these — it is the posture that neither grasps at self-rescue nor collapses into despair, but actively maintains orientation toward the God who will accomplish what the creature cannot (Isa 62:1; Hab 3:8).

**T1.8.1 — Dimension Classification — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary Hebrew term is **H3444 ye.shu.ah** (salvation, deliverance, rescue — from yasha', to save, deliver, give width/freedom to) and the primary Greek terms are **G4991 soteria** (salvation, deliverance, preservation), **G4990 sōtēr** (Savior, deliverer), and **G4982 sōzō** (to save, rescue, make whole). At the definitional level, before lexical depth, these terms reveal the following:

Ye.shu.ah names a concrete act of rescue — movement from constraint to spaciousness, from peril to safety. It is inherently transitive and relational: someone saves someone from something. The term used as the object of hope (Gen 49:18: "I wait for your ye.shu.ah") reveals that what the soul waits for is not an abstract state but a specific divine act of release.

Soteria in Greek similarly names a concrete rescue or preservation from threat — it carries the connotation of wholeness restored. G4990 sōtēr names the agent of that rescue as a personal title, concentrating the hope in a Person. G4982 sōzō as a verb (Rom 8:24) names the same rescue as a process. Taken together, at the pre-analytical level, these terms reveal that the characteristic is not hope-in-general but hope structured around a specific relational rescue — salvation by a named Savior from a real peril. The inner being is not oriented toward wellness or flourishing abstractly, but toward the act of a Person who rescues.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — At first encounter the characteristic appears structurally compound, with at least three identifiable constituent elements that the name already implies.

**Element 1 — Anticipation**: The inner-being act of directed, forward-oriented expectation. This is cognitive and intentional — the mind and will are directed toward something not yet received. It presupposes knowledge of what is anticipated (salvation, the Savior) and confidence in its coming.

**Element 2 — Hope**: The affective-volitional faculty of sustained trust toward the anticipated object. Hope is more than anticipation — it carries the element of personal trust in the one who has promised. 1Pe 1:5 (VCG-01) names faith as the faculty through which the believer is guarded; 1Ti 4:10 (VCG-03) identifies hope set on the living God as Savior.

**Element 3 — Sustaining/endurance**: The temporal dimension — this characteristic is not a momentary orientation but one that must be maintained under delay, suffering, and apparent absence (Rom 8:24; 2Pe 3:15; 2Cor 1:6). The "sustained" element is not automatic but requires something to maintain it.

These three elements are distinguishable but not separable — they co-constitute the characteristic. At first encounter the structure is not simple.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The evidence supports identifying several things this characteristic explicitly excludes or resists.

**It resists despair**: The sustained dimension of the characteristic is precisely the maintenance of orientation against the pull of despair that arises from deferred hope (Isa 59:11, VCG-01; Isa 26:18, VCG-07). The characteristic is constitutively anti-despair — to sustain hope is to refuse the inner collapse that the absence of received salvation would otherwise produce.

**It resists self-reliance as substitute**: Isa 26:18 (VCG-07) explicitly excludes the posture of self-achieved deliverance — labor that produces wind rather than salvation. The characteristic resists any inner turning away from God toward human capacity as the source of rescue.

**It resists the confusion of salvation-not-yet with salvation-never**: 2Pe 3:15 (VCG-05) — the characteristic reframes delay as mercy, resisting the interpretive move that reads God's patience as absence or failure.

**It resists premature closure**: The characteristic is structurally incompatible with a soul that has declared itself already arrived — the eagerly waiting posture of Heb 9:28 and Phili 3:20 excludes a settled presumption that the eschatological fullness is already possessed.

**It resists fear of death and cosmic dissolution**: 2Ti 1:10 (VCG-03) — the Savior's appearing abolished death and brought immortality to light; the characteristic resists existential terror by orienting the soul toward the One who has already undone mortality's finality.

This points the enquiry toward the question of which constitutional element (spirit, soul, mind, will) is the seat of this characteristic. The noun "hope" combined with the verb "sustained" implies both a faculty and an act of maintaining it — constitutional and volitional together.

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

The immediate and sustained effects share the same directional content (salvation/Savior as object) but differ in depth, stability, and constitutional level.

(3) **Argumentatively**: Within Char-6, Gen 49:18 functions as the **prototypical OT form** of the characteristic: a single naming of the inner posture ('I wait') + the object ('your salvation') + the source ('O LORD') — establishing the three-part structure that recurs across the corpus.

**T7.2.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

The logical form is: **eschatological imminence → present ethical obligation**. (2) **1Pe 1:5 structure** — Premise 1: God's power guards believers through faith; Premise 2: there is a salvation ready to be revealed at the last time; Conclusion: therefore the believer's present faith-posture is sufficient and secure. (4) **Heb 9:28 structure** — Premise 1: Christ bore sin in his first appearing; Premise 2: he will appear a second time not for sin but for salvation; Conclusion: therefore those who eagerly wait will receive final salvation. (5) **Rom 8:24 structure** — Premise 1: we were saved (aorist: completed); Premise 2: hope for what is already seen is not hope; Conclusion: therefore the inner posture of sustained forward-hope is the appropriate form of present salvation-possession. Logical form: **received-but-incomplete salvation → hope as the correct present inner-being form**.

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — **Gen 49:18** ('I wait for your salvation, O LORD') reveals what no other verse in the corpus reveals as directly: **the irreducible grammatical minimum of the characteristic**. This nakedness of the confession is unique in the corpus — no other verse strips the characteristic to so bare a form. What this reveals: (1) **The characteristic is not dependent on any theological argument for its existence**: it is simply named as an act of the soul ('I wait') directed toward a named object ('your salvation') before a named source ('O LORD'). (2) **The characteristic is fundamentally personal and first-person**: all other verses in the corpus address communities (Isaiah's communal laments), theological arguments (Rom 8:24, Heb 9:28), or metaphorical descriptions (1Th 5:8, Isa 26:1). Gen 49:18 alone gives the characteristic in the first-person singular — the individual soul's bare confession of waiting. (3) **The characteristic existed at the beginning of the canonical story** — Jacob's deathbed prayer establishes that salvation-anticipation is not a late development in Israel's inner-being vocabulary but a primordial one. The simplicity of the form at the canon's opening gives all subsequent, more complex expressions their root-meaning.

**T7.3.1 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The most useful human science framework for Char-6 is **cognitive-developmental psychology of hope**, particularly as theorized in **C. R. Snyder's Hope Theory** (motivation-oriented) and **developmental psychology's treatment of deferred gratification and future-oriented cognition**. Snyder's Hope Theory posits two cognitive components: (1) **Agency thinking** — belief that one can initiate and sustain movement toward a goal; (2) **Pathways thinking** — belief that viable routes to the goal exist. Char-6's evidence maps onto this framework with a significant modification: the agency is **transferred to God** rather than residing in the self (1Pe 1:5: 'guarded by God's power'; Isa 62:1: God will not rest until...). The pathways thinking is constituted by **covenant and promise** (the revealed route to salvation is through God's declared purpose). A second useful framework is **attachment theory's concept of secure-base functioning under delayed reunion**: Bowlby's observation that securely attached individuals can sustain hopeful waiting during separation because they have internalized the attachment figure's reliability maps onto the Char-6 posture where the soul sustains hope under the 'absence' of realized salvation because of the internalized trustworthiness of the divine Savior (Isa 51:6–8). A third framework is **terror management theory** — the verses that contrast salvation's permanence with cosmic dissolution (Isa 51:6) and death's abolition (2Ti 1:10) address the foundational anxiety of mortality.

**T7.3.2 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three illuminating applications: (1) **Snyder's Hope Theory + 1Pe 1:5 and Isa 62:1**: The framework reveals why the divine preservation and divine warrior declarations (VCG-04) are not merely theological assertions but **functional psychological infrastructure** for the soul's hope-posture. The soul's ability to sustain anticipation (Char-6) depends on its cognition of viable pathways (God's covenant promises as routes) and agency (God's power as the agent). Without these cognitive anchors, the hope would collapse into the despair of VCG-07 (Isa 26:18). The framework makes coherent why the corpus contains both divine-warrior declarations and the community's sustained hope in the same sub-group. (2) **Deferred gratification + 1Th 5:8 and Isa 56:1**: Research on future-oriented cognition confirms that the ability to defer gratification (continue waiting) is sustained by concrete representation of the future goal. Isa 56:1's mechanism (imminent salvation motivating present righteousness) and 1Th 5:8's armor-metaphor (hope as helmet protecting the mind) both function as **concrete cognitive representation mechanisms** that sustain the deferral of reception. The framework illuminates why the corpus employs such vivid imagery. (3) **Attachment theory + Isa 51:6–8 and Rom 8:24**: Secure-base functioning under delay maps precisely onto Rom 8:24's 'saved in hope' — the soul possesses an internalized security (the completed work of salvation) that permits it to sustain forward-hoping without the anxiety of potential abandonment. The framework reveals that Char-6 is a **secure-attachment expression**, not an anxious-attachment one, despite the absence of full reception.

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two significant divergences: (1) **Agency divergence**: Snyder's Hope Theory places agency (the capacity to initiate movement toward the goal) in the hoping subject. Char-6 consistently displaces agency onto God: 1Pe 1:5 ('guarded by God's power'), Isa 62:1 (God will not rest until...), Hab 3:8 (God rides the chariot). The soul's role is **receptive orientation**, not agentic pursuit. This divergence reveals that Char-6 is not well-described by any hope-theory that requires self-agency: the characteristic's distinctive structure is **hope sustained by transferred agency** — the soul hopes precisely because it is not the one doing the saving work. Human science frameworks built on self-determination theory would mischaracterize this as passive or helpless; the biblical evidence shows it as **a different form of active orientation** in which trust-in-the-agent replaces self-agency. (2) **Meaning-of-delay divergence**: From a motivational psychology standpoint, prolonged delay without feedback typically produces hope-extinction. Char-6 contains verses that reinterpret delay as salvifically meaningful (2Pe 3:15: 'the Lord's patience is salvation') — a move that human science would classify as a coping mechanism or motivated cognition. The verse evidence claims the reinterpretation is **ontologically true** (the delay is salvation, not merely experienced as such). The divergence reveals that Char-6 operates at an **ontological level** that human science frameworks cannot access from within their own methods: the claim is not that hope-sustaining reinterpretation is psychologically adaptive, but that God's purposes actually make the delay salvifically real.

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two aspects surfaced by human science that deserve further verse investigation: (1) **The phenomenology of hope's erosion**: Human science on deferred hope documents the specific psychological trajectory of hope declining toward despair — including the role of social support, repetition of promises, and community belonging in sustaining hope under extended delay. The Char-6 corpus contains VCG-07 (Isa 26:18: failed effort, despair) and Isa 59:11 (mourning of absence), but these are brief. The corpus does not extensively address **the mechanisms by which the soul's anticipatory posture is maintained over extended time**. Further investigation of texts such as Ps 130 ('I wait for the LORD, my soul waits... more than watchmen wait for morning') would address this gap. (2) **Communal vs. individual dimensions of hope-maintenance**: Human science documents that hope is socially constituted — communities sustain individual hope-postures through shared practice, liturgy, and narrative. Char-6 contains both communal (Isa 33:2, Luk 1:69, Luk 3:6) and individual (Gen 49:18, Job 13:16) expressions, but does not explicitly address the **social-maintenance mechanisms** of salvation-anticipation. Investigation of Heb 10:24–25 ('stirring one another up to love and good deeds... not neglecting to meet together') and related texts would address whether communal practice is explicitly linked to hope-maintenance in the broader biblical evidence base.
