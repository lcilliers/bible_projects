## 1. What this study is

The Bible's vocabulary for being rescued, restored, or saved is wider than the English word "salvation" suggests. Across the Old Testament and the New, this vocabulary organises into seven distinct inner-life realities — each describing what salvation looks like from a particular angle of the human person. This study takes the seven in turn and traces how they are named, where they live in the inner being, how they move, how they form, how they relate to each other, and what human and clinical sciences observe about the corresponding human capacities.

The seven characteristics can be sketched briefly. *Eschatological salvation received by faith* is the comprehensive inner-being movement of receiving, through faith, a salvation whose final term is rescue from the wrath to come and entry into the life of the age to come. *Physical rescue from mortal danger* is the crisis characteristic — the inner-being state of one whose life is materially imperilled and who turns toward the qualified rescuer. *Healing wholeness through faith exercised* is the constitutional-restoration characteristic — the inner movement of the one who, exercising trust, receives bodily, social, and conscience-level restoration. *Conscience cleansed through atonement received* is the moral-relational characteristic — the inner state of the one whose guilt has been covered by a qualified mediating act. *Priestly mediation — the machinery of atonement* is the vocational characteristic — the inner-being equipment of the qualified mediator who stands between holy God and guilty community. *Salvation anticipated and hope sustained* is the temporal-anticipatory characteristic — the inner-being orientation toward a salvation that is real but not yet possessed. *Ransomed identity, gratitude and memory* is the identity-constituting characteristic — the inner-being state of the one whose self-understanding is permanently structured by remembered ransom.

What binds the seven into one study is divine attribution and a shared semantic field. Across all seven, the salvation in view originates with God: the doxology of the redeemed multitude in heaven names it without qualification — "Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" (Rev 7:10). The Greek *sōzō* and its noun forms cover four of the seven characteristics, and the Hebrew root families *yāšaʿ*, *kāpar*, and *pādāh* cover the rest; the vocabulary is shared at the root level even where the operational forms differ. The seven are not seven words for one thing nor one word for seven different things — they are configurations of a shared salvific vocabulary applied to distinguishable inner-being domains.

One feature of the family deserves naming at the opening. Six of the seven characteristics describe the inner-being state, posture, or movement of the *recipient* of salvation. The seventh — priestly mediation — describes the inner-being equipment of the *enactor* of one part of the salvation-mechanism. This is not an inconsistency in the family; it is the structural shape of the salvation economy. Both sides are required, and Scripture treats both with care. The pattern of asymmetry — God always on the giving and initiating side; humans on the receiving side, with one vocational exception — runs through every characteristic in the study, and the chapters that follow trace it consistently.

The chapters take the seven in turn at depth. Chapter 2 introduces each characteristic in plain English. Chapter 3 examines what Scripture says about God in each — the divine pattern that grounds the human form. Chapter 4 places each characteristic in the inner person — where it lives, which capacities it engages. Chapter 5 sets the topography in motion — how each characteristic moves, forms, and operates across distinct modes. Chapter 6 maps the seven against each other within the family. Chapter 7 reflects briefly on what human and clinical sciences observe about the corresponding capacities. Three short appendices close the study with the term inventory, the verse table, and a method note.
