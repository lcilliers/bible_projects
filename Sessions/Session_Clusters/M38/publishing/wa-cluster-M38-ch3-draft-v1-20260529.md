## 3. The divine pattern

The most consistent thing Scripture says about God in this study is that he is always on the giving side of the saving transaction. Across the seven characteristics, the cumulative weight of the evidence presents a God who saves, rescues, heals, atones, designs the mediating institution, sustains the not-yet, and ransoms — without himself ever being saved, rescued, atoned-for, mediated for, or ransomed. The asymmetry is structural and runs through every characteristic. The doxology of the redeemed multitude names it in a single ascription: "Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" (Rev 7:10). The verse is structurally precise — salvation is named not as something God does on rare occasion but as something that belongs to him, as constitutive of who he is. The seven characteristics studied here are seven angles on this single divine reality.

The verses that establish the divine character behind these characteristics are striking for how consistently they ground the saving action in mercy rather than in justice-requiring-merit. Paul to Titus: "He saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" (Tit 3:5). The Psalmist on God's repeated atoning act: "He, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often" (Psa 78:38). Moses on the Exodus: "It is because the Lord loves you and is keeping the oath that he swore to your fathers, that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand and redeemed you from the house of slavery" (Deu 7:8). John on the origin of propitiation: "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1Jo 4:10). Four characteristics, four texts, one consistent grounding. The character that produces the saving acts is mercy, compassion, covenantal love — the same character expressed in four different operations.

God does not only act with saving character; he provides the means by which his character can reach the human condition. Leviticus 17:11 makes the divine institution of the atoning mechanism explicit: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life." The act is not a human technique applied to God; it is a divine provision applied to the human condition. Romans 3:25 carries the same logic into the New Testament focal point: "whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith." The propitiation is God's act of putting-forward; the human side is reception by faith. Across the entire arc — from the Levitical altar to the cross — the mechanism by which salvation reaches the creature is given, not generated.

One register in this study stands apart for its depth of divine self-involvement. The prophets present a God who is not merely the dispenser of salvation but its wearer and driver, restless until his saving purpose is complete. Isaiah sees the divine warrior: "He put on righteousness as a breastplate, and a helmet of salvation on his head" (Isa 59:17). The same prophet hears the divine commitment to non-rest: "For Zion's sake I will not keep silent, and for Jerusalem's sake I will not be quiet, until her righteousness goes forth as brightness, and her salvation as a burning torch" (Isa 62:1). Habakkuk sees the militant motion: "Was your wrath against the rivers, O Lord? Was your anger against the rivers, or your indignation against the sea, when you rode on your horses, on your chariot of salvation?" (Hab 3:8). These verses move the divine portrait past benevolent provision into committed warrior-zeal. The God of this study is not a distant giver who has provided what the creature needs and stepped back; he is invested in the completion, identity-bound to the saving work.

Across the seven characteristics a partition runs through the question of whether Scripture attributes the characteristic to God himself. Three characteristics are explicitly attributed to God: salvation as his act and desire (Tit 3:5; 1Ti 2:4), atonement as his provision (Psa 65:3; Lev 17:11), and salvation as his warrior-orientation (Isa 59:17; Isa 62:1). Two characteristics have an asymmetric attribution — God is named on the response side but not on the creaturely-exercise side: physical rescue (God hears, but does not himself cry in panic) and healing wholeness (God responds to faith, but does not himself exercise faith). Two characteristics have no parallel possession on the divine side at all: priestly mediation (God designs the institution but does not undergo it) and ransomed identity (God is the ransomer, not the ransomed). The partition is not deficiency. It is the structural shape of the salvation economy, and it tells the reader something the family-name "salvation" alone cannot: that what salvation is varies according to which side of the divine-creature relation it operates on.

A third pattern follows from the partition: typology in this study runs predominantly from divine instance downward to establish the human form, but the priestly characteristic introduces a real counter-current. The dominant direction appears across nearly every characteristic — the Exodus rescue establishes the type whose later instances of ransomed identity participate in; Noah's flood-rescue establishes the type whose antitype is baptism-as-conscience-appeal (1Pe 3:21); the divine warrior's helmet of salvation establishes the pattern that the believer's helmet of hope imitates (Isa 59:17 → 1Th 5:8). The counter-current appears in the priestly mediation: God designs the human priestly institution, and the institution itself — with its annual reset, the mediator's own required self-covering, the sanctuary still defiled year after year — points forward by its own incompleteness to a greater high priest. This is the one place where a human instance is, structurally, the type pointing toward a divine-human antitype.

One further pattern runs across all seven without exception: every characteristic points beyond its present operation toward a completion not yet reached. Salvation already received is also salvation still to be saved from wrath ("Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God" — Rom 5:9). Atonement already received in its Levitical form points toward the once-for-all act that fulfills it. Priestly mediation, by its own internal incompleteness, points to a greater high priest. Salvation anticipated is by definition forward. Ransomed identity, looking backward to the Exodus, reaches forward to the ransom from Death itself: "I shall ransom them from the power of Sheol; I shall redeem them from Death" (Hos 13:14). The eschatological orientation is universal across this study, and it is the final mark of who is on the saving side: the one who has begun the work is also the one who will complete it.


### 3.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

This is the characteristic where the divine pattern operates in its most explicit form. The attribution to God is direct and repeated: God saves, God desires that all be saved (1Ti 2:4), God exalts Jesus as Saviour (Act 5:31), salvation belongs to God (Rev 7:10). The triple negation in Paul's compressed statement to Titus — "not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" (Tit 3:5) — names the operative attribute. Mercy, not justice-requiring-merit, is what is moving on the divine side. The Ephesian formulation makes the gift-structure explicit: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8). The asymmetry runs cleanly: God acts; the human person receives by faith. Where the human side participates instrumentally — extending salvation to others by witness or persistence — the participation derives from the primary divine act rather than competing with it.


### 3.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

The divine pattern here is asymmetric. The rescue-capacity is divinely attributed: God hears, draws near, extends his hand. But Scripture is silent on God's experience of mortal terror; God does not cry in panic, does not face extinction, does not require rescuing from death. The asymmetry locates the creaturely faculty exactly: the inner-being collapse into the bare cry is the human side of a transaction whose divine side is sovereign responsiveness. Where this characteristic reaches closest to divine inner being is Hebrews 5:7, where the Son in his human nature "offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverence." Even here, the verse situates the cry in his creaturely human condition ("in the days of his flesh"); the divine side of the transaction is the Father who hears. The silence about a God who himself panics, despairs, or needs rescue is meaningful — it marks the boundary of the image. The image-bearer is made to address the rescuer; the rescuer does not himself need rescue.


### 3.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

The divine side of this characteristic is responsiveness: God in Christ responds to interior trust by healing the whole person — body, conscience, social standing, relational direction. The attribution formula "your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22) names the structure precisely. Christ heals; the human person's faith is the operative instrument by which the healing reaches them. Scripture is silent on God exercising faith as an inner faculty — faith presupposes a giver and a receiver, and God is always the giver. This silence is informative: faith-as-reception is the distinctively creaturely form of the relational structure between God and the image-bearer. The character of God disclosed by this characteristic is responsive integrative wholeness — God does not partition body from spirit in the act of rescue, and his responsive character is activated by the inner faith-movement that the creature directs toward him.


### 3.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

The atoning act is explicitly attributed to God. The Psalmist names God as the one who atones when iniquity has overwhelmed the creature ("When iniquities prevail against me, you atone for our transgressions" — Psa 65:3). The same Psalmist names the divine character moving the atoning act ("He, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them" — Psa 78:38). The Levitical institution names God as the provider of the mechanism (Lev 17:11). John names the prior love that sends the propitiation (1Jo 4:10). The receiving side is creaturely: the human person, on whose behalf the priest acts and whose conscience the act addresses, receives what God has provided. The divine character disclosed is compassion-driven covering — the holiness of God does not merely condemn but actively provides the means of its own satisfaction. Ezekiel names the inner response Scripture invites in the recipient: "that you may remember and be confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done" (Eze 16:63). The gravity of being-genuinely-covered shapes the inner response.


### 3.5 Priestly mediation machinery of atonement

This is the one characteristic in the study where the divine treatment runs in a counter-current. God designs the priestly institution: "And this shall be a statute forever for you, that atonement may be made for the people of Israel once in the year because of all their sins" (Lev 16:34). The institution is divinely commanded and divinely sustained. But God does not himself undergo the mediating act. He is not a priest within the Levitical system; he is its author. What this counter-current produces is an upward typology: the human institution, with its annual reset and the mediator's own required self-covering ("Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" — Lev 16:6), points beyond itself to a greater high priest. The institution is incomplete in itself, and the incompleteness is its own forward-pointing structure. The divine character disclosed is one whose holiness requires mediation across the gap between God and the guilt-laden creature, whose mercy provides the structure by which mediation can take place, and whose provision points beyond the institution to the One who fulfills it.


### 3.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

This is the characteristic where the divine treatment reaches its most striking depth. God does not merely give salvation; he wears it — "a helmet of salvation on his head" (Isa 59:17) — and rides toward it militantly ("on your horses, on your chariot of salvation" — Hab 3:8), restless until it is complete ("For Zion's sake I will not keep silent ... until her righteousness goes forth as brightness, and her salvation as a burning torch" — Isa 62:1). This is real sharing across the divine-creature relation: the salvation-orientation characterises both the divine actor and the human waiter, though the modes differ fundamentally. God drives salvation toward its completion; the creature awaits what God is bringing. The creaturely form of waiting under deprivation — fruitless human effort that "we have given birth to wind" (Isa 26:18) — belongs entirely to the human side, but the orientation toward the completion is shared. This is the only place in the study where the inner-being posture has a real divine analogue, asymmetric in mode but identical in object. Both God and the believer are oriented toward the same salvation; both are committed to its completion.


### 3.7 Ransomed identity, gratitude and memory

The ransom is divine; the ransomed identity is creaturely. God acts; the human person remembers and is constituted by what God has done. The verse evidence makes a further claim here that is distinctive of this characteristic: God ransoms to disclose who he is. David's prayer holds the structure: "the one nation on earth whom God went to redeem to be his people, making himself a name, and doing for them great and awesome things" (2Sa 7:23). The ransoming act is not only for the creature's sake; it is the act through which God discloses his own identity to the watching world. The covenantal love that grounds the ransom (Deu 7:8 — "It is because the Lord loves you ... that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand and redeemed you from the house of slavery") and the ransoming activity itself are both constitutive of divine self-revelation. The creaturely correlate — the inner-life of the ransomed person — is identity grounded in remembered deliverance, gratitude expressed in ethical generosity toward the vulnerable (Deu 15:15; Deu 24:18), and intercessory appeal grounded in the past act (Deu 9:26). The silence about a God who himself holds ransomed gratitude is structural: God by nature cannot be ransomed, and the gratitude that flows from having been ransomed therefore belongs entirely to the creaturely side of the image.
