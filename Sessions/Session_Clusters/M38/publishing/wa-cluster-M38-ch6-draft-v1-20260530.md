## 6. How each characteristic relates to the others

The seven characteristics in this study do not stand alone. They pair, oppose, produce, and follow each other across the verse evidence, and the family's deeper unity is best seen by tracing those relationships. The sections that follow take each characteristic in turn and map its position within the network.


### 6.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

Of the seven characteristics in this study, this one occupies a hub position — it is the event-state from which forgiveness, justification, peace, joy, assurance, and perseverance radiate, and to which faith and word-reception flow. Its strongest internal pairing is with forgiveness and conscience-cleansing: Luke pairs salvation with peace at the close of the woman's anointing — "and he said to the woman, 'Your faith has saved you; go in peace'" (Luk 7:50) — and Acts pairs the call with forgiveness — "repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins" (Act 2:38). The same pairing recurs in Rev 12:10 where the accuser is defeated and salvation announced together.

The strongest opposition is perishing. Paul sets the two states against each other: "for the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God" (1Co 1:18). 2Co 2:15 deploys the same contrast. The structural opposite is not a different inner-being characteristic competing with salvation; it is the inner state of those who have closed against reception — those who "refused to love the truth and so be saved" (2Th 2:10), those who neglect the saving message (Heb 2:3), those whose attempted ground is self-generated works (Eph 2:8–9; Tit 3:5; 2Ti 1:9).

This characteristic is produced by grace and word and faith together. Grace is the originating cause: "by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8). The word is the instrumental cause: "it pleased God through the folly of what we preach to save those who believe" (1Co 1:21); Eph 1:13 sequences "heard the gospel of salvation, believed, and were sealed with the promised Holy Spirit." Faith is the inner-being mode of reception. The three together — grace as origin, word as instrument, faith as reception — produce the salvation event.

This characteristic produces peace, joy, assurance, and perseverance as its downstream effects. Peace is the immediate inner-being consequence (Luk 7:50). Joy erupts in the doxological completion: "salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" (Rev 7:10; cf. Rev 19:1; 2Co 9:15). Assurance follows from the structure of grace received: "since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God" (Rom 5:9); the perpetual intercession of Christ secures it (Heb 7:25). Perseverance forms across time: "the one who endures to the end will be saved" (Mat 10:22); Phi 2:12 names the working-out.

The shared vocabulary that binds this characteristic to its neighbours in the family is concentrated in the *sōzō* root. The Greek verb generates *sōtēria* (salvation), *sōtēr* (Saviour), *sōtērion* (saving), and *sōtērios*; the same root operates across physical rescue (characteristic 2), healing wholeness (characteristic 3), and salvation anticipated (characteristic 6). The root unity at the lexical level reflects a theological unity: the same God saves from sin, from danger, from disease, and toward the eschatological completion. The same Eph 2:8 — "by grace you have been saved through faith" — names the gift-faith structure that also operates in the healing characteristic's attribution formula and in the anticipation characteristic's "saved in hope" (Rom 8:24).

A relational fact runs outward to a characteristic not in this study: this characteristic's nearest extra-family neighbour is justification (the forensic declaration of righteousness), which Paul names in the same breath at Rom 10:9–10 — "with the heart one believes and is justified, and with the mouth one confesses and is saved" — and at Rom 5:9. Justification is treated in the wider research programme; the relational point here is that within this study's family, salvation is the comprehensive event of which justification is the forensic-relational facet.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the comprehensive node — the inner-being event toward which the others tend, from which they radiate, and around which the family's vocabulary clusters.


### 6.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its tight coupling to acute bodily threat and to the inner-being responses that threat precipitates. Within the family, its closest neighbour is the eschatological-reception characteristic (above); the two share the *sōzō/sōtēria* root directly, and the boundary between them is held by the type of rescue in view rather than by lexical difference. "Lord, save me" (Mat 14:30) and "I want to remind you ... that Jesus, who saved a people out of the land of Egypt, afterward destroyed those who did not believe" (Jude 5) both use the same verb; one names physical rescue, the other names eschatological deliverance and warning.

Pairings within this characteristic concentrate at terror and the faith-cry. "Save us, Lord; we are perishing" (Mat 8:25) joins fear and petition in a single phrase. "And he was heard because of his reverence" (Heb 5:7) joins anguish and the divine response. The corpus also pairs reverent faith with corporate deliverance: "by faith Noah, being warned by God concerning events as yet unseen, in reverent fear constructed an ark for the saving of his household" (Heb 11:7). The pairings reveal that the characteristic does not stand as an isolated state; it always appears with the affective and volitional components that constitute it.

The strongest opposition is the inner-being state that prevents the cry. Faithless demand is one form — the criminal next to the cross who taunts "Are you not the Christ? Save yourself and us!" (Luk 23:39) demonstrates the inverse: the rescue-vocabulary present without the inner posture that makes it real. Despair-closure is another — "all hope of our being saved was at last abandoned" (Act 27:20). The structural opposite is not self-rescue (which the characteristic also excludes) but the inner ground that cannot or will not appeal.

The mockery sequence at the cross deserves separate naming. "He saved others; he cannot save himself" (Mar 15:31; Mat 27:42; Luk 23:35) presses the rescue-vocabulary into ironic service. The opposition here is not the absence of the characteristic but its misreading: the mockers assume a power-logic standard that the saving act inverts.

This characteristic is produced by mortal threat — the external precondition without which it does not arise. The corpus is silent on spiritual-being mediation; no angelic ministry, no demonic attack mediates the rescue. What it produces depends on the mode. In the cry-and-rescue mode it produces deliverance from the immediate threat. In the faith-obedience mode (Noah) it produces both household safety and the formed disposition of righteousness-by-faith. In the willed-surrender mode (Heb 5:7), it produces the perfected obedience the Son learned through suffering.

The shared vocabulary binds this characteristic to the eschatological-reception characteristic at *sōzō/sōtēria*, to the anticipation characteristic at the same root, and to the ransomed-identity characteristic at *pādāh* through Psa 69:18 ("Draw near to my soul, redeem me; ransom me because of my enemies"). The lexical bridges reveal that physical rescue, eschatological salvation, sustained hope, and ransomed identity are not unrelated phenomena but differentiated expressions of one semantic field — rescue from danger by a more powerful agent. The boundary between them is held by the type of danger and the temporal horizon, not by the vocabulary.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the crisis-entry point — the inner-being mode in which the rescuer is addressed at the limit of creaturely capacity, and from which extended formation (Noah) or perfected obedience (the Son) can flow.


### 6.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its convergence position: it is the point at which faith as activating faculty, wholeness as received outcome, peace as inner sealing, and conscience as deeper layer all meet. Within the family, the strongest internal pairing is with the eschatological-reception characteristic — the two share the *sōzō* root and the faith-instrumentation, and several verses in this characteristic carry both registers simultaneously. "And the prayer of faith will save the one who is sick, and the Lord will raise him up. And if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven" (Jam 5:15) holds physical healing and conscience-clearing in a single clause.

Pairings concentrate at peace and at the resurrection-grounded conscience appeal. The synoptic formula appends peace to the saving act: "Daughter, your faith has made you well; go in peace, and be healed of your disease" (Mar 5:34). Luk 8:48 carries the same closure. The Petrine extension grounds conscience-clearing in the resurrection: "baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you, not as a removal of dirt from the body but as an appeal to God for a good conscience, through the resurrection of Jesus Christ" (1Pe 3:21).

The strongest opposition is fear. Jesus sets the two against each other directly: "do not fear; only believe, and she will be well" (Luk 8:50). Fear is not an accompaniment to faith here; it is the structural alternative. Where fear governs, the inner being contracts; where faith governs, the inner being acts toward Christ. The corpus is silent on adversarial spiritual beings in this characteristic — the opposition is the inner-being state of fear, not an external power.

A second opposition is gratitude-deferred reception. The nine cleansed lepers who do not return receive the cure but not the wholeness; only the Samaritan hears the full declaration: "Rise and go your way; your faith has made you well" (Luk 17:19). The characteristic can be partly engaged without being fully realised.

This characteristic is produced by interior conviction forming under conditions of need. The woman's "if I only touch his garment, I will be made well" (Mat 9:21) names the form. The conditions — twelve years of haemorrhage, exhausted resources, social marginalisation — are not generative of the faith but the context within which the inner being forms it. The corpus does not name another inner characteristic as the producer; the faith here appears as the originating inner-being act.

This characteristic produces peace and discipleship and conscience-clearing. Peace is the named immediate state (Mar 5:34; Luk 8:48). Discipleship follows the healing in Bartimaeus's "followed him on the way" (Mar 10:52). Conscience-clearing operates at the depth in 1Pe 3:21 and Jam 5:15. The downstream effects compound the inner-being restoration; the wholeness is not exhausted in the bodily cure.

The shared vocabulary is the *sōzō* root, generating the family's organising verb across the eschatological-reception, physical-rescue, healing-wholeness, and anticipation characteristics. The root's spread across multiple registers — physical, spiritual, eschatological — reveals that the same divine action operates in differentiated modes. The boundary held in this study, that the characteristics are distinct in operational form, does not undo the lexical and theological unity at the root.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the integrative point at which body, conscience, social standing, and relational orientation toward Christ are restored together — and the faith-act is the operative instrument by which the divinely-effected wholeness reaches the person.


### 6.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its position as the moral-relational resolution node: it stands downstream of sin-awareness, guilt, and wrath-exposure, and upstream of forgiveness-peace, contrition-response, and transformed moral orientation. The strongest internal pairing is with the priestly-mediation characteristic — the two share the *kāpar/kpr* root directly, and the boundary between them is held by which side of the transaction is in view (the recipient's conscience versus the mediator's act). "The priest shall make atonement for him for his sin, and he shall be forgiven" (Lev 4:26) names both sides in a single sentence: the priestly act and the recipient's inner-being outcome.

Pairings concentrate at forgiveness, wrath-aversion, and contrition. The Levitical formula recurs eleven times across Lev 4–6 pairing atonement and forgiveness. Wrath-aversion is paired explicitly in Psa 78:38: "he, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often." Contrition pairs with atonement in the tax collector's "God, be merciful to me, a sinner!" (Luk 18:13) and in the post-atonement state of Eze 16:63: "that you may remember and be confounded ... when I atone for you for all that you have done."

This characteristic is produced by divine love and compassion. John names the structure with maximum precision: "in this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1Jo 4:10). The love is prior; the propitiation is the love's expression. Psa 78:38 names compassion as the inner-divine disposition that motivates the repeated atoning act. The production runs from divine character to atoning act, not from human seeking to divine response.

The opposition is the closed inner ground that refuses or cannot receive the offered covering. "I have sworn concerning the house of Eli that the iniquity of Eli's house shall not be atoned for by sacrifice or offering forever" (1Sa 3:14). "Surely this iniquity will not be atoned for you until you die" (Isa 22:14). The structural opposite is not an alternative inner-being characteristic competing with atonement; it is the sealed state of the inner ground where the divinely-instituted covering cannot land.

This characteristic produces forgiveness-peace as its primary downstream effect — the cleared conscience standing of one whose guilt has been covered. It produces transformed moral orientation: "by steadfast love and faithfulness iniquity is atoned for, and by the fear of the Lord one turns away from evil" (Pro 16:6). The atonement received works outward into the formed character that operates in the atoning mode toward others. It also produces, in the formed person, the silenced humility Ezekiel names — the inner shape of one who carries the memory of guilt as gravity even after the standing is restored.

The shared vocabulary binds this characteristic to the priestly-mediation characteristic at the *kāpar* root, which generates the verb (to atone, cover), the noun *kōper* (ransom payment), and the noun *kappōret* (mercy seat / atonement cover). The same root architecture spans the mediation machinery and the atoned state, revealing that the two characteristics are different facets of one divine provision. The vocabulary also links this characteristic to the ransom characteristic at the *kōper* derivative — Exo 30:15–16 frames atonement as ransom-payment.

A relational fact runs outward: this characteristic's nearest extra-family neighbour is divine love (treated in the M05 cluster), which 1Jo 4:10 holds in the same clause as propitiation. The relational point here is that love and the atoning act are not sequential events but a unified divine movement — the love is expressed as the sending of the propitiation.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the moral-relational resolution node — the inner-being state of one whose guilt has been covered by an act that did not originate in the self, whose conscience has been addressed at the level of standing before God.


### 6.5 Priestly mediation — the machinery of atonement

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its mediating structural position: it is never the terminus of the inner-being movement but the mechanism through which other states are reached. The strongest internal pairing is with the atonement-received characteristic — the two are the operative and the experiential sides of one divine provision. "On this day shall atonement be made for you to cleanse you. You shall be clean before the Lord from all your sins" (Lev 16:30) names the procedure (this characteristic) and the resultant state (the atonement-received characteristic) in a single sentence.

Pairings concentrate at guilt-state on the input side and at cleanness, forgiveness, and safety from wrath on the output side. The Day of Atonement extends the priestly act in widening circles: "he shall make atonement for the holy sanctuary, and he shall make atonement for the tent of meeting and for the altar, and he shall make atonement for the priests and for all the people of the assembly" (Lev 16:33). The pairing also reaches into the priest's own inner-being equipment: "Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" (Lev 16:6). The self-covering-first principle pairs the mediator's own atoned standing with his capacity to mediate for others.

This characteristic is produced by divine command. "And this shall be a statute forever for you, that atonement may be made for the people of Israel once in the year because of all their sins" (Lev 16:34). The institution is divinely commanded; the ordination of the priesthood is divinely commanded (Lev 8:34); the perpetual statute is divinely commanded. Without the command, the characteristic does not exist. The corpus is also silent on spiritual-being mediation — no angelic agency is required for the priestly act to operate, and no adversarial spiritual being is named as resisting it.

The opposition is the failure to enact the prescribed order — uncovered mediation, partial coverage, presumptuous self-assumption of the role. The opposition is structural and vocational rather than metaphysical. Where the priest acts without the required self-covering or outside the appointed sequence, the institution does not produce the outcome it was given to produce.

This characteristic produces the atoned-state in the community as its primary output — the cleared conscience and restored standing that the atonement-received characteristic names from the recipient's side. It also produces safety from wrath: Aaron running into the assembly with incense (Num 16:46–47); Phinehas's interposing zeal (Num 25:13); Moses's intercession after the golden calf (Exo 32:30). And it produces, structurally, the maintained covenantal standing of the community across time — the perpetual statute encodes ongoing relational integrity with God.

The shared vocabulary binds this characteristic to the atonement-received characteristic at the *kpr* root — the same Hebrew root generates both the priestly act (the *kipper* verb) and the cover that results (the *kappōret*, the atonement cover). The root unity at the lexical level reflects the operational unity at the theological level: the act and the resulting state are differentiated analytically but not separable in the divine provision.

The eschatological horizon of this characteristic, distinctively, is internal incompleteness rather than explicit promise — the annual reset, the mediator's own required self-covering, the sanctuary still defiled year after year. The relational fact runs forward to a greater high priest whose institution this one anticipates by its own structural inadequacy. The forward-pointing is the relationship.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the structural mechanism of one part of the salvation economy — the divinely-instituted office through which atonement reaches the community, and the office whose own internal logic points beyond itself to the One who fulfils what it could only anticipate.


### 6.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its temporal aspect: it is the salvation-orientation in its forward-looking mode. The strongest internal pairing is with the eschatological-reception characteristic — they share the same vocabulary (*sōzō*, *sōtēria*, *yeshûʿāh*) and the same divine saving act in view, differing in temporal stance. Paul holds both in a single line: "for in this hope we were saved. Now hope that is seen is not hope. For who hopes for what he sees?" (Rom 8:24). The salvation has been received in the mode of hope; the hope holds what is not yet visible.

Pairings concentrate at hope, faith, and endurance. The Pauline armour-imagery joins the helmet of hope with the breastplate of faith and love: "let us be sober, having put on the breastplate of faith and love, and for a helmet the hope of salvation" (1Th 5:8). Petrine theology pairs the divine guarding with the believer's anticipating: "who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time" (1Pe 1:5). Heb 9:28 names eager waiting as the inner posture toward Christ's second appearing.

This characteristic is produced by divine preservation and covenantal promise. 1Pe 1:5's "guarded by God's power through faith" names the custodial mechanism: the sustained inner-being posture is not self-generated but the product of divine guarding. Luk 1:69 names the covenantal-promise ground: "he has raised up a horn of salvation for us in the house of his servant David, as he spoke by the mouth of his holy prophets from of old." The production runs from God's power and God's spoken promise to the inner being's sustained orientation toward the awaited completion.

The opposition is despair-collapse and self-rescue striving. The first is named in Acts 27:20's "all hope of our being saved was at last abandoned" and in Isaiah's lamentation: "we hope for justice, but there is none; for salvation, but it is far from us" (Isa 59:11). The second is named directly in Isa 26:18: "we were with child, we writhed in pain, but we have given birth to wind. We have accomplished no deliverance in the earth." Human striving toward salvation produces only wind; the opposition operates on the inner ground that either collapses or grasps.

This characteristic produces righteous conduct, sobriety, and endurance. Isa 56:1 names the moral motivation: "keep justice, and do righteousness, for soon my salvation will come." 1Th 5:8 names the sobriety: the inner being put on hope as armour for the mind. 2Co 1:6 names the endurance: "if we are afflicted, it is for your comfort and salvation; and if we are comforted, it is for your comfort, which you experience when you patiently endure the same sufferings that we suffer." The anticipation does not produce passivity; it shapes outer life into the form of sustained moral discipline.

The shared vocabulary binds this characteristic to the eschatological-reception characteristic at the *yāšaʿ/sōz-* root family. The same lexical form names both what has been received and what is being awaited; the temporal distinction is held by the modifiers ("ready to be revealed," "will appear a second time," "eagerly waiting for him") rather than by separate words.

A relational fact runs outward: this characteristic's nearest extra-family neighbour is hope as the eschatological inner-being orientation (treated in the M18 cluster). The cross-register is explicit; Rom 8:24's "saved in hope" cannot be read as belonging to one without the other. The two are mutually constitutive at their core.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the temporally-forward face of salvation — the inner-being posture that holds the awaited completion across the interval between promise and fulfilment, sustained by divine preservation and by the moral-volitional alignment with the coming reality.


### 6.7 Ransomed identity, gratitude and memory

The most distinctive relational profile of this characteristic is its position as the integrating identity node: it provides the identity-constitutive content that memory holds and from which joy, gratitude, and ethical action flow. The strongest internal pairing within this study is loose — the other six characteristics in this family operate primarily in present or future modes, while this one operates in retrospective mode. The strongest pairings reach outside the family to remembrance, joy, and covenantal calling.

Pairings concentrate at memory, joy, and ethical extension. Memory is paired structurally: "you shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the Lord your God redeemed you; therefore I command you this today" (Deu 15:15). Deuteronomy 24:18 repeats the structure exactly. Psalm 78:42 names the negation: "they did not remember his power or the day when he redeemed them from the foe." Joy is paired at the eschatological completion: "the ransomed of the Lord shall return and come to Zion with singing; everlasting joy shall be upon their heads; they shall obtain gladness and joy, and sorrow and sighing shall flee away" (Isa 35:10; Isa 51:11). The ransomed identity and the everlasting joy are inseparable in the verse.

This characteristic is produced by covenantal love and the act of ransom itself. Deuteronomy 7:8 names the structure: "it is because the Lord loves you and is keeping the oath that he swore to your fathers, that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand and redeemed you from the house of slavery." The covenantal love is the motive ground; the ransoming act is the love's expression. The production runs from divine love through the act in time to the identity formed by the act.

The opposition is forgetting and deceptive self-direction. The forgetting is named directly: Psa 78:42 records the catastrophic effect of failing to remember. When the memory lapses, the identity dissolves. The deceptive self-direction is named at Hos 7:13: "I would redeem them, but they speak lies against me." The offered ransom is blocked by the inner-being state turned away from the truth of what is being offered.

This characteristic produces ethical generosity toward the vulnerable, intercessory appeal, freed will-direction, and sustained joy. Deuteronomic ethics flow from remembered ransom (Deu 15:15; Deu 24:18). Moses's intercession draws its standing from past ransom: "do not destroy your people and your heritage, whom you have redeemed through your greatness" (Deu 9:26). Psalm 119:134 names the volitional liberation: "redeem me from man's oppression, that I may keep your precepts" — ransom enables the obedience that bondage prevented. And Isa 35:10's everlasting joy is the affective culmination — sorrow and sighing flee where the ransom is fully received.

The shared vocabulary in this characteristic concentrates in the *pādāh* root (to ransom by payment), which generates the dominant verb and several derivatives. The same root reaches into the physical-rescue characteristic at Psa 69:18: "draw near to my soul, redeem me." The shared root reveals that ransoming reaches across both registers — historical ransom from bondage and immediate rescue from danger. The *zākar* (remember) root, while not exclusive to this characteristic in the wider corpus, operates here with structural centrality.

Relational facts run outward to characteristics not in this study. The remembrance characteristic (treated in the M41 cluster) is the formal home of the *zākar* function; this characteristic supplies the content that remembrance holds in its most identity-forming instances. The joy characteristic (treated in the M04 cluster) is the formal home of the sustained-rejoicing that Isa 35:10 names; the ransomed identity is the ground from which that joy specifically rises. The covenantal-calling characteristic (treated in the M37 cluster) is the elective precondition; the ransoming act is downstream of the prior covenantal love and oath. These are real relational facts; their fuller mapping belongs to those clusters' studies.

In its position within the family, this characteristic is the retrospective-identity node — the inner-being state of one whose self-understanding is constituted by a past divine act held in active memory, motivating present ethical action, grounding intercessory appeal, and oriented toward the eschatological ransom of which the historical Exodus was only the foundational type.
