## 3. The divine pattern

Salvation, in this study, comes from God. That is the doxology of the redeemed multitude in heaven — "Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" (Rev 7:10) — and it is the consistent witness of the prophets, the Psalmist, and the apostles whose words make up the evidence the chapters that follow examine in detail. Paul to Titus: God "saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" (Tit 3:5). The Psalmist on God's repeated atoning act: "He, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often" (Psa 78:38). Moses on the Exodus: "It is because the Lord loves you and is keeping the oath that he swore to your fathers, that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand and redeemed you from the house of slavery" (Deu 7:8). John on the origin of propitiation: "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1Jo 4:10). Four characteristics, four verses, one consistent grounding. What moves on the divine side in every saving act is mercy, compassion, covenantal love — the character of God expressed in different operations of the one saving will.

God is not only the agent of salvation; he provides what salvation requires. Leviticus names the divine giving of the atoning means in direct speech: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life" (Lev 17:11). The altar's covering of guilt is not a technique humans devised and offered to God; it is what God gave for the cover of souls. Romans 3:25 brings the same logic to its New-Testament focal point: Christ is the one "whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith." From the altar of Leviticus to the cross, the means by which God's mercy reaches the human condition is what God himself supplies. The creature does not bring the mechanism. The creature receives what God has given.

One register in this study goes further than provision. The prophets present a God whose involvement in salvation is not just to give and to act, but to wear the saving work and refuse to rest until it is complete. Isaiah sees the divine warrior arming for battle: "He put on righteousness as a breastplate, and a helmet of salvation on his head" (Isa 59:17). The same prophet hears the divine refusal of rest: "For Zion's sake I will not keep silent, and for Jerusalem's sake I will not be quiet, until her righteousness goes forth as brightness, and her salvation as a burning torch" (Isa 62:1). Habakkuk sees the militant motion: God rides "on your horses, on your chariot of salvation" (Hab 3:8). The God whose provision the altar manifests in cold blood is also the God whose commitment is hot enough to wear the saving work, to ride toward it, to refuse silence until it is finished. Salvation is not external to who God is; the completion of it has the shape of God's own restlessness.

Not every characteristic in this study names God in the same way. Some are characteristics God himself possesses — God saves; God atones; God wears salvation as armour. Some are characteristics where God responds, but the creaturely act belongs to the creature alone — God hears the storm-cry, but does not himself cry; Christ heals the woman through her faith, but does not himself exercise faith. Some are characteristics that, by the nature of who God is, no divine person could possess — God designs the priestly institution but does not undergo it; God ransoms his people but is not himself the ransomed. The varied attribution is not an inconsistency. It is the shape of what salvation looks like when it operates across the distance between the Creator and the creature he is saving. Where the creature and God stand on the same orientation, the verses say so directly; where the orientation belongs to one side only, the verses do not pretend otherwise.

Where Scripture uses these characteristics typologically — making one instance a pointer to another reality — the typology runs predominantly downward, from divine instance to human form. The Exodus rescue is the type whose later instances of ransomed identity participate in. Noah's rescue is the type whose antitype is the inner-being appeal of conscience that baptism enacts (1Pe 3:21). The divine warrior's helmet of salvation is the pattern; the believer's helmet of hope is the imitation (Isa 59:17 → 1Th 5:8). The priestly characteristic is the place where a counter-current operates. God designs the human priestly institution. The institution itself — by its annual reset, by the mediator's own required self-covering, by the sanctuary's perpetual defilement — points upward to a greater high priest. The institution God authored cannot, within itself, do what it was authored to do. Where this happens, the human instance is the type and what it points toward is divine.

One further pattern runs through all seven characteristics, and it makes the study readable as a single theological story. Each characteristic points beyond its present operation toward a completion not yet reached. Salvation has been received, but those received in justification "shall ... be saved by him from the wrath of God" (Rom 5:9) — the saving act is in time but reaches beyond it. Atonement in its Levitical form points toward the once-for-all act it could not itself accomplish. The priestly institution points beyond its annual rhythm to a greater high priest. Anticipated salvation is forward by definition. Ransomed identity, looking back to the Exodus, looks forward to a ransom from Death itself: "I shall ransom them from the power of Sheol; I shall redeem them from Death" (Hos 13:14). The God who has begun the saving work is also the God who will complete it. Salvation in this study is a doctrine in motion — what God has done, and what God will do.


### 3.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

Of the seven characteristics in this study, this is the one Scripture most directly attributes to God. The verses do not need to be inferred: God saves; God desires that all be saved (1Ti 2:4); God exalts Jesus as Saviour (Act 5:31); salvation belongs to God (Rev 7:10). And the operative character is named at every turn — not what the creature has done, but what God is. Paul to Titus: God "saved us, not because of works done by us in righteousness, but according to his own mercy" (Tit 3:5). Paul to the Ephesians: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8). What the creature contributes is the faith that receives the gift, not the act that earns it. Where the human side extends this salvation to others by witness or persistence, the extending is real but derived: the salvation extended is not the creature's own, and the act of extending it is participation in something God is already doing.


### 3.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

Here Scripture attributes salvation to God on one side of the encounter but not the other. God is the rescuer who hears, draws near, extends his hand into the storm. But Scripture is silent on God's experience of mortal terror; the God who hears the cry from drowning does not himself cry from drowning. The silence is meaningful. It locates the inner-being collapse — the panic, the bare imperative cry — on the creature's side, and it locates sovereign responsiveness on God's. Hebrews names the one moment where this characteristic reaches closest to divine inner life: Jesus, "in the days of his flesh ... offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverence" (Heb 5:7). The cry is in his human nature; the hearing is in the Father. The image-bearer was made to address the rescuer. The rescuer does not himself need rescue.


### 3.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

Christ heals; the human person's faith is the means through which the healing arrives. The attribution is precise: "Your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22) — the salvation is from Christ, but the faith is named as operative within the act. Scripture is silent on God exercising faith as an inner faculty, and the silence is meaningful: faith, in its operating form, is the act of one who receives. God is the source; the creature is made to reach toward the source in trust. What this characteristic reveals about God is integrative responsiveness. He does not partition body from spirit in his rescue, and the response is activated when the creature directs trust toward him.


### 3.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

Scripture names God as the one who atones. The Psalmist: "When iniquities prevail against me, you atone for our transgressions" (Psa 65:3). The same Psalmist on the character moving the act: "He, being compassionate, atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them; he restrained his anger often" (Psa 78:38). Leviticus on the provision of the mechanism: "I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life" (Lev 17:11). John on the prior love that sends the propitiation: "In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1Jo 4:10). What the creature receives is the cleared conscience, the restored standing, the covered guilt. The character of God revealed is not merely holiness that condemns but holiness that provides the means of its own satisfaction. Atoning is not what God does in response to a mechanism humans devised. Atoning is what God does.


### 3.5 Priestly mediation — the machinery of atonement

The divine treatment runs differently here. God designs the priestly institution and sustains it by commandment: "And this shall be a statute forever for you, that atonement may be made for the people of Israel once in the year because of all their sins" (Lev 16:34). The institution is divinely commanded. But God does not himself stand in the priestly role. He is not a priest within the Levitical system; he is its author. And the institution he authored is, by its own internal logic, incomplete. The high priest must first make atonement for himself before he can make atonement for others: "Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" (Lev 16:6). The mediator is himself someone whose standing requires addressing. The annual rhythm encodes the incompleteness: same act, year after year, because the mediator's condition never settles. Of the seven characteristics in this study, this is the one whose internal structure most clearly points beyond itself. The priest the Levitical institution gives is not the priest the Levitical institution requires. The reaching toward a greater high priest is in the institution from the beginning.


### 3.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

This is the characteristic where God is most closely involved in the inner-being posture itself. God does not only give salvation; he wears it — "a helmet of salvation on his head" (Isa 59:17). He drives toward it: "on your horses, on your chariot of salvation" (Hab 3:8). He refuses rest until it is complete: "For Zion's sake I will not keep silent, and for Jerusalem's sake I will not be quiet, until her righteousness goes forth as brightness, and her salvation as a burning torch" (Isa 62:1). The orientation toward salvation as a coming completion is shared between God and the creature, though in modes that are not identical. God drives the completion; the creature waits for it. The creature's specific form of waiting — fruitless human effort that "we have given birth to wind" (Isa 26:18) — belongs to the creature alone. But the direction of attention is shared, and that sharing is what is distinctive about this characteristic. The believer who anticipates salvation is oriented toward what God himself is committed to bringing.


### 3.7 Ransomed identity, gratitude and memory

The ransom is divine; the ransomed identity is creaturely. God acts; the human person remembers and is constituted by what God has done. The verses make a further claim distinctive of this characteristic: God ransoms in order to disclose who he is. David's prayer holds the structure: "the one nation on earth whom God went to redeem to be his people, making himself a name, and doing for them great and awesome things" (2Sa 7:23). The ransoming act is not only for the creature's sake; it is the act through which God makes himself known. The covenantal love that grounds the ransom — "It is because the Lord loves you ... that the Lord has brought you out with a mighty hand and redeemed you from the house of slavery" (Deu 7:8) — and the ransoming act itself are together what God shows of himself in the saving work. The creaturely correlate is identity grounded in remembered deliverance, gratitude expressed in ethical generosity toward the vulnerable (Deu 15:15; Deu 24:18), and intercessory appeal grounded in what God has already done (Deu 9:26). God by nature is not ransomed; the gratitude that flows from having been ransomed therefore belongs to the creature alone.
