## 2. The characteristics in this study

The Bible's vocabulary for being rescued, restored, or saved is wider than the English word "salvation" suggests. Across the Old Testament and the New, this vocabulary organises into seven characteristics — seven distinct inner-life realities, each describing what salvation looks like from a particular angle of the human person. The sections that follow introduce each in turn, in the order the study will treat them through the chapters that come.


### 2.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

Salvation in its fullest and most encompassing sense — rescue from sin, condemnation, and the judgment to come — is what most readers hear when they meet the English word "salvation" in Scripture. This is the characteristic the angel announces over the unborn Jesus: "She will bear a son, and you shall call his name Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins" (Mat 1:21). The inner-life content of this salvation is reception. Faith turns toward Christ; the person receives righteousness as gift; the resulting state of being-saved holds across present and future.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the others in this study is the breadth of what it covers and the eschatological horizon it reaches. The next characteristic also speaks of being saved, but the rescue is from mortal physical danger in a particular moment; here the rescue is from a condition of standing before God that reaches all the way to the day of judgment. Where the healing characteristic that follows holds physical and spiritual restoration together, this characteristic can stand purely in the moral and relational register without addressing bodily condition at all.

Paul's compressed statement to the Ephesians names the reception structure with theological precision: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8). The verse holds the three things this characteristic requires: a divine act (grace), an inner act of receiving (faith), and the underlying gift-status of the whole (not your own doing). Every other expression of salvation in this study rests on something that looks like this triad — but here the triad stands in its most explicit and most encompassing form.

### 2.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

Salvation in Scripture is not only the great eschatological reception. It is also, repeatedly, the urgent rescue of a body in danger of dying — the physical deliverance of someone whose creaturely resources have reached their limit. Peter sinking in the wind is the type-case: "But when he saw the wind, he was afraid, and beginning to sink he cried out, 'Lord, save me'" (Mat 14:30). The inner-life content of this characteristic is compressed: terror narrows the volitional and cognitive capacities to a single plea; the cry to the qualified rescuer is the whole of what the person can do.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the eschatological reception of the previous section is that the rescue is bodily and immediate. There is no question of standing before God on this side of the verses, no receiving of righteousness as gift — only the inner-being collapse of someone who knows that the present situation exceeds their resources and that someone within reach can act.

The same Greek verb that names eschatological salvation can be turned against the saving act when human power-logic distorts the inner-being expectation. The mockery at the cross is the inversion: "He saved others; he cannot save himself" (Mar 15:31). The mockers expect physical self-rescue as the proof of saving power; the act they ridicule — the refusal to self-rescue — is precisely what carries the saving work of the wider redemptive arc. The vocabulary of this characteristic crosses into the heart of the gospel by means of an irony the mockers cannot see.

### 2.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

A third characteristic of salvation in Scripture is the holistic restoration in which body, social standing, and moral-relational core are healed together through the exercise of trust toward Christ. The bleeding woman whose long affliction ends with a touch hears the formula that names this characteristic: "Take heart, daughter; your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22). The inner-life content here is the interior conviction that forms before any action — a settled trust strong enough to drive the person past social and physical obstacles to the source of the healing.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the physical rescue of the previous section is the role of inner conviction. The cry of someone drowning is bare; faith here is formed. The woman has reasoned her way to a position — "if I only touch his garment, I will be made well" — that the cry from sinking cannot reach. Distinct also from the eschatological reception of the opening section, where the rescue need not address bodily condition at all, this characteristic insists that the wholeness extends to the body itself. The healing is real bodily restoration; it is also social re-integration and moral-relational clearing.

Jesus's command to the synagogue ruler holds the structure of this characteristic in its starkest form: "Do not fear; only believe, and she will be well" (Luk 8:50). The directive arrives after news that the daughter has died. The perception reports finality; the directive contradicts the perception. The inner act required by this characteristic is the orientation of trust toward the rescuer's word against what the senses confirm. Where this orientation holds, the healing follows; where fear silences it, the healing stops.

### 2.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

The fourth characteristic of salvation names the state of the person whose guilt has been covered by a qualified mediating act, with the inner-life result of a cleared conscience and a restored standing before God. The Levitical formula names this characteristic in its institutional form: "And the priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" (Lev 4:20). The mechanism is the priest's act; the inner content is the offerer's release from the standing of guilt that the offering has covered.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the eschatological salvation of the opening section is the specific moral-relational territory it addresses. Eschatological salvation reaches the whole of the person before God; this characteristic addresses the particular weight of guilt and the question of how the inner person stands clean before holiness. Distinct from the priestly mediation characteristic that follows immediately after, this one is named from the receiving end — the person on whose behalf the priest acts and whose conscience the act addresses. The priestly machinery is the other side of the same transaction; this side is the conscience that the machinery addresses.

Leviticus 17:11 states the divine institution of the mechanism: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life." The verse names the divine origin of the means ("I have given it"), the locational target ("your souls"), and the operative reason. The act is not a human technique applied to God; it is a divine provision applied to the human condition. The inner content of having been atoned-for, as Ezekiel later names it, is not euphoria but silenced humility — the gravity of being-genuinely-covered shapes the response.

### 2.5 Priestly mediation machinery of atonement

This characteristic stands apart from the other six in the study, and the difference is the point. The first four characteristics name what the recipient of salvation experiences; the sixth and seventh name what the recipient anticipates or remembers; this characteristic alone names the inner-life equipment of the qualified mediator whose vocation is to enact one part of the salvation mechanism on behalf of others. The recipient and the enactor stand on different sides of the same transaction, and Scripture treats both with care.

The order of the priestly act establishes the inner shape of the vocation. The high priest must first be covered before he can cover others: "Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" (Lev 16:6). The mediator who would interpose for the community must himself be one whose standing has been addressed. This requirement is consistent across the corpus and structurally encoded in the annual rhythm of the Day of Atonement — the mediator's own ongoing condition is what makes the recurring act necessary.

The reach of the act is comprehensive. The high priest's day extends in widening circles outward from the inmost place: "He shall make atonement for the holy sanctuary, and he shall make atonement for the tent of meeting and for the altar, and he shall make atonement for the priests and for all the people of the assembly" (Lev 16:33). The list is enumerative because the institution names every layer — sacred space, sacred objects, the mediator's own household, the people — as objects requiring atonement. The vocational characteristic exists because direct human-divine standing requires a relationally-qualified intermediary, and that intermediary's inner-life equipment is the burden-bearing solidarity the institution forms.

### 2.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

A sixth characteristic of salvation names the inner-life posture of one whose deliverance is real but not yet possessed — the soul waiting forward, holding the tension between what has been promised and what has not yet arrived. The bare grammar of this characteristic appears in Jacob's interjected line in the middle of his blessing of the sons: "I wait for your salvation, O Lord" (Gen 49:18). Three Hebrew words name the whole characteristic: an inner act ("I wait"), an object ("your salvation"), and an addressee ("O Lord"). No further elaboration is needed; the structure of waiting expectantly toward a divinely-promised arrival is the whole.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the eschatological reception of the opening section is the temporal location of the inner being. There the salvation is already received and being received; here it is awaited. The two are not in conflict — Romans names the believer as "saved in hope", holding both at once — but the phenomenology of waiting is distinct from the phenomenology of having received. Distinct also from the physical rescue characteristic, where the cry comes from acute crisis, the waiting here is sustained and patient, often grounded in covenantal promise that reaches back into the history Scripture records.

Isaiah's contrast names the durability of what is awaited: "Lift up your eyes to the heavens, and look at the earth beneath; for the heavens vanish like smoke, the earth will wear out like a garment, and they who dwell in it will die in like manner; but my salvation will be forever, and my righteousness will never be dismayed" (Isa 51:6). The cosmos has an expiration; the awaited salvation does not. The inner-life posture of this characteristic is anchored in the certainty of what is promised, not in the visibility of what is present — and that anchoring is what makes the sustained waiting possible.

### 2.7 Ransomed identity gratitude and memory

The seventh characteristic of salvation names the inner-life state of the person whose self-understanding is permanently structured by the memory of having been bought back from bondage. Where the anticipation characteristic looks forward toward what God will bring, this one looks backward into a constituting past event. The Hebrew vocabulary is centred on the verb pa.dah (to ransom, to redeem by payment), and the identity that flows from being ransomed runs through the Old Testament as the inner formation that the Exodus left in Israel.

The Deuteronomic command names the structure precisely: "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the Lord your God redeemed you; therefore I command you this today" (Deu 15:15). The verse runs a syllogism — remembered ransom produces present ethical obligation. The inner act required of the ransomed person is the active retrieval of the past; the ransomed identity cannot survive without the memory that constitutes it. The institutional rhythms of remembrance — Passover, the firstborn redemption, the recurring feasts — are not optional embellishments but the infrastructure on which the ransomed identity rests.

What distinguishes this characteristic from the anticipation characteristic of the previous section is the temporal direction of the inner being. Anticipation looks forward toward what is promised; this characteristic looks backward toward what has been done. Moses's intercession after the golden calf shows the inner-life weight of this remembered ground: "And I prayed to the Lord, 'O Lord God, do not destroy your people and your heritage, whom you have redeemed through your greatness, whom you have brought out of Egypt with a mighty hand'" (Deu 9:26). The plea draws its standing from the past ransom; the future depends on what has already been done. Across the whole Old Testament, the ransomed person stands on a memory and acts from it; the identity is the memory in lived form.
