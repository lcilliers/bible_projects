**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary terms are: (1) **H3444 ye.shu.ah** (Hebrew noun, feminine) — derived from the root **yš'** (H3467), which carries the core sense of being brought into a wide, open space as opposed to being in a narrow, constrained place. The spatial metaphor of rescue is primary: yasha' means to make wide, to give room, to remove the constriction that endangers. Ye.shu.ah is the nominalization: salvation as the state or event of having been brought into safety/spaciousness. For Char-6, the root meaning is particularly illuminating: the soul in VCG-01 (Gen 49:18, Isa 59:11) is still in the narrow place, waiting for the widening. (2) **G4991 soteria** (Greek noun, feminine) — from **sōzō** (G4982, to save, preserve, make whole), itself from the adjective **sōs** (safe, sound, whole). The root sense is preservation of wholeness or soundness — rescue from the threat of destruction or dissolution. (3) **G4990 sōtēr** (Greek noun, masculine) — the agent noun: the Savior, the one who performs the sōzō act. (4) **G4992 sōtērion** (Greek neuter adjective used substantively) — the saving thing, the instrument or content of salvation. The Hebrew root's spatial metaphor (constriction → spaciousness) and the Greek root's integrity metaphor (dissolution → wholeness) together reveal that salvation-anticipation in Char-6 is the inner-being posture of one still in the narrow/broken place, consciously oriented toward the widening/wholeness that is promised but not yet received.

**T7.1.2 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — **H3444 ye.shu.ah** is exclusively a noun in the Hebrew evidence base — it does not function as a verb in the corpus verses. This is significant: salvation-as-noun places the emphasis on salvation as a **thing hoped for**, a **reality awaited**, an **object of expectation** — it is the substantive content toward which the inner being is oriented. The verbal action (yasha') is God's; the soul's relationship to salvation is nominal and receptive. This grammatical distribution confirms that in Char-6, the inner being is not the agent of salvation but the oriented recipient waiting for the agent to act. **G4991 soteria** is similarly predominantly nominal in the NT evidence (Heb 9:28, 1Pe 1:5, 1Pe 1:10, 2Cor 1:6, Phili 1:19, 2Pe 3:15). **G4990 sōtēr** is a substantive noun (agent). **G4982 sōzō** appears in the corpus as a verb: Rom 8:24 ('sōthēmen,' aorist passive indicative, 'we were saved') — the aorist passive reveals that salvation is **received by the soul, not performed by it**, and the indicative mood asserts the fact while the immediately following not-yet framing holds the temporal tension. The grammatical range thus reveals: Char-6 operates primarily through **nominal orientation** (the soul holds salvation as the noun-object of its hope) sustained by **passive verbal reception** (when salvation is verbalized, the soul is always grammatically passive — it is saved, not self-saving).

The vocabulary does not include a single dedicated term for the structural opposite (no antonym root is used); instead, the opposite appears through **negation** (lo' ye.shu.ah), **distance** ('salvation is far off'), and **failed effort** (Isa 26:18). The shadow-VCG (VCG-07) is the corpus's closest evidence of the structural opposite being named.

**T7.1.7 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

(3) **Longing/mourning as seeking**: Isa 59:11 ('we look for salvation, but it is far from us') — the looking/hoping (qiwwah) is the seeking act. The vocabulary thus includes a range of seeking-terms: petitionary address to God (Isa 33:2), intensive intellectual search (1Pe 1:10), and mourning-laden hope-directed longing (Isa 59:11) — showing that the seeking aspect of Char-6 operates at prayer, intellectual, and affective registers simultaneously.

**T7.1.8 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The relationship reveals strong **continuity at root and posture level with significant development at object-specification level**: (1) **Continuity**: The root meaning of yš' (spatial rescue from constriction) carries directly into sōzō (preservation of wholeness). The inner-being posture of waiting, hoping, and orienting toward an unfulfilled divine promise appears identically in Gen 49:18 (OT) and Heb 9:28 (NT) — the vocabulary differs but the posture is structurally identical: the soul names its waiting orientation toward divine deliverance not yet received. (2) **Development — object specification**: In the OT corpus (ye.shu.ah), the object of hope is broadly 'God's salvation' without a named personal mediator in most instances. In the NT corpus (soteria / sōtēr), the object becomes increasingly **person-specific**: Phili 3:20 (the Savior from heaven), Tit 2:13 (the great God and Savior Jesus Christ), Heb 9:28 (Christ appearing a second time). The NT narrows the eschatological horizon from diffuse divine saving to the **appearing of a specific person**. (3) **Development — assurance level**: NT Char-6 verses tend toward higher assurance (1Pe 1:5: 'guarded by God's power'; Heb 1:14: 'heirs of salvation') than the OT pattern of mourning-laden absence (Isa 59:11, Isa 26:18), suggesting the Incarnation and resurrection have reconfigured the affective register of the same posture.

**T7.1.9 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

The coinage reveals: (1) **Intensity of expectation**: The compound prefix apo- (away from) + ek- (out from) + dechomai (to receive) creates a compound that means something like 'to receive fully away from the present state' — the word encodes both the not-yet of the present and the certainty of the forthcoming reception. (2) **Eschatological specificity**: The term is not used in the LXX in the same technical sense; it appears to be a NT coinages for the distinctively Christian posture of eagerly waiting for the parousia. (3) **Corporate dimension**: In Phili 3:20 it is 'we await' — the posture is communally enacted. The coinage reveals that the NT found existing Greek vocabulary (elpizō, prosdechomai) insufficient for the specific quality of parousia-directed anticipation and generated a new compound to name the characteristic's distinctive intensity and object-orientation. This is the one term for which Char-6 has a near-proprietary NT vocabulary.

**T7.2.1 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The primary anchor verse for the sub-group is **Gen 49:18** (VCG-01 anchor, vc_id=55480): 'I wait for your salvation, O LORD' (לִישׁוּעָתְךָ קִוִּיתִי יְהוָה). The function of **ye.shu.ah** here: (1) **Grammatically**: ye.shu.ah is the **object of the verb qiwwah** ('to wait, to hope'). It is the noun toward which the waiting-action is directed — the content of hope. The pronominal suffix ('-kha,' 'your') binds the salvation to the LORD as its source, making ye.shu.ah simultaneously the object of the soul's hope and the possession of God. (2) **Rhetorically within the Blessing of Jacob (Gen 49)**: The verse is a brief, standalone prayer inserted in the middle of the tribal blessings. It functions as an **interjection** — Jacob's voice breaking through the prophetic pronouncements to cry out a personal confession of waiting hope. The ye.shu.ah term carries the entire weight of the prayer: it names what Jacob waits for, who holds it (the LORD), and what the inner-being stance is (qiwwah). (3) **Argumentatively**: Within Char-6, Gen 49:18 functions as the **prototypical OT form** of the characteristic: a single naming of the inner posture ('I wait') + the object ('your salvation') + the source ('O LORD') — establishing the three-part structure that recurs across the corpus.

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

(5) **Rom 8:24 structure** — Premise 1: we were saved (aorist: completed); Premise 2: hope for what is already seen is not hope; Conclusion: therefore the inner posture of sustained forward-hope is the appropriate form of present salvation-possession. The simplicity of the form at the canon's opening gives all subsequent, more complex expressions their root-meaning.

**T7.3.1 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — The most useful human science framework for Char-6 is **cognitive-developmental psychology of hope**, particularly as theorized in **C. R. Snyder's Hope Theory** (motivation-oriented) and **developmental psychology's treatment of deferred gratification and future-oriented cognition**. Snyder's Hope Theory posits two cognitive components: (1) **Agency thinking** — belief that one can initiate and sustain movement toward a goal; (2) **Pathways thinking** — belief that viable routes to the goal exist. Char-6's evidence maps onto this framework with a significant modification: the agency is **transferred to God** rather than residing in the self (1Pe 1:5: 'guarded by God's power'; Isa 62:1: God will not rest until...). The pathways thinking is constituted by **covenant and promise** (the revealed route to salvation is through God's declared purpose). A second useful framework is **attachment theory's concept of secure-base functioning under delayed reunion**: Bowlby's observation that securely attached individuals can sustain hopeful waiting during separation because they have internalized the attachment figure's reliability maps onto the Char-6 posture where the soul sustains hope under the 'absence' of realized salvation because of the internalized trustworthiness of the divine Savior (Isa 51:6–8). A third framework is **terror management theory** — the verses that contrast salvation's permanence with cosmic dissolution (Isa 51:6) and death's abolition (2Ti 1:10) address the foundational anxiety of mortality.

**T7.3.2 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Three illuminating applications: (1) **Snyder's Hope Theory + 1Pe 1:5 and Isa 62:1**: The framework reveals why the divine preservation and divine warrior declarations (VCG-04) are not merely theological assertions but **functional psychological infrastructure** for the soul's hope-posture. The soul's ability to sustain anticipation (Char-6) depends on its cognition of viable pathways (God's covenant promises as routes) and agency (God's power as the agent). Without these cognitive anchors, the hope would collapse into the despair of VCG-07 (Isa 26:18). The framework makes coherent why the corpus contains both divine-warrior declarations and the community's sustained hope in the same sub-group. (2) **Deferred gratification + 1Th 5:8 and Isa 56:1**: Research on future-oriented cognition confirms that the ability to defer gratification (continue waiting) is sustained by concrete representation of the future goal. Isa 56:1's mechanism (imminent salvation motivating present righteousness) and 1Th 5:8's armor-metaphor (hope as helmet protecting the mind) both function as **concrete cognitive representation mechanisms** that sustain the deferral of reception. The framework illuminates why the corpus employs such vivid imagery. (3) **Attachment theory + Isa 51:6–8 and Rom 8:24**: Secure-base functioning under delay maps precisely onto Rom 8:24's 'saved in hope' — the soul possesses an internalized security (the completed work of salvation) that permits it to sustain forward-hoping without the anxiety of potential abandonment. The framework reveals that Char-6 is a **secure-attachment expression**, not an anxious-attachment one, despite the absence of full reception.

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two significant divergences: (1) **Agency divergence**: Snyder's Hope Theory places agency (the capacity to initiate movement toward the goal) in the hoping subject. Char-6 consistently displaces agency onto God: 1Pe 1:5 ('guarded by God's power'), Isa 62:1 (God will not rest until...), Hab 3:8 (God rides the chariot). The soul's role is **receptive orientation**, not agentic pursuit. This divergence reveals that Char-6 is not well-described by any hope-theory that requires self-agency: the characteristic's distinctive structure is **hope sustained by transferred agency** — the soul hopes precisely because it is not the one doing the saving work. Human science frameworks built on self-determination theory would mischaracterize this as passive or helpless; the biblical evidence shows it as **a different form of active orientation** in which trust-in-the-agent replaces self-agency. (2) **Meaning-of-delay divergence**: From a motivational psychology standpoint, prolonged delay without feedback typically produces hope-extinction. Char-6 contains verses that reinterpret delay as salvifically meaningful (2Pe 3:15: 'the Lord's patience is salvation') — a move that human science would classify as a coping mechanism or motivated cognition. The verse evidence claims the reinterpretation is **ontologically true** (the delay is salvation, not merely experienced as such). The divergence reveals that Char-6 operates at an **ontological level** that human science frameworks cannot access from within their own methods: the claim is not that hope-sustaining reinterpretation is psychologically adaptive, but that God's purposes actually make the delay salvifically real.

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Two aspects surfaced by human science that deserve further verse investigation: (1) **The phenomenology of hope's erosion**: Human science on deferred hope documents the specific psychological trajectory of hope declining toward despair — including the role of social support, repetition of promises, and community belonging in sustaining hope under extended delay. The Char-6 corpus contains VCG-07 (Isa 26:18: failed effort, despair) and Isa 59:11 (mourning of absence), but these are brief. The corpus does not extensively address **the mechanisms by which the soul's anticipatory posture is maintained over extended time**. Further investigation of texts such as Ps 130 ('I wait for the LORD, my soul waits... more than watchmen wait for morning') would address this gap. (2) **Communal vs. individual dimensions of hope-maintenance**: Human science documents that hope is socially constituted — communities sustain individual hope-postures through shared practice, liturgy, and narrative. Char-6 contains both communal (Isa 33:2, Luk 1:69, Luk 3:6) and individual (Gen 49:18, Job 13:16) expressions, but does not explicitly address the **social-maintenance mechanisms** of salvation-anticipation. Investigation of Heb 10:24–25 ('stirring one another up to love and good deeds... not neglecting to meet together') and related texts would address whether communal practice is explicitly linked to hope-maintenance in the broader biblical evidence base.
