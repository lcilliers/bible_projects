**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus consistently presents God as the initiating, sovereign Savior whose nature is to rescue rather than to condemn. Joh 3:17 states God sent his Son "not to condemn but to save the world," and Joh 12:47 echoes this with identical logic: the mission is saving, not judging. Act 5:31 names God as the one who "exalted Jesus as Leader and Savior," locating salvific power in divine initiative and exaltation. Rom 5:15 (M38-A-VCG-03) declares that grace "abounds for many" against the backdrop of death's spread — the character of God is overflow, not meager sufficiency. Tit 3:5 distills the theological point most sharply: God "saved us not by our righteous deeds but by his mercy," so that mercy rather than justice-requiring-merit is the operative divine attribute. Eph 2:8 makes the gift-character of salvation explicit: salvation is "entirely God's gift, not human achievement." The heavenly doxologies of Rev 7:10 and Rev 19:1 confirm the cumulative testimony — the redeemed multitude ascribes salvation "entirely to God and the Lamb," naming salvation as belonging to the divine character as its source and home. What this characteristic reflects, therefore, is the merciful, gift-giving, initiative-taking nature of God whose primary orientation toward the fallen human person is rescue, not condemnation.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The verse evidence frames eschatological salvation received by faith primarily as a response to the fallen condition rather than a feature of original created design (see T0.2.2 for fuller treatment of that distinction), but several verses illuminate what salvation equips the person to be and become. Rom 5:17 (M38-A-VCG-03) describes the outcome as "reigning in life" through received righteousness — the saved person attains a royal inner state, a dominion-bearing dignity, through the gift. Eph 2:5 speaks of being "made alive with Christ" from a prior state of spiritual death, implying that salvation restores the person to the condition of full creaturely vitality. 1Pe 1:9 identifies "the salvation of the soul" as the "telos" of faith — salvation is the final purpose toward which faith orients the human person. Rev 7:10 and Rev 19:1 show the doxological terminus: the saved person becomes one who praises, whose inner being is re-oriented toward ascription of glory. Heb 5:9 links salvation to obedience, suggesting the saved person is equipped for moral alignment with God's will. The cumulative picture is that salvation through faith equips the person to: (a) be alive rather than dead inwardly, (b) stand in righteousness rather than condemnation, (c) reign rather than perish, and (d) worship rather than remain silent — a comprehensive re-equipping of the person's creaturely capacities toward their proper divine orientation.

**T0.3.1 — Image-Bearer Expression — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic as a whole operates on the receptive side of the divine-human relation, so the image-bearing dimension it instantiates is primarily the human person's capacity for faith — for trust-directed reception of what God gives. God is the Savior (sōtēr across M38-A-VCG-01); the human person images God not by saving others in the same mode but by being the kind of creature capable of receiving salvation through faith. However, several verses show a secondary image-bearing dimension: the missional orientation of salvation toward others. 1Cor 9:22 and 1Cor 10:33 (M38-A-VCG-05) depict the apostle whose entire inner identity is "shaped by the saving purpose" — self-adaptation, self-denial, all oriented toward that "many may be saved." Rom 11:14 and Jam 5:20 show person-to-person rescue as an image-bearing activity — one person's witness or persistence becomes the mediation of another's rescue. In this way the characteristic instantiates two aspects of the divine image: (a) the receptive capacity for trust-directed relation with God, which is the image's relational core, and (b) the outward-oriented care for others' rescue, which mirrors God's own saving character (Joh 3:16-17) in creaturely form.

**T0.4.1 — Typological Significance — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Multiple verses deploy salvation typologically with explicit christological and eschatological direction. Act 13:23 (M38-A-VCG-01) anchors Jesus as "Savior from David's line," fulfilling the covenantal-historical promise — the entire Israelite salvation-history is typologically consummated in the person of Christ. Jude 5 (M38-A-VCG-10) recalls the Exodus rescue from Egypt as a past saving act and uses it typologically as a warning: the same people were later destroyed for unbelief, so that the historical rescue points forward to the eschatological hinge of faith versus unbelief. Rom 11:26 (M38-A-VCG-08) cites Isaiah's Deliverer-from-Zion as a prophetic-typological ground for Israel's future national rescue, connecting present stumbling with ultimate eschatological restoration. Luk 1:77 (M38-A-VCG-04) frames salvation-knowledge given through forgiveness as the fulfillment of covenant expectation, with Simeon's witness in Luk 2:30 seeing salvation "made visible" in the child — both verses instantiate salvation as a covenantal type now arriving in person. Rev 7:10 and Rev 12:10 place salvation at the eschatological completion point, functioning typologically in reverse: earthly experiences of rescue are partial anticipations of the final heavenly declaration. The direction of typology runs from historical rescue (Exodus, covenant promise) through christological fulfillment toward eschatological consummation.

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Scripture explicitly and repeatedly attributes salvation to God as its primary subject. Act 5:31 states God "exalted Jesus as Leader and Savior." Joh 4:22 declares "salvation is from the Jews" — meaning it originates from the covenantal revelation God gave through Israel. 1Th 5:9 says "God's sovereign appointment" places believers in the category of salvation rather than wrath. Tit 3:5 credits God directly: "God saved us." Rev 7:10 has heaven's multitude cry "salvation belongs to our God and to the Lamb." 1Ti 2:4 declares God "desires all people to be saved" — salvation is the object of God's own will and desire. The significance for the human person is substantial: because salvation belongs constitutively to God rather than to the human person's own resources, its reception by faith is an act of profound creaturely dependence and relational alignment with God's own nature. The human person who receives salvation by faith is not merely obtaining a benefit — they are being drawn into the domain of what God fundamentally is and does. This renders the characteristic irreducibly theocentric: faith in salvation is faith in God's self-expressed character.

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence strongly indicates this characteristic is primarily a response to the fallen condition, though with roots that touch original design. The structural vocabulary of the corpus is consistently contrastive: salvation is from sin (Mat 1:21), from condemnation (Joh 3:17), from wrath (Rom 5:9), from death (Jam 5:20), from perishing (Rom 9:27 implied). These structural opposites presuppose a prior fallen condition that generates the need for rescue. Eph 2:5 makes this explicit: believers were "spiritually dead in trespasses" before being made alive — salvation addresses a deficit that is post-creational in its origin. 2Ti 1:9 introduces a nuance: God's saving grace was "purposed before time," suggesting that the divine intention behind salvation is pre-temporal and therefore not reactive in the divine mind even if redemptively responsive to the human fall. Tit 3:5 and Eph 2:8 confirm the pattern: salvation by grace through faith is precisely not self-generated but responds to a state of need. The characteristic is therefore: (a) functionally and experientially a response to the fallen condition — it addresses sin, death, guilt, condemnation; (b) but grounded in a divine purpose that pre-exists the fall, indicating that the capacity for faith-reception is part of created design even if the specific object (salvation from sin) is not.

**T0.3.2 — Image-Bearer Expression — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence indicates an asymmetric structure: God saves; the human person receives salvation by faith. The sōtēr vocabulary (M38-A-VCG-01) is applied to Christ as Savior and to God as the originating source (Rev 7:10, Act 5:31, 1Ti 2:4) — never to the human person in the primary salvific sense. Faith (pistis) as the reception-mechanism is exclusively creaturely: it presupposes a giver and a receiver, and God is always on the giver's side. However, the evidence does show a derived or participatory analogue: 1Cor 9:22, 1Cor 10:33, Rom 11:14, and Jam 5:20 all depict human persons acting instrumentally to bring about others' salvation — they "save some," they "turn a sinner from wandering." This instrumental participation in salvation's extension is a creaturely analogue to God's saving character, not an identity with it. The character of God as Savior is the original; the human person who, having received salvation, becomes an instrument of others' rescue is the image-bearing analogue. The characteristic is therefore not shared symmetrically but instantiated asymmetrically: God as primary agent, the human person as receptive beneficiary and derived participant.

**T0.4.2 — Typological Significance — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary typological direction is divine-to-human: divine saving acts establish the pattern that the human experience of salvation participates in and is shaped by. Act 13:23 shows God's covenant promise (divine) fulfilled in Christ (divine-historical) received by faith (human) — the divine act precedes and establishes all. Jude 5 uses the Exodus (a divine saving act) as the warning-type for the present community's experience: God's past rescue creates the pattern against which current unbelief is measured. The divine instance (God as Savior, Christ as Savior) always leads; human reception follows. The secondary typological movement is human-toward-eschatological: present experiences of rescue (forgiveness, justification, being-saved-now) function as anticipatory types of the eschatological completion (Rev 7:10, Rev 12:10, Rev 19:1). Here the direction reverses slightly — present human salvation points forward to divine eschatological declaration. But even this forward-pointing is grounded in what God has already done: the eschatological completion is God's own act, not a human achievement. The dominant direction is therefore: divine initiative establishes → human faith receives → eschatological consummation confirms. The typological architecture is theocentric throughout.

**T0.1.3 — Divine Nature Reflected — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus is notably not silent about God's possession of the saving character — God is actively named as Savior and saving agent throughout (see T0.1.2). However, there is a meaningful silence regarding one element: God does not receive salvation by faith. The faith-dimension of this characteristic — faith as the human reception point — is entirely absent from divine attribution. This silence is theologically significant: it locates the faith-act as irreducibly creaturely, a capacity that belongs to the image-bearer precisely because the image-bearer is not the original but the reflection. God saves; God does not need to exercise faith to receive salvation. This asymmetry suggests that faith-as-reception is not an imperfection or deficiency but the proper form of the human person's relationship to God — the creature is constituted as one who receives rather than self-generates ultimate rescue. The silence about God's faith-reception therefore illuminates what the divine image means in this characteristic: the image is borne not by sharing God's self-sufficiency but by bearing the creaturely capacity for receptive trust. The human person images God not in being a Savior in the same mode but in being the kind of being who can respond to God's saving character with faith.

**T0.2.3 — Created Purpose — Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they currently are?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the evidence is substantial and consistent. The characteristic is structurally eschatological in orientation — it points toward a future fullness that exceeds present possession. Rom 5:9 (M38-A-VCG-10) establishes the future dimension explicitly: "having been justified by Christ's blood, believers are assured of future salvation from God's wrath" — justification is present, full salvation from wrath is future. Rom 13:11 (M38-A-VCG-05) states "salvation is temporally nearer than when believers first believed," framing salvation as an approaching horizon. Mat 10:22 and Mat 24:13 (M38-A-VCG-05) tie eschatological salvation to endurance "to the end" — the end is not yet reached. 1Pe 1:9 names the salvation of the soul as the "outcome of faith," suggesting a telos that is received but not yet fully consummated. Rev 7:10, Rev 12:10, and Rev 19:1 (M38-A-VCG-10) depict salvation as the subject of heavenly doxology at the eschatological completion — this is salvation fully possessed and praised, beyond present progressive experience. Rom 11:26 projects the future rescue of "all Israel" as yet outstanding. The characteristic is therefore future-oriented at its core: present salvation is real (1Cor 1:18, Act 2:47, M38-A-VCG-04), but it exists as the down-payment and earnest of a coming fullness that constitutes the person's ultimate destiny.

**T0.3.3 — Image-Bearer Expression — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus draws a sharp and recurring contrast between those "being saved" and those "perishing" (1Cor 1:18, 2Cor 2:15, M38-A-VCG-04), between those who receive the word and those whose hearts the devil strips of it (Luk 8:12), between those who call on the Lord and those who do not (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13). These contrasts illuminate what the absence of this characteristic reveals. 2Th 2:10 (M38-A-VCG-08) identifies those perishing as those who "refused love of truth" — the will's deliberate closure is the mark of the characteristic's absence. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) names "neglect" of salvation as itself a fatal inner orientation. 2Pe 2:20 (M38-A-VCG-01) describes those who once escaped defilement through knowing the Savior but fell back as "worse off than before." The evidence suggests that the absence of this characteristic is not merely a neutral deficiency but an active condition: a closing of the will, a refusal of the gift, a perishing-state in the inner being. Conversely, its presence signals that the divine image is being renewed toward its intended relational orientation — the person is re-constituted as one who receives from God rather than refusing the gift. The characteristic's presence thus marks the image as active, responsive, and oriented toward the one whose likeness it bears; its absence marks the image as suppressed, turned inward, or willfully resistant.

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is named "Eschatological salvation received by faith." Each element of the name carries definitional weight. "Eschatological" signals that the salvation in view is not merely temporal, physical, or therapeutic but oriented toward ultimate destiny — rescue from final judgment and condemnation, participation in the coming kingdom. "Salvation" (from the Greek sōzō/sōtēr/sōtēria register) signals rescue, deliverance, and preservation from a real and structural danger — the name implies the existence of a threat from which rescue is needed. "Received" is the critical inner-being marker: salvation is not achieved, generated, or earned by the human person but accepted as a gift — the inner posture is receptive, not productive. "By faith" names the specific inner faculty through which reception occurs: trust directed toward Christ as the ground and source of rescue. Together the name signals that this characteristic is constitutionally relational (it exists between the divine giver and the human receiver), that it is directional (from God toward the person, not self-generated), and that its inner-being locus is the will and trust-faculty of the person as they turn toward and receive what God offers.

**T1.2.1 — Kind — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — At first encounter the corpus suggests this characteristic is composite: it appears simultaneously as an act, a condition, and a disposition, with different verse-groups emphasizing different aspects. The faith-reception verses (M38-A-VCG-02) present it as an inner act: heart-believing (Rom 10:9), calling on the name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13), confessing with the mouth (Rom 10:10) — these are punctiliar or decisive acts of the will turning toward Christ. The present-being-saved verses (M38-A-VCG-04) present it as an ongoing condition: believers "are being saved" (1Cor 1:18, 2Cor 2:15), the Lord "adds the saved daily" (Act 2:47) — a state being progressively inhabited. The endurance verses (M38-A-VCG-05) present it as a disposition of sustained will: "enduring to the end" (Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13) — not a single act but a persevering orientation. The gift-language verses (M38-A-VCG-03) present the saved state as a condition received and worn — righteousness bestowed, grace abounding, the inner being made alive. The most accurate preliminary classification is: this characteristic is a received condition, entered through an act of faith and sustained by a disposition of persevering trust, producing a progressive transformation of the inner being toward eschatological fullness.

**T1.3.1 — Boundary — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus identifies multiple structural opposites, all clustering around the same inner-being pole. The most explicit and recurrent is "perishing" (apollumi/apōleia): 1Cor 1:18 sets "those being saved" directly against "those perishing"; 2Cor 2:15 deploys the same contrast; 2Th 2:10 names perishing as the outcome of refusing truth; Luk 8:12 shows the devil removing the word precisely to prevent belief and avoid salvation. The second structural opposite is "condemnation" (katakrima): Joh 3:17 names it as the displaced alternative to salvation; Rom 5:16 sets the gift of justification against condemnation following trespass. The third is "wrath" (orgē): Rom 5:9 contrasts present justification with future salvation from wrath; 1Th 5:9 sets salvation against wrath as the two possible divine appointments. The fourth is "death" (thanatos): Rom 5:15-17 sets grace and life against the death that spread through Adam; Jam 5:20 sets soul-rescue against death. The fifth is "spiritual blindness/closed will": Luk 8:12 (heart where word cannot take root), 2Th 2:10 (refusing love of truth), Act 16:30 inverting to the positive (conscience awakened to danger). The primary structural opposite is therefore the state of perishing — an inner-being condition characterized by closed will, refused truth, condemnation, and movement toward death and final wrath rather than rescue and life.

**T1.4.1 — Modes of Operation — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences at least five distinct modes of operation within the inner person. (1) Reception-mode: faith as the inner act of receiving what God gives — heart-believing, calling, confessing (Rom 10:9-10, Eph 2:8, Act 16:31, M38-A-VCG-02). This is the decisive punctiliar moment of orientation toward Christ. (2) Awakening-mode: the conscience and will are aroused to their own danger and need, producing urgent seeking of rescue — the jailer's question in Act 16:30, the disciples' astonishment in Mat 19:25, the crowd's cry in Luk 18:26 (M38-A-VCG-07). (3) Transformation-mode: the inner being moves from death to life, from condemnation to justification, from guilt to righteousness — the Spirit renews, the conscience is cleansed, the self is remade (Tit 3:5, Rom 5:16-17, Eph 2:5, M38-A-VCG-03). (4) Perseverance-mode: the will sustains itself under pressure, holding fast to the gospel, enduring to the end — not a once-off act but a sustained inner orientation (Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, 1Cor 15:2, M38-A-VCG-05). (5) Doxological-mode: the saved inner being overflows in praise, gratitude, and ascription of glory to God — 2Cor 9:15 (doxology over the gift), Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1 (heavenly ascription), Luk 2:30 (Simeon's peace-filled release, M38-A-VCG-10). These modes are not strictly sequential but may overlap or recur across the life of the saved person.

**T1.5.1 — Immediate Response — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence across VCGs points to several immediate responses, the most consistent of which is faith exercised as an inner turning of will toward Christ. Act 16:30-31 shows the immediate response with narrative clarity: conscience is awakened to danger, the will urgently seeks rescue, and the answer is "believe in the Lord Jesus." Luk 7:50 captures the immediate experiential outcome: Jesus tells the woman "your faith has saved you — go in peace" — the immediate inner-being response is peace (eirēnē) following the faith-act. The Samaritan confession in Joh 4:42 shows that direct encounter produces immediate recognition and trust: "we know this is truly the Savior of the world." Act 2:21 and Rom 10:13 identify "calling on the name" as the immediate act. Luk 19:9 shows immediate household-encompassing rescue arriving with the word. In M38-A-VCG-07, the most immediate response to encountering the impossibility of salvation by human effort is astonished dependence — "who then can be saved?" — which redirects the inner being entirely onto God. The immediate responses thus cluster into: (a) faith exercised as the turning of will toward Christ, (b) peace received as the immediate experiential fruit of that turning, and (c) astonishment that drives the inner being off self-reliance and onto God.

**T1.6.1 — Sustained Effect — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus evidences multiple sustained effects developing within the inner being. (1) Growing assurance: the inner being moves from initial faith-reception toward settled confidence — Rom 5:9 shows future salvation from wrath secured by past justification; Heb 7:25 names Christ's perpetual intercession as the ground of unbroken rescue; 2Ti 4:18 depicts Paul's inner rest in "confident assurance of final eschatological preservation." (2) Persevering will: sustained possession of salvation shapes the will toward endurance — Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, 1Cor 15:2, Heb 2:3 all show that the saved inner being is one that holds fast rather than drifts. (3) Moral formation: Tit 2:11 states that grace "appeared bringing salvation" and then teaches the person to live rightly; 2Th 2:13 joins salvation to sanctification through the Spirit's transforming work; 1Ti 4:16 connects a teacher's watchful conscience to the ongoing saving of himself and others. (4) Doxological orientation: over time the saved inner being is re-oriented toward praise and gratitude — the trajectory from Eph 2:8 (gift received) to Rev 7:10 (gift ascribed in praise) traces this arc. (5) Missional orientation: 1Cor 9:22, 1Cor 10:33, and Rom 11:14 show the apostle's sustained inner identity shaped entirely by the goal of others' rescue — salvation received produces a will directed outward toward others' salvation.

**T1.7.1 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus identifies several enabling inner conditions. (1) Faith/trust directed toward Christ: this is the primary and irreducible condition — Eph 2:8, Mar 16:16, Act 16:31, 1Pe 1:9. Faith is not a work but the inner posture of receptive trust. (2) Awakened conscience: the jailer's question in Act 16:30 shows that genuine reception requires a conscience aware of danger — the inner being must recognize its need before it can receive rescue. (3) Meekness of will: Jam 1:21 identifies "meekness" as the inner orientation that allows the implanted word to save the soul — receptive humility enables the word's saving effect. (4) Repentance and inner turning: Act 2:38, Act 2:40, and 2Cor 7:10 (M38-A-VCG-09) show that genuine reception involves godly grief and will-turning away from sin — repentance is the inner movement that opens the will to receive salvation. (5) Hearing receptively: Joh 4:42 (the Samaritans' direct encounter), Act 11:14 (household saved through proclaimed message), and Act 13:26 (salvation-word sent to those who hear) all identify receptive hearing as an enabling condition. (6) Confession: Rom 10:9-10 includes mouth-confession alongside heart-belief as the paired inner-outer act that constitutes genuine reception.

**T1.8.1 — Dimension Classification — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic is the will/volitional dimension, with the faith-faculty as its specific operation. The decisive inner act throughout the corpus is the turning of the will toward Christ in trust: calling on his name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13), believing in the heart (Rom 10:9-10), exercising faith (Eph 2:8), confessing with the mouth (Rom 10:10), working out salvation "with reverent seriousness" (Phili 2:12). The self-surrender passages (M38-A-VCG-06: Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24, Luk 17:33) make the volitional character most explicit: the decisive inner movement is the will releasing its self-protective claim. The endurance passages (M38-A-VCG-05) confirm that the sustained form of the characteristic is the persevering will that does not defect. The structural opposite (refusing love of truth — 2Th 2:10; will seeking self-preservation — Mat 16:25) is equally volitional. While faith engages the mind (knowledge of Christ, truth received) and produces affective states (peace, joy, praise), the primary constitutional locus is the will — the inner faculty of trust-directed choice and sustained orientation toward God as Savior.

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The characteristic is anchored in four primary Greek terms, each revealing a distinct definitional facet. (1) sōzō (G4982): the verbal form — "to save, rescue, deliver, preserve" — signals an active divine intervention removing the person from a state of danger or destruction. Its verbal character emphasizes event and process: salvation is something being done to and for the person, not a static possession. (2) sōtēr (G4990): the noun naming the agent — "Savior" — definitionally concentrates all salvific power and identity in a single person (Christ), making salvation permanently personal and relational rather than abstract. (3) sōtēria (G4991): the abstract noun — "salvation, rescue, deliverance" — names the state or outcome achieved, suggesting that salvation is a knowable condition that can be possessed, proclaimed, received, and praised. (4) sōtērion (G4992): the adjectival/neuter noun — "saving, that which saves" — appears in contexts of public proclamation (Luk 2:30, Act 28:28, Eph 6:17) and suggests salvation as a visible, communicable reality available for encounter. Together these terms reveal that salvation is: definitionally active (sōzō), agentively personal (sōtēr), outcome-real (sōtēria), and publicly available (sōtērion). The dōrea/dōrēma register (G1431/G1434) adds the dimension of unearned gift, definitionally excluding all self-origination.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — At first encounter the characteristic appears structurally complex, comprising multiple constituent elements that operate in sequence and in tension. The constituent elements visible at the surface level are: (a) the divine initiative — God's gift of grace and saving purpose (Eph 2:8, 2Ti 1:9, Tit 3:5), which is logically and causally prior; (b) the human receptive act — faith, calling, confessing, repenting (M38-A-VCG-02, M38-A-VCG-09), which is the experiential entry point; (c) the resulting inner state — justification, peace, alive from death, conscience cleansed (Rom 5:9-10, Eph 2:5, Tit 3:5), which is the immediate condition following reception; (d) the ongoing process — being saved (1Cor 1:18, 1Cor 15:2, Act 2:47), persevering, working out salvation (Phili 2:12), which is the sustained temporal form; (e) the eschatological telos — full salvation from wrath, heavenly doxology, the soul's final rescue (Rom 5:9, Rev 7:10, 1Pe 1:9), which is the future completion. These elements are not independent but nested: divine initiative enables faith-reception, which produces inner transformation, which sustains perseverance, which reaches eschatological completion. The characteristic is therefore not simple but structurally layered, with each layer presupposing the one beneath it.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus is explicit on multiple exclusions. (1) Self-generated works as ground of salvation: Eph 2:8 states faith and salvation are "entirely God's gift, not human achievement"; Tit 3:5 excludes "righteous deeds"; 2Ti 1:9 excludes "works"; Act 15:11 refuses "law-observance" as the condition; Act 15:1 identifies the false circumcision-requirement as the contested opposite. The characteristic resists any works-based or merit-based self-construction. (2) Self-preservation as the fundamental life-orientation: the self-surrender VCG (M38-A-VCG-06) makes explicit that the instinct to "save one's life" by clinging to it is structurally excluded — it produces loss rather than salvation. (3) Purchasing or commodifying the gift: Act 8:20 explicitly excludes the attempt to buy the gift of God — salvation resists mercenary or transactional inner posture. (4) Passive indifference: Heb 2:3 names neglect of salvation as itself a fatal orientation — the characteristic resists the inner passivity that assumes rescue without receptive engagement. (5) Apostasy and abandonment: 2Pe 2:20 shows that returning to defilement after knowing the Savior is explicitly dangerous — the characteristic resists interior defection. (6) Human judging that usurps divine authority: Jam 4:12 identifies only God as Savior and Destroyer, excluding human pretension to that role.

---

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. Three contextual differentials are evident. (1) Direction — toward self versus toward others: in the self-directed mode the inner being receives salvation (M38-A-VCG-02), is transformed (M38-A-VCG-03), and perseveres (M38-A-VCG-05). In the other-directed mode the inner being is oriented toward others' rescue — apostolic self-adaptation (1Cor 9:22), self-denial for others (1Cor 10:33), intercessory prayer (Rom 10:1), person-to-person witness (Jam 5:20, Rom 11:14). The characteristic operates outwardly as a missional disposition when the self is already a recipient. (2) Temporal context — initial versus sustained: in the initial moment faith-reception is the primary operation (calling on the name, heart-believing, confessing). In sustained time the persevering will becomes dominant — holding fast (1Cor 15:2), enduring to the end (Mat 10:22), working out salvation (Phili 2:12). (3) Constitutional level — individual versus communal: at the individual level salvation is personally appropriated through faith (Act 16:31, Luk 7:50). At the communal level the Lord "adds to the community those being saved" (Act 2:47), household salvation is a unit (Act 11:14, Act 16:31), and "all Israel" is a collective eschatological object (Rom 11:26). The characteristic thus scales from individual inner act to communal and national scope without losing its inner-being character.

---

**T1.5.2 — Immediate Response — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The immediate response varies across the corpus, though several consistent patterns appear. The most consistent immediate response is faith exercised as a turning of the will — this appears across VCG-02, VCG-06, VCG-07, and VCG-09 as the decisive inner act. However, the affective texture of that turning varies significantly. In Luk 7:50 the immediate response is peace. In Act 16:30 it is urgent, alarmed seeking. In Joh 4:42 it is joyful recognition ("we know this is truly the Savior"). In Mat 19:25 and Luk 18:26 the immediate response to encountering salvation's human impossibility is astonishment. In Luk 2:30 (Simeon) the immediate response is quiet fulfillment — "now I can depart in peace." In 2Cor 7:10 the immediate response to godly grief is productive sorrow leading to repentance. In 2Cor 9:15 the immediate response to the gift is doxological overflow: "thanks be to God for his inexpressible gift." The variation is significant: the corpus does not model a uniform emotional or volitional experience of salvation-reception. What is consistent is the volitional orientation (turning toward Christ, trusting, calling) and the resolution of inner tension (from danger/guilt/death toward safety/peace/life). The affective form of the response varies by person, circumstance, and the specific aspect of salvation being encountered.

**T1.6.2 — Sustained Effect — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Sustained possession of this characteristic establishes the following states, qualities, capacities, and orientations in the inner being: (1) Settled assurance of eschatological security: Heb 7:25 grounds this in Christ's perpetual intercession; 2Ti 4:18 depicts it as "confident assurance of final eschatological preservation"; 1Th 5:9 names it as "election peace" replacing dread of condemnation. (2) Persevering will resistant to defection: Mat 10:22, 1Cor 15:2, Phili 2:12, and Heb 2:3 all presuppose that sustained salvation-possession requires and produces a will that holds rather than drifts. (3) Deepening knowledge of Christ: 2Pe 3:18 calls for growing in "grace and knowledge" of the Savior; 2Ti 3:15 shows Scripture making the mind "wise for salvation through faith." (4) Moral seriousness and holiness: Tit 2:11-12 shows salvation's grace teaching the person to live rightly; 2Ti 1:9 names the calling as one "to holiness"; 1Ti 2:15 ties sustained salvation to "faith, love, holiness, and self-control." (5) Missional orientation toward others: the apostle's sustained inner identity in 1Cor 9:22 and 1Cor 10:33 is entirely shaped by the goal of saving others — this is a capacity established over time, not merely an immediate response. (6) Doxological habituation: the arc toward Rev 7:10 and Rev 19:1 suggests that sustained salvation progressively re-orients the inner being toward praise as its natural mode.

**T1.7.2 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus identifies several blocking conditions with specificity. (1) Deliberate will-closure against truth: 2Th 2:10 names "refusing love of truth" as the inner condition that forfeits salvation; this is not ignorance but active rejection. (2) Heart preoccupied with wealth: Mar 10:26 (M38-A-VCG-07) identifies wealth as a barrier whose power generates the disciples' astonished question "who can be saved?" — riches orient the inner will toward self-sufficiency rather than receptive dependence. (3) Mercenary or transactional posture toward the gift: Act 8:20 shows Simon's heart as "mercenary" — attempting to purchase the Spirit's gift — which Peter identifies as a spiritually dangerous inner orientation. (4) Passive indifference or neglect: Heb 2:3 explicitly names neglect as a condition that blocks reception — the inner being that assumes rescue without engagement is in peril. (5) Seeking to preserve the self: Mat 16:25 and the VCG-06 parallels show that self-protective clinging — the instinct to save one's own life — is a structural blocker of eschatological salvation. (6) External word-theft before root: Luk 8:12 identifies the devil removing the word from the heart before it can produce belief — the condition blocked is faith, and the mechanism is the word failing to take root in the inner soil. (7) Apostasy after initial reception: 2Pe 2:20 and Heb 6:4 (M38-A-VCG-03) show that returning to defilement after tasting the gift places the inner being in greater peril than prior ignorance.

**T1.8.2 — Dimension Classification — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The classification of the will/volitional dimension as primary is supported by the following specific evidence. First, the acts of reception are volitional: calling on the name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13) is an act of the will directed toward God; confessing with the mouth alongside heart-believing (Rom 10:9-10) involves deliberate self-declaration; exercising faith (Eph 2:8) is a volitional orientation. Second, the self-surrender passages (M38-A-VCG-06: Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24, Luk 17:33) are the most explicitly volitional verses in the corpus — the inner act is the will releasing its self-protective grasp. Third, the endurance passages (M38-A-VCG-05) classify sustained salvation as a matter of will-persistence: "enduring to the end," "holding fast," "working out salvation with reverent seriousness" (Phili 2:12). Fourth, the blocking conditions (T1.7.2) are primarily volitional: refusing truth (2Th 2:10), neglecting salvation (Heb 2:3), seeking to save one's own life (Mat 16:25) — all are acts of misdirected will. Fifth, the awakened-conscience passages (M38-A-VCG-07) show the will's reorientation as the decisive inner event: Act 16:30 captures the moment of the will "urgently seeking rescue" as the pivot toward salvation. The mind and affections are engaged (knowledge of Christ, peace, praise) but always as expressions of and responses to the primary volitional turning.

**T1.1.3 — Name and Naming — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes on all three counts. (1) Directional: the name "received by faith" encodes a direction — salvation moves from God toward the human person, not from the human person toward God or outward from the self. The enquiry is oriented toward a downward-and-inward movement: divine grace descending, human faith receiving. This prevents any misreading of the characteristic as human achievement or self-construction. (2) Relational: "salvation received by faith" implies a relationship with a Savior — there is no impersonal mechanism here. The sōtēr-naming (M38-A-VCG-01) makes the relational structure explicit: Christ is Savior, the person is the one being saved. The name therefore orients the enquiry toward a two-party relation at its center. (3) Constitutional: "eschatological" carries constitutional implications about the depth-level of the characteristic — it is not surface behavior or social adjustment but the inner being's ultimate destiny and orientation. The name signals that what is at stake is the whole person's final condition: life or death, reigning or perishing, justification or condemnation. The enquiry is thus oriented from the outset toward the deepest constitutional layer of the person — not a behavioral trait but an inner-being state of ultimate significance.

**T1.2.3 — Kind — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Based on the corpus evidence at T0-T1 level, the working description is: Eschatological salvation received by faith is a received inner-being condition, grounded in divine grace and initiated through the will's trust-directed turning toward Christ, through which the human person is transferred from the state of condemnation, death, and perishing into the state of justification, life, and eschatological security. Constitutionally it is located in the will/volitional faculty as its primary locus, engaging also the conscience (which is awakened to danger and then cleansed), the mind (which receives truth and is made wise for salvation), and the affections (which respond in peace, gratitude, and praise). Its impact on the person is comprehensive and progressive: immediate impact includes faith-reception, peace, and conscience-cleansing; sustained impact includes persevering will, growing assurance, moral formation, and missional orientation toward others; ultimate impact is the soul's telos — full eschatological possession of the rescued self in doxological relation with God. The characteristic is not self-generated but wholly received, not once-only but progressively sustained, not individually isolated but communally realized, and not fully present but oriented toward a future fullness that constitutes its completion.

**T1.3.3 — Boundary — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus allows several boundary-defining observations. (1) This characteristic is not physical healing or temporal deliverance per se: while sōzō occasionally carries physical resonances (Mat 27:42 — "he saved others," Joh 10:9 — "find pasture"), the characteristic as defined is specifically eschatological and spiritual in scope — rescue from sin, condemnation, and final judgment. Physical preservation (Mat 24:22, Mar 13:20) appears in the corpus but is distinguished from the primary eschatological meaning by context. (2) This characteristic is not sanctification or moral formation as an independent category: Tit 2:11-12 and 2Th 2:13 link salvation to sanctification, but sanctification is a consequence and accompanying process of salvation, not identical with it. (3) This characteristic is not justification as a separate legal act, though justification is included within it: Rom 5:9 distinguishes present justification from future salvation-from-wrath, showing justification as a component of the larger salvation-complex, not equivalent to it. (4) This characteristic ends where self-generation begins: wherever the inner being treats rescue as self-produced, merited, or purchasable (Act 8:20, Act 15:1, Eph 2:8), the characteristic is excluded. (5) This characteristic ends where defection replaces perseverance: 2Pe 2:20 and Heb 6:4 show that abandonment of known salvation constitutes a transition out of the characteristic's territory.

**T1.4.3 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes. The corpus evidences a speech-based mode of operation at several points. (1) Confession (homologeō): Rom 10:9-10 (M38-A-VCG-02) is the primary text — "confessing with the mouth that Jesus is Lord" is paired with heart-belief as the dual inner-outer mechanism of salvation. The spoken confession is not merely a social performance but an inner-being act that completes and expresses the volitional turning of faith. Salvation is thus partly constituted by its verbal declaration. (2) Calling on the name: Act 2:21 and Rom 10:13 name "calling on the Lord's name" as the decisive speech-act through which salvation is received — the invocation is both a speech-act and a volitional act, inseparable from faith. (3) Proclamation received: Act 11:14, Act 13:26, and Eph 1:13 (M38-A-VCG-02) all identify hearing the proclaimed word as the channel through which salvation enters the inner being — the characteristic depends on a communicative event (proclamation-hearing) as its ordinary delivery mechanism. (4) Testimony and witness: Joh 4:42 shows salvation received through direct hearing of testimony; 1Jo 4:14 grounds assurance in apostolic testimony. (5) Doxological speech as culmination: Rev 7:10, Rev 12:10, Rev 19:1 show that salvation's completion is itself voiced — the speech-act of praise is the characteristic's doxological mode at full expression.

**T1.5.3 — Immediate Response — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Several VCGs are relatively silent on immediate response, focusing instead on the condition or condition-outcome without specifying the first experiential movement. M38-A-VCG-03 (gift-language verses) largely describes the gift and its ground rather than the inner-being response to receiving it — with the notable exception of 2Cor 9:15 (doxological overflow) and Act 10:45 (astonishment at Spirit's outpouring). The gift-reception verses of Rom 5:15-17 describe the inner-being state produced (righteousness, life, reigning) but do not narrate the immediate experiential response to receiving the gift. M38-A-VCG-08 (salvation's scope — remnant, Israel, Gentiles) is largely silent on immediate response; these verses address the scope and direction of salvation, not the experiential moment of reception. M38-A-VCG-10 (doxological completion) describes the culminating state rather than the immediate response to initial reception — Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1, and Rev 12:10 show the completed doxological state, not the first movement of faith. The evidence for immediate response is concentrated in VCG-02 (faith-reception mechanisms) and VCG-07 (human impossibility and awakened conscience), with other VCGs providing richer evidence of sustained or completed states than of first responses.

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the sustained effect differs meaningfully from the immediate response in kind, depth, and scope. The immediate response (T1.5.1) is characterized by the volitional act of faith-turning, accompanied by peace, urgency resolved, astonishment at divine sufficiency, or joyful recognition. These are punctiliar or near-punctiliar inner events. The sustained effect, by contrast, is characterized by: (1) Developed assurance replacing initial faith-exercise — whereas faith is the first movement, assurance (the inner confidence that rescue is secured and perpetual) is the sustained state. Heb 7:25 and 2Ti 4:18 show this as a deep, settled inner rest, not the initial urgency of calling on the name. (2) Persevering will replacing initial decision — Mat 10:22 and 1Cor 15:2 show that "holding fast" is the sustained form of what began as an act of believing; the same inner faculty is engaged but its sustained expression is endurance, not initial choice. (3) Moral formation replacing conscience-cleansing — the immediate effect on conscience is cleansing (Tit 3:5); the sustained effect is moral seriousness, holiness, and self-controlled living (Tit 2:11-12). (4) Missional orientation replacing self-focused reception — the immediate effect is reception of rescue for oneself; the sustained effect re-orients the inner being outward toward others' salvation (1Cor 9:22, Jam 5:20). The sustained effect is thus qualitatively richer, more integrated, and more other-oriented than the immediate response.

**T1.7.3 — Conditions of Reception — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus characterizes the non-receiving inner being with notable specificity. (1) Perishing: 1Cor 1:18 and 2Cor 2:15 name the structural state as perishing — not a neutral undecided condition but an active movement toward destruction. The same word, the same moment, the same proclamation produces opposite inner outcomes. (2) Willful truth-refusal: 2Th 2:10 identifies those perishing as those who "refused love of truth" — the inner state is not ignorance but deliberate rejection, a will actively turned away. (3) Closed perception: Act 7:25 shows people unable to perceive the salvation being offered — "understanding was closed" — suggesting a non-receiving inner state characterized by a failure of receptive comprehension. (4) Self-protective clinging: Mat 16:25 and its parallels (M38-A-VCG-06) show the non-receiving inner state as one that seeks to preserve its own life, grasping at self-security rather than releasing to Christ. (5) Increasing peril after partial encounter: 2Pe 2:20 and Heb 6:4 describe a particularly acute non-receiving state — those who have tasted the gift and turned away are "worse off than before," in a condition of greater inner danger than pure ignorance. (6) Condemnation: Mar 16:16 names the non-receiving state directly — unbelief yields condemnation. The inner state of the non-receiving person is therefore not passive neutrality but an active condition of perishing, self-enclosure, and increasing exposure to judgment.

**T1.8.3 — Dimension Classification — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Yes, the corpus evidences multiple secondary dimensions that enrich but do not displace the primary volitional classification. (1) Cognitive/noetic dimension: 2Ti 3:15 (scripture making one "wise for salvation through faith"), Act 13:26 (salvation-word sent to receptive hearers), Luk 1:77 (salvation-knowledge given through forgiveness), and Act 7:25 (understanding closed as a blocking condition) all show that the mind's reception of truth is a real component of the characteristic. The cognitive dimension is present but secondary — it is the will's trust-directed orientation that activates the mind's receptive knowledge, not vice versa. (2) Affective/experiential dimension: Peace (Luk 7:50), joy (Luk 19:9), astonishment (Mat 19:25), gratitude and praise (2Cor 9:15, Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1) are all affective responses arising from the characteristic. These are real inner-being states produced by salvation but they follow from the volitional reception rather than constituting its primary locus. (3) Conscience dimension: the awakened conscience seeking rescue (Act 16:30), the cleansed conscience after salvation (Tit 3:5), and the watchful conscience sustaining it (1Ti 4:16) constitute a conscience-dimension that is closely related to but distinguishable from pure volitional will. These secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary volitional classification; rather, they confirm it by showing the will's turning activating and integrating the other inner-being faculties around it.

**T2.1.1 — Spirit-Level Location — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus contains one explicit spirit-level reference. 1 Cor 5:5 (M38-A-VCG-10) distinguishes the destruction of the flesh from the salvation of the spirit ("the spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord"), naming the spirit as the locus of eschatological rescue that survives bodily judgment. Beyond this instance, the overwhelming majority of verses in M38-A do not use pneuma-language to locate salvation constitutionally at the spirit level. The characteristic is far more consistently located at the soul (psychē), heart (kardia), and will than at the explicit spirit level.

**T2.2.1 — Soul-Level Location — Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Soul-level location is explicitly evidenced in multiple verses. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) states that the implanted word "is able to save the soul" (psychēn). 1 Pe 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-04) names "the outcome of your faith" as "the salvation of your souls" (sōtērian psychōn). Jam 5:20 (M38-A-VCG-10) speaks of turning a sinner from wandering to "save a soul from death." Rev 7:10 and 19:1 (M38-A-VCG-10) frame the completed doxological response of the redeemed. These instances consistently identify the soul (psychē) as the constitutive locus of the salvation received — the level at which rescue is targeted and where the outcome of faith is deposited.

**T2.3.1 — Heart — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Heart-location is multiply and explicitly evidenced. Rom 10:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) ties salvation to "believing in your heart that God raised him from the dead." Rom 10:10 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies that "with the heart one believes and is justified." Luk 8:12 (M38-A-VCG-02) identifies the heart as the battleground where the word must take root to produce salvation, noting that the devil takes the word from the heart to prevent belief. Act 16:31 (M38-A-VCG-02) frames saving faith as an inner act of the heart trusting in the Lord Jesus. The heart is the primary receptive organ of saving faith across the corpus.

**T2.4.1 — Mind — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Mind-location is present but secondary and indirect. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) specifies that Scripture makes one "wise for salvation" — wisdom here is a mind-formation process that serves saving faith. Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) notes that rescue was offered but the people's "understanding was closed," making receptive comprehension a mind-level prerequisite for receiving salvation. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) links God's desire that all be saved with coming "to the knowledge of the truth," joining rescue with cognitive reception. These instances are genuine but do not constitute the primary location; the mind serves as an instrument of reception rather than the originating seat of the characteristic.

**T2.5.1 — Other Soul Subsets — Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The will (volitional faculty) surfaces as a distinct soul-level location beyond heart and mind. Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24, Luk 17:33 (M38-A-VCG-06) collectively establish the will's surrender and release of self-protective interest as the decisive inner movement through which eschatological life is received. Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, Mar 13:13 (M38-A-VCG-05) identify perseverance of will under trial as the ongoing inner condition of salvation. Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) calls believers to "work out" salvation with reverence, engaging a sustained volitional engagement. The conscience is also implicated: Act 16:30 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows the awakened conscience driving the jailer's urgent "what must I do to be saved?" — a soul-level location distinct from heart or mind per se.

**T2.6.1 — Body — Significance — Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two body-part links are evidenced. First, the mouth: Rom 10:9–10 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies that mouth-confession ("confesses with your mouth that Jesus is Lord") is paired with heart-belief as the dual pathway to salvation. Second, the head: Eph 6:17 (M38-A-VCG-10) names the "helmet of salvation" as armor worn on the head, linking salvation's protective reality to the body's most exposed and identity-bearing member. Beyond these two instances, the corpus does not systematically link this characteristic to other specific body parts.

**T2.7.1 — Body — Direction — Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — For the mouth-confession link (Rom 10:9–10, M38-A-VCG-02), the direction is soul-to-body: inner heart-belief expresses outward through the mouth's confession. The confession does not generate faith; it declares and confirms what the heart already holds. For the helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17, M38-A-VCG-10), the direction is ambiguous: salvation is an inner reality donned outwardly as protection, suggesting a soul-to-body-to-soul feedback loop where the inner possession is embodied in protective form. Neither link involves the body generating the characteristic; both show inner reality expressing or protecting through the body.

**T2.8.1 — Body — Deposit — Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The corpus is silent on constitutional deposit in the body, DNA, or generational consequence. No verse in M38-A makes a claim about biological or generational transmission of the salvific state. The closest generational language is Act 11:14 and Act 16:31 (M38-A-VCG-02) which speak of household salvation, but these describe communal reception through shared faith-response rather than genetic or biological transmission.

**T2.9.1 — Origin and Source — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus is emphatic and consistent: the origin of eschatological salvation is entirely external to the person and bestowed by God. Eph 2:8 (M38-A-VCG-02, ANCHOR) states that salvation is "by grace through faith — and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." Rom 5:15–17 (M38-A-VCG-03) frames salvation as dōrea/dōrēma — free gift — contrasting it with anything the person generates. 2 Ti 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies it is "not because of our works but because of his own purpose and grace." Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) explicitly excludes "our own righteous deeds" and locates origin in divine mercy through the Spirit. The person contributes no originating cause; faith is the receptive instrument, not the source.

**T2.10.1 — Constitutional Movement — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence supports a downward and outward constitutional movement. The initiative originates from God (divine/external), enters the inner being through the soul (the soul receives salvation as its telos, 1 Pe 1:9), is appropriated by the heart through faith (Rom 10:9–10), engages the will in either reception or surrender (M38-A-VCG-06), and expresses outward through the mouth in confession (Rom 10:9) and through embodied warfare in the helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17). 1 Cor 5:5 (M38-A-VCG-10) suggests an inward movement: even the body's destruction can serve the spirit's ultimate rescue, implying that the salvific reality can persist at the deepest constitutional level even when outer levels are stripped away.

**T2.1.2 — Spirit-Level Location — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence does not support spirit-level as the primary location or origin. The single explicit spirit-level reference (1 Cor 5:5: "that his spirit may be saved") describes the spirit as the object and destination of salvation under extreme disciplinary conditions, not its originating level. The characteristic originates from God (external divine gift) and is received primarily at the soul and heart level. Spirit-level is the deepest preservation-point of rescue in extremis, not the constitutional home or origin of the characteristic.

**T2.2.2 — Soul-Level Location — What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Soul-level location reveals that eschatological salvation penetrates to the innermost personal center of the human being — the psychē as the seat of life, personal identity, and experienced reality. 1 Pe 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-04) naming salvation as "the outcome of your faith" received at the soul level positions this characteristic as the ultimate telos of the entire personal economy: faith reaches its destination in the rescued soul. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies the "implanted word" operating at the soul to produce salvation, indicating that divine communication must reach this level to achieve rescue. The soul-level location establishes that salvation is not a surface or external change but an innermost re-constitution of the personal center.

**T2.3.2 — Heart — What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The heart's believing/trusting function is the primary aspect engaged. Rom 10:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies believing "in your heart," and Rom 10:10 pairs heart-belief with justification, indicating that the heart functions here as the organ of deep trust-commitment rather than cognitive assent alone. Luk 8:12 (M38-A-VCG-02) identifies the heart as the location where the saving word must take root — engaging the heart's receiving and holding function. Act 16:30–31 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows an awakened conscience within the heart driving the will to seek rescue. The heart's integrating function is thus engaged in its willing, trusting, and morally-aware dimensions simultaneously: the heart turns toward Christ (will), commits in trust (faith), and responds to the awareness of need (moral awareness). Knowing/cognition is present but subordinate.

**T2.4.2 — Mind — What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Where mind-level engagement is evidenced, it is the understanding/reception function rather than originating thought. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) shows Scripture forming the mind toward wisdom that grasps salvation — the mind's function is formative reception of revealed content. Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) shows that understanding must be open to perceive the salvation being offered — the mind's discernment of the saving act is a necessary precondition. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) links salvation with "knowledge of the truth" — the mind's reception of salvific truth. The mind does not generate this characteristic but must be oriented toward and receptive of it; its function is discernment and comprehension of an externally given gift rather than origination.

**T2.5.2 — Other Soul Subsets — If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two additional soul-subset locations are evidenced. **T2.6.2 — Body — Significance — If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The two body-part links serve different rhetorical and functional purposes. The mouth-confession link in Rom 10:9–10 (M38-A-VCG-02) is functional and mediating: the mouth's verbal declaration serves as the outward functional completion of inward heart-faith, and the pairing of heart-belief with mouth-confession suggests that salvation engages both dimensions of the person. The expression through the mouth is not merely decorative but a required element of the whole act of receiving salvation in its fullness. The helmet of salvation in Eph 6:17 (M38-A-VCG-10) is metaphorical and protective/indicative: salvation is named as a piece of armor worn on the body's most vital part, indicating that the inner reality of being saved functions as cognitive and identity protection against spiritual assault. The link is emphatic in its imagery — salvation shields what the person most fundamentally is.

**T2.7.2 — Body — Direction — What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent soul-to-body directionality (inner faith expressing outward through mouth-confession; inner salvation worn as helmet) establishes that eschatological salvation is constitutionally interior in origin and bodily in expression. This directionality has two consequences: (1) It confirms that salvation cannot be reduced to outward ritual or bodily act alone — the mouth's confession without heart-belief is incomplete (Rom 10:9–10). The body can only genuinely express what the inner being has genuinely received. (2) It establishes that received inner salvation has bodily consequences: the saved person expresses salvation verbally (confession), defends through it protectively (helmet), and inhabits it as a public stance (Act 2:40, separation from a corrupt generation). The characteristic is inner in origin, outer in manifestation.

Cleansing: Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) and 1 Ti 1:15 (M38-A-VCG-01) show salvation received by the "chief of sinners" — conscience that was weighted with guilt is addressed by the very salvation it sought. The helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17, M38-A-VCG-10) protects the mind/conscience from renewed accusation. Rev 12:10 (M38-A-VCG-10) explicitly shows the conscience vindicated as the accuser is cast down: "salvation has come" as the declaration that ends condemnation. The characteristic does not impair conscience; it first activates it (bringing to the surface what guilt is present) and then cleanses and vindicates it.

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic deepens conscientiousness as an integrated moral-volitional-action response. Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) is the fullest instance: "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" requires the integration of moral seriousness (fear and trembling), deliberate volition (working out), and sustained action — which is precisely the definition of conscientiousness. 1 Ti 4:16 (M38-A-VCG-05) deepens this: the faithful teacher "watches his life and doctrine carefully" in an integrated sustained moral watchfulness motivated by the salvific goal. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) calls for the "meekness" of receptive conscientiousness. The endurance cluster (Mat 10:22; Mat 24:13; Mar 13:13) represents conscientiousness sustained under external assault. The characteristic does not bypass or impair conscientiousness; it provides the motivational ground (the weight of salvation received and to be secured) that drives its deepening.

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic enables and deepens relational capacity in both vertical and horizontal directions. Vertically: Rom 5:10 (M38-A-VCG-03, ANCHOR) shows reconciliation as the constitutive relational act — former enmity transformed into ongoing relational security in God. Act 5:31 (M38-A-VCG-01) shows repentance and forgiveness as the relational restoration that salvation effects. Horizontally: the community of the saved (Act 2:47, M38-A-VCG-04) constitutes a relational body incorporated together. Jam 5:20 and 1 Cor 7:16 (M38-A-VCG-10) show salvation activating relational concern for others' rescue. Rom 11:14 (M38-A-VCG-08) shows Paul's relational investment in his kinspeople's salvation. The characteristic does not impair relational capacity; the one notable exception — 2 Pe 2:20 (M38-A-VCG-01) — shows that abandoned salvation correlates with deteriorated relational and moral capacity, confirming that received salvation is what sustains it.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation necessarily enters through the perceptive faculty but is not reducible to it. The dominant perceptive mode is hearing (word received, message heard, gospel proclaimed), followed by sight (Simeon's eyes, the witnessed Savior), and taste (heavenly gift tasted in Heb 6:4). The consistent pattern is: an externally given salvific reality must be perceptively received before it is cognitively processed and volitionally accepted. Perception is the entry point, not the seat, of the characteristic. The negative evidence — closed understanding (Act 7:25), word stolen from the heart (Luk 8:12) — confirms that blocked perception blocks salvation-reception. This reveals that the characteristic is not self-generated by the inner being; it requires an external input that the inner perceptive faculty must be open to receive. The characteristic is constitutionally dependent on receptive perception.

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic requires cognition without being generated by it. Cognition is consistently engaged as a receptive and discerning faculty: grasping the word, understanding salvation offered, being formed by Scripture toward wisdom. But 1 Cor 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) is the critical statement: human wisdom could not reach God through its own cognitive effort — "the world did not know God through wisdom." God bypassed autonomous cognitive route and provided the foolishness of proclamation, through which faith (not cognition alone) receives rescue. The pattern shows that cognition is necessary but insufficient: it must be redirected from self-originating reasoning toward receptive comprehension of revealed truth. The characteristic reveals that human cognition in its unsaved state is constitutionally incapable of generating its own rescue — a finding about cognitive limitation in the unredeemed condition.

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The limited but consistent pattern of memory-engagement reveals that eschatological salvation is a temporally extended rather than merely instantaneous characteristic. The initial reception (when one "first believed," Rom 13:11) must be held across time; the historical precedents of rescue (Jude 5) must be retained as warning resources; the received salvation must be maintained against drift (Heb 2:3; 1 Cor 15:2). This reveals that salvation is not only a point-event but an ongoing state that requires the memory faculty to anchor it against time-pressure. The modest engagement of memory — it appears in warning and stability contexts, not in the primary reception mechanism — suggests that memory is a supporting rather than constitutive faculty for this characteristic: it preserves and stabilizes received salvation but is not the means by which it is received.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The rich and consistent affective engagement reveals that eschatological salvation is constitutionally transformative of the affective life — it cannot be received without producing inner experiential change. The pattern shows three affective phases: (1) pre-salvific affect — godly grief (2 Cor 7:10), awakened urgency (Act 16:30), astonishment at the barrier to salvation (Mat 19:25); (2) reception affect — peace conferred (Luk 7:50; Luk 2:30), awe at unexpected grace (Act 10:45); (3) ongoing and eschatological affect — gratitude (2 Cor 9:15), joy in community (Act 2:47 context), and the full corporate doxological eruption of the redeemed (Rev 7:10; 19:1). The pattern reveals that the characteristic is affectively whole: it engages and transforms the feeling-life from guilt and urgency through peace to joy and praise. A salvation without affective engagement would be, in this evidence-base, anomalous.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The complete silence of the corpus on creativity reveals that eschatological salvation as evidenced in M38-A is entirely a received and responsive characteristic — nothing in its reception, sustaining, or completion requires or engages originative or imaginative capacity. The inner being's role is to receive, hold, confess, endure, and praise — all responsive modes. This consistent non-engagement of creativity is itself a structural finding: the characteristic is defined by its receptive rather than generative inner-being posture.

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pervasive and multidimensional engagement of volition reveals that eschatological salvation is not a purely passive inner-being state but a characteristic that structures, redirects, and requires the will throughout its reception and maintenance. Three volitional patterns emerge: (1) Initial turning — the will turns toward Christ in calling, confession, faith-reception, and surrender of self-preservation (M38-A-VCG-02, -06, -09); (2) Sustained perseverance — the will must hold under trial and not defect (M38-A-VCG-05); (3) Missionary direction — the will is reshaped toward others' salvation through self-adaptation, relational persistence, and turning wanderers back (M38-A-VCG-08, T3.11). Critically, volitional engagement does not imply volitional self-sufficiency: Eph 2:8 (ANCHOR) and 2 Ti 1:9 exclude works and self-origination. The pattern reveals a characteristic that fully engages the will as receptive and persevering instrument while leaving it constitutionally dependent on grace.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals a constitutive asymmetry in agency: God's agency is primary, unlimited, and originating; human agency is derivative, responsive, and enabled by divine initiative. The recurring structure is: God saves (active divine agency) → person calls, believes, confesses, endures (responsive human agency). The characteristic does not impair human agency but re-structures it: human agency is re-oriented away from self-generated rescue toward responsive participation in divinely initiated rescue. The most revealing instances are the missionary agency verses (1 Cor 9:22; Rom 11:14; Jam 5:20) — persons acting as agents of others' salvation, but always in the mode of servants of God's saving purpose (Act 13:47), not independent rescuers. The pattern discloses a characteristic that is simultaneously the most exclusively divine act (God alone saves) and the one that most fully mobilizes human agency in its service.

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent engagement of moral evaluation reveals that eschatological salvation is not morally neutral — it creates a new moral framework within which the inner being evaluates truth, faith, and action. The pattern shows moral evaluation operating in three modes: (1) Evaluating conditions — assessing what salvation requires (Act 15:1; Jam 2:14); (2) Evaluating culpability — judging those who refuse or neglect salvation as morally accountable (Heb 2:3; 2 Th 2:10); (3) Evaluating authenticity — testing whether faith is genuine saving faith (Jam 2:14). The cross creates a new moral evaluation standard: 1 Cor 1:18 (M38-A-VCG-04) shows that what those being saved evaluate as "the power of God" is evaluated by those perishing as "folly." Received salvation calibrates moral evaluation; its absence or rejection distorts it. The characteristic is both the object of moral evaluation (is this genuine rescue?) and its recalibrator (it reshapes what the inner being evaluates as true, good, and powerful).

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that conscience serves as the threshold faculty for salvation-reception: it must be activated before the will turns, and it must be cleansed after the will receives. This two-phase pattern — conscience as catalyst and conscience as object of cleansing — reveals that the characteristic addresses the inner being at its most morally sensitive point. The awakening of conscience (Act 16:30; 2 Cor 7:10) precedes the turn to salvation; the cleansing of conscience (Tit 3:5; Rev 12:10) is the mark of salvation received. The warning pattern (Heb 2:3; Jude 5) shows that an inactive conscience that fails to register the weight of salvation at risk is itself a sign of spiritual danger. The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation is the one inner-being reality that both requires a functioning conscience (to see the need for rescue) and constitutively addresses it (removing guilt and silencing accusation).

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation, once received, generates conscientiousness as its ongoing inner-being expression. The evidence concentrates in M38-A-VCG-05 and the perseverance cluster: Phili 2:12, 1 Ti 4:16, Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, and Mar 13:13 all show salvation sustained through integrated moral-volitional-action response. This reveals that the characteristic is not static or punctiliar in its inner-being effect — received salvation transforms the inner being into an agent of sustained conscientious response. The "working out" of salvation (Phili 2:12) is not an add-on to salvation but its present inner expression. The pattern suggests a constitutive connection: genuine eschatological salvation received generates conscientiousness; the absence of conscientiousness (endurance collapsing, life and doctrine unwatched) creates risk for the salvific state itself (1 Ti 4:16). Conscientiousness is both fruit and condition of maintained salvation.

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent and bilateral engagement of relational capacity reveals that eschatological salvation is constitutively relational in structure at every level. The pattern shows: (1) Salvation is received through a relational encounter (the Samaritans' direct encounter with Jesus, Joh 4:42; Simeon's personal witness, Luk 2:30; the jailer's direct appeal, Act 16:30–31); (2) Its content is relational restoration — reconciliation from enmity to access (Rom 5:10, ANCHOR); (3) Its communal expression is a body of persons incorporated together (Act 2:47); (4) Its ongoing motivation is relational concern for others' rescue (Rom 11:14; Jam 5:20; 1 Cor 7:16). The complete pattern reveals that this characteristic cannot be adequately described in purely transactional or forensic terms alone — it is simultaneously forensic (justification, condemnation reversed) and constitutively relational (reconciliation, community, person-to-person witness and mediation). Relational capacity is not incidental to the characteristic but belongs to its definition.

**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence overwhelmingly shows eschatological salvation operating primarily as a divine initiative directed toward the human person. God is the consistent grammatical and theological agent: in Joh 3:17 "God sent his Son not to condemn but to save the world"; in Eph 2:8 salvation by grace through faith is "God's gift"; in Tit 3:5 "God saved us not by our righteous deeds but by his mercy"; in 2Ti 1:9 "God saved believers and called them to holiness by grace purposed before time." The Spirit is given as dōrea (Act 2:38, Act 10:45, Act 11:17). Righteousness is bestowed as gift (Rom 5:15-17, M38-A-VCG-03). Christ is the Savior sent by divine appointment (Act 13:23, 1Jo 4:14, Act 5:31). The divine-to-human vector is structurally foundational — salvation does not originate in the human person and moves toward them from outside, grounded in divine purpose established before time.

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows multiple forms of human movement toward God in connection with eschatological salvation. Doxological response — the heavenly multitude ascribing salvation to God and the Lamb (Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1) — constitutes worship as the human-to-God culmination of salvation. The apostle's intercessory prayer for others' salvation (Rom 10:1) represents the saved person's movement toward God on behalf of others.

**T4.3.1 — Human Interface — Giving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

Jam 1:21 presents the "implanted word" as the medium through which salvation is received. The inner act required for reception is faith — hearing, trusting, and accepting the message rather than generating rescue from within.

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals a complex basis combining unconditional divine initiative with covenantal grounding and responsive appropriation. The initiative is unconditional in origin: Eph 2:8 states salvation is "God's gift, not of works"; 2Ti 1:9 places it in grace "purposed before time"; Tit 3:5 grounds it in "mercy" not human righteousness. The covenantal dimension is explicit: Act 13:23 roots the Savior in David's covenant line; Rom 11:26-27 grounds all Israel's future rescue in prophetic covenant promise. The responsive dimension is present but secondary: salvation is appropriated by those who call (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13), believe (Eph 2:8, Act 16:31), confess (Rom 10:9), and hear (Eph 1:13). The faith-response is itself not an originating condition but a reception point — Eph 2:8 explicitly denies that even faith is self-generated. The overall structure is: unconditional divine initiative, covenantally grounded, received through responsive faith.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence identifies multiple inner postures required for the human-toward-God movement in receiving salvation. Heart-belief (Rom 10:9-10) names an inner faculty of trust directed toward the risen Christ. Calling on the Lord's name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13) requires the will to invoke, an active turning of the person toward God. Repentance (Act 2:38, Act 2:40) involves a decisive inner turning away from sin and moral separation. Godly sorrow (2Cor 7:10) identifies a specific grief-quality that produces genuine repentance rather than worldly despair. Meekness/humility of will (Jam 1:21) enables the soul to receive the implanted word. The self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24) reveal that surrender of self-protective instincts is the posture through which the true self is eschatologically saved. An awakened conscience seeking rescue (Act 16:30) and a will striving to enter the narrow gate (Luk 13:23) also appear. The common inner posture is receptive, non-self-sufficient, will-turning orientation.

**T4.3.2 — Human Interface — Giving — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals specific inner conditions required in those who mediate salvation to others. Missional love is foundational: Paul's self-adaptation (1Cor 9:22) and self-denial (1Cor 10:33) are driven by love for others' rescue, with the will wholly oriented toward the eschatological good of others rather than personal advantage. Intercessory desire and prayer (Rom 10:1) name the heart's sustained longing as an enabling condition. Willingness to suffer for others' rescue (2Ti 2:10) identifies self-sacrifice as an enabling posture. Faithful teaching with a watchful conscience (1Ti 4:16) names doctrinal and moral integrity as enabling conditions for saving one's hearers. The rescue of wandering sinners (Jam 5:20) and snatching from fire (Jude 23) require courageous, fear-engaged moral seriousness. The common thread is: the giver must themselves be rooted in received grace (not self-originating rescue) and must be moved by other-directed will rather than self-interest.

**T4.4.2 — Human Interface — Receiving — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence identifies both enabling and blocking conditions for reception from a human intermediary. Enabling conditions include: a receptive, fear-reverent hearing stance (Act 13:26); an awakened conscience seeking rescue (Act 16:30); meekness of will before the implanted word (Jam 1:21); and belief in the message received (Eph 1:13, 1Cor 1:21). The will's openness to hearing is essential — Act 28:28 contrasts Gentiles "who will listen" with those who rejected the message. Blocking conditions include: love of truth refused (2Th 2:10, "perishing refused love of truth"), understanding closed to the deliverance offered (Act 7:25), and the devil removing the word before it can take root (Luk 8:12). Act 8:20 reveals a heart oriented toward purchasing grace as a commodity — a mercenary inner orientation that fundamentally misunderstands what is being offered. The primary blocker is the will's closing against truth; the primary enabler is the will's openness and receptive faith.

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence consistently shows eschatological salvation crossing covenantal boundaries rather than remaining confined to them. Joh 4:22 preserves the covenantal origin ("salvation is from the Jews") while Act 13:23 roots Jesus in Davidic covenant. **T4.6.2 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence explicitly identifies eschatological salvation as a site of adversarial attack and distortion. The adversary's primary strategy appears to be: remove or corrupt the word before faith can be exercised, or distort salvation's character from gift to transaction.

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals a divine disposition of unreserved, initiative-taking saving intent toward the human person. Joh 3:17 states the entire purpose of the Son's sending is to save, not condemn, revealing saving as God's primary disposition rather than a reluctant concession. 1Ti 2:4 shows God desiring "all people" to be saved — the disposition is universal in scope. Eph 2:5 shows God acting on an inner state of spiritual death — the person contributes nothing, God acts wholly from his own merciful disposition. Tit 3:5 grounds salvation in "mercy," not human worth. The aggregate picture is a God whose disposition toward the human person is actively, extravagantly, and unconditionally saving — prior to and without regard to human merit.

**T4.2.3 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The human-toward-God movement in eschatological salvation reveals a relationship of complete dependence, recognized need, and responsive trust. The disciples' astonished question "who then can be saved?" (Mat 19:25, Mar 10:26, Luk 18:26) reveals a relationship in which the human person recognizes their own complete inability — no self-sufficiency before God is possible. Confession of Christ as Lord (Rom 10:9) reveals the person's relational alignment under divine authority. Self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25 etc.) reveal that the relationship to God requires release of autonomous self-possession. The relationship is one of acknowledged need, receptive dependence, and grateful worship.

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence implies that genuine mediation of salvation to others requires prior reception of salvation oneself, though this is stated indirectly. Jude 23 urges rescue of others "with fear, hating even the garment stained by the flesh" — requiring a conscience already engaged by what sin means. Rom 10:1 reveals intercessory desire as a movement of one who has already received what they desire for others. The rescue of wandering sinners (Jam 5:20) presupposes the rescuer is not themselves wandering. The evidence consistently implies: to mediate salvation one must first have received the gift rather than originated it.

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence describes the non-receiving person in terms of several inner states. Refusal of truth's love (2Th 2:10) characterizes the perishing — the will is closed against the very content that would bring rescue. Those who reject the message in Act 28:28 are contrasted with Gentiles who will listen. The state of the non-receiver in Rev 12:10 is one of perpetual accusation without vindication. 2Pe 2:20 describes those who once knew the Savior but turned back — characterized as worse off than their original ignorance, with defilement returning. The non-receiver exists in a state of perishing (1Cor 1:18), wrath-facing (1Th 5:9), condemnation (Mar 16:16), and ongoing spiritual danger — the structural opposite of the rescued state.

**T4.5.3 — Human Interface — Boundaries — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence presents eschatological salvation with an explicitly universal intended scope bounded only by receptive response. Included: all who call (Rom 10:13, "everyone who calls"), all who believe (Act 16:31, "you and your household"), Samaritans (Joh 4:42), Gentiles to the ends of the earth (Act 13:47), all Israel eschatologically (Rom 11:26), all people as God's desire (1Ti 2:4), a multitude from every nation (Rev 7:10). **T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary anchor is Eph 2:8: "For by grace you have been saved through faith, and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." The phrase "by grace… through faith" provides the two prepositional frames: *grace* names the sphere or ground, *faith* names the instrument or channel. The closing clause ("not of yourselves; it is the gift of God") functions as an *explicit negation of self-origination*, which serves the argument of Eph 2:1–10 as a whole: dead persons do not raise themselves; God raises them.

Rom 5:9 names the ground (blood) without naming the inner-being reception instrument. Tit 3:5 names the exclusion of works without naming faith as the reception instrument. 1Cor 1:21 names faith-reception without specifying the gift-character of salvation. Only Eph 2:8 reveals that the inner-being act of faith is itself not the originating cause — the verse protects faith from becoming a human contribution by naming both grace *and* faith as gift-given, leaving the inner being with nothing to supply except openness to what God gives. This is the verse's unique contribution: it prevents faith from functioning as a hidden merit-system.

**T7.1.7 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two frameworks have meaningful but asymmetric value. (1) *Psychology of conversion and transformation* — particularly William James's analysis in *The Varieties of Religious Experience* and contemporary work on surrender, crisis-induced transformation, and the shift from self-reliance to trust — is the most useful single framework. The evidence base repeatedly narrates the inner-being movement from helplessness (Act 16:30 jailer's panic; Mat 19:25 disciples' astonishment; Luk 18:26 "who can be saved?") to trust-and-reception, which maps closely onto the psychology of crisis-conversion: the self reaches the limit of its own resources and undergoes a reorientation of dependency. (2) *Gift theory* (anthropological/sociological, drawing on Marcel Mauss's *The Gift* and its successors) is the second most useful framework, given the dōrea/dōrēma vocabulary cluster in VCG-03. Gift theory attends to the social dynamics of giving, receiving, and the asymmetry of the donor-recipient relationship, which illuminates the inner-being posture of pure reception that VCG-03 and Eph 2:8 require.

**T7.1.8 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The psychology-of-conversion framework illuminates VCG-07 (human impossibility) most directly. The disciples' astonishment ("who then can be saved?" Mat 19:25; Mar 10:26; Luk 18:26) and the jailer's awakened panic (Act 16:30) represent the inner-being threshold identified by conversion psychology as the *breaking point of self-reliance*: the moment when the self discovers its own resources are insufficient for the task at hand. Conversion psychology notes that transformation typically cannot begin until this threshold is crossed — the self must release its prior framework of self-sufficiency before a new orientation (trust/reception) can be structurally adopted. This makes the "human impossibility" material in VCG-07 not merely a theological claim but an accurate inner-being phenomenology: the question "who can be saved?" is the psychological entry-point that clears the way for "with God all things are possible" to function as a genuine reorientation rather than a religious formula. Gift theory illuminates VCG-03 by naming the irreversibility of gift-receipt: once a gift is received without condition, the recipient's relational standing is transformed.

**T7.1.9 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The divergence is significant in two areas. (1) *Conversion psychology typically frames transformation as the outcome of internal psychological dynamics* — latent tensions, unconscious processes, or developmental pressure reaching resolution. The verse evidence, by contrast, consistently frames salvation as initiated *from outside the inner being entirely*: God acts on the dead (Eph 2:5), the word is implanted (Jam 1:21), the Spirit is poured out (Act 2:38). The inner-being is not the source of its own transformation; it is the *site* where external divine action takes effect. Conversion psychology cannot account for this asymmetry without importing theological premises it does not natively hold. (2) *Gift theory* typically identifies an implicit obligation in gift-receipt: the receiver is placed under social pressure to reciprocate. Rom 5:15–17 and Eph 2:8–9 explicitly refuse this dynamic: the divine gift of salvation creates no debt, imposes no counter-gift obligation, and is specifically protected from reciprocal obligation by the insistence that it is "not of works." The divergence reveals that the Biblical gift-structure is *asymmetric in a direction gift theory does not normally occupy*: purely gratuitous, creating grateful love rather than social debt.

**T7.1.10 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The psychology-of-conversion framework surfaces one under-addressed aspect: the *long-term integration of salvation into identity over time*. Conversion psychology distinguishes the initial conversion experience from the subsequent process of identity consolidation — the converted self must restructure its autobiographical narrative, its social networks, and its habitual patterns to cohere with the new inner orientation. The verse evidence covers the initial reception (VCG-02), ongoing perseverance (VCG-05), and eschatological completion (VCG-10) but has relatively sparse coverage of the intermediate process by which the saved self incorporates salvation into stable identity between initial faith and final completion. Phili 2:12 ("work out your own salvation") and 2Pe 3:18 ("grow in grace and knowledge") gesture toward this, but the evidence base does not extensively map the developmental middle. This absence suggests that a further investigation of sanctification and identity-formation verses adjacent to the salvation cluster would enrich the picture of how salvation operates as an ongoing inner-being reality rather than only a punctiliar event or a distant eschatological horizon.
