Jesus consistently names faith as the causal factor operating from within the person outward ("your faith has made you well," Mat 9:22, vc_id=9011; Mar 5:34, vc_id=9014; Mar 10:52, vc_id=9016; Luk 8:48, vc_id=9017; Luk 17:19, vc_id=9019; Luk 18:42, vc_id=9020). **T2.9.1 — Origin and Source — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence points to a *convergent* origin. On the human side, faith is formed within the person — the woman's interior conviction pre-exists Jesus's word (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28), and Bartimaeus's persistent cry arises from within himself. On the divine side, the saving act is explicitly attributed to Christ's power (sōzō named in every anchor verse) and, in Jam 5:15 (vc_id=9024), to God who heals through the prayer of faith. In 1Pe 3:21 (vc_id=8992) the appeal is directed *to* God through Christ's resurrection, grounding the saving act in divine agency. The characteristic therefore originates at the intersection of internally-generated faith and externally-bestowed divine power — neither purely from within the person nor purely imposed from outside.

**T2.10.1 — Constitutional Movement — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, a clear downward-then-manifesting movement is evidenced. Interior faith (formed at the heart/soul level) moves outward through the body (the touch of the garment, the cry of Bartimaeus) and occasions a physical healing, which then feeds back into the soul as peace (Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) or into restored relational posture (Mar 10:52, discipleship following). In Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21 the movement extends further: from intercessory faith (inner being of intercessor) → prayer (speech act) → healing of body → cleansing of conscience (inner being of the sick). The constitutional movement thus runs: soul/heart → bodily act → bodily healing → soul/conscience restored — a multi-level circuit.

**T2.6.2 — Body — Significance — If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link?**

**T2.7.2 — Body — Direction — What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary soul-to-body direction, with secondary body-to-soul feedback (established in T2.7.1), has two consequences for understanding the characteristic. This means the characteristic is neither purely inner (quietist) nor purely outer (ritualist) but constitutionally integrated, requiring the soul-body axis to be engaged for the full healing-wholeness event to occur.

**T2.9.2 — Origin and Source — What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence reveals a *dual* origin structure. (1) Human interior: faith is formed within the person's own inner being prior to any external prompting in most M38-C cases (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28, Mar 10:46–47). These two origins are not competitive; the corpus presents them as structurally necessary to each other — faith without the divine respondent is inert, and divine power here is consistently accessed through faith as the activating human contribution. The origin is therefore irreducibly dual.

**T2.10.2 — Constitutional Movement — What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The sequence evidenced across M38-C VCG-01 and VCG-02 follows a consistent four-stage pattern: (1) *Formation*: interior faith/conviction arises in the heart or conscience of the seeker (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28, Mar 5:23, Luk 8:50). VCG-02 compresses stages 1–2 into a crisis posture where fear and faith co-exist before the sōzō word arrives. The pattern is consistent and directional, not reversible.

**T3.11.1 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, centrally. The relational capacity is the structural context within which the entire characteristic operates. Faith in M38-C is always *directed toward a person* (Jesus, God), not toward an impersonal mechanism. The woman seeks Jesus specifically (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28). Bartimaeus addresses Jesus by name and title (Mar 10:47). The father pleads with Jesus for his daughter (Mar 5:23). The Samaritan returns to give thanks *to Jesus* (Luk 17:19). The prayer of faith in Jam 5:15 is addressed to God. The baptismal appeal in 1Pe 3:21 is "to God." In every case, faith-activated healing is constitutionally relational: the inner faculty of faith is directed toward a relational other (Christ/God), and the outcome (peace, discipleship, forgiven conscience) is a restored or deepened relational standing. The characteristic cannot operate outside the relational axis.

**T3.1.2 — Perception — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic primarily *enables and deepens* spiritual perception. In the individual healing narratives, the faith-seekers already possess a perceptive grasp of Jesus's identity before the healing event; the healing deepens that perception retroactively (the Samaritan sees Jesus differently after his cleansing; Bartimaeus follows Jesus with open eyes). In Luk 8:50 (vc_id=9018), Jesus's command "only believe" works *against* the perceptive report (the girl is dead) — faith here partially *bypasses* the evidence of physical perception in favour of spiritual discernment. This suggests that when fear-driven perception of mortal reality threatens faith, the characteristic holds the spiritual perception in tension with and priority over the physical. The characteristic does not impair perception but reorders its authority.

---

**T3.2.2 — Cognition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic both *enables* minimal cognitive content (the belief-proposition about Jesus's power) and *partially bypasses* extended cognition. Faith in M38-C does not wait for full theological understanding before acting — the woman acts on a conditional inference (Mat 9:21) without a developed christology. Luk 8:50 commands faith precisely when the cognitive data (death of the child) would rationally preclude hope. Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21 require cognitive engagement with the connection between body-healing and conscience-forgiveness, representing a more developed cognitive integration. The characteristic thus *enables* a basic cognitive grasp that grounds faith and *bypasses* the cognitive processing that would counsel despair, while in more mature expressions it *deepens* cognitive integration of physical and spiritual registers.

---

**T3.3.2 — Memory — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *enables* the deployment of memorial knowledge of Jesus as background substrate (prior testimony about Jesus recalled by faith-seekers), but the corpus does not evidence memory being deepened, bypassed, or impaired as a distinct consequence of the characteristic. The engagement with memory is enabling and foundational rather than transformative. No verse describes a change in the memory faculty resulting from faith-activated healing.

---

**T3.4.2 — Affect — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *transforms* affect: it does not eliminate it but redirects and fulfils it. In VCG-02, fear is not simply bypassed but is actively displaced by faith under command (Luk 8:50, vc_id=9018) — the affect of fear is replaced by the affect of trust. Parental love and urgency (Mar 5:23, vc_id=9012) are not suppressed but are channelled through faith toward a redemptive end. Peace received as outcome (Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) represents an *enabling and deepening* of positive affect — the soul is brought into a settled affective state of wholeness. Gratitude (Luk 17:19) shows affect *deepened* by the sōzō event. The characteristic does not impair affect; rather, it is the faculty through which distressed affect (fear, grief, urgency) is transformed into restful, grateful affect (peace, discipleship).

**T3.5.2 — Creativity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The corpus is silent on any enabling, deepening, bypassing, or impairing of the creative faculty through faith-activated healing-wholeness. No evidence in M38-C addresses this relationship.

---

**T3.6.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *enables and strengthens* volition. The evidence shows willpower actively engaged and carried through against obstacles: the woman presses through a crowd despite her ritual uncleanness (Mar 5:28); Bartimaeus continues crying out despite being silenced by the crowd (Mar 10:47–48, context vc_id=9016); the father pleads on behalf of another person with urgent determination (Mar 5:23). Luk 8:50 commands volition ("only believe") as a counter-act to fear — here the characteristic directly addresses the volitional capacity, commanding it to override an emotionally compelling alternative. Faith as presented in M38-C does not bypass the will (it is not imposed) but *strengthens* it — the person's will is fully engaged and its exercise is the activating mechanism of the sōzō event. No evidence of impairment.

---

**T3.7.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic strongly *enables* agency. As noted in T3.7.1, every instance in M38-C involves the faith-seeker as an active initiator. Jesus's confirmation formula ("your faith has made you well") *deepens* the person's sense of agency retroactively — they are told that their inner act was causally operative in the healing. This divine confirmation of human agency is remarkable: Jesus does not say "I healed you" but "your faith has saved you," attributing causation to the person's inner faculty. This deepens rather than overrides human agency, presenting the characteristic as one that elevates the person's inner-being capacity to be a genuine secondary cause in the sōzō event. No bypassing or impairment of agency is evidenced.

---

**T3.8.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *deepens* moral evaluation at the point where physical healing opens onto spiritual forgiveness. Jam 5:15 (vc_id=9024) — "if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven" — invites the sick person and the praying elders to hold open the possibility that sin has contributed to illness, requiring moral self-evaluation. 1Pe 3:21 (vc_id=8992) grounds the saving appeal in the desire for "a good conscience" — meaning that the person has evaluated their conscience before God and desires it to be cleared. The characteristic does not impair or bypass moral evaluation; rather, the wholeness it delivers is partly constituted by the fulfilment of moral evaluation (conscience satisfied, sin forgiven). No bypassing or impairment is evidenced.

---

**T3.9.2 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *enables and clears* the conscience. In 1Pe 3:21 (vc_id=8992) the saving event is specifically the restoration of a good conscience — sōzō reaches its inner terminus in conscience-cleansing. In Jam 5:15 (vc_id=9024) forgiveness accompanies healing, meaning the conscience that was burdened by sin is released. The characteristic thus *enables* a good conscience to exist where a troubled one did before. There is no evidence of the characteristic impairing or bypassing conscience; rather, conscience is the deepest stratum that the sōzō event must reach for healing-wholeness to be complete. Peace granted (Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) may be understood as a conscience at rest, further supporting this pattern.

---

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *enables and expresses* conscientiousness. The Samaritan leper's return in Luk 17:19 (vc_id=9019) is the paradigmatic instance: moral awareness of gift received + volitional return + physical prostration = a complete conscientious act. Jesus distinguishes this one returning leper from the nine who did not return, suggesting that the wholeness of sōzō is more fully realised in the person who completes the conscientious circuit. Jam 5:15 (vc_id=9024) requires the elders to act with integrated moral awareness (acknowledging sin's possible role), volition (choosing to pray), and action (anointing) — a conscientious process. The characteristic does not impair conscientiousness; in its fullest expressions it *calls conscientiousness forth* as the appropriate inner-being response to sōzō received.

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic *enables and deepens* relational capacity. The relational outcome of faith-activated healing is consistently a strengthened or newly initiated relationship with Jesus: Bartimaeus "followed Jesus" (Mar 10:52, vc_id=9016) — discipleship as deepened relational engagement; the Samaritan returned to give thanks and was told "your faith has made you well" — relational recognition sealed the wholeness. Peace received (Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) names a relational state (no longer at enmity or distance, but at rest before Jesus). The baptismal appeal of 1Pe 3:21 is "to God" — establishing a cleared-conscience relational standing before the Father. The characteristic does not impair or bypass relational capacity; it is inherently a relational event, and its deepest effect is the restoration or inauguration of genuine personal connection with Christ and God.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern of perception engagement in M38-C reveals that this characteristic is *trans-sensory* in its perceptive requirement. The faith-healings consistently involve persons whose physical perception is compromised (blind Bartimaeus, vc_id=9016, 9020; the hemorrhaging woman who cannot see the source of power, vc_id=9013) or irrelevant (leper, dying child), yet whose inner spiritual perception is highly active. This inverse pattern — physical sense impaired or bypassed, spiritual apprehension operative — reveals that faith-activated healing-wholeness depends on and exercises an *inner* perceptive faculty (discernment of Jesus's identity and power) that is independent of, and in some cases prior to, restored physical perception. The characteristic therefore reveals that the deepest perceptive capacity is not sensory but relational-spiritual.

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic operates at the *threshold* of cognition: it requires enough cognitive content to be intentional (a belief-proposition about Jesus's power) but is not dependent on extended cognitive processing. The fact that faith precedes Jesus's healing word in most cases (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28) and is confirmed rather than created by Jesus's statement reveals that the characteristic's cognitive content is *anticipatory and minimal* — a prior grasp that generates expectation rather than a post-hoc interpretation. The bypassing of cognition in Luk 8:50 (faith commanded against the cognitive data of death) further reveals that the characteristic is *supra-cognitive*: it can hold its belief-content against contrary evidence. This distinguishes faith-activated healing from mere rational hope or calculated expectation.

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The partial and inferential engagement with memory (T3.3.1, T3.3.2) reveals that this characteristic is *dependent on received testimony* but not primarily a memorial act. The background presence of prior knowledge of Jesus as substrate suggests that faith-activated healing cannot arise ex nihilo — it requires that something has been heard, seen, or told about Jesus that memory holds available. Yet the characteristic's activating moment is not memorial recall but present-tense conviction and action. Memory thus functions as the *enabling precondition* rather than the operative faculty. This reveals that the characteristic stands at the intersection of received testimony (memorial substrate) and present-tense personal faith (operative faculty) — a structure consistent with the broader Pauline "faith comes from hearing" frame, even where that frame is not lexically present in M38-C.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern of affect engagement across M38-C reveals that this characteristic does not require affective calm as its precondition but can operate through and transform a wide range of affective states. Faith is exercised in conditions of desperation (Mar 5:23), fear (Luk 8:50), chronic suffering (Mar 5:28), marginalisation (Mar 10:46), and leprosy-induced exclusion (Luk 17:19). The characteristic is therefore *affectively robust*: it does not require the person to achieve emotional equilibrium before faith becomes possible. Rather, the distressed affective state becomes the occasion for faith's exercise, and peace/gratitude is the affective transformation that follows sōzō. This reveals that the characteristic bridges the most extreme negative affective states (mortal fear, chronic anguish) and the most positive (peace, wholeness), with faith as the interior faculty that makes the transit.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** S — The consistent non-engagement with the creative faculty across M38-C reveals that this characteristic does not require or produce imaginative origination as part of its operation. The inner-being faculty engaged is trust directed toward a known person (Jesus/God), not the generative production of new inner-being content. This silence reveals that faith-activated healing-wholeness is a *receptive and relational* characteristic rather than an originating or creative one — it receives and trusts rather than imagines and creates.

---

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The consistent and strong engagement of volition across all three VCGs reveals that faith-activated healing-wholeness is *constitutively volitional*: it cannot occur without the person's determined exercise of will. This is theologically significant: the characteristic does not represent a passive divine imposition but a genuine human willing-toward-God that is confirmed and honoured by divine response. The fact that Jesus attributes causation to the person's faith ("your faith has made you well") rather than to his own sovereign act reveals that the volitional structure of the characteristic is not incidental but definitional. Simultaneously, the command "only believe" (Luk 8:50) shows that volition is addressable and commandable — it is not self-generated absolutely but can be evoked by authoritative word. The pattern reveals a *commanded-yet-genuine* volition: real human choice operating in response to divine invitation.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern of consistent, strong, and divinely-affirmed agency engagement across M38-C reveals that this characteristic exemplifies a distinctive biblical anthropology: human inner-being agency is genuinely operative as a secondary cause within a divinely-effected event. Jesus's repeated attribution formula establishes that the person's inner-being act (faith exercised) is the named human mechanism through which divine saving power flows. This is not a diminution of divine sovereignty (sōzō is always ultimately God's act) but an elevation of human agency: the person is constitutionally capable of directing an inner-being faculty in such a way that it becomes the receiving and activating conduit of divine wholeness. The pattern reveals that the characteristic is *co-agential* — divine and human agency operating in structured complementarity, with faith as the hinge.

---

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The partial engagement with moral evaluation — present in VCG-03 but largely absent from VCG-01 and VCG-02 — reveals that moral evaluation is not the *activating* faculty of this characteristic but its *deepening* faculty. The individual synoptic healings do not require the faith-seeker to evaluate their moral standing before faith can be exercised — faith is directed at physical healing, and the spiritual register is implied or supplementary. In Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21, the characteristic's reach has expanded to explicitly include moral evaluation as part of the wholeness sought. The pattern thus reveals a developmental structure: the characteristic begins (in VCG-01/02) as faith-toward-physical-healing with spiritual overtone, and matures (in VCG-03) into a faith that explicitly addresses moral standing. Moral evaluation is the endpoint-deepening of the characteristic, not its starting point.

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern reveals that conscience is the *ultimate inner-being terminus* of this characteristic. In VCG-01 and VCG-02 the conscience is implicitly addressed through peace (Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) but not named. In VCG-03 (1Pe 3:21, Jam 5:15) it is explicitly named as the inner organ that sōzō clears and restores. This progressive revelation — from implied peace (soul at rest) to explicitly cleared conscience — reveals that healing-wholeness in M38-C is not complete at the physical level but has a necessary inner-being terminus in conscience-restoration. The pattern further reveals that faith-activated wholeness is not merely therapeutic (body restored) but forensic and relational (conscience cleared before God). The full characteristic is not present unless conscience is reached. The non-engagement of conscience in VCG-01 and VCG-02 does not contradict this; rather, the peace granted in those texts is the experiential form of the conscience-at-rest that VCG-03 names explicitly.

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pattern reveals that conscientiousness is an *integrating response-faculty* through which the fullness of sōzō is expressed and confirmed. Where conscientiousness is present (Luk 17:19, Jam 5:15), the healing-wholeness circuit is completed by a person who integrates moral awareness, will, and action in response to what has been received. Where it is absent (the nine lepers, context of Luk 17:19), healing is received but the wholeness-circuit is arguably incomplete — a point Jesus himself marks. This reveals that the characteristic, while activated by faith, finds its fullest human expression in conscientious response: the person who receives sōzō and conscientiously responds to it enters more completely into the inner-being state of wholeness that the characteristic names. Conscientiousness is thus the *completion faculty* of M38-C.

---

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The pervasive and consistent engagement of relational capacity across all three VCGs and all twelve verses reveals that this characteristic is *constitutively relational*: it cannot be abstracted from the person-to-person encounter between the faith-seeker and Jesus/God. Faith in M38-C is never generic trust in a principle or force; it is always specifically directed toward a named person (Jesus, "Son of David," God). The characteristic's outcomes (peace, discipleship, cleansed conscience, good standing before God) are all relational states — they describe a changed quality of connection between the person and the divine. The pattern thus reveals that the deepest structure of healing-wholeness through faith exercised is *relational repair and deepening*: physical healing is the visible sign of a relational reality (the person is now in right standing with the source of life), and the wholeness of sōzō is ultimately constituted by the quality of that relational bond. The characteristic's inner-being depth is therefore identical with the depth of relational capacity engaged.
