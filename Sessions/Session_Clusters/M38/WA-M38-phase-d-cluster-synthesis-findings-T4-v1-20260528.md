**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics present God-to-human operation as either the dominant or foundational vector, without exception. This unanimity is itself the first and most significant cluster-level finding: no characteristic within M38 Salvation registers silence or even ambiguity on this axis. The convergence, however, conceals meaningful structural differentiation. Characteristics 1, 6, and 7 present the divine-to-human movement as originating entirely within God's own character and covenantal commitment — grace purposed before time (M38-A), warrior-love self-impelled toward human rescue (M38-F), redemptive love grounded in oath rather than merit (M38-G). Characteristics 4 and 5 reveal a different structural emphasis: God's extension is mediated through an instituted machinery (the levitical priesthood, the blood-mechanism of Lev 17:11) that God himself designs and mandates — the divine-to-human movement is architecturally constructed rather than episodically responsive. Characteristics 2 and 3 occupy a middle register: God-in-Christ extends rescue responsively (to the drowning Peter's cry, to the woman's touch, to Bartimaeus's shout), revealing a disposition of proximate attentiveness that answers human crisis with immediate directedness. Two asymmetries sharpen across the seven: first, Characteristic 5 is the only one where God's extension is formally institutionalized with no episodic, crisis-responsive dimension in the corpus — the atonement machinery is calendrical and structural, not triggered by individual distress. Second, Characteristics 2 and 3 show the divine-to-human movement tied most tightly to the moment of human petition or faith-expression, while Characteristics 1, 6, and 7 root it in pretemporal or covenant-prior divine purpose that does not wait for the human turn. Shared verse anchors across multiple characteristics include Heb 5:7 (divine hearing and response, M38-B and M38-A), the gift-language of grace (Eph 2:8, M38-A; Lev 17:11's "I have given," M38-D and M38-E), and the sent-Son formula (Joh 3:17, M38-A; 1Jo 4:14, M38-A and M38-G). The cluster converges on a single structural judgment: in M38, God's disposition toward the human person across every register of salvation is initiative-taking, provision-bearing, and directed — but the form of that directedness varies from architectural institution (M38-E) to personal attentiveness (M38-B, M38-C) to unconditional covenantal love (M38-G).

**T4.2.1 — Divine Interface — Human to God — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics show human-to-God movement, but the cluster reveals a sharp divergence in the nature and necessity of that movement across the characteristics. In Characteristics 1, 2, and 3, the human-toward-God movement is the point of contact through which the divine saving act is appropriated: calling, faith-cry, and faith-touch are the operative instruments that connect the endangered person to the saving source. In Characteristic 4, the human movement is more complexly structured — the approach toward God is mediated, ritualized, and preceded by acknowledged guilt (the offerer bringing the prescribed animal, laying hands on the sacrifice); the movement is toward God through an institutional channel rather than direct. Characteristic 5 presents the human-toward-God movement at its most formally concentrated: the high priest's solitary entry into the Holy Place is the entire community's directed approach at its most distilled point — a single person carrying the many toward the Holy. This contrasts starkly with Characteristic 3's immediate, individual, faith-driven press through a crowd to touch the hem. A significant asymmetry surfaces: in Characteristics 1, 2, and 3, the movement can be anguished, terrified, or even desperate while still being efficacious (Peter's panic cry; the woman's silent touch); in Characteristics 4 and 5, the movement requires proper form — unconsecrated or defiant approach either fails or is excluded. Characteristic 6's human-toward-God movement occupies a distinctive register: it is characterized by sustained waiting and honest complaint (Isa 59:11, Job 13:16), a posture of acknowledged incompletion rather than present possession. Characteristic 7's human-to-God movement is grounded in memory — the ransomed soul moves back toward God through recalled deliverance (Psa 71:23, Deu 9:26) and lament from defeat (Psa 44:26), revealing that the direction is not always triumph but can be urgency from failure. The cluster-level finding is that human-toward-God movement in M38 is not a single posture but a spectrum: immediate crisis-cry (M38-B), quiet interior conviction (M38-C), ritualized institutional approach (M38-D, M38-E), sustained hope-anchored waiting (M38-F), and memory-grounded appeal (M38-G) — all of them receiving the divine response, but through structurally distinct forms of orientation.

**T4.3.1 — Human Interface — Giving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The human-giving direction is attested across all seven characteristics, but the cluster reveals a decisive split between characteristics where human giving is structurally central and those where it is peripheral or derivative. Characteristics 4 and 5 are uniquely built on human giving: priestly mediation is the entire mechanism by which the atoning benefit reaches its recipient — the priest's action, bearing, intercession, and sacrifice are the channel through which what God has provided is actually delivered to the person. The giving here is formal, authorized, and constitutive of the characteristic's operation. Characteristic 1 is the only other case where human giving is systematically sustained and elaborated: apostolic proclamation, missionary self-sacrifice, intercessory prayer, and deliberate teaching are all named as modes of person-to-person extension of salvation. Characteristics 2, 3, 6, and 7 present human giving as more episodic or derivative: Noah's rescue of his household (M38-B), Jairus's and the elders' intercessory faith (M38-C), Paul's affliction-borne witness (M38-F), and the ransomed master's generous treatment of slaves (M38-G) all show the pattern, but none of these characteristics is structurally organized around human giving the way M38-D and M38-E are. The cluster also reveals a shared deep logic: across Characteristics 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6, the giving person does not generate the saving benefit themselves — they mediate what originates with God. This makes the human giving in M38 functionally instrumental rather than generatively salvific. A structural contrast surfaces between M38-D and M38-C: in the former, the priest gives atonement coverage to specific persons by performing a prescribed ritual; in the latter, a parent (Jairus) or community (Jam 5:15) gives by exercising faith on behalf of another — the giving is relational-intercessory rather than institutional-formal. Characteristic 7 introduces the most distinctive form of human giving in the cluster: ransomed memory as ethical generator, where a person's prior experience of being rescued becomes the motivational basis for extending generous treatment to others (Deu 15:15, 24:18).

**T4.4.1 — Human Interface — Receiving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Reception from another person is attested across all seven characteristics, though the cluster reveals important differences in the thickness and structural weight of the receiving direction. In Characteristics 4 and 5, reception is the primary consequence the corpus is designed to describe: the OT atonement system exists precisely to deliver the outcome "he shall be forgiven" to the individual who has brought the offering — the entire apparatus terminates in a person receiving cleansed standing. The formulaic repetition of forgiveness declarations across Lev 4–6 (VCG-01 of M38-D) and the categorical comprehensive coverage language of Lev 16:33 (M38-E) make reception the dominant register of those two characteristics. In Characteristics 2 and 3, reception is immediate and embodied: Peter receives rescue at the instant of the faith-cry; the woman receives healing at the instant of the touch confirmed by Jesus's word — reception is co-incident with the moment of faith-expression. In Characteristic 1, reception operates through a structural sequence: hearing the proclaimed gospel → believing → being sealed with the Spirit (Eph 1:13), making reception a multi-stage event rather than a single moment. A significant divergence appears between M38-C and M38-D regarding the recipient's posture: in M38-C, the recipient is the active faith-exerciser (the woman presses through; Bartimaeus cries out); in M38-D, the recipient brings an animal and is otherwise passive before the priest's mediating action. The passivity of the recipient is most extreme in M38-E, where the comprehensive Day of Atonement covers the entire community without requiring individual active reception — reception is corporate and structural. Characteristics 6 and 7 add temporally distinctive reception modes: in M38-F, reception can be deferred and hoped-for across generations; in M38-G, reception of the ransomed community's generosity (Deu 15:15) is the fruit of another's prior rescue rather than direct atoning action. The cluster finding is that reception in M38 ranges from immediate-individual-embodied (M38-B, M38-C) to structured-corporate-formal (M38-E) to generationally transmitted (M38-F, M38-G).

**T4.5.1 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals a structured tension between covenantal particularity and boundary-crossing universality, but the tension resolves differently across the seven characteristics. Characteristics 1, 6, and 7 all share the same trajectory: covenantal origin generating universal scope. In each, the saving act originates within a particular people (Israel, Davidic covenant, Exodus community) and moves outward — Joh 4:42, Isa 49:6, Luk 3:6 for M38-A and M38-F; Deu 24:18's stranger-inclusion for M38-G. This is not boundary erasure but covenantal expansion. Characteristic 5 stands at the opposite extreme: the Levitical atonement machinery is the one characteristic in the cluster that operates exclusively within covenantal boundaries, with no verse in the corpus showing operation for non-Israelites. This is the only case in M38 where the answer to the boundary question is structurally contained. Characteristic 4 shows a trajectory from bounded toward boundary-crossing across its OT-to-NT arc: Exo 29:33's exclusion of outsiders from ordained status gives way to 1Jo 2:2's "propitiation for the whole world." Characteristics 2 and 3 occupy an intermediate position: both primarily operate within existing relational bonds (disciples with Jesus; the covenant community), but both show notable boundary-crossing instances — Act 27:20's mixed ship crew (M38-B) and the Samaritan leper of Luk 17:19 (M38-C), where the characteristic operates most fully precisely with the outsider. The shared anchor across the boundary-crossing characteristics is the language of "all" and "ends of the earth" (Isa 49:6, Act 13:47, Rom 10:13, 1Ti 2:4) — universal scope language that appears across M38-A, M38-F, and implicitly M38-G's sojourner-inclusion. The sharpest asymmetry in the cluster is between M38-E (exclusively within covenant, no expansion texts in corpus) and M38-A, M38-F (explicitly universal in scope, boundary-crossing is constitutive of the characteristic's full realization).

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across all seven characteristics, the evidence refuses a clean single-category answer and instead reveals a multi-modal basis that combines elements of unconditional initiative, covenantal structure, and responsive attentiveness — but the weighting of these elements varies significantly across characteristics. The most purely unconditional register is found in Characteristics 1, 6, and 7: grace "purposed before time" (2Ti 1:9), the divine warrior who "will not rest" until salvation blazes forth (Isa 62:1), and love grounded in oath rather than merit (Deu 7:8). These three characteristics share a common anchor: God's basis is his own character and covenantal commitment, not a response to human qualification. Characteristic 5 presents the most strictly covenantal-mechanical basis: the atonement machinery operates within a given system where specific offerings activate specific atonement effects — God does not extend atonement apart from the prescribed mechanism, making the basis formally conditional on ritual compliance even while the system itself is divinely provided. This creates a structural paradox unique to M38-E: the mechanism is unconditionally given but conditionally activated. Characteristic 4 shows a complex layering — covenantal-systematic (Lev 4–6's guaranteed outcomes), compassion-responsive (Psa 78:38's "being compassionate he atoned"), name/glory-driven (Psa 79:9), and mercy-declarative (Heb 8:12's hileōs) — the most multi-modal basis in the cluster. Characteristic 2 is the most purely responsive: rescue follows the cry, heard "because of reverence" (Heb 5:7) — quality of petition shapes the basis. Characteristic 3 introduces a distinctive sub-finding: in Luk 8:50, Jesus commands faith before healing, suggesting God's extension can precede and summon the human response rather than reward it — a covenantal-initiative dimension that disrupts purely responsive framing. The cluster finding is that no characteristic in M38 is purely unconditional or purely conditional: all seven occupy a spectrum anchored in divine character at one pole (M38-A, M38-F, M38-G) and in structured covenantal-ritual compliance at the other (M38-E), with responsive and compassion-driven elements distributed throughout.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals a spectrum of inner postures across the seven characteristics, with faith as the one common element and significant differentiation in the form, texture, and intensity that faith takes. Characteristics 1, 2, and 3 share faith as the primary named posture, but with different affective textures: Characteristic 1's faith includes heart-trust, volitional calling, and confessional alignment; Characteristic 2's faith can be panic-driven and terrified (Mat 8:25's disciples) while still efficacious, yet also reverentially surrendered (Heb 5:7's eulabeia) — the range within this characteristic alone is remarkable; Characteristic 3's faith is quiet interior conviction (the woman of Mat 9:21), publicly persistent and socially costly (Bartimaeus), and returned-in-gratitude (the Samaritan leper). This differentiation within faith across Characteristics 2 and 3 establishes that the required posture is not a uniform emotional tone but a consistent orientation of directedness toward the divine agent as sufficient. Characteristic 4 introduces the most explicitly named posture cluster in the corpus: acknowledged sin-awareness (Lev 5:6), contrition and self-abasement (Luk 18:13's eyes-downcast, breast-beating tax collector), dependence on divine provision, and the impossibility of self-coverage (Lev 1:4) — a posture sequence that moves from recognition through humiliation to dependent approach. Characteristic 5 adds the mediator's own required posture: self-atonement must precede intercession for others, establishing a structural requirement that the inner posture of the one who moves toward God on behalf of others must itself be resolved first. Characteristic 6's required posture is forward-leaning expectancy combined with honest incompletion — Job 13:16's willingness to approach despite danger, Isa 33:2's distress-driven supplication without self-sufficiency. Characteristic 7's posture is memory-grounded appeal — anchored in prior experience of rescue rather than present triumph. Two structural asymmetries emerge: first, only Characteristics 4 and 5 require formal self-reckoning and guilt-acknowledgment as explicit prerequisites; the others allow terrified, desperate, or incomplete faith to qualify. Second, only Characteristic 5 requires that the mediator's own inner posture be covered before the movement toward God is valid — a precondition that does not appear in any other characteristic.

**T4.3.2 — Human Interface — Giving — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals both convergences and genuine divergences in the inner conditions enabling human giving of salvation across the seven characteristics. The most widely shared enabling condition is some form of missional or sacrificial love: M38-A's herald is enabled by love for others' rescue (1Cor 9:22); M38-B's Noah is enabled by obedient reverence that builds for others; M38-E's priest is enabled by solidarity with those for whom he mediates. Across Characteristics 1, 4, 5, and 6, a second shared condition emerges: the giver must be giving from a covered, received, or tested position rather than from self-sufficiency. M38-D and M38-E make this explicit through the self-atonement-before-mediation rule (Exo 29:33, Lev 9:7, 16:6); M38-A implies it through the logic of Paul's already-received grace motivating his giving; M38-F shows it through the principle that Paul's affliction-tested hope is what gives salvific benefit to others (2Cor 1:6). This cluster-wide principle — receive before give — is stated most formally in M38-D and M38-E but distributed structurally across most characteristics. The sharpest divergence is between Characteristics 4 and 5, which require authorized consecration as an enabling condition (not just personal faith but institutionally validated standing), and Characteristics 3, 6, and 7, which require no institutional authorization — a parent's faith, a prophet's longing, or a ransomed person's memory are sufficient enabling conditions for giving in those registers. Characteristic 7 introduces the most distinctive enabling condition: formative ransomed memory (Deu 15:15, 24:18) — the giver is enabled by an internalized narrative of personal vulnerability and divine rescue, not by any present capacity or commissioning. No other characteristic in the cluster names experiential memory of having been rescued as the primary enabling condition for giving. The cluster-level finding is thus: personal covered/received status is the common precondition across most characteristics, but the form it takes ranges from institutional consecration (M38-D, M38-E) to experienced, internalized rescue (M38-G).

**T4.4.2 — Human Interface — Receiving — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals that enabling and blocking conditions for person-to-person reception are unevenly evidenced: Characteristics 1, 2, 4, and 7 are the most explicit, while Characteristics 3 and 5 are largely silent or restricted to inferences, and Characteristic 6 is a noted gap. The most thoroughly articulated blocking condition across the cluster is the conjunction of faithlessness and entitlement: Luk 23:39 (M38-B) presents the criminal who demands rescue without any genuine directedness toward the rescuer — wanting the outcome without the relationship or posture it requires. This is the clearest single case of blocked reception in the cluster. Act 8:20 (M38-A) presents a parallel blocking condition: treating the graciously given as a purchasable commodity — a category confusion about the nature of what is being received. Both blockers share a common structure: misapprehension of what is being offered and what posture is required to receive it. The enabling conditions show more convergence: receptive hearing (M38-A), faith-cry directed toward the specific rescuing presence (M38-B), acknowledged guilt (M38-D), sincere heart-orientation (M38-D's 2Ch 30:18), willingness to submit to the purification process (M38-E), and activated formative memory of vulnerability (M38-G) all function as enabling inner orientations. Fear is explicitly named as a blocker in M38-C (Luk 8:50: "do not fear, only believe") — the only characteristic where an affective state rather than a volitional orientation is named as the blocking condition. Characteristic 6's gap is significant: it is the one characteristic that documents successful person-to-person reception (2Cor 1:6, Phili 1:19) but says nothing about what inner conditions in the receiver enable or block it. This gap may reflect the characteristic's primary interest in the giving side of suffering-solidarity rather than the receiving side. The cluster finding is that the common enabling condition is some form of genuine other-directed receptivity and the common blocking condition is some form of self-protective closure — whether entitled demand (M38-B), truth-refusal (M38-A), or abstracted memory-loss (M38-G).

**T4.5.2 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals a clean partition on this question, which is unusual in its sharpness. Characteristic 5 (M38-E) stands alone as the only characteristic that operates exclusively within covenantal boundaries across its entire evidenced corpus — not a single verse shows the priestly atonement machinery extended to non-Israelites, and no expansion or universalizing text appears. All other characteristics cross covenantal boundaries or show clear trajectories toward crossing them. This asymmetry is structurally intelligible: M38-E is the characteristic most bound to a particular institutional form (the Levitical priesthood), and the Mosaic covenant structure that generates it is itself covenant-bounded by definition. The boundary-crossing trajectory across Characteristics 1, 3, 4, 6, and 7 moves along two distinct paths: explicit universalization (Isa 49:6, Act 13:47, 1Jo 2:2, Luk 3:6) and boundary-crossing in specific narrative instances (Luk 17:19's Samaritan leper, Act 27:20's mixed ship crew). Characteristic 2 is the most ambiguous: primarily covenantal in its evidence but with Act 27:20 suggesting the temporal-physical dimension of rescue can extend beyond covenant. The shared anchor across boundary-crossing characteristics is covenantal origin as the launching point for universal extension — no characteristic claims that the covenantal foundation is abandoned, only that what began there moves outward. The NT propitiation cluster (M38-D VCG-05, 1Jo 2:2) is the most explicit theological moment of boundary dissolution: "propitiation for the whole world" reframes what was structurally bounded in VCG-01's Lev 4–6 as now universally available. This OT-to-NT trajectory is shared across Characteristics 4 and 5, though M38-E's corpus ends before demonstrating the expansion that M38-D's NT material achieves.

**T4.6.2 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals a stark concentration of adversarial spiritual activity: of the seven characteristics, only Characteristic 1 (M38-A, eschatological salvation received by faith) is explicitly evidenced as a site of adversarial attack. All other characteristics register silence on adversarial activity — Characteristics 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 provide explicit silence notations, and Characteristic 7 is consistently silent across its entire corpus. M38-A is the characteristic most directly concerned with the interior act of faith as the point of reception — and it is precisely this interiority that Luk 8:12 identifies as the devil's target: the word is removed from hearts to prevent belief. The cluster finding is that adversarial vulnerability in M38 is concentrated precisely where the individual human will and interior faith-act are the operative reception mechanism — making the God-to-human characteristic that is most completely "received by faith" also the one most explicitly subject to adversarial attack on that faith. This is the sharpest interface between Characteristic 1 and all other characteristics: the uniqueness of M38-A's evidenced adversarial exposure is a function of its unique structural reliance on the individual interior faith-act.

**T4.1.3 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster generates a composite portrait of divine disposition across M38, with each characteristic contributing a distinct facet and several characteristics sharing anchor-language. The most consistent element across all seven is what might be called active, initiative-taking mercy: Psa 78:38's "being compassionate, he atoned" (M38-D), God's warrior self-arming with "helmet of salvation" (M38-F), the Son sent "not to condemn but to save" (M38-A), and God personally going out to redeem his people (M38-G, 2Sa 7:23) all portray a God who moves toward the human person with saving intent that does not wait for the person to first qualify. The dispositions named vary by characteristic in revealing ways: M38-A discloses overflowing generosity — the gift "abounds far beyond" what the trespass produced (Rom 5:15-17); M38-B discloses proximate attentiveness — willingness to close relational distance in crisis (Psa 69:18's "draw near"); M38-C discloses whole-person concern — Jesus sends the healed away "in peace" addressing not just body but inner state; M38-D discloses compassion toward persistent failure — Psa 78:38 explicitly names the disposition as triggered by the people's repeated sin, not their virtue; M38-E discloses holiness held in creative tension with provision — God is both the one who requires atonement and the one who provides every element of the mechanism, revealing that holiness does not produce abandonment but structured accessibility; M38-F discloses permanence and urgency together — salvation "to all generations" and a God who "will not rest"; M38-G discloses proprietary love — redemption creates belonging, not merely release. Two structural asymmetries emerge: M38-E is the only characteristic where the disposition is revealed primarily through architecture (the perpetual statute, the scaled provisions for the poor) rather than through named emotional quality — God's care is evident in design rather than in affective attribution. M38-D uniquely names compassion as the trigger for atoning action when destruction would be deserved — the disposition disclosed is not merely benevolence but specifically mercy-under-provocation.

**T4.2.3 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster produces a multi-faceted portrait of the human-God relationship as revealed through the direction of human movement in each characteristic, and the portrait contains both converging and diverging elements. The shared convergence across Characteristics 1, 2, 3, and 6 is the asymmetry of complete dependence: the disciples cannot save themselves from the storm; the woman cannot heal herself; the human person cannot work their own eschatological rescue; the soul waits exclusively on the LORD as its only source (Gen 49:18). This asymmetry is not experienced as alienation but as the basis for legitimate and expected appeal — the relationship is one of recognized creaturely need before a willing and able God. The most explicitly shame-marked disclosure comes in Characteristic 4: the tax collector's posture of "standing far off" and not lifting eyes reveals a relationship in which moral asymmetry is acutely felt — the human person experiences disqualification rather than natural access, making the movement toward God an act of dependent appeal rather than confident approach. This contrasts with Characteristic 3, where the woman's interior faith-speech ("if I touch his garment") and Bartimaeus's persistent crying out reveal a relationship structured by confident expectation of Jesus's response, even from a position of social marginalization. Characteristic 5 contributes a unique structural finding: the relationship revealed through the mediatorial approach is one of ordered, not direct, access — the community relates to God through a required intermediary, revealing that the relationship is formally structured rather than relational-immediate. Characteristic 6 contributes the finding that the relationship is resilient enough to hold lament and unresolved longing (Isa 59:11's complaint that salvation is "far off") without rupture — the person can voice unfulfilled expectation without abandoning the relationship. Characteristic 7 reveals that the relationship is defined by a formative event — the ransom creates the terms of belonging, and every subsequent human movement toward God is anchored in that founding act rather than in present achievement. The cluster finding is that the human-to-God direction in M38 reveals a relationship of asymmetric dependence that is nonetheless personally intimate, lamentable, bold, and anchored in a prior divine act.

**T4.3.3 — Human Interface — Giving — What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — This question generates the sharpest cluster-level finding: across every characteristic where the evidence speaks to this question, the same structural principle emerges — the giver must first have been a receiver. Characteristics 4 and 5 state this most formally: the priest must receive his own atonement before he can mediate atonement for others (Exo 29:33, Lev 9:7, Lev 16:6); the Levites require Aaronic atonement before taking up their atoning service (Num 8:12). The logic is non-negotiable in these characteristics: the uncovered cannot give coverage. Characteristic 1 states the same principle less formally but structurally: Paul's self-denial for others' rescue is intelligible only as a response to grace already received; the teacher who saves hearers must have received truth before transmitting it. Characteristic 2's Heb 11:7 shows that Noah gives rescue to his household after receiving divine warning — the giving capacity flows from prior reception of divine communication that formed him into a man of reverent faith. Characteristic 6 adds a distinctive variant: the prophets of 1Pe 1:10 gave salvific hope to future generations without themselves having received the final realization — suggesting that genuine engagement with and longing for the salvation, even without possession, can enable transmission. This is the only case in the cluster where the principle is modulated: partial reception (earnest longing, not final arrival) enables giving. Characteristic 7 articulates the most concrete version: the ransomed master can extend generous treatment to others precisely because they have internalized the memory of their own slavery and rescue (Deu 15:15) — the giving flows from a narrative identity formed by the reception event. Characteristic 3 is a gap, with Jam 5:15 implying but not specifying what the intercessor must have received. The cluster-level finding is that the receive-before-give principle is the most consistently distributed structural finding across M38 — making it a cluster-defining axiom: no genuine giving of salvation in any register is possible except from a person who has themselves encountered the saving reality they extend.

**T4.4.3 — Human Interface — Receiving — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — This question produces a notable distribution of evidence versus silence: Characteristics 1, 2, and 4 provide the most explicit descriptions; Characteristic 7 provides structural inference; Characteristics 3, 5, and 6 are silent or gap. The descriptions that do appear cluster around a small number of inner-being states. The most developed negative portrait is in Characteristic 4's VCG-06: permanent guilt-weight without resolution (1Sa 3:14), judgment exposure (Isa 22:14), and the experience of having no pathway through the guilt — an inner state of unrelieved burden and foreclosed relief. This is the most fully described non-reception state in the cluster, and its severity is directly proportional to the stakes of the characteristic: conscience-atonement, when not received, leaves the inner person carrying what they were designed not to carry. Characteristic 1 contributes the portrait of truth-refusal: the inner state of the non-receiver is characterized by a closed will — truth's love refused (2Th 2:10), understanding closed (Act 7:25), the word removed before faith can form (Luk 8:12). The internal state is not neutral but actively resistant or precluded. Characteristic 2 provides two distinct non-reception states: the entitled-demand state (Luk 23:39 — wanting rescue without genuine directedness toward the rescuer) and the despair-collapse state (Act 27:20 — hope abandoned, not refused). These are structurally opposite: one is hostile closure, the other is exhausted surrender. The cluster's most striking finding at this level is the contrast between M38-D's non-reception state (guilt-weight permanently borne, judgment exposed) and M38-B's despair-collapse state — in the former, the burden remains internal and moral; in the latter, the burden is existential and temporal. The silence of Characteristics 3, 5, and 6 on this question may reflect those characteristics' structural orientation toward successful reception rather than non-reception cases, but it leaves the cluster's portrait of non-reception incomplete on the healing and hope-mediated registers.

**T4.5.3 — Human Interface — Boundaries — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster presents a composite scope structure that combines an expansive default inclusion with a set of specific exclusion markers concentrated in particular characteristics. The most expansive inclusion language across M38 comes from Characteristics 1, 5, and 6: "everyone who calls" (Rom 10:13), "all flesh shall see God's salvation" (Luk 3:6), and the priestly system's internally comprehensive coverage across gender, economic status, and tribal identity (Lev 16:33, Lev 12:8, 14:21). The inclusivity within M38-E is notable for its internal thoroughness: poor and rich receive the same atonement through scaled offerings, both male and female discharge are addressed with identical mechanisms, and the Levites as a tribe are included within the atoning coverage they administer for others. Exclusion markers are concentrated in Characteristics 1 and 4: the non-receiver who refuses truth (2Th 2:10), the person under God's judicial refusal of atonement due to persistent defiant sin (1Sa 3:14, Isa 22:14), the person whose guilt involves blood-guilt beyond standard atonement provision (Num 35:33), and the proud and self-reliant (Isa 47:11). These exclusions share a common structure: they are responsive to human posture rather than to prior identity — the excluded are not excluded by category but by volitional orientation. The one structural identity-based exclusion in the OT corpus is Exo 29:33's outsider-exclusion from the ordained status, but this is progressively overridden in the NT (1Jo 2:2). Characteristic 7's scope is layered: "the ransomed of the LORD" is a specific identity designation (Isa 35:10), but it generates ethical obligation toward the stranger and vulnerable (Deu 24:18), extending the characteristic's practical reach beyond the named community. The cluster-level finding is that M38's scope of inclusion is structurally generous, bounded primarily by receptive posture rather than identity, and carries a clear trajectory from covenant-particular toward universal — with the single notable exception of M38-E's corpus, which remains institutionally bounded throughout.

**T4.6.3 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry in the evidence?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The cluster reveals a minimal but structurally significant angelic presence concentrated in three characteristics, with four characteristics entirely silent. The silence of M38-E (the priestly machinery characteristic) is particularly significant in light of M38-D's single angelic instance: the elaborated institutional machinery of atonement contains no angelic dimension, while the visionary-prophetic atonement of Isa 6:7 is angelically mediated. The cluster-level finding is that angelic ministry in M38 operates primarily at announcement (M38-A), sustained ministry-orientation (M38-F), and a single extraordinary mediation (M38-D), but is absent from every characteristic's operational core.

The cluster-level finding is that the giving direction in M38 is fully attested but exhibits the widest internal diversity of any interface: from formal institutional mediation to proclamatory extension to memory-motivated ethical transformation.

**T4.4.4 — Human Interface — Receiving — If the evidence is silent on the receiving direction, note this explicitly.**

Person-to-person reception is derivative, secondary, and mediated — the priest mediates what God provides, the apostle announces what God accomplished, the parent's faith opens what Jesus does. The receiving direction is fully present but consistently structured as mediated derivation from the primary divine-to-human reception rather than as an independent alternative source.
