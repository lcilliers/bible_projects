**T2.1.1 — Spirit-Level Location — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, partially. Luk 1:47 (M38-F-VCG-06) provides the clearest explicit spirit-level location: "My spirit exults in God my Savior" — the inner faculty named is the spirit (*pneuma*), and the act performed is joyful exultation directed toward God as Savior. This is the one verse in the corpus where the spirit is directly and explicitly named as the seat of salvation-related response. No other verse in the 37-verse corpus uses the term "spirit" as the explicit anthropological locus of this characteristic, though Tit 3:6 refers to the Spirit poured out through the Savior — this is divine Spirit rather than the human spirit and does not locate the characteristic at the human spirit level.

**T2.2.1 — Soul-Level Location — Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-6]** E — Yes, with significant convergence. Multiple verses identify the characteristic as involving soul-level faculties without always using the explicit term "soul." The defining posture of the sub-group — waiting, longing, and hope-sustained-toward-salvation — is consistently inner-being language. Gen 49:18 names an "I" that waits for God's salvation (the personal inner subject). 1Pe 1:5 names faith as the active faculty of perseverance — a soul-level act. Rom 8:24 situates salvation as already possessed in hope by the inner person. 1Th 5:8 images hope as a helmet protecting the inner faculties. Heb 9:28 names those who "eagerly wait" — an inner posture of the whole person. Isa 59:11 names the community's inner mourning and groaning. The characteristic is predominantly a soul-level phenomenon: the longing, hoping, waiting, and trusting that constitute it are all inner-being activities, not merely behavioral ones.

(1) Divine bestowal is the dominant pattern: 1Pe 1:5 states believers are "guarded by God's power through faith" — the preservation that sustains hope toward salvation is divine in origin. Heb 1:14 figures angels ministering to those who will inherit salvation — the entire framework of anticipation is divinely ordered and sustained. Tit 3:4–6 presents the Savior's appearing as the revelation of divine goodness and the Spirit as poured out richly — both are gifts from outside. (2) However, the inner faculty of hope itself appears as constitutionally resident in the person and activated by faith (1Pe 1:5; 1Th 5:8; Rom 8:24) — faith is an inner act, not merely a passive reception. (3) The prophetic tradition of anticipated salvation (1Pe 1:10) suggests it is also transmitted through revelatory tradition — a kind of generational or covenantal passing-on. The origin is therefore primarily divine bestowal, mediated through revelation, and requiring an inner constitutive response of faith.

**T3.4.2 — Affect — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?**
