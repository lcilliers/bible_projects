In VCG-05 (Act 27:20), the collapse into despair effectively impairs perception of any positive possibility — the perceptive capacity for hope is closed off. The characteristic thus variously: intensifies sensory perception under threat (VCG-01), deepens spiritual discernment through faith (VCG-04), and impairs open-horizoned perception in despair (VCG-05).

---

**T3.2.2 — Cognition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic primarily bypasses deliberate cognition in its panic-cry mode (VCG-01), and deepens it in its deliberative mode (VCG-03). In Mat 8:25 and Mat 14:30, the terror-and-cry is instantaneous — there is no deliberation; the crisis bypasses the cognitive process. In contrast, Joh 12:27's explicit "What shall I say?" demonstrates cognition deepened and held steady under extreme anguish — Jesus evaluates options and reaches a settled purposive conclusion. Act 27:20 (VCG-05) shows cognition producing despair: the accurate assessment that all means of rescue have failed is itself a cognitive act, but one that terminates hope. Cognition is therefore variously bypassed (panic), deepened (deliberative surrender), and driven to terminal conclusion (despair).

---

**T3.3.2 — Memory — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The evidence does not indicate that this characteristic has a determinate effect on the memory faculty in either direction. No verse in the sub-group activates, suppresses, or deepens memory as part of the fear-faith dynamic or the despair-and-rescue sequence. The characteristic operates in the present moment of mortal threat without evidence of memorial engagement. This finding is silent.

---

**T3.4.2 — Affect — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic powerfully enables and intensifies affect — it is the primary affective-activation event in the sub-group. Terror, panic, distress, anguish, and despair are all deeply felt affective states engaged by mortal threat (VCG-01, VCG-03, VCG-05). In VCG-03 (Heb 5:7), the affect reaches its maximum intensity: Jesus weeps and cries aloud in anguish — affect is not merely present but is so intense it cannot be contained within the inner being. In VCG-04 (Heb 11:7), the dominant affective quality is reverent awe (eulabeia-adjacent faith), which is a different but still pronounced affective engagement. No verse suggests that this characteristic impairs or suppresses affect; rather, it is constitutively affect-intensifying, producing the sharpest possible emotional experience in the inner being.

---

**T3.5.2 — Creativity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — In the panic-cry mode (VCG-01) and despair mode (VCG-05), this characteristic bypasses creativity entirely — the inner being under acute mortal threat or in terminal despair has no access to creative origination; its only available act is the cry or the silence of exhausted hope. In VCG-04 (Heb 11:7), the characteristic enables a kind of creative-productive action: Noah's reverent faith generates the imaginative and practical capacity to build the ark for an unseen catastrophe. This is creativity in the sense of constructing something not-yet-existing in response to a received vision. The evidence thus shows creativity bypassed (panic and despair modes) and enabled (faith-obedience mode), with a clear contextual dependency.

---

**T3.6.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition, including its three aspects (capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and constraints)?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic produces a spectrum of volitional outcomes across the sub-group. Regarding capacity: in VCG-01, volition is not impaired but is radically reduced to a single available act — the cry; all other volitional options are eliminated by the mortal threat. In VCG-03, volitional capacity is at its maximum: Jesus deliberates, considers options, and makes a willed surrender that is the most complete act of volition in the sub-group. Regarding interaction: in VCG-01, terror interacts with volition to narrow it to the cry; in VCG-03, anguish interacts with volition but does not impair it — the will remains sovereign over the anguish. Regarding constraints: in VCG-05 (Act 27:20), volition operates under a constraint of complete exhaustion — all volitional acts have been performed and have failed, leaving despair as the residue when volition can no longer find an available act. The characteristic thus enables the cry-act under terror, preserves and deepens volitional deliberation in surrender-mode, and exhausts volition in despair-mode.

---

**T3.7.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic produces contrasting agency-effects across VCGs. In VCG-01 (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30), mortal threat eliminates most forms of agency while enabling the single act of the cry — agency is compressed to its minimal form (vocal appeal) but not completely eliminated. In VCG-04 (Heb 11:7), reverent faith deepens and extends agency: Noah's faith generates sustained, purposive, productive agency over an extended period, resulting in the largest physical act in the sub-group (building an ark). In VCG-05 (Act 27:20), the characteristic — despair — represents the collapse of agency: all acts have been tried and hope abandoned, leaving the inner being with no available agency. In VCG-03 (Joh 12:27; Heb 5:7), the key agency is the willed act of surrender, which is an exercise of the deepest form of agency (the choice not to act for one's own preservation). The characteristic thus shows: agency compressed but preserved (VCG-01), agency deepened and sustained (VCG-04), agency collapsed (VCG-05), and agency as willed inaction (VCG-03).

---

**T3.8.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic bypasses moral evaluation in its panic mode (VCG-01, VCG-05) and deepens it in its deliberative and obedience modes (VCG-03, VCG-04). In the panic-cry of Mat 8:25 and Mat 14:30, there is no moral deliberation — the inner being moves immediately from terror to cry, with no evaluative process in between. In Act 27:20, the despair represents a state in which moral evaluation is irrelevant to the experience. In contrast, in Joh 12:27 and Heb 5:7 (VCG-03), Jesus's deliberation "for this purpose I came" involves moral-purposive evaluation of the highest order — the characteristic deepens moral evaluation to the point of self-giving surrender. In Heb 11:7 (VCG-04), Noah's faith-obedience is described as issuing in a moral verdict on the world ("by this he condemned the world"), indicating that the faith-action characteristic enables moral evaluation that becomes a judgment on others.

**T3.9.2 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The evidence does not indicate that this characteristic has a determinate effect on the conscience faculty in either direction. The sub-group's characteristic states — terror, anguished petition, despair, mockery-irony, reverent faith — do not engage the conscience's acute witness of sin and guilt as an operative phenomenon. Luk 23:39 (VCG-02) provides a marginal contrast between unrepentant demand and penitent trust, but the M38-B evidence for this verse is anchored in the physical-rescue demand rather than conscience-engagement. The evidence is silent on conscience being enabled, deepened, bypassed, or impaired.

---

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic bypasses conscientiousness in its crisis modes (VCG-01: panic-cry; VCG-05: despair) and enables it in its faith-action mode (VCG-04). In the panic-cry, there is no integration of moral awareness, volition, and sustained action — the crisis demands immediate response, not integrated conscientiousness. In despair (Act 27:20), conscientiousness is exhausted: all integrated effort has been made and has failed. In VCG-04 (Heb 11:7), Noah's response represents the fullest conscientiousness — moral awareness (reverence before divine warning), volition (choosing to act), and sustained action (building) integrated over time. The characteristic thus bypasses conscientiousness under extreme crisis conditions and enables it under conditions of received divine direction and faith-obedience.

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic primarily enables and deepens relational capacity, particularly in the vertical (human-divine) register. In VCG-01 (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30; Psa 69:18), mortal crisis activates the cry to a divine rescuer, enabling the most urgent and direct form of relational appeal — the relationship between creature and Creator is rendered explicit and operational by the mortal threat. In VCG-03 (Heb 5:7), the Father-Son relational intimacy reaches its most intense expression: Jesus's anguished prayer is heard by the Father because of eulabeia, and the relationship holds under conditions of the Son's extreme suffering. In VCG-04 (Heb 11:7), relational capacity extends to the household — Noah's faith-obedience delivers those entrusted to him, demonstrating that the human-divine relational response has horizontal consequences for those in his care. In VCG-05 (Act 27:20), relational capacity toward the divine is absent — no cry is made — suggesting that despair impairs the activation of this relational cry, even if it does not eliminate the capacity structurally.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic is triggered by perception of mortal reality but is not itself primarily perceptive in nature. Perception is the precipitating input (Peter sees the wind; Noah receives divine warning) rather than the characteristic's core content. The characteristic itself is what happens after perception — terror, cry, reverent faith, despair. The contrast between VCG-01's threat-perception and VCG-04's spiritual-discernment-perception reveals that the characteristic can be activated by two different perceptive modes: ordinary sensory threat-recognition and extraordinary spiritual reception of divine warning. This suggests that the characteristic's nature is fundamentally responsive — it is activated by perception but constituted by the inner-being response to what is perceived. The characteristic is perception-responsive rather than perception-constituted.

---

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The pattern reveals that cognition is engaged by this characteristic differentially according to the intensity and type of the crisis. In acute mortal panic (VCG-01), cognition is bypassed — the characteristic operates below the threshold of deliberation. In anguished purposive surrender (VCG-03), cognition is engaged at its deepest and most integrated level. This differential engagement reveals that the characteristic does not have a fixed relationship with cognition; rather, the intensity and nature of the mortal threat, and the quality of the inner being's resources, determine whether cognition is engaged or bypassed. This suggests that the characteristic is pre-cognitive in its panic mode and trans-cognitive (involving cognition but moving beyond it) in its surrender mode. It is not fundamentally a cognitive phenomenon but can incorporate cognition at depth.

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The consistent silence on memory across the entire sub-group reveals that this characteristic is constitutively present-tense and situationally immediate. It does not require memorial retrieval to be operative — the mortal threat in the present moment is sufficient to generate the full terror-faith-cry or despair dynamic. This stands in contrast to characteristics (such as gratitude or lament) that require remembering past events or past experiences of God's faithfulness. M38-B's silence on memory reveals its nature as an acute, present-moment phenomenon rather than a temporally extended or memorial one.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The consistent and intense engagement of affect across every VCG in the sub-group — terror (VCG-01), anguish and weeping (VCG-03), despair (VCG-05), reverent awe (VCG-04) — reveals that affect is not merely an accompanying phenomenon but is constitutive of this characteristic. The fear-faith dynamic is not faith exercised despite the absence of fear, nor fear experienced in the absence of faith: it is the co-presence and interaction of both as affect-states that defines the characteristic. The absence of affect in VCG-02 (mockery verses) — where the irony is a literary and theological phenomenon rather than an affective experience in the subject — points by contrast to the centrality of affect in the genuine expression of this characteristic. Affect is not what this characteristic produces: affect is what this characteristic is.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The consistent bypassing of creativity in the crisis modes (VCG-01, VCG-05) and its marginal enabling in VCG-04 reveals that this characteristic is not constitutively creative. The mortal threat conditions under which the characteristic primarily operates eliminate the creative faculty from the inner being's available repertoire — survival demand is not a creative state. The fact that VCG-04 (Noah's ark-building) is the only instance where creativity is engaged, and that this instance is distinctive precisely for its extended time-horizon and faith-obedience quality, suggests that creativity is accessible to this characteristic only when it has been transformed from reactive panic-cry into proactive faith-obedience. This reveals a developmental or contextual range within the characteristic: at its crisis-reactive extreme it bypasses creativity, at its faith-obedience extreme it enables it.

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence shows volition present across the entire sub-group but in radically different modes — crisis-cry, willed surrender, and exhausted collapse. This reveals that the characteristic does not eliminate volition but reconfigures it under pressure. The most significant finding is the contrast between VCG-01 (volition compressed to the single cry-act) and VCG-03 (volition at its most fully deliberate and sovereign). This contrast reveals that the quality of the inner being's resources — faith, depth, relationship with God — determines whether mortal threat compresses volition to a panic-cry or deepens it to a willed, purposive surrender. The characteristic is therefore volitionally formative: it reveals and shapes what the person's volition is capable of at its limits. The complete exhaustion of volition in despair (VCG-05) represents the end-state when mortal threat finds no relational or faith-resource to sustain it.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic is constituted by the relationship between agency and its limits. In all VCGs, the characteristic emerges precisely at the point where normal human agency is inadequate to the mortal threat — the disciples cannot save themselves from the storm, Noah cannot prevent the flood, the crucified Jesus cannot exercise independent self-rescue, the sailors cannot stop the storm. What the characteristic then reveals is what happens to the inner being when agency is at or beyond its limit: it either cries out (VCG-01), builds in anticipatory obedience (VCG-04), surrenders in purposive trust (VCG-03), or collapses in despair (VCG-05). The agency-pattern thus reveals that M38-B is a characteristic of agency-at-the-limit: its defining phenomenology emerges when and because ordinary agency is overwhelmed. This is why the cry is the characteristic act — it is the only agency left.

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The differential pattern — moral evaluation bypassed in panic-cry mode, deepened in surrender-and-obedience mode, absent in despair mode — reveals that this characteristic is not primarily a moral phenomenon but that it intersects with morality at depth when the inner being has resources beyond immediate terror. The VCG-02 mockery verses are the most morally complex in the sub-group: the taunts deploy a moral logic (power should rescue) that the narrative deconstructs, revealing that the deepest moral evaluation is precisely the one the mockers fail to make. The fact that this moral complexity is visible only in retrospect (through irony) confirms that the characteristic in its primary crisis-form does not operate through moral evaluation — it operates through the more fundamental faculty of creaturely faith-cry. Moral evaluation is a secondary and contextually dependent engagement.

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** S — The consistent silence of conscience across the sub-group reveals that this characteristic is phenomenologically distinct from guilt, conviction, or moral-self-evaluation. The fear-faith dynamic is terror in the face of physical mortal threat, not the conscience's distress in the face of moral transgression. This silence is definitionally significant: it confirms the sub-group's boundary with other M38 characteristics that might involve guilt-driven cry (such as penitential prayer). M38-B is characterized by the absence of conscience as an operative faculty — the inner being is endangered, not convicted; the cry is for rescue, not for forgiveness. The silence on conscience is thus not a gap but a definitional marker.

---

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The pattern — conscientiousness bypassed in crisis-modes, enabled only in VCG-04's faith-obedience mode — reveals that this characteristic in its primary form is sub-conscientious rather than conscientious. The panic-cry and despair states represent the inner being operating below the level of integrated moral-volitional-action response. This is not a deficiency but a structural feature: the mortal threat reduces the inner being to its most fundamental creaturely response, bypassing the integrated-character operation that conscientiousness requires. Only where the threat is known in advance (Noah) and the inner being can respond over time does conscientiousness become operative. This reveals that M38-B spans from the sub-conscientious (panic) to the conscientious (faith-obedience) along a temporal and resource axis.

---

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The consistent engagement of relational capacity — present in every VCG where genuine faith-cry or anguished petition is the operative phenomenon, and absent precisely in VCG-05 where despair has extinguished hope — reveals that relational capacity is the functional core of this characteristic's life. The characteristic is not fundamentally about terror or panic as private inner states: it is about the movement from terror toward Another. The cry "Lord, save me" (Mat 14:30) is intelligible only as a relational act — it presupposes an interpersonal reality in which the called-upon Other can hear and respond. The Father hearing the Son's anguished cry (Heb 5:7) because of eulabeia reveals that the relational capacity at the deepest level is not merely activated by crisis but is perfected through it — the quality of the relational bond determines whether the petition is heard. The despair of VCG-05, where no such cry is made, confirms by contrast that when relational capacity toward the divine is absent or inactive, the characteristic's life-giving dynamic fails, and what remains is pure hopeless endurance. Relational capacity is not peripheral to this characteristic — it is what distinguishes its living form from its dead form.
