**T2.1.1 — Spirit-Level Location — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Psa 31:5 (VCG-03) provides the clearest spirit-level marker: "Into your hand I commit my spirit (rûaḥ)" — the soul's total self-surrender to God is grounded in past ransom by the faithful redeemer. The word used is rûaḥ (spirit), and the ransomed identity is the explicit basis of that surrender. No other verse in the corpus names the spirit (rûaḥ/pneuma) as the explicit locus, but Psa 31:5 establishes that the ransomed characteristic does reach to the spirit level in at least one instance.

---

**T2.2.1 — Spirit-Level Location — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The corpus does not indicate that ransomed-identity gratitude primarily originates at the spirit level. The dominant register across VCG-01 through VCG-08 is the soul (nepeš) and the covenantal-historical memory operating through the whole inner person. Psa 31:5 shows the characteristic reaching the spirit, but the preponderance of evidence places origin in divine initiative received at the soul level, not generated from the spirit. The characteristic is received, not indigenously spirit-generated.

---

**T2.3.1 — Spirit-Level Location — What does spirit-level location reveal about the nature and depth of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The single spirit-level instance (Psa 31:5, VCG-03) reveals that ransomed identity can penetrate to the deepest constitutional level of the person: total self-surrender (committing the rûaḥ) is made possible precisely because the redeemer is trusted as the one who ransomed. This indicates that the characteristic, while not exclusively spirit-level, is capable of reaching the innermost layer of personhood, where the self is wholly entrusted to God. Depth is confirmed by this single but weighty instance.

**T2.6.3 — Origin and Source — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The corpus is unambiguous: the origin of ransomed-identity gratitude is entirely in divine initiative received from outside the person. Every pa.dah instance in VCG-01 through VCG-08 presents God as the subject of ransoming: "the LORD your God… redeemed you" (Deu 15:15), "God who has redeemed my soul" (2Sa 4:9), "I will ransom them from the power of Sheol" (Hos 13:14). The inner-being states of gratitude, identity, and memory arise as responses to what God has done, not as capacities generated from within. Hos 7:13 (VCG-07) confirms this by showing that when the inner will refuses the offered ransom, the characteristic cannot form.

---

**T2.7.3 — Origin and Source — What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The origin is singular: divine initiative is the sole generative source across all 42 verses. There is no evidence of any alternative source generating this characteristic — not human effort, not inherent capacity, not accumulated virtue. Psa 49:7 (VCG-07) explicitly eliminates human capacity as an origin: "no man can by any means redeem his brother." The multiplicity that does exist is in the modes of reception (personal rescue, corporate Exodus, eschatological promise, substitutionary ritual) and the inner-being states that follow, but the origin remains singular throughout.

---

**T2.8.3 — Origin and Source — Does the origin of this characteristic change across different contexts evidenced in Scripture?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The origin (divine initiative) does not change across contexts, but the mode and register of divine ransoming varies significantly: historical-corporate (Exodus, VCG-01), individual-experiential (personal rescue from enemies or death, VCG-03), eschatological-ultimate (from Sheol and Death, VCG-05), and substitutionary-ritual (firstborn ransoming, VCG-08). In every context God is the ransoming agent; the variation is in what domain of threat the ransom addresses and what inner-being state it generates, not in the origin itself.

**T3.5.1 — Cognition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?**

**T3.5.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Volition is strongly engaged across multiple VCGs. The volitional faculty is engaged both as recipient of ransom (the freed will) and as that which can refuse it.

**T3.6.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — This characteristic primarily enables and frees volition. Capacity: ransom expands volitional capacity by removing oppression. Interaction: ransomed identity interacts with moral-evaluative and memory faculties to shape the will's direction. Constraints: Hos 7:13 (VCG-07) shows the will can resist ransom, which is the one way this characteristic is not enabling but resisted — a will-against-ransom is evidenced.

**T3.7.2 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that ransomed-identity gratitude has a constitutive relationship with volition: it is specifically designed to free and direct the will. VCG-04 exists as a distinct sub-group precisely because ransom's primary inner-being effect in those verses is the liberation and reorientation of the will. This distinguishes this characteristic from purely affective or cognitive states: it has a specifically volitional telos — the ransomed person is freed to choose, to obey, to repent, to walk in integrity. The fact that the will can refuse the offered ransom (Hos 7:13) confirms that genuine volition is engaged, not overridden.

**T3.8.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Agency appears in several registers. VCG-02 shows intercessory agency: Moses (Deu 9:26), Nehemiah (Neh 1:10), and the psalmists (Psa 25:22, 44:26) actively deploy remembered ransom as the ground of their petitions — they act by appealing to God. VCG-08 (1Sa 14:45) shows communal agency: the people ransom Jonathan by collective action. VCG-01 (Deu 15:15, 24:18) shows ethical agency motivated by remembered ransom: the ransomed person acts justly toward others because of what God did for them. The characteristic does not make the person passive but generates specific agentic outputs.

---

**T3.9.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — This characteristic enables and directs agency. Deu 15:15 and 24:18 (VCG-01) show that remembered ransom generates specific ethical actions (treating slaves generously, caring for the vulnerable). VCG-04 shows ransom enabling the will to act in obedience. The pleading prayers of VCG-02 are themselves agentic acts — the ransomed identity provides the standing and the ground from which the person approaches God in petition. There is no evidence of the characteristic impairing or bypassing agency; its consistent effect is to generate purposive action.

---

**T3.10.2 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic is not merely contemplative or affective but actively generative of agency across multiple domains: intercessory prayer (VCG-02), ethical action toward the vulnerable (VCG-01), communal solidarity (VCG-08), and obedient response to God's commands (VCG-04). The characteristic functions as an agency-motivating ground: the person's awareness of being ransomed provides the identity-basis from which specific actions are initiated. Agency is not the characteristic itself but its necessary and consistent overflow.

**T3.11.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes, prominently. VCG-01 establishes redemption as the moral standard by which present conduct is evaluated: Deu 15:15 and Deu 24:18 use past ransom as the benchmark against which ethical treatment of others is measured. Deu 13:5 (VCG-01) frames rebellion against the Redeemer as an aggravated moral wrong specifically because of the prior redemption — the moral evaluation of betrayal is calibrated against the magnitude of the ransom received. Isa 1:27 (VCG-04) conditions ransom on repentance and righteousness, showing that the moral evaluation faculty assesses whether the condition for redemption has been met.

---

**T3.1.3 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — This characteristic deepens moral evaluation by grounding it in the concrete covenantal history of ransom. Deu 13:5 (VCG-01) shows that moral evaluation of rebellion becomes sharper when measured against received redemption — the person knows that betrayal of God is not simply wrong in the abstract but constitutes a specific aggravated betrayal of one who ransomed them. Psa 78:42 (VCG-01) shows the inverse: loss of the redemptive memory impairs moral evaluation, leading to faithlessness and ingratitude. The characteristic provides the moral-evaluative standard, and active holding of it sharpens; loss of it dulls.

---

**T3.2.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic functions as a moral-evaluative reference point: the ransomed identity is both a status and a standard. The person evaluates their own conduct (Psa 26:11 — walking in integrity, VCG-04), their community's conduct (Deu 15:15, 24:18 — treating the vulnerable), and the gravity of betrayal (Deu 13:5) through the lens of having been ransomed. The characteristic does not create moral standards de novo but calibrates existing moral awareness against the weight of received grace — making ingratitude, injustice, and rebellion specifically legible as betrayals of the one who ransomed.

**T3.3.3 — Conscience — Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Conscience is implicitly engaged at several points. Deu 21:8 (VCG-02) — the appeal for atonement of blood guilt uses the redeemed people's identity as the relational standing from which atonement is sought, implying that the conscience's awareness of guilt is addressed through the ransomed identity. Psa 130:8 (VCG-05) — "God will ransom Israel from all its iniquities" — presents total ransom from sin as the hope of the conscience burdened by accumulated guilt. Isa 1:27 (VCG-04) implies that repentance (conscience's function) is the prerequisite for redemptive ransom. The conscience is not named explicitly but its functions (guilt, conviction, moral-witness) are engaged.

---

**T3.4.3 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The characteristic both enables and is enabled by conscience. In the positive direction: the hope of total ransom from iniquity (Psa 130:8, VCG-05) provides the conscience with a resolution — the guilt the conscience witnesses can be fully covered by God's ransoming act. The characteristic does not silence the conscience but gives it an answer. In the reverse direction: the ransomed identity heightens the conscience's sensitivity to betrayal (Deu 13:5) — being ransomed makes the person more acutely conscience-aware of what it would mean to defect. No evidence of bypassing or impairing conscience.

---

**T3.5.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic operates as both a conscience-sensitiser (making the ransomed person more aware of the weight of betrayal and ingratitude) and a conscience-resolver (Psa 130:8: total ransom from iniquity addresses the conscience's burden). This dual function — sharpening and resolving — is consistent with the characteristic's covenantal logic: the ransomed person knows both the gravity of sin against their Redeemer and the completeness of the promised ransom from that sin. Conscience is neither bypassed nor crushed but properly calibrated and ultimately satisfied by the ransoming act.

---

**T3.6.3 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Yes. Conscientiousness is most directly engaged in VCG-01 (Deu 15:15, 24:18) and VCG-04 (Psa 26:11, Psa 119:134). In Deu 15:15, the integrated response is: moral awareness (knowing I was ransomed) + volitional motivation (therefore I am commanded) + ethical action (treat your slave generously). In Psa 119:134, the integrated response is: plea for ransom → freed will → obedience to precepts. These are not isolated faculties but their coordinated operation — precisely what conscientiousness names. Psa 26:11 pairs integrity (conscientiousness in action) with the ransomed plea, showing them as inseparable.

---

**T3.7.3 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — This characteristic enables and deepens conscientiousness. VCG-04 as a whole presents ransom as the precondition for the integrated response: without ransom the will is constrained (Psa 119:134 — oppression prevents obedience), making conscientiousness impossible. After ransom, the integrated response becomes possible: moral awareness informs the will, the will acts, and action reflects the ransomed identity. Deu 15:15 and 24:18 (VCG-01) show conscientiousness as the expected outcome of ransomed memory — the coordinated response of remembering, being motivated, and acting. No evidence of bypassing or impairing.

---

**T3.8.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that ransomed-identity gratitude functions as the motivational infrastructure for conscientiousness. The characteristic does not merely produce moral feelings or ethical knowledge in isolation; it generates the integrated coordination of moral awareness, volitional direction, and concrete action that constitutes conscientiousness. This is most visible in the Deuteronomic pattern (VCG-01, VCG-04): the commands to "remember your ransom and therefore act" are appeals to conscientiousness, not just to feeling or knowing alone. The characteristic is, in its ethical register, fundamentally conscientiousness-generating.

**T3.9.3 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — Relational capacity is engaged across multiple registers. Vertically (toward God): the ransomed identity is constitutively relational — it exists only in connection with the God who ransomed. Deu 7:8 (VCG-01) — "because the LORD loved you and kept his oath" — establishes the ransom as an expression of relational love and covenant faithfulness. The intercessory prayers (VCG-02) are relational acts, appealing to God as the remembered Ransomer. Horizontally (toward others): Deu 15:15 and 24:18 (VCG-01) ground just treatment of others in the ransomed relationship with God — the vertical relationship generates horizontal relational obligation. 1Sa 14:45 (VCG-08) shows communal solidarity as a direct expression of relational capacity.

---

**T3.10.3 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — This characteristic deepens relational capacity in both vertical and horizontal directions. Vertically: the memory of being ransomed by God's covenantal love (Deu 7:8) deepens the person's capacity for trust, gratitude, and allegiance toward God — it provides a relational history that grounds present connection. Horizontally: Deu 15:15 and 24:18 show that ransomed identity generates empathic relational capacity — having been enslaved and rescued, the person is equipped to relate justly and compassionately to the vulnerable. Psa 78:42 (VCG-01) shows the inverse: loss of redemptive memory impairs the relational bond with God. No evidence of the characteristic bypassing relational capacity.

---

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-7]** E — The pattern reveals that ransomed-identity gratitude is constitutively relational: it cannot exist outside of the relational bond between the ransomed person and the ransoming God. The characteristic is not an individual achievement or inner state that happens to have relational implications; it is itself a relational identity — the person is defined as the one whom God ransomed, and that definition is inherently dyadic. Furthermore, this vertical relational identity generates and calibrates horizontal relational capacity (treatment of others, communal solidarity). The characteristic reveals that inner-being identity in the Hebrew scriptures is inseparable from covenantal relationship — to be ransomed is to be in permanent relation with the Ransomer.
