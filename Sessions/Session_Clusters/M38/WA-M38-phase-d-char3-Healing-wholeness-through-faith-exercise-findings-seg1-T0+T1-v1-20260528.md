**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence consistently positions God — specifically Jesus as the agent of sōzō — as one whose healing power responds to faith exercised from within the human person. In Mat 9:22 and Mar 5:34, Jesus names faith as the operative cause: "your faith has made you well," revealing that God's saving-healing character is not capricious or arbitrarily selective but structurally responsive to interior trust. In Luk 8:50, Jesus commands "only believe" as the direct counter to mortal fear, indicating that God's character includes a disposition to save precisely at the boundary of death when faith is the presenting inner posture. Jam 5:15 (M38-C-VCG-03) reveals a further dimension: God is the one who, when addressed by the prayer of faith, heals body and forgives sin in a single integrated act, showing that the divine character holds physical and spiritual restoration together rather than separating them. 1Pe 3:21 deepens this: God is one who receives the inner appeal of conscience and answers it through resurrection power. Taken together, the evidence images a God whose nature is integrative wholeness — one who does not partition body from spirit in the act of rescue, and whose responsive character is activated by interior faith-movement in the creature.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence positions faith-exercised-toward-wholeness as the inner-being faculty that enables the human person to receive the full, integrated rescue God offers. The hemorrhaging woman's interior conviction in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 — forming before any outward act is complete — suggests that this faculty is the point of contact between human need and divine power: it equips the person to reach toward God at the place where bodily and spiritual need converge. Bartimaeus in Mar 10:52 and the blind man in Luk 18:42 illustrate that this characteristic also equips the person for discipleship — Bartimaeus "followed him on the way" after receiving sight, indicating that wholeness-through-faith does not end at physical cure but opens the person toward continued relational orientation to Christ. The Samaritan leper's return in Luk 17:19 further shows that the characteristic equips the person for grateful recognition of the giver of wholeness. In sum, the created purpose evidenced is: this characteristic equips the human person to move from fragmentation (disease, blindness, death-threat) through faith-exercise toward integrated personhood that is bodily, spiritual, and relationally directed toward God.

**T0.3.1 — Image-Bearer Expression — In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it instantiate?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The divine image dimension evidenced here is the capacity for responsive trust — the ability of the inner being to form an interior conviction directed toward God as the source of wholeness. The women in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 exhibit a pre-verbal, pre-contact interior speech — "if I touch his garment I will be made well" — which is an act of the person's image-bearing rationality and will oriented toward the divine. This is not animal instinct but structured interior faith: purposive, directional, and theologically oriented. The image-bearer expression is therefore the relational-receptive capacity: the human person, unlike other creatures, can form interior trust oriented toward God and, by that trust, become the site where divine saving power acts. 1Pe 3:21's "appeal to God for a good conscience" (M38-C-VCG-03) sharpens this further — conscience as image-bearing faculty is the organ capable of seeking right standing before God, and it does so through interior appeal rather than external rite. The characteristic thus instantiates the relational and volitional aspects of the image: the capacity to turn inwardly toward God in trust, which is itself a creaturely reflection of the covenantal relationality that belongs to God's nature.

**T0.4.1 — Typological Significance — Does Scripture use this characteristic typologically — deploying it to point toward or participate in a reality beyond the immediate (covenantal, eschatological, christological)?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Typological deployment is partially evidenced. The most explicit christological extension is Luk 17:19 where the Samaritan leper — a double outsider, racially and religiously — is the one whose faith is declared to have made him whole, while the nine Jewish lepers do not return. The implicit typological register here is that faith-exercised-wholeness transcends ethnic covenant membership and anticipates the gospel's extension to gentile inclusion. Mar 10:52 carries a further christological-eschatological register: Bartimaeus's title "Son of David" (the cry that occasions the healing) situates the faith-wholeness event within messianic expectation, so that the individual healing participates in the arrival of the kingdom. Jam 5:15 (M38-C-VCG-03) extends the topology ecclesiologically: the prayer of faith within the community of elders names a corporate faith-practice that images the church as the ongoing site of integrated rescue, pointing toward eschatological wholeness. 1Pe 3:21 is the most structurally typological: baptism is explicitly read against the backdrop of Noah's rescue through water, and conscience-appeal through resurrection is positioned as the eschatological fulfilment of that earlier rescue. The direction of typology is examined in T0.4.2.

**T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence does not explicitly attribute faith-exercised-as-inner-faculty to God (faith in the Pauline sense of God's faithfulness is a separate register not present in this sub-group's corpus). However, the evidence reveals an important asymmetry: faith is consistently located in the human person as the activating faculty, while God — through Jesus — is the one who names, confirms, and responds to it. Jesus's declarations in Mat 9:22, Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48, Luk 17:19, Luk 18:42, and Mar 10:52 all have the same structure: the human person exercises faith inwardly, and Jesus recognises and names it as the operative cause of sōzō. This asymmetry is itself revelatory: what it discloses is that God in his nature is responsive to faith — not as a constraint on divine freedom but as a covenantal disposition to honour the interior trust that the image-bearer directs toward him. The significance for the human person is that this characteristic is precisely that which aligns the creature with the responsive structure of God's saving character: by exercising faith, the human person enters into the relational dynamic that God's covenantal nature has prepared.

**T0.2.2 — Created Purpose — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence does not address the pre-fall state directly, but the internal logic of the evidence suggests a dual register. The conditions that occasion the faith-exercises in M38-C are uniformly conditions of bodily breakdown: haemorrhage (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28), blindness (Mar 10:52; Luk 18:42), leprosy (Luk 17:19), death-threat (Mar 5:23; Luk 8:50), and sickness requiring communal intercession (Jam 5:15). These are all markers of the fallen condition — mortality, decay, disease — suggesting that the operative expression of this characteristic is a response to the fallen condition. However, the structural capacity beneath the expression — the capacity to form interior trust directed toward God as source of wholeness — appears to belong to the created design as image-bearer rationality and will. The characteristic is therefore best described as both: the deep faculty is created-design (the relational-volitional capacity for interior faith directed toward God), while its particular form as faith exercised in the face of bodily dissolution is a response to fallenness. Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21 (M38-C-VCG-03) reinforce the fallen-response register by pairing healing with forgiveness and conscience-cleansing, indicating that sin's effects on the inner person are also part of what this characteristic addresses.

**T0.3.2 — Image-Bearer Expression — Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to something in God?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence suggests this characteristic is exclusively a creaturely analogue rather than a shared property. As noted in obs_id=225 (T0.1.2), God is not the subject of faith-exercised in this corpus; he is the object and respondent. The structure is consistently: human person → exercises faith inwardly → receives sōzō from God. There is no verse in the M38-C corpus where God or Jesus is described as exercising faith in the same mode. What the divine side of the relationship does exhibit — and what this characteristic analogises — is responsive reliability: God's character as one who honours interior trust. The creaturely analogue relationship therefore runs thus: God's faithful covenantal responsiveness is the archetype; the human person's capacity to exercise faith and receive integrated wholeness is the creaturely image-bearing reflection of that covenantal structure. 1Pe 3:21's "appeal to God for a good conscience" most clearly maps this asymmetry — the human person appeals, and God through resurrection answers. The characteristic is thus exclusively creaturely in its operational form while being relationally structured toward the divine.

**T0.4.2 — Typological Significance — If typological use is present, what is the direction of the typology — does the human instance point toward the divine, or does the divine instance establish the pattern for the human?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Where typological use is evidenced (see T0.4.1), the direction is consistently from divine pattern to human instance, not the reverse. 1Pe 3:21 is the clearest case: Noah's rescue through water (divine pattern established in Genesis) becomes the type of which baptism-as-conscience-appeal is the antitype — the divine salvific act precedes and establishes the human participatory form. In the Synoptic healing narratives, Jesus's declarations ("your faith has made you well") function as divine naming that establishes what faith-exercised-toward-wholeness is and does — the human instances of the woman, Bartimaeus, and the Samaritan leper are not archetypes for subsequent humans to imitate in a typological sense but rather instances within the divine pattern of kingdom-wholeness that Jesus is enacting. The christological direction in Mar 10:52 (Son of David healing the blind on the road to Jerusalem) places the human faith-exercise within the unfolding messianic pattern established by Old Testament prophetic anticipation of restored sight as eschatological sign (cf. Isa 35:5 — outside this corpus but setting the background). The typological direction is therefore divine → human throughout: God's pattern of integrated rescue, established in covenant and announced in prophecy, becomes the framework within which individual faith-exercised-wholeness events participate and point forward.

**T0.1.3 — Divine Nature Reflected — Where Scripture is silent about God's possession of this characteristic, what does that silence suggest about the characteristic's place in the divine image?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus is silent on God exercising faith as an inner faculty (see T0.1.2 and T0.3.2). This silence is structurally significant rather than merely negative. It suggests that faith-exercised is precisely a creaturely faculty — one that belongs to the image-bearer as image-bearer, not to God as the original. The divine image in the human person is not a simple copy of God's properties but includes capacities that arise from the creature's position as dependent being-in-relation-to-God. Faith-exercised is one such capacity: it only makes sense for a being who is not self-sufficient, who must reach toward another for wholeness. God possesses wholeness inherently; the image-bearer is constituted to reach for it. The silence therefore locates this characteristic squarely within the creatureliness dimension of the imago Dei — it is not a reflection of a divine attribute in the sense that love or justice are, but rather a distinctively creaturely form of the relational structure that the image is designed to sustain. Its place in the divine image is as the inner-being faculty by which the creature maintains its constitutive orientation toward God as source — an orientation that God himself does not need because he is the source.

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The programme names this characteristic "Healing wholeness through faith exercised." The name signals several structural commitments simultaneously. "Healing" locates the primary presenting register: the inner being is in a condition requiring restoration, not merely improvement. "Wholeness" signals that the outcome is integrated and total — not partial cure but a convergence of physical and spiritual restoration, which is the sōzō semantic field's distinctive contribution. "Through" indicates instrumentality and directional movement: the characteristic is not a state received passively but a result achieved by means of something. "Faith exercised" is the instrument named — not faith as disposition only, but faith in active, operative mode; the participle "exercised" marks intentional, directional inner-being action. The name as a whole therefore signals: this is an active inner-being phenomenon (not passive), its activity is faith directed toward an object (God/Christ), and its goal is the bridging of physical and spiritual need in a single integrated rescue event. The word "exercised" is the name's most decisive signal — it distinguishes this characteristic from faith as settled doctrinal assent and positions it as a dynamic, moment-bound inner act that produces ontological change.

**T1.2.1 — Kind — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence positions this characteristic primarily as an act — a specific, directional inner-being movement — rather than a stable disposition or quality. The woman's pre-touch interior monologue in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 ("if I touch his garment I will be made well") is a dateable inner event with a before and after, not a background disposition. Bartimaeus's persistent cry in Mar 10:52, the father's plea in Mar 5:23, and the Samaritan's return in Luk 17:19 all exhibit discrete, directional acts. However, the characteristic is not pure momentary act either — the woman presses through a crowd, suggesting sustained inner-being commitment over time. Jam 5:15 extends this to communal intercessory practice, which implies a more sustained mode. The most accurate classification is: faith exercised is an act with dispositional depth — a moment-bound inner-being movement that is possible because of an underlying trust-orientation, but which is itself distinct from that underlying orientation. It is the actualization of faith in a specific movement toward God for specific wholeness. In programme vocabulary, it is best described as an inner-being act arising from and expressing a deeper dispositional trust.

**T1.3.1 — Boundary — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus directly names the structural opposite: fear. In Luk 8:50, Jesus sets "do not fear" against "only believe" in an explicit antithetical parallelism — the inner being must displace fear in order to exercise faith toward wholeness. Fear is therefore not merely an accompanying affect but the structural opposite that this characteristic must overcome or displace. The father's situation in Mar 5:23 (urgent, death-facing plea) also operates in the fear-faith opposition: the urgency of his plea names the mortal dread that faith-exercised moves against. A secondary structural opposite is evidenced in the nine lepers of Luk 17:17-19: their failure to return suggests that self-sufficient continuation — taking healing without returning in faith — is also an opposing inner posture, though this is less explicitly marked than fear. The primary structural opposite is therefore fear (phobos) as the inner-being reality of self-protective withdrawal in the face of mortal threat, which directly excludes the outward movement of trust toward God that this characteristic requires.

**T1.4.1 — Modes of Operation — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence reveals at least three distinct modes of operation. (1) Interior conviction mode (M38-C-VCG-01): the characteristic operates as pre-verbal, pre-contact inner monologue or formed conviction — the woman in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 forms the conviction inwardly before touching, and this interior act is itself causally efficacious; Jesus confirms it was operative ("your faith has made you well"). (2) Crisis-displacement mode (M38-C-VCG-02): the characteristic operates as active counter-movement against mortal fear — in Luk 8:50 and Mar 5:23, faith is not quiet conviction but urgent, desperate exercise that displaces fear as the governing inner state; the faith-act is precipitated by extreme threat and operates through intensity rather than composure. (3) Intercessory/conscience mode (M38-C-VCG-03): the characteristic operates through the conscience as primary organ (1Pe 3:21 — "appeal to God for a good conscience") and through intercessory faith on behalf of another (Jam 5:15 — "the prayer of faith will save the sick"). In this mode, the characteristic's operation is not self-directed but other-directed, and the restoration is explicitly conscience-level rather than somatic only. These three modes are genuinely distinct in their presenting dynamics, though all three share the structural feature of faith as the inner-being activating movement toward God.

**T1.5.1 — Immediate Response — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus evidences two distinct immediate responses depending on the mode of encounter. (1) Where faith has been exercised and sōzō received, the immediate inner response is peace: Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 both close with Jesus granting the woman "peace" (eirēnē) — this is the direct inner-being marker of what the person first experiences upon receiving healing-wholeness through faith. Peace here functions not as a general well-being but as the specific inner-being state that attends the settling of the faith-crisis: the striving of faith has reached its object, and the inner being rests in that reception. (2) In the blindness-healing cases (Mar 10:52; Luk 18:42), the immediate response is discipleship-orientation — "followed him on the way" — suggesting that in the sight-restoration mode, the first inner response is not peace-as-rest but forward-movement toward Christ. (3) In the conscience-appeal mode (1Pe 3:21), the immediate inner response is not named explicitly but the structure implies conscience-cleared: the appeal to God is answered by resurrection, and the good conscience is the immediate inner result. Across all three modes, the immediate response involves an inner-being shift from the state that preceded the faith-exercise (fear, darkness, troubled conscience) to a new integrated state (peace, sight/following, clear conscience).

The sustained effect is therefore ongoing conscience-integrity. (3) Luk 17:19 implies that grateful return to the giver of wholeness is the sustained posture, though this is an inference from the single act. Taken together, the sustained effects evidenced are: re-oriented discipleship (following Christ), conscience-integrity maintained before God, and gratitude as an ongoing orientation. These are inner-being changes at the level of constitution, not merely momentary affective states.

**T1.8.1 — Dimension Classification — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic is volitional-faith: the characteristic operates as a specific act of the will directed by trust toward God. The evidence for this classification is concentrated in M38-C-VCG-01 and VCG-02: the woman's interior monologue (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28), Jesus's repeated formula "your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22; Mar 5:34; Luk 8:48; Luk 17:19; Luk 18:42; Mar 10:52), and the command "only believe" against fear (Luk 8:50) all locate the primary faculty in an act of the will shaped by trust. Secondary dimensions are present — conscience (1Pe 3:21), relational-hope (Mat 9:21's expectation of wholeness), and somatic-spiritual bridging (Jam 5:15) — but these function as correlates or outcomes of the volitional-faith act rather than the primary dimension. The characteristic's classification is therefore volitional-faith as primary, with conscience and relational-hope as secondary dimensions.

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic's primary Greek term is sōzō (G4982), with the inner-faculty term being pistis/pisteuō (faith/believe) as the instrument named by Jesus in the healing declarations. At the definitional level, sōzō carries a semantic range that refuses separation of physical and spiritual rescue — it can denote rescue from physical danger, healing of bodily disease, and soteriological salvation, with these senses intersecting rather than being mutually exclusive. The programme's researcher direction explicitly notes a "sōzō sense-split" — this sub-group is held at the intersection where healing and saving are inseparable, which is sōzō's own semantic structure. Pistis/pisteuō at the definitional level denotes trust, reliance, and commitment directed toward an object — it is inherently relational and directional, presupposing both a truster and a trusted. The combination of sōzō and pistis in the standard Synoptic formula ("your faith has sōzō'd you") therefore produces at the definitional level: trust-directed-toward-God/Christ as the inner act that releases integrated rescue (physical-plus-spiritual). The Hebrew background of shālōm (peace, wholeness, completeness) — evoked by Jesus's "go in peace" in Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 — adds the dimension that the result of sōzō-through-pistis is wholeness as restored-right-order, not merely absence of disease.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic is composite at first encounter, not simple. Three constituent elements are immediately visible in the verse evidence. (1) An inner conviction or trust-forming act: the woman's interior monologue (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28) shows that faith must be formed as a specific directed conviction before it becomes operative — this is an epistemic-volitional element. (2) An outward expression or movement: touching the garment, crying out persistently (Bartimaeus, Mar 10:52), returning to give thanks (Luk 17:19), praying for the sick (Jam 5:15) — the inner conviction must pass through some expression or directional act; faith exercised is not purely interior but moves outward. (3) A reception and confirmation event: Jesus's naming of faith as the cause ("your faith has made you well") and the bestowing of peace or sight or cleansed conscience — the characteristic is only complete when the inner act has reached its object and been received as sōzō. These three elements — formation, expression, reception — are distinguishable but not separable in the evidence. A fourth element is present in M38-C-VCG-03: intercessory extension, where faith operates not only for the self but on behalf of another (Jam 5:15). The characteristic is therefore structurally composite with at least three to four constituent elements.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes or resists the following, drawn from direct verse evidence. (1) Fear as governing inner state: Luk 8:50 sets "do not fear" as the direct prerequisite for "only believe" — faith exercised resists fear's governance of the inner being. (2) External-only or ritual-only cleansing: 1Pe 3:21 explicitly states that sōzō through baptism is not the removal of dirt from the body but an appeal of conscience — the characteristic resists reduction to external rite; the inner faculty is the real locus. (3) Gratitude-deferred or ungrateful reception: the nine lepers in Luk 17:17-19 (implied contrast with v.19) suggest that receiving physical cure without the returning faith-exercise of acknowledgment is excluded from full sōzō — the characteristic resists being dissolved into bare physical benefit without relational response. (4) Self-sufficiency as alternative to faith: the act of forming interior conviction directed toward Christ as the source of wholeness excludes self-reliance as the operative inner posture — one cannot simultaneously rely on oneself and exercise faith toward Christ for the same wholeness. These exclusions collectively define the boundary: the characteristic ends where fear, external-only ritual, ungrateful passivity, or self-sufficiency begin.

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, the evidence supports contextual and directional variation in operation, as distinct from the three structural modes identified in T1.4.1. (1) Self-directed vs. other-directed: In M38-C-VCG-01 and VCG-02, faith is exercised by the healing-seeker for their own wholeness or by a parent for their child (Mar 5:23). In M38-C-VCG-03 (Jam 5:15), faith operates intercessorily — the elders' prayer of faith operates on behalf of a sick person who may themselves be passive. The inner-being engagement differs: self-directed faith involves the person's own volitional-trust act; intercessory faith involves the inner-being of a third party acting as conduit. (2) Crisis-intensity vs. composure-level: VCG-02's crisis-displacement mode (Luk 8:50; Mar 5:23) operates at higher intensity than VCG-01's quiet interior conviction mode (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28). The constitutional level at which faith engages the inner being appears to shift with urgency — both are genuine faith-exercises but with different phenomenological registers. (3) Bodily-healing register vs. conscience-register: The Synoptic healing narratives operate primarily in the somatic register that carries spiritual overtone, while 1Pe 3:21 operates primarily in the conscience register with the somatic (baptism) as outward correlate. The characteristic therefore differentiates in operation across these contextual-constitutional dimensions while retaining the same structural core.

(1) Peace (eirēnē): Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 both close with Jesus pronouncing peace — in the haemorrhage narrative, peace is the named immediate inner-being state following reception of healing-through-faith. (2) Sight and discipleship-movement: Mar 10:52 and Luk 18:42 close with physical restoration (sight received immediately) and then forward movement (Bartimaeus "followed him on the way"). The immediate response here is not peace-as-rest but sight-enabled following — a directional movement rather than a settled state. (3) Conscience-cleared: 1Pe 3:21 implies that the immediate result of the conscience-appeal is a good conscience established before God — the conscience's inner register shifts from troubled to clear. (4) Gratitude-return: Luk 17:19 positions the Samaritan's return as the faith-act that precedes the declaration of wholeness, so the immediate response is the completion of the faith-act in grateful recognition. The responses are consistent in one respect — each names an inner-being shift from a prior fragmented or distressed state to an integrated one — but the specific form of the shift varies with the mode of operation and the dimension primarily engaged (somatic-spiritual, volitional-directional, or conscience-level).

**T1.6.2 — Sustained Effect — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence for sustained states is limited but specific. (1) Reoriented discipleship: Mar 10:52 establishes that the sustained state following faith-exercised-wholeness is a following-orientation toward Christ — "on the way" in Markan theology implies ongoing journeying with Jesus toward Jerusalem and beyond; the capacity established is continued relational-volitional alignment with Christ. (2) Conscience-integrity: 1Pe 3:21's "good conscience" maintained "through the resurrection of Jesus Christ" implies that the sustained quality is conscience-integrity — an ongoing inner-being state of right standing before God, not merely momentary clearance. This is a quality of the inner person's relationship to God that becomes their new constitution. (3) Gratitude as dispositional orientation: Luk 17:19 suggests that the wholeness received through faith generates a sustained grateful orientation toward the giver — the Samaritan's return is both an immediate act and an indicator of a dispositional posture. (4) Intercessory capacity: Jam 5:15 within a community-practice frame suggests that sustained faith-exercise develops intercessory capacity — the characteristic, when practiced in the VCG-03 mode, establishes the person/community as ongoing sites of faith-activated rescue for others. These sustained effects establish the person at the level of inner constitution rather than episodic experience.

**T1.7.2 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence names or implies several blocking conditions. (1) Fear as governing state: Luk 8:50's structure — "do not fear; only believe" — positions fear as the primary blocking condition. When fear governs the inner being, faith-exercise cannot proceed because fear is the opposite directional movement (inward self-protection rather than outward trust-extension toward God). (2) Failure to bring faith to completion in grateful recognition: Luk 17:17-19 implies that the nine lepers who received physical cure but did not return failed to receive full sōzō-wholeness. The blocking condition here is the inner posture of taking benefit without relational acknowledgment — a form of inner-being self-sufficiency that receives the physical without opening to the spiritual register. (3) Reliance on external rite as substitute for inner appeal: 1Pe 3:21 explicitly blocks the distortion of treating baptism as physical cleansing rather than conscience-appeal — the blocking/distorting condition is ritualism that does not engage the conscience's inner faculty. (4) Crowd-pressure or social discouragement: Bartimaeus is rebuked and told to be silent (Mar 10:48) — while he persists, the implicit blocking condition is social pressure to suppress the faith-cry; those who capitulate to such pressure would not exercise the faith that activates sōzō. These blocking conditions identify the inner-being vulnerabilities that prevent reception.

**T1.8.2 — Dimension Classification — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The classification of this characteristic's primary dimension as volitional-faith (established in T1.8.1) is supported by the following specific evidence. (1) The standard Synoptic formula "your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22; Mar 5:34; Luk 8:48; Luk 17:19; Luk 18:42; Mar 10:52) directly attributes the operative cause to an inner-being faculty — faith — that is consistently located in the person exercising it. The formula names faith as the instrument, not emotion, not knowledge, not bodily action alone. (2) The interior monologue in Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 shows faith operating as a formed volitional act — "I will be made well" — which is a directional willing, not merely feeling or thinking. (3) The command "only believe" in Luk 8:50 positions believing as a volitional act that can be commanded — emotions cannot be commanded directly; will can. This supports volitional primacy. (4) 1Pe 3:21's "appeal to God for a good conscience" identifies the operative inner faculty as conscience-mediated volitional petition — the word "appeal" (eperōtēma) carries the sense of a formal directed request, which is an act of the will. (5) Jam 5:15's "prayer of faith" similarly identifies the operative mode as volitional-directed-trust expressed through prayer. All evidence converges on volitional-faith as the governing dimension.

**T1.1.3 — Name and Naming — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The name "Healing wholeness through faith exercised" carries all three implications noted in the prompt. (1) Directional: "through" establishes a vector — the characteristic moves from a starting condition (brokenness, disease, fear, troubled conscience) through faith as the mechanism toward wholeness. The name is not static but describes a trajectory with an originating point, a mediating act, and a destination. (2) Relational: "faith exercised" is inherently relational — faith has an object; it is directed toward someone. Though the name does not name the object (Christ), the verse evidence makes clear that this faith is always exercised toward Jesus as the source of sōzō. The name's faith-exercised language presupposes a truster-trusted relationship. (3) Constitutional: "wholeness" — not "cure" or "recovery" — signals that the result affects the person's constitution, not merely their somatic condition. Wholeness is an integrated ontological state. "Healing" paired with "wholeness" prevents reduction to merely spiritual salvation (the characteristic does include physical cure) while "wholeness" prevents reduction to merely physical cure. The name therefore orients the enquiry from the outset toward a trajectory that is directional (movement through faith), relational (faith toward an object-person), and constitutional (the result is integrated personhood), all three dimensions being necessary for a correct understanding of what the characteristic is and does.

**T1.2.3 — Kind — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Best working description, drawn from the cumulative verse evidence: This characteristic is a composite inner-being act — constitutionally located at the intersection of will, trust, and conscience — by which a person in a condition of physical or spiritual fragmentation forms an explicit, directed interior conviction of reliance upon Christ for integrated wholeness, and through that inner act receives a sōzō event that bridges bodily healing and spiritual restoration. The faculties primarily engaged are: (a) the volitional faculty (the act of believing/trusting is an act of the will directed toward Christ); (b) the cognitive-formative faculty (an interior conviction is formed, sometimes as explicit inner speech as in Mat 9:21); (c) the conscience (as the organ of moral-spiritual standing before God, engaged particularly in Jam 5:15 and 1Pe 3:21). The impact on the person operates at three levels: (a) immediate — peace, sight, conscience-cleared, or gratitude-return; (b) sustained — reoriented discipleship, conscience-integrity, capacity for intercessory faith; (c) constitutional — the person is repositioned from fragmented self-sufficiency or fear toward integrated wholeness-in-relation-to-Christ. The characteristic is not a passive condition but an active, crisis-precipitated or Spirit-prompted act that, when complete, leaves the person constitutionally altered.

**T1.3.3 — Boundary — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Boundary definition from the verse evidence. (1) This characteristic is not soteriological salvation as standalone M38-A or M38-B event: the programme's researcher direction (sōzō sense-split) explicitly holds this sub-group distinct from general salvation (M38-A) and physical-only rescue (M38-B). Where sōzō denotes spiritual salvation without explicit physical healing context, or physical rescue without faith-as-named-activator, it belongs to a different sub-group. (2) It is not faith as general disposition or background virtue: this characteristic is faith exercised — activated, operative, directional — not faith as settled doctrinal orientation or character trait. Where faith names a person's general orientation to God without the specific movement toward wholeness, it is a different phenomenon. (3) It is not medical healing or natural recovery: the characteristic requires sōzō (not iaomai or therapeuō alone) with faith explicitly named as activating cause; healing that occurs without this faith-attribution does not belong to this characteristic. (4) It is not ritual as external act: 1Pe 3:21 explicitly marks the boundary — baptismal water as external physical cleansing does not belong to this characteristic; it is the interior conscience-appeal that constitutes the sōzō-event. (5) It is not fear-motivated superstition: the woman's touch is not magical thinking but formed interior conviction directed toward a specific person (Jesus), which is the structural difference between faith-exercised and mere ritual contact.

**T1.4.3 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, the verse evidence documents a speech-based mode, though it operates at two distinct levels. (1) Inner speech mode: Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 show the woman's faith operating as interior monologue — "if I touch his garment I will be made well" — which is a verbally-formed interior conviction, not merely affective hope. The inner speech structures the faith-act, giving it propositional content and directional commitment. This inner speech is causally prior to the outward act and is what Jesus names ("your faith has made you well") — the faith was operative as formed inner speech before any touch. (2) Outer cry mode: Bartimaeus in Mar 10:52 cries out verbally — "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me" (v.47-48, preceding v.52) — as the faith-speech that occasions the healing. The outer cry is the external expression of inner faith, and Jesus responds to it as the indicator of faith. The communicative function in both modes is the same: speech (inner or outer) articulates the conviction, identifies the object of trust, and thereby enacts the faith-exercise. Speech is not decorative but constitutive of this mode — the forming of the words (inner or outer) is the formation of the faith-act. Jam 5:15 adds a third speech mode: the communal prayer of faith spoken by elders over the sick, where spoken intercession is the operative vehicle of healing-through-faith.

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes, the sustained effect differs from the immediate response in both register and duration. (1) In the haemorrhage narrative, the immediate response is peace (Mar 5:34; Luk 8:48) — this names the inner-being state at the moment of reception. The sustained effect evidenced from the Bartimaeus parallel is discipleship-orientation (Mar 10:52 — "followed him on the way") — a long-term relational-directional constitution that goes beyond the immediate peace-experience. The immediate response is a state (peace); the sustained effect is an orientation (following). These are different in kind: one is receptive and settling, the other is active and directional. (2) In the conscience-appeal mode (1Pe 3:21), the good conscience appears to be both immediate result and sustained state — it is both what is received and what is maintained through the resurrection ground. Here the distinction between immediate and sustained is less sharp. (3) For intercessory faith (Jam 5:15), no immediate inner response is named for the healed person (see T1.5.3 silences), but the sustained effect of community-practiced intercessory faith would be the establishment of the community as a site of ongoing integrated rescue — a capacity-building sustained effect that exceeds the individual healing event. The general pattern is: immediate response = reception of specific inner-being shift (peace, sight, clear conscience); sustained effect = constitutional reorientation (discipleship, conscience-integrity, intercessory capacity) that endures beyond the precipitating event.

**T1.7.3 — Conditions of Reception — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The corpus provides partial evidence for this through contrast cases. (1) The nine lepers (Luk 17:17-19): those who receive physical cure but do not return with faith-in-gratitude encounter the possibility of sōzō-wholeness but do not receive it in its full register. Their inner-being state is one of benefit-reception without relational acknowledgment — they remain in a posture of self-continuation after healing rather than return-and-recognition. The verse does not name their inner state as fear or hostility, but as ungrateful self-directedness. (2) The crowd that rebukes Bartimaeus (Mar 10:48): those who tell him to be silent are in a state of social conformity that suppresses faith-cry — they encounter the possibility of faith-exercised but remain in a normalised expectation that healing is not available or appropriate. Their inner state is one of resigned normalcy rather than faith-urgency. (3) Luk 8:50 implies that those who remain in fear rather than believing encounter the saving-healing possibility but cannot receive it — their inner state is fear-governed. (4) 1Pe 3:21 implies that those who treat baptism as external rite rather than conscience-appeal encounter the form of the characteristic without its inner reality — their state is formalistic externalism, with conscience unengaged. The common thread is: the person who does not receive the characteristic remains in a state of inner fragmentation — fear-governed, self-directed, socially conformist, or externally-focused — without the inner-being movement toward God that reception requires.

**T1.8.3 — Dimension Classification — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Secondary dimensions are evidenced and do not compete with the primary volitional-faith classification but supplement it. (1) Conscience dimension: 1Pe 3:21 and Jam 5:15 both engage the conscience as a primary inner-being organ — "appeal to God for a good conscience" (1Pe 3:21); sins forgiven alongside healing (Jam 5:15). The conscience dimension is real and irreducible in M38-C-VCG-03, but it appears as the inner-being locus that faith-exercised restores rather than as the operating faculty of faith itself. Conscience is the dimension addressed; volitional-faith is the dimension that addresses it. (2) Relational-hope dimension: the woman's interior conviction (Mat 9:21; Mar 5:28) contains an anticipatory hope — "I will be made well" — which is a hope-oriented projection of the inner being toward future reception. This is a secondary relational-hope dimension active in the faith-formation stage. (3) Somatic-spiritual bridging dimension: the characteristic consistently bridges physical and spiritual registers, which means that the somatic dimension of the inner person (or its analogue — the person's embodied condition) is engaged alongside the volitional-spiritual. This is not a competing classification but a distinctive feature that marks this characteristic as embodied-faith, not disembodied mysticism. None of these secondary dimensions displace volitional-faith as primary; each names an aspect of what faith-exercised engages or produces rather than an alternative primary faculty.
