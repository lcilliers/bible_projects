**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence strongly supports God-to-human operation, mediated through Jesus as divine agent. In Mat 9:22 and Mar 5:34, Jesus speaks healing into existence ("your faith has made you well"), functioning as the active divine source of the sōzō event. In Mar 10:52 and Luk 18:42, Jesus restores sight to Bartimaeus and the blind man, again naming faith as the instrument but himself as the giver. Luk 8:50 is particularly explicit: Jesus commands "only believe, and she will be made well," positioning himself as the one who will accomplish the rescue from death — the human's faith opens the door, but the saving act flows from Jesus as divine agent. In Jam 5:15, the prayer of faith heals, but the Lord is implicitly the healer who raises up the sick person. In 1Pe 3:21, salvation comes through resurrection (grounded in Christ's), not human effort — God through Christ is the source. The pattern across M38-C-VCG-01, VCG-02, and VCG-03 is consistent: God in Christ is the originating agent of wholeness, and the human's faith is the receptor-channel through which divine action flows.

---

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence reveals a primarily responsive basis: God in Christ responds to faith as the presented condition, but the response is neither mechanical nor merely transactional. In Mat 9:22, Jesus "turned" and saw the woman — the response is personal and relational before the pronouncement. In Mar 5:34, "your faith has made you well" — faith is named as the operative factor, making the extension conditional upon faith exercised. Similarly Mar 10:52 and Luk 18:42 both credit faith as the factor Jesus acknowledges. Yet in Luk 8:50 (VCG-02), Jesus does not merely respond to already-present faith but commands it: "only believe" — suggesting that God's extension of healing wholeness can precede and summon faith rather than simply reward it, introducing a covenantal-initiative dimension. Jam 5:15 adds a corporate dimension: the prayer of faith as condition for healing and forgiveness. The evidence does not support unconditional extension of healing wholeness; faith is consistently named as the operative inner condition. The basis is best characterised as responsively covenantal — God in Christ responds to faith, but also calls forth the faith to which he will respond.

**T4.1.3 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence reveals a disposition of active willingness toward the whole person, not merely the body. In Mar 5:34, Jesus does not merely cure but sends the woman away "in peace" (eis eirēnēn) — the inner-being dimension is deliberately addressed. In Luk 8:48, the same peace-grant appears, confirming that Jesus's disposition extends to the inner state, not only the physical condition. In Luk 17:19, the Samaritan leper's faith-wholeness is distinguished from the other nine's physical cure by Jesus's explicit word — God's extension through Christ is directed toward the whole person whose inner being (faith, gratitude, return) is engaged. Bartimaeus's immediate following of Jesus (Mar 10:52) is noted by the narrative as a response to total restoration, suggesting that Jesus's healing intends discipleship-relation, not merely physical relief. Jam 5:15 confirms that God's extension encompasses forgiveness of sin alongside bodily healing, showing divine disposition toward comprehensive restoration. The cumulative picture across all three VCGs is a disposition of generous, holistic benevolence toward the whole human person — body, conscience, and relational standing before God.

**T4.2.1 — Divine Interface — Human to God — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence shows clear human-toward-God movement across multiple registers. In Mat 9:21 (VCG-01), the woman's interior conviction — "if I touch his garment I shall be made well" — is a movement of faith directed toward Jesus as divine agent; her press through the crowd is a bodily enactment of this God-ward seeking. In Mar 5:28, the same interior conviction operates as a pre-verbal act of trust directed toward Jesus. In Mar 5:23 (VCG-02), Jairus falls at Jesus's feet with a desperate plea for his dying daughter — a posture of supplication. In Luk 8:50, the command "only believe" implies that faith is the instrument of human movement toward God in the crisis moment. In Luk 17:19, the Samaritan leper's return and prostration before Jesus is explicitly a movement of grateful worship, and Jesus distinguishes it as the act of faith that saves. In Jam 5:15 (VCG-03), intercessory prayer is the form of human movement toward God, with the prayer of faith as the vehicle through which healing and forgiveness flow. In 1Pe 3:21, baptism functions as "an appeal to God for a good conscience" — a formal covenantal movement of the inner person toward God. Human-to-God movement is thus evidenced in modes of seeking, supplication, interior conviction, intercessory prayer, and covenantal appeal.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence reveals faith as the primary and consistently named inner posture. In Mat 9:22, Mar 5:34, Mar 10:52, Luk 8:48, Luk 17:19, and Luk 18:42, Jesus's formula "your faith has made you well" names faith as the operative inner posture without which the saving event would not occur. The character of that faith is further specified by the individual accounts: the woman of Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 exhibits quiet interior conviction, formed before any word is spoken; Bartimaeus (Mar 10:52, Luk 18:42) exhibits persistent, publicly expressed, socially costly faith — he cries out despite rebuke. Jairus (Mar 5:23) exhibits urgent, desperate supplication. The Samaritan leper (Luk 17:19) adds gratitude and return-worship as components of the faith-posture that distinguishes wholeness from mere cure. In Luk 8:50, "only believe" positions faith as the counter-posture to fear — the required inner orientation is trust deliberately held against dread. Jam 5:15 names the "prayer of faith" — implying confident, believing intercession rather than anxious petition. Across the evidence, the required inner posture is faith characterised by conviction, persistence, and willingness to direct the whole self toward Jesus as the source of wholeness.

The consistent picture across M38-C is that the human-to-God movement in this characteristic expresses a relationship of transparent need, personal trust, and recognition of divine sufficiency.

**T5.2.2 — Sequence of Inner States — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal about how this characteristic works?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Drawing on the full VCG evidence, the sequence is: (1) Acute need registered in the inner being — physical suffering, mortal threat, or burdened conscience; (2) Faith formed as interior conviction directed toward Jesus as the source of wholeness — this is the characteristic's distinctive inner-being event, occurring before any outward confirmation; (3) Faith externalised in action — pressing through crowds, crying out, falling at Jesus's feet, intercessory prayer, baptismal appeal; (4) Divine response — Jesus acts, speaks, or the prayer of faith achieves its result; (5) Reception and transformation — body healed, conscience cleared, peace received, wholeness given; (6) Reorientation — peace, following of Jesus, return in worship, or ongoing appeal grounded in resurrection. The sequence reveals that this characteristic works through the inner being as the generative site: faith does not merely accompany an external event but precedes and activates it. The sequence also reveals that wholeness (sōzō) is the terminus of the sequence, not merely health — indicating that the characteristic aims at a state of integrated being rather than symptom relief.

**T5.3.1 — Mechanism of Change — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The dominant mechanism in the M38-C evidence is encounter-mediated sudden transformation. In Mat 9:22 and Mar 5:34, the healing is immediate upon Jesus's word; in Mar 10:52, Bartimaeus receives sight "immediately" (euthys); in Luk 8:48 and Luk 17:19, the transformations are reported as completed events rather than processes. Luk 8:50 situates the mechanism as crisis-faith encounter — the command "only believe" activates the transformation in the context of a death situation. There is no evidence in M38-C of gradual formation or disciplinary process as the operative mechanism for the sōzō event itself. However, the aftermath of Bartimaeus's healing (Mar 10:52: "followed him on the way") suggests that the sudden encounter opens into an ongoing formation trajectory, though this is not the mechanism of the initial transformation. In Jam 5:15 (VCG-03), the mechanism is intercessory prayer — a deliberate communal faith-act — which also appears to produce the result without a described gradual process. In 1Pe 3:21, the mechanism is the baptismal appeal, a ritual act of conscience-direction toward God grounded in Christ's resurrection, which is a specific covenantal encounter rather than gradual formation.

**T5.3.2 — Mechanism of Change — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence does distinguish, though subtly. The mechanisms thus vary by context: individual quiet conviction (VCG-01), crisis faith-counter-to-fear (VCG-02), communal intercession (Jam 5:15), and covenantal conscience-appeal (1Pe 3:21). **T5.4.1 — Suffering and Affliction — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Suffering and affliction are the consistent presenting context across the entire M38-C corpus. In Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28, the woman with the haemorrhage has suffered for twelve years (Mar 5:25-26, contextual); her suffering is the context within which faith forms and is exercised. In Mar 5:23 (VCG-02), Jairus's daughter is at the point of death — mortal affliction is the precise context of the faith-exercise. In Luk 8:50, fear accompanies news of the daughter's death — suffering and grief are the setting for the command to believe. In Mar 10:52 and Luk 18:42, blindness is the long-term affliction within which Bartimaeus exercises faith. In Luk 17:19, leprosy — with all its social and physical suffering — is the affliction context. In Jam 5:15, sickness (asthenōn) is the presenting condition addressed by the prayer of faith. In 1Pe 3:21, the guilty conscience is the affliction — an inner suffering — that the appeal in baptism addresses. This characteristic is not operative in the absence of need; it is precisely the condition of suffering or affliction that activates the faith-exercise and makes the sōzō event possible. Suffering is the context and the occasion; faith is the response; wholeness is the result.

**T5.4.2 — Suffering and Affliction — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic in the person?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The evidence suggests that suffering reveals and occasions this characteristic rather than explicitly deepening or producing it. In Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28, the woman's twelve years of suffering have not produced faith by a described formation process; rather, the account presents her faith as already formed and operative when she encounters Jesus — the suffering is the occasion, not the pedagogy. In Mar 5:23, Jairus's desperate faith emerges at the extreme of affliction (daughter dying), suggesting that the acuteness of need functions to strip away intermediate resources and direct the person wholly toward Jesus — suffering as the clearing agent that reveals faith's necessity. Bartimaeus (Mar 10:52) has lived with blindness long enough that his cry is urgent and socially unashamed — his chronic affliction may have formed in him the persistence that characterises his faith. In Luk 8:50, the arrival of death-news sharpens the moment of faith-test acutely.
