**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — Across all seven characteristics, the root meanings converge on a single underlying logic: rescue from a dangerous constraint toward a condition of safety, wholeness, or freedom. The Hebrew roots cluster around two motifs: *yāšaʿ* (spatial rescue — movement from narrow to wide) and *kāpar* (covering or wiping — removal of an obstructing substance) and *pādāh* (transactional release — freedom obtained through payment or exchange). The Greek *sōzō* family inherits *yāšaʿ*'s spatial-rescue sense while integrating the covering and transactional registers. What the comparison surfaces is that no single root captures the whole: physical preservation, guilt-removal, and identity-release are semantically distinct operations that the biblical vocabulary holds together under the umbrella of "salvation" without flattening. The cluster's distinctive finding is that root meanings reveal salvation as an irreducibly multi-dimensional act — bodily, moral, relational, and eschatological — and that different characteristics foreground different root dimensions without contradiction. The *kāpar* roots (Char-4, Char-5) emphasize obstacle-removal from the divine-relational surface; the *pādāh* root (Char-7) emphasizes the transactional cost of release; the *yāšaʿ* root (Char-1, Char-3, Char-6) emphasizes the spatial-experiential state of having been brought out. Together they reveal that M38 Salvation's semantic architecture is not a synonym cluster but a faceted structure in which each root names a distinct pressure-point of the human condition that salvation addresses.

The grammar distributes responsibility across the salvation event according to who is doing what.

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The logical structures across the seven characteristics reveal three distinct argument-forms that recur and that together constitute the cluster's inferential architecture. The first is the *conditional-guarantee* form (Char-4, Char-5): if the prescribed mechanism is enacted, the outcome is certain — the logic is juridical and procedural. The second is the *retro-attributive* form (Char-3): the outcome has occurred, therefore the enabling cause (faith) is named — the logic is diagnostic and declaratory. The third is the *eschatological imminence* form (Char-1, Char-6): because the salvation is certain and/or near, present conduct or posture is warranted — the logic is prospective and motivational. Char-7 uniquely employs the *memory-ethics* form: past ransom received obligates analogous present mercy — the logic is analogical and moral. Char-2's argument is the *inference to the only available agent*: mortal incapacity exhausted → only Jesus can supply — the logic is crisis-driven and urgent. The cluster-level finding is that salvation is argued for differently depending on which dimension is in view: mechanism arguments dominate where the characteristic concerns divine-side action (Char-4, Char-5); attribution arguments dominate where the human faith-instrument is in view (Char-3); and motivational arguments dominate where the inner being must act in light of salvation's status (Char-1, Char-6, Char-7).

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge — what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The divergences across characteristics share a common structural feature: human science consistently locates causal agency in human processes or social dynamics, while the verse evidence consistently displaces agency onto God. In Char-3, health psychology attributes healing to the patient's inner conviction; the text attributes it to divine action through faith as instrument. In Char-4 and Char-5, moral psychology and ritual anthropology treat guilt-resolution and sacrifice as human techniques; the text insists divine command and divine acceptance are the operative mechanism. In Char-6, hope theory attributes sustained anticipation to the hopeful subject's agency and pathways-cognition; the text transfers agency to God's guardianship (1Pe 1:5). In Char-7, social identity theory locates group identity formation in mutual social categorization; the text locates it in unilateral divine act. In Char-1, conversion psychology frames transformation as internally-driven; the text frames the inner being as the *site* of external divine action, not its source. In Char-2, crisis psychology and attachment theory predict either rescue or despair; the text adds a third option (Joh 12:27, Heb 5:7) unavailable to the framework: anguish transformed into willed surrender. The cluster-level finding is that every human science framework illuminates the *shape* of what happens within the inner being while systematically misidentifying its *ultimate cause*. The divergences collectively reveal M38 Salvation's most theologically distinctive claim: salvation is received, not achieved.

**T7.1.4 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include terms that distinguish distinct aspects of this characteristic — disposition versus act, received versus given, condition versus quality?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics exhibit internal vocabulary differentiation between act and state, received and given, condition and ongoing quality — and the cluster-level comparison reveals that this differentiation is not incidental but structurally necessary. The act-state distinction appears in every characteristic: *sōzō* (verb) versus *sōtēria* (noun) in Char-1, Char-2, Char-3, Char-6; *kipper* (Piel verb) versus *sālach* (passive resultant) in Char-4; *kipper* (act) versus the clean/consecrated state in Char-5; *pādāh* (verbal act) versus *pĕdûyê* (passive participial identity) in Char-7. The cluster-level finding is that this consistent vocabulary differentiation encodes a theological anthropology: the inner being's relation to salvation is irreducibly *receptive* (it receives what is given), *temporal* (the act precedes the state), and *relational* (the given comes from a giver). No characteristic's vocabulary allows salvation to be self-generated, self-sustained, or atemporal.

**T7.1.5 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a term for the structural opposite or absence of this characteristic?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics surface vocabulary for their structural opposite, but the opposites cluster into two types: *permanent exclusion* and *present absence awaiting resolution*. The cluster-level finding is that the structural-opposite vocabulary encodes the full soteriological stakes of M38: some opposites name the remediable human situation that salvation addresses, while others name the irreversible consequence of salvation permanently refused — and the vocabulary itself distinguishes these two categories with precision.

**T7.1.8 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The OT-NT vocabulary relationships across all seven characteristics reveal a consistent pattern: strong structural continuity at the level of root meaning and inner-being posture, with significant developmental concentration and christological reidentification in the NT. The continuity is documented in every characteristic: *yāšaʿ → sōzō* (Char-1, Char-3, Char-6), *kāpar → hilaskomai/hilastērios* (Char-4), *pādāh → lytrooō/apolytrōsis* (Char-7). Char-5's gap (no NT verses in its evidence block) marks precisely this concentration — the NT treatment of the priestly machinery is its fulfillment-and-transcendence in Hebrews, which lies outside the supplied corpus. Char-4's Rom 3:25 places Christ in the structural position of the mercy seat. The cluster-level finding is that the OT-NT vocabulary relationship across M38 is best described as *typological intensification*: the NT does not replace the OT semantic field but concentrates its distributed institutional, historical, and promissory structure into a single christological referent, while the inner-being postures required for reception remain structurally identical across both Testaments.

**T7.1.9 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

**[CLUSTER]** E — The NT coinages and re-coinages across characteristics, compared together, reveal a consistent strategy: not the invention of entirely new vocabulary but the *semantic redirection* of existing terms to carry a christological referent that transforms their range without abandoning their prior meaning. *Sōtēr* (Char-1) was an established honorific for Hellenistic rulers; its application to Jesus as the *only* Savior (Act 4:12) transforms it from a comparative political title into an exclusive theological identity-claim. *Hilastērios* (Char-4) moves from adjectival modifier or cultic object (the kapporet) to a christological predicate — Christ is not the mechanism of propitiation but its personal embodiment, a semantic shift from object to subject. *Apekdechomai* (Char-6) intensifies existing reception-vocabulary with a compound prefix that encodes the full tension of eager anticipation across temporal delay. *Apolytrōsis* (Char-7) concentrates *pādāh*'s transactional register with an intensifying *apo-* prefix marking complete release. Chars 2, 3, and 5 do not surface genuine NT neologisms — their vocabulary is pre-existing Greek applied with concentrated or extended reference. The cluster-level finding is that NT vocabulary innovation in M38 is primarily *semantic* rather than *lexical* — the characteristic's NT development is expressed not in new words but in the redirection of existing words toward a Person, transforming what were general categories (rescue, covering, release, awaiting) into names for what one Person has done and is.
