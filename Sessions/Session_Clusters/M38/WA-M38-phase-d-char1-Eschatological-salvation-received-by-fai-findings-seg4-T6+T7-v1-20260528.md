**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary Greek terms are: **sōzō** (G4982, verb), **sōtēria** (G4991, noun), **sōtēr** (G4990, agent noun), **sōtērion** (G4992, adjectival noun), and the gift-of-salvation register: **dōrea** (G1431) and **dōrēma** (G1434). The root of sōzō is *sōs* ("safe, whole, unharmed"), revealing that salvation's root meaning is the restoration of wholeness and safety from a condition of danger, damage, or loss. This root conceives rescue not primarily as forensic verdict but as restored integrity — the rescued person is made *sound*. The Hebrew background lies in **yāšaʿ** (to save, deliver, give victory — root of Yeshua/Jesus) and **pālaṭ/nāṣal** (to deliver, rescue from peril). The *yāšaʿ* root names active intervention by a stronger party to bring one hemmed in by enemies or danger into open space and safety. The dōrea/dōrēma family derives from *didōmi* ("give"), specifying that what arrives is unrequested, unearned, and lavishly bestowed.

**T7.2.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The relationship reveals both deep continuity and significant development. *Continuity*: The NT sōzō/sōtēria vocabulary is the LXX's translation of Hebrew yāšaʿ and its derivatives (yešuʿā, yēšaʿ), preserving the root concept of rescue by a stronger deliverer from a state of danger or enemy-siege. Act 2:21 and Rom 10:13 ("everyone who calls on the Lord will be saved") quote Joel 2:32 (LXX), explicitly identifying the OT salvation-promise as the referent of NT eschatological rescue. Act 13:23 grounds Jesus as Savior in the Davidic covenant, and Rom 11:26 quotes Isa 59:20–21 for eschatological national rescue. *Development*: What in the OT is primarily national-historical deliverance (from Egypt, Exod 14; from Babylon; from enemies) is in the NT primarily *inner-being* and *eschatological* rescue — from sin, condemnation, and wrath. The structural form (divine rescue of the endangered through a saving agent) is identical; the nature of the danger and the mode of rescue deepen from political-historical to spiritual-eschatological.

**T7.3.3 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The term **sōtēr** as applied to Jesus Christ is the most significant development, though not a newly coined Greek word (it was used for Hellenistic rulers and divine beings). The gift vocabulary cluster — **dōrea** and **dōrēma** applied specifically to the salvation-event (VCG-03, particularly Rom 5:15–17) — also represents a distinct NT application: the deliberate use of pure-gift language (not earned, not owed, lavished) to specify the inner-being ground of salvation as utterly non-meritocratic. This gift-framing is not an OT vocabulary feature in the same explicit form.

**T7.1.4 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The full vocabulary arc — sōzō (verb), sōtēria (noun), sōtēr (agent), sōtērion (visible event), dōrea/dōrēma (gift ground), yāšaʿ (Hebrew root), epikaleō (supplication) — reveals a characteristic whose semantic range is simultaneously *wider* and *deeper* than any single English equivalent captures. *Wider*: it encompasses physical preservation (Mat 24:22), healing of the whole person (Luk 7:50), eschatological rescue from wrath (Rom 5:9), and cosmic completion (Rev 7:10) — all under one root. *Deeper*: the root meaning of sōs ("safe, whole") reveals that salvation is not primarily the negation of guilt but the *restoration of wholeness* — the rescued person is safe, sound, and intact. The gift vocabulary then specifies that this wholeness arrives as pure bestowal, not achievement. The agent-noun sōtēr centres the entire arc on a person (Jesus) rather than a process or a state: salvation is characterised not primarily as an abstract benefit but as the result of encounter with and reception of a specific Savior.

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The primary anchor is Eph 2:8: "For by grace you have been saved through faith, and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." The term **sesōsmenoi** (perfect passive participle of sōzō) functions as the *main predicate* of the sentence, describing the present resultant state of the addressees ("you have been saved and remain saved"). Grammatically, the perfect passive participle is significant: the perfect tense names a completed past action with ongoing present consequences (not merely "you were saved" but "you stand in the state of having been saved"), while the passive voice names God as the unstated agent. The phrase "by grace… through faith" provides the two prepositional frames: *grace* names the sphere or ground, *faith* names the instrument or channel. The closing clause ("not of yourselves; it is the gift of God") functions as an *explicit negation of self-origination*, which serves the argument of Eph 2:1–10 as a whole: dead persons do not raise themselves; God raises them.

**T7.3.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence spans six literary forms. (1) *Epistle* (the majority — Eph 2:8; Rom 5:9–17; 1Cor 1:18; 2Cor 7:10): epistolary argument requires attention to the sustained logical case being made, the audience being addressed, and the polemical context (e.g., Eph 2 argues against self-salvation; Rom 5 argues for security through Christ's work). (2) *Narrative/Gospel* (Mat 1:21; Luk 7:50; Joh 3:17; Act 4:12): requires attention to speech-act and narrative setting — who is speaking, to whom, in what scene — since salvation-declarations in narratives are often performative or confessional rather than didactic. (3) *Apocalyptic* (Rev 7:10; Rev 12:10; Rev 19:1): requires attention to visionary register and doxological function — these verses do not argue for salvation but *declare* it in its completed form. (4) *Prophecy* (Act 2:21 citing Joel; Rom 11:26 citing Isaiah): requires attention to intertextual fulfillment logic. The epistle form dominates and requires sequential argument-tracing; the apocalyptic form requires recognition that doxology is itself a form of theological statement.

**T7.1.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Three key arguments are traceable. (1) *Eph 2:8 argument* (VCG-02 anchor): P1: Humans were dead in trespasses (v.1–3). P2: Dead persons cannot contribute to their own rescue. P3: God made them alive and saved them by grace. C: Therefore salvation is entirely gift, received through faith, not self-originated (v.8–9). (2) *Rom 5:9–10 argument* (VCG-03 anchor): P1: Justification was achieved through Christ's blood while we were enemies. P2: If God accomplished the more difficult work (justification of enemies), he will certainly accomplish the less difficult work (final salvation of the reconciled). C: Final eschatological salvation from wrath is assured by the logic of divine consistency. This is an *a fortiori* argument. (3) *1Cor 1:18 argument* (VCG-04 anchor): P1: Human wisdom failed to reach God. P2: God chose what seemed foolish (preached message) to save believers. C: The inner mechanism of salvation is not wisdom but faith-reception of proclaimed word. The structural logic in all three is *inversion*: human impossibility → divine act → inner transformation.

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Multiple contextual settings carry different portions of the evidence. (1) *Judicial/forensic*: Rom 5:9–10 (justified → saved from wrath), Rom 5:16 (condemnation → justification-gift), Rev 12:10 (accuser defeated → salvation declared). These reveal salvation as a verdict-reversal: condemnation is overturned and the inner being is declared safe. (2) *Covenantal*: Act 13:23 (Davidic covenant → Jesus as Savior), Rom 11:26 (prophetic promise → all Israel saved), Joh 4:22 ("salvation is from the Jews"). These reveal salvation as covenant-fulfillment: the inner-being reception of rescue is simultaneously reception of promised covenant-blessing. (3) *Eschatological*: Mat 10:22; Mat 24:13; 1Th 5:9; Rev 7:10. These reveal salvation as oriented toward final completion — the inner being lives now in an in-between time, enduring toward a realized rescue that is both present and approaching. (4) *Communal/liturgical*: Rev 7:10 and 19:1 (heavenly liturgy), Act 2:47 (community of the saved). These reveal salvation as a corporate event producing a worshipping community, not merely isolated individual experience.

**T7.1.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Eph 2:8 ("For by grace you have been saved through faith, and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God") is the strongest candidate for primary anchor, and the evidence base designates it as such in VCG-02. It is the most syntactically complete statement of the characteristic's essential structure: (a) the ground — grace; (b) the instrument — faith; (c) the state — "have been saved" (perfect passive); (d) the exclusion — "not your own doing"; (e) the designation — "gift of God." No other single verse holds all five of these elements simultaneously. Mat 1:21 names the agent and mission. Rom 5:9–10 names the basis and assurance. 1Cor 1:18 names the power and the community. But Eph 2:8 alone names the ground, the instrument, the state, the exclusion, and the designation in a single statement, making it the definitional anchor for the inner-being logic of this characteristic.

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Eph 2:8 uniquely reveals the *simultaneous triple negation* that defines salvation's inner-being structure: it is not of yourself, not of works, not self-originating — and this triple exclusion is stated *within the same clause* as the positive affirmation of grace and faith. No other verse in the evidence base holds this simultaneous positive-and-negative structure with equal explicitness. Mat 1:21 names the mission without naming the mechanism. Rom 5:9 names the ground (blood) without naming the inner-being reception instrument. Tit 3:5 names the exclusion of works without naming faith as the reception instrument. 1Cor 1:21 names faith-reception without specifying the gift-character of salvation. Only Eph 2:8 reveals that the inner-being act of faith is itself not the originating cause — the verse protects faith from becoming a human contribution by naming both grace *and* faith as gift-given, leaving the inner being with nothing to supply except openness to what God gives. This is the verse's unique contribution: it prevents faith from functioning as a hidden merit-system.

**T7.1.7 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two frameworks have meaningful but asymmetric value. (1) *Psychology of conversion and transformation* — particularly William James's analysis in *The Varieties of Religious Experience* and contemporary work on surrender, crisis-induced transformation, and the shift from self-reliance to trust — is the most useful single framework. The evidence base repeatedly narrates the inner-being movement from helplessness (Act 16:30 jailer's panic; Mat 19:25 disciples' astonishment; Luk 18:26 "who can be saved?") to trust-and-reception, which maps closely onto the psychology of crisis-conversion: the self reaches the limit of its own resources and undergoes a reorientation of dependency. (2) *Gift theory* (anthropological/sociological, drawing on Marcel Mauss's *The Gift* and its successors) is the second most useful framework, given the dōrea/dōrēma vocabulary cluster in VCG-03. Gift theory attends to the social dynamics of giving, receiving, and the asymmetry of the donor-recipient relationship, which illuminates the inner-being posture of pure reception that VCG-03 and Eph 2:8 require.

**T7.1.8 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The psychology-of-conversion framework illuminates VCG-07 (human impossibility) most directly. The disciples' astonishment ("who then can be saved?" Mat 19:25; Mar 10:26; Luk 18:26) and the jailer's awakened panic (Act 16:30) represent the inner-being threshold identified by conversion psychology as the *breaking point of self-reliance*: the moment when the self discovers its own resources are insufficient for the task at hand. Conversion psychology notes that transformation typically cannot begin until this threshold is crossed — the self must release its prior framework of self-sufficiency before a new orientation (trust/reception) can be structurally adopted. This makes the "human impossibility" material in VCG-07 not merely a theological claim but an accurate inner-being phenomenology: the question "who can be saved?" is the psychological entry-point that clears the way for "with God all things are possible" to function as a genuine reorientation rather than a religious formula. Gift theory illuminates VCG-03 by naming the irreversibility of gift-receipt: once a gift is received without condition, the recipient's relational standing is transformed.

**T7.1.9 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The divergence is significant in two areas. (1) *Conversion psychology typically frames transformation as the outcome of internal psychological dynamics* — latent tensions, unconscious processes, or developmental pressure reaching resolution. The verse evidence, by contrast, consistently frames salvation as initiated *from outside the inner being entirely*: God acts on the dead (Eph 2:5), the word is implanted (Jam 1:21), the Spirit is poured out (Act 2:38). The inner-being is not the source of its own transformation; it is the *site* where external divine action takes effect. Conversion psychology cannot account for this asymmetry without importing theological premises it does not natively hold. (2) *Gift theory* typically identifies an implicit obligation in gift-receipt: the receiver is placed under social pressure to reciprocate. Rom 5:15–17 and Eph 2:8–9 explicitly refuse this dynamic: the divine gift of salvation creates no debt, imposes no counter-gift obligation, and is specifically protected from reciprocal obligation by the insistence that it is "not of works." The divergence reveals that the Biblical gift-structure is *asymmetric in a direction gift theory does not normally occupy*: purely gratuitous, creating grateful love rather than social debt.

**T7.1.10 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The psychology-of-conversion framework surfaces one under-addressed aspect: the *long-term integration of salvation into identity over time*. Conversion psychology distinguishes the initial conversion experience from the subsequent process of identity consolidation — the converted self must restructure its autobiographical narrative, its social networks, and its habitual patterns to cohere with the new inner orientation. The verse evidence covers the initial reception (VCG-02), ongoing perseverance (VCG-05), and eschatological completion (VCG-10) but has relatively sparse coverage of the intermediate process by which the saved self incorporates salvation into stable identity between initial faith and final completion. Phili 2:12 ("work out your own salvation") and 2Pe 3:18 ("grow in grace and knowledge") gesture toward this, but the evidence base does not extensively map the developmental middle. This absence suggests that a further investigation of sanctification and identity-formation verses adjacent to the salvation cluster would enrich the picture of how salvation operates as an ongoing inner-being reality rather than only a punctiliar event or a distant eschatological horizon.
