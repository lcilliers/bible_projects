**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The corpus reveals God as fundamentally a covering, wrath-restraining, compassion-driven being whose inner nature is oriented toward the removal of human guilt rather than its permanent retention. Psa 78:38 describes God as repeatedly atoning for iniquity out of compassion and restraining wrath rather than destroying — the inner-being movement of atonement-giving flows from divine mercy as its source. Psa 65:3 shows God as the one who personally atones for overwhelming transgressions, acting when the human person is helpless. 1Jo 4:10 (M38-D-VCG-04) makes the structural claim explicit: the origin of propitiation is not human initiative but God's prior love — he is the sender of the sin-covering sacrifice. Lev 17:11 (M38-D-VCG-02) reveals that God himself provides the blood-mechanism for atonement — "I have given it for you on the altar." This characteristic therefore images a God whose holiness does not merely condemn but actively provides the means of its own satisfaction, and whose compassion drives him toward the covering of guilt as a core divine act.

**T1.1.1 — Name and Naming — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic is named "Conscience cleansed through atonement received." The name is structurally composite and signals several things about its essential nature. First, the primary inner-being locus is the conscience — not the intellect, will, or emotion as such, but the moral-relational faculty that registers guilt and standing before God. Second, the operative verb is "cleansed" — a passive transformation verb, indicating that the conscience does not cleanse itself but is cleansed by an act done to or for it. Third, "through atonement" names the mechanism — the characteristic is not simply forgiveness-as-abstract-declaration but is specifically mediated through the atonement structure (sacrifice, substitution, propitiation). Fourth, "received" confirms the directional asymmetry: the person is recipient, not agent, of the atonement. The name therefore signals at the definitional level: this is a passively-received, mechanism-mediated, conscience-located transformation from guilt-laden to cleared — and it is structurally dependent on an external atoning act.

**T1.4.1 — Modes of Operation — In what distinct ways does this characteristic operate within the inner person?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The verse evidence supports at least five distinct modes of operation within the inner person:

1. **Forensic-declarative mode** (M38-D-VCG-01): The priest declares forgiveness as the direct outcome of sacrifice — guilt is objectively removed, and the inner person receives a declared standing-change. Lev 4:20, 4:26, 5:18 all follow the pattern: act → pronouncement → inner state altered.

2. **Substitutionary-identification mode** (M38-D-VCG-02): Through hand-laying (Lev 1:4), the person's inner being is linked to the sacrifice — the covering comes not through declaration alone but through personal identification with the substituted life. The conscience is cleared because another life has been accepted in the person's place.

3. **Ransom-sustaining mode** (M38-D-VCG-03): Atonement operates as ongoing relational coverage — keeping the person "remembered" before God (Exo 30:16), sustaining the person in divine awareness and protection rather than resolving a discrete sin-event.

4. **Gift-reception mode** (M38-D-VCG-04): God himself is the agent — the inner person receives atonement as pure gift, without initiating or completing the mechanism (Psa 65:3, Psa 78:38, 1Jo 4:10). The conscience is cleared by divine action alone.

5. **Transformative-shame mode** (M38-D-VCG-07): Eze 16:63 uniquely shows that received atonement operates not only to clear but to humble and silence — the inner person is left in wordless contrition, shame, and remembered guilt coexisting with forgiveness. This is a secondary mode where the clearing itself becomes a transformative moral event.

The most consistent immediate inner-being response across the full corpus is transition from burdened to relieved — guilt-laden to forgiven — but the texture of that relief ranges from shame-mixed-with-gratitude (Eze 16:63) to readiness-for-action (Isa 6:7) to dependent-contrite-expectation (Luk 18:13).

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The primary Hebrew term is H3722B ka.phar (the atoned-state use), and the primary Greek terms are G2433 hilaskomai (verb), G2434 hilasmos (noun), G2435 hilastērios (noun), and G2436 hileōs (adjective). At the definitional level, before deeper lexical work:

- **Ka.phar** carries the basic sense of covering — something placed over guilt so that it is no longer exposed to divine view or wrath. The inner-being state named is one of covered-ness: the person is no longer naked before judgment.
- **Hilaskomai** is to propitiate or make propitious — to effect a change in the standing between a holy party and a guilty party, removing the obstacle (guilt/wrath). The inner-being register is relational-positional: the person's standing has shifted.
- **Hilasmos** names the propitiation as a thing — the sin-covering provision that produces the changed standing. Christ himself is the hilasmos (1Jo 2:2; 1Jo 4:10).
- **Hilastērios** names the place or person as the mercy-seat/propitiation-locus — Christ set forth as the place where wrath meets blood and is satisfied (Rom 3:25).
- **Hileōs** is the adjectival state — God being propitious/merciful, favorably disposed toward rather than opposed (Heb 8:12).

Together these terms define the characteristic as: covered, propitiated, standing in a divinely-shifted relational position — guilt hidden, wrath averted, God favorably disposed. The definitional core is relational-positional covering.

**T1.2.2 — Kind — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it appear to have constituent elements at first encounter?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic is composite rather than simple at first encounter. The characteristic is therefore structurally composite: it involves (1) a sin-burdened inner state, (2) an external atoning act, (3) an inner act of reception (faith, contrition, identification), and (4) the resulting inner state of cleared conscience. These are not alternative descriptions but constituent phases of a single phenomenon.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes and resists:

1. **Self-generated moral improvement as a substitute for atonement**: The Levitical corpus makes clear that specific sacrificial acts are required — personal effort, good intentions, or moral reform alone do not produce the inner state of guilt-removed. Isa 22:14 and 1Sa 3:14 (M38-D-VCG-06) show that even religious activity (sacrifice and offering) is insufficient where the atonement-pathway is closed — much less unaided moral effort.

2. **Persistent self-accusation after atonement received**: Heb 8:12's promise that sins will be "remembered no more" structurally resists ongoing self-condemnation in the person who has genuinely received atonement — the conscience-cleared state excludes continued guilt-bearing for covered sin.

3. **Pride and self-sufficiency in approach**: Luk 18:13 (M38-D-VCG-05) implicitly excludes the Pharisaic self-justifying posture (the immediate narrative foil) — the characteristic resists approaching the atonement-mechanism from a position of self-righteousness.

4. **Defiance and hardened indifference**: Isa 47:11 (M38-D-VCG-06) implies that the defiant city's proud self-reliance is precisely what puts atonement out of reach — the characteristic resists the inner posture of "I have no need of covering."

5. **Ritual compliance without sincere heart-seeking**: 2Ch 30:18's positive case (God covers sincere but ritually irregular seekers) implies the exclusion of its negative: ritual compliance without sincere heart-orientation does not guarantee genuine reception.

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Yes, the corpus evidence supports context- and level-dependent variation in operation:

**By context (individual vs. corporate)**: M38-D-VCG-01 operates primarily at the level of individual sin-event resolution (Lev 4:20–6:7 — named individuals, specific offences, personal forgiveness). M38-D-VCG-03 operates at corporate and communal levels: Exo 30:15–16 covers all Israel through the ransom-levy; Deu 21:8 (VCG-07) addresses community blood-guilt through intercession; 2Ch 30:18 covers an entire worshipping assembly through royal intercession.

**By direction (human-initiated vs. divine-initiated)**: In M38-D-VCG-01 and VCG-02, the mechanism is human-initiated — the offerer brings the sacrifice, the priest performs the act. **T1.3.3 — Boundary — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The verse evidence permits the following boundary demarcations:

**T1.4.3 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

The verb is the hinge of the entire sentence — it is what the priest *does* (subject: the priest; verb: kipper; object: "for them/on account of the sin"), and the consequence clause ("they shall be forgiven") is grammatically subordinated to it as result. In the argument of Lev 4 as a whole, this verse functions as the **programmatic statement** of the sin-offering's purpose: after several verses establishing the correct procedure, v.20 delivers the guaranteed outcome. The kipper-verb thus carries the entire weight of the chapter's inner-being promise — it is the mechanism that activates the forgiveness guarantee.

For the NT anchor **Rom 3:25**, ἱλαστήριον (hilastērios) functions as a **predicate accusative or object complement** in the clause "whom God set forth as a propitiation through his blood, received through faith." It names what Christ *is* in his atoning function — the term carries Christological identification and functional definition simultaneously, making it the argumentative centre of Paul's justification argument in Rom 3:21–26.

**T7.3.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence base spans multiple literary forms, each requiring distinct interpretive handling:

(1) **Legal/priestly instruction (Lev 4–6, 17, 19; Exo 29–30; Num 35; Deu 21)**: VCG-01, VCG-02, VCG-03 — The dominant form. Responsible interpretation requires reading these as prescriptive legal texts establishing the *conditions* under which atonement operates, not descriptive narratives or metaphorical statements. The repetitive structure (offense + procedure + outcome) is intentional and juridical; each verse's forgiveness-promise is conditional on the specified sacrificial act.

(2) **Psalm/lament and praise (Psa 65, 78, 79)**: VCG-04 — Poetic register requires attention to the affective and communal dimensions of atonement language that legal texts omit. Psa 78:38's portrayal of God's compassionate atonement is theological reflection on covenant history, not legal instruction; its inner-being register is doxological rather than prescriptive.

(3) **Prophecy (Isa 6, 22, 27, 47; Eze 16; Dan 9)**: VCG-04, VCG-06, VCG-07 — Prophetic texts require attention to the rhetorical situation (judgment, restoration, intercession) which shapes the atonement language. Isa 22:14's atonement-withheld is a judgment oracle; Eze 16:63's atonement-producing-shame is a restoration promise.

(4) **Epistle (Rom 3:25; Heb 2:17; 8:12; 1Jo 2:2; 4:10)**: VCG-05 — Argumentative/doctrinal form; interpretation requires following the logical argument of which the propitiation-term is a part.

(5) **Gospel parable (Luk 18:13)**: VCG-05 — Narrative-illustrative form; the hilaskomai-plea is embedded in a parable contrasting two inner-being postures, requiring that the term be read within the parable's rhetorical purpose.

**T7.1.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Three argumentative structures are identifiable:

(1) **Levitical priestly argument (VCG-01)**: Structure: *Premise 1*: This person has sinned and requires covering; *Premise 2*: God has designated the specific sacrificial procedure for this type of sin; *Premise 3*: The priest enacts the designated procedure on behalf of the offerer; *Conclusion*: The offerer is forgiven. This is a *conditional guarantee* structure — the outcome is certain given the premises. The logical force is judicial: compliance with prescribed mechanism yields guaranteed outcome.

(2) **Pauline propitiation argument (Rom 3:21–26)**: *Premise 1*: All have sinned and are under condemnation (3:23); *Premise 2*: God set forth Christ as hilastērios through his blood (3:25); *Premise 3*: This is received by faith (3:25); *Conclusion*: Former sins are passed over and God is shown to be both just and justifier (3:25–26). The logical structure is *satisfaction-of-divine-justice* through substitutionary provision.

(3) **Johannine love-atonement argument (1Jo 4:7–10)**: *Premise 1*: God is love (4:8); *Premise 2*: God's love was expressed in sending his Son as hilasmos (4:10); *Premise 3*: This was prior to any human love-initiative (4:10b); *Conclusion*: We are to love because God first loved us (4:11). The logical structure is *grace-prior-to-response*, placing conscience-cleansing atonement as the *ground of human ethical life* rather than its reward.

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence base distributes across five settings, each revealing a distinct dimension:

(1) **Liturgical setting (VCG-01, VCG-02; Lev 1–6, 17, 19)**: The dominant setting — the sacrificial cult as the formal mechanism for conscience-cleansing. This reveals that atonement is *institutionally embedded* — not a purely private transaction but a publicly-enacted, ritually-structured event. The inner-being conscience-change is secured through participation in communal liturgical life.

(2) **Judicial setting (1Sa 3:14; Isa 22:14; Num 35:33; VCG-06)**: The atonement-refused verses operate in a judicial frame — God as judge declaring guilt permanent or punishment decreed. This reveals that Char-4 operates against a judicial background: conscience-cleansing is the relief of a judicial verdict, not merely the soothing of a psychological burden.

(3) **Covenantal setting (Exo 30:15–16; Deu 21:8; 2Ch 30:18; Heb 8:12)**: Atonement as the maintenance of the covenant relationship. Heb 8:12 ("I will be merciful toward their iniquities … I will remember their sins no more") is the New Covenant's atonement-promise, revealing that conscience-cleansing is covenantally guaranteed and definitive.

(4) **Eschatological setting (Dan 9:24; Eze 16:63)**: Atonement as cosmic culmination — the once-for-all resolution of accumulated sin in the final era. This reveals that Char-4 is not only a present inner-being event but the goal of redemptive history.

(5) **Communal/national setting (Psa 78:38, 79:9; Deu 21:8; VCG-03)**: Atonement as corporate-conscience event, not only individual. Israel's collective guilt can be collectively covered, revealing that the inner-being dimension extends beyond individual psychology to corporate identity.

**T7.1.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Two verses compete for the role of primary anchor, functioning at different levels:

(1) **Lev 4:20** (vc_id=29586, designated ANCHOR in VCG-01): This verse most fully expresses the *structural mechanism* of the characteristic in its OT form — it states the priest-enacts-ka.phar → sinner-is-forgiven structure in its simplest, most programmatic form, applicable to all the VCG-01 variants. As the foundational statement of priestly atonement-to-forgiveness, it anchors the entire sacrificial-atonement complex.

(2) **Rom 3:25** (vc_id=29069, designated ANCHOR in VCG-05): This verse most fully expresses the characteristic's *NT christological fulfilment* — Christ as hilastērios through blood, received by faith, resulting in the passing-over of sins. It contains all the essential elements: the mechanism (hilastērios through blood), the receptor condition (faith), the scope (former sins), and the theological rationale (divine justice and justification).

If a single primary anchor must be named, **Rom 3:25** carries the fullest integration of the characteristic's essential elements as the NT resolution of the entire OT ka.phar complex: it identifies the mechanism's ultimate referent (Christ), the reception condition (faith), and the inner-being outcome (sins passed over, standing before a just God established). However, Lev 4:20 remains the structural origin-anchor of the OT sacrificial complex that Rom 3:25 fulfils.

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — **Rom 3:25** reveals what no other verse in the corpus makes explicit: the **simultaneous justification of God and the sinner** as the dual outcome of propitiation. The verse states: "to show God's righteousness … so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus" (3:26). This reveals that conscience-cleansing atonement is not merely a benefit to the sinner's inner being — it is the means by which *God's own character* (his justice) is vindicated in the act of forgiving. No OT ka.phar verse makes this theological argument; they present forgiveness as God's gift but not as the resolution of a tension within God's own nature between justice and mercy. Rom 3:25 alone reveals that the propitiation resolves that tension: God is shown to be both *just* (sin genuinely punished) and *justifier* (sinner genuinely freed). The inner-being conscience-cleansing of the receiver thus rests on a theologically complete foundation that only this verse articulates.

**Lev 4:20** (the OT anchor) reveals what no other verse quite captures: the *guaranteed, outcome-certain* structure of the priestly mechanism. The declarative "they shall be forgiven" (nisgal lahem) is not a conditional hope but a juridical guarantee — when the priest performs ka.phar correctly, forgiveness is certain. This certainty-structure is present in all VCG-01 verses but most clearly stated here as the programmatic principle.

**T7.1.7 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The most useful framework is **moral psychology**, specifically the contemporary psychology of *moral emotions* and *guilt-resolution*. Char-4 is fundamentally concerned with the inner-being state produced when guilt — an aversive moral emotion tied to specific transgression — is *objectively removed* through an act external to the self. Moral psychology's distinction between **guilt** (specific act-focused self-condemnation), **shame** (self-focused identity-condemnation), and **conscience** (the evaluative inner faculty) directly illuminates the characteristic's architecture. The verse evidence engages all three: guilt as the presenting state (VCG-01's sin-burdened offerers); shame as the post-atonement response (Eze 16:63, Luk 18:13); conscience as the cleansed faculty (the characteristic's definitional term).

Second most useful is **cultural anthropology of sacrifice**, particularly theories of substitutionary exchange (Rene Girard's mimetic theory, though contested, and older Hubert-Mauss functionalism). These frameworks illuminate why the substitutionary mechanism (Lev 1:4's hand-laying, Lev 17:11's blood-for-soul) functions as an inner-being resolution rather than merely a physical transaction.

Third: **relational/attachment psychology** — the atonement-received state is fundamentally relational (restored standing before God), making attachment theory's account of rupture-and-repair in secure relationships relevant to VCG-03 and VCG-07.

**T7.1.8 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Moral psychology's guilt-resolution framework illuminates Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) in a way that the verse alone does not fully explain. The verse depicts shame *persisting* after atonement has been fully granted: "you will be ashamed and confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you." Moral psychology distinguishes between **guilt-relief** (which ends when the transgression is forgiven) and **shame** (which can deepen when forgiveness is received precisely because the severity of what was forgiven becomes clearer). The verse evidences this psychological phenomenon — the conscience-cleansed state does not erase the memory or shame of the original transgression; it transforms it from burden-of-condemnation into humbled-gratitude. Moral psychology names this as the *integration of forgiven guilt into identity* — a secondary inner-being process distinct from the initial guilt-relief. Without this framework, the verse appears paradoxical (why is shame intensified by forgiveness?); with it, the verse reveals a psychologically coherent secondary phase of Char-4's inner-being movement.

Similarly, the ransom-equality principle in Exo 30:15–16 (VCG-03) is illuminated by **social psychology of fairness**: every soul requiring identical ransom regardless of status creates a moral-psychological equalizer — the rich person's conscience is no more securely covered than the poor person's, and vice versa. This equality-before-atonement dynamic reveals Char-4 as a social-equalizer in the inner-being landscape as well as a personal event.

**T7.1.9 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The most significant divergence occurs at the point of **objective versus subjective guilt resolution**. Contemporary moral psychology and psychotherapy operate primarily with *subjective* guilt — the felt sense of wrongdoing — and treat its resolution as primarily a matter of cognitive reframing, relational repair, or therapeutic processing. The verse evidence in VCG-01 through VCG-05 consistently treats guilt as *objective* — standing before God as a real condition requiring an objective external act for its removal, regardless of the offender's felt state. Lev 5:17–18 explicitly addresses *unintentional* sins where the person did not even know they were guilty (Lev 5:18: "he did not know it, and he is guilty") — the guilt is real and requires atonement irrespective of subjective awareness.

This divergence reveals that Char-4 is not primarily a psychological phenomenon in the modern therapeutic sense. The inner-being change is not produced by the person coming to *feel* forgiven or restructuring their self-narrative — it is produced by an *objective* event (the atoning act) that changes the person's *actual standing* before God. The psychological sensation of relief or shame may or may not accompany this, but Char-4's core is the *status-change*, not the feeling-change. Contemporary frameworks that reduce guilt to subjective experience cannot account for the unintentional-guilt of Lev 5:18 or the objectivity of atonement as a mechanism at all.

**T7.1.10 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Moral psychology surfaces one aspect that the verse evidence addresses only partially: **the persistence of the cleansed-conscience state over time**. The verse evidence establishes conscience-cleansing as an event (moment of atonement and forgiveness) and gives glimpses of its lasting effect (Heb 8:12: "I will remember their sins no more"; 1Jo 2:2: ongoing advocate). However, moral psychology would ask about *recurrence of guilt* — the well-documented phenomenon where guilt returns for already-forgiven transgressions, requiring either repeated reassurance or a deepening of the forgiveness event's internalization. The repeated ka.phar events of VCG-01 (covering different sins and different occasions) may implicitly address this through the provision of an ongoing ritual mechanism, but the corpus does not directly address the inner-being dynamics of *holding onto* the cleared-conscience state.

This absence *may* warrant further verse investigation into texts addressing assurance of forgiveness, the once-for-all finality of Christ's atonement (Heb 10:1–18 would be a relevant domain), and the pastoral theology of conscience-management in an already-atoned state. These are not represented in the current 39-verse corpus and their absence is a genuine gap.
