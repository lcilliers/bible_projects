**T0.1.1 — Divine Nature Reflected — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The verse evidence reveals God as a rescuing, responsive deity who draws near to the endangered creature. In Psa 69:18 (MB-VCG-01), the plea is explicitly for God to "draw near" and "ransom the soul" — the psalmist's distress presupposes a God whose proximity is saving and whose character is that of a redeemer who responds to cry. In Mat 8:25 and Mat 14:30 (MB-VCG-01), the disciples' and Peter's cries are answered: Jesus rebukes the storm and extends his hand, imaging a God who intervenes in material, physical peril. In Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03), the Father "hears" the anguished petition of the Son — a God who is not deaf to creaturely distress even at its most extreme. Across MB-VCG-04, the delivering character of God extends corporately: Luk 1:71 describes God as the one who rescues "from enemies and those who hate," while Heb 11:7 shows that reverent faith directed toward God produces physical household deliverance. The characteristic thus images a God who is present to mortal threat, responsive to desperate cry, and capable of intervening in the physical order — a God whose saving character is not confined to the eschatological but extends into the mortal emergency of creaturely existence.

**T0.2.1 — Created Purpose — What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the person to be, do, or become?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The verse evidence suggests that the fear-faith dynamic operative in physical mortal threat equips the human person to remain in relational orientation toward God even when the creaturely will is overwhelmed. In Mat 14:30 (MB-VCG-01), Peter's cry "Lord, save me" — however panic-driven — is a relational act: the person in extremis does not turn inward or collapse into mute despair but turns outward to the rescuer. In Heb 11:7 (MB-VCG-04), Noah's sustained reverent faith under threat of destruction equips him to act protectively for those under his care. The characteristic appears to serve the purpose of keeping the human person anchored to divine dependence precisely when self-sufficiency fails most completely — equipping the creature to be a dependent being who exercises will under duress by crying, petitioning, and trusting rather than by self-rescue. Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05) marks the limit case: when this relational-faith orientation collapses entirely, what remains is pure despair, suggesting the characteristic's purpose is to prevent that extinction of hope.

In Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03), the Son's anguished prayer models the image from the inside: even the one in whom the image is most perfectly instantiated approaches the Father with urgent petition and reverence. **T0.1.2 — Divine Nature Reflected — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Scripture does not attribute the fear-faith dynamic or the panic-cry to God directly; however, the rescue side of the characteristic — the capacity and willingness to deliver from mortal danger — is explicitly and repeatedly attributed to God in this evidence. Psa 69:18 (MB-VCG-01) addresses God as the one who ransoms and redeems; Luk 1:71 (MB-VCG-04) attributes the act of rescue from enemies to God's covenantal faithfulness. Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03) attributes the hearing of the anguished petition specifically to the Father. The Father is the one who hears and can save — meaning the receiving end of the cry is divine. This asymmetry is significant: God does not cry out in mortal terror, but God is the one to whom such cries are properly directed and by whom physical deliverance is effected. The human experience of terrified petition thus reflects not God's inner life but God's relational posture — God as rescuer. The significance for the human person is that the capacity to cry out in extremis is the creature's analogue to divine initiative in salvation: the human exercises the only act available (desperate petition), which mirrors in reverse the divine act of rescue.

The storm-cry passages (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30, MB-VCG-01) occur in a world subject to mortal physical threat. The crucifixion passages (MB-VCG-02, 03) are by definition post-fall in their context. No verse in this sub-group explicitly addresses the pre-fall state or attributes this characteristic to original creation design. The most that can be said from the evidence is that the characteristic as instantiated here is a response to the fallen condition of creaturely vulnerability to mortal danger — but the underlying capacity (petition, dependence, reverent faith under crisis) may belong to created nature as such. The evidence does not permit confident resolution of whether this capacity would exist in an unfallen world; the question registers as partially evidenced.

**T1.2.1 — Kind — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be — an act, a disposition, a condition, a quality, or something else?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic appears to be a dynamic inner-being condition — not a stable disposition but a transient state activated by acute threat. The evidence across MB-VCG-01 through MB-VCG-05 consistently presents the inner being in movement: terror → desperate faith-cry → rescue (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30); anguish → petition → surrender (Joh 12:27; Heb 5:7); reverent faith → sustained obedient action → household deliverance (Heb 11:7); hope → hope's extinction → despair (Act 27:20). The characteristic is not a quality one possesses continuously but a condition the inner being enters under specific circumstances of mortal threat. It has the structure of a crisis-state: provoked by external danger, involving affective, volitional, and relational faculties simultaneously, and resolving (or failing to resolve) through either rescue received or hope lost. The crucifixion mockery passages (MB-VCG-02) are the anomaly: the inner-being phenomenon there is primarily ironic observation rather than personal crisis-state, which is why those verses sit somewhat uncomfortably in the sub-group's phenomenological core.

**T1.3.1 — Boundary — What is the structural opposite of this characteristic — the inner-being reality that stands against or excludes it?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The structural opposite of this characteristic, as evidenced by the corpus, is the inner-being state of self-sufficiency under threat — the confidence that no cry toward an external rescuer is needed or possible. This takes two distinct forms in the evidence. First, faithless demand: Luk 23:39 (MB-VCG-02) presents the criminal demanding rescue without petition, faith, or surrender — treating the would-be rescuer as an instrument rather than approaching him in dependent relation. Second, extinct hope: Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05) presents the state in which even the cry is given up — not defiant self-sufficiency but a collapse into despair that no longer reaches outward. Both modes exclude the characteristic because the faith-cry is absent: the inner being has either never formed the relational dependency (faithless demand) or has abandoned it (hope's extinction). The structural opposite is therefore: inner closure to the possibility of received rescue, whether through presumptuous demand or through despairing resignation.

**T1.7.1 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?**

**T1.8.1 — Dimension Classification — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic from the programme's dimension vocabulary?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic is the affective-volitional dimension: it is constitutively an experience of fear, terror, or despair (affective) that drives an act of will (the cry, petition, or surrender). The two components are inseparable in the evidence: the cry of Mat 14:30 is simultaneously an emotional registration of panic and a volitional act of faith-petition; the anguish of Heb 5:7 is simultaneously emotional distress and disciplined reverent prayer. Secondary dimensions include the relational dimension (the cry is directed toward a person) and the somatic dimension (the body is physically imperilled), but these are conditions and contexts rather than the primary inner-being locus.

**T1.1.2 — Name and Naming — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level — before deeper lexical analysis begins?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary Greek term is G4982 sōzō (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30; Mat 27:40; Mat 27:49; Mar 15:30; Mar 15:31; Luk 23:35; Luk 23:37; Luk 23:39; Joh 12:27; Act 27:20; Heb 5:7) and G4991 soteria (Luk 1:71; Heb 11:7). The Hebrew term is H6299 padah (Psa 69:18). At the definitional level before deeper analysis: sōzō carries at its root the sense of making safe, preserving from destruction, bringing through to safety — it is a transitive rescue verb whose object is a person in danger of perishing. The word operates on a spectrum from physical to spiritual, but in all its instances it implies that the person/thing being saved would otherwise be lost. Soteria is the noun form — salvation, deliverance, safety as a state achieved. Padah (H6299) means to ransom, to redeem by paying a price or by acting to release — it carries the sense of rescue through decisive intervention, often in contexts of redemption from slavery or enemy. The common definitional thread is: a being in genuine danger of destruction, and an external agent who intervenes to bring that being to safety. The terms are all transitive — rescue always has a subject (rescuer), object (endangered one), and a situation of threat. They define the characteristic from the outside (what is happening to the person) before revealing anything about the inner-being state.

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic is evidently composite in structure at first encounter. The characteristic is therefore structurally composite: it has an affective element (fear, terror, anguish, despair), a volitional element (cry, petition, surrender, or hopeless closure), a relational element (direction toward rescuer or absence thereof), and a resolution element (rescue received, rescue withheld, or hope lost). These elements co-occur in varying configurations across the sub-group.

**T1.3.2 — Boundary — What does this characteristic explicitly exclude or resist?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic explicitly excludes or resists three inner-being states evidenced in the corpus: (1) Faithless demand (Luk 23:39, MB-VCG-02): the criminal's demand for rescue "without repentance or faith" resists the characteristic's constitutive element of relational petition — demanding rescue is not the same as crying for it in faith. Self-sufficiency under mortal threat is thus structurally excluded.

**T1.4.2 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the evidence shows variation across context, direction, and constitutional level. By constitutional level: in the crucifixion passages (MB-VCG-02) and Joh 12:27/Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03), the one who could exercise physical self-rescue chooses not to — the characteristic operates at the level of the will's disposition toward sacrifice rather than at the level of terror-response. **T1.5.2 — Immediate Response — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The immediate response varies significantly across the verse evidence. In MB-VCG-01 (Mat 8:25; Mat 14:30; Psa 69:18), the immediate response to mortal threat is the urgent cry — consistent in its panic-driven, directional character. In MB-VCG-02 (Mat 27:40; 27:49; Mar 15:30; 15:31; Luk 23:35; 23:37; 23:39), the "immediate response" is not a cry from the endangered but rather mockery or demand from observers — the phenomenological register is entirely different; no personal terror-cry is present. In MB-VCG-03 (Joh 12:27; Heb 5:7), the immediate response is anguished petition combined with reverent surrender — more layered and deliberate than the panic-cry. In MB-VCG-04 (Luk 1:71; Heb 11:7), the immediate response is not an acute cry but either corporate anticipatory relief (Luk 1:71) or reverent, obedient action (Heb 11:7). In MB-VCG-05 (Act 27:20), the immediate response is the extinction of hope — no cry, only despair. The response is therefore not consistent: it ranges from panic-cry to anguished petition to obedient action to hopeless silence, depending on VCG context.

**T1.6.2 — Sustained Effect — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained exposure to or possession of this characteristic establish?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence permits identification of the following sustained states and orientations: (1) Faith-heir identity (Heb 11:7, MB-VCG-04): sustained obedient response to divinely-warned mortal threat establishes the person as an "heir of righteousness by faith" — a dispositional-identity state that transcends the original crisis. The evidence suggests that sustained exposure establishes orientations in the direction the original response was set: faith-oriented response deepens faith-identity; despair-closure deepens despair.

**T1.7.2 — Conditions of Reception — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Several blocking conditions are evidenced: (1) Absent faith / faithless demand (Luk 23:39, MB-VCG-02): the criminal who demands rescue "without repentance or faith" demonstrates that treating the rescuer as an instrument rather than as a sovereign person to whom petition is owed blocks genuine reception — the demand-posture forecloses the relational space in which rescue is received. (2) Despairing closure (Act 27:20, MB-VCG-05): when hope is abandoned entirely, the inner movement toward the rescuer ceases — despair functions as a blocking condition insofar as it extinguishes the very capacity to cry out. (3) Presumption/mockery (MB-VCG-02 generally): the mockers who taunt Jesus to save himself are not receiving the characteristic but inverting it — mockery distorts by treating rescue as a demonstration of power to be performed on demand rather than a gift to be received. (4) Self-referential orientation: the crucifixion mockery passages implicitly show that an inner orientation focused on spectacle or self-interest (the mockers' scoffing, the criminal's self-serving demand) prevents reception by keeping the inner being turned toward itself rather than toward the rescuer.

**T1.8.2 — Dimension Classification — What evidence from the verse evidence supports this classification?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The evidence supporting the affective-volitional dimension classification (established at T1.1.8) is as follows: Affective evidence: Mat 8:25 records the disciples' terror-laden cry "we are perishing" — acute affective distress is the precondition of the cry; Mat 14:30 records that Peter "saw the wind" and "was afraid" (edeilasen, the fear is explicitly named) before crying out — fear is the affective register that activates the volitional cry; Joh 12:27 has Jesus say "my soul is troubled" (tetataktai hē psychē mou) — the affective disturbance is named as the inner-being precondition of the petition "save me from this hour"; Heb 5:7 describes "loud cries and tears" (krauge kai dakrua) — affective intensity is the phenomenological marker of the prayer. Volitional evidence: in all MB-VCG-01 and MB-VCG-03 verses, the cry is an act of will — not passive collapse but active petition directed at a person; in Heb 11:7 (MB-VCG-04), the volitional element is even more prominent — Noah "moved with reverent fear" (eulabētheis) and "constructed an ark" — will expressed through sustained obedient action. The affective-volitional dual structure is thus consistently evidenced; the evidence strongly supports this as the primary dimension classification.

**T1.1.3 — Name and Naming — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications that orient the enquiry from the outset?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The name "Physical rescue from mortal danger" carries all three types of implication. Directional: the name implies a movement — rescue is from somewhere (mortal danger) to somewhere else (safety) — establishing the characteristic as inherently directional and dynamic. The enquiry is thereby oriented toward the trajectory of the endangered inner being rather than toward a stable state. Relational: "rescue" requires a rescuer and a rescued — the name implicitly posits a two-party relational structure (endangered creature + rescuing agent) without which "rescue" is not a coherent concept. The enquiry is thereby oriented toward the relationship between the endangered inner being and the divine rescuer rather than toward the inner being in isolation. Constitutional: "physical" specifies the constitutional level — this is not spiritual or eschatological rescue but rescue of the embodied, mortal person, situating the characteristic at the intersection of inner being and physical vulnerability. The enquiry is thereby oriented toward the somatic-affective interface: how mortal bodily danger registers in and through the inner being. All three orientations are present from the outset in the name itself.

**T1.2.3 — Kind — What is the current best working description of this characteristic — encapsulating its constitutional location, the faculties it primarily engages, and its impact on the person?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Working description: Physical rescue from mortal danger is a composite, crisis-activated inner-being condition located at the intersection of affective life and volitional agency, triggered by acute or sustained mortal physical threat, in which the person's ordinary inner equilibrium is overwhelmed by terror, anguish, or despair, and the will is pressed to its sole available movement: a desperate, directional cry or reverent petition toward the divine rescuer. The characteristic primarily engages the affective faculty (fear, terror, anguish, despair), the volitional faculty (the act of crying out, petitioning, surrendering, or in failure-cases, closing off in hopelessness), and the relational faculty (the inner being's orientation toward God as the only available source of rescue). Its impact on the person when received in faith is relief from terror, deepened relational dependence on the rescuer, and progressive formation as a faith-heir; when blocked or absent, its impact is despair, faithless demand, or despairing resignation. The characteristic is not a stable disposition but a dynamic state that has temporal structure — it is entered, navigated, and resolved (or failed) — and its resolution either deepens faith-identity or deepens despair.

**T1.3.3 — Boundary — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The characteristic is not: (1) Eschatological salvation (M38-A): per the researcher's boundary resolution, verses in which sōzō refers to final spiritual deliverance from sin and judgment are held in a distinct sub-group; M38-B ends where eternal destiny begins. The fear-faith dynamic in this sub-group is time-bound, body-oriented, and does not engage eschatological content. (2) Penitential faith: the characteristic does not involve repentance of sin as a constitutive element — the disciples' storm-cry and Peter's sinking are not acts of confession; even Heb 5:7 does not present the Son's prayer as penitential. Where penitential faith appears (the contrasting criminal of Luk 23:40–43, not in this corpus), it is adjacent but distinct. (3) Confident assurance: the characteristic involves crisis-state, not the settled trust of one who knows deliverance is secured — the faith-cry is the opposite of triumphant confidence; it is faith exercised at its most fragile and desperate. (4) The characteristic ends where the crisis is resolved: once rescue is received and the mortal threat is removed, the characteristic's crisis-state dissolves — what may follow (deepened faith-identity, gratitude, witness) belongs to other characteristics. The characteristic is bounded by the onset and resolution of acute mortal threat.

**T1.4.3 — Modes of Operation — Does this characteristic have a communicative or speech-based mode of operation — and if so, how does it function?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the characteristic has a prominently communicative-speech mode of operation. In Mat 8:25 (MB-VCG-01), the disciples cry out with speech: "Save us, Lord; we are perishing" — the characteristic operates as voiced petition, a speech act directed at the rescuer. In Mat 14:30 (MB-VCG-01), Peter "cried out" (ekraxen) — a loud vocal act. In Psa 69:18 (MB-VCG-01), the psalmist speaks directly to God: "Draw near to me, redeem me." In Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03), the Son's prayer is described with "loud cries and tears" (krauge kai dakrua) — vocal anguish is the medium. Joh 12:27 (MB-VCG-03) presents spoken deliberation: "What shall I say? 'Father, save me from this hour'?" — the speech mode is there even in its considered, deliberative form. The speech mode functions as the externalisation of the inner-being state: terror or anguish that cannot be resolved internally is directed outward in voice toward the rescuer. The cry is not merely expressive (emoting) but petitionary (seeking response): it is a speech act that expects an answer. This communicative mode is the characteristic's primary mode in its crisis-register; in Heb 11:7 and Luk 1:71 (MB-VCG-04), the mode shifts to action and communal declaration rather than individual voiced cry.

**T1.5.3 — Immediate Response — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The verse evidence is silent on immediate inner-being response in the following instances: Mat 27:40; Mat 27:49; Mar 15:30; Mar 15:31; Luk 23:35; Luk 23:37 (MB-VCG-02) — these verses record the words and postures of mockers and observers; they do not record the immediate inner-being response of Jesus to the taunts, nor do they describe any inner movement of the mockers themselves upon their own speech. The immediate inner state of Jesus in response to the mockery is not the subject of these verses (that is addressed separately in Joh 12:27 and Heb 5:7 in MB-VCG-03). Luk 1:71 (MB-VCG-04) — the Benedictus is a declarative statement about what God has done or will do; it does not record the immediate inner response of any individual who encounters the rescue. The evidence for immediate response is therefore concentrated in MB-VCG-01 (terror → cry), MB-VCG-03 (anguish → petition), and MB-VCG-05 (threat → hopeless despair); MB-VCG-02 and most of MB-VCG-04 are silent on immediate personal inner response.

**T1.6.3 — Sustained Effect — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Yes, the sustained effect differs from the immediate response in kind and register. The immediate response is crisis-acute: terror, panic-cry, anguished petition, or hopeless despair — all are time-bound states of extremis. The sustained effect, where evidenced, moves into dispositional and identity territory that the immediate response does not. In Heb 11:7 (MB-VCG-04), the immediate response to divine warning is reverent fear and the beginning of ark construction; the sustained effect across the duration of the construction and deliverance is the establishment of Noah as "heir of righteousness by faith" — a dispositional-identity state entirely distinct from the original crisis-fear. In Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03), the immediate response is anguished, tearful prayer; the sustained effect (within the Heb 5 context) is being "perfected" through the suffering — a completion of obedience that is structurally different from the distress that initiated the prayer. In MB-VCG-01, the evidence for sustained effect is implicit: the disciples who cry in the storm and are rescued are the same disciples who will be formed by repeated encounter with Jesus's rescuing power, but the text does not elaborate the sustained effect within these specific verses. Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05) shows that the sustained effect of abandoned hope is simply deeper despair — there is no positive trajectory from hopeless resignation.

**T1.7.3 — Conditions of Reception — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The verse evidence provides two distinct profiles of non-reception: (1) Faithless demand — Luk 23:39 (MB-VCG-02): the criminal who encounters Jesus and demands rescue without repentance or faith exhibits an inner state of entitlement and self-focus. The demand "save yourself and us" treats rescue as an owed service rather than a gift of grace. The inner-being state is one of unbelief and self-referential urgency — the person is in the right situation (adjacent to the rescuer in mortal danger) but the inner orientation is inverted. (2) Despairing closure — Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05): those aboard the ship who abandon all hope of rescue exhibit an inner state of resigned hopelessness — not actively resistant but passively closed. The inner being has ceased to reach toward rescue because it has concluded that no rescue is possible. This is non-reception through collapse rather than through refusal. In both cases, the person fails to receive the characteristic because the inner being is not positioned in dependent, relational faith-petition toward the rescuer: either it is turned defiantly toward its own demands (Luk 23:39) or it has turned inward in collapsed resignation (Act 27:20). The two modes are affectively opposite — demanding vs. despairing — but structurally identical in their closure to received rescue.

**T1.8.3 — Dimension Classification — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they, and do they compete with the primary classification?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary classification is affective-volitional (established at T1.1.8 and supported at T1.2.8). Secondary dimensions present in the verse evidence are: (1) Relational dimension: the characteristic is constitutively relational — the cry is always directed toward a person (God, the Father, the Lord Jesus); the rescue requires a responding agent; even the despair of Act 27:20 (MB-VCG-05) is the relational dimension inverted (no rescuer anticipated). This secondary relational dimension is present in virtually every verse. (2) Somatic-constitutional dimension: the characteristic is triggered by physical mortal threat — the body is imperilled — and this somatic context is not incidental but constitutive; without the body's actual danger, the characteristic does not arise in this form. Heb 5:7 ("in the days of his flesh") explicitly marks the somatic dimension. (3) Volitional-obedience dimension: in Heb 11:7 and Heb 5:7 (MB-VCG-03, MB-VCG-04), the characteristic operates through disciplined obedience and surrender — this is more volitional than affective, and these verses could conceivably be classified primarily under volitional dimensions. These secondary dimensions do not compete with the primary affective-volitional classification in most verses, but in Heb 11:7 the volitional-obedience dimension is arguably as prominent as the affective dimension, creating mild internal tension in the classification. The relational dimension is pervasive but best understood as the structural form through which the affective-volitional content is expressed, not a competing primary dimension.
