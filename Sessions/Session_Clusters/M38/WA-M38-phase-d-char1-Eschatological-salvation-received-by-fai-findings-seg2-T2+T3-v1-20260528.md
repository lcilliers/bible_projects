# M38 Phase D — Char 1 (Eschatological salvation received by faith) — T2+T3 findings

**Cluster:** M38
**Characteristic:** Eschatological salvation received by faith (char_seq=1)
**Tier-segment:** T2+T3 — 64 prompts

---

**T2.1.1 — Spirit-Level Location — Is this characteristic explicitly located at the spirit level in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus contains one explicit spirit-level reference. 1 Cor 5:5 (M38-A-VCG-10) distinguishes the destruction of the flesh from the salvation of the spirit ("the spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord"), naming the spirit as the locus of eschatological rescue that survives bodily judgment. Beyond this instance, the overwhelming majority of verses in M38-A do not use pneuma-language to locate salvation constitutionally at the spirit level. The characteristic is far more consistently located at the soul (psychē), heart (kardia), and will than at the explicit spirit level.

---

**T2.2.1 — Soul-Level Location — Is this characteristic identified in the verse evidence as a soul-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Soul-level location is explicitly evidenced in multiple verses. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) states that the implanted word "is able to save the soul" (psychēn). 1 Pe 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-04) names "the outcome of your faith" as "the salvation of your souls" (sōtērian psychōn). Jam 5:20 (M38-A-VCG-10) speaks of turning a sinner from wandering to "save a soul from death." Rev 7:10 and 19:1 (M38-A-VCG-10) frame the completed doxological response of the redeemed. These instances consistently identify the soul (psychē) as the constitutive locus of the salvation received — the level at which rescue is targeted and where the outcome of faith is deposited.

---

**T2.3.1 — Heart — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the heart?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Heart-location is multiply and explicitly evidenced. Rom 10:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) ties salvation to "believing in your heart that God raised him from the dead." Rom 10:10 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies that "with the heart one believes and is justified." Luk 8:12 (M38-A-VCG-02) identifies the heart as the battleground where the word must take root to produce salvation, noting that the devil takes the word from the heart to prevent belief. Act 16:31 (M38-A-VCG-02) frames saving faith as an inner act of the heart trusting in the Lord Jesus. The heart is the primary receptive organ of saving faith across the corpus.

---

**T2.4.1 — Mind — Does the verse evidence locate this characteristic in the mind?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Mind-location is present but secondary and indirect. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) specifies that Scripture makes one "wise for salvation" — wisdom here is a mind-formation process that serves saving faith. Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) notes that rescue was offered but the people's "understanding was closed," making receptive comprehension a mind-level prerequisite for receiving salvation. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) links God's desire that all be saved with coming "to the knowledge of the truth," joining rescue with cognitive reception. These instances are genuine but do not constitute the primary location; the mind serves as an instrument of reception rather than the originating seat of the characteristic.

---

**T2.5.1 — Other Soul Subsets — Does the verse evidence surface any soul-level location beyond heart and mind for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The will (volitional faculty) surfaces as a distinct soul-level location beyond heart and mind. Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24, Luk 17:33 (M38-A-VCG-06) collectively establish the will's surrender and release of self-protective interest as the decisive inner movement through which eschatological life is received. Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, Mar 13:13 (M38-A-VCG-05) identify perseverance of will under trial as the ongoing inner condition of salvation. Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) calls believers to "work out" salvation with reverence, engaging a sustained volitional engagement. The conscience is also implicated: Act 16:30 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows the awakened conscience driving the jailer's urgent "what must I do to be saved?" — a soul-level location distinct from heart or mind per se.

---

**T2.6.1 — Body — Significance — Does the verse evidence link this characteristic to a specific body part?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two body-part links are evidenced. First, the mouth: Rom 10:9–10 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies that mouth-confession ("confesses with your mouth that Jesus is Lord") is paired with heart-belief as the dual pathway to salvation. Second, the head: Eph 6:17 (M38-A-VCG-10) names the "helmet of salvation" as armor worn on the head, linking salvation's protective reality to the body's most exposed and identity-bearing member. Beyond these two instances, the corpus does not systematically link this characteristic to other specific body parts.

---

**T2.7.1 — Body — Direction — Where a body-characteristic link exists, which direction does it run — does the soul express through the body, does the body feed back to the soul, or does it run in both directions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — For the mouth-confession link (Rom 10:9–10, M38-A-VCG-02), the direction is soul-to-body: inner heart-belief expresses outward through the mouth's confession. The confession does not generate faith; it declares and confirms what the heart already holds. For the helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17, M38-A-VCG-10), the direction is ambiguous: salvation is an inner reality donned outwardly as protection, suggesting a soul-to-body-to-soul feedback loop where the inner possession is embodied in protective form. Neither link involves the body generating the characteristic; both show inner reality expressing or protecting through the body.

---

**T2.8.1 — Body — Deposit — Does sustained operation of this characteristic leave a constitutional deposit in the body or its design — including DNA or generational consequence?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The corpus is silent on constitutional deposit in the body, DNA, or generational consequence. No verse in M38-A makes a claim about biological or generational transmission of the salvific state. The closest generational language is Act 11:14 and Act 16:31 (M38-A-VCG-02) which speak of household salvation, but these describe communal reception through shared faith-response rather than genetic or biological transmission.

---

**T2.9.1 — Origin and Source — Where does this characteristic originate constitutionally — is it generated from within the person, received from outside, bestowed by God, or carried generationally?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The corpus is emphatic and consistent: the origin of eschatological salvation is entirely external to the person and bestowed by God. Eph 2:8 (M38-A-VCG-02, ANCHOR) states that salvation is "by grace through faith — and this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." Rom 5:15–17 (M38-A-VCG-03) frames salvation as dōrea/dōrēma — free gift — contrasting it with anything the person generates. 2 Ti 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies it is "not because of our works but because of his own purpose and grace." Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) explicitly excludes "our own righteous deeds" and locates origin in divine mercy through the Spirit. The person contributes no originating cause; faith is the receptive instrument, not the source.

---

**T2.10.1 — Constitutional Movement — Does this characteristic move across constitutional levels — from spirit to soul, from soul to body, or across boundaries in other directions?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence supports a downward and outward constitutional movement. The initiative originates from God (divine/external), enters the inner being through the soul (the soul receives salvation as its telos, 1 Pe 1:9), is appropriated by the heart through faith (Rom 10:9–10), engages the will in either reception or surrender (M38-A-VCG-06), and expresses outward through the mouth in confession (Rom 10:9) and through embodied warfare in the helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17). 1 Cor 5:5 (M38-A-VCG-10) suggests an inward movement: even the body's destruction can serve the spirit's ultimate rescue, implying that the salvific reality can persist at the deepest constitutional level even when outer levels are stripped away.

---

**T2.1.2 — Spirit-Level Location — Does the evidence indicate that this characteristic originates in or is primarily a spirit-level phenomenon?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence does not support spirit-level as the primary location or origin. The single explicit spirit-level reference (1 Cor 5:5: "that his spirit may be saved") describes the spirit as the object and destination of salvation under extreme disciplinary conditions, not its originating level. The characteristic originates from God (external divine gift) and is received primarily at the soul and heart level. Spirit-level is the deepest preservation-point of rescue in extremis, not the constitutional home or origin of the characteristic.

---

**T2.2.2 — Soul-Level Location — What does soul-level location reveal about this characteristic's place in the innermost personal experience?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Soul-level location reveals that eschatological salvation penetrates to the innermost personal center of the human being — the psychē as the seat of life, personal identity, and experienced reality. 1 Pe 1:9 (M38-A-VCG-04) naming salvation as "the outcome of your faith" received at the soul level positions this characteristic as the ultimate telos of the entire personal economy: faith reaches its destination in the rescued soul. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies the "implanted word" operating at the soul to produce salvation, indicating that divine communication must reach this level to achieve rescue. The soul-level location establishes that salvation is not a surface or external change but an innermost re-constitution of the personal center.

---

**T2.3.2 — Heart — What does the heart-location reveal — what aspect of the heart's integrating function (knowing, willing, feeling, moral awareness) does this characteristic engage?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The heart's believing/trusting function is the primary aspect engaged. Rom 10:9 (M38-A-VCG-02) specifies believing "in your heart," and Rom 10:10 pairs heart-belief with justification, indicating that the heart functions here as the organ of deep trust-commitment rather than cognitive assent alone. Luk 8:12 (M38-A-VCG-02) identifies the heart as the location where the saving word must take root — engaging the heart's receiving and holding function. Act 16:30–31 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows an awakened conscience within the heart driving the will to seek rescue. The heart's integrating function is thus engaged in its willing, trusting, and morally-aware dimensions simultaneously: the heart turns toward Christ (will), commits in trust (faith), and responds to the awareness of need (moral awareness). Knowing/cognition is present but subordinate.

---

**T2.4.2 — Mind — What does mind-location reveal — what aspect of the mind's function (thought, discernment, understanding) does this characteristic engage?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Where mind-level engagement is evidenced, it is the understanding/reception function rather than originating thought. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) shows Scripture forming the mind toward wisdom that grasps salvation — the mind's function is formative reception of revealed content. Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) shows that understanding must be open to perceive the salvation being offered — the mind's discernment of the saving act is a necessary precondition. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) links salvation with "knowledge of the truth" — the mind's reception of salvific truth. The mind does not generate this characteristic but must be oriented toward and receptive of it; its function is discernment and comprehension of an externally given gift rather than origination.

**T2.5.2 — Other Soul Subsets — If so, what is that location, and what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Two additional soul-subset locations are evidenced. (1) The will: Mat 16:25 (M38-A-VCG-06, ANCHOR) and parallels reveal that the will's release of self-preservation is the decisive inner act through which eschatological life is entered — the will must surrender its self-protective reflex. Mat 10:22 and Mat 24:13 (M38-A-VCG-05, ANCHOR) reveal that the will must sustain its orientation under sustained pressure, making perseverance of will the ongoing condition of received salvation. This reveals that salvation reception is not volitionally passive despite being entirely grace-given; the will is actively engaged as receptive faculty. (2) The conscience: Act 16:30 (M38-A-VCG-02) reveals the conscience awakened to danger as the driver of the jailer's turn toward salvation, indicating that the conscience functions as the inner witness that mobilizes the will toward rescue. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) warns that neglect of salvation indicts the conscience as culpably passive.

**T2.6.2 — Body — Significance — If so, what is Scripture doing by making that link — is it emphatic, functional, expressive, indicative, or mediating?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The two body-part links serve different rhetorical and functional purposes. The mouth-confession link in Rom 10:9–10 (M38-A-VCG-02) is functional and mediating: the mouth's verbal declaration serves as the outward functional completion of inward heart-faith, and the pairing of heart-belief with mouth-confession suggests that salvation engages both dimensions of the person. The expression through the mouth is not merely decorative but a required element of the whole act of receiving salvation in its fullness. The helmet of salvation in Eph 6:17 (M38-A-VCG-10) is metaphorical and protective/indicative: salvation is named as a piece of armor worn on the body's most vital part, indicating that the inner reality of being saved functions as cognitive and identity protection against spiritual assault. The link is emphatic in its imagery — salvation shields what the person most fundamentally is.

**T2.7.2 — Body — Direction — What is the consequence of that directionality for understanding the characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent soul-to-body directionality (inner faith expressing outward through mouth-confession; inner salvation worn as helmet) establishes that eschatological salvation is constitutionally interior in origin and bodily in expression. This directionality has two consequences: (1) It confirms that salvation cannot be reduced to outward ritual or bodily act alone — the mouth's confession without heart-belief is incomplete (Rom 10:9–10). The body can only genuinely express what the inner being has genuinely received. (2) It establishes that received inner salvation has bodily consequences: the saved person expresses salvation verbally (confession), defends through it protectively (helmet), and inhabits it as a public stance (Act 2:40, separation from a corrupt generation). The characteristic is inner in origin, outer in manifestation.

**T2.9.2 — Origin and Source — What does the evidence reveal about whether the origin is singular or multiple?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The origin is singular and unambiguous across all ten VCGs: God alone is the originating source of eschatological salvation. Eph 2:8 (M38-A-VCG-02, ANCHOR) states the gift is entirely of God, excluding human origination. Rom 5:15–17 (M38-A-VCG-03) locates origin in the "grace of God and the free gift by the grace of that one man, Jesus Christ." Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) attributes origin to God's mercy and the Spirit's work. 1 Cor 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) states God "chose" the means of salvation. Joh 3:17 (M38-A-VCG-01) grounds origin in the Father's sending of the Son. While the mode of appropriation is multiple (faith, confession, calling, hearing, endurance), the ontological source is uniformly singular: divine grace. Human faith is the receptive instrument, not a co-originating cause.

**T2.10.2 — Constitutional Movement — What does the evidence reveal about the sequence or pattern of that movement?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — A discernible sequence emerges across the corpus: (1) Divine initiative external to the person (God sends, God saves, grace appears — M38-A-VCG-01, -03); (2) Word/message enters through proclamation and hearing (Eph 1:13, Luk 8:12, 1 Cor 1:21 — M38-A-VCG-02); (3) The conscience is awakened to need (Act 16:30; 2 Cor 7:10 — M38-A-VCG-09); (4) The will turns, the heart believes, and calling/confession occurs (Rom 10:9–10; Act 2:21 — M38-A-VCG-02); (5) The soul receives salvation as its telos (1 Pe 1:9; Jam 1:21 — M38-A-VCG-04); (6) Ongoing salvation is sustained through perseverance of will (Mat 10:22; Phili 2:12 — M38-A-VCG-05); (7) At consummation the inner being rests in completed rescue and doxological response (Rev 7:10; Rev 19:1 — M38-A-VCG-10). The pattern moves from divine-external inward through hearing, conscience, will, and heart, depositing in the soul, and then expressing outward in confession, endurance, and praise.

**T3.1.1 — Perception — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty — the inner senses including hearing, sight, taste, touch, smell, and spiritual discernment — and if so, which inner sense and how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Inner hearing is the most consistently engaged perceptive sense. Act 13:26 (M38-A-VCG-02) frames the "word of salvation" as sent to receptive hearers; Eph 1:13 (M38-A-VCG-02) sequences "hearing the gospel of salvation" before believing and being sealed. Luk 8:12 (M38-A-VCG-02) identifies the word entering the heart through hearing as the receptive pathway that must not be stolen. Act 28:28 (M38-A-VCG-07) contrasts those who will "listen" with those who rejected salvation. Spiritual sight is evidenced in Luk 2:30 (M38-A-VCG-04), where Simeon declares "my eyes have seen your salvation" — a personal visual-spiritual witness. Heb 6:4 (M38-A-VCG-03) uses tasting: those who have "tasted the heavenly gift" have received real inner-perceptive experience of salvation. Inner hearing dominates; sight and taste are evidenced but subordinate.

**T3.2.1 — Cognition — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty — knowing, understanding, discerning — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Cognition is engaged as a necessary but subordinate faculty. Luk 1:77 (M38-A-VCG-04) explicitly joins "knowledge of salvation" with forgiveness of sins — the cognitive reception of the saving message is integral to the experience. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) pairs God's desire that all be saved with coming "to the knowledge of the truth," making cognitive apprehension inseparable from rescue. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) describes Scripture forming wisdom-for-salvation, which is a cognitive shaping process. Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) negatively demonstrates the necessity of cognitive openness: "they did not understand" that God was offering salvation through a chosen deliverer. Cognition is engaged in the mode of receptive comprehension — grasping what has been externally given — not as independent discovery.

**T3.3.1 — Memory — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty — the holding and retrieving of inner-being reality across time — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Memory is partially and indirectly engaged. Jude 5 (M38-A-VCG-10) explicitly invokes remembered history: "I want to remind you... that Jesus, who saved a people out of Egypt," deploying past rescue as a warning to the present inner being. Rom 13:11 (M38-A-VCG-05) uses temporal awareness: "salvation is nearer to us now than when we first believed," which requires holding the memory of initial faith against the present moment. 1 Cor 15:2 (M38-A-VCG-04) warns against having "believed in vain," implying the need to hold and maintain what was received at first. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) warns against "neglecting" salvation — a memory-and-retention failure. Memory functions here as the faculty that holds the received salvific reality against time-pressure and drift.

**T3.6.1 — Volition — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty — the capacity to choose — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Volition is pervasively and centrally engaged across the corpus. The entire M38-A-VCG-06 cluster (Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24, Luk 17:33) hinges on the will's surrender of self-preservation as the decisive act through which life is received. M38-A-VCG-05 (Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, Mar 13:13, Phili 2:12) establishes the persevering will as the ongoing condition of salvation. The will functions in three modes: (1) initial turning/receiving, (2) sustained perseverance, and (3) surrender of self-interest — all in service of appropriating divine gift.

**T3.7.1 — Agency — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty — the capacity to act, initiate, and make happen — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Agency is engaged, but with a critical asymmetry: primary agency belongs to God, and human agency is derivative and responsive. God is consistently the saving agent: "God saved us" (2 Ti 1:9), "the Lord added to their number" (Act 2:47), "God sent the Son to save the world" (Joh 3:17), "God chose... to save those who believe" (1 Cor 1:21). Human agency appears in the responsive mode: "calling on the name" (Act 2:21; Rom 10:13), "working out salvation" (Phili 2:12), "striving to enter" (Luk 13:23), Paul's self-adaptation to save some (1 Cor 9:22), turning sinners back (Jam 5:20). The inner-being's agency is genuine but receptively structured — it acts in response to divine initiative and toward salvation as goal, not as originator of rescue.

**T3.8.1 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty — the capacity to assess against a standard of right, wrong, good, and true — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Moral evaluation is engaged in several ways. Act 15:1 (M38-A-VCG-02) surfaces the explicit moral-evaluative debate: is circumcision morally/covenantally required for salvation? — a formal evaluation against the standard of the law. Jam 2:14 (M38-A-VCG-04) applies moral evaluation to faith itself: "can that faith save him?" — assessing whether faith without works meets the standard of genuine saving faith. 2 Th 2:10 (M38-A-VCG-08) evaluates those who "refused the love of truth" as morally culpable for forfeiting salvation. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) evaluates neglect of salvation as morally indefensible. Jude 23 (M38-A-VCG-10) calls for moral evaluation of sin-contamination in the act of snatching others from destruction. Moral evaluation is engaged in assessing the conditions, authenticity, and culpability around salvation.

**T3.9.1 — Conscience — Does this characteristic engage the conscience — the acute inner witness of sin, guilt, and conviction — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Conscience is engaged as an activating and diagnostic faculty. Act 16:30 (M38-A-VCG-02) presents the paradigmatic instance: the jailer's "what must I do to be saved?" emerges from a conscience awakened to existential danger — acute inner witness driving the will toward rescue. 2 Cor 7:10 (M38-A-VCG-09, ANCHOR) specifies that "godly grief" (conscience pierced by recognition of sin) "produces a repentance that leads to salvation" — the conscience is the causal pathway into the salvific turning. 1 Ti 1:15 (M38-A-VCG-01) shows the apostle identifying himself as "chief of sinners," a conscience fully aware of its own guilt as the backdrop against which grace reaches. Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) implicitly cleanses conscience through divine mercy. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) warns the conscience against the passive guilt of neglect. Conscience functions as both catalyst (driving toward salvation) and diagnostic (remaining as unclean background against which grace is received).

**T3.10.1 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness — the integrated response of moral awareness, volition, and action — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Conscientiousness is engaged in the perseverance and working-out dimensions of the characteristic. Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) is the clearest instance: "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" — this is the integrated response of moral seriousness (fear and trembling), volition (working out), and sustained action (ongoing process). 1 Ti 4:16 (M38-A-VCG-05) shows the teacher who "watches his life and doctrine carefully" — conscientiousness as sustained moral-volitional watchfulness that mediates salvation for self and others. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) calls for "receiving with meekness the implanted word" — an integrated humble-receptive stance of moral awareness and active reception. The endurance cluster (Mat 10:22; Mar 13:13; M38-A-VCG-05) represents conscientiousness under sustained external pressure.

**T3.11.1 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity — the constitutional equipment for genuine connection with another person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — Relational capacity is multiply engaged. (1) Vertically toward God: Joh 5:34 (M38-A-VCG-02) frames salvation as the goal of Jesus's testimony — a relational response to personal witness. Act 5:31 (M38-A-VCG-01) frames salvation as bestowed through the relational posture of repentance and forgiveness received from Christ as leader and savior. Rom 5:10 (M38-A-VCG-03, ANCHOR) defines the salvific movement as reconciliation — the restoration of broken vertical relation — through which rescue flows. (2) Horizontally: 1 Cor 7:16 (M38-A-VCG-10) names the relational hope of a believing spouse saving an unbelieving partner. Jam 5:20 (M38-A-VCG-10) frames the turning of a wandering sinner as person-to-person rescue mediated through relational persistence. Rom 11:14 (M38-A-VCG-08) names Paul's relational witness strategy toward his kinspeople as person-saving. Salvation is fundamentally relational in structure — both in its vertical reception from God and its horizontal mediation between persons.

**T3.1.2 — Perception — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic enables and deepens perceptive capacity. Luk 2:30 (M38-A-VCG-04) shows Simeon's eyes perceiving what was spiritually invisible to others — salvation enables spiritual sight. Heb 6:4 (M38-A-VCG-03) describes those who have "tasted the heavenly gift" as having a deepened inner sensory experience unavailable to the unsaved. Act 13:26 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows the receptive hearing that receives salvation as a qualitatively distinct perceptive openness. Negatively, Act 7:25 (M38-A-VCG-07) shows that closed perception blocks reception of salvation, implying that opened perception enables it. Where salvation is refused or abandoned (2 Pe 2:20, M38-A-VCG-01), perceptive capacity is impaired relative to initial reception — those who knew the Savior and fell back are worse off than those who never perceived. The direction of effect is: salvation received opens and deepens perception; salvation rejected or abandoned impairs it.

**T3.2.2 — Cognition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic enables and deepens cognition. Luk 1:77 (M38-A-VCG-04) names "knowledge of salvation" as part of salvation's content — the saved receive knowledge they previously lacked. 2 Ti 3:15 (M38-A-VCG-10) shows salvation deepening cognitive formation through Scripture's wisdom-shaping. 1 Ti 2:4 (M38-A-VCG-08) joins salvation with "knowledge of the truth," suggesting that genuine cognitive reception of truth is inseparable from rescue. The characteristic does not bypass cognition: 1 Cor 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) is explicit that human wisdom failed to reach God by its own path, but God's proclamation creates a new cognitive pathway through faith. Cognition is redirected and deepened rather than bypassed. Negatively, the gospel appears as "folly" to those perishing (1 Cor 1:18, M38-A-VCG-04), suggesting that unsaved cognition is impaired in its capacity to evaluate the salvific reality correctly.

**T3.3.2 — Memory — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic engages memory in an enabling and stabilizing direction, though evidence is partial. Jude 5 (M38-A-VCG-10) uses recalled historical salvation to stabilize present inner faithfulness — the memory of past rescue functions as a warning resource. Rom 13:11 (M38-A-VCG-05) assumes the believer holds memory of "when we first believed" as a temporal anchor for present wakefulness. 1 Cor 15:2 (M38-A-VCG-04) warns against "vain" belief, implying the need to genuinely hold what was received — memory as a retention faculty that must remain active. The warning function is most prominent: where memory of salvation is not actively maintained, drift and neglect result (Heb 2:3, M38-A-VCG-05). No evidence suggests this characteristic impairs memory. The evidence is indirect and partial; the corpus does not explicitly theorize the characteristic's effect on memory.

**T3.4.2 — Affect — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic consistently enables and deepens affect. Peace is conferred (Luk 7:50; Luk 2:30, M38-A-VCG-02 and -04). Joy is produced (Rev 7:10; 19:1, M38-A-VCG-10; the jailer's response in Act 16:30–31). Gratitude and praise overflow in 2 Cor 9:15 (M38-A-VCG-03) and Rev 7:10. Godly grief that leads to salvation (2 Cor 7:10, M38-A-VCG-09) is itself a deepened affective capacity — the capacity to feel appropriate sorrow over sin as a salvific movement. Astonishment at grace received by the unexpected (Act 10:45, M38-A-VCG-03) represents a widening of affective range. The characteristic does not impair affect; it transforms it from fear and guilt toward peace, gratitude, and joy. The "fear and trembling" of Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) represents a refined and serious affective engagement with salvation rather than impairment.

**T3.5.2 — Creativity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The corpus is silent on creativity. No verse in M38-A evidences this characteristic enabling, deepening, bypassing, or impairing the creative or imaginative faculty. This silence is consistent with the characteristic's entirely receptive structure: salvation is received, not created. The absence of creativity-engagement is itself a datum about the characteristic's nature.

**T3.6.2 — Volition — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition in the person — including its three aspects: capacity, interaction with other characteristics, and the constraints under which it operates?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic both presupposes and transforms volition. (1) Capacity: salvation reception requires a functioning will — the will must call (Act 2:21; Rom 10:13), turn (2 Cor 7:10), confess (Rom 10:9), surrender (Mat 16:25), and endure (Mat 10:22). The characteristic does not bypass the will's capacity but requires its engagement. (2) Interaction: the will interacts with conscience (which awakens it — Act 16:30), grace (which enables it — Eph 2:8; 2 Ti 1:9), and the word (which activates it — Jam 1:21). The characteristic enables the will by providing the divine power within which it operates: "it is God who works in you to will and to do" (Phili 2:13, implied by Phili 2:12 context in M38-A-VCG-05). (3) Constraints: the will operates under the constraint of grace — it cannot originate salvation but must receive it. Eph 2:8 (ANCHOR) explicitly excludes self-origination while requiring faith-reception. The will is constrained from self-sufficiency and enabled for receptive action.

**T3.7.2 — Agency — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic enables and reshapes human agency without bypassing it. The primary salvific agency belongs to God throughout the corpus (M38-A-VCG-01: God sends, saves, exalts; M38-A-VCG-03: gift given). Human agency is enabled as a derivative, responsive capacity: calling (Act 2:21; Rom 10:13), confessing (Rom 10:9), working out (Phili 2:12), striving (Luk 13:23), adapting for others' salvation (1 Cor 9:22), turning sinners back (Jam 5:20). Salvation does not produce passivity — it redirects and empowers agency toward God and others. Where salvation is refused (2 Th 2:10; Luk 8:12), agency is impaired in its direction: the will actively refuses truth, and that refusal is judged as agentic failure. The characteristic does not bypass agency; it structures it as response to divine initiative.

**T3.8.2 — Moral Evaluation — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic deepens and clarifies moral evaluation. The gospel of salvation provides a new moral framework: 1 Cor 1:18 (M38-A-VCG-04) reveals that those being saved correctly evaluate the cross as God's power while those perishing evaluate it as folly — salvation sharpens the inner being's capacity to discern the true from the apparent. Jam 2:14 (M38-A-VCG-04) shows salvation enabling moral evaluation of faith's authenticity: "can that faith save him?" is a rigorous self-evaluative question. Heb 2:3 (M38-A-VCG-05) calls the inner being to evaluate the seriousness of neglecting rescue. Jude 23 (M38-A-VCG-10) shows moral evaluation operating in "holy revulsion toward sin-contamination" as part of rescuing others. The characteristic does not impair moral evaluation; it elevates its standard and sharpens its application.

**T3.9.2 — Conscience — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic both activates and cleanses conscience. Activating: 2 Cor 7:10 (M38-A-VCG-09) shows conscience activated through godly grief as the precondition for repentance-leading-to-salvation. Act 16:30 (M38-A-VCG-02) shows conscience already activated, urgently driving the jailer to seek rescue. Cleansing: Tit 3:5 (M38-A-VCG-03) and 1 Ti 1:15 (M38-A-VCG-01) show salvation received by the "chief of sinners" — conscience that was weighted with guilt is addressed by the very salvation it sought. The helmet of salvation (Eph 6:17, M38-A-VCG-10) protects the mind/conscience from renewed accusation. Rev 12:10 (M38-A-VCG-10) explicitly shows the conscience vindicated as the accuser is cast down: "salvation has come" as the declaration that ends condemnation. The characteristic does not impair conscience; it first activates it (bringing to the surface what guilt is present) and then cleanses and vindicates it.

**T3.10.2 — Conscientiousness — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic deepens conscientiousness as an integrated moral-volitional-action response. Phili 2:12 (M38-A-VCG-05) is the fullest instance: "work out your own salvation with fear and trembling" requires the integration of moral seriousness (fear and trembling), deliberate volition (working out), and sustained action — which is precisely the definition of conscientiousness. 1 Ti 4:16 (M38-A-VCG-05) deepens this: the faithful teacher "watches his life and doctrine carefully" in an integrated sustained moral watchfulness motivated by the salvific goal. Jam 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) calls for the "meekness" of receptive conscientiousness. The endurance cluster (Mat 10:22; Mat 24:13; Mar 13:13) represents conscientiousness sustained under external assault. The characteristic does not bypass or impair conscientiousness; it provides the motivational ground (the weight of salvation received and to be secured) that drives its deepening.

**T3.11.2 — Relational Capacity — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity in the person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — This characteristic enables and deepens relational capacity in both vertical and horizontal directions. Vertically: Rom 5:10 (M38-A-VCG-03, ANCHOR) shows reconciliation as the constitutive relational act — former enmity transformed into ongoing relational security in God. Act 5:31 (M38-A-VCG-01) shows repentance and forgiveness as the relational restoration that salvation effects. Horizontally: the community of the saved (Act 2:47, M38-A-VCG-04) constitutes a relational body incorporated together. Jam 5:20 and 1 Cor 7:16 (M38-A-VCG-10) show salvation activating relational concern for others' rescue. Rom 11:14 (M38-A-VCG-08) shows Paul's relational investment in his kinspeople's salvation. The characteristic does not impair relational capacity; the one notable exception — 2 Pe 2:20 (M38-A-VCG-01) — shows that abandoned salvation correlates with deteriorated relational and moral capacity, confirming that received salvation is what sustains it.

**T3.1.3 — Perception — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation necessarily enters through the perceptive faculty but is not reducible to it. The dominant perceptive mode is hearing (word received, message heard, gospel proclaimed), followed by sight (Simeon's eyes, the witnessed Savior), and taste (heavenly gift tasted in Heb 6:4). The consistent pattern is: an externally given salvific reality must be perceptively received before it is cognitively processed and volitionally accepted. Perception is the entry point, not the seat, of the characteristic. The negative evidence — closed understanding (Act 7:25), word stolen from the heart (Luk 8:12) — confirms that blocked perception blocks salvation-reception. This reveals that the characteristic is not self-generated by the inner being; it requires an external input that the inner perceptive faculty must be open to receive. The characteristic is constitutionally dependent on receptive perception.

**T3.2.3 — Cognition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with cognition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic requires cognition without being generated by it. Cognition is consistently engaged as a receptive and discerning faculty: grasping the word, understanding salvation offered, being formed by Scripture toward wisdom. But 1 Cor 1:21 (M38-A-VCG-02) is the critical statement: human wisdom could not reach God through its own cognitive effort — "the world did not know God through wisdom." God bypassed autonomous cognitive route and provided the foolishness of proclamation, through which faith (not cognition alone) receives rescue. The pattern shows that cognition is necessary but insufficient: it must be redirected from self-originating reasoning toward receptive comprehension of revealed truth. The characteristic reveals that human cognition in its unsaved state is constitutionally incapable of generating its own rescue — a finding about cognitive limitation in the unredeemed condition.

**T3.3.3 — Memory — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with memory reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The limited but consistent pattern of memory-engagement reveals that eschatological salvation is a temporally extended rather than merely instantaneous characteristic. The initial reception (when one "first believed," Rom 13:11) must be held across time; the historical precedents of rescue (Jude 5) must be retained as warning resources; the received salvation must be maintained against drift (Heb 2:3; 1 Cor 15:2). This reveals that salvation is not only a point-event but an ongoing state that requires the memory faculty to anchor it against time-pressure. The modest engagement of memory — it appears in warning and stability contexts, not in the primary reception mechanism — suggests that memory is a supporting rather than constitutive faculty for this characteristic: it preserves and stabilizes received salvation but is not the means by which it is received.

**T3.4.3 — Affect — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with affect reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The rich and consistent affective engagement reveals that eschatological salvation is constitutionally transformative of the affective life — it cannot be received without producing inner experiential change. The pattern shows three affective phases: (1) pre-salvific affect — godly grief (2 Cor 7:10), awakened urgency (Act 16:30), astonishment at the barrier to salvation (Mat 19:25); (2) reception affect — peace conferred (Luk 7:50; Luk 2:30), awe at unexpected grace (Act 10:45); (3) ongoing and eschatological affect — gratitude (2 Cor 9:15), joy in community (Act 2:47 context), and the full corporate doxological eruption of the redeemed (Rev 7:10; 19:1). The pattern reveals that the characteristic is affectively whole: it engages and transforms the feeling-life from guilt and urgency through peace to joy and praise. A salvation without affective engagement would be, in this evidence-base, anomalous.

**T3.5.3 — Creativity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with creativity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** S — The complete silence of the corpus on creativity reveals that eschatological salvation as evidenced in M38-A is entirely a received and responsive characteristic — nothing in its reception, sustaining, or completion requires or engages originative or imaginative capacity. The inner being's role is to receive, hold, confess, endure, and praise — all responsive modes. This consistent non-engagement of creativity is itself a structural finding: the characteristic is defined by its receptive rather than generative inner-being posture.

**T3.6.3 — Volition — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with volition reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pervasive and multidimensional engagement of volition reveals that eschatological salvation is not a purely passive inner-being state but a characteristic that structures, redirects, and requires the will throughout its reception and maintenance. Three volitional patterns emerge: (1) Initial turning — the will turns toward Christ in calling, confession, faith-reception, and surrender of self-preservation (M38-A-VCG-02, -06, -09); (2) Sustained perseverance — the will must hold under trial and not defect (M38-A-VCG-05); (3) Missionary direction — the will is reshaped toward others' salvation through self-adaptation, relational persistence, and turning wanderers back (M38-A-VCG-08, T3.11). Critically, volitional engagement does not imply volitional self-sufficiency: Eph 2:8 (ANCHOR) and 2 Ti 1:9 exclude works and self-origination. The pattern reveals a characteristic that fully engages the will as receptive and persevering instrument while leaving it constitutionally dependent on grace.

**T3.7.3 — Agency — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with agency reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals a constitutive asymmetry in agency: God's agency is primary, unlimited, and originating; human agency is derivative, responsive, and enabled by divine initiative. The recurring structure is: God saves (active divine agency) → person calls, believes, confesses, endures (responsive human agency). The characteristic does not impair human agency but re-structures it: human agency is re-oriented away from self-generated rescue toward responsive participation in divinely initiated rescue. The most revealing instances are the missionary agency verses (1 Cor 9:22; Rom 11:14; Jam 5:20) — persons acting as agents of others' salvation, but always in the mode of servants of God's saving purpose (Act 13:47), not independent rescuers. The pattern discloses a characteristic that is simultaneously the most exclusively divine act (God alone saves) and the one that most fully mobilizes human agency in its service.

**T3.8.3 — Moral Evaluation — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with moral evaluation reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent engagement of moral evaluation reveals that eschatological salvation is not morally neutral — it creates a new moral framework within which the inner being evaluates truth, faith, and action. The pattern shows moral evaluation operating in three modes: (1) Evaluating conditions — assessing what salvation requires (Act 15:1; Jam 2:14); (2) Evaluating culpability — judging those who refuse or neglect salvation as morally accountable (Heb 2:3; 2 Th 2:10); (3) Evaluating authenticity — testing whether faith is genuine saving faith (Jam 2:14). The cross creates a new moral evaluation standard: 1 Cor 1:18 (M38-A-VCG-04) shows that what those being saved evaluate as "the power of God" is evaluated by those perishing as "folly." Received salvation calibrates moral evaluation; its absence or rejection distorts it. The characteristic is both the object of moral evaluation (is this genuine rescue?) and its recalibrator (it reshapes what the inner being evaluates as true, good, and powerful).

**T3.9.3 — Conscience — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscience reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that conscience serves as the threshold faculty for salvation-reception: it must be activated before the will turns, and it must be cleansed after the will receives. This two-phase pattern — conscience as catalyst and conscience as object of cleansing — reveals that the characteristic addresses the inner being at its most morally sensitive point. The awakening of conscience (Act 16:30; 2 Cor 7:10) precedes the turn to salvation; the cleansing of conscience (Tit 3:5; Rev 12:10) is the mark of salvation received. The warning pattern (Heb 2:3; Jude 5) shows that an inactive conscience that fails to register the weight of salvation at risk is itself a sign of spiritual danger. The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation is the one inner-being reality that both requires a functioning conscience (to see the need for rescue) and constitutively addresses it (removing guilt and silencing accusation).

---

**T3.10.3 — Conscientiousness — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with conscientiousness reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The pattern reveals that eschatological salvation, once received, generates conscientiousness as its ongoing inner-being expression. The evidence concentrates in M38-A-VCG-05 and the perseverance cluster: Phili 2:12, 1 Ti 4:16, Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, and Mar 13:13 all show salvation sustained through integrated moral-volitional-action response. This reveals that the characteristic is not static or punctiliar in its inner-being effect — received salvation transforms the inner being into an agent of sustained conscientious response. The "working out" of salvation (Phili 2:12) is not an add-on to salvation but its present inner expression. The pattern suggests a constitutive connection: genuine eschatological salvation received generates conscientiousness; the absence of conscientiousness (endurance collapsing, life and doctrine unwatched) creates risk for the salvific state itself (1 Ti 4:16). Conscientiousness is both fruit and condition of maintained salvation.

---

**T3.11.3 — Relational Capacity — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with relational capacity reveal about the nature of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The consistent and bilateral engagement of relational capacity reveals that eschatological salvation is constitutively relational in structure at every level. The pattern shows: (1) Salvation is received through a relational encounter (the Samaritans' direct encounter with Jesus, Joh 4:42; Simeon's personal witness, Luk 2:30; the jailer's direct appeal, Act 16:30–31); (2) Its content is relational restoration — reconciliation from enmity to access (Rom 5:10, ANCHOR); (3) Its communal expression is a body of persons incorporated together (Act 2:47); (4) Its ongoing motivation is relational concern for others' rescue (Rom 11:14; Jam 5:20; 1 Cor 7:16). The complete pattern reveals that this characteristic cannot be adequately described in purely transactional or forensic terms alone — it is simultaneously forensic (justification, condemnation reversed) and constitutively relational (reconciliation, community, person-to-person witness and mediation). Relational capacity is not incidental to the characteristic but belongs to its definition.
