## 2. The characteristics in this study

The Bible's vocabulary for salvation and deliverance organises into seven related characteristics, each a distinct way the inner person inhabits or enacts the reality of being saved. They share the same root — rescue initiated by God, received by or through the human person — but they are not the same experience. Each names something specific: a different register of the event, a different position relative to it, a different inner-being form it takes. This chapter introduces each one in turn.

---

### 2.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

Eschatological salvation received by faith is the broadest and deepest of the seven characteristics — the inner-being state of the person who has received, or is in the process of receiving, rescue from sin, condemnation, and the judgment that awaits the unreconciled. It is salvation in its fullest spiritual register: not physical preservation or temporary relief, but the permanent transfer of the person from condemnation to life. As the angel's declaration to Joseph makes the mission explicit, "he will save his people from their sins" (Mat 1:21). The object of the rescue is the person's standing before God; the target is the inner bondage to sin as the root of all lostness.

The inner-being content of this characteristic is above all the act of faith — the will and heart turning toward Christ in trust and receiving what grace gives. The apostle's precision in Eph 2:8 is definitive: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God." Faith is the instrument of reception, not the source of salvation; grace is the ground, and neither grace nor faith originates in the person. The inner movement is simultaneously receptive and active — the heart believes, the will surrenders its self-protecting reflex, the mouth confesses — but none of these acts produce salvation; they are the form that receiving it takes.

What distinguishes this characteristic from its closest relatives in the family is its scope and finality. Physical rescue from mortal danger and healing wholeness through faith both engage the same Greek vocabulary but in a temporal, creaturely register; eschatological salvation is the root category's deepest extension — rescue not from storm or sickness but from the ultimate danger. Salvation anticipated and hope sustained shares its vocabulary but describes the inner posture of one who waits; this characteristic describes the inner state of one who has arrived, or is arriving. Ransomed identity gratitude and memory describes what follows in the person who has been delivered; this characteristic names the reception itself.

Across the verse evidence, the sustained effect is an assurance of standing that deepens over time: "since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God" (Rom 5:9). The present tense of rescue and the future certainty of completion belong to the same inner reality.

### 2.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

Physical rescue from mortal danger names the inner-being state of the person at the extreme edge of creaturely existence — facing drowning, storm, plague, mortal violence, or a death that feels certain and near. What defines this characteristic is not the event of rescue alone but the inner experience that surrounds it: the terror that precedes the cry, the desperate turning toward God that constitutes the cry, and the collapse of self-sufficient composure that makes the cry genuine. Peter, sinking into the waves, says everything this characteristic carries in four words: "Lord, save me" (Mat 14:30). The inner movement is from confidence to panic to petition in an instant.

What this characteristic is not is equally important. It is not eschatological salvation in another register — the rescue sought is physical, the danger mortal, and the inner-being content is the fear-faith dynamic under immediate threat. The word of the cross is not what Peter cries for in the waves. Scripture holds the distinction firmly: the same vocabulary that names eternal rescue also names this one, but the two are not the same. This characteristic is the creaturely encounter with the limit of mortal life — the moment when only God can deliver and the inner being knows it.

A further complexity: physical rescue from mortal danger is the most exclusively fallen-condition characteristic in this family. Every instance in the evidence — storm, crucifixion, plague, flood-judgment — belongs to a world shaped by threat and death. The relational-vocative capacity that drives the cry (the person's ability to address God under extremity) is itself an image-bearing faculty; but the conditions that occasion it belong entirely to a broken world. As the storm on Paul's voyage reached its worst, "all hope of our being saved was at last abandoned" (Act 27:20) — the inner being in pure despair-collapse. It is precisely from that extreme that this characteristic most sharply distinguishes itself from hope sustained, which holds on. Physical rescue from mortal danger meets the person at the moment when holding on has stopped.

Strikingly, the evidence places Christ himself in this register: "In the days of his flesh, Jesus offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverence" (Heb 5:7). The Son's anguished petition under the weight of mortal extremity establishes that this characteristic is not merely creaturely weakness but the proper human inner posture before God under the threat of death — and it establishes the pattern for all creaturely prayer in crisis.

### 2.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

Healing wholeness through faith exercised names the inner-being characteristic of the person whose act of faith opens the way to a rescue that is simultaneously physical and spiritual — where healing and saving are inseparable. "Take heart, daughter; your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22). The word translated "made you well" is the same word translated "saved" elsewhere in the same Gospels. Here it means both: the woman's bodily condition is restored and something deeper is declared whole. The two registers cannot be cleanly separated because Scripture does not separate them.

The inner faculty engaged in this characteristic is faith as an activating and directional force. It is not passive confidence but a formed interior conviction that moves the person toward Christ — the woman in the crowd reaching through to touch his garment, Bartimaeus refusing to be silenced, Jairus's father hearing the word "do not fear; only believe, and she will be well" (Luk 8:50) and holding. The faith this characteristic describes is interior trust expressed through action, against the pressure of fear and discouragement.

This characteristic is distinguished from eschatological salvation received by faith by its register rather than its mechanism — faith is the operative instrument in both, but here the specific object of faith is wholeness in the present, bodily-spiritual healing in the encounter with Christ. It is distinguished from physical rescue from mortal danger by its texture: rescue from mortal danger is terror-driven and immediate; healing wholeness through faith is formed, directed, and activating — the interior movement of a person who has come to believe that wholeness is available in this encounter. And it is distinguished from conscience cleansed through atonement in that the primary dynamic here is the person's faith as the activating instrument rather than a priestly mechanism operating on their behalf.

The characteristic carries a distinctly intercessory mode as well: "the prayer of faith will save the one who is sick, and the Lord will raise him up. And if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven" (Jam 5:15). Another's faith, prayed in solidarity, becomes the mechanism of the sick person's rescue — the two dimensions of wholeness, physical and relational, converging in a single act.

### 2.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

Conscience cleansed through atonement received names the inner-being state of the person who stands at the receiving end of atonement — guilt removed, wrath averted, sin covered. It is the experience, from the inside, of what the priestly system was designed to produce and what Christ definitively accomplished: the conscience delivered from its burden, the standing before God restored. "The priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" (Lev 4:20). The outcome is named plainly: forgiveness — and what forgiveness means is a conscience no longer laden, a person no longer standing under condemnation.

The characteristic is constituted by what is received rather than what is done. The atoned-for person does not enact the atonement; they receive its effect. The blood on the altar, provided by God himself ("I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls," Lev 17:11), covers what the person could not cover for themselves. Christ is set forth by God "as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith" (Rom 3:25). The inner-being content is the resultant state: guilt removed, conscience cleared, relational access to God restored.

This characteristic differs from priestly mediation in its direction: one is the state of the atoned-for, the other the work of the atoning mediator. They share the same vocabulary root but are structurally distinct — the programme has identified them as the two registers of a single Hebrew word, with this characteristic naming what is received and the next naming what is enacted. Conscience cleansed through atonement received also differs from eschatological salvation in its primary register: the accent here is specifically on moral-relational restoration, the conscience cleared and the guilt-burden lifted, rather than on the comprehensive rescue from condemnation that eschatological salvation describes. A singular and honest complexity resides in Eze 16:63, where God's act of atonement for all Jerusalem's sins produces in the atoned-for person not only freedom but enduring shame: "that you may remember and be confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done." The sustained effect of atonement includes appropriate humility before what was forgiven.

### 2.5 Priestly mediation of atonement

Priestly mediation of atonement names the inner-being characteristic of the person who acts not for themselves but on behalf of others — the mediator whose vocation is to stand between a holy God and a guilty community, enacting the covering that others require and cannot provide for themselves. Where conscience cleansed through atonement received describes what the atoned-for person experiences, this characteristic describes what the atoning mediator inhabits. They are two sides of a single transaction.

The inner-being content of the mediator is distinct and demanding. Aaron is commanded to offer atonement for himself first, before he can begin to act for others: "Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" (Lev 16:6). Self-coverage is the precondition of intercession for others — the mediator must be a covered person before becoming a covering presence. The inner-being sequence is: burden of one's own need, then the assumption of others' need. The comprehensive scope of the vocation is named plainly: "He shall make atonement for the holy sanctuary, and he shall make atonement for the tent of meeting and for the altar, and he shall make atonement for the priests and for all the people of the assembly" (Lev 16:33). Nothing within the worshipping community is left unaddressed.

This characteristic stands structurally apart from every other in this family. All other characteristics describe inner-being states of the person who receives rescue; this one describes the inner-being of the person who enacts it for others. Its place in this family is itself a finding: Scripture treats the mediatorial vocation as part of the inner-life of salvation, not merely its procedural mechanism. The mediator has an inner life — solidarity-burden, sequenced faithfulness, crisis-responsiveness — and that inner life is the subject here. A crisis mode is most vividly on record in the plague narrative: Moses commands Aaron to "take your censer… and make atonement for them, for wrath has gone out from the Lord; the plague has begun" (Num 16:46). The mediator is not a functionary; the mediator is a person whose inner being is engaged by the weight of others' survival.

### 2.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

Salvation anticipated and hope sustained names the inner-being posture of the person who has not yet received what they are waiting for, but who is actively, persistently, and directionally oriented toward it. It is not passive resignation or bare endurance. It is the deliberate turning of the whole self toward a salvation that is real, promised, and coming — held open as a living expectation rather than collapsed into the present moment. As Jacob's deathbed word makes the posture plain: "I wait for your salvation, O Lord" (Gen 49:18). The waiting is willed. The trust is active. The object is definite.

This characteristic is distinguished from eschatological salvation received by faith by its temporal register: the person described here has not yet arrived but is moving toward the destination. The soul lives "in this hope" (Rom 8:24) — saved already in one sense, but not yet in possession of what is promised in fullness. The key verse states the logic precisely: "Now hope that is seen is not hope. For who hopes for what he sees?" The characteristic belongs to the gap between the already and the not-yet, and its inner-being content is the capacity to inhabit that gap without collapse. It is distinguished from ransomed identity gratitude and memory, which looks backward toward what has already been given; this characteristic looks forward toward what is still coming.

The evidence gives the characteristic two complementary sources of sustaining energy. The first is the eternal durability of the salvation toward which the person waits: "for the heavens vanish like smoke, the earth will wear out like a garment, and they who dwell in it will die in like manner; but my salvation will be forever, and my righteousness will never be dismayed" (Isa 51:6). The cosmos is mortal; the salvation is not. The second source is the warrior-character of God himself, whose commitment to the completion of salvation is identity-constitutive rather than conditional. The soul that waits in hope waits for a God who does not abandon what he began.

A meaningful silence in this characteristic: where the evidence for other characteristics is often silent on the inner experience of the mediator or the procedurally framed, here the silence is at the opposite extreme — the characteristic has no verse from which God waits for salvation in unfulfilled longing. God drives salvation; the creature awaits it. The longing under deprivation is exclusively creaturely. That gap between divine fullness and creaturely need is precisely what this characteristic inhabits.

### 2.7 Ransomed identity gratitude and memory

Ransomed identity gratitude and memory names the inner-being characteristic of the person who knows themselves to be the ransomed — delivered by God's covenantal initiative from slavery, bondage, oppression, or death — and whose inner life is shaped by that knowledge: shaped by gratitude, formed by memory, grounded in an identity that is constituted by the ransom received. The past event of rescue is not merely historical information; it is the anchor of the self. "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the Lord your God redeemed you; therefore I command you this today" (Deu 15:15). The memory generates the will. The ransom shapes the obligation.

This characteristic is distinguished from eschatological salvation received by faith by its temporal orientation: where that characteristic describes the moment and ongoing reality of reception, this one describes what the person becomes over time as a result of having been ransomed. The inner-being content is sustained, formative, and identity-constituting. It is also distinguished by its ethical reach: ransomed identity produces a specific will toward others, particularly the vulnerable. The ransomed person remembers what it was to need rescue and cannot treat another person's need as indifferent. The cross-register with the wider theme of gratitude-producing obligation is built into the evidence from the beginning.

The range of the ransom Scripture evidences in this characteristic is striking — from the Exodus liberation to the innermost existential threat: "I shall ransom them from the power of Sheol; I shall redeem them from Death" (Hos 13:14). The ransomed identity is not merely the identity of someone delivered from historical bondage; at its fullest extent it is the identity of one whom God has claimed back from Death itself. From that deepest rescue flows an inner life of trust, intercession, gratitude, and sustained joy — most vividly expressed in the ransomed returning to Zion: "everlasting joy shall be upon their heads; they shall obtain gladness and joy, and sorrow and sighing shall flee away" (Isa 35:10). The ransomed person is, in the end, a person whose characteristic inner state is joy — the settled, unsentimental joy of one who knows what they were redeemed from and by whom.
