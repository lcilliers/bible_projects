## 1. What this study is

The Bible's vocabulary of salvation, redemption, and deliverance maps a remarkably large territory of the inner life. Seven related characteristics shape that territory together — and together they describe what happens to a human being, from the inside, when God saves. This study examines each of those characteristics in turn, working from the verse evidence to understand where each one lives in the person, how it moves, how it relates to the others, and what it looks like from every angle the evidence affords.

The seven characteristics belong together because they share a structural logic: in every case, the initiating agency is God, not the human person. Whether the event is eschatological rescue from condemnation, physical deliverance from mortal danger, healing wholeness through faith, the cleansing of a conscience burdened by guilt, the priestly enactment of atonement on behalf of others, the sustained forward-orientation of a soul waiting for salvation not yet arrived, or the formative memory and gratitude of one who knows themselves to be ransomed — the saving act originates outside the person and moves toward them. As the apostle states with unusual precision, "by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8). That sentence governs the cluster. Salvation in all its forms is gift before it is anything else.

This shared architecture generates a further cluster-wide pattern: the characteristics together constitute a comprehensive picture of how God's saving work engages the full range of human inner-being. Across the seven, salvation touches the will, the heart, the conscience, the memory, the capacity for hope, the faculty of trust, and the deep awareness of identity. No single part of the person is untouched. The analytical evidence shows that all seven characteristics involve multiple inner capacities simultaneously — they are not simple events but layered, complex encounters between the human person and a God whose nature is, as the evidence consistently demonstrates, constitutively oriented toward the rescue, covering, and restoration of the creature.

Within this unity there is real variation. The characteristics differ in their temporal register: some are primarily retrospective — shaped by a ransom already paid and remembered — while others face forward, sustained by what has not yet arrived. Some are explicitly creaturely, occupying a register that belongs to the image-bearer specifically because God by definition does not share it: God is not atoned for, not the ransomed, not the one who cries in panic-terror under mortal threat. Others share a real, if asymmetric, quality with the divine: God himself wears salvation as warrior-clothing (Isa 59:17), and the human soul that hopes toward salvation images, in receptive and dependent form, the inexhaustible saving energy that belongs to the divine nature. These distinctions are findings, not merely variations in emphasis — they reveal that the inner-being theology of salvation Scripture presents is genuinely multi-dimensional, not a single experience repeated in different words.

One characteristic stands structurally apart: the priestly mediation of atonement, which describes not the inner life of the one who receives but the inner life of the one who enacts — the mediator standing between a holy God and a guilty community, covered before interceding, bearing others' weight in solitary faithfulness. Its presence in this study alongside the other six is itself a finding: salvation as Scripture presents it requires not only the receiving person but the mediating person, and both have an inner life shaped by what they inhabit.

The chapters that follow examine each characteristic in turn — what it is, what it looks like under God, where it lives in the person, how it works, how these related characteristics relate to each other, and what the human and clinical sciences observe about the corresponding human capacities — before a final chapter accounts honestly for what the study has not yet resolved.

**T0 — Divine Image and Created Design**

- *T0.1.1* — _What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?_
  → **[CLUSTER]** E — All seven characteristics converge on a single, composite portrait of God: the one whose inner nature is constitutively oriented toward the rescue, covering, and restoration of the creature. This convergence is not merely thematic repetition but a structural finding — each characteristic approaches the same divine reality from a different angle, and together they triangulate a character that cannot be reduced to any single attribute. The dominant terms emerging across characteristics are: mercy (M38-A, M38-D), responsiveness to the creature's cry (M38-B, M38-C), initiative that precedes human action (M38-D, M38-E, M38-G), warrior-zeal sustaining the saving commitment (M38-F), and covenantal love that grounds ransoming in relational fidelity rather than merit (M38-G, M38-A).

A significant convergence appears across M38-A, M38-D, and M38-G: in all three, the operative divine attribute is explicitly not justice-requiring-merit but mercy, compassion, or love as the originating source of the saving act. Tit 3:5, Psa 78:38, and Deu 7:8 form a triangulated cluster — mercy, compassion-restrained-wrath, and freely given covenantal love — that establishes this as a structural divine-character claim, not merely a local textual note.

A significant divergence appears between M38-B and M38-F on one hand and M38-E on the other. M38-B and M38-C present God as responsive — drawing near when cried to, naming faith as the operative cause of healing. M38-E presents God as the designer of a system, initiating the machinery before the human creature has yet presented a specific need. These two modes (responsive and preemptive) are not contradictory but are genuinely distinct divine-character dimensions: God who responds to the cry and God who has already provided the mechanism before the cry is made.

The most structurally unexpected finding emerges from M38-F: God does not merely give salvation but is himself clothed in it, restless until it is complete (Isa 59:17; Isa 62:1; Hab 3:8). This moves the divine-character portrait beyond benevolent dispenser to committed warrior whose zeal for salvation is identity-constitutive. No other characteristic reaches this depth of divine self-involvement. M38-G adds a further dimension from the same angle: God ransoms to "make himself a name" (2Sa 7:23) — the divine character is disclosed and constituted by the ransoming act itself, not merely expressed through it.

Across all seven, the silence about God being rescued, atoned-for, or exercising faith toward another reveals the asymmetric shape of the portrait: God's nature is wholly on the giving, initiating, covering, and completing side of every salvific transaction.
