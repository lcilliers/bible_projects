## 3. The divine pattern

Before the study turns to the inner life of the person being saved — where salvation lives in them, how it moves, how it relates to the other dimensions of their inner world — it must first ask what Scripture says about God in all of this. Each characteristic in the family carries a divine dimension. Taken together, they build a composite portrait of the God who saves. That portrait is the subject of this chapter.

---

### The arc across all seven characteristics

The first thing the evidence establishes is that God is on the giving side of every salvific transaction in this family, never on the receiving side. He does not cry in panic-terror under mortal threat. He is not atoned for. He does not exercise faith as a creaturely faculty reaching toward another. He is not the ransomed. Across all seven characteristics, the consistent finding is that the human person inhabits the receiving, dependent, creaturely position — and God inhabits the giving, initiating, covering, completing position. This asymmetry is not a deficiency in the human characteristics; it is their proper shape. They belong to the image-bearer as image-bearer, precisely because the image-bearer is not the original.

But the asymmetry is not simply that God gives and the human person receives. The evidence reveals something richer: the divine character is constitutively oriented toward the rescue, covering, and restoration of the creature. This is not a reluctant concession, not a minimal response to emergency. It is the shape of who God is. "God sent his Son not to condemn the world but to save the world" (Joh 3:17). Mercy, not justice-requiring-merit, is the originating source of the saving act. Tit 3:5 states plainly that God "saved us — not because of our righteous deeds, but according to his own mercy." Psalm 78:38 names God atoning for iniquity "out of compassion," restraining wrath rather than destroying. Deuteronomy 7:8 grounds the Exodus ransom not in Israel's worth but in God's love and the oath he swore to their fathers — covenantal fidelity given freely. These three texts together — mercy, compassion, freely given love — establish that what drives salvation across all its registers is not human deserving but divine character.

The evidence presses this further. God does not merely provide salvation from a safe distance; he is himself clothed in it, restless until it is complete. Isaiah 59:17 pictures God arming himself with a helmet of salvation; Habakkuk 3:8 sees him riding on a chariot of salvation into cosmic battle; Isaiah 62:1 records his declaration that he will not be silent or rest until Jerusalem's salvation blazes forth. These images move the portrait beyond a benevolent dispenser of benefits to a committed warrior whose zeal for salvation is identity-constitutive. The saving character of God is not incidental to who he is; it is constitutive of his self-disclosure. Second Samuel 7:23 adds a remarkable dimension: God went out to ransom Israel "to make himself a name" — meaning the divine character is disclosed and established by the ransoming act itself. The ransomed people exist as the living testimony to who God is.

The evidence also reveals a decisive structural partition when it comes to explicit divine attribution. Three characteristics are attributed to God in their operative form: eschatological salvation, conscience cleansed through atonement received, and salvation anticipated and hope sustained. For these three, God is not merely the source but the exemplar — he saves, he gives atonement, he is clothed in salvation as his own. For two others — physical rescue from mortal danger and healing wholeness through faith exercised — the rescue-capacity belongs to God, but the experiential phenomenology does not: God hears the cry and extends his hand, but does not himself cry in panic-terror or exercise faith as a creaturely faculty. For the remaining two — priestly mediation and ransomed identity gratitude — explicit divine attribution is absent or structurally impossible: God designs the priestly system but does not undergo it; God is the ransomer, not the ransomed. This partition is itself a finding. It reveals that the characteristics of this family instantiate the divine image in different modes — some by mirroring a divine attribute in creaturely form, others by occupying the creaturely-response register that has no direct divine counterpart but is entirely structured by who God is.

Every characteristic in the family is oriented toward a future fullness — this is among the evidence's most consistent findings. Justification is present, but full rescue from wrath is future (Rom 5:9). The Levitical atonement's annual reset points toward a definitive once-for-all covering. The soul that hopes is "saved in hope" but not yet in sight (Rom 8:24). The ransomed are moving toward a homecoming where "everlasting joy shall be upon their heads" (Isa 35:10). Salvation in all its forms participates in a trajectory that reaches beyond its present expression — every characteristic is eschatologically oriented. And the Exodus event functions as the shared typological reservoir across the cluster — appearing as the reference point for five of the seven characteristics, the pattern against which all subsequent rescue is understood and toward which the final comprehensive ransom points.

### Variation per characteristic

**Eschatological salvation received by faith** follows the spine most closely. God is named as the explicit Savior throughout — "salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!" (Rev 7:10). God's desire is universal ("God desires all people to be saved," 1Ti 2:4), and the saving act is grounded in pre-temporal divine purpose ("the grace purposed before time," 2Ti 1:9). The one meaningful silence is that God does not receive salvation by faith — the faith-dimension is irreducibly creaturely, the proper form of the image-bearer's dependence on the God who saves. This asymmetry illuminates the characteristic's significance: faith-as-reception is not an imperfection but the appropriate inner posture of a being constituted to receive rather than to self-generate ultimate rescue.

**Physical rescue from mortal danger** departs from the spine in one striking direction. God's rescuing responsiveness is fully present — he hears the cry, extends his hand, responds to the petition. But the defining phenomenology of the characteristic belongs exclusively to the creature: terror, panic, the desperate turning, the anguished cry. The evidence brings the Son himself into this register: "In the days of his flesh, Jesus offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death" (Heb 5:7). The anguished petition is located in the creaturely condition — "in the days of his flesh" — not in the divine nature. This characteristic is the most exclusively fallen-condition characteristic in the family: every verse belongs to a world of mortal threat. Yet the evidence deploys it typologically in its most paradoxical form: the crucifixion mockery's "he saved others; he cannot save himself" (Mar 15:31) presses the physical rescue vocabulary into ironic christological service, so that the refusal of self-rescue enacts the rescue of others.

**Healing wholeness through faith exercised** shares the responsive pattern of the previous characteristic — God through Christ responds to the interior faith of the person with integrative rescue — but the silence on divine attribution is here the most instructive. God does not exercise faith as an inner faculty in this corpus. That silence locates faith-exercised as a specifically creaturely faculty: it belongs to the image-bearer as a being in dependence, capable of reaching toward God as the source of wholeness. The divine image expressed here is the relational-receptive capacity: the human person, unlike any sub-personal creature, can form interior trust directed toward God and, by that trust, become the site where divine saving power acts. The created design dimension is visible here more than in most other characteristics: the structural capacity for interior trust directed toward God belongs to the created image, even though the specific conditions that occasion its exercise — disease, blindness, mortal threat — are fallen.

**Conscience cleansed through atonement received** receives one of the most explicit divine attributions in the family. God is not only the source but the provider: "I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls" (Lev 17:11). First John 4:10 makes the theological structure unmistakable: "In this is love — not that we loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins." The operative divine attribute is love as prior cause; the human person receives the effect. The silence is also unmistakable: God does not receive atonement, because God has no guilt requiring coverage, no wrath turned upon himself. That silence locates this characteristic entirely on the creaturely side of the Creator-creature distinction — it names the relational space between a holy God and a guilty creature where divine holiness, compassion, and covering-will meet human finitude and moral failure. The characteristic belongs to the image not as a direct reflection of a divine attribute but as the receptive orientation toward God that makes divine-to-human acts like atonement possible. Eze 16:63 is unique in this characteristic's evidence: God's act of atonement produces in the atoned-for person "shame" and "silence" alongside freedom — the sustained effect includes an appropriate, enduring humility before what was forgiven, a complexity found nowhere else in the family.

**Priestly mediation of atonement** has no divine attribution of the characteristic itself — God does not undergo the priestly mediation machinery, does not require coverage before mediating, does not function as a Levitical priest. This silence is structurally informative rather than merely negative: it places the characteristic in the domain of creaturely vocation, not divine self-expression. God designs and commands the human system — "the LORD commanded the ordination ritual for atonement" (Lev 8:34) — but the enacted vocation belongs to the creature. What is distinctive about this characteristic's typological structure is its direction of pointing: unlike most characteristics in the family where the divine instance establishes the pattern and the human instance follows, here the human institution points upward. The Levitical system's internal incompleteness — the annual reset, the mediator's own sinfulness requiring coverage first, the sanctuary still defiled year after year — signals that this human institution is itself a type oriented toward a greater high priest. The characteristic points by inadequacy rather than by explicit promise.

**Salvation anticipated and hope sustained** carries one of the most striking divine attributions in the family: God himself is clothed in salvation, drives toward it as a warrior, and will not rest until it blazes forth. The divine side of this characteristic is not the waiting but the completing — God does not wait for salvation in unfulfilled longing; God drives it irresistibly. The human soul's waiting is the creaturely mirror of the divine completing. That gap between divine fullness and creaturely longing is what this characteristic inhabits — it is not a reflection of a divine experience but of the divine provision toward which the creature reaches. As the evidence states most nakedly: "saved in hope. Now hope that is seen is not hope. For who hopes for what he sees?" (Rom 8:24). The specifically creaturely form of the characteristic — longing under deprivation, anguish that salvation is deferred, human effort producing nothing — belongs exclusively to the creature. The divine side exists; the creaturely form does not.

**Ransomed identity gratitude and memory** has no divine attribution of the characteristic in its operative form — God is the ransomer, not the ransomed. He does not hold ransomed identity gratitude because he is not the beneficiary of rescue from another. Yet this silence is surrounded by the richest divine attribution in the family: God ransoms "to make himself a name" (2Sa 7:23), meaning the ransoming act is constitutive of divine self-disclosure. The human characteristic of ransomed gratitude therefore exists as the creaturely reception of an act that is simultaneously constitutive of who God is. To possess ransomed identity gratitude is to be correctly calibrated to the most defining thing God does — it is the appropriate inner-being posture of the creature before the saving Creator. The eschatological reach of this characteristic is among the most expansive in the family: God announces ransom "from the power of Sheol" and "from Death" itself (Hos 13:14). The ransomed identity is not merely retrospective; it is moving toward a final homecoming whose joy is everlasting.
