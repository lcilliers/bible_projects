## 6. How each characteristic relates to the others

The seven characteristics in this study do not stand in isolation. They pair, oppose, produce, and follow each other in patterns the verse evidence makes visible. This chapter maps those relationships — what each characteristic travels with, what it stands against, what generates it, and what it generates in turn.

---

### 6.1 Eschatological salvation received by faith

Eschatological salvation received by faith occupies a hub position in this family — it is the event-state from which the other characteristics radiate as effects, or to which they flow as conditions, rather than one node among equals.

Its most consistent pairing is with conscience cleansed through atonement received. The two appear together wherever salvation is experienced as the clearing of guilt: forgiveness and conscience-cleansing are the immediate inner-being consequences of salvation received, naming the specific terrain that salvation transforms. They are not the same characteristic — eschatological salvation is the comprehensive rescue event, conscience cleansed through atonement received is its forensic-relational dimension — but they are inseparable in the verse evidence. "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God" (Eph 2:8): the act of rescue and the clearing of standing before God arrive together.

Repentance — the will's turning away from sin toward God — consistently precedes or accompanies reception. "Godly grief produces a repentance that leads to salvation without regret" (2Cor 7:10). The repentance is not a cause of salvation (salvation is entirely grace-given) but is the inner movement through which the will aligns with what grace offers. It is the inner threshold of reception rather than its source. Faith is the operative instrument of reception: not the cause of salvation but the inner-being mode through which grace reaches the person, engaging the heart's trust, the will's surrender, and the mouth's confession simultaneously.

What does eschatological salvation produce? Peace is the most consistent downstream inner state: "your faith has saved you; go in peace" (Luk 7:50). The inner tension between guilt and condemnation resolves into settled relational rest when salvation is received. Doxological joy is the further downstream effect — the redeemed ascribing salvation entirely to God and the Lamb (Rev 7:10). And missional orientation toward others is a sustained downstream product: the apostle whose whole inner identity is reorganised around others' rescue (1Cor 9:22) is salvation received generating its most outward-directed form.

What produces this characteristic? Grace is the originating cause; Christ's atoning work is the meritorious ground; the proclaimed word is the instrumental channel; and faith is the receptive condition. These four operate at different levels — none of which are equivalent or interchangeable. The word proclaimed must be heard; the heart must believe; the will must surrender; the mouth must confess. These are not conditions in the sense of human merit but the forms through which reception takes place.

The most important relational distinction: this characteristic is not simply justification in another name. Justification (the forensic declaration of the sinner as righteous) is one constitutive dimension of eschatological salvation — "much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God" (Rom 5:9, where justification is named as the preceding basis). Salvation is the wider event; justification is its specific forensic-relational inner dimension. The two are present together in the evidence, but salvation encompasses more: it includes regeneration, sanctification, preservation, and final completion that justification as a concept does not fully name.

Salvation anticipated and hope sustained stands in a distinctive sequential relationship to this characteristic: anticipatory waiting precedes and moves toward received salvation, so that this characteristic is the developmental culmination of what hope sustains. The soul that waits in hope (salvation anticipated) is moving toward the inner-being state of salvation received; both inhabit the same salvific reality but from different temporal positions — one inside the not-yet, the other crossing into the already.

### 6.2 Physical rescue from mortal danger

Physical rescue from mortal danger is most distinctively paired with faith — specifically with faith as the activating instrument of the cry under threat. "Lord, save me" (Mat 14:30). The faith and the cry are not sequential; they are the same inner act: the will turning toward God as the only possible agent of rescue. The characteristic travels with urgent, affect-laden trust rather than with composed theological reflection.

The characteristic is opposed most sharply by two inner conditions: despair-as-closure and faithless demand. "When neither sun nor stars appeared for many days, and no small tempest lay on us, all hope of our being saved was at last abandoned" (Act 27:20) — this is the faith-cry extinguished, hope replaced by silent resignation. The other opposite is the demand that commands rescue without turning toward God in dependence (Luk 23:39). The characteristic stands between these: it is the cry of the one who knows themselves endangered and directs that knowledge toward God rather than collapsing into self or toward God in demand rather than petition.

What does this characteristic produce? In the extended mode (Noah), it produces household rescue and inherited righteousness — "by this he condemned the world and became an heir of the righteousness that comes by faith" (Heb 11:7). The faith-cry under mortal threat deepens, over time, into a covenantal identity as a person of faith. In the crisis mode, it produces relief — and in Jesus's case it produces willed surrender rather than physical rescue: anguish heard and transformed into reverent obedience (Heb 5:7). The characteristic produces different downstream states depending on which mode it operates in.

What relationship does this characteristic bear to eschatological salvation received by faith? The two share the same lexical root — sōzō names both physical rescue and spiritual salvation — but they are distinguished by kind, not degree. Physical rescue from mortal danger addresses temporal, creaturely life; eschatological salvation addresses eternal standing. The same word operates in two conceptually distinct registers, and the evidence insists on the distinction. Physical rescue from mortal danger is the family member most directly paired with fear as its precipitating inner condition — terror is what activates the cry. But fear in this context is the occasion of the characteristic rather than its opposition; it is the existential pressure that strips away self-sufficiency and reveals the relational-vocative capacity (the ability to address God) as the last remaining act.

A relational finding worth naming explicitly: there is no evidence that this characteristic depends on memorial engagement. It is constitutively present-tense — the crisis is now, the cry is now, the rescue is now. This contrasts sharply with ransomed identity gratitude and memory, which is constitutively backward-looking. The two are the temporal extremes of the family: one lives only in the present moment of extremity; the other lives in the formative memory of past rescue.

### 6.3 Healing wholeness through faith exercised

Healing wholeness through faith exercised is most consistently paired with peace as its immediate downstream inner-being state. The faith-cry or formed interior conviction produces the sōzō event, and the sōzō event is sealed by peace: "your faith has made you well" (Mat 9:22), and the peace follows as the relational completion of the rescue. The pairing is not incidental — peace names what integrated restoration feels like from the inside.

The characteristic is also paired with conscience cleansed through atonement received in its epistolary extension. "The prayer of faith will save the one who is sick, and the Lord will raise him up. And if he has committed sins, he will be forgiven" (Jam 5:15). When the characteristic operates at its fullest range, physical healing and conscience-cleansing converge: sōzō encompasses both body and moral-relational standing. This pairing reveals that wholeness in this family is not merely physical but integrated — the faith that activates healing also opens the person to moral restoration when that is needed.

What does this characteristic produce? Three downstream states are evidenced. First, peace — given by Jesus as the authoritative relational completion of faith-activated wholeness (Mar 5:34; Luk 8:48). Second, conscience cleansed — the deeper inner layer addressed when faith exercises the characteristic fully (Jam 5:15; 1Pe 3:21). Third, discipleship initiated — Bartimaeus, upon receiving sight, "followed him on the way" (Mar 10:52). The wholeness of person that faith activates reorients the person toward Jesus; the healed disciple follows. This third downstream state is the characteristic's most explicit outward-movement: rescue received opening into sustained relational following.

What produces this characteristic? Faith is the operative cause, named explicitly and repeatedly by Jesus's own formula. But the verse evidence also shows that faith here arises from urgency and desperation in the crisis mode: Jairus's parental desperation (Mar 5:23), the grief at Lazarus's death, Luk 8:50's mortal threat. These are not the causes of faith but the pressure-contexts within which faith is formed and exercised. The characteristic does not require crisis to operate — the composed interior conviction of the woman in Mat 9:21 precedes any crisis — but crisis is the most vivid evidenced context.

The most important relational distinction in this family is between healing wholeness through faith exercised and eschatological salvation received by faith. They share the sōzō root, they both engage faith as the operative instrument, and they both produce inner-being transformation. But the distinction is one of direction: healing wholeness through faith is oriented toward present bodily-spiritual integration in the encounter with Christ; eschatological salvation is oriented toward eternal standing before God in the judicial-covenantal register. The faith is the same inner faculty operating in different directions toward different objects.

"Do not fear; only believe, and she will be well" (Luk 8:50) — the opposition named in this verse is fear. Fear is the presenting inner condition that this characteristic displaces. Where physical rescue from mortal danger is activated by terror (the cry arises from the fear), healing wholeness through faith exercised is the inner movement that overcomes and replaces fear with formed trust. The two characteristics are therefore related across the fear-axis: one moves through fear toward crisis-cry; the other moves against fear into formed conviction.

### 6.4 Conscience cleansed through atonement received

Conscience cleansed through atonement received is most consistently paired with forgiveness as its immediate inner-being outcome — and forgiveness is not a separate characteristic but the conscience-register name for what atonement produces. "The priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven" (Lev 4:20). The pairing is so tight as to be definitional: this characteristic is the inner-being state of being forgiven, and forgiveness is the name for what the conscience registers when the covering mechanism has done its work.

It is also structurally paired with priestly mediation of atonement — the two are the two sides of a single transaction, sharing the same Hebrew root. Priestly mediation of atonement is the enacted cause; conscience cleansed through atonement received is the received effect. Neither exists without the other: there is no conscience-cleansed state without the enacted covering, and no point to the enacted covering without the conscience-clearing effect. They are related as mechanism to outcome. The programme draws the boundary between them at the directional level — the mediator's inner life on one side, the recipient's inner life on the other — but the two are conceptually fused in every verse that names both.

What does conscience cleansed through atonement received produce? Three downstream states are evidenced. First, forgiven standing before God — the relational access that guilt had blocked is restored. This is the characteristic's most immediate product. Second, enduring shame and silenced contrition — uniquely evidenced in Eze 16:63: "that you may remember and be confounded, and never open your mouth again because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done." The shame here is not guilt retained but the proper humility of one who has been comprehensively covered and knows it. This is the family's most distinctive sustained emotional product — a permanent affective state that accompanies forgiveness rather than replacing it. Third, moral turning and vocation — the atoned person is reoriented toward righteousness (Pro 16:6) and enabled for prophetic service (Isa 6:7–8). The cleared conscience opens into commissioned purpose.

What produces this characteristic? The enacted atonement mechanism — sacrifice, blood, priestly declaration — is the external instrumental cause. Divine love is the originating cause: "In this is love — not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins" (1Jo 4:10). Faith is the receptive condition in the NT register: "whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received by faith" (Rom 3:25). The enabling inner conditions are contrition and sin-awareness (Luk 18:13), sincere heart-seeking (2Ch 30:18), and personal identification with the mechanism. The blocking conditions are entrenched unrepentant sin (1Sa 3:14), pride, and willful defiance.

The relationship to eschatological salvation received by faith is one of intersection and distinction. The two share the inner-being terrain of guilt-before-God and its resolution; they co-occur wherever forgiveness and right standing before God are named together. But conscience cleansed through atonement received is the moral-relational dimension specifically: it addresses the conscience as the faculty that registers guilt and receives clearing. Eschatological salvation encompasses this but also names the comprehensive rescue from condemnation, death, and the future wrath that conscience-clearing alone does not name.

The characteristic's relationship to wrath is its structural boundary on the other side: where wrath is active and unaddressed, this characteristic is absent or blocked. Psa 78:38 shows God atoning for iniquity "out of compassion, restraining wrath" — the act of atonement and the averted wrath are two sides of the same event. Conscience cleansed through atonement received is the inner-being register of the person on whose behalf that wrath is averted; the wrath-facing, unatoned state is the characteristic's structural opposite.

### 6.5 Priestly mediation of atonement

Priestly mediation of atonement is most distinctively related to conscience cleansed through atonement received — the two are the closest pairing in the family and the most carefully differentiated. The priestly mediator's inner life and the recipient's inner life inhabit the same transaction from opposite sides. Every verse that shows priestly mediation enacted is simultaneously the occasion for conscience-cleansed atonement received in the beneficiary. Their relationship is structural complementarity: mechanism and outcome, bridge and destination.

The characteristic is produced by two concurrent generating conditions. From above: divine command and mandate. "Aaron shall offer the bull as a sin offering for himself and shall make atonement for himself and for his house" (Lev 16:6) — the rite is God-instituted and God-commanded (Lev 8:34); it exists because God established it, not because human creativity invented it. From below: the existence of sin and uncleanness. The mediatorial machinery is activated by the community's need; without antecedent guilt or defilement there is no occasion for mediated atonement. Divine mandate and human need are the co-generative conditions.

What does this characteristic produce? Two downstream states are evidenced. First, the atoned/clean inner state of the beneficiary — what the programme holds as conscience cleansed through atonement received. "He shall make atonement for the holy sanctuary, and he shall make atonement for the tent of meeting and for the altar, and he shall make atonement for the priests and for all the people of the assembly" (Lev 16:33) — the comprehensive scope of the mediation produces comprehensive coverage of the community's standing. Second, physical safety from divine wrath as a community-protective outcome: "carry it quickly to the congregation and make atonement for them, for wrath has gone out from the Lord; the plague has begun" (Num 16:46) — Aaron's intercession halts the plague. The mediatorial act produces both inner-being restoration for individuals and communal protection from active judgment.

The characteristic's most important structural feature is its bridging position in the family. The chain runs: guilt/uncleanness → priestly mediation → conscience cleansed/community safe. Priestly mediation of atonement is the middle term — never the origin (guilt precedes it) and never the terminus (the beneficiary's cleared state follows it). It is the structural mechanism through which the family's central transaction is enacted. This bridge-position explains why the characteristic's inner-being content is dominated by the will's ordered execution rather than by the affective or conscience dimensions that dominate other characteristics: the mediator's job is to enact, not to feel.

The characteristic stands in a typological relationship to eschatological salvation received by faith that the evidence carries implicitly. The annual reset, the mediator's own sinfulness requiring self-coverage, the sanctuary still defiling — these signal that the mechanism points beyond itself. Aaron and his sons make atonement for all Israel "according to all that Moses the servant of God had commanded" (1Ch 6:49). The faithful adherence to the prescribed sequence maintains what the eschatological resolution will one day complete. The two characteristics are therefore related as anticipatory-type to receiving-of-antitype: the priestly machinery prepared for and pointed toward the once-for-all mediatorial act that resolves what the annual cycle only maintained.

### 6.6 Salvation anticipated and hope sustained

Salvation anticipated and hope sustained is most distinctively paired with faith exercised as its constitutive inner mechanism. The soul cannot sustain salvation-anticipation without faith as the active faculty that holds the posture together. "I wait for your salvation, O Lord" (Gen 49:18) — the waiting is an act of faith, not a passive sentiment; if faith collapses, the waiting collapses with it into either despair or self-reliance. Faith is not a separate characteristic alongside hope sustained but is internal to it: hope sustained is faith exercised in the temporal mode of not-yet.

The characteristic is closely related to — though distinct from — eschatological salvation received by faith. They share the same root vocabulary, the same saving object (God as Savior), and the same inner-being reality (salvation). The distinction is temporal direction: this characteristic orients the inner being forward toward an approaching salvation; eschatological salvation received by faith orients it into or upon a received salvation. The boundary is stated most precisely in Rom 8:24: "saved in hope. Now hope that is seen is not hope. For who hopes for what he sees?" — the aorist passive marks the received dimension while the sentence immediately opens onto the not-yet. This characteristic inhabits the not-yet side of that boundary. Where salvation anticipated crosses into salvation possessed, this characteristic transitions into eschatological salvation received by faith.

The characteristic also relates closely to hope as it appears elsewhere in the programme — particularly eschatological hope in its broader form. Salvation anticipated and hope sustained is the salvation-specific content of a wider hope-posture: hope-as-form finds its filling in the specific object of divine rescue. The two are related as form to content rather than as genus to species.

What does salvation anticipated and hope sustained produce? Three downstream characteristics are evidenced. First, righteous conduct and aligned will — "for the heavens vanish like smoke, the earth will wear out like a garment... but my salvation will be forever" (Isa 51:6) anchors the soul in an eternal salvation; Isa 56:1 shows the nearness of that salvation motivating righteous conduct in the present. The eschatological orientation reshapes present ethical behavior. Second, sobriety and vigilance — hope of salvation functioning as a "helmet" (1Th 5:8) guards the mind and will against disorientation. Third, endurance under affliction — the characteristic's sustained posture of forward-oriented waiting forms the capacity to persist under pressure without collapse.

What produces this characteristic? Divine preservation is the primary source: "guarded by God's power through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed" (1Pe 1:5). God's power maintains the soul's faith-posture through the interval of waiting; the characteristic is not self-generated. Covenantal promise is the second source: the soul waits because it has received a promise that grounds the anticipation. And God's own warrior declarations ("he will not rest until... salvation blazes forth," Isa 62:1) generate the inner-being assurance that sustains the wait.

"Waiting for our blessed hope, the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior Jesus Christ" (Tit 2:13) — the characteristic points forward to the eschatological terminus toward which the entire family is oriented: the appearing that transforms anticipation into reception and completes what hope has sustained through the long interim.

### 6.7 Ransomed identity gratitude and memory

Ransomed identity gratitude and memory is most distinctively related to joy — specifically, to the eschatological joy that ransomed homecoming generates. "The ransomed of the Lord shall return and come to Zion with singing; everlasting joy shall be upon their heads; they shall obtain gladness and joy, and sorrow and sighing shall flee away" (Isa 35:10). The joy is not separate from the ransomed identity but is its sustained emotional expression: the ransomed person's characteristic inner state is joy precisely because they know they are ransomed. The pairing is causal — ransomed identity produces joy, and the joy carries and expresses the identity.

The characteristic is also closely related to formative memory — the mind's capacity to hold the redemptive past as identity-constituting. "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt, and the Lord your God redeemed you; therefore I command you this today" (Deu 15:15). Memory is not a separate characteristic running alongside ransomed identity gratitude but its cognitive infrastructure: the identity persists only as long as the memory is held. Forgetting the ransom (Psa 78:42) is not merely cognitive failure but covenantal dissolution — the loss of identity itself.

What does ransomed identity gratitude and memory produce? Three downstream states are named by the evidence. First, eschatological joy — as evidenced in Isa 35:10 and the returning-to-Zion imagery (Isa 51:11). The joy is the affective signature of the ransomed identity in its fullest expression. Second, ethical will and obedience — Deu 15:15 and 24:18 both generate justice toward the vulnerable from the memory of ransom: because you were ransomed, you treat the enslaved and the alien with generosity. The ransomed identity is generative of moral conduct; it does not remain private but moves outward into ethical action. Third, intercessory confidence — "O Lord God, do not destroy your people and your heritage, whom you have redeemed through your greatness, whom you have brought out of Egypt with a mighty hand" (Deu 9:26). Moses's intercession deploys the ransomed identity as the grounds for prayer: identity as the ransomed people constitutes the basis from which the intercessor approaches God on others' behalf.

What produces this characteristic? The divine ransoming act is the external generative cause — the Exodus, the rescue from Sheol, the substitutionary firstborn ransom (Exo 13:15). The will's ongoing reception and rehearsal of the memory is the internal maintenance condition. The characteristic requires active cultivation: the memory must be commanded and rehearsed (Deu 15:15; 24:18; Psa 78:42), and the identity must be inhabited rather than merely acknowledged. The blocking condition is the will turned away in deceptive rebellion: "I would redeem them, but they speak lies against me" (Hos 7:13). Ransom offered, identity refused.

The relationship to eschatological salvation received by faith is one of temporal perspective: eschatological salvation is oriented primarily toward the eschatological future (salvation from future wrath); ransomed identity gratitude and memory is oriented toward the constitutive past (the Exodus, the ransom already given). Both are comprehensive in scope — they both name the same saving God and the same saving relationship — but from opposite temporal directions. The family thus spans the full temporal compass: one characteristic names the eschatological future toward which the soul moves; another names the formative past from which the soul draws identity. Both are required for the full inner life of the person who inhabits salvation.

A meaningful relational silence: this characteristic evidences no close pairing with conscience cleansed through atonement received. The ransomed person's inner-being state is dominated by gratitude, memory, and joy — not by the moral-forensic register of guilt and clearing that conscience cleansed through atonement received addresses. Both involve divine rescue, but the inner faculties primarily engaged are different, and the evidence does not bring them into close structural relationship within this family. The silence is a genuine boundary marker: ransomed identity lives at the identity and gratitude level, not at the guilt-and-covering level.
