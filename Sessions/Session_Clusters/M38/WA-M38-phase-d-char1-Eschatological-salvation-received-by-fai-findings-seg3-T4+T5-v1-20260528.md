**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence overwhelmingly shows eschatological salvation operating primarily as a divine initiative directed toward the human person. God is the consistent grammatical and theological agent: in Joh 3:17 "God sent his Son not to condemn but to save the world"; in Eph 2:8 salvation by grace through faith is "God's gift"; in Tit 3:5 "God saved us not by our righteous deeds but by his mercy"; in 2Ti 1:9 "God saved believers and called them to holiness by grace purposed before time." The Spirit is given as dōrea (Act 2:38, Act 10:45, Act 11:17). Righteousness is bestowed as gift (Rom 5:15-17, M38-A-VCG-03). Christ is the Savior sent by divine appointment (Act 13:23, 1Jo 4:14, Act 5:31). The divine-to-human vector is structurally foundational — salvation does not originate in the human person and moves toward them from outside, grounded in divine purpose established before time.

---

**T4.2.1 — Divine Interface — Human to God — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows multiple forms of human movement toward God in connection with eschatological salvation. Calling on the name of the Lord (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13) is an act of invocation directed toward God. Heart-belief and mouth-confession (Rom 10:9-10) are inner and outer acts of orientation toward God and Christ. The jailer's urgent question "what must I do to be saved?" (Act 16:30) represents conscience-awakened seeking directed toward the divine. Simeon's reception of salvation (Luk 2:30) and his readiness to depart in peace represent fulfilled hope resting in God. Doxological response — the heavenly multitude ascribing salvation to God and the Lamb (Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1) — constitutes worship as the human-to-God culmination of salvation. The apostle's intercessory prayer for others' salvation (Rom 10:1) represents the saved person's movement toward God on behalf of others.

---

**T4.3.1 — Human Interface — Giving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows eschatological salvation being extended person-to-person in several modes. Peter exhorts the crowd to be saved from a corrupt generation (Act 2:40), functioning as a herald extending the opportunity of rescue. Paul adapts himself to all people in order to save some (1Cor 9:22) and denies himself to seek others' rescue (1Cor 10:33). Paul endures suffering so the elect may obtain salvation (2Ti 2:10). The apostle aims to provoke jealousy in his kinspeople to save some (Rom 11:14). A teacher who watches his doctrine and life will save his hearers (1Ti 4:16). Jam 5:20 names rescuing a wandering sinner as saving a soul from death. Jude 23 urges snatching others from fire. The human giving of this characteristic is consistently mediated through proclamation, witness, relational persistence, and costly self-sacrifice — the person does not originate salvation but acts as its conduit.

---

**T4.4.1 — Human Interface — Receiving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence frequently presents salvation as received through a human intermediary bearing a proclaimed message. Act 11:14 links household salvation to a message proclaimed by Cornelius's messenger. Act 13:26 presents the word of salvation as dispatched to receptive hearers. Eph 1:13 structures reception as: "hearing the gospel of salvation → believing → being sealed with the Spirit." Rom 1:16 identifies the gospel as God's power unto salvation received through faith in the message. 1Cor 1:21 states God chose the "foolishness of preaching to save those who believe," making the proclaimed word the human channel. Jam 1:21 presents the "implanted word" as the medium through which salvation is received. The inner act required for reception is faith — hearing, trusting, and accepting the message rather than generating rescue from within.

**T4.5.1 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows salvation operating across relational distance as a characteristic feature. Joh 4:42 identifies Jesus as "Savior of the world," confirmed by Samaritans — crossing ethnic and religious boundaries. Act 13:47 mandates salvation to the ends of the earth. Rom 11:11 and 11:26 show salvation moving from Israel's stumbling to Gentile inclusion and back. Act 10:45 and Act 11:17 present the Spirit-gift given equally to Gentiles as astonishing boundary-crossing. 1Ti 2:4 states God desires "all people" to be saved. Within existing bonds, 1Cor 7:16 shows a believing spouse holding uncertain hope for an unbelieving partner, and 1Ti 2:15 links salvation to faithful domestic conduct. The evidence suggests no operation restricted to pre-existing relational bonds — salvation characteristically crosses ethnic, social, and religious distance — but relational proximity also functions as a channel (household salvation, Act 11:14, Act 16:31).

**T4.6.1 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings — angelic or adversarial — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows both angelic and adversarial engagement with eschatological salvation. On the angelic side: Luk 2:11 presents an angel announcing the Savior's birth; Rev 7:10 and Rev 12:10 depict heavenly beings witnessing and proclaiming salvation's completion. Rev 12:10 specifically names the accuser's defeat as the moment salvation is declared — the heavenly accuser is cast down, conscience of the accused is vindicated. On the adversarial side: Luk 8:12 shows the devil actively removing the word from hearts to prevent belief and thereby block salvation — faith is the battleground. Rev 12:10 identifies Satan as the one who "accuses them day and night," framing eschatological salvation as the resolution of adversarial accusation. The adversary works against the reception of salvation; angelic beings witness and declare it.

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals a complex basis combining unconditional divine initiative with covenantal grounding and responsive appropriation. The initiative is unconditional in origin: Eph 2:8 states salvation is "God's gift, not of works"; 2Ti 1:9 places it in grace "purposed before time"; Tit 3:5 grounds it in "mercy" not human righteousness. The covenantal dimension is explicit: Act 13:23 roots the Savior in David's covenant line; Rom 11:26-27 grounds all Israel's future rescue in prophetic covenant promise. The responsive dimension is present but secondary: salvation is appropriated by those who call (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13), believe (Eph 2:8, Act 16:31), confess (Rom 10:9), and hear (Eph 1:13). The faith-response is itself not an originating condition but a reception point — Eph 2:8 explicitly denies that even faith is self-generated. The overall structure is: unconditional divine initiative, covenantally grounded, received through responsive faith.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence identifies multiple inner postures required for the human-toward-God movement in receiving salvation. Heart-belief (Rom 10:9-10) names an inner faculty of trust directed toward the risen Christ. Calling on the Lord's name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13) requires the will to invoke, an active turning of the person toward God. Repentance (Act 2:38, Act 2:40) involves a decisive inner turning away from sin and moral separation. Godly sorrow (2Cor 7:10) identifies a specific grief-quality that produces genuine repentance rather than worldly despair. Meekness/humility of will (Jam 1:21) enables the soul to receive the implanted word. The self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35, Luk 9:24) reveal that surrender of self-protective instincts is the posture through which the true self is eschatologically saved. An awakened conscience seeking rescue (Act 16:30) and a will striving to enter the narrow gate (Luk 13:23) also appear. The common inner posture is receptive, non-self-sufficient, will-turning orientation.

**T4.3.2 — Human Interface — Giving — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals specific inner conditions required in those who mediate salvation to others. Missional love is foundational: Paul's self-adaptation (1Cor 9:22) and self-denial (1Cor 10:33) are driven by love for others' rescue, with the will wholly oriented toward the eschatological good of others rather than personal advantage. Intercessory desire and prayer (Rom 10:1) name the heart's sustained longing as an enabling condition. Willingness to suffer for others' rescue (2Ti 2:10) identifies self-sacrifice as an enabling posture. Faithful teaching with a watchful conscience (1Ti 4:16) names doctrinal and moral integrity as enabling conditions for saving one's hearers. The rescue of wandering sinners (Jam 5:20) and snatching from fire (Jude 23) require courageous, fear-engaged moral seriousness. The common thread is: the giver must themselves be rooted in received grace (not self-originating rescue) and must be moved by other-directed will rather than self-interest.

**T4.4.2 — Human Interface — Receiving — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence identifies both enabling and blocking conditions for reception from a human intermediary. Enabling conditions include: a receptive, fear-reverent hearing stance (Act 13:26); an awakened conscience seeking rescue (Act 16:30); meekness of will before the implanted word (Jam 1:21); and belief in the message received (Eph 1:13, 1Cor 1:21). The will's openness to hearing is essential — Act 28:28 contrasts Gentiles "who will listen" with those who rejected the message. Blocking conditions include: love of truth refused (2Th 2:10, "perishing refused love of truth"), understanding closed to the deliverance offered (Act 7:25), and the devil removing the word before it can take root (Luk 8:12). Act 8:20 reveals a heart oriented toward purchasing grace as a commodity — a mercenary inner orientation that fundamentally misunderstands what is being offered. The primary blocker is the will's closing against truth; the primary enabler is the will's openness and receptive faith.

**T4.5.2 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence consistently shows eschatological salvation crossing covenantal boundaries rather than remaining confined to them. The Samaritans confess Jesus as "Savior of the world" (Joh 4:42); salvation is declared available to "all who call" without ethnic restriction (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13); Act 13:47 mandates carrying salvation to "the ends of the earth"; Act 10:45 and 11:17 record the Spirit-gift crossing to Gentiles; Rom 11:11-14 describes salvation reaching Gentiles through Israel's stumbling; 1Ti 2:4 states God desires "all people" to be saved. Joh 4:22 preserves the covenantal origin ("salvation is from the Jews") while Act 13:23 roots Jesus in Davidic covenant. The pattern is: covenantal origin that generates universal scope — salvation originates within covenant history but is not bounded by covenantal membership. The ethnic and merit-based restrictions are explicitly refused (Act 15:11, Act 15:1 contrast).

**T4.6.2 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity — something that can be attacked, distorted, or weaponised by adversarial spiritual powers?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence explicitly identifies eschatological salvation as a site of adversarial attack and distortion. Luk 8:12 names the devil as the agent who "removes the word from hearts to prevent belief and salvation" — the inner act of faith is the battleground and the devil targets it directly. Rev 12:10 presents Satan as the perpetual accuser whose activity works against the inner-being assurance that salvation secures — his defeat is the occasion for salvation's declaration. Act 8:20 shows the gift of God being targeted for commodification — a distortion of salvation's grace-character into a purchasable commodity, which Peter identifies as a spiritually dangerous inner orientation. False teaching that adds conditions to salvation (Act 15:1, Act 15:11) also functions as a distorting force — placing works-requirements where grace-reception belongs. The adversary's primary strategy appears to be: remove or corrupt the word before faith can be exercised, or distort salvation's character from gift to transaction.

**T4.1.3 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals a divine disposition of unreserved, initiative-taking saving intent toward the human person. Joh 3:17 states the entire purpose of the Son's sending is to save, not condemn, revealing saving as God's primary disposition rather than a reluctant concession. 1Ti 2:4 shows God desiring "all people" to be saved — the disposition is universal in scope. Tit 2:11 presents grace "appearing" to bring salvation to all, revealing a disposition of generous self-disclosure. Eph 2:5 shows God acting on an inner state of spiritual death — the person contributes nothing, God acts wholly from his own merciful disposition. Rom 5:15-17 (M38-A-VCG-03) shows the gift abounding "far beyond" what the trespass produced, revealing an overflowing rather than minimal generosity. Tit 3:5 grounds salvation in "mercy," not human worth. The aggregate picture is a God whose disposition toward the human person is actively, extravagantly, and unconditionally saving — prior to and without regard to human merit.

**T4.2.3 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the human-to-God direction of this characteristic reveal about the person's relationship with God?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The human-toward-God movement in eschatological salvation reveals a relationship of complete dependence, recognized need, and responsive trust. The disciples' astonished question "who then can be saved?" (Mat 19:25, Mar 10:26, Luk 18:26) reveals a relationship in which the human person recognizes their own complete inability — no self-sufficiency before God is possible. Calling on the name (Act 2:21, Rom 10:13) reveals a relationship of invocation — the person as one who calls, God as the one who saves. Confession of Christ as Lord (Rom 10:9) reveals the person's relational alignment under divine authority. The doxological response (Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1) reveals that at completion the relationship is one of joyful ascription — the person giving all credit for rescue to God and the Lamb, holding nothing for themselves. Self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25 etc.) reveal that the relationship to God requires release of autonomous self-possession. The relationship is one of acknowledged need, receptive dependence, and grateful worship.

**T4.3.3 — Human Interface — Giving — What does the evidence reveal about what the person must have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence implies that genuine mediation of salvation to others requires prior reception of salvation oneself, though this is stated indirectly. Paul's self-adaptation (1Cor 9:22) and self-denial (1Cor 10:33) are intelligible only as responses to grace already received — the motivation is "that many may be saved" from a position of already being saved. 2Ti 2:10 shows Paul enduring suffering for the elect's salvation as one who has himself received it. The teacher who saves his hearers (1Ti 4:16) must watch his own life and doctrine — implying a prior integrity of received truth. Jude 23 urges rescue of others "with fear, hating even the garment stained by the flesh" — requiring a conscience already engaged by what sin means. Rom 10:1 reveals intercessory desire as a movement of one who has already received what they desire for others. The rescue of wandering sinners (Jam 5:20) presupposes the rescuer is not themselves wandering. The evidence consistently implies: to mediate salvation one must first have received the gift rather than originated it.

**T4.4.3 — Human Interface — Receiving — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence describes the non-receiving person in terms of several inner states. Refusal of truth's love (2Th 2:10) characterizes the perishing — the will is closed against the very content that would bring rescue. Act 7:25 describes understanding that was closed to the deliverance offered — the people "did not understand" that God was working salvation, a failure of perception combined with unreceptive inner state. Luk 8:12 shows the word removed before faith can form — the inner state remains unchanged, without the transformation faith would bring. Those who reject the message in Act 28:28 are contrasted with Gentiles who will listen. The state of the non-receiver in Rev 12:10 is one of perpetual accusation without vindication. 2Pe 2:20 describes those who once knew the Savior but turned back — characterized as worse off than their original ignorance, with defilement returning. The non-receiver exists in a state of perishing (1Cor 1:18), wrath-facing (1Th 5:9), condemnation (Mar 16:16), and ongoing spiritual danger — the structural opposite of the rescued state.

**T4.5.3 — Human Interface — Boundaries — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope of this characteristic — who is included and who is not?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence presents eschatological salvation with an explicitly universal intended scope bounded only by receptive response. Included: all who call (Rom 10:13, "everyone who calls"), all who believe (Act 16:31, "you and your household"), Samaritans (Joh 4:42), Gentiles to the ends of the earth (Act 13:47), all Israel eschatologically (Rom 11:26), all people as God's desire (1Ti 2:4), a multitude from every nation (Rev 7:10). The gift is characterized as given equally — not differentiated by ethnicity, gender, or social standing. Excluded by their own response: those who refuse truth (2Th 2:10), those who do not call or believe (Mar 16:16), those who neglect salvation (Heb 2:3), those who do not hold fast (1Cor 15:2). The remnant-election texts (Rom 9:27) preserve divine sovereign selectivity within this universal scope. The structure is: universal divine intent meeting particular response, with no human category intrinsically excluded from the offer.

**T4.6.3 — Spiritual Beings Interface — Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry in the evidence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows angelic involvement at two points, though not as primary mediators of salvation itself. Luk 2:11 records an angel announcing the Savior's birth — angelic ministry at the point of disclosure. Rev 7:10 and Rev 19:1 depict heavenly beings (angelic and redeemed multitudes) participating in the doxological proclamation of salvation's completion — their role is witness and worship rather than mediation. Rev 12:10 presents the heavenly voice declaring salvation at the moment the accuser is cast down — an angelic or heavenly figure announcing salvation's vindication. The birth announcement (Luk 2:11) is the clearest case of angelic communication of salvation-news. However, the primary mediatorial role is assigned to Christ (Heb 7:25, his ongoing intercession), to the proclaimed word (1Cor 1:21), and to the Spirit (Eph 1:13, Tit 3:5). Angelic ministry in the evidence is more declaratory and doxological than structurally constitutive of how salvation reaches the human person.

**T5.1.1 — Nature of Transformation — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows transformation operating at the level of condition, orientation, and both simultaneously. Condition is changed: in Eph 2:5 the person moves from spiritual death to being "made alive with Christ"; in Rom 5:15-17 the inner state moves from condemnation to justification; in Tit 3:5 the Spirit's regenerating work changes the person's inner constitution; in Act 2:47 persons are added to the community of the saved from a prior state of being outside it. Orientation is also changed: Rom 5:9-10 shows the person's orientation shifting from enemy-of-God to reconciled-through-Christ; the self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25, Mar 8:35) show the will reoriented from self-preservation to Christ-following; 2Cor 7:10 shows godly grief reorienting the person away from sin toward life. The doxological completion texts (Rev 7:10, Rev 19:1) show both a condition of being fully delivered and an orientation of praise. Both condition and orientation are transformed, with condition-change typically preceding and enabling orientation-change in the evidence's sequence.

**T5.2.1 — Sequence of Inner States — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states through which this characteristic moves the person — a before, during, and after?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence describes sequences explicitly in several texts. Eph 1:13 gives a clear sequence: hearing the gospel → faith exercised → sealed with the Spirit — three distinguishable inner states. 2Cor 7:10 sequences: encounter with godly grief → repentance → salvation without regret, contrasting with worldly grief → death. Rom 5:10 moves: enemy status → reconciliation through Christ's death → present and future salvation through risen life — three stages. The jailer sequence (Act 16:30-31): conscience-awakened danger → urgent seeking → faith directed at Christ → promised rescue. Eph 2:5 implies: spiritual death → divine action on that dead state → made alive with Christ. The self-renunciation texts (Mat 16:25) structure: self-preservation will → surrender → eschatological finding/keeping. The before-state is consistently: sin-laden, condemned, dead, lost, or enemy; the during-state involves the will turning, faith exercised, or word received; the after-state is saved, justified, reconciled, sealed, and secured.

**T5.3.1 — Mechanism of Change — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence reveals multiple mechanisms. The primary mechanism is encounter with the proclaimed word meeting the act of faith: 1Cor 1:21 (God's "foolishness of preaching" saves those who believe), Eph 1:13 (gospel heard and believed), Jam 1:21 (implanted word saves the soul). This encounter-mechanism can be sudden (Act 16:30-31, the jailer's immediate crisis and response) or presupposed as a completed event from which ongoing transformation flows. A second mechanism is divine regenerative action: Tit 3:5 names the Spirit's "regenerating and renewing work" as the mechanism, acting on the inner person directly rather than through cognitive encounter alone. A third mechanism is the sustained will under pressure: M38-A-VCG-05 presents endurance as the mechanism through which salvation is reached (Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, Heb 5:9). A fourth is self-renunciation: the will releasing self-interest (Mat 16:25 register) as a mechanism of eschatological finding. The mechanisms differ by context — crisis produces sudden encounter, formation produces gradual endurance, Spirit-action produces regeneration — but all converge on the same transformation.

**T5.4.1 — Suffering and Affliction — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to suffering or affliction — as a response to it, a product of it, or a context for it?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows suffering functioning in multiple relations to eschatological salvation. Suffering as a context requiring endurance: Mat 10:22, Mat 24:13, and Mar 13:13 present persecution and hatred as the context within which the persevering will holds until salvation is reached. Suffering as the path of the mediator: 2Ti 2:10 shows Paul accepting suffering as the context in which the elect obtain salvation; Heb 2:10 describes Christ as "made perfect through suffering" as the founder of salvation — suffering is structurally part of the path to eschatological completion. Self-renunciation as voluntary suffering: Mat 16:25 register frames willingness to lose life as the condition of eschatological finding — suffering-of-self-surrender as the mechanism. Salvation as deliverance from affliction: Heb 2:3, Heb 5:9, and Rev 12:10 position salvation as the resolution of danger, affliction, and accusation. The evidence treats suffering not as incidental but as structurally present — both in the mediator's path (Christ and apostles) and in the believer's perseverance context.

**T5.5.1 — Formation and Sanctification — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification — shaping the person over time toward greater likeness?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence shows partial but genuine engagement with ongoing formation. 1Cor 1:18 and 1Cor 15:2 use present-tense sōzō ("being saved"), indicating salvation as an ongoing state rather than solely a past event. Phili 2:12 urges "working out your own salvation with reverent seriousness," indicating that salvation engages the will, conscience, and sustained moral effort over time. 1Ti 4:16 links watching one's life and doctrine to saving both self and hearers — an ongoing formational discipline. 1Ti 2:15 conditions ongoing salvation on persisting in faith, love, holiness, and self-control — naming virtues that must be sustained over time. 2Ti 1:9 grounds the calling in a "holy calling" — linking salvation's reception to ongoing sanctified identity. 2Pe 3:18 calls those who know the Savior to grow in grace and knowledge — salvation-identity as a starting point for ongoing formation. The evidence does not present sanctification as the mechanism of salvation but does show salvation generating an ongoing formational trajectory in the person.

**T5.1.2 — Nature of Transformation — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-1]** E — The evidence presents a genuinely mixed picture, with both irreversibility and reversibility indicated in different texts, creating partial disjointedness. Irreversibility is implied by: Heb 7:25 ("saves completely," with unbroken intercession), Eph 2:5 (made alive, a past completed state), 2Ti 1:9 (grace "purposed before time"), 1Th 5:9 (appointed for salvation, not wrath), and the assurance language of Rom 5:9-10. Reversibility or serious risk is indicated by: 2Pe 2:20 (those who escaped defilement through knowing the Savior but fall back are "worse off"), 1Cor 15:2 (believing "in vain" is a genuine risk if the gospel is not held fast), Heb 2:3 (neglecting salvation risks inescapable judgment), Heb 5:9 (salvation conditioned on obedience), and Mat 10:22 (salvation tied to enduring to the end). The evidence does not resolve this tension — it holds both irreversible-divine-purpose and genuine-human-risk as live data points.
