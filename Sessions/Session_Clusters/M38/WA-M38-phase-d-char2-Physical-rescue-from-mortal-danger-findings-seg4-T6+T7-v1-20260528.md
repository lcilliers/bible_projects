**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary terms are:

**Greek: sōzō (G4982)** — verb, from the root *sao-/sōs-* meaning 'safe, whole, unharmed.' The root denotes **preservation of life and bodily integrity**. In M38-B, sōzō consistently means to rescue from physical peril: Mat 8:25 ('save us, we perish'), Mat 14:30 ('Lord, save me'), and the crucifixion taunts (VCG-02). Root meaning reveals: rescue as **restoration to wholeness** from a state of threatened dissolution.

**Greek: soteria (G4991)** — noun, same root as sōzō, meaning 'safety, preservation, deliverance.' Luk 1:71 ('rescue from enemies') and Heb 11:7 ('saved his household') use this form, emphasising **the state or event of being made safe** rather than the act.

**Hebrew: padah (H6299)** — verb, meaning 'to ransom, redeem, deliver by paying a price or exercising superior power.' Psa 69:18 uses padah in the context of divine rescue from enemies. Root meaning reveals: rescue is **transactional or power-assertive** — a deliberate act of intervention that breaks the threat's hold. This distinguishes padah from the Greek sōzō, which stresses the resultant condition of safety rather than the mechanism of extraction.

**T7.2.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **sōzō** operates primarily as a **verb** across M38-B's evidence (Mat 8:25: aorist imperative 'save [us]'; Mat 14:30: aorist imperative 'save me'; Mat 27:40, 49: aorist imperative/infinitive; Mar 15:30–31: aorist infinitive; Luk 23:35–39: aorist infinitive; Joh 12:27: aorist subjunctive; Heb 5:7: aorist infinitive). The dominance of the **imperative and infinitive forms** reveals that M38-B is primarily an **action-seeking** characteristic: the inner being does not contemplate rescue abstractly but demands, pleads, or contemplates it as an urgent act to be performed. **soteria** (Luk 1:71, Heb 11:7) as a **noun** reveals the **resultant-state** register: rescue that has been accomplished or is anticipated as a corporate possession, not a moment of crisis-cry.

**padah** (Psa 69:18) as a **verb** (imperative: 'redeem/ransom [my soul]') confirms the action-demanding grammar of M38-B's inner-being posture.

(5) **The collapse of rescue-hope** (Act 27:20, sōzō negated): the inner-being state when rescue is not forthcoming.

The arc reveals M38-B as a **complete phenomenological cycle** from threat to cry to rescue-or-despair, with vocabulary covering every stage. The characteristic's semantic range is therefore not a single state but a **dynamic arc** — and the vocabulary is precisely calibrated to each node within it.

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The primary anchor for the sub-group's hallmark phenomenon is **Mat 14:30** (VCG-01 anchor): 'Lord, save me' (Kyrie, sōson me). **sōzō** here functions as the **main verb in an imperative clause** addressed to Jesus. In the argument of Mat 14, this imperative functions as the **turning point**: it marks the transition from Peter's sinking (the failure of self-sufficient faith) to Jesus's hand-reach (the enactment of rescue), demonstrating that the faith-cry — however small — is sufficient to precipitate divine response. The term's role is therefore **syntactically minimal but argumentatively pivotal**.

**T7.1.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — Several logical structures are identifiable:

**VCG-01 (Mat 14:30)**: P1: Peter is sinking and cannot save himself. P2: Jesus is present and has demonstrated power over wind and waves. C: Therefore, 'Lord, save me' is the only rational act available to faith. The argument is an **inference to the only available agent** under conditions of mortal incapacity.

**VCG-02 (Mar 15:31)**: P1: Jesus saved others (prior acts of physical rescue/healing). P2: He does not come down from the cross to save himself. C (mockers' intended): Therefore he is powerless. C (evangelist's irony): Therefore his refusal is the mechanism of others' deeper salvation. The argument is a **two-level ironic syllogism**.

**VCG-03 (Heb 5:7)**: P1: Jesus prayed with anguish and reverence to be saved from death. P2: He was heard because of his reverence. C: Therefore reverent anguish is the condition under which petition is heard, not the content of rescue. The argument is a **theology of heard prayer** under conditions of ordained suffering.

**VCG-04 (Heb 11:7)**: P1: Noah acted in reverent faith by building the ark. P2: This action delivered his household. C: Therefore faith expressed in obedient action produces physical rescue. The argument is **faith-as-instrument-of-salvation**.

**T7.1.7 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The most useful human science framework for M38-B is **crisis psychology / terror management theory**, supplemented by **attachment theory**. Crisis psychology (particularly the work on acute stress response and crisis intervention) directly addresses the inner-being states documented in M38-B: acute fear, panic, the collapse of agency, and the urgent appeal to an available helper. The phenomenological content of Mat 8:25, Mat 14:30, and Act 27:20 maps closely onto the documented psychological trajectory of acute mortal threat: threat appraisal → autonomic arousal (fear/panic) → agency collapse → crisis-cry or despair. **Attachment theory** (Bowlby, Ainsworth) further illuminates the crisis-cry dynamic: the infant/adult under threat instinctively orients toward an attachment figure (the 'safe haven' function). Peter's 'Lord, save me' (Mat 14:30) and the disciples' 'Save us' (Mat 8:25) are formally identical to attachment-system activation — the self in mortal danger reaches toward a trusted, proximate, powerful other.

**T7.1.8 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — **Attachment theory** most directly illuminates why the faith-cry in M38-B takes the imperative grammatical form of direct address rather than a conditional or reflective prayer. Bowlby's attachment system activates not reasoned supplication but **proximity-seeking behaviour** — the cry to the attachment figure is instinctive, non-reflective, and immediate. This explains the grammatical minimalism of Mat 14:30 ('Lord, save me!' — two words) and Mat 8:25 ('Save us, we perish!' — four words): these are not theological propositions but **attachment cries** — the raw inner-being output of a self whose survival instinct has activated its deepest relational resource. The attachment framework also illuminates the contrast between VCG-01 (attachment cry → rescue) and VCG-05 (Act 27:20: hope abandoned — no attachment figure perceived as available). When the attachment figure is perceived as absent or inaccessible, the crisis-cry is not made; despair is the result of attachment-figure unavailability.

**T7.1.9 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-2]** E — The most significant divergence between the human science frameworks and the verse evidence appears at **VCG-03** (Joh 12:27, Heb 5:7). Crisis psychology and attachment theory would predict that the subject under mortal threat either cries out or despairs. But Jesus in Joh 12:27 and Heb 5:7 undergoes a **third trajectory unavailable in the human science frameworks**: anguish → petition → heard → willed surrender to the appointed purpose. This is not attachment-seeking resolved by rescue (VCG-01) nor attachment-seeking abandoned in despair (VCG-05) — it is anguish **transformed by the relational knowledge that the Father's will is good**, producing a consent to the threat rather than escape from it. The divergence reveals that M38-B, at its highest register (VCG-03), transcends the psychology of crisis: the inner being of the Son demonstrates a **tertium quid** — a way through mortal threat that is neither flight (rescue) nor collapse (despair) but willed participation in the threat's redemptive purpose. Human science frameworks have no category for this.
