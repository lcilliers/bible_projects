### M38-C-VCG-01

- Verse count: 8
- Anchor vc_id: 9011

**Context description:** These verses centre on the inner faith-speech or interior conviction that precedes and accompanies physical healing, where the person's trust is the operative inner-being faculty that activates sōzō. The characteristic topology is: the healing-seeker forms an interior conviction (spoken or unspoken) before any outward act is complete, Jesus then names faith as the cause, and the sōzō event carries a spiritual overtone that exceeds mere physical cure. The woman's pre-touch inner monologue and Jesus's confirmation ('your faith has made you well') together with Bartimaeus's persistent cry and the Samaritan leper's return all exhibit this structure: faith as the named interior activator, wholeness as the named result. Where peace or discipleship follows, it signals that the inner being — not only the body — has been restored to right standing.

### M38-C-VCG-02

- Verse count: 2
- Anchor vc_id: 9018

**Context description:** These verses cluster around faith exercised in the face of death or mortal threat, where sōzō names urgent physical rescue and faith is explicitly set against fear or desperation. The inner-being phenomenon here is not quiet interior conviction building toward a hopeful touch, but crisis-driven faith — a father's desperate plea for his dying daughter, the hemorrhaging woman's desperate press through a crowd, and Jesus's counter-command 'only believe' set directly against fear when news of death arrives. The sōzō event is still healing-with-spiritual-overtone, but the presenting inner-being register is urgency, fear displacement, and faith as the active counter-posture to mortal dread rather than the composure of pre-formed interior conviction.

**Member vc_ids:** [9012, 9018]

### M38-C-VCG-03

- Verse count: 2
- Anchor vc_id: 9024

**Context description:** These verses extend sōzō beyond individual touch-and-heal episodes into mediated or corporate faith-acts that restore the whole person at the level of conscience and body together. James 5:15 names the prayer of faith as the vehicle by which the sick are healed and sins forgiven — the inner faculty engaged is intercessory faith on behalf of another, and the result converges healing of body with cleansing of conscience. First Peter 3:21 moves the same conscience-and-body axis into the baptismal appeal: baptism saves not as external washing but as an interior petition to God for a good conscience grounded in resurrection. Both verses share the distinctive inner-being topology of conscience as the primary organ restored, with the physical or ritual dimension serving as the outward correlate of an inward appeal — a register absent from the individual synoptic healing narratives.

**Member vc_ids:** [9024, 8992]
