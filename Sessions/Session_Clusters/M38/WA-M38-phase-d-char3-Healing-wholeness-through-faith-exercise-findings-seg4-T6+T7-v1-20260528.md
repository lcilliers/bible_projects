**T7.1.1 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary Greek term is **G4982 sōzō**, appearing in all 12 verses across VCG-01, VCG-02, and VCG-03. Its root meaning is to rescue, preserve, or make safe — derived from the adjective *saos* (safe, unharmed). The root implies the restoration of an endangered entity to a state of safety and wholeness, covering physical rescue from harm, healing from disease, and preservation of life.

No Hebrew term appears directly in the verse evidence (all 12 verses are NT). However, the Hebrew antecedent most relevant to this characteristic is **יָשַׁע (yāšaʿ)**, root of *yeshūʿāh* (salvation), which in the OT covers rescue from enemies, healing from illness (Psa 107:19-20 — "he sent out his word and healed them"), and deliverance from death. The semantic overlap between sōzō and yāšaʿ is substantial and deliberate in the LXX, where sōzō regularly translates yāšaʿ. The root meaning of yāšaʿ — to be wide, spacious, liberated from confinement — adds a spatial-liberation dimension to the characteristic: wholeness is a state of being released from the constraint (disease, death, sin) into open space.

A secondary relevant Hebrew term is **שָׁלוֹם (shālōm)**, appearing in the peace-grant formula ("go in peace," Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48). Its root (šlm — to be complete, whole, unbroken) directly names the inner-being state that sōzō produces: integrity of the whole person, nothing lacking or damaged.

**T7.1.2 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What is the grammatical range of the primary term (noun, verb, adjective, participle) — and what does that range reveal about how the characteristic operates?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — G4982 sōzō appears across the 12 verses in the following grammatical forms:

- **Perfect indicative passive** ("has been made well / has saved you"): Mat 9:21 (*sōthēsomai* — future indicative passive: "I shall be saved/made well"), Mat 9:22, Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48, Luk 17:19, Luk 18:42 use perfect or aorist active/passive forms ("your faith has saved/made you well"). - **Imperative/future**: Luk 8:50 uses a future form tied to the condition of believing ("she will be saved/restored if you believe"). Mar 5:23 uses a subjunctive of purpose ("so that she may be made well").

The grammatical range reveals that sōzō in this characteristic operates **eventively** (a bounded rescue-event) rather than as a continuing state or habitual disposition. The passive constructions confirm that healing-wholeness is received, not achieved: faith is active (the person exercises it), but the sōzō is a divine act directed toward the person. The characteristic is constitutionally **bidirectional**: human faith activates; divine saving completes.

The characteristic is expressed in the verse evidence through narrative particulars (the woman, Bartimaeus, the father, the leper) rather than through a generalized person-type term.

The nearest approach to a person-type within the evidence is the Jam 5:15 construction, which names **"the elders of the church"** as the intercessors whose prayer of faith heals — but this is a functional role designation, not a person-type term for the inner-being characteristic.

In 1Pe 3:21, the person exercising the characteristic is identified implicitly as the baptismal candidate making the appeal — again, a contextual role rather than a coined person-type term.

The characteristic is thus lexically person-type *absent* within this corpus; the inner-being faculty (faith) is named, but no Greek noun for the one who habitually exercises healing-faith has emerged in these 12 verses.

**T7.1.7 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Does the vocabulary include a supplication or seeking term — a term for the act of seeking this characteristic from another?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. Two seeking/supplication terms appear:

(1) **Parakalōn (Mar 5:23)** — Jairus "urges/implores" Jesus earnestly for his daughter. This is *parakaleō* (G3870), meaning to call to one's side, to beseech or implore. It is a strong supplication term marking the father's inner-being posture of urgent entreaty.

(2) **Ekrazen (Mar 10:47-48, implied for Luk 18:38-39)** — Bartimaeus "cried out" (*krazō*, G2896) persistently despite attempts to silence him. The crying-out is a seeking-act, the verbal enactment of faith reaching toward Jesus.

(3) **Proseuchē (Jam 5:15)** — "the prayer of faith" (*hē euchē tēs pisteōs*) is a formal supplication term. Proseuchē (G4335) names the act of seeking through prayer; combined with "of faith" it constitutes Char-3's distinctive seeking-vocabulary in VCG-03.

(4) **Eperotēsen (1Pe 3:21, implied)** — the "appeal" (*eperōtēma*, G1906) in 1Pe 3:21 — baptism as "an appeal [*eperōtēma*] to God for a good conscience" — is a seeking/supplication term with legal-petition overtones (asking God for a favourable verdict on conscience).

**T7.1.8 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the relationship between the OT Hebrew vocabulary and the NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development of this characteristic across the Testaments?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The verse evidence is entirely NT, but the Hebrew background vocabulary is traceable through the LXX. The relationship reveals **strong continuity with developmental intensification**:

(1) **Continuity**: The Hebrew yāšaʿ / yeshūʿāh and the Greek sōzō occupy overlapping semantic domains — both cover rescue from enemies, healing from disease, and preservation of life. Psalms 107:17-20 ("he sent his word and healed them... he saved them from destruction") anticipates the Synoptic pattern of word-and-healing as sōzō-act. The OT framework of God as the one who heals (Exod 15:26 — "I am the LORD who heals you") establishes the theological continuity within which Jesus's healing-sōzō formula operates.

(2) **Development**: The NT introduces two elements absent or underdeveloped in the OT healing vocabulary: (a) the explicit naming of *the individual person's faith* as the operative cause ("your faith has made you well") — OT healing is typically attributed to God's act in response to prayer, without isolating the individual's faith as the causal mechanism; (b) the convergence of physical healing with conscience-restoration in a single sōzō-event (Jam 5:15, 1Pe 3:21), linking the body and the moral-relational standing before God in a way that the OT healing traditions keep more separated (bodily healing in Psalms; forgiveness of sin in Levitical sacrificial system).

**T7.1.9 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period for this characteristic — and if so, what does that coinage reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — No term in the M38-C corpus appears to be a genuine NT hapax or newly coined neologism unique to this characteristic. G4982 sōzō is attested in classical and Hellenistic Greek prior to the NT. G4102 pistis (faith) is similarly pre-NT in Hellenistic usage.

However, the phrase **"your faith has saved/made you well" (hē pistis sou sesōken se)** as a formulaic declaration by Jesus may represent a **distinctive NT usage pattern** rather than a coined term. The formula is not attested in classical Greek or LXX in the exact form Jesus uses it. The formula's distinctive contribution is the attribution of sōzō to the individual's pistis in a healing context — this is a conceptual innovation even if the individual words are not newly coined.

In VCG-03, **eperōtēma (G1906)** in 1Pe 3:21 ("appeal to God for a good conscience") is a rare term whose application to baptism as a conscience-appeal is specifically NT and may represent a terminological development specific to early Christian baptismal theology. Its coinage-in-context reveals that Char-3's epistolary extension required new vocabulary to express what the Synoptic healing narratives had expressed narratively.

**T7.1.10 — Lexical and Semantic Analysis — What does the full vocabulary arc reveal about this characteristic's complete semantic range?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The full vocabulary arc of M38-C runs from:

**Desperate seeking** (parakaleō, krazō — imploring, crying out) →
**Interior conviction** (inner speech, Mar 5:28, Mat 9:21 — "I shall be saved") →
**Faith named as cause** (pistis, hē pistis sou — your faith) →
**Sōzō enacted** (across all 12 verses — physical healing, rescue from death) →
**Peace received** (eirēnē — Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48) →
**Conscience restored** (syneidēsis — 1Pe 3:21; forgiveness in Jam 5:15) →
**Discipleship initiated** (following Jesus, Mar 10:52)

This arc reveals that Char-3's semantic range is not a single point on the inner-being map but a trajectory from vulnerability and urgent faith-seeking through the sōzō-event to a stable inner-being state of peace, conscience-integrity, and relational orientation toward Jesus. The vocabulary is not simply healing-vocabulary but transformation-vocabulary: the person who enters the arc (desperate, diseased, dying, or unclean of conscience) exits it different in both body and inner being. The conscience-restoration terms in VCG-03 confirm that the arc extends to the deepest inner-being register — the moral-relational standing before God — making the semantic range of this characteristic one of the broadest within the sōzō cluster.

**T7.2.1 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the function of this characteristic's primary term within its primary verse — what role does it play in the sentence and argument?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary anchor verse is **Mat 9:22** (vc_id=9011, VCG-01 ANCHOR): "Take heart, daughter; your faith has made you well [*hē pistis sou sesōken se*]. And instantly the woman was made well."

Within the sentence, **sōzō (sesōken)** functions as the main predicate verb of the causal declaration. Its grammatical role is to name the *outcome* of faith's exercise as its *direct object* — but crucially, the grammatical subject is *pistis* (faith), making faith the agent and sōzō the divine act attributed to the faith-instrument. The formula "hē pistis sou sesōken se" is a nominative-subject + perfect-active-indicative + accusative-object construction in which faith is grammatically the one doing the saving, though theologically the act is Jesus's.

Within the argument of Mat 9:21-22, the verse functions as Jesus's **retrospective confirmation** of what was already happening: the woman's interior faith-speech (v.21) preceded Jesus's awareness of the touch (in Matthew's compressed account), and v.22 is Jesus's authoritative naming of the causal structure. The function of sōzō here is declarative and confirmatory — it closes the loop between the faith-act (interior conviction → touch) and the outcome (wholeness), establishing faith as the named inner-being mechanism.

**T7.2.2 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What literary form carries the primary verse evidence (narrative, psalm, wisdom, prophecy, epistle, apocalyptic) — and what does that form require for responsible interpretation?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary evidence in VCG-01 and VCG-02 (Mat 9:21-22, Mar 5:23, 5:28, 5:34, 10:52, Luk 8:48, 8:50, 17:19, 18:42) is carried in **Synoptic Gospel narrative** — specifically miracle-healing narratives (pericopes). VCG-03 (Jam 5:15, 1Pe 3:21) is carried in **epistle** form.

Responsible interpretation of the Synoptic healing narratives requires:

(1) **Attention to the pericope structure**: Each healing narrative has a presenting-condition, a faith-act (often described interior to the petitioner), the healing event, and Jesus's confirmatory declaration. Interpretation must not flatten the sequence; the interior faith-dimension (particularly in Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28) is pre-verbal and must be read as the narrator's disclosure of inner-being, not as reported speech.

(2) **Recognition of the Synoptic parallel tradition**: Mat 9:21-22, Mar 5:28, 5:34, and Luk 8:48 are parallel accounts of the same event. Responsible interpretation requires noting how each Gospel's particular emphasis shapes the sōzō-faith formula (Matthew compresses; Mark gives interior speech; Luke adds peace-grant with identical formula to Mark).

(3) **For the epistles (Jam 5:15, 1Pe 3:21)**: The epistle form requires reading both verses within their argumentative contexts. Jam 5:15 is part of a community-practice instruction (prayer for the sick); 1Pe 3:21 is part of a theological argument about baptism and Noah's ark. Both require attention to the author's argument before the verse is extracted for characteristic-analysis.

**T7.2.3 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What is the logical structure of key arguments in the verse evidence — what are the premises and conclusions?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The core argument structure embedded in the Synoptic healing formula (Mat 9:22, Mar 5:34, Luk 8:48, 17:19, 18:42) follows a repeated logical form:

**Premise 1**: This person exercised faith (interior conviction, persistent cry, returning gratitude).
**Premise 2**: The sōzō-event has occurred (healing is observable).
**Conclusion (Jesus's declaration)**: Therefore, *your faith* has activated this sōzō-event ("your faith has made you well").

This is a **retro-attributive causal argument**: the conclusion is drawn after the event by the one who alone can authoritatively name the cause. The logical force is Jesus's authoritative naming, not the healed person's self-understanding.

For VCG-02 (Luk 8:50):
**Premise 1**: Your daughter has died.
**Premise 2**: Fear is the natural response.
**Counter-command**: Do not fear; only believe.
**Implicit conclusion**: Faith, not fear, is the appropriate inner-being posture, and it is the condition under which sōzō is possible.

For Jam 5:15:
**Premise 1**: The prayer of faith is offered by elders on behalf of the sick.
**Conclusion 1**: The Lord will save (*sōsei*) the sick.
**Conditional sub-argument**: If sins are involved, forgiveness follows.
**Overall conclusion**: Faith-prayer restores the whole person (body + conscience).

**T7.2.4 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What contextual setting carries the primary verse evidence (judicial, liturgical, covenantal, communal, eschatological) — and what does that setting reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary Synoptic evidence (VCG-01, VCG-02) is set in **public encounter contexts** — crowds, roads, synagogues, homes — that are neither formally judicial nor liturgical but represent the **communal/relational** setting of Jesus's ministry. The setting reveals:

(1) **Vulnerability before community**: The hemorrhaging woman (12 years ill, ritually unclean, financially depleted — Mar 5:25-26) approaches within a crowd, covertly. The setting reveals that Char-3 operates in social-communal space where shame, exclusion, and public visibility are stakes in the healing event.

(2) **Household/family setting** (Mar 5:23, Luk 8:50): The dying/dead daughter places the faith-act within family crisis. The communal setting here is domestic and kinship-oriented.

(3) **Luk 17:19** — the Samaritan leper's setting is **post-community return**: the healed man returns from the priest-verification mission (commanded by Jesus) to give thanks. The setting is the interface between ritual-communal obligation and personal gratitude.

For VCG-03, **Jam 5:15** is set in the **early Christian communal/liturgical** context (elders, prayer over the sick, anointing with oil) — an ecclesial-ritual setting. **1Pe 3:21** is set in the **theological-catechetical** context of baptism — a covenantal-initiatory setting. Both epistolary verses are located in more formally institutional contexts than the Synoptic narratives.

**T7.2.5 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — Does any verse function as the primary anchor for this characteristic — the verse that most fully and directly expresses its essential character?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — **Mar 5:34** (vc_id=9014, VCG-01) is the strongest candidate for the single verse most fully expressing Char-3's essential character, though Mat 9:22 (the VCG-01 ANCHOR) is the formally designated anchor.

Mar 5:34 reads: "And he said to her, 'Daughter, your faith has made you well; go in peace, and be healed of your disease.'"

This verse contains every essential element of Char-3:
- **Faith as named cause**: "your faith has made you well" — explicit causal attribution.
- **Sōzō in the healing-with-spiritual-overtone register**: "made you well" (*sesōken*) — physical healing with the relational register of "daughter."
- **Peace as inner-being outcome**: "go in peace" (*eis eirēnēn*) — the wholeness of inner being sealed.
- **Physical healing confirmed**: "be healed of your disease" — body restored.
- **Relational address**: "Daughter" — spiritual-relational recognition by Jesus.

No other single verse in the corpus combines all five elements. Mat 9:22 contains faith + sōzō; Luk 8:48 contains faith + sōzō + peace; Mar 5:34 contains all of these plus the explicit disease-healing confirmation. Mar 5:34 is therefore the maximally expressive anchor verse.

**T7.2.6 — Verse and Literary Interpretation — What does the primary anchor verse reveal that no other verse reveals?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Taking **Mar 5:34** as the maximally expressive anchor (see T7.2.5), and **Mat 9:22** as the formally designated anchor, together they reveal the following that other verses do not individually achieve:

**Mat 9:22** uniquely reveals: The compressed Matthean version omits the woman's interior monologue (carried in Mat 9:21) from the declaration itself, placing the full causal weight on Jesus's authoritative word alone — no interior faith-speech is acknowledged by Jesus; he simply names faith as the cause. This reveals that the declaration is not dependent on Jesus knowing the interior process; it is an authoritative retrospective naming regardless of how it was perceived externally.

**Mar 5:34** uniquely reveals: The combination of relational address ("Daughter"), causal declaration ("your faith"), peace-grant ("go in peace"), and healing-confirmation ("be healed of your disease") within a single verse reveals that sōzō in Char-3 is not merely physical and not merely relational but **constitutionally inclusive of both simultaneously**. The verse will not allow a reduction to either dimension. Additionally, "daughter" reveals a **new relational status**: the woman who approached covertly and anonymously exits with a named relational identity before Jesus and the crowd — an identity-conferral that no other healing-faith verse provides.

**T7.3.1 — Human Science Frameworks — Which human science framework (psychology, moral philosophy, developmental psychology, sociology, anthropology, or other) is most useful as an interpretive lens for this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The most useful human science framework for Char-3 is **health psychology / psychoneuroimmunology combined with relational attachment theory**, with a secondary contribution from **phenomenology of illness and healing**.

(1) **Health psychology**: The structure of Char-3 — interior conviction preceding physical change, the role of trust in the healer, the social and relational context of healing, and the integration of physical and psychological well-being as a unified outcome — maps onto contemporary health-psychology concepts of placebo effect (where expectation and trust affect physiological outcomes), therapeutic alliance, and the psychological dimensions of physical illness-recovery.

(2) **Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)**: The faith-directed-toward-Jesus as healer mirrors secure-attachment seeking: the distressed person (ill, dying, unclean) moves toward the attachment figure (Jesus) under threat, and the resolution of threat through secure contact produces the characteristic "felt security" (peace, wholeness). Mar 5:34's "daughter" and "go in peace" are paradigmatically secure-base language.

(3) **Phenomenology of illness and healing (Gadamer, Frank)**: The interior conviction of Mat 9:21 and Mar 5:28 — "if I touch, I shall be made whole" — is a first-person phenomenological act, the person's own anticipatory experience of healing before it is externally confirmed. Phenomenological frameworks that take the patient's interior experience as primary data are essential for reading these verses responsibly.

---

**T7.3.2 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence — making a finding more coherent or complete — what does it reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The attachment-theory framework illuminates Mar 5:34 and Luk 8:48 with particular clarity. The peace-grant ("go in peace") following healing maps onto what attachment theory calls the "haven of safety" response: the distressed person reaches the attachment figure, is held (metaphorically — through healing touch and word), and is then released into the world in a state of felt security. The inner-being change (peace) is not simply an emotional reaction; it is the reorganization of the person's inner orientation from threat-and-seeking to security-and-rest. This makes the peace-grant the inner-being completion of the sōzō event, not a secondary addendum.

The phenomenology framework illuminates the pre-touch interior conviction (Mat 9:21, Mar 5:28): the woman's self-speech ("if I touch, I shall be made whole") reveals an anticipatory healing narrative that precedes external confirmation. In phenomenological health-studies, this is consistent with findings that the patient's interior narrative of their own recovery is often the primary psychological mechanism of healing. The verse evidence is not diminished but enriched by this framework: the interior conviction is not mere self-suggestion but a divinely-congruent faith-act that corresponds to an actual healing event.

---

**T7.3.3 — Human Science Frameworks — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge, what does the divergence reveal?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — The primary divergence is at the point of **causal agency**. The human science frameworks (health psychology, phenomenology) locate causal agency in the patient's inner processes — the faith/expectation/conviction functions as a mechanism that produces healing through psychological and physiological pathways. In this model, the healing is ultimately a natural consequence of the person's own inner state.

The verse evidence diverges sharply: Jesus explicitly names faith as the activating mechanism but himself as the agent — "your faith has *made* you well" in the passive construction means the wholeness was *given*, and Jesus is the giver. Luk 8:50 ("only believe, and she will be saved") makes clear that the faith does not mechanistically produce healing but opens the relational channel through which Jesus acts. The healing of a dead girl (not a chronically ill adult exercising personal conviction) cannot be explained by psychophysiological mechanisms at all.

This divergence reveals that Char-3 requires a **theistic causality** that human science frameworks cannot accommodate without reductionism. The frameworks are useful for understanding the inner-being dynamics of the faith-act and the phenomenology of receiving wholeness, but they reach their explanatory limit at the point where the data requires divine agency as a non-reducible cause.

---

**T7.3.4 — Human Science Frameworks — Does the human science framework surface any aspect of this characteristic that the verse evidence has not yet addressed — and does that absence require further verse investigation?**

**[CHAR-3]** E — Yes. The human science frameworks surface two aspects insufficiently addressed in the 12-verse corpus:

(1) **The long-term inner-being trajectory after healing**: The verse evidence records the sōzō event and the immediate peace-grant or discipleship-initiation but does not address what happens to the inner being of the healed person over time. Health psychology raises questions about whether healing-faith produces ongoing dispositional change or is a one-time event. The woman's post-healing inner life, Bartimaeus's discipleship trajectory beyond Mar 10:52, and the Samaritan leper's subsequent inner-being development are all unaddressed. This suggests further investigation into NT passages that track the sustained inner-being life of those healed through faith.

(2) **The social-reintegration dimension**: Medical anthropology and illness-narrative studies emphasize that healing from stigmatized illness (hemorrhage, leprosy, blindness) requires social reintegration as much as physical cure. The verse evidence touches this in Mar 5:34 ("daughter" — public identity restoration) and Luk 17:14 (commanded to show priests — ritual-social verification), but does not develop the social-inner-being dimension systematically. Further verse investigation in the healing pericopes' aftermath and in Levitical purity contexts could address this gap.
