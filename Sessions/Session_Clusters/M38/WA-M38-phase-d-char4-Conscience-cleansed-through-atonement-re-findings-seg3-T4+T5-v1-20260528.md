**T4.1.1 — Divine Interface — God to Human — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence shows God-to-human operation as the dominant structural axis of this characteristic. VCG-04 is the concentrated locus: Psa 65:3 states that when iniquities "overwhelm" the psalmist, God himself atones for the transgressions — the act is entirely from God's side. Psa 78:38 shows God compassionately atoning for Israel's iniquity and restraining his own wrath "repeatedly rather than destroying them." Psa 79:9 frames God's atoning action as tied to his name and his saving power. Isa 6:7 presents a direct divine-to-human atoning touch: the burning coal cleanses Isaiah's lips and the angelic messenger declares "your guilt is removed, your sin is atoned." Heb 8:12 records God's New Covenant promise to "be merciful (hileōs) toward iniquities and remember sins no more" — pure divine-to-human gift. 1Jo 4:10 (VCG-05 anchor) makes the structure explicit: "not that we loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son as the hilasmos" — divine prior love is the initiating movement. VCG-02 adds Lev 17:11, where God himself provides the blood-mechanism: "I have given it for you on the altar to make atonement for your souls." Even the OT priestly ritual system is framed as God-given provision. The characteristic operates from God to human as its primary directional axis throughout the corpus.

**T4.2.1 — Divine Interface — Human to God — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God — in seeking, supplication, worship, or covenant — and if so, how?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence shows a secondary but significant human-toward-God movement, primarily expressed as seeking, supplication, and sacrificial approach. VCG-01 (Lev 4–6 cluster) presents the offerer who brings a specified animal and the priest who mediates — the sinner's act of bringing the offering is a movement toward God through the prescribed ritual channel (e.g., Lev 4:20, 4:26, 5:6). Lev 1:4 (VCG-02) shows the worshipper actively laying hands on the sacrifice — a physical movement of identification and self-presentation before God. VCG-03 includes Exo 30:15–16 (ransom payment) and Num 31:50 (warriors voluntarily bringing gold as atonement) — both are human initiatory acts of approach. 2Sa 21:3 shows David seeking to know what atoning act is required — active inquiry in movement toward resolution before God. 2Ch 30:18 presents Hezekiah in intercessory prayer on behalf of others. Luk 18:13 (VCG-05) is the purest human-toward-God posture: the tax collector standing at distance, beating his breast, crying "God, be merciful (hilaskomai) to me, a sinner" — wholly dependent, approaching only through appeal to divine mercy. Human movement toward God in this characteristic is always posited on acknowledged need and inability to self-cover.

**T4.3.1 — Human Interface — Giving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another — and if so, how does it operate in that giving?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence shows human-to-human extension operating primarily through priestly mediation and intercession, rather than direct peer-to-peer giving. The priest as mediator is the dominant figure: throughout VCG-01, the priest "makes atonement" for another person — he presents the offering, performs the ritual, and the outcome of forgiveness accrues to the offerer (e.g., Lev 4:20, 4:26, 5:10, 6:7). This is a form of giving: the priest extends atoning coverage to another through his office. Deu 21:8 (VCG-07) shows community leaders and priests together performing an intercession that covers corporate blood guilt. 2Ch 30:18 (VCG-03) shows Hezekiah's royal prayer as extended covering for ritually unprepared worshippers — one person's intercession seeking atoning coverage for others. The evidence does not show one ordinary person simply granting atonement to another; the giving direction is structurally mediated — through priestly office or intercessory prayer — and always requires divine ratification. Human giving of this characteristic is instrumental rather than originating.

**T4.4.1 — Human Interface — Receiving — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another — and if so, how does it operate in that reception?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — Reception is extensively documented, though the receiving person always receives from the priest-mediator who acts on God's behalf. VCG-01 repeatedly presents the individual sinner as recipient: the person brings the offering, the priest atones, and "he shall be forgiven" (Lev 4:20, 4:26, 4:31, 4:35, 5:10, 5:13, 5:16, 5:18, 6:7, 19:22). The inner-being movement in reception is consistently named: from "sin-laden" or "guilt-burdened" to "forgiven" and "conscience cleansed." Lev 1:4 (VCG-02) shows the worshipper receiving the "accepted" status through the priest's mediation on their behalf. In Isa 6:7, Isaiah receives atoning cleansing directly — "your guilt is removed, your sin covered." In the NT, Luk 18:13 (VCG-05) shows the tax collector receiving mercy (the text reports he "went home justified"). Rom 3:25 frames propitiation as received "by faith" — the inner posture of receiving is faith exercised toward the blood of Christ. Heb 8:12 presents the New Covenant recipient as one whose iniquities are "remembered no more." Reception in this characteristic produces an objective change in standing — guilt objectively removed — and a corresponding inner-being shift from condemnation to peace.

**T4.5.1 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance or difference?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence reveals meaningful boundary distinctions. Within the covenant community, atonement operates through the prescribed priestly system with guaranteed outcomes when properly enacted (VCG-01, Lev 4–6). Exo 30:15–16 (VCG-03) establishes that within Israel every soul — rich and poor equally — receives the same atonement coverage through the ransom payment, indicating that internal status differences do not differentiate access. However, certain relational and moral boundaries limit operation: Exo 29:33 (VCG-02) notes that "outsiders" who eat the ordination meal face exclusion — the atoned status belongs to those consecrated, not those outside the covenant. VCG-06 shows cases where atonement is withheld even within the covenant — when persistent priestly sin has closed the pathway (1Sa 3:14) or when judicial pronouncement is irreversible (Isa 22:14). 2Ch 30:18 (VCG-03) shows atonement extended even to ritually unprepared Israelites who sought sincerely, suggesting that sincere heart-orientation can override ritual boundary within the covenant. In the NT, 1Jo 2:2 explicitly crosses all prior covenantal boundaries: Christ's propitiation extends to "the whole world," not a limited community — representing the characteristic's maximum boundary expansion.

**T4.1.2 — Divine Interface — God to Human — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic — is it conditional, unconditional, covenantal, or responsive?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence reveals a complex basis that is simultaneously covenantal, compassion-driven, and responsive to human posture, but never unconditional in the sense of requiring nothing. Several distinct bases appear: (1) Covenantal-systematic: VCG-01 (Lev 4–6) presents atonement as God's given system within the covenant — the mechanism is divinely established and guaranteed when properly enacted. God provides the means (Lev 17:11: "I have given it for you"). (2) Compassion-responsive: Psa 78:38 explicitly states God's compassion as the basis — "being compassionate, he atoned for their iniquity and did not destroy them" — suggesting a responsive divine disposition toward the persistently sinful. (3) Name/glory-driven: Psa 79:9 appeals to God's atoning action "for the glory of your name," indicating divine reputation as a motivation. (4) Love-originated: 1Jo 4:10 makes the basis divine prior love — the initiative comes before human seeking. (5) Faith-conditioned reception: Rom 3:25 specifies that the hilastērios is "received through faith" — God sets forth the provision but it is accessed through faith exercised. (6) Withholdable: VCG-06 shows God explicitly refusing atonement when sin has become persistent and defiant (1Sa 3:14, Isa 22:14). The overall basis is covenantal-compassionate with faith as the reception condition, neither purely unconditional nor purely merit-based.

**T4.2.2 — Divine Interface — Human to God — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence reveals several inner-posture markers that characterize genuine human movement toward God in atonement-seeking. (1) Acknowledged sin-awareness: VCG-01 consistently presents the offerer as one who "has sinned" (Lev 4:22, 5:6) — the approach requires acknowledging real guilt. Lev 5:6 names the inner movement as guilt surfacing before the act of bringing the offering. (2) Contrition and self-abasement: Luk 18:13 (VCG-05) presents the archetypal posture — standing at distance, eyes downcast, beating the breast, confessing unworthiness. This is the most explicitly described inner-posture text. (3) Dependence on divine provision: The entire VCG-02 substitution cluster (Lev 1:4, Lev 17:11) implies the offerer's recognition that their own life cannot cover their own guilt — another life must substitute. (4) Sincerity over ritual precision: 2Ch 30:18 (VCG-03) shows God accepting those who "set their heart to seek God" even when ritually irregular — sincerity of orientation is required. (5) Faith exercised: Rom 3:25 identifies faith as the specific inner posture through which Christ as hilastērios is received. (6) Corporate solidarity: Deu 21:8 (VCG-07) and Psa 79:9 show communal approach as valid — individual and corporate postures both appear. Defiance and presumption are the negative contrasts, marking the postures that close the pathway (VCG-06).

**T4.3.2 — Human Interface — Giving — What inner conditions or orientations in the giver enable genuine extension of this characteristic?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence addresses this primarily through the priestly and intercessory giving registers. The conditions that appear in the text are: (1) Proper office and consecration: Throughout VCG-01, effective priestly mediation requires that the priest himself be in atoned and consecrated standing (Exo 29:33, VCG-02 — priestly ordination atonement). A priest whose own sin is unpardoned cannot validly mediate (cf. the contrast with 1Sa 3:14, VCG-06, where persistent priestly sin nullifies the atonement pathway). (2) Compassion and pastoral intent: Heb 2:17 (VCG-05) describes Christ as a "merciful high priest" — mercy and solidarity with the recipient's condition are named as the inner orientation enabling effective propitiation. The priest must be "sympathetic" to make effective atonement. (3) Intercessory love: 2Ch 30:18 (VCG-03) shows Hezekiah's appeal grounded in genuine pastoral concern for the people. (4) Absence of punitive intent: The inverse in Jer 18:23 (VCG-06) shows a giver deliberately withholding atoning intercession — the contrast highlights that genuine giving requires benevolent intent toward the recipient. The evidence does not describe ordinary peer-to-peer giving of atonement, so the conditions named are primarily those of the mediator-role.

**T4.4.2 — Human Interface — Receiving — What inner conditions enable or block reception of this characteristic from another person?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence identifies both enabling and blocking conditions for reception. Enabling conditions: (1) Acknowledged guilt and contrition: VCG-01 consistently shows the recipient approaching "because they have sinned" — the sin must be named and owned. Eze 16:63 (VCG-07) shows the atoned person as one who is silenced in shame — receptivity is marked by inability to self-justify. Blocking conditions: (1) Defiance and persistent unrepentance: VCG-06 (1Sa 3:14, Isa 22:14) shows that persistent, unrepentant sin can permanently close the reception pathway — the inner state of continued defiance blocks atonement. (2) Pride and self-sufficiency: Isa 47:11 (VCG-06) presents the "proud city" as one to whom atonement is unavailable — the inner-being posture of self-reliance is contrasted with the contrite posture of the tax collector. (3) Blood-guilt without restitution: Num 35:33 (VCG-06) shows murder-guilt as a category where standard atonement-reception is structurally blocked.

**T4.5.2 — Human Interface — Boundaries — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence shows a clear trajectory from covenantal-bounded toward boundary-crossing operation. In the OT corpus, atonement operates primarily within Israel's covenant framework: Lev 4–6 (VCG-01), Exo 30:15–16 (VCG-03), and the Day of Atonement structures address Israelites specifically. Exo 29:33 (VCG-02) explicitly marks outsiders as excluded from the atoned-and-consecrated status. Even within Israel, Num 35:33 (VCG-06) marks categories of persons for whom atonement is structurally unavailable. The prophetic texts (Isa 6:7, VCG-04; Dan 9:24, VCG-07) begin to expand the horizon: Isaiah's individual cleansing appears independent of standard ritual structure, and Daniel's seventy-weeks atonement has cosmic scope. The NT propitiation cluster (VCG-05) decisively crosses covenantal boundaries: 1Jo 2:2 explicitly states Christ's hilasmos is "not for our sins only but also for the whole world," removing the ethnic-covenantal restriction. Heb 8:12 (VCG-04) presents the New Covenant promise as directed to "the house of Israel and the house of Judah" but in Christ's application extends to all. Rom 3:25 frames propitiation as available to "all who believe," which contextually (Rom 3:22–29) includes both Jew and Gentile. The evidence thus shows progressive boundary-crossing, reaching universality in the NT.

**T4.4.3 — Human Interface — Receiving — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic from another but does not receive it?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — VCG-06 provides the primary evidence for non-reception. The inner-being state is consistently characterized as guilt unresolved and wrath unrelieved: (1) Permanent guilt-weight: 1Sa 3:14 ("the iniquity of Eli's house shall not be atoned for by sacrifice or offering ever") describes a state where the atoning mechanism is permanently foreclosed — the inner being bears guilt that has no pathway to resolution. (4) Conscience remaining condemned: The structural logic of the entire corpus implies that the mirror-image of conscience-cleansed is conscience-condemned — the person who does not receive atonement remains in the state the atonement was designed to resolve. Eze 16:63 (VCG-07), in its positive form (shame after atonement received), implies by contrast that prior to atonement the inner state would have been guilt-laden without the relief that follows covering. Non-reception leaves the person in an unrelieved, condemned, and exposed inner state.

**T5.1.1 — Nature of Transformation — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person — and if so, does it change the person's condition, the person's orientation to their condition, or both?**

Dan 9:24 (VCG-07) frames atonement in the context of "bringing in everlasting righteousness" — suggesting a comprehensive transformation rather than only pardon. The corpus predominantly emphasizes objective condition change (guilt removed, forgiven) but consistently implies that genuine reception produces orientation transformation as well.

**T5.2.1 — Nature of Transformation — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible in the verse evidence?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence presents a mixed picture with variation across contexts. Evidence for irreversibility: Heb 8:12 (VCG-04) states sins will be "remembered no more" — the New Covenant promise implies a permanent removal of remembered guilt. Dan 9:24 (VCG-07) frames atonement as an eschatological, once-for-all act producing "everlasting righteousness" — the permanence marker ("everlasting") implies irreversibility. 1Jo 2:2 frames Christ's hilasmos as a standing provision rather than a repeated act. Evidence for conditional/reversible transformation within the OT system: VCG-01's repeated atonement formulas (Lev 4–6) imply that each new sin requires new atonement — the transformed state does not prevent future guilt-accumulation. The persistent-sin cases in VCG-06 show that ongoing unrepentance can close the atonement pathway (1Sa 3:14) — suggesting that the transformed state requires sustained relational alignment to remain available. Psa 78:38 (VCG-04) notes God "atoning repeatedly" for Israel's recurrent sin — suggesting the OT atonement was regularly renewed rather than once-for-all. The evidence distinguishes between the OT sacrificial system (requiring repeated atonement, transformation potentially disrupted by new sin) and the NT once-for-all propitiation (Heb 8:12, 1Jo 2:2) where transformation is presented as more permanently secured. The irreversibility question resolves differently under each system.

**T5.7.1 — Mechanism of Change — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person — discipline, encounter, gradual formation, sudden transformation, or something else?**

**T5.1.2 — Mechanism of Change — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?**

**[CHAR-4]** E — The evidence shows two distinguishable mechanism-contexts. The mechanism-difference between OT and NT is: OT = prescribed sacrifice + priestly mediation + repeated; NT = Christ's blood + faith-reception + once-for-all. VCG-04's divine-initiative cases (Psa 65:3, Psa 78:38, Isa 6:7) represent a third mechanism-type: direct divine-compassion atonement outside the standard sacrificial system, suggesting God is not mechanistically bound to the ritual system even while providing it.
