## T3 — The Inner Faculties

---

### T3.1.1 — Does this characteristic engage the perceptive faculty, and if so, which inner sense and how?

**[D-VCG-03]**
E — Yes. Act 17:16 (M02-D-VCG-03) is the definitive evidence: Paul's spirit is provoked by sight — he sees Athens filled with idols, and this visual moral perception ignites the inner provocation. The inner sense engaged is moral-spiritual sight: the perceptive faculty reads the moral significance of what is observed (idol-worship = violation of God's exclusive claim) and the anger is the response to that reading. The inner sense is not merely physical seeing but moral discernment of what is seen.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Neh 5:6 (M02-B-VCG-04): Nehemiah hears the outcry of the oppressed poor and becomes very angry — the inner sense of hearing is the channel through which righteous anger is aroused. The perceptive faculty receives the moral cry and anger responds.

**[B-VCG-01]**
E — God's anger in M02-B-VCG-01 is consistently aroused by perception of Israel's acts: Num 25:3 (kindled by Baal worship), Jos 7:1 (kindled by one man's covenant breach) — the divine perceptive faculty registers the moral violation and the anger is the responsive state.

---

### T3.1.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair the perceptive faculty?

**[B-VCG-06, A-VCG-03]**
E — Anger impairs perception. Isa 42:25 (M02-B-VCG-06): when God's rage is poured out on Israel in battle, "the people failed to perceive or take it to heart — the inner faculty of understanding is closed off." The rage impairs the perceptive capacity of those under it — they cannot read what is happening to them. Pro 27:4 (M02-E-VCG-04) describes jealousy as irresistible and overwhelming — implying it overrides the moderating functions of perception and discernment.

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Job 36:18 (M02-B-VCG-03) shows rage as "inward enticement — it can seduce a person into scoffing" — the rage distorts moral perception rather than enabling it. The filling, possessing form of anger bypasses moral perceptive capacity and substitutes its own driven energy.

---

### T3.1.3 — What does the pattern of engagement or non-engagement with perception reveal?

**[D-VCG-03 vs B-VCG-03]**
E — The pattern reveals a fundamental asymmetry: morally-calibrated anger is triggered by accurate moral perception (Act 17:16; Neh 5:6) and enhances the moral clarity of the person who experiences it. Ungoverned/possessing anger bypasses perception and drives action from its own momentum (Est 3:5; Dan 3:13). The characteristic's relationship to perception is the primary marker of whether the anger is rightly ordered or disordered.

---

### T3.2.1 — Does this characteristic engage the cognitive faculty, and if so, how?

**[B-VCG-02, B-VCG-04]**
E — Yes. Gen 4:6 (M02-B-VCG-02): God questions Cain directly about his incensed state — "the emotion is subject to moral examination and is not beyond the person's control." The cognitive faculty of self-examination is engaged by the anger. Job 32:2–5 (M02-B-VCG-04): Elihu's anger arises from theological reasoning — he perceives a logical failure (Job's self-justification before God) and the anger is the response to that cognitive assessment. The anger is cognitively grounded.

**[A-VCG-03]**
E — Rom 2:5 (M02-A-VCG-03): a hard and impenitent heart "actively accumulates wrath as a mounting store of divine judgment" — the cognitive orientation of stubborn unbelief is the mechanism that accumulates the wrath-consequence. The cognitive faculty of assessment (of one's own moral state before God) is the faculty engaged.

---

### T3.2.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair cognition?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage bypasses cognition. Dan 3:13 (M02-B-VCG-03): Nebuchadnezzar's furious rage "bypasses deliberation and demands instant compliance." Est 3:5 (M02-B-VCG-03): rage filling Haman "takes possession of him and will drive all subsequent planning against Mordecai" — the planning is driven by rage rather than by free deliberation. The possessing form bypasses the normal deliberative function of cognition.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Righteous anger deepens cognition toward its moral target: Elihu's anger at theological error (Job 32:2–5) drives him to articulate a careful theological case (Job 33 onwards). The anger intensifies cognitive engagement with the problem.

---

### T3.2.3 — What does the pattern reveal about the nature of this characteristic?

**[B-VCG-02 vs B-VCG-03]**
E — The pattern reveals that the characteristic's relationship to cognition depends entirely on its form. When anger arises from accurate cognitive-moral assessment, it can deepen and sharpen that assessment (righteous anger, M02-B-VCG-04). When it arises from wounded pride and takes a possessing form, it bypasses cognition and drives from its own momentum (M02-B-VCG-03). The characteristic is not inherently cognitive or anti-cognitive — its relationship to cognition depends on its moral grounding.

---

### T3.3.1 — Does this characteristic engage the memory faculty?

**[A-VCG-01]**
E — Yes. Heb 3:11 and 4:3 (M02-A-VCG-01) deploy the wilderness generation's wrath-experience as a canonical memory that functions normatively for future generations — the oath of wrath is remembered and its memorial function is explicit in how Hebrews uses it. Deu 9:7 (M02-A-VCG-04): "Remember how you provoked God to wrath" — memory of past divine anger is commanded as a formative inner exercise for the present generation.

**[A-VCG-03]**
E — Rev 16:19 (M02-A-VCG-03): God's wrath toward Babylon is described as "remembered, purposeful divine disposition that delivers the full penalty long stored up" — the wrath itself is a form of divine memory, holding the record of accumulated offenses until their full reckoning.

---

### T3.3.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair memory?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage temporarily impairs memory and related faculties: Est 2:1 (M02-B-VCG-03) — "the rage's subsiding allows memory and reflection to return, showing rage as a consuming inner state that temporarily displaces other faculties." While the rage was full, memory and reflection were displaced. Impairment is temporary but real.

**[A-VCG-01/03]**
E — Divine wrath as a memorial-category (Deu 9:7; Heb 3:11) engages and deepens memory in the recipient — the past wrath-event is to be held in formative memory as a warning. The characteristic, in this register, is a tool for embedding formative moral memory in the covenant people.

---

### T3.3.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[A-VCG-01 vs B-VCG-03]**
E — The pattern reveals a structural contrast: wrath-as-recorded-verdict engages and builds memory (it is held in the canonical record and commanded to be remembered), while possessing rage displaces memory temporarily. Wrath in its judicial form is memory-constructive; rage in its possessing form is memory-disruptive.

---

### T3.4.1 — Does this characteristic engage the affective faculty, and if so, how?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — The affective faculty is the primary faculty engaged — this characteristic is constitutively affective. All the thermal vocabulary (burning, kindling, filling, pouring) names affective states of high arousal. Eze 3:14 (M02-B-VCG-03): rage as felt inner heat in the spirit — the felt quality of the experience is central to what the term names. Dan 3:19: filling with fury — a fully affective state visibly transforming the person.

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02): indignation as one of several strong inner responses produced by godly grief — the affective register of indignation is explicitly named as a moral-emotional response to an encounter with wrongdoing. The affective faculty is engaged as the resonance chamber for moral assessment.

---

### T3.4.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair affect?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — In its possessing form, anger bypasses other affective states and displaces them: Est 2:1 (M02-B-VCG-03) shows rage displacing memory and reflection; Est 5:9 shows anger contrasting with and overriding earlier joy. The possessing form crowds out other affective states.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — In its righteous form, anger deepens moral-emotional clarity: Nehemiah's anger (Neh 5:6) does not displace other faculties but activates a focused, goal-directed affective energy toward righting the injustice.

---

### T3.4.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The affective faculty is universally engaged but the quality of its engagement varies by register. Righteous anger focuses and calibrates affect toward its proper moral target. Possessing rage floods and displaces affect — other feelings become inaccessible. The characteristic is constitutively affective, and its governing virtue is whether the affect is calibrated or consuming.

---

### T3.5.1 — Does this characteristic engage the creative faculty?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Yes, but in a distorted form. Est 3:5 (M02-B-VCG-03): Haman, "filled with fury," uses the creative-planning faculty to scheme Mordecai's destruction — "it takes possession of him and will drive all subsequent planning." The creative faculty is engaged but instrumentalised by the rage. The anger drives creative planning toward destructive ends.

**[B-VCG-06]**
E — Eze 22:20 (M02-B-VCG-06): God's rage compared to a smelting furnace that "gathers and melts metals" — a creative-refining metaphor applied to divine judgment. The creative faculty of design (gathering for refining) is engaged within the wrath-context.

---

### T3.5.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair creativity?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage instrumentalises creativity — it uses the creative planning capacity for destructive purposes. The evidence does not show creativity being deepened by anger in its possessing form; rather, creativity is co-opted and driven toward the anger's object.

---

### T3.5.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — The pattern reveals that anger, especially in its possessing form, does not destroy the creative faculty but redirects it destructively. This is consistent with the observation that Haman's planning (Est 3:6–15) is elaborate and systematic — the creative capacity remains functional but has been commandeered by rage. The characteristic's engagement with creativity is one of instrumentalisation, not enhancement.

---

### T3.6.1 — Does this characteristic engage the volitional faculty, and if so, how?

**[B-VCG-02, A-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, pervasively. The action-driving register (M02-B-VCG-02) is definitionally about anger engaging and driving the will: Exo 32:19 — "the incensed state is immediately acted upon — he smashes the tablets — showing anger as an inner force that drives decisive, destructive outward action." The will is the faculty through which anger issues into action. Judg 2:14 (M02-B-VCG-01) shows God's incensed anger "moving him inwardly to an active decision — giving Israel over to plunderers." The will's decision emerges from the anger.

**[C-VCG-01]**
E — Divine indignation as sustained stance (Psa 7:11 / M02-C-VCG-01) engages the volitional faculty in a different mode: as a persistent moral will-direction toward the wrong, maintained as an ongoing disposition rather than a single decision-event.

---

### T3.6.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair volition?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage bypasses deliberative volition: Dan 3:13 (M02-B-VCG-03) — "a sudden, imperious inner fire that bypasses deliberation and demands instant compliance." The will is overridden by the rage — the person acts without deliberate choice. Est 7:7 (M02-B-VCG-03) shows the king's wrath "driving him physically from the banquet" — the will is moved from the outside of itself by the anger's force.

**[B-VCG-04, D-VCG-03]**
E — Righteous anger enables and energises volition toward its proper target. Nehemiah's anger (Neh 5:6) enables decisive confrontation of exploitation — the anger provides the volitional energy that confronts what would otherwise be left unchallenged. Luk 15:28 (M02-B-VCG-02) shows the elder son's anger "paralysing his will — he refuses to enter" — the anger can also immobilise the will when it is resentment-driven.

---

### T3.6.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The characteristic consistently engages and drives the will, but the quality of volitional engagement varies dramatically: righteous anger energises the will toward just action; resentful anger paralyses the will (Luk 15:28); possessing rage bypasses deliberation and drives involuntary action. The will is the primary faculty through which anger issues outward — but the anger can drive the will, energise it, or bypass it depending on its form and grounding.

---

### T3.7.1 — Does this characteristic engage the agency faculty, and if so, how?

**[B-VCG-02, A-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, extensively. The action-driving corpus (M02-B-VCG-02) is definitionally about anger issuing into agency: Moses smashing tablets (Exo 32:19), Asa imprisoning the prophet (2Ch 16:10), Samson striking thirty men (Judg 14:19). In each case anger is the inner engine driving the external act. Agency is not just associated with anger — it is what anger characteristically produces.

**[D-VCG-01]**
E — The provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-01) also engages agency: God's anger, once aroused, drives active decisions — giving Israel over to enemies (Judg 2:14), sending plague (Psa 106:29). The agency is divine, but the structure is the same: anger is the inner energy, agency is the outward expression.

---

### T3.7.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair agency?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Righteous anger enables agency where there would otherwise be inaction. Nehemiah, hearing oppression, becomes angry — and acts (Neh 5:6–12). Samuel, angry at Saul's disobedience, intercedes all night — the anger drives sustained agential intercession. The characteristic is an agency-enabler for the person who would otherwise not act.

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage produces driven agency that bypasses self-governance: Dan 3:13 (M02-B-VCG-03) — the command "demands instant compliance." The agency of those around the enraged person is also constrained — they must comply immediately. The driven agency of possessing rage bypasses others' agency as well.

**[B-VCG-02]**
E — 2Sa 13:21 (M02-B-VCG-02) provides a counter-evidence: David's great anger at Amnon's rape of Tamar "does not translate into action or discipline, leaving the incensed state inwardly contained and unresolved." Here anger impairs agency — the inner state fires but the will does not act. The failure of anger to issue into agency can itself be a moral failure.

---

### T3.7.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[B-VCG-04 vs B-VCG-02]**
E — The characteristic's relationship to agency reveals its defining moral role: anger is designed to be the inner energy of just action. When it issues appropriately into agency (righteous anger → just act), the divine image is functioning. When it drives ungoverned agency (possessing rage → destructive act) the image is distorted. When it fires without issuing into agency (2Sa 13:21 — David's contained anger → no action), the moral failure is the disconnection of anger from its agency-function.

---

### T3.8.1 — Does this characteristic engage the moral evaluation faculty?

**[B-VCG-04, C-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, constitutively. Moral evaluation is the faculty through which anger is aroused in its properly ordered form. Gen 4:6 (M02-B-VCG-02): God questions Cain's anger — "the emotion is subject to moral examination." Psa 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-01): God's indignation is a constant, daily disposition against wickedness — the indignation is the felt moral verdict of the righteous judge. Jam 1:20 (M02-A-VCG-04): human wrath "does not produce God's righteousness" — the moral evaluation faculty is explicitly invoked to assess the moral inadequacy of human anger.

**[D-VCG-03]**
E — 1Cor 13:5 (M02-D-VCG-03): love is defined by being unprovokable — the moral evaluation faculty of love reads provocations and chooses not to take offense. The moral evaluation faculty is the faculty that manages the provocation-anger threshold.

---

### T3.8.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair moral evaluation?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage bypasses moral evaluation: Job 36:18 (M02-B-VCG-03) — "rage can seduce a person into scoffing, showing its capacity to distort judgment and lead the inner will astray." The possessing form corrupts the moral evaluation faculty, substituting rage-momentum for principled assessment.

**[B-VCG-04, C-VCG-02]**
E — Righteous anger deepens moral evaluation: 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02) — indignation as proof of genuine repentance shows that moral anger (indignation) is itself an evidence of the moral evaluation faculty working accurately — the person has correctly evaluated the wrong they did.

---

### T3.8.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The pattern reveals that the characteristic is the affective dimension of the moral evaluation faculty — it is what moral evaluation feels like at intensity. When moral evaluation is functioning accurately, anger is the appropriate felt response to wrongdoing. When moral evaluation is distorted (by pride, wounded honour, or rage-possession), the anger is misdirected. The two are not separate systems; anger is the felt force of moral verdict.

---

### T3.9.1 — Does this characteristic engage the conscience?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Yes. Gen 34:7 (M02-B-VCG-02): "the sons of Jacob are incensed because Shechem's act violated a moral order ('such a thing must not be done') — meaning their anger is conscience-driven, arising from outrage at an objective wrong done to their family." The conscience perceives the violation of the moral order and anger is the response. Neh 5:6 (M02-B-VCG-04): Nehemiah's anger at oppression is explicitly a conscience-response to injustice among his people.

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02): indignation produced by godly grief represents the conscience operating accurately — the person has genuinely reckoned with their wrongdoing and the indignation is the conscience's verdict on the wrong done.

---

### T3.9.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscience?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage bypasses conscience. Pro 6:34 (M02-B-VCG-03): "jealousy ignites a man's rage to the point where he shows no mercy in taking revenge" — the "no mercy" in the meaning signals that conscience's moderating function (which would counsel proportionality) has been bypassed. Job 36:18 (M02-B-VCG-03): rage seducing toward scoffing shows conscience being overridden by the anger's momentum.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Righteous anger deepens conscience: Job 42:7 (M02-B-VCG-04) — God's anger against Eliphaz for speaking falsely is simultaneously an act of moral endorsement (deepening the conscience of the community by naming theological dishonesty as culpable). The righteous anger of God functions as a deepening of moral conscience in the community.

---

### T3.9.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The pattern reveals that conscience is the moral faculty through which the anger-trigger is assessed, and the anger is the conscience's felt response when that assessment finds genuine violation. Well-ordered anger is conscience-governed anger. Disordered anger is conscience-bypassing anger. The conscience is the faculty that governs the anger → action sequence; when the conscience is active, anger can be morally calibrated; when rage bypasses it, the action becomes destructive.

---

### T3.10.1 — Does this characteristic engage conscientiousness?

**[B-VCG-04, A-VCG-04]**
E — Yes, through its integration of moral awareness, volition, and action. Samuel's anger at Saul's disobedience (1Sa 15:11 / M02-B-VCG-02) drives intercessory action all night — the anger is integrated with moral awareness of the theological failure and issues through the volitional act of intercession. This is conscientiousness: moral awareness activating will activating sustained action. Nehemiah 5:6 (M02-B-VCG-04) likewise: moral awareness → anger → confrontation → systemic reform. The full integrated sequence is conscientiousness-in-action.

---

### T3.10.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair conscientiousness?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Righteous anger deepens conscientiousness by providing the energetic integration of moral awareness and action that conscientiousness requires. Without the anger-energy, the moral awareness might remain inert (as in David's case, 2Sa 13:21, where anger without action represents conscientiousness failing to complete).

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Possessing rage impairs conscientiousness by separating moral awareness from the action: the rage drives action from its own momentum, bypassing the integrated moral-awareness → will → action sequence that conscientiousness requires.

---

### T3.10.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — The pattern reveals that righteous anger is the experiential energiser of conscientiousness — it is what makes the integrated moral response feel urgent and necessary. When anger is rightly ordered, it serves conscientiousness by providing the affective energy that turns moral awareness into action. When it is disordered, it undermines conscientiousness by substituting its own momentum for genuine moral integration.

---

### T3.11.1 — Does this characteristic engage the relational capacity?

**[D-VCG-01, E-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, constitutively — this characteristic is inherently relational. The provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-01) is entirely about the relational dynamic of anger: one party provokes, another is angered. Jer 11:17 (M02-D-VCG-01): the idolatrous act "reaches into God's inner being, stirring indignation by rejection and contemptuous dismissal of him" — the relational capacity for being affected by another's action is the vehicle through which the anger arises. M02-E-VCG-01 (divine jealousy) is even more relational: jealousy exists only where there is an exclusive relational claim and a potential rival. The characteristic is definitionally relational.

**[F-VCG-01]**
E — Strife and quarrel (M02-F) are the relational outworking of anger: Gal 5:20 names strife as a work of the flesh that "generates hostile conflict in relationships." The relational capacity is both the context in which anger operates and the dimension most damaged by its disordered expression.

---

### T3.11.2 — Does this characteristic enable, deepen, bypass, or impair relational capacity?

**[F-VCG-01, B-VCG-03]**
E — Disordered anger impairs relational capacity: M02-F-VCG-01 corpus shows quarreling as a form of relational destruction (1Cor 1:11 — divisive inner conflict in community relationships; Gal 5:20 — hostile conflict as a work of the flesh). Est 3:5 (M02-B-VCG-03): Haman's rage taking possession of him locks his entire relational focus on one object (Mordecai's destruction) — relational capacity narrows to a single destructive aim.

**[D-VCG-03]**
E — 1Cor 13:5 (M02-D-VCG-03): love's refusal to be provoked shows that the mature relational capacity governs and moderates the anger-response. The development of relational capacity (toward love's pattern) deepens the ability to receive provocation without anger. Inversely, anger's governance deepens relational capacity.

---

### T3.11.3 — What does the pattern reveal?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The pattern reveals that this characteristic is both produced by relational capacity (because anger requires a relational context — it is always about something or someone in relation to the person) and the primary threat to it (because disordered anger is the primary force that fractures relationship). The cluster is constitutively relational — it is an inner state about relationship — and its governance is the key condition for relational health.

## T4 — Relational Interfaces

---

### T4.1.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating from God toward the human person?

**[A, B, C]**
E — Yes, this is the dominant pattern across the cluster. God's wrath toward human sin is the most frequently evidenced relational direction across all sub-groups. M02-A-VCG-01: divine wrath resulting in exile and covenantal exclusion (1Ki 8:46; Deu 29:28). M02-A-VCG-02: wrath coming as future judgment toward those outside Christ (Mat 3:7; Rev 19:15). M02-A-VCG-03: wrath revealed from heaven against ungodliness (Rom 1:18); wrath going out against Jehoshaphat's alliance with enemies of God (2Ch 19:2). M02-B-VCG-01: divine burning anger directed against Israel's persistent covenant violation (Num 25:3; Judg 2:14). The God-to-human direction is the structurally dominant direction across the entire cluster.

---

### T4.1.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the basis on which God extends this characteristic?

**[D-VCG-01, B-VCG-01]**
E — The basis is consistently moral and relational: idolatry, covenant violation, faithlessness, and deliberate provocation are the conditions that trigger divine anger. Jer 11:17 (M02-D-VCG-01): Baal worship specifically names the basis. 2Ki 22:17 (M02-D-VCG-01): "the intent behind the idolatry was explicitly to provoke God to anger" — the willfulness of the offense matters to the basis. The basis is conditional on moral failure and responsive — it is not arbitrary or unconditional.

**[C-VCG-01]**
E — Psa 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-01) reveals a continuous moral basis: divine indignation is a daily disposition against wickedness as such — not episodic response to specific acts but an enduring stance. The basis is God's moral nature (his holiness) rather than Israel's specific acts alone.

---

### T4.1.3 — What does God's extension of this characteristic reveal about his disposition toward the human person?

**[A-VCG-03]**
E — Rom 9:22 (M02-A-VCG-03): "God's wrath is something he desires to display against vessels prepared for destruction, yet he withholds it with great patience" — reveals that even toward objects of wrath, God's disposition includes patience and restraint. Isa 57:16 (M02-A-VCG-04): "God will not sustain his anger indefinitely because unrelenting wrath would crush the very spirit and breath of life he created" — reveals that God's disposition toward the human person includes concern for the creaturely life he made, which moderates his wrath's expression.

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — Psa 78:38 (M02-B-VCG-05): "God's rage, though fully warranted, is repeatedly held back — his compassion leads him to atone for iniquity" — reveals that God's wrath toward his covenant people is consistently moderated by compassionate disposition. The wrath is real but the disposition toward the people is not purely punitive.

---

### T4.1.4 — If the evidence is silent on God-to-human operation, note this explicitly.

S — Not applicable. The God-to-human direction is the most extensively evidenced direction in the entire cluster. There is no silence here.

---

### T4.2.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in the human person's movement toward God?

**[D-VCG-03]**
E — Yes. Act 17:16 (M02-D-VCG-03): Paul's spirit provoked by idolatry shows moral-anger as the inner state that drives toward God in righteous concern — the provocation orients him toward God's honor. M02-E-VCG-03: Elijah (1Ki 19:10; 19:14) and the psalmist (Psa 69:9; 119:139) show jealous zeal for God's honor as the inner state of the person who moves toward God in devoted service. The characteristic in its jealous-zeal register is a movement-toward-God characteristic.

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — Gen 18:30 and 18:32 (M02-B-VCG-05): Abraham's intercession is structured around managing and not triggering God's anger — the human person's movement toward God in petition is shaped by the real threat of God's anger. The prayer is a movement toward God precisely because the anger is real.

---

### T4.2.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the inner posture required for this movement?

**[E-VCG-03]**
E — M02-E-VCG-03 (Elijah, Phinehas, psalmists): the inner posture is consuming exclusive devotion — the person who moves toward God in zeal has entirely identified their own honor with God's honor (Psa 69:9: "others' reproach of God falls upon him personally"). The posture required is a self-giving identification with God's claim.

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — Gen 18:30/32 (M02-B-VCG-05): intercession toward God while navigating his anger requires boldness constrained by reverence — Abraham presses the petition but does not exceed what the anger-threshold allows. The posture is bold but not presumptuous.

---

### T4.2.3 — What does the human-to-God direction reveal about the person's relationship with God?

**[E-VCG-03]**
E — The jealous-zeal corpus (M02-E-VCG-03) reveals a relationship of passionate identification: the person who is very jealous for God (1Ki 19:10) treats God's honor as coextensive with their own purpose. This is not merely loyalty but identification at the level of purpose and meaning.

**[D-VCG-03]**
E — Act 17:16 (M02-D-VCG-03): Paul's spirit provoked by idolatry reveals a person whose inner life is so aligned with God's exclusive claim that what violates God's honor provokes his spirit as personal affront. The relationship is one of moral solidarity with God's holiness.

---

### T4.2.4 — If the evidence is silent on human-to-God operation, note this explicitly.

### T4.3.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being extended by one person toward another?

**[D-VCG-02, B-VCG-02]**
E — Yes. The provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-02) evidences deliberate human-to-human provocation: Peninnah deliberately and repeatedly provoking Hannah (1Sa 1:6–7 / M02-D-VCG-02). The action-driving corpus (M02-B-VCG-02) shows anger from one person directed against another and producing outward action: Eliab's anger against David (1Sa 17:28); Saul's anger against Jonathan (1Sa 20:30); officials' anger against Jeremiah (Jer 37:15).

**[F-VCG-01]**
E — Strife and quarrel (M02-F-VCG-01) are definitionally person-to-person: eris (quarrel) as an inner disposition that "generates hostile conflict in relationships" (Gal 5:20).

---

### T4.3.2 — What inner conditions in the giver enable genuine extension?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — For righteous anger extended toward another (as correction, confrontation, or prophetic challenge): the enabling condition is conscience-grounded moral perception of genuine wrongdoing. Nehemiah (Neh 5:6) — righteous anger toward oppressors — requires that the person has accurately perceived a real injustice and the inner state arises from that perception. Elihu's anger toward Job (Job 32:2) requires that the person has a genuine theological concern, not personal grievance.

**[D-VCG-02]**
E — For destructive anger extended toward another: the enabling condition is wounded pride meeting non-compliance. Peninnah's sustained provocation of Hannah (1Sa 1:6) is a form of anger-extension enabled by rivalry and status-competition.

---

### T4.3.3 — What must the person have received or become before they can genuinely give this characteristic?

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — For righteous anger to be genuinely extended, the person must have received or developed moral perception calibrated to genuine wrongdoing rather than self-interest. Moses' anger at the golden calf (Exo 32:19) is possible because Moses had been in God's presence receiving the law — he knows what has been violated. Nehemiah's anger at oppression is possible because Nehemiah is himself a person of covenant integrity whose anger is aligned with God's own standards.

**[E-VCG-03]**
E — For the jealous-zeal anger to be genuinely extended toward God's honor: Phinehas's jealousy (Num 25:11) was recognised as matching God's own jealousy — the person must have received enough of God's own jealous devotion to mirror it. The condition is a prior alignment with God's own inner life.

S — Not applicable. The giving direction is evidenced through both the provocation and righteous-anger registers.

### T4.4.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic being received by a person from another?

**[B-VCG-07, D-VCG-02]**
E — Yes. The recipient-experience corpus (M02-B-VCG-07) evidences receiving wrath from God: Job 6:4 (poison his spirit drinks); Psa 88:16 (sweeping destroying force assaulting from within); Mic 7:9 (willingly bearing God's rage as consequence). The provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-02) evidences receiving provocation from another human: Hannah receiving Peninnah's sustained provocation (1Sa 1:6–7); Eph 6:4 (children receiving provocation from fathers).

### T4.4.2 — What inner conditions enable or block reception?

**[A-VCG-03, B-VCG-06]**
E — Rom 2:5 (M02-A-VCG-03): "a hard and impenitent heart actively accumulates wrath as a mounting store" — the hard heart enables the accumulation of wrath-consequence. The inner condition that enables reception of wrath-consequence is impenitence. Isa 42:25 (M02-B-VCG-06): the inner faculty of understanding being closed off blocks genuine reception of the wrath's meaning — the people receive the physical consequence but do not receive its significance.

**[D-VCG-02]**
E — Eph 6:4 (M02-D-VCG-02): the child's vulnerability to parental provocation reveals that the dependent relational position enables reception — the power differential creates the condition in which provocation takes hold.

### T4.4.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A-VCG-03, B-VCG-06]**
E — Isa 42:25 (M02-B-VCG-06): "the inner faculty of understanding is closed off" — the state of non-reception is cognitive-moral closure, a state of inner darkening where the significance of the wrath cannot be processed. The person experiences the consequences (physical, social, historical) but does not receive the meaning. This is a state of profound inner blindness.

### T4.5.1 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic operates differently within existing relational bonds versus across relational distance?

**[B-VCG-01, E-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, clearly. The covenant-relationship context produces the most intense and sustained anger: God's burning anger against Israel is repeatedly deeper, more persistent, and more personally significant precisely because of the covenant bond that has been violated. Num 32:13 (M02-B-VCG-01): forty years of sustained anger because Israel is God's covenant people who betrayed covenant. Deu 32:21 (M02-E-VCG-02): jealousy stirred by Israel "worshipping a non-god" — the violation is intensified because the devotion was owed covenantally. The covenant bond intensifies the anger when it is violated.

**[B-VCG-06]**
E — Against foreign nations, the anger operates differently — as a categorical judicial force rather than a covenantally personal one: Isa 34:2 (M02-B-VCG-06): "the Lord's fury is directed against all nations" — this is comprehensive, judicial, and less personally relational than the Israel-specific corpus.

---

### T4.5.2 — Does this characteristic operate within covenantal contexts only, or does it cross covenantal boundaries?

**[A-VCG-02/03, B-VCG-06]**
E — It crosses covenantal boundaries. The eschatological wrath (M02-A-VCG-02) addresses all humanity — Eph 2:3 (children of wrath by nature); Rev 6:16–17 (universal). The pouring-and-spending wrath (M02-B-VCG-06) is poured on Egypt, Babylon, Edom, and all nations — not only covenant Israel. The characteristic operates within and beyond covenant, with the covenant-context producing the most personally engaged expression.

---

### T4.5.3 — What does the evidence reveal about the relational scope?

**[A-VCG-02, D-VCG-01]**
E — The relational scope is total: divine anger has no exclusion in terms of who can be its object. Eph 2:3 (M02-A-VCG-02): all humanity is by nature "children of wrath." The scope narrows for specific expressions: covenant anger is more personal, sustained, and morally complex; anger at foreign nations is more categorical and judicial; anger at spiritual enemies (Rev 12:17 / M02-B-VCG-02) crosses into the spiritual-beings interface.

---

### T4.5.4 — If the evidence is silent on relational boundaries, note this explicitly.

S — Not applicable. The relational scope is extensively evidenced.

### T4.6.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic operating in relation to other spiritual beings?

**[B-VCG-02]**
E — Yes. Rev 12:17 (M02-B-VCG-02): "the dragon's fury is a specific inner reaction to being thwarted by the woman; it drives him outward into active warfare against her offspring." Spiritual beings (the dragon/adversary) experience and act from anger. The characteristic is not exclusively human or divine — it is operative in adversarial spiritual beings.

**[A-VCG-02]**
E — Rev 11:18 (M02-A-VCG-02): "God's wrath arrives as a decisive, timed response to the nations' own raging" — the nations' rage (which may include demonic spiritual influence, per the Revelation context) is the occasion for divine wrath's arrival. The characteristic operates across the spiritual-beings interface as both a spiritual-beings state (dragon's fury) and a response to spiritual-beings activity (God's wrath responding to collective human-spiritual rage).

---

### T4.6.2 — Is this characteristic a site of adversarial activity?

**[B-VCG-02, B-VCG-03]**
E — Yes. The driving-to-destructive-action register (M02-B-VCG-02/03) is a site of adversarial activity: Judg 14:19 shows Samson's anger as Spirit-empowered toward righteous violence, but the general pattern of anger driving to impulsive violence is the adversary's terrain. Rev 12:17 shows adversarial rage as a spiritual force driving warfare. Job 36:18 (M02-B-VCG-03): "rage can seduce a person into scoffing" — the seduction language implies an adversarial dynamic. The possessing-rage register is particularly vulnerable to adversarial instrumentalisation.

### T4.6.3 — Is this characteristic communicated, strengthened, or mediated through angelic ministry?

S — No angelic mediation of anger is evidenced in the corpus. The destroying angels of Psa 78:49 (M02-B-VCG-06) are agents of God's anger but are not described as mediators of the anger to the human inner person — they are instruments of its enacted consequence. Silence noted: angelic mediation of anger is not evidenced.

### T4.6.4 — If the evidence is silent on the spiritual beings interface, note this explicitly.

S — The evidence is not entirely silent — adversarial spiritual anger is evidenced (Rev 12:17). Angelic mediation is silent.
