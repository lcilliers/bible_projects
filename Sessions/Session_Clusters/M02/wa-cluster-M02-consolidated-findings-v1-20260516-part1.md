### T0.1.1 — What does the verse evidence reveal about the nature or character of God that this characteristic reflects or images?

**[A]**
E — The judicial-wrath corpus reveals God as a moral agent for whom wrongdoing cannot pass unmarked. Wrath is not arbitrary emotion but a structured response to moral violation: Rom 1:18 names it as revealed against those who suppress the truth; Heb 3:11 shows it as the ground of a binding oath; Rom 4:15 frames it as the judicial product of law meeting transgression. The characteristic images God as the one who holds the moral order with weight — what is wrong incurs consequence.

**[B]**
E — The burning-rage corpus reveals God as a being of intense moral passion who takes covenant betrayal with consuming seriousness. Cha.rah and che.mah across M02-B-VCG-01 depict God's incensed anger as reactive to specific acts of faithlessness (Num 25:3; Jos 7:1; Judg 2:14). The fire/heat vocabulary images a God who is not indifferent — a being whose inner state is genuinely engaged by the moral condition of those in covenant with him.

**[C]**
E — The indignation corpus (M02-C-VCG-01) reveals God as one whose moral displeasure is principled and sustained: Psa 7:11 names indignation as a constant, daily disposition — "a God who feels indignation every day." This images a God whose moral character is not episodic but enduring.

**[D]**
E — The provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-01) reveals God as a relational being who can be genuinely affected by the behaviour of those in covenant with him. Jer 11:17 names Baal worship as the specific act provoking God's anger; Jer 7:19 introduces the question whether provocation truly wounds God or rebounds on the provokers. Together these reveal God as genuinely responsive — not impassive — to human covenant action.

**[E]**
E — The jealousy-as-identity corpus (M02-E-VCG-01) reveals God as a being of exclusive passionate devotion: Exo 34:14 names jealousy as God's own name — not a description of God but a claim about what he is constitutionally. The characteristic images a God for whom exclusive devotion is not a preference but an identity-level reality.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Across the cluster, the verse evidence reveals a God whose character includes genuine, structured, morally calibrated anger. He is not the unmoved mover of philosophical abstraction but a being who burns with moral passion, holds wrath as judicial verdict, maintains indignation as a standing moral stance, and jealously protects exclusive covenantal relationship. The cluster as a whole images God as the one in whom moral seriousness is constitutively absolute.

### T0.1.2 — Does Scripture explicitly attribute this characteristic to God — and if so, what does that attribution reveal about its significance in the human person?

**[A, B, C, D, E]**
E — Scripture explicitly and pervasively attributes anger, wrath, indignation, burning rage, and jealousy to God. The attribution is so dominant across M02 that the human instances are the minority. Specific attributions: wrath (orgē) to God at Rom 1:18; burning anger (cha.ron) to God at Exo 15:7; indignation (za.am noun) to God at Psa 78:49; provocation of God to anger (ka.as) from Deu 4:25 through Hos 12:14; divine jealousy (qan.na) at Exo 20:5; Exo 34:14; divine jealousy (qin.ah) at Eze 36:5. The significance for the human person is that anger/wrath in the human inner being is not a regrettable infirmity but an image-bearing capacity — the human person is designed to bear, in creaturely form, something that God himself constitutionally is. When a human is rightly angry at injustice, they reflect the moral seriousness of God.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The cluster's preponderance of divine-subject verses is itself a finding about significance. Anger is not primarily framed in Scripture as a human problem to be solved but as a divine attribute to be respected and, in the human person, rightly ordered. The frequency of divine attribution places this characteristic at the centre of Scripture's portrait of God's moral nature.

**[E-VCG-03]**

This sub-register of the characteristic equips the person for the kind of fierce, exclusive loyalty that covenant demands.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The cluster's verse evidence suggests anger serves as the inner-being energy that refuses indifference to moral violation. It equips the person to register injustice with weight, to pursue just response, and to protect what is covenantally claimed. The created purpose is a morally calibrated reactivity — the capacity to care enough about what is right and wrong that the inner being fires with energy when the moral order is threatened.

### T0.2.2 — Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?

**[A, B, E]**
E — The evidence indicates both, clearly distinguished. Divine anger and jealousy are presented as eternal divine attributes (Exo 34:14; Psa 7:11; Nah 1:2), not responses to the fall — they pre-exist and transcend creaturely conditions. Human anger appears in the narrative before and after the fall with no indication that its existence is a fallen condition: Cain's anger in Gen 4:5 (M02-B-VCG-02) is the first human anger in Scripture, and the verse does not present the anger itself as a product of the fall — rather, Gen 4:6 shows God questioning its source and moral legitimacy, treating it as a faculty subject to examination, not as an intrinsic disorder. Eph 4:26 ("be angry, and do not sin") explicitly acknowledges anger as a legitimate inner response whose persistence, not existence, is the problem.

**[C]**
E — The indignation corpus supports the both/and: divine indignation is constitutive and pre-fall (Psa 7:11; Mal 1:4), while the human anger that requires governance (Jam 1:19; Eph 4:31) reflects the fallen condition's distortion of the original capacity.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The cluster's evidence distinguishes between the capacity for anger (part of original design, reflected from God's own nature) and the disorder of anger (disproportionate, misdirected, self-serving anger as a product of the fall). The characteristic is not simply a fallen reality — it is a created capacity that the fall has distorted but not abolished.

### T0.3.3 — What does the presence or absence of this characteristic in a person reveal about the condition of the divine image in that person?

Nehemiah's anger at oppression (Neh 5:6) and Samuel's anger at disobedience (1Sa 15:11) are presented as appropriate moral responses — their absence would be moral indifference. **[C-VCG-02]**

**[A-VCG-04]**

When anger is self-serving, disproportionate, or directed at the messenger of God's word, it reveals the image distorted rather than expressed. **[CLUSTER]**
E — The presence of rightly-ordered anger signals an operating divine image — a moral being who cares about what God cares about. The presence of disordered anger signals a distorted image — the capacity is present but misdirected. The absence of anger at genuine injustice may signal image-suppression or moral detachment.

## T1 — Definition

---

### T1.1.1 — What is this characteristic called in the programme — and what does the name itself signal about its essential nature?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The cluster is named "Anger, Wrath and Indignation." The name signals three distinct registers within one essential phenomenon: (1) anger as the broad category of the reactive morally-charged inner state; (2) wrath, implying the heavier judicial and covenantal register — settled, purposeful, and consequential; (3) indignation, implying the more principled and sustained moral-displeasure register. The name correctly signals that this cluster is not a simple single phenomenon but a family of related states sharing a common root in moral reactive passion.

---

### T1.1.2 — What do the primary Hebrew and Greek terms reveal at the definitional level?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The primary Hebrew terms: *cha.rah* (to kindle/be incensed) — a verb root meaning to burn or kindle, foregrounding the heat metaphor as definitional; *che.mah* (rage/wrath) — related to heat, the noun of the burning interior state; *cha.ron* (burning anger) — an intensifying noun specifically for the burning quality of anger at its most intense; *orgē* (Greek, wrath) — at the definitional level, a settled, purposeful inner disposition; *orgizō* (Greek, to anger) — the verb of the anger-event, the eruption. These terms reveal that the characteristic is definitionally thermal and reactive at root — it is the inner being heating up in response to moral violation. The Greek *orgē* adds the judicial-structural dimension absent from the pure-heat Hebrew vocabulary.

---

### T1.1.3 — Does the name carry directional, relational, or constitutional implications?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The name carries relational implications from the outset: anger, wrath, and indignation are inherently directed — they require an object (the wrongdoing, the person who committed it, the situation that is wrong). The cluster is not about a free-floating inner temperature but about a reactive state with a specific directional structure: something is perceived as wrong and the inner being fires against it. The directionality is intrinsic to the characteristic's definition.

---

### T1.2.1 — What kind of inner-being phenomenon does this characteristic appear to be?

**[A, B]**
E — Wrath and burning rage in the primary corpora appear to be primarily a condition — a state of the inner being that arises, intensifies, persists, and resolves. Joh 3:36 (M02-A-VCG-05) presents wrath as a settled abiding condition; Gen 27:44 (M02-B-VCG-03) presents rage as a state requiring time and distance to resolve; Psa 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-01) presents indignation as a persistent disposition. But the condition has a strong dispositional element — orgilos (Tit 1:7, M02-C-VCG-02) names it as a character trait — and an act element in the eruption moments (orgizō in Mat 5:22; Exo 32:19, M02-B-VCG-02). The phenomenon is a condition that generates both dispositions and acts.

**[C]**
E — Indignation (M02-C) is primarily a disposition — a principled inner stance maintained toward wrongdoing. Psa 7:11 makes this explicit: not an occasional emotion but a daily disposition.

**[D]**
E — Provocation (M02-D) is primarily relational dynamic and act — the action of one party that kindles anger in another. Ka.as is consistently the act of provoking rather than the state of anger itself.

**[E]**
E — Jealousy (M02-E) is a condition (Num 5:14 describes it as a spirit that comes over a person) and a disposition (divine jealousy as constitutional identity, Exo 34:14).

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The cluster evidences a complex phenomenon that is simultaneously: a condition (a state of the inner being that persists through an episode), a disposition (a constitutional orientation that makes one prone to anger responses), and an act (the specific eruption moment). Different VCGs capture different faces of the same fundamental phenomenon.

---

### T1.2.2 — Is this characteristic simple in structure or does it have constituent elements?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The characteristic has constituent elements, clearly evidenced. The Jeremiah corpus (Jer 21:5, M02-B-VCG-06) explicitly lists anger, fury, and great wrath as three layered and escalating elements accompanying a single divine action. Psa 78:49 (M02-C-VCG-01) lists indignation as a distinct inner dimension alongside wrath and distress. The jealousy corpus shows jealousy containing possessive-protectiveness plus anger at violation plus driving-energy toward responsive action. Indignation contains a moral-verdict element plus a relational-direction element (toward the offender/offense). The characteristic is not simple — it is a structured composite of moral assessment, thermal arousal, directional energy, and driving will.

---

### T1.2.3 — What is the current best working description of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Drawing from the verse evidence: Anger, Wrath and Indignation is the cluster of inner-being states that constitute the human person's morally charged reactive passion — a thermally intense, directionally structured, action-driving condition that arises when a moral claim (personal, relational, or covenantal) is perceived as violated. It operates at multiple intensities (from brooding displeasure at M02-C-VCG-03 through scorching indignation at M02-B-VCG-04 to rage that fills and possesses at M02-B-VCG-03), across multiple sub-registers (wrath as judicial settled verdict, burning as thermal eruptive force, indignation as principled moral stance, jealousy as possessive-protective passion, provocation as the arousal-dynamic, strife as the relational outworking), and in response to both personal and moral offense. The constitutional location is primarily the inner person as a whole, with somatic expression through heat, face, and bodily comportment.

**[C-VCG-02]**

**[CLUSTER]**

**[A-VCG-04]**

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — Eph 4:26 (M02-C-VCG-02) and Eph 4:31 (M02-A-VCG-04) show anger in its entrenched form excluding the Spirit's presence and the relational health of the community.

**[F-VCG-01]**
E — Strife explicitly excludes the community of the Spirit: Gal 5:20 (M02-F-VCG-01) lists it as a work of the flesh; 1Ti 2:8 (M02-A-VCG-04) names anger as incompatible with holy prayer.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The characteristic, in its disordered form, excludes mercy, effective righteous action, the Spirit's community, genuine prayer, and restored relationship. In its ordered form (righteous anger), it excludes indifference to moral violation.

### T1.3.3 — What is this characteristic not — where does it end and something else begin?

**[A vs B]**
E — Wrath-as-judicial-verdict (M02-A) is not the same as burning-heat-rage (M02-B). Wrath is settled, purposeful, and structurally operative; burning rage is thermally intense, eruptive, and consuming. The distinction is within the cluster — the same base reality operating at different registers and with different structural emphases.

**[B vs C]**
E — Burning rage (M02-B) is not indignation (M02-C). Burning rage is thermally intense and erupts; indignation is a principled moral posture that may be maintained without eruption. The difference is intensity and form of expression.

**[M02 vs M06]**
E — Anger is not hate (M06). Hate is an inner disposition of directed aversion toward a person or thing; anger is a reactive response to perceived violation. A person can hate without anger (sustained cold contempt); a person can be angry without hate (the anger can coexist with genuine care for the person whose action caused the anger). Cross-routing flag from Phase 7: Neh 4:7's "collective hostility reaction" toward Israel's restoration sits at the M02/M06 boundary — the inner content there is closer to hostile enmity than to burning anger.

**[M02-E vs M28]**
E — Jealousy-as-possessive-protective-passion (M02-E-VCG-01/02/03) is not envy-as-covetous-desire (M28). Jealousy protects what is already claimed; envy desires what belongs to another. The phase 3 debate retained the jealousy vocabulary in M02 because the dominant register is protective-possession, but M02-E-VCG-04 (possessive covetous envy) sits closest to the M28 register.

**[D-VCG-01]**

**[CLUSTER]**

### T1.4.2 — Does this characteristic operate differently depending on context, direction, or constitutional level?

**[A vs B]**
E — Context produces distinct operation: in covenant-legal context, wrath operates as judicial verdict (M02-A); in covenant-violation narrative, wrath operates as consuming disciplinary fire (M02-B-VCG-01). The same divine anger looks structurally different in the courtroom register (orgē / Heb 3:11) versus the narrative-relational register (cha.rah / Num 25:3).

**[B-VCG-04 vs B-VCG-02]**
E — Direction matters: righteous anger against injustice (M02-B-VCG-04: Nehemiah, Samuel) operates through confrontation, intercession, and speech. Anger driven by wounded pride (M02-B-VCG-02: Saul, Eliab) operates through punitive action and verbal attack. Same thermal intensity, different moral direction, different outworking.

**[E-VCG-01/02 vs E-VCG-04]**
E — Constitutional level: divine jealousy operates at identity level (Exo 34:14 — God's name is Jealous); human possessive envy operates at the level of a volatile emotional episode (Gen 30:1 — Rachel's jealousy merging with existential despair). The same vocabulary operates at different constitutional depths depending on subject.

**[C-VCG-01]**

**[F]**
E — Strife and quarrel are explicitly speech-based (M02-F-VCG-01): logomachia (1Ti 6:4) is word-quarrels; the dispute-loving disposition (1Cor 11:16) is named in the context of community speech patterns.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The characteristic has a significant speech-based mode: anger presses toward verbal expression; divine indignation is often expressed through prophetic speech or enacted-judgment as communicative act; word-quarrels are the quintessential speech-form of M02-F.

### T1.5.1 — What is the first or most immediate inner-being response to receiving or encountering this characteristic?

**[B-VCG-07]**
E — The recipient-experience corpus (M02-B-VCG-07) directly addresses this: Job 6:4 names it as inner spiritual poisoning — a toxic assault on the self that mirrors God's fury. Psa 88:16 names it as a sweeping, destroying force assaulting from within. Mic 7:9 shows a posture of willingly bearing the rage as consequence, trusting for shift to vindication. The most immediate inner response to encountering wrath is a sense of being overwhelmed and undone.

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — For the one who receives provocation that kindles their own anger: Est 3:5 (Haman filled with fury when Mordecai refuses to bow) shows the most immediate response is the filling of the inner being, taking possession.

**[B-VCG-02]**
E — For an observer of anger: Gen 44:18 (Judah entreating Joseph not to let anger burn against him) shows the immediate response to another's anger is apprehension and the protective petition to deflect it.

---

### T1.5.2 — Is the immediate response consistent across the verse evidence, or does it vary?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The immediate response varies by position: the one under divine wrath is overwhelmed and undone (Psa 88:7; 90:7 / M02-B-VCG-06); the one in whom anger erupts is taken possession of and driven to action (Dan 3:19; Exo 32:19 / M02-B-VCG-02/03); the one provoked is stirred and galvanised (Act 17:16 / M02-D-VCG-03); the one who witnesses another's anger is apprehensive and seeks appeasement (Gen 44:18; Pro 16:14 / M02-B-VCG-05). The character of the immediate response is shaped by the person's position relative to the anger.

### T1.5.3 — Where the verse evidence is silent on immediate response, note this explicitly.

**[C-VCG-01]**
S — The indignation corpus (M02-C-VCG-01) is largely silent on the immediate response to encountering divine indignation from the recipient's perspective. Most verses describe the indignation from the side of God's maintenance of it, not from the recipient's immediate inner experience. This contrasts with the burning-rage corpus where recipient-experience is explicitly evidenced.

### T1.6.1 — What does this characteristic produce in the inner being over time?

**[A-VCG-01]**
E — Sustained wrath produces permanent exclusion and relational rupture. Heb 3:11; 4:3 show that wrath as the ground of an oath produces a permanent closure: the rest remains inaccessible. This is wrath's sustained effect — not a temporary correction but a definitive relational verdict.

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Sustained possession by rage displaces other faculties: Est 2:1 shows that after Ahasuerus's rage abated, memory and reflection could return — confirming that during the possession, these faculties were displaced. Rage's sustained presence crowds out the rational and relational.

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — Sustained ungoverned rage produces ingrained disposition: Pro 19:19 (M02-B-VCG-05) shows a man of great rage bearing inescapable consequences — the cycle perpetuates itself. The sustained effect of ungoverned anger is a deepening groove of reactivity that becomes its own trap.

**[E-VCG-04]**
E — Sustained covetous jealousy produces physical deterioration: Pro 14:30 (M02-E-VCG-04) names envy as rotting the bones — the sustained inner state of covetous jealousy destroys the person who harbours it. Pro 27:4 calls it the most irresistible and devastating of destructive passions.

---

### T1.6.2 — What states, qualities, capacities, or orientations does sustained possession of this characteristic establish?

**[B-VCG-02]**
E — Sustained anger toward a person establishes lethal intent: 1Sa 20:7 (M02-B-VCG-02) shows Saul's anger as a diagnostic signal that harm toward David is already decided in the will — sustained anger crystallises into purposeful violence.

**[C-VCG-01]**
E — Sustained divine indignation establishes a period or era: Dan 8:19; 11:36 (M02-C-VCG-01) name indignation as defining a historical period with a fixed end point — the sustained inner disposition shapes the entire temporal structure of events within it.

**[F-VCG-01]**
E — Sustained quarrelsome disposition establishes a community character: 1Cor 3:3 (M02-F-VCG-01) shows quarreling as evidence of a fleshly community condition — it becomes the quality of the shared life. Strife is infectious and self-perpetuating.

---

### T1.6.3 — Does the sustained effect differ from the immediate response — and if so, how?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Yes, clearly. The immediate response to rage is eruption and possession (Dan 3:13–19); the sustained effect is the displacement of other faculties (Est 2:1 — while rage was active, reflection was displaced), and at the longest duration, ingrained disposition (Pro 19:19). The immediate is eruption; the sustained is grooved character.

**[A-VCG-01 vs A-VCG-05]**
E — Wrath-as-oath produces immediate exclusion (Heb 3:11); the sustained effect is permanent inaccessibility (Heb 4:3 — the rest remains closed). The sustained effect is not a different emotion but the permanent structural consequence of the oath. Joh 3:36 (M02-A-VCG-05) is entirely about the sustained state — wrath as an abiding condition, not an episode.

### T1.7.1 — What inner conditions or orientations enable genuine reception of this characteristic?

**[D-VCG-01]**
E — The provocation corpus shows that the primary enabling condition for anger in the divine being is covenant betrayal — idolatry is named consistently as the specific provocation (Jer 11:17; Deu 4:25). Anger is enabled when the moral claim is genuinely violated.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — In the human person, the enabling condition for righteous anger is conscience-alignment: Neh 5:6 shows Nehemiah's anger triggered by a genuine moral violation he perceives clearly; Job 32:2 shows Elihu's anger rooted in moral indignation at theological error he perceives accurately. The enabling condition is moral perception of actual wrongdoing.

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — For possessing rage, the enabling condition is the combination of wounded honour and an unyielding object: Est 3:5 (Haman filled with fury because Mordecai refuses to bow) — the enabling conditions are pride invested in status plus the non-compliance of the other.

---

### T1.7.2 — What inner conditions block, distort, or prevent reception?

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — The restraint corpus shows that God's compassion blocks the full expression of his warranted rage: Psa 78:38 (M02-B-VCG-05) — God repeatedly holds back because compassion modifies the release of wrath. The blocking condition for divine rage is divine mercy.

**[A-VCG-04]**
E — Human wrath is blocked by inner governance: Jam 1:19 (M02-A-VCG-04) prescribes slowness to anger as a deliberate inner discipline — the blocking condition is the practice of restraint.

**[E-VCG-04]**
E — The Proverbs envy warnings (Pro 23:17; 24:1; M02-E-VCG-04) show that the will, properly directed toward fearing God, blocks covetous jealousy — the blocking condition is active will-redirection.

### T1.7.3 — What is the inner-being state of the person who encounters this characteristic but does not receive it?

**[A-VCG-03]**
E — Isa 42:25 (M02-B-VCG-06) is the most explicit evidence: "God's rage is poured out as consuming fire on Israel in battle, yet the people failed to perceive or take it to heart — the inner faculty of understanding is closed off, blocking recognition of divine wrath." The state of non-reception is cognitive closure — the inner faculty of understanding is not functioning. The person under wrath who does not receive (recognise, respond to) it is in a state of inner darkening.

**[D-VCG-01]**
E — Jer 7:19 (M02-D-VCG-01) asks whether Israel's provocation wounds God or rebounds on them as shame — the person who provokes anger but does not receive the intended moral signal is in a state of self-destructive moral blindness.

### T1.8.1 — What is the primary inner-being dimension of this characteristic?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — The primary dimension is the affective-emotional dimension, evidenced by the cluster's dominant vocabulary: burning, heat, kindling, eruption, filling, pouring — all describe an inner emotional state of high arousal. The programme's dimension vocabulary includes the emotional dimension as the primary classification for states like anger, and the verse evidence consistently places this characteristic in the register of felt inner experience.

Est 1:12 (M02-B-VCG-03) locates the inner fire "within him." These are primary affective-emotional location evidences.

### T1.8.3 — Does this characteristic carry secondary dimensions — and if so, what are they?

**[A]**

The volitional dimension is secondary but pervasive.

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Secondary dimensions confirmed: volitional (wrath drives purposeful decision); relational (provocation is inherently dyadic); moral-evaluative (anger consistently involves a moral assessment of wrongdoing); and in M02-E-VCG-01, constitutional-identity.
