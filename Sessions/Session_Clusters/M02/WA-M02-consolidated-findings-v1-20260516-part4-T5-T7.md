## T5 — Formative and Developmental Dimension

---

### T5.1.1 — Does this characteristic produce transformation in the person?

**[A-VCG-01, B-VCG-01]**
E — Yes. Wrath as covenant verdict produces permanent transformation of condition: the wilderness generation's exclusion from rest (Heb 3:11 / M02-A-VCG-01) is an irreversible transformation of their relational standing. God's burning anger resulting in exile (1Ki 8:46; Judg 3:8 / M02-B-VCG-01) transforms the national condition — from inhabitance of the land to displacement.

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02): indignation produced by godly grief transforms the Corinthians' inner orientation — it is a marker of genuine inner change (repentance). The transformation here is of inner orientation, not merely condition.

**[E-VCG-04]**
E — Pro 14:30 (M02-E-VCG-04): envy rots the bones — sustained jealousy transforms the person's condition toward deterioration. This is transformation in the negative direction.

---

### T5.1.2 — Is the transformation produced by this characteristic reversible or irreversible?

**[A-VCG-01]**
E — For wrath-as-covenantal-verdict: largely irreversible within the historical frame. Heb 3:11 (M02-A-VCG-01) — the oath permanently bars from rest. However, Isa 12:1 (M02-A-VCG-01): "God's anger has turned away" shows that even covenantal wrath can relent when conditions are met — suggesting conditional reversibility.

**[B-VCG-01]**
E — For burning covenant discipline: reversible through repentance and covenant return. Deu 13:17 (M02-B-VCG-01): burning anger must be "turned from" — turning is possible. Jon 3:9 (M02-B-VCG-01): Ninevites understand that God's burning anger "may be turned aside by repentance." The transformation of national condition (exile) is reversible through the covenant response.

---

### T5.1.3 — If the evidence is silent on transformation, note this explicitly.

**[F-VCG-01]**
S — The strife-and-quarrel corpus (M02-F-VCG-01) is largely silent on transformation in the developmental sense. It describes the state of the contentious person and warns against it, but does not describe a developmental arc of transformation through strife.

---

### T5.2.1 — Does the verse evidence describe a sequence of inner states?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — Yes. Est 3:5–7:10 (M02-B-VCG-03) traces a clear sequence: (1) Haman is filled with fury (inner arousal); (2) fury drives all planning (inner taking possession); (3) fury fills him again at each reminder (sustained); (4) fury abates only when Haman is executed (resolution through removal of the anger's object). The sequence is arousal → possession → sustaining → resolution.

**[B-VCG-01/05]**
E — The divine-anger sequences show a recurring pattern: provocation → kindling → expression as judgment → potential relenting through intercession or repentance. Exo 32:10–14 (M02-B-VCG-01/05): anger kindled → Moses intercedes → anger relents. The sequence includes before (faithlessness), during (kindled anger), and after (relenting or enacted consequence).

---

### T5.2.2 — What are those states, and what does the sequence reveal?

**[B-VCG-03]**
E — The sequence (arousal → possession → sustaining → resolution) reveals that anger does not self-resolve — it requires either an external resolution (the offense is addressed, the person removed) or deliberate inner governance (Psa 78:38 — God's compassion repeatedly holding back his rage). Left ungoverned, anger sustains and drives. The sequence reveals the characteristic as inherently dynamic — it does not simply exist but moves through recognizable phases that have predictable end-states depending on whether they are governed.

---

### T5.2.3 — If the evidence is silent on sequence, note this explicitly.

**[C-VCG-01]**
S — The sustained-indignation corpus (M02-C-VCG-01) is largely silent on internal sequence — it describes indignation as a persistent state rather than tracing its developmental arc. Psa 7:11 (daily disposition) is a description of a stable state, not a sequence.

---

### T5.3.1 — What mechanism does this characteristic use to produce change in the person?

**[D-VCG-01, B-VCG-01]**
E — The primary mechanism in the covenant-discipline register is consequence-based: divine anger produces judgment as discipline (exile, subjugation, plague) that is intended to move the person/community toward covenant return. Deu 13:17 (M02-B-VCG-01): "burning anger must be fully turned from before mercy and compassion can flow" — the mechanism is satiation of the anger through proper covenant response (atonement, repentance, execution of justice). The change-mechanism is moral consequence producing moral response.

**[C-VCG-02]**
E — 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02): godly grief → indignation → repentance — the mechanism here is emotional-moral encounter (grief over wrongdoing) producing a specific inner state (indignation as moral self-verdict) that confirms and deepens the change (proved sincere repentance). The mechanism is affective-moral.

---

### T5.3.2 — Does the evidence distinguish between mechanisms in different contexts?

**[B-VCG-01 vs B-VCG-04]**
E — Yes. In the covenant-discipline context (M02-B-VCG-01), the mechanism is external consequence → repentance → relenting of anger. In the righteous-anger context (M02-B-VCG-04), the mechanism is inner perception of injustice → anger → confrontation → reform (Neh 5:6–12 shows the full sequence). The covenant-discipline mechanism is consequence-driven; the righteous-anger mechanism is perception-and-confrontation-driven.

---

### T5.3.3 — If the evidence is silent on mechanism, note this explicitly.

**[F-VCG-01]**
S — The strife-and-quarrel corpus (M02-F-VCG-01) is silent on the mechanism of change through the characteristic. It warns against strife (Tit 3:9; Gal 5:20) but does not describe how the person changes through experiencing it.

---

### T5.4.1 — Does this characteristic operate in relation to suffering or affliction?

**[B-VCG-07, A-VCG-01]**
E — Yes, extensively. M02-B-VCG-07 is specifically the corpus of receiving wrath as suffering: Job 6:4 (spiritual poisoning), Psa 88:16 (sweeping destroying force), Mic 7:9 (willingly bearing God's rage as consequence of sin). The characteristic produces suffering when directed at a person (wrath received is the suffering of being under divine anger), and it is aroused by suffering when righteous anger responds to the suffering of others (Neh 5:6 — Nehemiah's anger at the oppressed poor's suffering).

---

### T5.4.2 — Does suffering deepen, test, reveal, or produce this characteristic?

**[B-VCG-07]**
E — Mic 7:9 (M02-B-VCG-07) shows suffering under God's rage as the context for a deep acceptance and trust: "the speaker willingly bears God's rage as a consequence of personal sin, trusting that God's inner disposition will ultimately shift from judgment to vindication." The suffering under wrath is accepted as deserved and becomes the occasion for trust's deepening. The characteristic does not produce the suffering's resolution — but suffering under it produces a specific inner posture of trusting endurance.

**[B-VCG-04]**
E — Suffering of others tests and reveals righteous anger: Nehemiah's anger at oppression (Neh 5:6) is aroused by hearing the suffering of the poor — their suffering produces his righteous anger, which is revealed as a marker of his covenant moral character. The suffering of others is the test that reveals whether a person has rightly-ordered anger (responding to injustice) or not.

---

### T5.4.3 — If the evidence is silent on the relationship to suffering, note this explicitly.

**[F-VCG-01]**
S — The strife-and-quarrel corpus (M02-F) is largely silent on suffering as context or producer of the characteristic. Strife is warned against as a cause of communal disorder but not specifically related to suffering in the developmental sense.

### T5.5.1 — Does the verse evidence show this characteristic participating in the longer arc of character formation and sanctification?

**[B-VCG-04, C-VCG-02]**
E — Yes. The righteous-anger register participates in the character-formation arc. 2Cor 7:11 (M02-C-VCG-02): indignation as a mark of proven repentance is a character-formation milestone — it evidences that the inner person has genuinely engaged the moral reality of wrongdoing, which is a step in sanctification. Psa 37:8 (M02-B-VCG-05): the command to relinquish fretting anger is itself a character-formation instruction — the sustained practice of releasing anger and trusting divine timing is a sanctification exercise.

**[A-VCG-04]**
E — Jam 1:19–20 (M02-A-VCG-04): "be slow to anger" is a formation practice — the deliberate cultivation of slowness to anger is one of the explicit character-formation instructions in the NT epistle genre. Eph 4:31 (M02-A-VCG-04): the removal of wrath, bitterness, and clamor as part of "putting off the old self" is explicitly part of the sanctification arc.

---

### T5.5.2 — What does the evidence reveal about the role of this characteristic in that longer arc?

**[B-VCG-05, A-VCG-04]**
E — The role is dual: the characteristic is both something to be governed and disciplined as part of sanctification (Jam 1:19; Eph 4:31) and something that, when rightly ordered, contributes positively to the formation arc (indignation proving repentance; righteous anger enabling just action). Sanctification involves both the purging of disordered anger (the old self's wrath, malice, and strife — Col 3:8) and the cultivation of rightly-ordered anger (the capacity for moral indignation that refuses to be indifferent to wrongdoing).

The eschatological-wrath corpus (M02-A-VCG-02) is definitionally eschatological: Rev 19:15 (the wrath behind the winepress of final judgment) is the fullness toward which all historical expressions of wrath point. 1Th 5:9 (M02-A-VCG-02): "God's purpose for believers is not wrath but salvation" — the eschatological destiny of the redeemed is specifically the negation of wrath, toward which the present deliverance from wrath points.

**[E-VCG-02]**

### T6.5.1 — Which adjacent characteristic most closely resembles this one — and what precisely distinguishes them?

**[M02 vs M06]**
E — The nearest neighbour is M06 Hate, Contempt and Hostility. The distinction: anger (M02) is reactive and thermally intense — it arises in response to a perceived violation and is aimed at the wrong-act or wrong-condition. Hate (M06) is dispositional and directed aversion toward a person or thing — it is maintained as a settled orientation, not necessarily thermally hot. Anger can exist without hate (the parent angry at a child's behaviour without hating the child); hate can exist without anger (cold contempt is hate without the heat of anger). Cross-routing flag from Phase 7: Neh 4:7 sits at this boundary — the coalition's hostility toward Israel's restoration is closer to M06's hostile-enmity register than to M02's burning heat.

**[M02 vs M28]**
E — The near neighbour for the jealousy register is M28 Envy, Greed and Lust. The distinction: jealousy (M02-E) is protectively possessive — it fires at the threat to what is already claimed. Envy (M28) is covetously acquisitive — it desires what belongs to another. The Hebrew qin.ah carries both dimensions but the dominant M02-E-VCG-04 register is possessive-relational; the M28 register is covetous. The boundary is the presence or absence of a prior claim: jealousy presupposes a claim; envy presupposes the absence of the desired object.

---

### T6.5.2 — Where the evidence shows apparent overlap, what is the precise boundary?

**[M02 vs M06]**
E — Isa 41:11 (M02-B-VCG-04): "those incensed against God's servant are moved by an inner hostility that will result in their shame" — this verse sits at the M02/M06 boundary. The incensed state is anger, but the hostility toward the righteous is the M06 content. The precise boundary: the anger is M02 (the thermal inner state); the sustained hostile orientation toward the servant of God as a person is M06.

---

### T6.5.3 — Is the distinction one of degree, kind, direction, or constitutional level?

**[M02 vs M06]**
E — The distinction between anger (M02) and hate (M06) is primarily of kind: anger is reactive and episodic (fires in response to perceived violation, can subside when violation is addressed); hate is dispositional and enduring (maintained as a persistent orientation). They may co-occur (hatred can generate anger; sustained anger can harden into hate) but they are distinct kinds of inner-being state.

**[M02 vs M28]**
E — The distinction between jealousy (M02-E) and envy (M28) is primarily of direction: jealousy is directed inward toward what is claimed/held; envy is directed outward toward what is observed in another. The directional asymmetry is the decisive marker.

### T7.1.1 — What are the primary Hebrew and Greek terms for this characteristic — and what do their root meanings reveal?

### T7.1.2 — What is the grammatical range of the primary term?

**[CLUSTER]**

### T7.1.8 — What does the relationship between OT Hebrew vocabulary and NT Greek vocabulary reveal about continuity or development across the Testaments?

**[CLUSTER]**

Continuity: the thermal vocabulary of anger (burning, kindling, heat) is preserved across Testaments — the Hebrew burning imagery (cha.rah, cha.ron, che.mah) is carried forward in the Greek by the heat-metaphor implicit in orgē's "swelling/pressing" root and by the explicit anger-as-fire imagery of Revelation. The provocation structure (D sub-group) continues across Testaments: the NT parorgizō and paroxunō carry forward the Hebrew ka.as and za.am provocation-dynamic. The jealousy vocabulary qin.ah is carried into the NT by the Greek zēlos (not in this cluster's 43 terms but operative in NT jealousy contexts). Development: the NT specifically develops the eschatological register of wrath (M02-A-VCG-02) with a fullness absent from the OT: the Revelation corpus (Rev 6:16; 14:10; 19:15) frames wrath in the context of the Lamb, the last day, and the undiluted cup — a fuller eschatological structure than the OT covenant-discipline context provides. The NT also develops the negative-governance register: Jam 1:19–20 and Eph 4:26–31 provide explicit formation instruction about anger-governance that is more directly anthropological-ethical than the OT corpus, which is more narrative and prophetic in form.

### T7.1.9 — Is there a term newly coined in the NT period?

**[C-VCG-02, F-VCG-01]**

### T7.2.1 — What is the function of the primary term within its primary verse?

**[A-VCG-01 anchor: Heb 3:11 / orgē]**
E — In Heb 3:11, orgē (wrath) functions as the affective ground of the oath: "as I swore in my wrath, they shall not enter my rest." The term is predicative — it names the inner state within which the divine oath was made, making the inner state legally constitutive of the oath's binding force. Grammatically, wrath is the prepositional object ("in my wrath") — the inner state is the context in which the consequential act (oath-swearing) occurred. The function is to name the moral-emotional foundation of a legal-covenantal act.

### T7.3.1 — Which human science framework is most useful as an interpretive lens?

**[CLUSTER]**
E — Three frameworks are relevant and identified in the science extract: (1) Clinical and social psychology (emotion theory) for personal anger — Averill, Berkowitz, Tavris, Spielberger, Lakoff-Kövecses. (2) Moral psychology for indignation — Haidt, Aristotle's orge, third-party punishment research. (3) Evolutionary and social psychology for jealousy — Buss, Parrott and Smith, Festinger. The most useful single framework for the cluster as a whole is the appraisal theory of emotion (Lazarus, Smith and Ellsworth), which specifies that anger is produced by appraising an event as negative, caused by another agent, and deserving of blame. This framework captures the blame-attribution structure that underlies virtually all M02's provocation corpus (M02-D-VCG-01: idolatry is blameworthy, it causes God's anger, it has a responsible agent).

### T7.3.2 — Where the human science framework illuminates the verse evidence?

**[D-VCG-01]**
E — Appraisal theory illuminates M02-D-VCG-01 (idolatry provoking divine anger): the blame-attribution structure is explicit across the 47-verse corpus — the wrongdoing is assessed as blameworthy (wilful, deliberate, in defiance of repeated warnings: 2Ki 22:17; Jer 44:3), caused by identifiable agents (Israel's kings, the people), and deserving of consequence. This is the full appraisal structure Lazarus describes. The science framework makes explicit what the biblical text presupposes.

**[B]**
E — The heat metaphor research (Lakoff-Kövecses) illuminates M02-B: the thermal vocabulary (burning, kindling, scorching, pouring) is not merely figurative but captures the physiological reality of anger — increased body temperature, arousal, heat. The science extract confirms: "anger is a hot, high-arousal state. Cognitively, it is constituted by the appraisal of wrongdoing and the attribution of blame. The cluster's thermal vocabulary sits at the intersection of the somatic and metaphorical dimensions of anger — both are real." The thermal vocabulary in M02-B is simultaneously metaphorical and physically accurate.

**[E-VCG-04]**
E — Evolutionary psychology's jealousy research (Buss) illuminates M02-E-VCG-04: spousal jealousy (Num 5:14–30) is explained by evolutionary psychology as an adaptation protecting valued relationships — the biblical law creates formal adjudication procedures for jealousy because it is treated as a real, judicially significant inner state whose validity God must adjudicate. The science framework illuminates why the law takes this seriously: jealousy over a valued relational bond is a cross-culturally consistent, deep psychological response.

### T7.3.3 — Where the verse evidence and the human science framework diverge?

**[A-VCG-01/02, E-VCG-01]**
E — Three key divergences: (1) Divine wrath as holy, ontologically grounded response: science has no category for wrath as the morally appropriate response of a holy being to evil — science treats divine wrath either as a projection of human anger or as a theological category outside its scope. The science extract notes: "divine wrath as a category is the deepest divergence — science has no analogue for holy wrath as a morally appropriate, ontologically grounded response to evil." (2) Righteous anger as virtue: science more readily pathologises anger (treatment typically aims at reduction); Scripture presents certain anger as not only permissible but required by moral seriousness. Aristotle's orge is the closest science comes to righteous anger as virtue, and the science extract acknowledges this explicitly. (3) Catharsis model failure: Tavris's research shows that venting anger typically amplifies rather than reduces it — this is consistent with some biblical wisdom (anger stored and managed rather than vented), but the biblical pattern of God's anger being "poured out" and "spent" describes a divine anger whose expression resolves it, not amplifies it. The human catharsis-model failure does not straightforwardly apply to the divine-anger register.

### T7.3.4 — Does the human science framework surface any aspect not yet addressed by the verse evidence?

**[B-VCG-05]**
E — Yes. The science extract notes two potential gaps: (1) "The role of rumination in sustaining anger (Nolen-Hoeksema applied to anger) may surface gaps in how the cluster's sustained-wrath vocabulary is classified." The sustained-wrath corpus (M02-B-VCG-05: Psa 37:1–8 warning against fretting/agitation) addresses the rumination-adjacent phenomenon — "fretting" is a form of sustained mental engagement with the anger-producing stimulus. The biblical counsel to cease fretting is consistent with rumination research showing that sustained mental re-engagement with the anger-stimulus prolongs the anger. This suggests M02-B-VCG-05 may be partially about breaking the rumination cycle, a connection the verse evidence supports but does not name explicitly.

(2) "Jealousy's attachment dimension (anxious versus secure attachment predicting jealousy sensitivity) may illuminate differences in the cluster's jealousy terms." M02-E-VCG-04's possessive spousal jealousy (Num 5:14 — jealousy as a spirit coming over a man regardless of wife's guilt or innocence) is consistent with anxious-attachment-driven jealousy in the science literature — the jealousy is not proportionate to actual threat but to the internal attachment anxiety. This connection is noted as a gap for further verse investigation: are there indicators in the corpus that distinguish secure-attachment jealousy (appropriate protective response) from anxious-attachment jealousy (disproportionate possessive fear)?
