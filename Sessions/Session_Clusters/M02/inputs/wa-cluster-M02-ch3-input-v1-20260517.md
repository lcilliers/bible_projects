Specific attributions: wrath (orgē) to God at Rom 1:18; burning anger (cha.ron) to God at Exo 15:7; indignation (za.am noun) to God at Psa 78:49; provocation of God to anger (ka.as) from Deu 4:25 through Hos 12:14; divine jealousy (qan.na) at Exo 20:5; Exo 34:14; divine jealousy (qin.ah) at Eze 36:5. The significance for the human person is that anger/wrath in the human inner being is not a regrettable infirmity but an image-bearing capacity — the human person is designed to bear, in creaturely form, something that God himself constitutionally is. When a human is rightly angry at injustice, they reflect the moral seriousness of God.
- *T0.2.1* — _What does the verse evidence suggest about the purpose this characteristic serves in the human person as created — what does it equip the pe…_
  → The jealousy-toward-God corpus reveals a purpose of consuming devotion: Elijah (1Ki 19:10; 19:14) and the psalmist (Psa 69:9; 119:139) show jealous zeal for God's honour equipping single-minded prophetic and devotional identity. This sub-register of the characteristic equips the person for the kind of fierce, exclusive loyalty that covenant demands.
- *T0.2.2* — _Does the evidence indicate whether this characteristic is part of the original created design, a response to the fallen condition, or both?_
  → The evidence indicates both, clearly distinguished. Divine anger and jealousy are presented as eternal divine attributes (Exo 34:14; Psa 7:11; Nah 1:2), not responses to the fall — they pre-exist and transcend creaturely conditions. Human anger appears in the narrative before and after the fall with no indication that its existence is a fallen condition: Cain's anger in Gen 4:5 (M02-B-VCG-02) is the first human anger in Scripture, and the verse does not present the anger itself as a product of the fall — rather, Gen 4:6 shows God questioning its source and moral legitimacy, treating it as a faculty subject to examination, not as an intrinsic disorder. Eph 4:26 ("be angry, and do not sin") explicitly acknowledges anger as a legitimate inner response whose persistence, not existence, is the problem.
- *T0.2.3* — _Is there evidence that this characteristic is oriented toward a future fullness — something the person is moving toward, not only what they …_
  → The jealousy corpus carries a restorative future: Joe 2:18 shows divine jealousy moving from judgment to pity and restoration; Isa 11:13 promises the abolition of inter-tribal jealousy in the messianic age. The future fullness for the human person is a freedom from destructive covetous jealousy and an entry into the divine jealousy's restorative mode.
- *T0.3.1* — _In what way does this characteristic express the divine image in the human person — what aspect of being made in God's likeness does it inst…_
  → Divine jealousy as identity-attribute is instantiated in the human capacity for passionate exclusive devotion. That God's name is "Jealous" (Exo 34:14) means the human image-bearer can carry, in creaturely form, something structurally analogous: a fierce protectiveness of what is covenantally claimed and loved.
- *T0.3.2* — _Does the evidence suggest that this characteristic is shared between God and the human person, or is it exclusively a creaturely analogue to…_
  → The evidence is clear that the characteristic is genuinely shared — not merely analogical. The vocabulary used of divine anger (orgē, cha.rah, che.mah, qin.ah) is identical to the vocabulary used of human anger across the corpus. Num 25:11 is the strongest single verse: Phinehas's jealousy is described as the same inner quality as God's own jealousy — not an analogy to it but a sharing of it (qa.na appearing for both). 1Sa 11:6 shows Saul's incensed anger as Spirit-empowered, suggesting that in that moment the human anger participates in the divine. The evidence supports genuine sharing, not merely creaturely analogy.
