## M02-B-VCG-01 — Divine Burning Anger as Covenant Discipline

**Context description:** God's burning inner heat directed against Israel's persistent covenant violation — not as final eschatological judgment but as the disciplinary fire that drives historical consequences: exodus plagues, wilderness wandering, exile, and territorial devastation. The verses in this VCG present the divine burning anger as something that: kindles at specific acts of faithlessness (Num 25:3; Jos 7:1; Judg 2:14; Deu 7:4), produces forty years of wandering (Num 32:13), results in national subjugation (Judg 3:8; 10:7), can be turned away through repentance and sacrifice (Jos 7:26; Exo 32:12; Deu 13:17; 2Ch 29:10; 30:8), and intensifies with repeated provocation (Num 32:14; 2Ch 36:16). The inner-being characteristic is God's burning anger as a covenantal disciplinary heat — a hot, consuming inner state that rises against faithlessness and drives specific punitive outcomes, responsive to repentance and intercession.

## M02-B-VCG-02 — Human Anger Erupting into Outward Action

**Context description:** Anger in human persons understood as an inner force that immediately erupts outward — driving speech, physical action, punitive decisions, and violent behaviour. The verses in this VCG consistently evidence the transition from inner burning to outward act: Jacob confronting Laban (Gen 31:36), Potiphar's anger triggering imprisonment (Gen 39:19), Moses smashing tablets (Exo 32:19), Balaam striking his donkey (Num 22:27), Samson striking thirty men (Judg 14:19), Saul's anger deciding David's fate (1Sa 20:7), David's anger at Nathan's parable (2Sa 12:5), Asa's anger imprisoning the prophet (2Ch 16:10). In these verses, anger is not delayed or contained — it moves directly from inner state to outward consequence. The inner-being characteristic is anger as an action-driving force: the inner heat that produces immediate, outward, consequential behaviour.

## M02-B-VCG-03 — Rage Filling and Possessing the Inner Person

**Context description:** Anger understood as a force that floods, fills, and takes possession of the inner person — an inner state that wells up to maximum capacity, consumes all other inner faculties temporarily, and visibly transforms the person from within. The verses in this VCG present rage as: filling Haman completely so it drives all subsequent planning (Est 3:5), welling up in Nebuchadnezzar until visible on his face (Dan 3:13–19), the king's inner fire located explicitly 'within him' (Est 1:12), an inner state that 'abates' when satisfied (Est 2:1; 7:10), Esau's fury requiring distance and time to 'turn away' (Gen 27:44), and the prophetic experience of rage pressing from within demanding release (Jer 6:11). The inner-being characteristic is anger as a filling, possessing force — an inner intensity that takes over the person and drives all subsequent thought and action until it is spent or satisfied.

## M02-B-VCG-04 — Righteous Human Anger Against Injustice and Folly

**Context description:** Anger in human persons understood as a morally calibrated response to injustice, theological error, or moral failure — an anger that is not impulsive but arises from conscience and covenantal concern, and which drives not violence but moral confrontation, intercession, or speech. The verses in this VCG present: Nehemiah's righteous anger at oppression of the poor (Neh 5:6), Samuel's anger at Saul's disobedience driving all-night intercession (1Sa 15:11), Jonathan's burning anger at his father's shaming of David consuming his appetite (1Sa 20:34), Elihu's moral indignation at Job's self-justification (Job 32:2–5), Moses' hot anger of righteous indignation at Pharaoh (Exo 11:8), God's anger being incensed at theological dishonesty (Job 42:7), Paul's spirit provoked (paroxunō - M02-D, but noted here for proximity). The inner-being characteristic is human anger as a morally structured response — anger that arises from conscience and drives righteous confrontation rather than impulsive action.

## M02-B-VCG-05 — Restrained and Governed Anger

**Context description:** Anger understood as an inner state that requires management, deliberate restraint, or is consciously held back — either by God (whose compassion leads him to atone rather than unleash), by wisdom (the wise person not befriending the rageful, not letting anger persist), or by divine command (cease fretting; be angry but do not sin). The verses in this VCG present anger not as an eruptive force but as a force present in the inner person that must be governed, relinquished, or redirected: rage as something to be abandoned because it leads to evil (Psa 37:8), a volatile state responsive to a soft answer (Pro 15:1), a disposition that infects through companionship (Pro 22:24), something no one can withstand when allowed to fully release (Pro 27:4), and God's own rage repeatedly held back by compassion (Psa 78:38). The inner-being characteristic is anger as a force subject to inner governance — an inner heat that is present but whose expression, duration, and consequences are determined by will and wisdom.

## M02-B-VCG-06 — Divine Rage as Pouring and Spending

**Context description:** God's burning inner rage understood through the sustained imagery of pouring, spending, exhausting, and releasing — a concentrated inner intensity that builds up, is held back, and then is discharged completely against its objects. The verses in this VCG present divine wrath as: poured out like fire (Lam 2:4; Eze 22:22; Jer 44:6), described as having 'gone out' as an active moving force (Jer 23:19; 30:23), a cup compelled upon nations (Jer 25:15), spent entirely until satisfaction (Eze 5:13; 6:12; 13:15; 21:17; 24:13), and concentrated to full strength (Rev 14:10 via orgē context; Eze 7:8). The imagery is predominantly Jeremiah-Ezekiel. The inner-being characteristic is divine rage as a stored and discharged intensity — an inner force that accumulates, is released, and exhausts itself through enacted judgment.

## M02-B-VCG-07 — Anger Experienced as Crushing Weight by Its Recipient

**Context description:** Anger understood from the perspective of the person who bears it — the inner experience of being under wrath, crushed, saturated, overwhelmed, and unable to stand. These verses evidence anger not from the agent's side (as heat or discharge) but from the recipient's side (as weight, burden, physical diminishment, and inner collapse). Job describes God's arrows carrying poison that his spirit drinks (Job 6:4); the psalmist pleads that God's correction not come through this fierce disposition (Psa 6:1; 38:1); Jerusalem has drunk the full cup to dregs (Isa 51:17–20); Israel's sons are so saturated they lie collapsed (Isa 51:20); God's rage is a crushing physical and emotional weight (Psa 88:7); the anointed laments the enduring fire burning against him (Psa 89:46). The inner-being characteristic is the experience of bearing anger — what it is like to be the recipient of burning wrath, felt as weight, saturation, and collapse.
