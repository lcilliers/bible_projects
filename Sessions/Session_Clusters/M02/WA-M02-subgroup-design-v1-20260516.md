## M02-A — Divine Wrath as Judicial Force

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of anger understood as settled, purposeful, and judicially operative — a divine inner disposition that responds to moral failure with binding consequence. The meanings in this sub-group consistently present wrath not as a momentary flare but as a force with legal and covenantal weight: it is the ground of oaths and exclusion (Heb 3:11; 4:3), a revealed reality descending from heaven against ungodliness (Rom 1:18), the destiny of those outside Christ (1Th 5:9; Eph 2:3), an impending eschatological event from which people flee (Mat 3:7; Rev 6:16–17; 19:15), and a divine disposition that 'goes out' against communities for collective covenant violation (Num 1:53; 16:46; Jos 22:20). The subject is almost entirely divine; where human anger appears it is either condemned as incapable of producing righteousness (Jam 1:20) or noted as an inner state requiring governance (Jam 1:19; 1Ti 2:8). What distinguishes M02-A from M02-B is not heat but judicial structure: the wrath here is measured, purposeful, and institutionally expressed — through governing authorities (Rom 13:4), through the law (Rom 4:15), through covenant curses (Deu 29:28), and through eschatological judgment. The inner-being characteristic is wrath as settled moral verdict rather than as erupting fire.

- Joh 3:36 — God's wrath is not a momentary reaction but a settled, abiding condition that remains on the person who refuses to obey the Son, contrasted with eternal life as its structural opposite.
- Heb 3:11 — God's wrath is the affective state that grounded his oath excluding the wilderness generation from rest, showing wrath as the settled divine disposition that produces definitive, lasting exclusion.
- Rev 19:15 — The wrath of God the Almighty is enacted through the conquering Word; it is portrayed as the force behind the winepress of final judgment, making it the supreme expression of divine justice against the nations.
- Num 1:53 — Wrath is an impersonal dangerous force that could break out upon Israel if unauthorized persons approach the tabernacle; the Levites' guard duty is the structural safeguard against this deadly anger being triggered.

## M02-B — Burning Rage and Inner Heat

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of anger understood as a consuming inner fire — an intensity of heat that wells up inside, fills the person, pours out, and burns. The meanings in this sub-group consistently use imagery of ignition, fuel, heat, and flame: God's burning anger is sent against adversaries like fire consuming stubble (Exo 15:7); Nebuchadnezzar is 'filled with fury' and the change shows on his face (Dan 3:19); Esau's fury must 'turn away' over time, requiring distance and waiting (Gen 27:44); Ezekiel experiences rage as a felt inner heat in his spirit (Eze 3:14); the scorching indignation that seizes the psalmist from within consumes him (Psa 119:53). This sub-group includes both divine and human subjects and spans Hebrew terms for burning, rage, fury, and heat. The inner-being characteristic is anger as thermal force — an inner intensity that kindles, ignites, fills, pours, and burns. What distinguishes M02-B from M02-A is phenomenology: M02-A captures wrath's judicial structure; M02-B captures wrath's inner heat and eruptive quality. The two sub-groups have significant overlap in the largest terms (especially che.mah and cha.rah), which carry both; placement decisions follow the dominant corpus register of each term.

**Representative meanings:**
- Gen 27:44 — Esau's fury is described as something that must 'turn away' over time, portraying rage as a volatile inner state that subsides rather than being reasoned away, requiring distance and waiting.
- Dan 3:19 — Nebuchadnezzar is 'filled with fury' and the change shows on his face, revealing rage as an inner state that wells up to full capacity and visibly transforms a person's outward expression.
- Eze 3:14 — Ezekiel experiences rage as a felt inner heat in his spirit—a bitterness and burning intensity in his own inner being that accompanies the Lord's strong hand upon him.
- Psa 119:53 — A scorching, burning indignation seizes the psalmist from within when he sees the wicked abandon God's law; it is an intense, consuming inner heat provoked by moral outrage.
- Exo 32:19 — Moses' anger burns hot when he sees the calf and dancing; the incensed state is immediately acted upon — he smashes the tablets — showing anger as an inner force that drives decisive, destructive outward action.

## M02-C — Indignation and Moral Displeasure

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of anger understood as a standing moral posture — a principled inner displeasure at wrongdoing that is less explosive than burning rage and more characteristic than momentary kindling. The meanings here describe an indignation that persists as a settled inner condition (Psa 7:11 — God's indignation as a constant, daily moral stance against wickedness), a righteous moral anger arising from repentance and conscience (2Cor 7:11), a brooding sullen displeasure at frustrated will (1Ki 20:43; 21:4), and the specific tone of contained or simmering anger before it erupts. What distinguishes M02-C from M02-B is that heat is not the primary register: the inner-being characteristic here is displeasure held as a moral verdict — the person knows what is wrong and maintains that position inwardly. From this sub-group also comes the moral calibre of anger: aganaktēsis is righteous indignation proving repentance; parorgismos names the overnight anger that must not harden into sin. The sub-group primarily covers human indignation (including righteous prophetic anger), divine moral displeasure, and brooding vexed displeasure.

**Terms (primary):**
- mti_id=922 · G0024 · aganaktēsis (indignation — 1 verse)
- mti_id=1411 · G3950 · parorgismos (anger — 1 verse)
- mti_id=114 · H2194 · za.am (be indignant — verb)
- mti_id=115 · H2195 · za.am (indignation — noun)
- mti_id=6801 · H2198 · za.eph (vexed — 2 verses)
- mti_id=91 · H0888 · be.esh (be displeased — 1 verse)
- mti_id=1410 · G3711 · orgilos (quick-tempered — 1 verse)

**Note on dual placement:** H2194/H2195 (za.am) are placed primary in M02-C for their standing-moral-displeasure register, but a portion of the za.am noun corpus (especially Psa 78:49; Isa 10:5; Nah 1:6; Hab 3:12) describes God's indignation as a burning, overwhelming force that sits closer to M02-A or M02-B. These are secondary to M02-A at the verse level. H2198 (za.eph) evidences brooding sullen anger — rightfully here rather than in M02-B. H0888 (be.esh) evidences inner displeasure at a situation rather than at a person — mildest register in the cluster, placed here as moral displeasure. G3711 (orgilos) names the dispositional tendency toward quick anger — a character-level indignation-liability, fitting this sub-group.

**Representative meanings:**
- 2Cor 7:11 — Indignation is one of several strong inner responses that godly grief produced in the Corinthians — a righteous moral anger against the wrong done, proving their sincere repentance.
- Psa 7:11 — God's indignation is presented as a constant, daily disposition — not an occasional emotion but a persistent inner moral stance that the righteous judge maintains against wickedness.
- 1Ki 21:4 — Ahab's vexed inner state over Naboth's refusal leads to withdrawal, lying down, and refusing food — the vexation is a brooding, sullen anger that shuts down normal life and signals deep inner disturbance.
- Tit 1:7 — Quick-temperedness disqualifies a person from church leadership; it is listed as an inner character flaw — an easily triggered anger that is incompatible with the measured, self-controlled temperament God's steward must have.
- Eph 4:26 — The command not to let the sun go down on one's anger warns against allowing anger to become entrenched overnight, showing anger as an inner state that must be resolved before it hardens.

## M02-D — Provocation — Anger Aroused in Another

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of anger understood from the side of its being stirred — the dynamic of one party's action kindling anger in another. The meanings here name provocative acts directed at God (Israel's idolatry provoking divine anger across dozens of verses in ka.as), the act of provoking a person's anger (parorgizō — fathers stirring children to anger; God stirring Israel), the inner motion of one's spirit being stirred by moral outrage (paroxunō — Paul's spirit provoked at Athens), the physical signal of anger's arrival (na.phal — Cain's face falling as anger seizes him), and the act of human provocation that enrages God and issues in judgment (re.gaz — the fathers enraging God). What unifies this sub-group is a relational, directional structure: provocation always has an agent who provokes and a subject whose anger is aroused. The inner-being characteristic is not the anger itself but the arousal dynamic — the movement from offense to ignition. M02-D is the hinge between M02-A/B (what anger is once aroused) and M02-F (what anger produces in relationships).

**Terms (primary):**
- mti_id=149 · H3707 · ka.as (to provoke — 51 verses)
- mti_id=40 · G3949 · parorgizō (to anger — 2 verses)
- mti_id=39 · G3947 · paroxunō (to provoke — 2 verses)
- mti_id=1141 · H4808 · me.ri.vah (provocation — 2 verses)
- mti_id=177 · H5307K · na.phal (to fall: angry — 1 verse)
- mti_id=227 · H7265 · re.gaz (to enrage — 1 verse)
- mti_id=91 · H0888 · be.esh (be displeased — 1 verse)

**Note:** be.esh (Dan 6:14) sits at the boundary of M02-C and M02-D: the king is displeased at a situation that was thrust upon him (a form of provoked displeasure). Placed primary in M02-C for its displeasure-register; listed as secondary here for the provoked-by-circumstance element.

**Representative meanings:**
- Deu 4:25 — Making idols is framed as an act that provokes God to anger, showing that human inner corruption — expressed in idolatry — acts as a direct stimulus that arouses God's inner anger against his people.
- Rom 10:19 — God deliberately provokes Israel to anger through a foolish nation, using angry indignation as an instrument directed against Israel's heart to awaken them to their own rejection of God.
- Act 17:16 — Paul's spirit was provoked deep within him — a sharp inner distress and indignation stirred by the sight of Athens filled with idols, locating the provocation in the spirit/inner person.
- Gen 4:5 — Cain's anger expresses itself through his face falling, showing that the inner fury has an immediate outward physical signal — the downcast face is the visible collapse of the angered inner person.
- Ezr 5:12 — The fathers enraged the God of heaven, resulting in the Babylonian exile and destruction of the temple; the enraging is the human act that provoked God's retributive inner response.

## M02-E — Jealousy, Zeal and Possessive Passion

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of jealousy and zealous passion as a distinct register of anger — an anger rooted in exclusive claim, possessive devotion, and the response to rival attachment. The meanings here span two closely related registers that the corpus does not cleanly separate: divine jealousy as a constitutional identity-attribute (God's very name is 'Jealous' — Exo 34:14; Deu 4:24), divine jealousy as a fierce protective ardor for his people and name (Zec 8:2; Eze 39:25; Isa 42:13), human jealousy as possessive spousal suspicion requiring adjudication (Num 5:14–30), and human zeal as jealous passion consuming its bearer in service of God's honor (Psa 69:9; 119:139; 1Ki 19:10–14). Where human jealousy is competitive/covetous (Gen 26:14; 30:1; Gen 37:11; Psa 73:3; Ecc 4:4) it retains M02 character because the corpus frames it as a fierce, destructive inner passion (Pro 27:4 — jealousy exceeds wrath and anger; Pro 6:34 — jealousy ignites rage; Song 8:6 — jealousy fierce as the grave). What distinguishes M02-E from M02-B is the source of the anger's energy: M02-B describes heat as the phenomenology of anger; M02-E describes jealousy as the possession-logic from which anger springs. The inner-being characteristic is a passionately possessive inner claim that generates fury when that claim is violated.

**Terms (primary):**
- mti_id=341 · H7065 · qa.na (be jealous — 29 verses)
- mti_id=344 · H7068 · qin.ah (jealousy — 41 verses)
- mti_id=2528 · H7067H · qan.na (jealous — 5 verses)
- mti_id=2756 · H7072 · qan.no (jealous — 2 verses)

**Representative meanings:**
- Exo 34:14 — God's very name is declared to be 'Jealous,' making jealousy a defining attribute of his inner being — an exclusive, identity-level claim that totally forbids worship of any other god.
- Num 25:11 — Phinehas's jealousy is described as sharing the same inner quality as God's own jealousy, making his act of zeal not personal rivalry but a mirroring of divine possessive love outraged by Israel's unfaithfulness.
- Pro 6:34 — Jealousy is named as the force that makes a man furious and drives him to merciless revenge; it is an inner passion that generates uncontrollable anger and retaliatory action.
- Song 8:6 — Jealousy is likened to the grave in its fierce, unyielding intensity and to fire in its consuming force; it is the most relentless and powerful expression of passionate love's inner drive.
- Psa 69:9 — Zeal for God's house has consumed the psalmist from within — this jealous ardor absorbs him so completely that others' reproach of God falls upon him personally.

## M02-F — Strife, Quarrel and Contentious Anger

**Core description:** This sub-group captures the phenomenon of anger understood as contentious relational conflict — the inner disposition that generates, sustains, and seeks dispute. The meanings here describe quarreling as an inner disposition of the corrupt person producing social disorder (Rom 1:29), strife as a work of the flesh that fractures community (Gal 5:20), a dispute-loving inclination that finds no support in apostolic practice (1Cor 11:16), a quarrelsome fury that drives political conflict (Act 12:20), word-quarrels springing from a craving for controversy rooted in conceit (1Ti 6:4), strife rooted in proud inner refusal to accept counsel (Pro 13:10), and a willful inner appetite toward conflict morally aligned with sin (Pro 17:19). The inner-being characteristic is anger expressed outward as relational contention — not the heat of erupting fire (M02-B) or the judicial weight of settled wrath (M02-A), but the disputatious inner will that generates and sustains conflict. This sub-group also includes orgilos (quick-tempered) as its dispositional anchor, and the Hebrew strife terms mats.tsah and mats.tsut which evidence anger-driven contention in relationship and community.

**Terms (primary):**
- mti_id=1139 · G2054 · eris (quarrel — 9 verses)
- mti_id=1431 · G2371 · thumomacheō (to quarrel — 1 verse)
- mti_id=6464 · G3055 · logomachia (quarrel — 1 verse)
- mti_id=560 · G5380 · filoneikos (dispute-loving — 1 verse)
- mti_id=1140 · H4683 · mats.tsah (strife — 3 verses)
- mti_id=6467 · H4695 · mats.tsut (strife — 1 verse)
- mti_id=38 · G3710 · orgizō (to anger — 8 verses)

**Note on orgizō:** orgizō spans M02-B (anger as erupting inner state driving action) and M02-F (anger as contentious relational dynamic). Primary placement M02-B for its burning-anger phenomenology; secondary here for its quarrel-producing social dynamic (Luk 15:28; Rev 11:18).

**Representative meanings:**
- Gal 5:20 — Strife appears as a work of the flesh alongside enmity and jealousy, identifying it as an inner drive of the sinful nature that generates hostile conflict in relationships.
- 1Cor 11:16 — A dispute-loving disposition — the inner inclination to be contentious — is named as something to be checked; it finds no support in apostolic or church practice, marking it as an inner tendency to be resisted.
- Act 12:20 — Herod's quarrelsome fury toward Tyre and Sidon is an intense, hostile inner disposition that drives political conflict, with the dependent cities seeking peace to resolve it.
- Pro 13:10 — Strife is the invariable product of insolence, while wisdom belongs to those who accept counsel; the verse roots strife in a proud inner refusal to submit to others' input.
- 1Ti 6:4 — Word-quarrels spring from an unhealthy inner craving for controversy in a person puffed up with conceit; such disputes are internally driven by pride and produce destructive social outcomes.
